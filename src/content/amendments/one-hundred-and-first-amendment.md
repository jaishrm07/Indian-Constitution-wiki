## Why it matters

This amendment is essential for understanding how constitutional design adapts to economic integration while redistributing fiscal power.

## What specifically changed

The 101st Amendment inserted Articles 246A (special power to make laws on GST), 269A (levy and collection of IGST on inter-state supply), and 279A (GST Council) and amended the Seventh Schedule to remove most commodities and services from the Union and State Lists, placing them under the unified GST framework. Petroleum, liquor, and electricity were temporarily excluded from GST. The amendment transferred significant fiscal sovereignty from states to a jointly governed council. Both Parliament and state legislatures were given concurrent power to legislate on GST for intra-state supplies, with Parliament exclusively governing inter-state supplies. The Seventh Schedule changes were significant because they dismantled decades of separately administered Union and state indirect tax regimes in a single constitutional move.

## The political and constitutional context

GST was a decade-long project to replace a complex web of central and state indirect taxes — central excise, state VAT, service tax, octroi, entry tax — with a unified destination-based tax. It required a constitutional amendment because taxation heads in Schedule VII gave both the Union and states independent tax sovereignty over different goods and services. The GST Council, with both Union and state representation, was designed as a cooperative federalism institution, a novel attempt to constitutionalise joint governance over an important revenue domain. Negotiations over state compensation guarantees, the treatment of excluded commodities, and the design of the Council's voting weights took years to conclude across successive Union governments. The passage of the amendment in 2016 ended a long deadlock between the Centre and several major opposition-governed states.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The GST Council has become a significant institutional experiment in cooperative federalism — decisions by weighted majority (the Union has one-third weight and states together two-thirds) give states a real voice in indirect tax policy. Disputes over GST compensation to states and Council functioning exposed tensions in the cooperative design, particularly during the post-pandemic period when compensation shortfalls became acute. The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Mohit Minerals (2022) clarified that GST Council recommendations are not binding but carry persuasive weight, reinforcing the Council's advisory rather than legislative character. The amendment is the most consequential federal fiscal change since the original Constitution. How the Council functions in practice continues to test whether constitutional cooperative structures can hold under political and fiscal stress.
