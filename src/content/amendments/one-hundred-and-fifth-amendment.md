## Why it matters

The One Hundred and Fifth Amendment matters because it corrected the federal imbalance created by the One Hundred and Second Amendment. It confirms that SEBC list-making is not only a national constitutional question; it also remains a state and Union territory question for local purposes.

## Reading note

Read this page as the companion to the One Hundred and Second Amendment. The pair shows how constitutional text can centralise one part of a policy area and then later restore federal competence through a follow-up amendment.

## What specifically changed

The One Hundred and Fifth Amendment rewrote Article 342A by inserting a second clause clarifying that the term "Central List" refers only to the list prepared and maintained by the President for the purposes of the Union, and that states and Union territories are empowered to prepare and maintain their own lists of socially and educationally backward classes for the purposes of their own laws and policies. The definition of "socially and educationally backward classes" in Article 366(26C) was amended to align with this bifurcation, making clear that the definition applies to the Central List and does not displace state identification. Article 338B, which governs the National Commission for Backward Classes, was consequentially amended to reflect that the NCBC's constitutional role relates to the Central List while state-level identification processes remain outside its exclusive mandate. The overall effect was to restore a two-track system in which a central constitutional list coexists with state-level SEBC lists.

## The political and constitutional context

The One Hundred and Second Amendment of 2018 had inserted Articles 338B and 342A and amended Article 366(26C) to constitutionalise the NCBC and create a Central List of SEBCs. The Supreme Court's Constitution Bench judgment in Maratha Reservation Case (Jaishri Laxmanrao Patil v. Chief Minister, State of Maharashtra) held in 2021 that the One Hundred and Second Amendment had the effect of taking away the states' power to identify SEBCs for their own reservation purposes and that only the President, acting on the NCBC's advice, could now add to or modify the SEBC list. This was politically explosive because it threatened every state's OBC reservation list. Parliament responded within months with the One Hundred and Fifth Amendment, designed specifically to reverse that interpretation and restore state competence over SEBC identification for state purposes without disturbing the constitutional status of the Central List.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The One Hundred and Fifth Amendment is a direct instance of Parliament using the amendment power to reverse a specific Supreme Court interpretation of a prior amendment. By clarifying Article 342A and the definition in Article 366(26C), Parliament reasserted that the federal logic of SEBC identification was not extinguished by the One Hundred and Second Amendment's constitutionalisation of the Central List. Courts have since read the two amendments as a constitutional pair, treating the Central List as the reference for Union purposes and state lists as valid for state reservation schemes. The episode is significant for the study of constitutional dialogue in India: a constitutional amendment was interpreted by the Court in a way that had consequences the enacting Parliament had not anticipated, and Parliament corrected that interpretation through a further amendment rather than through legislation alone. The pair also shows the practical limits of using constitutional text to resolve questions about federalism when the text itself is ambiguous about the relationship between central and state competences.
