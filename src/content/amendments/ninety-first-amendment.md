## Why it matters

The Ninety-First Amendment is one of the most practical constitutional reforms of the coalition era. It tries to make executive government leaner and defection less rewarding.

## What specifically changed

The Ninety-First Amendment amended Article 75(1A) to insert a constitutional ceiling on the size of the Union Council of Ministers: the total number of ministers, including the Prime Minister, shall not exceed fifteen percent of the total strength of the House of the People. Article 164(1A) inserted the identical rule for State Councils of Ministers, capping them at fifteen percent of the total membership of the legislative assembly. The amendment also inserted Article 75(1B) and the corresponding provision in Article 164, which provide that a member of either House who is disqualified on grounds of defection under the Tenth Schedule is also disqualified from holding ministerial office for the remainder of the term, closing the practice under which defecting members could be rewarded with cabinet posts before the disqualification process under the Tenth Schedule was concluded. Paragraph 3 of the Tenth Schedule, which had allowed a split of at least one-third of a party's legislature members to escape disqualification, was simultaneously repealed.

## The political and constitutional context

The amendment arose directly from the dysfunction of coalition politics in the 1990s and early 2000s. The practice of building unstable coalitions by distributing cabinet posts to smaller parties had led to councils of ministers in several states ballooning to sizes that were constitutionally and practically indefensible. At the Union level the problem was compounded by the use of the split provision in the Tenth Schedule: small groups of legislators could split off from a parent party and secure cabinet posts for the splinter group, making defection financially and politically rewarding. The National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution chaired by M.N. Venkatachaliah recommended both the size cap and the repeal of the split provision. Parliament acted on those recommendations in 2003 as part of a broader effort to stabilise government and reduce the incentive structure that the existing rules created for opportunistic defection.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The fifteen-percent ceiling has been applied across the country and has altered the arithmetic of coalition-building, forcing parties to be more selective about distributing ministerial portfolios. The repeal of paragraph 3 of the Tenth Schedule has been particularly consequential: the split defence, which had been used with increasing frequency and creativity in state politics, is no longer available, making it constitutionally harder to organise a formally protected breakaway group. Courts have applied the amendment straightforwardly, upholding size-of-council challenges where states exceeded the ceiling and confirming that the disqualification-linked bar on ministerial office operates as soon as a disqualification proceeding reaches a final determination. The amendment has not resolved all defection problems — the Speaker's power to delay disqualification proceedings has itself become a contested constitutional question — but it removed the most easily exploitable structural incentives for defection.
