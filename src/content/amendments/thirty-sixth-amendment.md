## Why it matters

The Thirty-Sixth Amendment is the constitutional record of Sikkim's accession to India. The amendment is significant not only because it added a new state — expanding the Union for the first time since Goa in 1961 — but also because it created Article 371F, a special provisions article tailored to Sikkim's unique historical, cultural, and legal inheritance as a former independent monarchy and British protectorate.

## What specifically changed

The amendment amended the First Schedule to add Sikkim as the twenty-second state of India with its four districts. It amended Article 80 to allocate one seat to Sikkim in the Rajya Sabha, and Article 81 to allocate one seat in the Lok Sabha. Most significantly, it inserted Article 371F, which contains multiple special provisions: it validates the Constitution of Sikkim of 1974 and laws made thereunder; it protects the rights and interests of different sections of the Sikkim population; it provides that the Governor of Sikkim shall have special responsibility for peace and equitable arrangement for ensuring the social and economic advancement of different sections; it protects existing laws in Sikkim, including those relating to inheritance of property, from challenge under Articles 14 and 19; and it preserves the pre-integration arrangements for representation of different communities in the Sikkim Legislative Assembly.

## The political and constitutional context

Sikkim had been a monarchy under the Chogyal (hereditary ruler) since the seventeenth century, and became a British protectorate under the 1890 Calcutta Convention. After Indian independence, Sikkim remained a protectorate of India under the 1950 Treaty of Sikkim. A pro-merger movement within Sikkim gained strength through the early 1970s, and a referendum in April 1975 produced an overwhelming majority in favour of merger with India. The Sikkim Assembly passed a resolution requesting merger, and Parliament enacted the Thirty-Sixth Amendment just days later. The accession was politically sensitive — China did not recognise it for decades, and the speed of the process drew criticism internationally — but it completed what the government presented as the voluntary integration of the last remaining non-integrated territory in the subcontinent.

## Long-term constitutional effect

Article 371F has generated significant litigation, particularly around the provision protecting Sikkim laws from Article 14 and 19 challenge. The Supreme Court in State of Sikkim v. Surendra Prasad Sharma (1994) and subsequent cases established that Article 371F creates a genuine constitutional exception for Sikkim-specific laws, including reservation arrangements and property inheritance rules that would not survive ordinary constitutional scrutiny. The provision protecting the income of Sikkimese residents from tax under certain arrangements — one of the more notable inheritances — has been subject to periodic political controversy. Article 371F remains in force as the constitutional charter of Sikkim's special status within the Union, representing one of the most elaborate special-provisions articles in the Constitution.

## Reading note

The Thirty-Sixth Amendment should be read alongside Article 371F in its entirety, and in the context of the broader pattern of special provisions for specific states — Articles 370 (Jammu and Kashmir, now significantly modified), 371A through 371J — that reflect the Constitution's accommodation of regional diversity within a formally unitary federal structure.
