## Why it matters

The One Hundred and Second Amendment is important because it moved backward-class identification from a mainly statutory and administrative space into the constitutional text itself. That move made the dispute over lists much more consequential.

## Reading note

This amendment is best read together with the One Hundred and Fifth Amendment. The two pages form a constitutional pair: first the Central List was entrenched, then state list-making had to be restored.

## What specifically changed

The 102nd Amendment inserted Articles 338B (establishing the National Commission for Backward Classes as a constitutional body with investigative and recommendatory functions) and 342A (which gives the President power to specify Socially and Educationally Backward Classes for each state and Union Territory, with Parliament making changes to that central list). It transferred the NCBC from statutory to constitutional status, giving it the same standing as the National Commission for Scheduled Castes and the National Commission for Scheduled Tribes. The amendment also amended Article 366 to introduce the term "socially and educationally backward classes" as a defined constitutional expression. The insertion of Article 342A introduced ambiguity about whether states retained independent power to identify OBCs for their own reservation purposes — a question that would take three years and the 105th Amendment to resolve.

## The political and constitutional context

The amendment responded to longstanding demand from backward-class organisations to give the NCBC constitutional status similar to the commissions for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, so that it could not be dissolved or weakened by ordinary legislation. The insertion of Article 342A also reflected a central impulse to create a standardised, judicially robust identification framework for backward classes at the national level, following years of inconsistent state-level list-making and litigation. The 102nd Amendment's framing of the President's list created significant concern among states that controlled their own OBC lists, because the constitutional text was read — particularly in the State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh litigation — as potentially displacing state authority. That ambiguity made the 102nd Amendment itself a source of constitutional uncertainty even as it was intended to strengthen the backward-class constitutional framework.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The 105th Amendment (2021) clarified that states retain the power to identify SEBCs for state purposes — the President's list under Article 342A applies only to central purposes and does not override state lists. The 102nd Amendment's NCBC provision gave backward-class communities a constitutional body to approach for non-implementation complaints, a structural upgrade from the earlier statutory commission. The pair of the 102nd and 105th Amendments is now a significant case study in how constitutional amendments can produce unintended interpretive consequences that require corrective amendments to repair. The NCBC's constitutional standing has also changed how governments and courts approach backward-class policy review at the national level.
