## Why it matters

The Twenty-Fourth Amendment is the key textual response to early judicial skepticism about amendment power. It is one of the foundation stones for later basic-structure litigation and the constitutional politics of Article 368.

## What specifically changed

The amendment made two targeted textual interventions in the Constitution. First, it inserted Article 13(4) to provide that nothing in Article 13 — which voids laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights — shall apply to any amendment of the Constitution made under Article 368. This directly overruled the Supreme Court's majority holding in I.C. Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967) that constitutional amendments are "law" within Article 13(2) and therefore cannot abridge Fundamental Rights. Second, it rewrote Article 368 to state explicitly that Parliament has the power to amend "any provision" of the Constitution — including the provisions in Part III — by the procedure laid down in that article, and that every such amendment shall be presented to the President for assent, who shall not withhold assent.

## The political and constitutional context

The Golaknath judgment of 1967, decided by an eleven-judge Bench with a six-to-five majority, had ruled that Parliament could not use its amending power under Article 368 to abridge or take away any Fundamental Right because such an amendment would be "law" within Article 13(2) and void to the extent of inconsistency. This created an immediate constitutional crisis for the Congress government because the property rights provisions in Part III were shielding zamindari interests and large landholdings from land reform legislation. The 24th Amendment was introduced alongside the 25th and 29th Amendments as part of a coordinated legislative response designed to restore Parliament's ability to enact social and economic reform. All three amendments were immediately challenged, and the Supreme Court's hearing of those challenges culminated in the thirteen-judge Kesavananda Bharati Bench.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Kesavananda Bharati judgment (1973) largely accepted the 24th Amendment's textual position: the Court held by majority that Parliament does have the power to amend any provision of the Constitution including Fundamental Rights, thereby upholding the amendment's core textual change. However, the Court simultaneously imposed the basic structure limitation as a constitutional ceiling — Parliament can amend rights but cannot use that power to destroy the basic structure of the Constitution. The 24th Amendment is therefore the necessary foundation on which the basic structure doctrine was constructed: without the amendment establishing that Parliament can amend rights, there would have been nothing to limit. The requirement of presidential assent to amendment bills, also introduced by this amendment, has never generated significant controversy in practice because no President has withheld assent to a constitutional amendment.
