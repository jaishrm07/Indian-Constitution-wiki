## Why it matters

The Fifty-Second Amendment is the starting point for modern anti-defection analysis. It affects how parties, legislatures, and government stability are understood in constitutional practice.

## What specifically changed

The 52nd Amendment inserted the Tenth Schedule to the Constitution, which defines defection as either voluntarily giving up party membership or voting contrary to party direction, and prescribes disqualification as the consequence. Adjudication of defection disputes was assigned to the Speaker or Chairman of the relevant House, making presiding officers constitutional decision-makers in a new and contentious domain. The Schedule created an exception for mergers, originally set at one-third of party members, which the 91st Amendment later increased to two-thirds. A further exception protected legislators who had been elected on a different ticket from the party they subsequently joined. The amendment also amended Articles 101, 102, 190, and 191 to align the disqualification framework across Parliament and the state legislatures.

## The political and constitutional context

The amendment came after years of political instability caused by widespread floor-crossing — the era that gave rise to the phrase "aaya ram gaya ram" after a Haryana MLA, Gaya Lal, famously changed parties multiple times in a single day in 1967. The problem was not merely theatrical: governments fell or survived on the strength of purchased loyalty, and the electoral mandate of voters was routinely negated by post-election defections. The Tenth Schedule was Parliament's attempt to impose constitutional discipline on legislator loyalty and to anchor government stability to the will expressed at elections rather than to private negotiation after them. The amendment was passed during the Rajiv Gandhi government's first term, when the ruling party had an overwhelming majority and cross-party consensus on the anti-defection reform was achievable.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Kihoto Hollohan judgment (1992) upheld the Tenth Schedule as constitutionally valid but struck down the finality clause in paragraph 6(2), which had sought to immunise Speaker decisions from judicial review, holding that it violated the basic structure by excluding the courts. That ruling opened Speaker decisions to judicial review and made defection cases a recurring area of constitutional litigation. The 91st Amendment (2003) tightened the merger exception by raising the threshold to two-thirds and simultaneously capped the size of Council of Ministers. The Supreme Court in Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker (2020) and Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker (2020) held that Speakers must decide disqualification petitions within a reasonable time, and endorsed a three-month norm, limiting the strategic use of delay by Speakers sympathetic to defectors.
