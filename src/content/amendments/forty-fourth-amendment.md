## Why it matters

The amendment represents a constitutional attempt at repair. It is useful not only for historical study but also for understanding how the Constitution responds after periods of institutional stress.

## What specifically changed

The most structurally significant change was the deletion of Article 19(1)(f) — the fundamental right to acquire, hold, and dispose of property — and Article 31, which governed compulsory acquisition. Property was relocated to Article 300A as a constitutional right enforceable against the state but no longer a Fundamental Right, meaning it cannot be enforced directly through Article 32 or attract the Article 19 reasonableness standard. The amendment reversed the 42nd Amendment's expansion of Article 31C, restoring it to its original 25th Amendment scope of protecting only laws giving effect to Articles 39(b) and (c). It amended Article 359 to provide that the right to move courts for enforcement of Articles 20 and 21 cannot be suspended even during a national Emergency — directly responding to ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976), in which the Supreme Court had held that even the right to life could be suspended during an Emergency. It also tightened the conditions for proclaiming a national Emergency, requiring Cabinet approval in writing and enabling Parliament to revoke an Emergency by a simple majority.

## The political and constitutional context

The Janata Party government, which had defeated the Congress at the 1977 general elections fought immediately after the Emergency was lifted, enacted the 44th Amendment as part of a deliberate programme of constitutional correction. The removal of property from the Fundamental Rights chapter had long been debated even before the Emergency — Constituent Assembly members had objected to property's presence among enforceable rights, and the government saw the change as freeing social and economic legislation from judicial invalidation on rights grounds. The non-derogability of Articles 20 and 21 was a direct parliamentary verdict on the ADM Jabalpur judgment, which had shocked legal opinion by holding that detainees during the Emergency had no right to approach courts to challenge arbitrary detention. The tighter Emergency proclamation conditions were designed to prevent a repetition of the political circumstances of June 1975.

## Long-term constitutional effect

Article 300A now provides a weaker but still judicially active layer of property protection: the Supreme Court has held that deprivation of property under Article 300A requires lawful authority and cannot be arbitrary, even though the full Article 19 and Article 32 apparatus no longer applies. The non-derogability of Articles 20 and 21 has become constitutionally foundational — it means that the right to life and personal liberty and protection against retrospective criminal laws survive even a declared Emergency, and this has influenced how courts read the relationship between emergency power and constitutional rights more broadly. The ADM Jabalpur judgment was formally overruled by the Supreme Court in K.S. Puttaswamy (Privacy) (2017), making the 44th Amendment's textual response to that decision a precursor to its full judicial correction. The amendment stands as the clearest example in Indian constitutional history of the Constitution being revised in explicit response to judicial overreach during a period of institutional failure.
