## Why it matters

The One Hundred and Fourth Amendment is not a broad doctrinal amendment, but it matters for how the Constitution manages temporary representation arrangements. It shows how transitional representation can be renewed, then allowed to expire, without rewriting the larger equality framework.

## Reading note

Read this page alongside Article 334 and the reservations topic. It is a reminder that constitutional representation sometimes works through sunset clauses, not permanent rules.

## What specifically changed

The amendment substituted the words "seventy years" for "sixty years" in Article 334(a), extending the period during which seats shall be reserved for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in the House of the People and in the Legislative Assemblies of the States. Since the Constitution was adopted in 1950, the ten-year successive extensions mean that reserved seats have now been renewed multiple times; this amendment extended the provision to run until 2030. At the same time, the amendment omitted the clause in Article 334 that had provided for the representation of the Anglo-Indian community in the House of the People under Article 331 and in Legislative Assemblies under Article 333, allowing those nomination provisions to lapse. Article 334 now contains only the SC/ST reservation sunset clause without any reference to Anglo-Indian nominated representation, which means that nominations under Articles 331 and 333 are no longer constitutionally available.

## The political and constitutional context

Article 334 had originally been understood to provide for a ten-year transitional period, on the assumption that reservation would become unnecessary as constitutional goals of substantive equality were achieved. In practice, each successive government has extended the provision, reflecting both political realities and the continuing underrepresentation of SC and ST communities in elected bodies measured against their population shares. The decision to allow the Anglo-Indian nomination provisions to lapse was a separate policy choice: the Anglo-Indian community had diminished in size and political visibility, and the nominated-representation mechanism had come to be seen as an anachronism. The Lok Sabha in 2019 passed the amendment with very large majorities on both counts, reflecting a near-consensus on continuing SC/ST reservation and ending Anglo-Indian nomination.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The extension of Article 334 without any revisit to the underlying constitutional logic has prompted continuing scholarly debate about whether a provision originally described as transitional has become constitutionally permanent. The deletion of the Anglo-Indian nomination clause is constitutionally final in the sense that the mechanism is gone from the text, and no Anglo-Indian members have been nominated since the amendment's commencement. The amendment has not generated significant litigation, reflecting the settled political consensus on SC/ST legislative reservation even as debates about the adequacy of reservation, the inclusion of other communities, and the relationship between legislative representation and executive power continue. It also raises the broader question of whether Article 334 will continue to be renewed indefinitely or whether a future Parliament will frame the question of legislative representation for SC/ST communities in terms of a permanent rather than transitional constitutional provision.
