## Why it matters

The Twenty-Fifth Amendment shows the Constitution moving from a pure rights-centered frame to a more explicit balancing of rights, social reform, and directive principles. It remains central to the history of Article 31C.

## What specifically changed

The amendment made two distinct changes to Part III. It amended Article 31(2) to substitute the word "amount" for the existing "compensation" language, removing the judicially developed requirement that the amount paid upon compulsory acquisition of property must be a "just equivalent" or otherwise satisfy a standard of adequacy reviewable in court — Parliament was now to determine the amount, and courts could not examine whether it was adequate. It inserted Article 31C to provide that no law giving effect to the policy contained in Articles 39(b) and (c) — the Directive Principles requiring that the material resources of the community be distributed to serve the common good and that the operation of the economic system does not result in concentration of wealth — shall be deemed void on the ground that it is inconsistent with or takes away rights under Articles 14, 19, or 31. Article 31C further provided, in its original enacted form, that no law containing a declaration that it was for giving effect to those two Directive Principles could be questioned in any court — a clause that was itself later struck down.

## The political and constitutional context

The immediate trigger was the Supreme Court's judgment in R.C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970), which struck down the Bank Nationalisation Act 1969 partly on the ground that the compensation formula was illusory and violated the right to property. The government had also sought, through the 25th Amendment, to address the broader problem that courts were using Articles 14, 19, and 31 to invalidate social and economic legislation aimed at redistribution. The amendments were part of the same cluster as the 24th and 29th Amendments, all passed in 1971 when the Congress had won a renewed parliamentary majority on a platform centred on Garibi Hatao — the poverty removal programme — and the government needed constitutional space to legislate without being blocked by property rights litigation.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Supreme Court in Kesavananda Bharati (1973) struck down the second part of Article 31C as enacted — the provision purporting to make parliamentary declarations of directive-principle purpose unchallengeable in court — but upheld Article 31C in its remaining form, meaning laws genuinely giving effect to Articles 39(b) and (c) remain shielded from challenge under Articles 14 and 19. The 42nd Amendment attempted to expand Article 31C's protection to all Directive Principles, but Minerva Mills (1980) restored it to its 25th Amendment scope: only Articles 39(b) and (c). Article 31C in this narrowed form remains operative, and courts have regularly had to determine whether a challenged law genuinely gives effect to those two Directive Principles or merely invokes them as a device to avoid rights scrutiny. The amendment's compensation language change became largely moot after Article 31 was deleted by the 44th Amendment and property moved to Article 300A.
