## Why it matters

The Eighty-Sixth Amendment is the pivot from aspiration to enforceability in education rights. It makes childhood education a constitutional guarantee rather than only a policy commitment.

## What specifically changed

The 86th Amendment inserted Article 21A as a Fundamental Right, making free and compulsory education for children between the ages of six and fourteen a justiciable constitutional entitlement enforceable against the state. It simultaneously amended Article 45 (Directive Principle) to shift its focus from primary education generally — which had been its concern since 1950 — to early childhood care and education for children below six years, preserving directive-principle status for that younger age group. Article 51A was amended to add a new Fundamental Duty requiring every parent or guardian to provide opportunities for education to their child or ward between the ages of six and fourteen. The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act (2009), enacted under the authority of Article 21A, created the operational framework including neighbourhood school requirements, no-detention policy, teacher qualification norms, and 25-percent admission reservation for economically weaker sections in unaided private schools.

## The political and constitutional context

The amendment came after decades of unfulfilled constitutional aspiration under the original Article 45, which had set a ten-year target from 1950 for providing free and compulsory education that was never met. The Supreme Court's landmark judgment in Unni Krishnan v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993) had recognised a partial right to education implicit in Article 21 read with Article 45, but that judicial improvisation was limited and uncertain in scope. Parliamentary consensus eventually formed around a constitutional amendment rather than reliance on judicial construction, and the amendment was passed in 2002. The Right to Education Act followed seven years later, indicating the gap between constitutional text and implementation infrastructure even after the amendment was enacted.

## Long-term constitutional effect

Article 21A, combined with the RTE Act, made education policy constitutionally justiciable, allowing courts to scrutinise both state inaction and private school compliance. The 25-percent reservation requirement for economically weaker sections in unaided private schools generated sustained litigation, with the Supreme Court in Society for Unaided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012) upholding its applicability to non-minority unaided schools while exempting minority educational institutions. The amendment also set the template for other aspirational rights being elevated from Directive Principles to Part III, influencing debates about constitutional recognition of the right to housing, health, and nutrition. The exclusion of children below six from Article 21A remains a significant gap, as early childhood is educationally critical and the directive-principle framework for that age group carries no enforcement mechanism.
