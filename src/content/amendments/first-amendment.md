## Why it matters

The First Amendment shows how quickly constitutional politics moved from adoption to contestation. It also reminds readers that rights interpretation and amendment politics developed together from the beginning, with Parliament willing to use the formal amending power within months of commencement to reverse adverse judicial decisions.

## What specifically changed

The amendment inserted Article 15(4), enabling the state to make special provisions for the advancement of socially and educationally backward classes of citizens and for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes — a provision specifically designed to override the Supreme Court's ruling in State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951) that communal reservations in educational admissions violated Article 15(1)'s non-discrimination guarantee. It amended Article 19(2) to add "public order" and "friendly relations with foreign states" as permissible grounds on which Parliament could impose restrictions on free speech and expression, responding to early cases in which courts had struck down press restrictions. Article 31A was amended to extend protection to more categories of zamindari abolition legislation, and the Ninth Schedule was inserted through Article 31B, providing that laws listed in the Schedule would not be deemed void on grounds of inconsistency with Fundamental Rights and that courts could not examine their validity on those grounds.

## The political and constitutional context

Prime Minister Nehru's government treated the amendment as an emergency legislative measure, passing it within months of the Constitution coming into force in January 1950. Three near-simultaneous judicial interventions had unsettled the government's programme: Champakam Dorairajan had struck down the Madras communal Government Order reserving seats in state medical and engineering colleges, early press cases were threatening restrictions on newspapers publishing content the government considered inflammatory, and the Bihar and Uttar Pradesh zamindari abolition laws were facing constitutional challenge on compensation grounds. Nehru personally regarded the Supreme Court's early rights jurisprudence as an obstacle to social reform and was prepared to use the amending power immediately to reassert parliamentary authority over the reform agenda.

## Long-term constitutional effect

Article 15(4) became the textual foundation for the entire modern reservation framework in India, including the subsequent controversy over OBC reservations resolved in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) and later challenges to reservations in promotion and in private educational institutions. The Ninth Schedule, inserted with a short list of agrarian reform laws, expanded dramatically over subsequent decades to include over 280 enactments, and Parliament regularly added laws it wanted to insulate from judicial review — until the Supreme Court held in I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007) that laws added to the Ninth Schedule after the Kesavananda Bharati judgment in 1973 would still be subject to basic structure review. The First Amendment's legitimacy has been questioned by constitutional scholars on the ground that Parliament used the amending power essentially to overrule a constitutional court decision within months of the Constitution commencing, establishing a pattern of rapid legislative correction that shaped the entire subsequent history of rights-reform conflict.

## Key cases arising from this amendment

Champakam Dorairajan v. State of Madras (1951) was the immediate trigger for Article 15(4): the Supreme Court struck down the Madras communal Government Order on the ground that Article 15(1) prohibited discrimination by religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth, and Parliament's direct response was to insert a new clause enabling special provisions for backward classes and Scheduled Castes and Tribes. Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras (1950) and Brij Bhushan v. State of Delhi (1950) were the early press-freedom cases which established that speech restrictions must fall within the grounds listed in Article 19(2); because "public order" was not originally listed, state laws restricting inflammatory publications were struck down, and the amendment added that ground. I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007) is the culminating Ninth Schedule judgment, holding that Article 31B's immunity does not extend to laws added to the Schedule after the basic structure doctrine crystallised.

## Reading note

The First Amendment should be read alongside Article 15(4), Article 19(2), Articles 31A and 31B, and the Ninth Schedule. It is an indispensable starting point for understanding both the reservation jurisprudence that developed over seven decades and the recurring constitutional argument over whether Parliament can use the amending power to overturn an adverse Supreme Court ruling.
