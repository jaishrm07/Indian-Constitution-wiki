## Why it matters

The amendment brought local democracy into the constitutional frame instead of leaving it to fluctuating statutory preference.

## What specifically changed

The 73rd Amendment inserted Part IX (Articles 243 to 243O) and the Eleventh Schedule, which lists 29 subjects including agriculture, land improvement, minor irrigation, social forestry, rural housing, and poverty alleviation programmes. It gave constitutional status to Panchayati Raj institutions at the village, intermediate, and district levels, and mandated five-year election cycles enforced by independent State Election Commissions rather than state governments. Seats are required to be reserved for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and Other Backward Classes in proportion to their population, and not less than one-third of all seats must be reserved for women. States are required to establish Finance Commissions to review the financial position of panchayats and to recommend principles for devolution of taxes, duties, and grants from the consolidated fund of the state.

## The political and constitutional context

Despite the Directive Principle in Article 40, which called for organising village panchayats with sufficient powers for self-governance, local self-government remained constitutionally frail and practically underfunded for the first four decades after independence. The amendment followed a failed earlier attempt in 1989 under the Rajiv Gandhi government, when a similar bill passed the Lok Sabha but lapsed in the Rajya Sabha. The recognition driving the reform was that decentralisation could not be left to ordinary state legislation, which states routinely neglected or designed to remain under state control. The Narasimha Rao government enacted both the 73rd and 74th Amendments together in 1992, reflecting a broader consensus that third-tier democracy needed constitutional shelter.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The amendment created a constitutional floor for local democracy, but enforcement has been deeply uneven because states retain control over how much of the Eleventh Schedule's subject-matter is actually devolved in terms of funds, functions, and functionaries. The election timing obligation has been enforced through Supreme Court mandamus proceedings, with the Court in Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of Ahmedabad making clear that elections cannot be indefinitely postponed. OBC reservation requirements produced their own complicated jurisprudence, culminating in the triple test laid down in K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India, which required states to conduct empirical surveys of backwardness before implementing OBC quotas in local body elections. Women's reservation at the local level has been more robustly implemented than at other levels of government and is now viewed as a significant democratic outcome of the amendment.
