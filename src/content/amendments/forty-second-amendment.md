## Why it matters

The Forty-Second Amendment is central to understanding why later courts and political actors treated amendment power with suspicion. It is one of the clearest demonstrations of how constitutional form can be used to pursue structural transformation.

## What specifically changed

The amendment altered Article 368 itself, inserting clauses (4) and (5) to declare that constitutional amendments could not be questioned in any court on any ground and that no limitation could be placed on Parliament's constituent power — a direct attempt to make amendments beyond judicial review. It amended the Preamble to insert "socialist" and "secular" between "sovereign" and "democratic republic", and "unity and integrity" in place of "unity" in the closing phrase. Article 31C was expanded beyond its 25th Amendment scope so that any law giving effect to any of the Directive Principles — not merely Articles 39(b) and (c) — would be immune from challenge under Articles 14, 19, and 31. Article 31D was inserted to protect laws against anti-national activities from Fundamental Rights challenge. High Court jurisdiction under Articles 226 and 227 was curtailed, and Article 257A was inserted enabling the Union to deploy armed forces in a state without that state's consent.

## The political and constitutional context

The amendment was passed during the Internal Emergency proclaimed in June 1975, when the Indira Gandhi government commanded a near-total parliamentary majority and political opposition had been substantially suppressed. It was a direct parliamentary response to a sequence of adverse judicial decisions: Kesavananda Bharati (1973) had imposed the basic structure limitation on amendment power, and Indira Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975) had struck down the 39th Amendment's retrospective validation of the Prime Minister's election. The government's stated rationale was that parliamentary sovereignty had been unduly curtailed by judicial intervention and that social reform legislation needed constitutional protection from rights-based challenge. The Emergency context meant that normal legislative deliberation and political opposition were absent, making the amendment the product of a single concentrated institutional actor.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Minerva Mills judgment (1980) struck down Articles 368(4) and (5) as themselves violating the basic structure by destroying the essential balance between rights and directive principles, and simultaneously cut down the expanded Article 31C back to its 25th Amendment scope protecting only Articles 39(b) and (c). The Janata government, which had defeated the Congress in 1977, enacted the 44th Amendment to reverse several other Emergency-era changes before Minerva Mills was decided. The Preamble insertions of "socialist" and "secular", however, were not challenged successfully and are now treated as part of the Constitution's settled textual identity — courts have repeatedly declined to excise them. The amendment became the defining reference point for the proposition that formal amendment power, deployed without political constraint, poses the most serious threat to constitutional structure, and it is this background that gives the basic structure doctrine its continuing institutional justification.
