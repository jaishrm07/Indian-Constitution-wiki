## Why it matters

The Sixty-Ninth Amendment is the constitutional starting point for modern Delhi governance disputes. It is central to understanding how the national capital fits into federal design.

## What specifically changed

The amendment inserted Article 239AA into the Constitution, transforming Delhi from an ordinary Union territory administered solely through the Lieutenant Governor under Article 239 into the National Capital Territory with a directly elected Legislative Assembly and a Council of Ministers responsible to that assembly. Article 239AA(3) gave the Delhi Legislative Assembly the power to make laws on all subjects in the State List and Concurrent List except for three matters — public order, police, and land — which remained with Parliament and the Union executive. Article 239AB was simultaneously inserted to provide for situations where constitutional governance in the NCT breaks down, allowing the President to issue a proclamation assuming direct administration. The Representation of the People Act was also amended so that Delhi would send members to the Rajya Sabha, aligning its representational status more closely with that of a state while stopping short of full statehood.

## The political and constitutional context

Delhi had previously been administered under the Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi Act, 1991, but that statute required a clear constitutional basis. The amendment was the culmination of a political process that had begun with the Balakrishnan Committee report of 1989, which recommended giving Delhi a legislature while retaining the Centre's special interest in the capital. The core political tension was between residents of Delhi who demanded democratic self-governance comparable to other states and the Centre's insistence that the national capital could not be handed over entirely to a state government given its unique significance. The 1991 amendment represented a compromise: real legislative and executive power would be given to an elected government, but in a constitutionally asymmetric way that preserved certain Union functions.

## Long-term constitutional effect

Article 239AA became the subject of sustained constitutional litigation, with the Supreme Court delivering a Constitution Bench judgment in Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India in 2018 that held the Lieutenant Governor was bound by the aid and advice of the elected Council of Ministers on all matters other than the three reserved subjects, establishing that the democratic principle required the LG to act on ministerial advice in ordinary administration. The Court reinforced and expanded this holding in subsequent Constitution Bench decisions in 2023, clarifying that services and administrative control over civil servants fell within the legislative and executive competence of the elected Delhi government rather than the Union — a ruling that was then reversed by Parliament through the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023, which restored Union control over services. The Delhi governance dispute thus became a live constitutional laboratory for questions about asymmetric federalism, the democratic accountability of elected governments, and the limits of Parliament's power to use its legislative majority to restructure the constitutional arrangement of a particular territorial unit.
