## Why it matters

The Ninety-Ninth Amendment is the modern judiciary amendment that every constitutional reader has to understand. It became central to the larger question of whether Parliament can redesign judicial appointments without undermining judicial independence.

## Reading note

Read it with the judicial appointments articles and the basic-structure topic. The amendment is not just about staffing the courts; it is about who gets to shape the constitutional conditions of adjudication.

## What specifically changed

The 99th Amendment inserted Articles 124A, 124B, and 124C to establish a National Judicial Appointments Commission consisting of the Chief Justice of India, the two most senior Supreme Court Judges, the Union Law Minister, and two eminent persons selected by a committee comprising the Prime Minister, the Chief Justice, and the Leader of the Opposition. It replaced the collegium system — the judge-dominated appointments process built through the Three Judges Cases — as the constitutional mechanism for appointing and transferring Supreme Court and High Court judges. Any two members of the NJAC could veto an appointment, giving both the Law Minister and the eminent persons a structural role in blocking judicial nominees. The companion NJAC Act 2014 specified the selection procedure for eminent persons and the manner in which the Commission would carry out its mandate.

## The political and constitutional context

The 99th Amendment was passed with nearly unanimous parliamentary support in both Houses and was ratified by the required majority of state legislatures, reflecting deep and cross-party dissatisfaction with the collegium system's opacity, its tendency toward self-referential appointments, and its insulation from democratic accountability. The government and many commentators argued that a purely self-selecting judicial appointments system — where serving judges chose their own successors — was incompatible with the constitutional logic of democratic governance and separation of powers. Critics of the collegium had pointed to favouritism in appointments, lack of diversity, and the absence of any written criteria or transparent process. The amendment came soon after the formation of the Modi government in 2014, though the reform debate had preceded it by many years across different administrations.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Supreme Court struck down both the 99th Amendment and the NJAC Act in Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2016) 5 SCC 1, by a majority of four to one, holding that judicial independence is part of the basic structure of the Constitution and that the NJAC's design — which gave non-judicial members an effective veto — threatened it. The collegium system was restored to its pre-amendment form, but the Court acknowledged its flaws and initiated a process of reform memoranda and transparency measures that has been slow and contested. The sole dissent by Justice Chelameswar argued that the majority was wrong to invalidate a constitutional amendment supported by the full democratic process. The tension between judicial self-governance and democratic input into appointments remains unresolved, and the reform debate over collegium transparency, criteria, and diversity continues.
