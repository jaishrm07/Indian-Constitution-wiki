## Why it matters

The Thirty-Ninth Amendment is a core Emergency-era amendment because it shows how constitutional form can be used to shield political outcomes from ordinary judicial correction. That is why it remains tied to the story of constitutional limits on amendment power.

## Reading note

Read it alongside Article 329A and the basic-structure era. Its significance is not just historical; it marks the point where election law, constitutional review, and institutional self-protection collided directly.

## What specifically changed

The Thirty-Ninth Amendment inserted Article 329A, which provided that disputes relating to the election of the President, Vice-President, Prime Minister, and Speaker of the Lok Sabha could not be heard or decided by any court and could only be resolved by such authority as Parliament might by law prescribe. The amendment also specifically sought to validate, retrospectively, the election of Indira Gandhi as a Member of Parliament and as Prime Minister, purporting to remove the legal effect of the Allahabad High Court judgment that had set aside her election. Article 71, which previously gave the Supreme Court exclusive jurisdiction over presidential and vice-presidential election disputes, was simultaneously amended to bring it into consistency with the new framework. The net effect was to carve out the most powerful constitutional offices from the ordinary scope of judicial supervision over elections.

## The political and constitutional context

The immediate trigger was the Allahabad High Court's judgment in Raj Narain v. Indira Nehru Gandhi, delivered in June 1975, which found that Indira Gandhi had committed electoral corrupt practices and set aside her election from Rae Bareli. Rather than wait for the Supreme Court's decision on the stay she had obtained, the Government used the Emergency, proclaimed on 25 June 1975, to amend the Constitution and retrospectively nullify the judgment's effect. The amendment was part of a larger Emergency-era legislative package that also included the Thirty-Eighth and Forty-Second Amendments, all aimed at insulating the executive from judicial accountability. The political crisis was thus the direct cause: Parliament was being used to reverse a judicial finding that the Prime Minister had won her seat through unlawful means.

## Long-term constitutional effect

In Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain, the Supreme Court struck down the most important part of Article 329A — the clause that retrospectively validated Mrs Gandhi's election and purported to decide a pending judicial proceeding by legislative fiat — as a violation of the basic structure of the Constitution, specifically the separation of powers and the principle that a legislature cannot act as a court and decide a dispute between specific parties. This was the first major application of the basic structure doctrine to invalidate an actual constitutional amendment. The judgment also made clear that democracy and free and fair elections are themselves part of the basic structure, meaning that constitutional provisions which hollow out electoral accountability are vulnerable to review. The Forty-Fourth Amendment later repealed Article 329A entirely, removing it from the constitutional text, but the doctrinal contribution of this episode — that the Constitution protects free and fair elections as a basic feature — has continued to shape Indian constitutional law.
