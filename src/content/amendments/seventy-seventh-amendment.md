## Why it matters

The Seventy-Seventh Amendment shows that equality in public employment is not only about entry into office. It also shapes advancement, hierarchy, and how the state measures substantive representation over time.

## Reading note

Read this amendment with Article 16 and the broader reservations topic. It is one of the clearest examples of the Constitution explicitly responding to a doctrinal gap in service jurisprudence.

## What specifically changed

The Seventy-Seventh Amendment inserted Article 16(4A) into the Constitution, which reads that nothing in Article 16 shall prevent the State from making any provision for reservation in matters of promotion, with consequential seniority, to any class or classes of posts in the services under the State in favour of the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes, where the State is of the opinion that they are not adequately represented in the services under the State. The phrase "with consequential seniority" was significant because it validated the practice of carry-forward reservation, under which an SC or ST employee promoted under the reservation quota would also carry forward the seniority position that went with a promotion given at that stage rather than being placed below general-category employees who had been promoted earlier. This language directly responded to the ambiguity that the Indra Sawhney judgment had created around promotional reservation by making the constitutional text explicitly permissive on the point.

## The political and constitutional context

In Indra Sawhney v. Union of India, the Supreme Court's nine-judge bench in 1992 upheld the Mandal Commission recommendations on OBC reservation in initial appointments but held that reservation could not extend to promotions. The judgment had the immediate practical effect of threatening the promotional reservations that SC and ST employees had relied on for decades. There was intense political pressure from SC and ST communities and their political representatives to restore a constitutional basis for promotional reservation, and the Narasimha Rao government introduced the amendment in 1994 as a direct response. The amendment was not contested and was passed the following year, making it one of the clearer examples of constitutional text being revised to address a specific judicial holding rather than to initiate a new policy.

## Long-term constitutional effect

Article 16(4A) was immediately challenged, and in M. Nagaraj v. Union of India the Supreme Court upheld its constitutional validity while imposing conditions: a State wishing to use the power must demonstrate, on the basis of quantifiable data, that the SC/ST community is not adequately represented in the cadre, that the overall efficiency of administration is maintained, and that the reservation does not lead to a breach of the fifty percent ceiling. This set of conditions became the framework within which all promotional reservation schemes had to be justified, creating a continuing litigation burden on state governments. The Court revisited these conditions in Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta and held that states do not need to collect separate data on backwardness of SC/ST employees for the purpose of promotions — since their backwardness is presumed from their constitutional classification — while keeping the other Nagaraj requirements intact. The amendment and the doctrine it has generated now form the central reference point for all litigation about promotional reservation in public employment.
