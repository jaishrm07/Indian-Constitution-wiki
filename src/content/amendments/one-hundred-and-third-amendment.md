## Why it matters

The One Hundred and Third Amendment is a major equality amendment because it does not simply expand an older reservation category. It creates a separate constitutional route based on economic disadvantage and changes how readers think about substantive equality.

## Reading note

Read this amendment with Articles 15 and 16 together. It is the clearest example of the Constitution building a new reservation logic directly into the equality chapter rather than borrowing from older categories.

## What specifically changed

The 103rd Amendment inserted Articles 15(6) and 16(6) enabling the state to make special provisions, including reservations, for the economically weaker sections of citizens other than those already covered by existing SC, ST, and OBC provisions. The 10 percent EWS reservation applies in educational institutions (including private unaided institutions, unlike OBC reservations) and in public employment. It is the first reservation provision based solely on economic criteria, without any requirement of social or educational backwardness. The amendment explicitly excludes SC, ST, and OBC categories from the EWS category, so the new 10 percent sits on top of the existing reservation ceiling rather than being carved out of it. This structural choice — adding 10 percent rather than redistributing existing headroom — was central to both the political design and the constitutional challenge.

## The political and constitutional context

The amendment was presented as addressing the needs of economically poor citizens from general categories who did not qualify for OBC, SC, or ST reservations and therefore had no access to affirmative action. Critics argued that economic poverty alone, without social or educational backwardness, was not the constitutional basis for reservation as developed in the Indra Sawhney judgment, and that the 50 percent ceiling established in that case was being breached by adding the new category on top. The amendment passed within days of introduction in January 2019, months before a general election, making its political timing a subject of comment. The Supreme Court's nine-judge bench framework from Indra Sawhney — which had held that economic criteria alone cannot be the basis for reservation and that the 50 percent ceiling is a constitutional requirement — was directly challenged by the amendment's design.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Supreme Court upheld the 103rd Amendment (3-2) in Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (2022). The majority held that economic criteria alone are a permissible basis for special provisions, and the 50 percent ceiling from Indra Sawhney is not an inflexible constitutional rule. The two-judge dissent argued the amendment violated the basic structure by excluding the most disadvantaged communities — SC, ST, and OBC — from the new EWS category, inverting the original constitutional rationale for reservations. The case has not finally resolved the ceiling question, and the sub-classification debates from Davinder Singh continue alongside EWS litigation. The 103rd Amendment has also reopened questions about whether reservations in private unaided educational institutions are constitutionally sustainable.
