## Why it matters

The One Hundred and Sixth Amendment is the constitutional framework for women’s reservation in legislatures. It is a major equality-and-representation amendment and will remain important once delimitation and commencement questions activate it.

## What specifically changed

The 106th Amendment inserted Articles 330A (reservation of seats for women in the Lok Sabha), 332A (reservation of seats for women in State Legislative Assemblies), and 334A (a sunset provision under which the reservation lapses after fifteen years from commencement). At least one-third of the total seats in each House, including seats already reserved for SC and ST, must be reserved for women. The reservation is to be allocated by rotation among constituencies at each general election, preventing permanent entrenchment in specific seats. Article 239AA was also amended to extend the same framework to the Delhi Legislative Assembly. The insertion of the reservation within the existing SC and ST reservation structures means that one-third of SC-reserved seats and one-third of ST-reserved seats must also be held by women.

## The political and constitutional context

A women’s reservation bill had been introduced in Parliament multiple times since 1996 without passing, blocked partly by demands for sub-quotas within the quota for OBC women and partly by parliamentary arithmetic. The 106th Amendment passed in September 2023 — but with a crucial deferred commencement condition built into Article 334A: the reservation only takes effect after the delimitation exercise following the first census after the commencement of the Amendment. The 2021 census was significantly delayed, meaning the operational start date is constitutionally guaranteed but practically uncertain. The structure — passing the amendment but deferring commencement — allowed political credit to be claimed while avoiding the immediate disruption of reconfiguring existing constituency boundaries and seat allocations.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The reservation exists as constitutional text but cannot be exercised until delimitation happens, and delimitation cannot happen until the census is complete, making this a constitutionally entrenched but temporally suspended right. The intersection with the One Nation One Election exercise — which also depends on delimitation — adds further complexity to the commencement timeline and raises questions about whether both exercises can or should proceed simultaneously. The fifteen-year sunset clause in Article 334A means the reservation will lapse unless renewed, creating a recurring political obligation to legislate its extension. The amendment also embeds the OBC sub-quota question in any future political negotiation over the reservation’s operation, since the 106th Amendment does not create an OBC sub-category within the women’s quota.
