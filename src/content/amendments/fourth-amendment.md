## Why it matters

The Fourth Amendment is part of the series of early amendments through which Parliament incrementally reduced the constitutional space for property rights litigation to interfere with land reform. It responded directly to the judicial interpretation of the First Amendment's changes to Articles 31 and 31A, closing gaps that courts had identified and expanding the categories of state action that were beyond challenge.

## What specifically changed

The amendment made three substantive changes. First, it amended Article 31(2) to clarify that where a law specifies the amount of compensation or the principles for determining it, neither the amount nor the adequacy of the amount shall be called in question in any court — Parliament was asserting that the quantum of compensation is a matter of legislative judgment, not judicial review. Second, it widened Article 31A by adding three new categories of law that cannot be challenged under Articles 14 or 19: laws relating to the taking over of the management of any property by the state for a limited period in the public interest; laws relating to the amalgamation of two or more corporations in the public interest; and laws extinguishing or modifying the rights of directors, managing agents, shareholders, or holders of debentures in commercial and industrial undertakings. Third, several additional laws were added to the Ninth Schedule under Article 31B.

## The political and constitutional context

The First Amendment had inserted Articles 31A and 31B and the Ninth Schedule to protect agrarian reform legislation, but by 1955 litigation had identified multiple gaps. State governments attempting to acquire urban property, nationalise management, or modify commercial arrangements were finding that their legislation was still subject to Article 14 and Article 31 challenge because the First Amendment's protection was limited to agrarian and land reform narrowly defined. The Fourth Amendment was Parliament's direct response to those gaps, reflecting the broad consensus across the political spectrum that social and economic reform legislation should not be held hostage to compensation litigation in the courts. The Nehru government's socialist economic programme — expanding state management of industry and mining — required this constitutional clearance.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Fourth Amendment's expansion of Article 31A to cover nationalisation and management takeovers became constitutionally important when later governments used those provisions to acquire banks, insurance companies, and industrial undertakings. The progressive restriction on judicial review of compensation amounts, cumulatively achieved through the First, Fourth, and later Twenty-Fifth Amendments, was eventually made largely moot when the Forty-Fourth Amendment (1978) deleted Article 31 altogether and moved property to a non-fundamental right position in Article 300A. The Fourth Amendment is therefore one link in the chain leading from the original constitutional protection of property to its eventual demotion from the Fundamental Rights chapter, a process driven by Parliament's determination to maintain legislative supremacy over the terms of economic redistribution.

## Reading note

The Fourth Amendment is best read alongside the First Amendment (1951), the Twenty-Fifth Amendment (1971), and the Forty-Fourth Amendment (1978) as successive steps in the same project of insulating land reform and economic redistribution legislation from property rights challenges. Article 31A in its current form reflects the cumulative effect of all these amendments.
