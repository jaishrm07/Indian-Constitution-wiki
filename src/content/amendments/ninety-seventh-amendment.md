## Why it matters

The Ninety-Seventh Amendment shows how the Constitution can constitutionalise institutions that were previously treated as ordinary statutory creatures. It is a useful bridge between rights, devolution, and institutional design.

## What specifically changed

The amendment made three distinct constitutional changes. First, it added the words "or cooperative societies" to Article 19(1)(c), which had previously protected only the right to form associations or unions, thereby elevating the right to form a cooperative society to the level of a fundamental right. Second, it inserted Article 43B into Part IV as a Directive Principle, directing the State to promote voluntary formation, autonomous functioning, democratic control, and professional management of cooperative societies. Third, and most extensively, it inserted Part IXB, comprising Articles 243ZH to 243ZT, which created a detailed constitutional framework specifying minimum requirements for the membership of boards of cooperative societies, the frequency of elections, the maximum term of elected boards, the grounds for supersession, and the maintenance of reserve funds. The combined effect was to bring cooperatives within the constitutional governance framework in a manner modelled loosely on the Panchayati Raj and municipal governance provisions inserted by the Seventy-Third and Seventy-Fourth Amendments.

## The political and constitutional context

Cooperative societies in several states, particularly in the sugar and dairy sectors, had long been used as instruments of political patronage, with boards superseded and administrators appointed for extended periods whenever the ruling party chose to favour or disfavour a cooperative's leadership. The absence of constitutional rules meant that ordinary state legislation could defer elections indefinitely and concentrate control of financially significant institutions in government-appointed hands. The UPA government's amendment was partly a response to demands for reform from cooperative movements themselves and partly a governance reform initiative aimed at depoliticising cooperative management. The model largely drew on the approach of the Seventy-Third and Seventy-Fourth Amendments, which had used constitutional minima to discipline state legislative discretion over local self-government.

## Long-term constitutional effect

The Ninety-Seventh Amendment was challenged in Gujarat Urja Vikas Nigam Ltd. v. Essar Power Ltd., though the more directly relevant challenge came when the Supreme Court considered whether Part IXB applied to multi-state cooperatives, which fall under Union legislative competence. In a significant ruling, the Court held that a portion of Part IXB, insofar as it purported to apply to cooperatives under the Union List, was invalid because the amendment had been passed without ratification by at least half the state legislatures under the proviso to Article 368(2), which requires such ratification when an amendment affects the legislative powers of states. The Court severed the invalid portions from those that validly applied to cooperatives within state legislative competence. The decision is constitutionally important as a rare instance of a constitutional amendment being partially struck down on procedural ratification grounds rather than basic structure grounds, clarifying the scope of the ratification requirement in Article 368(2).
