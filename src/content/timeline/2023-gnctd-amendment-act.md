## What happened

The constitutional status of services — meaning the control over civil servants posted in Delhi — had been in dispute between the elected Government of NCT of Delhi and the Lieutenant Governor (LG) for years. A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in Government of NCT of Delhi v Union of India, delivered a unanimous judgment on 11 May 2023 holding that the elected government of Delhi had legislative and executive power over "services" (Entry 41 of the State List) except in relation to the three subjects — public order, police, and land — expressly excluded from Delhi's legislative competence by Article 239AA. The Court held that the principle of constitutional governance required that the Council of Ministers responsible to the Legislative Assembly must be able to control the officers implementing its policies, and that the Lieutenant Governor was constitutionally obliged to act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers in matters within the legislative competence of the Assembly.

Within weeks of this judgment, Parliament passed the Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi (Amendment) Act 2023, which inserted provisions creating a National Capital Civil Services Authority (NCCSA) — a body comprising the Chief Minister and two senior IAS officers — to advise the LG on matters relating to services, with the LG having overriding power to act in his individual discretion. The Act came into force on 19 August 2023. The Government of Delhi challenged the Act before the Supreme Court, where the matter remained pending.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The GNCTD Amendment Act raised the question of whether Parliament can, through ordinary legislation (as opposed to constitutional amendment), nullify the effect of a constitutional interpretation by the Supreme Court. The Constitution Bench had interpreted Article 239AA as granting substantive self-government to Delhi's elected administration in the field of services. Parliament's response — enacted under its plenary power to legislate for Union Territories under Article 246(4) — did not amend Article 239AA but created a statutory regime that transferred effective control back to the LG. The constitutional question was whether this legislative override was consistent with the Court's interpretation of the constitutional scheme, or whether it amounted to an attempt to circumvent constitutional adjudication through the backdoor of statutory law.

## Its constitutional legacy

The GNCTD dispute became the leading contemporary example of Centre-state (or Centre-Union Territory) tension being played out simultaneously in the courts and in Parliament. The episode demonstrated the vulnerability of judicially-declared rights under constitutional provisions that Parliament can effectively override without formal constitutional amendment, and revived academic and political debate about whether ordinary legislative power under Article 246(4) is subject to implied limitations when it conflicts with a self-governance guarantee in Article 239AA.
