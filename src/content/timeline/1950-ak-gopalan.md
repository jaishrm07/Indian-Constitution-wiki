A.K. Gopalan is the first major liberty marker in the Constitution's history. It shows how much later doctrine had to work to connect liberty, fairness, and judicial review.

## What happened

A.K. Gopalan, a prominent Communist leader, was detained under the Preventive Detention Act 1950 and challenged his detention as a violation of Articles 19, 21, and 22 of the Constitution. The Supreme Court, by a majority, upheld his detention and rejected the argument that Articles 19, 21, and 22 should be read together as an integrated code of personal liberty. The majority held that each article operated in a self-contained sphere: as long as a law prescribing the procedure for deprivation of liberty existed, Article 21 was satisfied regardless of whether that procedure was fair or reasonable. Only Justice Fazl Ali dissented, arguing for an interconnected reading of the fundamental rights provisions.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The majority's compartmentalised reading of fundamental rights meant that Parliament could validly authorise detention through any procedure it chose, however arbitrary, without triggering review under Article 19's reasonableness standards. This interpretation gave the executive wide preventive detention powers with minimal judicial oversight and treated the Constitution's liberty guarantees as mutually exclusive rather than mutually reinforcing. The case also raised the foundational question of whether "procedure established by law" in Article 21 meant any procedure prescribed by statute, or only a procedure that was fair and just — a question the Court answered narrowly in favour of the state.

## Its legacy

Gopalan cast a long shadow over Indian liberty jurisprudence for more than two decades, effectively insulating preventive detention laws from meaningful constitutional challenge. The case was decisively overruled in spirit by Maneka Gandhi, which adopted the interconnected reading that Justice Fazl Ali had advocated, and it became the negative reference point against which the post-Emergency expansion of Article 21 measured its progress.
