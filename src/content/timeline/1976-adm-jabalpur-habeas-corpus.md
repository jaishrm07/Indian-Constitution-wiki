## What happened

On 26 June 1975, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi proclaimed an Internal Emergency under Article 352 citing internal disturbance. Simultaneously, a Presidential Order under Article 359 suspended the right of individuals to move courts for enforcement of the rights under Articles 14, 21, and 22 for the duration of the Emergency. Tens of thousands of persons were detained without trial under the Maintenance of Internal Security Act (MISA) and the Defence of India Rules. Many of them filed habeas corpus petitions in various High Courts seeking release, arguing that their detention was mala fide or violated the procedural requirements of the detention laws.

Several High Courts, including Allahabad, Bombay, Delhi, Karnataka, and Madhya Pradesh, ruled in favour of the detainees, holding that courts retained some residual power to examine whether detentions were in accordance with the statutory framework, even if the underlying fundamental rights were suspended. The Union Government appealed to the Supreme Court. The five-judge Constitution Bench — comprising Chief Justice A.N. Ray, Justice P.N. Bhagwati, Justice Y.V. Chandrachud, Justice M.H. Beg, and Justice H.R. Khanna — heard the consolidated appeals and delivered judgment on 28 April 1976.

The majority of four judges held that when Article 359 suspended the right to move courts for enforcement of Article 21, no person could seek habeas corpus to challenge detention, regardless of whether the detention was authorised by law or was wholly without legal authority. The State's contention — that even if a detention order was fraudulent or clearly beyond the statutory power, the detainee had no remedy in court during the Emergency — was accepted. Justice Beg's concurrence was even more extreme, expressing satisfaction with the government's conduct and suggesting that the custodians of the state should be trusted.

Justice H.R. Khanna, alone, dissented. He held that Article 21 was not the sole repository of the right to life and personal liberty — that the right to not be deprived of life and liberty without authority of law existed independently of Article 21, derived from the rule of law itself. Even with Article 21's enforcement suspended, a detention without any legal authority at all could still be challenged. He drew on English common law, the history of habeas corpus, and the Constitution's structure to conclude that the majority had gone dangerously wrong.

## Constitutional significance

ADM Jabalpur is widely regarded as the lowest point in the Supreme Court's institutional history. The majority's reasoning required courts to hold that the executive could imprison any person indefinitely, for any reason or no reason, during an Emergency, with no judicial remedy whatsoever. The practical consequence was that the writ of habeas corpus — the oldest and most fundamental remedy for unlawful detention in common law systems — was extinguished during the period when it was most urgently needed.

The judgment accelerated a pattern of judicial deference to executive authority that characterised much of the Emergency period and that the Court itself later acknowledged with regret. Justice Bhagwati, one of the majority judges, subsequently wrote an open letter to Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi in 1988 describing his vote in ADM Jabalpur as his greatest regret and a "grievous error." Justice Chandrachud later served as Chief Justice and presided over a court that progressively expanded civil liberties, seemingly in partial reparation for the Emergency-era record.

Justice Khanna's dissent, by contrast, became an enduring benchmark of judicial courage. His refusal to capitulate despite knowing it would cost him the Chief Justiceship — Justice M.H. Beg was elevated over him as Chief Justice of India — gave his reasoning a moral authority that far outlasted the majority's holding. The New York Times ran a front-page editorial specifically praising Khanna's dissent at the time of his supersession.

## Long-term impact

The Emergency ended in 1977 and the Forty-Fourth Amendment was enacted in 1978, making Articles 20 and 21 non-suspendable even during a proclaimed Emergency under Article 359. This legislative correction directly overruled the constitutional position that ADM Jabalpur had endorsed, ensuring that the right to life and personal liberty and the protection against self-incrimination could never again be formally suspended.

The formal overruling of ADM Jabalpur came four decades later. In K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017), a nine-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that ADM Jabalpur was wrongly decided. Justice D.Y. Chandrachud — son of Justice Y.V. Chandrachud, one of the original majority judges — wrote a separate concurrence specifically addressing ADM Jabalpur, describing it as an aberration that "had to be explicitly overruled" because "it directly imperilled the most fundamental of human rights — the right to life." He explicitly vindicated Justice Khanna's dissent as correct in law and in principle.

The case also had a generative effect on the post-Emergency expansion of Article 21. The Maneka Gandhi judgment in 1978 and the long line of Article 21 decisions that followed were in part a corrective response to the bareness of rights protection that ADM Jabalpur had revealed. Courts and Parliament together worked to ensure that the constitutional framework could never again be reduced to the position the majority had endorsed in April 1976.
