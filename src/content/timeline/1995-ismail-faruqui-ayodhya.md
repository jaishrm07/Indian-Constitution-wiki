The Ismail Faruqui judgment is a foundational ruling at the intersection of religious freedom, the essential religious practices doctrine, and the state's power of eminent domain. It settled the constitutional validity of the Central Government's extraordinary acquisition of the disputed Ayodhya site while simultaneously redefining the outer boundary of the protection that mosques enjoy under Articles 25 and 26.

## What happened

After the demolition of the Babri Masjid on 6 December 1992, the Union Government promulgated the Acquisition of Certain Area at Ayodhya Ordinance, which was subsequently replaced by the Acquisition of Certain Area at Ayodhya Act, 1993. The Act acquired approximately 67 acres of land in and around the disputed site in Ayodhya, placing it under the Central Government pending a resolution of the dispute. The Act also referred the question of whether a Hindu temple had existed at the disputed site before the construction of the Babri Masjid to the Supreme Court under Article 143. Dr. M. Ismail Faruqui and others challenged the acquisition as unconstitutional, arguing that it violated Articles 25 and 26 (religious freedom), Articles 14 and 15 (equality), and was designed to benefit one religious community at the expense of another. A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court decided the matter on 24 October 1994.

The Bench, by a three-to-two majority, upheld the Act's constitutional validity in most respects. It held that the Act as a whole was a secular acquisition aimed at maintaining public order and resolving a long-standing communal dispute, and that the State has the power to acquire any property, including property attached to religious use, under Article 300A read with the general doctrine of eminent domain, provided just compensation is paid. However, the Court struck down Section 4(3) of the Act, which had abated all pending suits in the courts relating to the disputed area, on the ground that it amounted to an unconstitutional executive interference with judicial proceedings.

## Constitutional significance

The most doctrinally consequential part of the judgment is the Court's holding on Article 25. The Court applied the essential religious practices test — first articulated in the Shirur Mutt case (1954) — to Islamic worship and held that while the practice of saying prayers (namaz) is an integral part of Islam, it does not have to be performed in a mosque. A mosque, the Court said, is not an essential part of the practice of Islam in the sense that protection under Article 25 would be lost if a particular mosque is unavailable. This holding drew a sharp distinction between the right to practice religion (protected) and the right to use a specific piece of land for religious purposes (not inherently a fundamental right). The majority also reaffirmed that the right to religion under Articles 25 and 26 is subject to public order, morality, and health, and that the State may regulate or acquire religious property in the interest of maintaining order.

The judgment also clarified that Article 300A, which protects persons from deprivation of property save by authority of law, does not immunise religious property from acquisition — the requirement is that the acquisition be backed by valid law with just compensation, not that religious property is categorically exempt.

## Long-term impact

The essential religious practices holding in Ismail Faruqui became one of the foundational precedents for the doctrine's subsequent development. Courts invoked the test in a wide range of disputes — from the entry of women into temples (Sabarimala, 2018) to the performance of specific rituals. The ruling that a mosque is not essential to Islamic practice was specifically noted, though not followed without qualification, in later judgments. In the Ayodhya title suit judgment (M. Siddiq v. Mahant Suresh Das, 2019), a five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court ultimately awarded the disputed site to Ram Lalla and directed the Central Government to allot an alternative five-acre site for the mosque — an outcome that the constitutional framework of Ismail Faruqui had made possible by treating the loss of the specific land as constitutionally compensable.
