## What happened

In 2001 the People's Union for Civil Liberties filed a writ petition under Article 32 before the Supreme Court highlighting that deaths from hunger and malnutrition were occurring across drought-affected states while the Food Corporation of India held record grain stocks that were rotting in storage. The petition asked the Court to direct distribution of those stocks to the hungry. The Supreme Court treated the petition as raising a question of fundamental rights under Article 21 and began issuing a series of interim orders — ultimately exceeding two hundred — directing the central and state governments to implement specific food security programmes: the Midday Meal Scheme in schools, the Antyodaya Anna Yojana for the poorest households, the Integrated Child Development Services programme, and the Public Distribution System. The Court appointed two Commissioners — N.C. Saxena and S.R. Sankaran, later succeeded by others — to monitor compliance, receive complaints, and report to the Court. Parliament enacted the National Food Security Act in 2013, which came into force progressively from September 2014, giving statutory entitlements to subsidised grain to approximately two-thirds of the population and incorporating many of the programmes the Court had directed be implemented by interim order.

## Constitutional significance

The PUCL right to food litigation is constitutionally significant for three distinct reasons. First, it operationalised the connection between Article 47 — a non-justiciable Directive Principle directing the State to raise the level of nutrition and the standard of living of its people — and Article 21's judicially enforceable right to life. The Court held that the right to live with dignity, which forms the core of Article 21 as developed in Francis Coralie Mullin and later cases, necessarily includes the right to at least subsistence nutrition, and that the State cannot cite food policy discretion to escape constitutional accountability when people are dying of hunger while government warehouses overflow. Second, the litigation institutionalised the technique of continuing mandamus in social rights enforcement: rather than issuing a single judgment and closing the matter, the Court retained jurisdiction for over a decade, issuing periodic directions and using commissioners as eyes and ears on the ground. Third, the case demonstrated that Article 37 — which declares Directive Principles non-justiciable but fundamental to governance — does not bar the Court from reading their content into the enforceable core of a fundamental right, a method of constitutional interpretation with broad implications for other socio-economic entitlements.

## Long-term impact

The right to food litigation shaped the institutional design of the National Food Security Act 2013, which included State Food Commissions and District Grievance Redress Officers as monitoring bodies modelled in part on the judicial commissioner mechanism. The Act created legal entitlements rather than mere welfare benefits, a constitutional shift that makes denial of food grain a matter subject to statutory redress rather than administrative discretion alone. The PUCL litigation also became the template for other continuing mandamus cases addressing rights to education, housing, and healthcare, and the commissioner model was adopted in education rights litigation following the 86th Amendment and the Right to Education Act 2009. More broadly, the right to food case confirmed that the Supreme Court will treat severe socio-economic deprivation as engaging Article 21 when the State possesses the resources to remedy it but fails to act — a principle that continues to be invoked in public interest litigation on malnutrition, food adulteration, and the rights of migrant workers to access subsidised food outside their home states.
