## What happened

In early 2026, a fresh series of collegium resolutions recommending appointments and transfers to the High Courts and the Supreme Court were issued, accompanied by a pattern of executive responses that included both acceptances and prolonged silence on a number of names. Some recommendations were returned with the government's objections; the collegium reiterated several of them in accordance with the procedure established in the Second and Third Judges Cases, which obliges the executive to act once the collegium reiterates. The cycle attracted attention because of the continued gap between the number of sanctioned positions and actual strength in several High Courts, and because some long-pending recommendations remained unacted on even after reiteration.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The 2026 appointments cycle illustrated the ongoing tension within the collegium system between the formal constitutional position — that executive non-compliance with a reiterated recommendation is constitutionally untenable — and the practical reality that the Constitution provides no direct enforcement mechanism when the executive delays or withholds notification. It also highlighted the absence of any constitutional or statutory time limit on executive processing of judicial appointment recommendations, a gap that successive Law Commission reports had identified but Parliament had not addressed. The episode kept the structural weaknesses of the post-NJAC constitutional position on appointments in the foreground of constitutional practice.

## Its legacy

The 2026 cycle reinforced the position of those arguing that a more formal and time-bound statutory framework for judicial appointments — one that preserves judicial primacy while imposing procedural discipline on the executive — is constitutionally necessary. The combination of the 2025 transparency release and the 2026 appointments cycle has made the functioning of the collegium system one of the most actively contested questions in contemporary Indian constitutional governance, with implications for judicial independence, separation of powers, and the rule of law.
