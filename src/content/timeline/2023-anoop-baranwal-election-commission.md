The judgment forced appointment design into the constitutional foreground and set the stage for later statutory redesign under Article 324(2).

## What happened

Anoop Baranwal and other petitioners filed a writ petition arguing that the existing practice of appointing Election Commissioners solely on the advice of the executive — with no independent selection mechanism — was constitutionally inadequate to protect the Election Commission's independence under Article 324. A five-judge Constitution Bench, deciding the matter in March 2023, unanimously held that until Parliament enacted a law under Article 324(2), Election Commissioners would be appointed by a committee consisting of the Prime Minister, the Leader of the Opposition in the Lok Sabha, and the Chief Justice of India. The bench based its reasoning on the constitutional requirement that the Election Commission be genuinely independent and insulated from executive capture.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Court used the space opened by Article 324(2)'s reference to a "law made by Parliament" to hold that the silence of Parliament did not leave the executive free to act unilaterally — the constitutional design of independence required a selection mechanism that was itself structurally independent. The judgment applied the principle from the Second Judges Case and the NJAC judgment that institutional independence is a constitutional value, not merely a statutory preference. It also raised important questions about the extent to which the Court can prescribe interim constitutional arrangements in areas where Parliament has not legislated, even on sensitive matters of appointment to constitutional bodies.

## Its legacy

The Anoop Baranwal ruling lasted only months before Parliament enacted the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023, which replaced the Court's interim committee with a different committee that excluded the Chief Justice of India. The episode illustrated how a Supreme Court direction on constitutional design can promptly be superseded by legislation, and renewed debate about the relationship between judicial directions and legislative responses in the design of independent institutions.
