The ADR and PUCL judgments together constitute the constitutional basis for the candidate affidavit system that operates in every Indian election today. They established that informed voting is a constitutional right, not a legislative concession that Parliament can freely withdraw.

## What happened

The Association for Democratic Reforms, an NGO, obtained a direction from the Delhi High Court in 2000 requiring candidates to disclose their criminal antecedents, assets and liabilities, and educational qualifications at the time of filing nominations. The Union of India challenged this order. A three-judge bench of the Supreme Court, in Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India decided in May 2002, upheld the direction and held that voters have a constitutional right to information about those who seek to represent them, grounded in Article 19(1)(a) and in the election provisions of Articles 324 and 326. Parliament then amended the Representation of the People Act through Section 33B to narrow the scope of required disclosures. The People's Union for Civil Liberties challenged this legislative response. A Constitution Bench in PUCL v. Union of India, decided in March 2003, struck down Section 33B as unconstitutional, holding that Parliament could not by ordinary legislation abridge the constitutional right to voter information.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

These two cases established that free and fair elections, as guaranteed by the constitutional scheme, require more than the mechanical act of voting; they require conditions under which voting is genuinely informed. By locating the right to voter information within Article 19(1)(a) — the right to receive information — and in the Election Commission's plenary power under Article 324, the Court gave the right a constitutional character that was immune to legislative retreat through ordinary statute. The PUCL judgment made this explicit: Section 33B of the RP Act was struck down because it sought to negate a right that had its source not in statute but in the Constitution itself.

## Its constitutional legacy

The candidate affidavit system created by these rulings has fundamentally altered the information environment in Indian elections. Disclosures of criminal antecedents and declared assets are now filed and made publicly available before every election, enabling civil society scrutiny at scale. Subsequent litigation — including Public Interest Foundation v. Union of India (2018) on the criminalisation of politics, and challenges around false declarations — has built on the constitutional premise established here. The cases are also a significant example of judicial creation of enforceable rights in the electoral domain where legislative action was absent or contrary.
