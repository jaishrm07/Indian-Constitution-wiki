Delhi’s constitutional design is an exercise in asymmetry. This amendment is the starting point for later disputes over the elected government, the Lieutenant Governor, and Union authority.

## What happened

The Sixty-Ninth Amendment to the Constitution was passed by Parliament and came into force in February 1992. It inserted Articles 239AA and 239AB into Part VIII of the Constitution, creating a special constitutional status for the National Capital Territory of Delhi. Article 239AA established a Legislative Assembly and a Council of Ministers for Delhi, while preserving the primacy of Parliament and carving out explicit Union control over three subjects — public order, police, and land — regardless of what the Delhi Legislative Assembly might legislate. A Lieutenant Governor appointed by the President was positioned as the constitutional head of the territory, with defined but contested powers.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The amendment created a constitutional hybrid: Delhi would have an elected legislature and government accountable to it, but would not be a full state. Union authority over core administrative subjects and the LG’s structural position generated permanent ambiguity about where democratic accountability ran — to Parliament and the Centre through the LG, or to the elected Delhi government. This asymmetry was constitutionally novel and could not be resolved by analogy with any existing state or Union Territory. The amendment thus planted the seeds of decades of disputes about Cabinet advice, file-routing, and the scope of elected authority in the capital.

## Its legacy

The constitutional architecture of Article 239AA has been interpreted by the Supreme Court in multiple rounds of litigation. A Constitution Bench in 2018 held that Delhi’s elected government must have real power over subjects within its legislative competence, and that the LG is bound by Cabinet advice on those subjects. A further round of litigation on services led to a 2023 Constitution Bench ruling in favour of the elected government’s control over the civil service — only for Parliament to override that ruling by amending the Constitution within months, illustrating how the structural tension built into the Sixty-Ninth Amendment has no stable resolution through courts alone.
