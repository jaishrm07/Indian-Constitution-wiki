PESA represents Parliament's most direct attempt to reconcile the universal framework of Panchayati Raj with the constitutionally protected distinctiveness of tribal society. It created a rights-bearing gram sabha at the centre of governance in Scheduled Areas and gave tribal communities a statutory floor of autonomy over land, forests, and local resources.

## What happened

The Seventy-Third Amendment (1992) inserted Part IX into the Constitution to establish a three-tier system of Panchayati Raj institutions across rural India. However, Article 243M(4)(b) excluded Scheduled Areas under the Fifth Schedule and tribal areas under the Sixth Schedule from Part IX's automatic application, leaving Parliament with the power — and obligation — to extend Panchayati Raj to those areas by law with such exceptions and modifications as it thought fit. In 1995, the Government appointed the Bhuria Committee to examine how the Panchayati Raj framework could be extended to Scheduled Areas in a manner consistent with tribal customs and traditions. The Committee's recommendations formed the basis of the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996, which received the President's assent on 24 December 1996 and came into force immediately.

PESA applied to the Fifth Schedule areas in nine states: Andhra Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Gujarat, Himachal Pradesh, Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Odisha, and Rajasthan. The Act required each such state to amend its Panchayati Raj legislation within one year to bring it into conformity with PESA. The central innovation of PESA was to vest the gram sabha — the general assembly of all adult voters of a village — with specific powers over natural resources, cultural practices, and local dispute resolution that went well beyond the powers granted to panchayats in non-Scheduled Areas.

## Constitutional significance

PESA is constitutionally significant on at least three grounds. First, it operationalised Article 243M(4)(b) by giving tribal communities a governance framework calibrated to their constitutional status under the Fifth Schedule and Article 244 rather than simply importing the standard Panchayati Raj template. Second, it recognised the gram sabha as an institution of self-governance with original authority — over the management of natural resources, minor forest produce, minor water bodies, and village markets — rather than as a subordinate body of the elected panchayat. This was a significant doctrinal recognition that tribal self-governance has constitutional roots in custom and community, not merely in delegated statutory authority.

Third, PESA imposed restrictions on the acquisition and alienation of tribal land in Scheduled Areas and required consultation with gram sabhas before land is acquired for development projects. This brought PESA into direct contact with the constitutional protection of tribal land rights under the Fifth Schedule, the Samatha v. State of Andhra Pradesh judgment (1997) of the Supreme Court, and later with the Forest Rights Act, 2006. PESA thus served as a bridge between the constitutional framework of tribal autonomy in the Fifth Schedule and the rights-based framework of later legislation.

## Long-term impact

PESA's implementation has been uneven. Most covered states took years to amend their Panchayati Raj laws, and the amendments frequently diluted the gram sabha's powers. The Act became a baseline reference in judicial and parliamentary debates about the rights of Scheduled Tribes over land, forests, and minerals. In Samatha v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1997), decided shortly after PESA came into force, the Supreme Court held that the Fifth Schedule prohibits transfer of tribal land — including leases for mining — to non-tribals, relying on the same constitutional protection for tribal territory that underpins PESA. The Forest Rights Act, 2006, later reinforced the gram sabha's central role, creating significant overlap with PESA's framework. PESA remains the primary statutory expression of the constitutional guarantee of tribal self-governance and continues to be invoked in disputes over mining leases, displacement, and customary resource rights in the Fifth Schedule states.
