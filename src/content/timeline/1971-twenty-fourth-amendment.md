## What happened

The Twenty-Fourth Amendment was Parliament's legislative riposte to the Supreme Court's 1967 decision in Golaknath v. State of Punjab, in which an eleven-judge Constitution Bench had held, by a six-to-five majority, that a constitutional amendment is "law" within the meaning of Article 13 and that Parliament therefore could not use its amending power under Article 368 to abridge or take away fundamental rights. That ruling had left the government's land reform and nationalisation programmes in constitutional uncertainty, since most of the redistributive legislation challenged in Golaknath had been placed in the Ninth Schedule and was protected from Part III challenge — but the ruling cast doubt on whether future amendments could add new entries to the Ninth Schedule or otherwise modify fundamental rights.

Parliament enacted the Twenty-Fourth Amendment on 5 November 1971, making two targeted changes. It amended Article 368 to clarify that Parliament's power to amend the Constitution extended to any provision of the Constitution, and it inserted a new clause 3 into Article 368 providing explicitly that nothing in Article 13 would apply to any amendment made under Article 368. The amendment also substituted the word "shall" for "may" in the clause requiring Presidential assent to a constitutional amendment bill, settling the question of whether the President had any discretion to withhold assent. A further technical amendment to Article 13 itself clarified that the word "law" in that article did not include a constitutional amendment made under Article 368.

## Constitutional significance

The Twenty-Fourth Amendment is one of the clearest examples in Indian constitutional history of Parliament using its constituent power to respond to and reverse a judicial decision interpreting the Constitution. Golaknath had placed fundamental rights outside the reach of the amending power; the Twenty-Fourth Amendment explicitly reasserted that no part of the Constitution was immune from amendment by Parliament acting in its constituent capacity. The distinction Parliament drew — between ordinary legislative power (constrained by Part III) and constituent amending power (not so constrained) — was precisely the distinction the Golaknath majority had collapsed.

The amendment's insertion of Article 368(3), expressly excluding constitutional amendments from Article 13's prohibition on "law" inconsistent with fundamental rights, was a direct textual correction of the Golaknath majority's reasoning. The majority had relied on the proposition that the word "law" in Article 13(2) was wide enough to include amendments; the Twenty-Fourth Amendment made the constitutional text itself declare the contrary. This technique — amending the Constitution not to change a substantive right but to alter the interpretive framework that courts were applying — was constitutionally novel and demonstrated Parliament's confidence that it controlled the meaning of the Constitution's own amending provisions.

The amendment was immediately challenged, and the challenge reached a thirteen-judge bench in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973). The Court upheld the Twenty-Fourth Amendment as valid, accepting that Parliament did have the power to amend any provision of the Constitution including fundamental rights. However, by a narrow seven-to-six majority, the Court simultaneously announced the basic structure doctrine — holding that even a validly enacted amendment could not destroy the Constitution's basic or essential features. The Twenty-Fourth Amendment was thus upheld on its own terms while a new, judicially constructed limit on amendment power was announced that Golaknath's text-based approach had not anticipated.

## Long-term impact

The Twenty-Fourth Amendment settled, for the purposes of formal constitutional text, the question of whether Parliament could amend fundamental rights: it plainly could. What it did not and could not settle was the more fundamental question of whether there were limits on the substance of what those amendments could achieve. That question was answered by Kesavananda's basic structure doctrine, which has governed Indian constitutional amendment ever since.

The interaction between the Twenty-Fourth Amendment and the basic structure doctrine produced a distinctive constitutional settlement: Parliament can amend any provision of the Constitution, including the fundamental rights, but it cannot use amendments to damage or destroy the Constitution's basic features — which include the supremacy of the Constitution itself, the republican and democratic form of government, the separation of powers, and judicial review, among others. This settlement has been tested repeatedly but has held, shaping every major constitutional controversy from Minerva Mills (1980) to the NJAC case (2015). The Twenty-Fourth Amendment's significance thus lies not in what it finally resolved, but in the constitutional confrontation it provoked and the doctrine that confrontation ultimately produced.
