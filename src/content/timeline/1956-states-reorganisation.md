The States Reorganisation era turned territorial change into a recurring constitutional technique. It is the historical setting in which Articles 3 and 4 become practical tools rather than abstract text.

## What happened

The States Reorganisation Act 1956, which came into force on 1 November 1956, comprehensively redrawn India's internal territorial boundaries primarily along linguistic lines. The reorganisation dissolved the earlier system of Part A, B, and C states inherited from the independence settlement, replacing it with fourteen states and six union territories. It was preceded by the report of the States Reorganisation Commission, constituted in 1953 following the death of Potti Sriramulu during a hunger strike demanding a separate Telugu-speaking state, which had forced the earlier creation of Andhra State in 1953. The 1956 reorganisation represented the most extensive redrawing of the Indian federal map undertaken at a single moment and was implemented through parliamentary legislation under Articles 3 and 4.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The reorganisation confirmed that India's federal units were not constitutionally entrenched in the way that American states are: Parliament retained the power under Articles 3 and 4 to alter state boundaries, names, and areas without the consent of the affected state's legislature, requiring only that the concerned state be given an opportunity to express its views. This made Indian federalism structurally asymmetric compared to classical federal models, since the constituent units exist at the pleasure of central legislation in a way that has no parallel in the United States or Australia. The reorganisation also demonstrated how linguistic identity could operate as a legitimate organising principle for sub-national units within a constitutional framework that formally prohibited discrimination on the basis of language.

## Its legacy

The 1956 reorganisation did not end territorial demands but institutionalised the process through which they would be addressed, establishing a precedent that legitimate linguistic and regional aspirations could be accommodated through the constitutional mechanism of state creation. Subsequent reorganisations — including the creation of Maharashtra and Gujarat in 1960, Haryana and Himachal Pradesh, and later Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and Uttarakhand in 2000 — all proceeded under the same Articles 3 and 4 framework. The reorganisation era also seeded the demand for smaller states that has continued to recur in Indian politics.
