Minerva Mills matters because it shows that the basic structure doctrine did not remain an isolated statement from 1973. It became an operative limit in later constitutional conflict.

## What happened

The Minerva Mills case arose when Minerva Mills Ltd challenged the Forty-Second Amendment, which had inserted clauses into Article 368 declaring that Parliament's constituent power was unlimited and that no constitutional amendment could be questioned in any court. The petitioners also challenged nationalisation of the textile mill under the Sick Textiles Nationalisation Act, which had been placed in the Ninth Schedule. A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, deciding the matter in July 1980, struck down the clauses that claimed unlimited amendment power. The Court held that the Constitution's balance between Parts III and IV — Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles — was itself a basic feature that Parliament could not obliterate.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Minerva Mills answered a crucial question left open after Kesavananda Bharati: could Parliament insulate its own amendment power from judicial scrutiny by amending Article 368 itself? The Court said no, because an unlimited amending power would transform a controlled constitution into an autocratic one, destroying the very features it was designed to protect. The judgment also reaffirmed that Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles are complementary, not hostile, and that tilting the balance entirely toward either would damage the basic structure. This dual holding — against unlimited amendment power and in favour of constitutional balance — gave the basic structure doctrine its working operational content.

## Its legacy

Minerva Mills became an indispensable companion to Kesavananda, together forming the twin pillars of the basic structure doctrine that every subsequent amendment challenge must address. The ruling made it constitutionally impossible to formally codify parliamentary supremacy over the Constitution, cementing judicial review as a structural guarantee. Later decisions including I.R. Coelho and the NJAC case drew on its reasoning to test whether specific constitutional changes had crossed the line from permissible amendment into impermissible transformation.
