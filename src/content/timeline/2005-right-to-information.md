The RTI Act is the most significant transparency legislation in independent India. It operationalised a constitutional right that courts had been recognising since the 1970s and created an institutional architecture — Public Information Officers, Information Commissions, and a tiered appeals structure — through which citizens could enforce their entitlement to government information.

## What happened

The right to information had a long constitutional prehistory before 2005. The Supreme Court had recognised as early as S.P. Gupta v. Union of India in 1981 that open government — meaning citizens' access to information about government actions — is implicit in the freedoms of Article 19(1)(a). The Freedom of Information Act 2002 was a weak first attempt at legislation but was never fully notified and lacked effective enforcement mechanisms. A sustained campaign by civil society groups, particularly rooted in rural Rajasthan through the Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan and its jan sunwai (public hearing) model, created political pressure for a stronger law. The UPA government enacted the Right to Information Act in June 2005, which came into force in October 2005. The Act requires every public authority to maintain and proactively disclose information, appoints Public Information Officers in each public body, creates Central and State Information Commissions as first appellate authorities, and imposes penalties for non-compliance.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The RTI Act gave concrete institutional form to the constitutional right of access to information. The Supreme Court had previously held, in State of U.P. v. Raj Narain and Bennett Coleman v. Union of India, that Article 19(1)(a) includes the right to receive information and that an informed citizenry is essential to constitutional democracy. The RTI Act translated these general constitutional propositions into enforceable individual entitlements: a citizen could file a request, receive a legally binding response within thirty days, and appeal non-compliance to a statutory commission. The Act also recognised Article 21 implications: access to information about government welfare schemes directly affects the ability of citizens to claim entitlements that touch their livelihood and dignity.

## Its constitutional legacy

The RTI Act transformed the relationship between citizens and the State by making government information a right rather than a favour. Two decades of experience have produced a large body of case law on what counts as a public authority, the scope of exemptions under Section 8, and the relationship between transparency and privacy. The Act has been used to expose corruption in public procurement, land allocation, and social welfare programmes. Its reach has been contested — with recurring debates about whether political parties, judicial appointments, and intelligence agencies fall within its scope. The Act stands as a legislative embodiment of the constitutional principle that open, accountable government is not merely good administration but a constitutional value.
