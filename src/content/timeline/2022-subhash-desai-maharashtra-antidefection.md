## What happened

In June 2022 a faction of the Shiv Sena led by Eknath Shinde broke away from the party led by Uddhav Thackeray, who was then Chief Minister of Maharashtra at the head of the Maha Vikas Aghadi coalition. The Shinde faction claimed it constituted the real Shiv Sena, relying on the support of a majority of the party's MLAs and MPs. The Governor called for a trust vote on the basis of communications from the rebel MLAs. Thackeray resigned before the trust vote. Eknath Shinde was subsequently sworn in as Chief Minister with BJP support. The Thackeray faction filed disqualification petitions against the rebel MLAs under the Tenth Schedule and challenged the Governor's action before the Supreme Court.

A five-judge Constitution Bench delivered its judgment on 11 May 2023. It held that the Governor had erred in directing a trust vote, because the materials before him — communications from discontented MLAs — did not provide sufficient objective basis to conclude that the government had lost majority support in the Assembly. It further held that the Speaker who had been elected with the support of the Shinde faction was not disqualified from adjudicating the disqualification petitions, but it could not reinstate Thackeray as Chief Minister because he had resigned voluntarily. It referred the question of which faction was the real Shiv Sena to the Election Commission, which subsequently ruled in favour of the Shinde faction.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Subhash Desai is the leading contemporary statement on the constitutional duties of the Governor under Articles 163 and 164, and on the relationship between the anti-defection law and the Governor's exercise of constitutional discretion. The Court's finding that the Governor had acted improperly confirmed that a Governor cannot be used as a constitutional instrument to destabilise a functioning government at the request of legislators who wish to switch sides. It also addressed a structural gap in the Tenth Schedule: the Speaker's power to decide disqualification petitions — when the Speaker's own position depends on the outcome — creates a conflict of interest that the Court was asked but unable to fully resolve. The case added to the series of judgments, from Kihoto Hollohan through Nabam Rebia and Keisham Meghachandra Singh, defining the Tenth Schedule's application to fragmented party politics.

## Its constitutional legacy

The Maharashtra judgment produced no clear winner: Thackeray could not be reinstated, Shinde remained Chief Minister, and the disqualification petitions were left to a newly elected Speaker. This practical outcome — significant constitutional violations found, but no effective remedy available — reinforced concerns about the adequacy of the anti-defection framework as a deterrent against organised rebellion. The judgment is now the primary authority on the Governor's role in trust-vote situations and on the interaction between the Tenth Schedule and the political question doctrine.
