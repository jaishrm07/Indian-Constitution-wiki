Lily Thomas is the baseline case on criminalisation of politics because it closes the gap between conviction and constitutional disqualification.

## What happened

Lily Thomas, a lawyer, filed a writ petition challenging Section 8(4) of the Representation of the People Act, which allowed sitting members of Parliament and state legislatures who were convicted of an offence to retain their seats during the period in which an appeal against conviction was pending. This provision had long been criticised as creating an unjustifiable distinction between sitting legislators and candidates, since candidates with pending convictions were disqualified at the point of filing nomination while sitting members could continue serving even after conviction. A two-judge bench of the Supreme Court struck down Section 8(4) in July 2013, holding that it was beyond Parliament's competence and contrary to Articles 102 and 191, which provide that disqualification operates on conviction regardless of any appeal.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment held that Parliament cannot legislate to dilute or defer a disqualification that the Constitution itself makes operative. Articles 102 and 191 leave no gap through which ordinary legislation can grant convicted legislators a grace period that the constitutional text does not. The Court confirmed that the constitutional scheme treats conviction as the disqualifying event, not the exhaustion of the appeals process, and that Section 8(4) attempted to modify this constitutional position in a manner that was ultra vires. The ruling was a strong assertion that constitutional disqualification provisions are self-executing and not subject to legislative override.

## Its legacy

The immediate effect of Lily Thomas was that several sitting legislators faced immediate disqualification upon conviction, most prominently in high-profile cases that followed shortly after the judgment. The ruling also prompted significant political controversy, with the then-government briefly attempting to promulgate an ordinance to protect sitting legislators — an attempt that was publicly withdrawn after widespread criticism. Lily Thomas has since been cited as a constitutional anchor in debates about criminalisation of politics, and it stands as a demonstration that the Supreme Court will enforce constitutional disqualification provisions against legislative attempts to dilute them, even when those provisions have immediate political consequences.
