The ruling resets the modern privilege debate by making clear that legislative immunity cannot be used as a shield against prosecution for accepting a bribe.

## What happened

Sita Soren, a member of the Jharkhand Mukti Morcha, was accused of accepting a bribe in exchange for voting in a particular manner in a Rajya Sabha election. She claimed immunity from prosecution under Article 194(2), arguing that her vote in the House fell within the protection of legislative privilege. A seven-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in March 2024, unanimously held that the privilege under Articles 105 and 194 does not extend to the act of accepting a bribe, and expressly overruled the five-judge majority holding in P.V. Narasimha Rao to the contrary. The Court held that P.V. Narasimha Rao had wrongly interpreted legislative privilege and had produced an outcome that placed the Constitution in service of corruption.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The P.V. Narasimha Rao ruling had created a paradox in which a member who accepted a bribe and voted as directed was immune from prosecution while a member who accepted the bribe but voted differently was not. The seven-judge bench rejected this distinction as irrational and inconsistent with the constitutional text and purpose of Articles 105 and 194, which protect deliberative freedom in the House, not pre-House corrupt bargains. The decision clarified that legislative privilege is a functional immunity tied to the exercise of legislative duties, not a personal immunity that can be extended to criminal conduct outside the chamber even if that conduct is causally connected to a subsequent vote.

## Its legacy

The Sita Soren ruling substantially narrowed the scope of legislative privilege as a defence in bribery and corruption prosecutions against legislators, with direct implications for the prosecution of elected officials. It also strengthened the constitutional case that democratic accountability requires that the criminal law apply to legislators in the same way it applies to others when they engage in corrupt transactions, regardless of whether the corruption is connected to their legislative conduct.
