## What happened

On 31 October 1984, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi was shot by two of her Sikh bodyguards at her residence in New Delhi. Within hours of the news becoming public, violent attacks on Sikh communities broke out in Delhi and spread to Kanpur, Bokaro, and other cities across northern India. Over the next three days, more than three thousand Sikhs — some estimates place the figure considerably higher — were killed. Homes, businesses, and gurudwaras were burned. Survivors described systematic, organised violence: attackers arrived with voter rolls, identified Sikh households, and carried out killings with a coordination that pointed to advance preparation. The Delhi Police was conspicuously absent, and the Army was not deployed for the critical first forty-eight hours. Senior Congress leaders were subsequently accused of inciting or directing the violence.

The Indian state's response to the violence was, constitutionally, a failure on multiple levels. The immediate duty to protect life under Article 21 was not discharged. The equal protection guarantee of Article 14 and the prohibition on discrimination on grounds of religion under Article 15 were visibly suspended in practice during the days of violence. No significant prosecution followed in the immediate aftermath. The Marwah Commission, the Ranganath Misra Commission, the Jain-Banerjee Committee, the Potti-Rosha Committee, the Ahuja Committee, the Mathew Commission, and the Nanavati Commission were all constituted over succeeding decades — a litany of official inquiries that produced limited accountability. It was not until 2005 that the Nanavati Commission, the last and most thorough official inquiry, named senior Congress politicians as having played an instigating role, findings that the government accepted in part.

In 2018, the Supreme Court constituted a Special Investigation Team to re-examine cases that had been closed without prosecution, acknowledging that the earlier investigations had been inadequate. Further convictions followed in subsequent years, including a sessions court conviction in 2018 of a Congress leader in connection with killings at Sultanpuri and Mongolpuri — the first significant conviction of a named political figure connected to the violence.

## Constitutional significance

The 1984 violence and its prolonged legal aftermath raised questions that go to the heart of what constitutional guarantees mean when state institutions fail to enforce them. Article 21's guarantee of the right to life is directed at the State: the State must not take life arbitrarily, but the Court has also developed a positive obligation under Article 21 — affirmed in cases from Hussainara Khatoon onwards — that the State must take reasonable steps to protect life. Where the State not only fails to protect but where evidence points to active facilitation of violence by state actors or their political principals, the constitutional failure is of a different and more serious character.

The repeated constitution of inquiry commissions without consequential prosecution illustrated the distinction between formal constitutional machinery and effective constitutional justice. Article 32 guarantees the right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights, but that guarantee is only as effective as the institutional will to use it on behalf of those who lack access or resources. Survivors and the families of victims pursued remedies for decades before obtaining partial redress, demonstrating the gap between constitutional text and constitutional reality that the Supreme Court has acknowledged must be bridged through sustained institutional commitment.

The Nanavati Commission's findings also raised structural constitutional questions about the role of the Governor and the Centre under Article 356 and about the Centre's obligation to prevent communal violence. If a State government had been responsible for such organised communal killings, Article 356 might have been invoked. The fact that the violence was substantially directed or permitted by the Central government itself exposed a gap in the constitutional framework: its accountability mechanisms were designed primarily for abuse by State governments, not by the Centre.

## Long-term impact

The 1984 violence became a persistent reference point in Indian debates about constitutional accountability, communal violence, and impunity. It informed the legislative response to communal violence in subsequent decades, including demands for a comprehensive Communal Violence (Prevention, Control and Rehabilitation of Victims) Bill, which was drafted but never enacted, partly because its provisions for Central accountability for communal violence proved politically contentious.

The Supreme Court's reconstitution of SIT investigations in 2018 established an important precedent that the Court could intervene to revive dormant investigations where the ordinary machinery of criminal justice had demonstrably failed constitutional obligations. That power of judicial supervision over investigative and prosecutorial decisions — while constitutionally sensitive given the separation of functions — has become an accepted tool in cases where Article 21 violations are alleged and state machinery appears to be protecting rather than prosecuting those responsible.

The 1984 violence remains a marker against which the State's discharge of its Article 21 duties in subsequent communal violence episodes — whether in 1992-93, 2002, or later — has been measured, both in the courts and in public constitutional discourse. Its constitutional significance lies not only in the legal proceedings it eventually generated but in what it exposed about the conditions under which constitutional guarantees either function or collapse.
