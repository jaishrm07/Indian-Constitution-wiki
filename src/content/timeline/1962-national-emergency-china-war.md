## What happened

On 20 October 1962, Chinese forces launched a large-scale military offensive across the McMahon Line in the North-East Frontier Agency (NEFA, now Arunachal Pradesh) and in the Aksai Chin region in the west. The offensive caught the Indian Army in a severely disadvantaged position, resulting in rapid territorial reverses. On 26 October 1962, President Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan proclaimed a National Emergency under Article 352 of the Constitution, on the ground of threat of external aggression. This was the first time the emergency power in Article 352 had been invoked since the Constitution came into force in 1950.

China declared a unilateral ceasefire on 21 November 1962 and withdrew its forces to positions it claimed predated the conflict. Despite the military situation having stabilised, the Emergency proclamation was not revoked. It remained in force when India and Pakistan went to war in September 1965. A second Emergency proclamation was made on 6 December 1971 during the Bangladesh Liberation War before the 1962 proclamation was finally revoked on 10 January 1968 — more than five years after it had been issued and more than two years after the 1965 war had ended.

## Constitutional effects of the Emergency

The proclamation of a National Emergency under Article 352 activated a series of constitutional consequences. Under Article 353, Parliament acquired the power to legislate on subjects in the State List, and the executive power of the Union extended to giving directions to states. Under Article 354, Parliament could direct that provisions of Articles 268 to 279 relating to distribution of revenues between Union and states would apply with modifications, permitting the Centre to adjust fiscal federalism during the Emergency. Under Article 358, the freedoms in Article 19 were automatically suspended for the duration of the Emergency. Under Article 359, the President could by separate order suspend the right to move any court for the enforcement of specified fundamental rights other than Articles 20 and 21.

The Emergency effectively centralised power in the Union for its duration, enabling Parliament to legislate across the entire concurrent and state domains without the ordinary federal limitations. Defence legislation was enacted or expanded, and the Defence of India Act 1962 and the Defence of India Rules gave the government wide powers of detention, requisition, and censorship that operated alongside the constitutional Emergency powers.

## Constitutional significance and lessons

The 1962 Emergency is constitutionally notable for several reasons beyond its immediate context. It was the first test of whether India's emergency provisions, drawn largely from the Government of India Act 1935, would function within a democratic constitutional framework. The proclamation was made and approved by Parliament, which passed the requisite resolution, demonstrating the basic procedural framework in operation. However, the prolonged duration of the Emergency — long after any genuine military exigency — highlighted the absence of meaningful mechanisms to compel the executive to revoke it once proclaimed.

The Emergency also had significant implications for civil liberties in practice, even if the formal rights suspensions under Articles 358 and 359 were partial. The Defence of India Rules enabled widespread preventive detention without the normal procedural safeguards, and this experience contributed to later debates about the adequacy of Articles 21 and 22 as protections against executive detention — debates that culminated in A.K. Gopalan (1950), A.D.M. Jabalpur (1976), and ultimately Maneka Gandhi (1978). The 1962 Emergency's extended duration also informed the drafting of the Forty-Fourth Amendment in 1978, which tightened the procedural requirements for proclaiming and continuing a National Emergency and required parliamentary approval by special majority.
