The ruling shows that decentralisation and equality are constitutionally linked. Representation in local institutions is not an afterthought to constitutional democracy.

## What happened

K. Krishna Murthy and connected cases challenged state-level reservation policies for local body elections, particularly the reservation of seats in Panchayats and municipalities for Other Backward Classes. The questions before the Supreme Court included whether backward-class reservations in local bodies required the same kind of empirical justification as reservations in employment, and whether the overall reservation levels could breach the 50 per cent ceiling affirmed in Indra Sawhney. A Constitution Bench decided the matter in May 2010. The Court held that reservations for OBCs in local bodies are not automatic and require the state to undertake a contemporaneous empirical inquiry into the backwardness of the communities concerned and the inadequacy of their representation in local governance before making such reservations.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment applied a rigorous justificatory standard to OBC reservations in local bodies that was stricter than what had typically been required in service reservations. The Court reasoned that the constitutional mandate under Articles 243D and 243T for reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women is categorical and needs no separate empirical foundation, but that OBC reservation is conditional on a dedicated inquiry. This created a triple-test framework — a dedicated commission, empirical data, and a proportionality assessment — that states had to satisfy before notifying OBC reservations in local body elections.

## Its legacy

K. Krishna Murthy became an important constraint on state power in the local body reservation domain. States that bypassed the required inquiry found their reservation notifications struck down in subsequent litigation. The triple-test requirement the Court articulated was confirmed and its contours further elaborated by later benches. The case also highlighted the constitutional distinctiveness of local body reservations as a category — neither identical to employment reservations under Articles 15 and 16 nor wholly separate from the equality framework they share — and has been a central reference in ongoing debates about OBC reservation policy in municipal elections across states.
