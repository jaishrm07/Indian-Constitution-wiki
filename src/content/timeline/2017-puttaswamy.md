This judgment changed how constitutional law responds to digital systems, surveillance, and personal autonomy.

## What happened

Justice K.S. Puttaswamy, a retired High Court judge, challenged the Aadhaar biometric identity scheme, arguing that the compulsory collection and use of personal biometric data violated a constitutionally protected right to privacy. The central government's preliminary position was that there was no fundamental right to privacy under the Constitution, relying on the earlier decisions in M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh. A nine-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in August 2017, unanimously rejected that position and held that the right to privacy is a fundamental right protected under Article 21 and forms part of the broader guarantee of life and personal liberty. The bench produced several concurrent opinions exploring privacy through dignity, autonomy, and informational control.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment overruled M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh insofar as they denied a free-standing right to privacy, settling a question that had been constitutionally unresolved for decades. Privacy was grounded not just in Article 21 but in the broader structure of Part III as protecting individual autonomy against state overreach. The decision established that privacy is not an absolute right and may be limited by law that is proportionate to a legitimate state aim, importing proportionality as an explicit constitutional standard into Indian fundamental rights analysis. It also confirmed that dignity forms an essential component of the constitutional protection of liberty.

## Its legacy

Puttaswamy became the constitutional anchor for subsequent challenges involving data protection, surveillance, and identity systems, directly shaping the legal and legislative debates around the Personal Data Protection framework. Its proportionality standard has been cited across rights cases ranging from Navtej Singh Johar to the Electoral Bonds judgment, making it one of the most broadly applicable doctrinal statements of the modern Supreme Court.
