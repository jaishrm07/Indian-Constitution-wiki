## What happened

In March 2016 a political crisis developed in Uttarakhand when nine Congress MLAs rebelled against Chief Minister Harish Rawat and supported a BJP-sponsored Appropriations Bill in the Assembly. The Governor, H.K. Shukla, reported to the President that the Rawat government had lost majority support. On 27 March 2016 the President issued a proclamation under Article 356 dismissing the elected government and imposing President's Rule. Harish Rawat challenged the proclamation before the Uttarakhand High Court, which, sitting as a division bench, stayed the imposition of President's Rule and directed that a floor test be conducted. The Rawat government subsequently won the floor test and was reinstated. The Union of India appealed to the Supreme Court, which upheld the High Court's approach, confirming that the proclamation had been premature and that the floor test was the constitutionally mandated mechanism for resolving questions of legislative majority.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Uttarakhand is the clearest post-Bommai example of courts actively enforcing the floor-test requirement rather than merely articulating it in principle. The episode showed that judicial scrutiny of Article 356 proclamations had become sufficiently robust to reverse a presidential proclamation in a matter of days. The case raised important questions about the Governor's constitutional role: the Governor's report had treated the rebellion of a few MLAs and the passage of a financial bill as equivalent to the loss of majority support, a conflation that the courts found unsustainable. The case also tested whether the political calculations underlying a Governor's recommendation could be reached by reviewing courts, and the answer — on the particular facts — was yes.

## Its constitutional legacy

The Uttarakhand case, coming over two decades after Bommai, showed that the doctrine was alive and operational rather than merely advisory. It added to a series of decisions — including the Arunachal Pradesh matter decided by the Supreme Court in the same period — in which courts intervened at speed to correct the misuse of President's Rule. The episode became a standard reference point in constitutional law teaching on Article 356, demonstrating that the combination of speedy judicial process and the floor-test requirement had materially reduced the window available to the Centre to dismiss elected state governments without a sustainable constitutional basis.
