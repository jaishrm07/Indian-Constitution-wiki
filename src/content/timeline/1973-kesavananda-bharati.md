## What happened

Kesavananda Bharati, the head of Edneer Mutt in Kerala, challenged the Kerala Land Reforms Act under Articles 25 and 26 as a violation of his right to manage the religious institution's property. The case was placed before a thirteen-judge Constitution Bench — the largest ever convened in the Supreme Court's history — primarily to resolve the conflict created by the Golaknath decision (1967) and the Twenty-Fourth, Twenty-Fifth, and Twenty-Ninth Amendments through which Parliament had sought to restore its amending power and make clear that fundamental rights could be amended.

On 24 April 1973, seven of the thirteen judges joined a summary statement holding that Parliament's amending power under Article 368 did not extend to altering or destroying the basic structure or essential features of the Constitution. The other six judges dissented or did not join the summary statement. The majority upheld Parliament's power to amend fundamental rights — overruling Golaknath on that point — but held that this power had an implied substantive limit: it could not be used to destroy what the judges identified as the Constitution's basic structure.

## Constitutional significance

The basic structure doctrine was a creative act of constitutional interpretation with no direct textual foundation. The judges derived an implicit limitation on the formal amending process from the Constitution's overall structure, character, and purpose — particularly the idea that a power to amend cannot be used to destroy the thing being amended. The doctrine created a category of constitutional essentials — identified over subsequent cases to include the supremacy of the Constitution, separation of powers, judicial review, federalism, democracy, the rule of law, and the unity and sovereignty of India — that no amendment may remove.

This resolved the Parliament-judiciary conflict over constitutional supremacy by drawing a line: Parliament may amend any provision, including fundamental rights, but cannot use that power to destroy the Constitution's identity. The judgment simultaneously acknowledged Parliament's constituent power and asserted judicial authority to review whether that power had been exercised within its limits.

## Long-term impact

The basic structure doctrine became the most influential contribution of Indian constitutional jurisprudence to comparative constitutional law, cited and adapted by courts in Bangladesh, Pakistan, Kenya, and other jurisdictions. Within India, it has been invoked to strike down constitutional amendments affecting the independence of the judiciary (Minerva Mills, 1980), the electoral process (Indira Nehru Gandhi, 1975), and the appointment process for judges (NJAC case, 2015). The judgment's survival through the Emergency period — when the government explicitly sought to circumvent it through the Forty-Second Amendment — gave the doctrine a legitimacy that made it effectively irreversible in Indian constitutional practice.
