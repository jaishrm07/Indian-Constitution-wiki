The case shows how older speech principles were translated into the constitutional governance of digital communication.

## What happened

Shreya Singhal, a law student, filed a writ petition before the Supreme Court challenging Section 66A of the Information Technology Act, 2000, which made it a criminal offence to send through a computer or communication device information that was "grossly offensive," had "menacing character," or caused "annoyance, inconvenience, danger, obstruction, insult, injury, criminal intimidation, enmity, hatred, or ill will." The provision had been widely criticised for enabling the arrest of citizens for social media posts on matters of public interest. A two-judge bench of the Supreme Court decided the matter in March 2015, striking down Section 66A in its entirety. The Court held that the provision's terms were so vague and overbroad that they could not be saved by reading down, and that it fell outside the permissible restrictions on speech under Article 19(2).

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Shreya Singhal is doctrinally significant for several reasons. The Court applied the principle of vagueness with full rigour to a speech-restricting provision, holding that a law which ordinary citizens cannot understand with reasonable certainty as to what it prohibits cannot survive Article 19(1)(a) scrutiny. It also distinguished clearly between discussion and advocacy on the one hand and incitement on the other — a distinction drawn from older free speech jurisprudence — and held that Section 66A captured vast amounts of protected expression along with any legitimately restricted content, making it constitutionally unsalvageable. The judgment confirmed that the constitutional guarantee of freedom of speech and expression applies fully to digital and online communication.

## Its legacy

Shreya Singhal became the foundational case for digital free speech law in India and is the starting point for any constitutional analysis of internet speech regulation. The striking down of Section 66A was later found to be only partially effective because lower courts and police continued to apply and register cases under the struck-down provision, leading the Supreme Court to take up the question of its continued enforcement. The case also established that overbreadth and vagueness are independent constitutional infirmities under Article 19, providing a tool for challenging poorly drafted speech laws that would otherwise survive a narrower proportionality analysis.
