## What happened

The Twenty-Fifth Amendment was enacted by Parliament in April 1971, in the immediate aftermath of the Supreme Court's landmark decision in R.C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970), which had struck down the Bank Nationalisation Act by holding that it violated Articles 14 and 19. The Cooper ruling exposed the Indira Gandhi government's nationalisation programme to successful constitutional challenge, because courts could scrutinise the adequacy of compensation offered to dispossessed property owners and the reasonableness of the restrictions imposed on the right to carry on business.

The Amendment made two principal changes. First, it amended Article 31(2) to replace the phrase "equivalent of the amount" with "amount," removing the judicially enforceable requirement that compensation for compulsorily acquired property reflect the property's true market value; courts could no longer review the adequacy of compensation. Second, and more far-reaching, it inserted Article 31C. This new provision stated that a law enacted to give effect to the policy in Articles 39(b) or 39(c) — which direct the State to ensure that material resources are distributed to serve the common good and that wealth is not concentrated in the hands of a few — would not be deemed void merely because it was inconsistent with the rights under Articles 14, 19, or 31. Article 31C also provided that no court could question whether a law in fact gave effect to those Directives, removing judicial scrutiny of legislative purpose in this domain.

## Constitutional significance

The Twenty-Fifth Amendment crystallised a fundamental tension running through the Constitution since its adoption — the conflict between the justiciable fundamental rights in Part III and the non-justiciable Directive Principles in Part IV. The drafters had treated Parts III and IV as complementary, and Nehru's government had always maintained that the Constitution permitted the State to pursue socialist economic objectives. But successive Supreme Court rulings — from State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan through Golaknath to Cooper — had made it clear that Acts implementing property redistribution or nationalisation could be invalidated if they infringed Articles 14, 19, or 31.

Article 31C resolved this tension in Parliament's favour by creating a hierarchy: laws serving the specified Directive Principles would prevail over conflicting fundamental rights. The immunity from judicial review of legislative purpose was particularly striking — it attempted to remove courts' power to examine whether a given piece of legislation genuinely advanced the Directive Principles it invoked, or merely used that language as a shield.

In Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973), the Supreme Court partially upheld Article 31C. The majority sustained the provision insofar as it protected laws giving effect to Articles 39(b) and 39(c) from Articles 14 and 19 challenges, but struck down the clause ousting judicial review of whether a law genuinely served those purposes. The basic structure doctrine announced in Kesavananda simultaneously constrained how far future amendments could extend Article 31C's logic — an issue that arose directly when the Forty-Second Amendment expanded the provision.

## Long-term impact

The Twenty-Fifth Amendment set the template for the Parliament-judiciary conflict over property rights and economic legislation that dominated Indian constitutional politics through the 1970s and into the 1980s. The Forty-Second Amendment (1976) attempted to expand Article 31C to protect laws giving effect to any Directive Principle — not merely Articles 39(b) and (c) — but this expansion was struck down in Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980) as violating the basic structure by damaging the essential balance between fundamental rights and directive principles.

The amendment also contributed to the eventual deletion of the right to property from the fundamental rights chapter. By 1978, the Forty-Fourth Amendment removed Article 31 from Part III and made the right to property a mere constitutional right under Article 300A, enforceable against the State but no longer carrying the same justiciable status as the other fundamental rights. This change resolved much of the tension the Twenty-Fifth Amendment had addressed, though Article 31C survived in its Kesavananda-endorsed form and continues to protect property redistribution legislation targeting concentration of wealth.
