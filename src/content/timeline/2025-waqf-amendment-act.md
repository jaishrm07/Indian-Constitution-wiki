The enactment did not close the constitutional question. It shifted the dispute from legislative design to commencement, implementation, and possible judicial challenge.

## What happened

Parliament enacted the Waqf (Amendment) Act, 2025, which received Presidential assent on 5 April 2025. The legislation introduced significant changes to the administration and governance of waqf properties in India, including modifications to the composition of the Central and State Waqf Boards, changes to the survey and registration of waqf properties, and alterations to the tribunal framework for waqf disputes. Several provisions were contested on the ground that they interfered with the autonomy of Muslim religious and charitable endowments in ways that engaged Articles 25, 26, 29, and 30 of the Constitution. Petitions challenging the Act's constitutional validity were filed promptly after assent, and the Supreme Court listed the matter for hearing.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Act raised layered constitutional questions about the limits of parliamentary power to regulate religious endowments without violating the protection of religious autonomy under Articles 25 and 26 and the special protection for religious minorities under Articles 29 and 30. It also engaged Article 14's equality guarantee, with petitioners arguing that the regulatory framework imposed on waqf is distinctively more onerous than that applicable to non-Muslim religious endowments. The question of whether Parliament's power to regulate waqf property — a species of religious endowment — under entry 10 of the Concurrent List extends to altering the essential structure of Muslim charitable governance is at the heart of the constitutional challenge.

## Its legacy

The Waqf Amendment Act is the most significant constitutional challenge involving minority religious institutions since P.A. Inamdar and T.M.A. Pai, and the judicial proceedings on its validity will test the outer boundaries of what legislative reform of religious endowments is constitutionally permissible. The litigation will also develop the doctrine on when regulatory differences between religious communities constitute invidious discrimination under Article 14 as opposed to legitimate differential treatment reflecting different regulatory contexts.
