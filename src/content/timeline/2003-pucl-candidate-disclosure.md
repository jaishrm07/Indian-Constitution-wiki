PUCL is still the central disclosure case whenever electoral transparency is discussed as part of the right to know.

## What happened

After the Supreme Court's ruling in Association for Democratic Reforms in 2002 directed candidates to disclose criminal antecedents, assets, and educational background, Parliament amended the Representation of the People Act to limit the scope of those disclosures. The People's Union for Civil Liberties challenged these amendments. A Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court decided the matter in March 2003, striking down the parliamentary amendments as unconstitutional to the extent they sought to dilute or curtail the voter's right to know. The Court reaffirmed and strengthened the disclosure requirement, confirming that a voter's right to be informed about candidates standing for election is a fundamental constitutional right, not merely a statutory convenience that Parliament could freely modify.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

PUCL is significant because it placed the disclosure requirement beyond ordinary legislative retreat. By holding that the constitutional right to information about candidates could not be abridged by Parliament through ordinary legislation, the Court used Articles 19(1)(a) and 324 to constrain legislative autonomy in the electoral field. The ruling demonstrated that constitutional courts could hold the line on reforms enacted through judicial direction even when the legislature sought to roll them back. It also reinforced the broader principle that the right to free and fair elections, including the information necessary to exercise that choice meaningfully, has a constitutional foundation that statute cannot displace.

## Its legacy

PUCL and Association for Democratic Reforms together form the constitutional basis for the candidate affidavit system that operates in all Indian elections today. The disclosures required under court direction and subsequent Election Commission practice have made candidate backgrounds part of the public record before every election, enabling civil society scrutiny of criminal antecedents and wealth declarations. Subsequent litigation around false declarations in affidavits, and around the NOTA option, has built on the constitutional premise that these cases established — that elections must enable genuinely informed choice.
