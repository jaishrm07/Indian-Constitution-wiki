The Eighty-Eighth Amendment addressed one of the structural gaps in India's fiscal federalism: the Constitution's original scheme had not allocated service tax clearly between the Union and the States at a time when the service sector was small. By the early 2000s, services constituted a large and growing share of GDP, making the question of who could levy and collect service tax constitutionally significant.

## What happened

Service tax was first introduced in India by the Finance Act, 1994, initially covering three services: telephone, non-life insurance, and stockbroking. The constitutional basis for the levy was contested from the outset: the Union relied on residuary power under Entry 97 of the Union List (List I), since "services" did not appear as a distinct subject in either the Union List or the Concurrent List (List III), and the States had no independent power to levy service tax. As the service sector grew and the Union progressively expanded the list of taxable services, the question of revenue sharing between the Union and the States became pressing, because service tax was then a wholly Union revenue collected under Article 270's framework only to the extent Parliament included it in the divisible pool.

The Eighty-Eighth Amendment, which received presidential assent on 15 January 2003, sought to resolve this by inserting Article 268A, which provided that taxes on services would be levied by the Union Government but would be collected by both the Union and the States in accordance with such principles of collection as Parliament might by law formulate. It also amended the Constitution by inserting Entry 92C in List I of the Seventh Schedule. This gave service tax an explicit constitutional anchor and opened the door for States to share in its collection, addressing a federalism objection that had gained force as service tax revenue increased sharply.

## Constitutional significance

Article 268A was conceived as part of the fiscal federalism architecture under Part XII of the Constitution. Articles 268 to 270 distribute the levy, collection, and allocation of various taxes between the Union and the States, with Article 270 governing the divisible pool of taxes whose net proceeds are distributed between the Union and the States as per Finance Commission recommendations. By creating a specific article for service tax rather than relying on residuary power, the amendment gave the levy clearer constitutional standing and potentially brought it within the ambit of the Finance Commission's consideration for revenue-sharing purposes.

However, the amendment's practical implementation was incomplete: Parliament never enacted the enabling legislation contemplating the joint collection mechanism under Article 268A, and service tax continued to be administered as a central levy. The amendment thus functioned more as a constitutional signpost for eventual reform than as an immediately operative fiscal instrument.

## Long-term impact

The Eighty-Eighth Amendment was superseded by the Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act, 2016, which implemented the Goods and Services Tax. The GST reform involved a comprehensive restructuring of the Seventh Schedule and the introduction of Article 246A, which gives both Parliament and State Legislatures concurrent power to make laws on the supply of goods and services. Article 268A and Entry 92C were deleted by the One Hundred and First Amendment, along with several other entries that had been absorbed into the GST framework. The Eighty-Eighth Amendment is therefore constitutionally significant less for its lasting textual legacy than as an early formal recognition that India's federal fiscal structure required adjustment to accommodate a services-dominated economy — a recognition that ultimately produced the most significant restructuring of India's fiscal federalism since 1950.
