The Eighty-Sixth Amendment is one of the most significant expansions of fundamental rights since the original Constitution. It moved education for the six-to-fourteen age group from the aspirational register of Part IV — where it had languished for fifty years — into the enforceable regime of Part III, creating a constitutional entitlement that would later be given legislative flesh by the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009.

## What happened

Article 45, as originally enacted in the Constitution, directed the State to endeavour to provide free and compulsory education for all children until they completed the age of fourteen within ten years of the Constitution's commencement. This directive was not met within the stipulated ten years, or in the five decades that followed. In 1993, the Supreme Court in Unni Krishnan J.P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh held that the right to education was implicit in the right to life under Article 21, but limited that reading to education up to age fourteen and emphasised its conditionality on state capacity. After sustained advocacy by civil society and following the recommendations of multiple committees, Parliament decided to give the right explicit constitutional status. The Constitution (Eighty-Sixth Amendment) Act received presidential assent on 12 December 2002.

The Amendment made three interlocking changes. First, it inserted Article 21A, which reads: "The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State may, by law, determine." Second, it substituted a new text for Article 45, restricting the directive to early childhood care and education for children below six years — the age group excluded from Article 21A. Third, it inserted Article 51A(k) as a new fundamental duty, requiring every citizen who is a parent or guardian to provide opportunities for education to his child or ward between the ages of six and fourteen years.

## Constitutional significance

Article 21A is significant as a textual fundamental right rather than as a judicial implication under Article 21, because its express inclusion gives Parliament and the courts a clear constitutional mandate for implementing legislation. The phrase "in such manner as the State may, by law, determine" preserved legislative space for the details of implementation — which school, what standards, how funded — while establishing the entitlement itself as non-negotiable. This formulation meant that the right would require a parliamentary statute to become fully operational, which came in the form of the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (the RTE Act), which came into force on 1 April 2010.

The amendment also restructured the relationship between Articles 21A and 45. The original Article 45 had covered the same six-to-fourteen age group and was now given over to younger children, whose care and education remains a directive principle. This restructuring reflected the constitutional choice that the right of six-to-fourteen-year-olds was mature enough for justiciability, while the State's obligations to children under six remained aspirational.

## Long-term impact

The Eighty-Sixth Amendment triggered extensive litigation about what Article 21A requires. The most significant challenge came in Society for Unaided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012), where the Supreme Court upheld the RTE Act's requirement that private unaided schools reserve 25 per cent of their entry-level seats for children from economically weaker sections and disadvantaged groups. The Court held that this was a reasonable restriction on the right to carry on an occupation under Article 19(1)(g). The amendment also brought minority educational institutions into constitutional tension with Article 21A: in the same case, the Court partially exempted minority institutions from the RTE Act's application, holding that such imposition would infringe Article 30. The PA Inamdar line of cases had already made this boundary a live constitutional issue, and Article 21A deepened the complexity by adding a children's rights dimension to the minority institutions debate.
