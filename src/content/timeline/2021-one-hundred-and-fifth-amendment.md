This amendment rebalanced the federal side of reservation governance. It is a good example of Parliament correcting a constitutional reading that had shifted too far toward the Union.

## What happened

After the Supreme Court, in proceedings related to the Maratha reservation controversy, held that the 102nd Amendment had transferred the exclusive power to identify and notify socially and educationally backward classes to the Union, Parliament enacted the One Hundred and Fifth Amendment in August 2021 to reverse that reading. The amendment clarified the text of Article 342A to expressly preserve the power of state governments and Union Territories to maintain their own lists of socially and educationally backward classes for the purposes of state and centrally sponsored reservations. It was passed with bipartisan support, reflecting a broad parliamentary consensus that states must retain this function.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The amendment was a direct legislative response to a judicial interpretation that had the effect of significantly centralising an area of social policy that had previously been a concurrent domain in practice. It illustrated that Parliament can use the amendment power to restore constitutional understandings that a court's reading has shifted, provided the correction does not itself conflict with the basic structure. The amendment also confirmed that the identification of backward classes for reservation purposes is a politically and constitutionally sensitive function that the federal structure assigns differently depending on whether the reservation is for Union or state purposes.

## Its legacy

The 105th Amendment resolved the immediate legal uncertainty, allowing states to continue notifying their own OBC lists without depending on the Central List process under Article 342A. It reinforced the principle that cooperative federalism in the domain of social justice policy requires preserving meaningful state autonomy, and it was subsequently invoked in litigation and academic commentary as an example of Parliament acting quickly to protect federal balance when judicial interpretation threatens to undermine it.
