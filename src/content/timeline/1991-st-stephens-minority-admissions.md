The case became a baseline for later disputes over minority education, admissions policy, and the boundary between regulation and control.

## What happened

St. Stephen's College, a Christian minority institution affiliated with the University of Delhi, maintained an admissions policy that gave preference to Christian applicants over non-Christian ones. The college applied this preference even for centrally funded seats, leading to a dispute with the University over whether the institution could depart from the common merit list. A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court decided the matter in 1991, holding that minority institutions have the right under Article 30 to prefer candidates from their own community, but that this preference cannot extend so far as to exclude merit entirely or to discriminate in a manner that undermines national interest.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment attempted to set workable limits on the right of minority institutions under Article 30(1) to administer their affairs in the matter of admissions. The Court held that the State may regulate minority educational institutions to ensure standards and fairness, but that regulation cannot become a device to negate the minority character of the institution. The 50 per cent guideline that emerged from the case — allowing minority institutions to reserve up to half their seats for their own community even in State-aided colleges — became a reference benchmark, though it remained contested in subsequent cases.

## Its legacy

St. Stephen's established that minority institutions are not exempt from all state oversight simply because of their character under Article 30, but that oversight must stop short of destroying institutional autonomy. The decision shaped the next decade of minority education litigation, directly influencing how courts approached T.M.A. Pai Foundation, P.A. Inamdar, and the later rounds of debate over the Right to Education Act. It remains the starting point for any analysis of the admissions autonomy of aided minority institutions.
