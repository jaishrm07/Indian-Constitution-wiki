M. Nagaraj is the high-water mark of the Supreme Court's attempt to impose judicially manageable conditions on reservation-in-promotions schemes while simultaneously upholding Parliament's constituent power to insert those powers into the Constitution. The conditions the Court laid down — quantifiable data on backwardness, inadequate representation, and administrative efficiency — generated a decade of satellite litigation before being partially displaced in Jarnail Singh (2018).

## What happened

The background to M. Nagaraj lies in the Supreme Court's 1992 ruling in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India, where the nine-judge bench held that reservation under Article 16(4) could not extend to promotions. Parliament responded with a sequence of constitutional amendments. The Seventy-Seventh Amendment (1995) inserted Article 16(4A) to permit reservation in promotions for SCs and STs. The Eighty-First Amendment (2000) inserted Article 16(4B) to permit the carry-forward of unfilled reserved promotion vacancies across years without such carry-forward being counted against the 50 per cent ceiling. The Eighty-Second Amendment (2000) restored to the proviso to Article 335 the power to relax qualifying marks for SCs and STs in promotions. The Eighty-Fifth Amendment (2001) amended Article 16(4A) to add consequential seniority for SC/ST promotees, reversing the Supreme Court's decision in Ajit Singh (No. 2) (1999).

Employees from the general category and OBC employees challenged all these amendments as violating the basic structure of the Constitution, arguing that they destroyed the right to equality under Article 16(1), the principle of merit in public services, and the efficiency principle under Article 335. A five-judge bench, including the Chief Justice, decided the matter on 19 October 2006.

The Court unanimously upheld all four amendments. It held that Articles 16(4A), 16(4B), and the amended Article 335 proviso were not unconstitutional per se, because they did not abrogate the right to equality — they were facets of equality enabling the State to correct historic imbalances in representation. The amendments did not violate the basic structure. However, the Court held that the State could not mechanically invoke these provisions: it must first gather quantifiable data to establish three conditions — that the community in question is backward, that it is inadequately represented in the posts in question, and that granting reservation will not adversely affect the overall efficiency of administration under Article 335.

## Constitutional significance

M. Nagaraj operates at the intersection of constitutional amendment power and the basic structure doctrine. By upholding the amendments while imposing conditions on their exercise, the Court threaded a needle: Parliament had valid constituent power to insert Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B), but those provisions were not self-executing rights — they were enabling powers whose exercise required the State to satisfy substantive and empirical conditions. The Court thus treated the basic structure not as a fixed list of forbidden changes but as a set of substantive requirements that constrain how constitutional powers may be used, not just what the text may say.

The "compelling necessity" test — or the three-condition requirement of backwardness, inadequate representation, and administrative efficiency — was a significant judicial innovation because it imported into the constitutional law of promotions the same kind of proportionality analysis that courts in other jurisdictions apply to affirmative action schemes, while grounding it specifically in Indian constitutional text through Article 335.

## Long-term impact

The M. Nagaraj conditions generated extensive litigation in the years following the decision, as states attempted to introduce reservation-in-promotion schemes and were repeatedly required to demonstrate the quantifiable data the Court had demanded. The Supreme Court revisited M. Nagaraj in Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018), where a five-judge bench modified the framework by holding that States are not required to collect quantifiable data to demonstrate the backwardness of SCs and STs specifically — because SCs and STs are, by the constitutional categorisation itself, presumed to be backward — but that the requirements of inadequate representation and efficiency remain. The Jarnail Singh court expressly declined to refer M. Nagaraj to a larger bench, preserving its core holding on administrative efficiency while removing the most litigated requirement. The overarching constitutional question of whether reservation in promotions is consistent with the basic structure, answered affirmatively in M. Nagaraj, was reaffirmed and has not been disturbed.
