The case marked a major shift in reservation doctrine by validating an amendment-backed affirmative-action design built around economic disadvantage.

## What happened

Janhit Abhiyan and several other petitioners challenged the One Hundred and Third Amendment before a five-judge Constitution Bench, arguing that the EWS reservation violated the basic structure of the Constitution, particularly the equality code and the principle that caste-based social backwardness — not mere economic criterion — is the constitutionally legitimate basis for affirmative action. The bench, deciding the case in November 2022, upheld the amendment by a majority of three to two. The majority held that the amendment was a permissible exercise of constituent power and did not damage the equality code because it used a different criterion for a different target group. The minority dissents, written by Justices Ravindra Bhat and S. Ravindra Bhat, held that exclusion of Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and OBCs from EWS benefits amounts to treating the already disadvantaged as inherently less worthy of upliftment, which struck at the heart of the equality code.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment departed from the understanding in Indra Sawhney that economic criterion alone cannot ground reservation under the Constitution, which the majority read as having been superseded by the new constitutional text of Articles 15(6) and 16(6). The case also addressed whether the fifty-percent ceiling articulated in Indra Sawhney is a constitutional absolute — the majority held it is not an inviolable rule but a general caution, while the minority disagreed. The result produced a doctrinal division in the Court on whether the basic structure constrains the scope of permissible reservation models.

## Its legacy

Janhit Abhiyan substantially expanded the constitutional space for affirmative action policies based on economic disadvantage, paving the way for states to consider EWS-linked schemes across sectors. The dissenting opinions became important scholarly resources for the argument that the equality code limits not only Parliament's ordinary legislative power but also its constituent power when the basic structure is implicated by an exclusionary design within a reservation framework.
