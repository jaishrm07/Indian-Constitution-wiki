The First Judges Case is the baseline from which all subsequent judicial appointments jurisprudence departs. Its holding in favour of executive primacy, and the constitutional reasoning that supported it, shaped a decade of friction between the executive and the judiciary before being overruled in 1993.

## What happened

S.P. Gupta v. Union of India arose from a batch of writ petitions that included challenges to the non-renewal of the additional judgeship of a High Court judge whose continued appointment the Chief Justice of India had recommended but the government had declined to act upon. The petitions were argued before a seven-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, which delivered its judgment on 30 December 1981. The case raised fundamental questions about the appointment and transfer of judges under Articles 124 and 217 and about the meaning of the constitutional requirement that the President shall "consult" the Chief Justice of India before making appointments to the Supreme Court and High Courts.

The majority held that "consultation" in Articles 124(2) and 217(1) did not mean "concurrence." The constitutional obligation on the President was to consult, meaning to seek and consider the Chief Justice's opinion, but the President — and by convention the Cabinet whose advice the President acts upon — was not bound to follow that opinion. If the government disagreed with the Chief Justice's recommendation, it was free to override it. The majority reached this conclusion by a textual argument (the Constitution used "consultation" in some places and "concurrence" in others, and the difference was intentional), a structural argument (placing appointment power primarily in the judiciary would be inconsistent with democratic accountability), and a historical argument (the framers had considered and rejected a model of judicial control over appointments).

The seven-judge bench was not unanimous. Justice P.N. Bhagwati, writing the principal opinion, also opened important questions about judicial accountability and public interest litigation in his judgment, developing concepts that would have long influence beyond the appointments question itself. Several judges wrote separately, and the dissenters or concurrers offered different readings of the consultation requirement that would later provide intellectual material for the Second Judges Case's overruling.

## Constitutional significance

The First Judges Case gave the executive a formally unchecked power over the composition of the higher judiciary. Because the President acts on Cabinet advice under Article 74, the practical effect was that the ruling government of the day could decline to appoint any judge recommended by the Chief Justice, could determine which additional judges would be made permanent, and could transfer judges between High Courts with the Chief Justice's opinion being a factor but not a constraint. This raised acute concerns about judicial independence: a government facing adverse judgments could, in principle, use its appointments and transfer power to shape judicial composition in its favour.

The case also raised the constitutional question of whether "consultation" is a formal or a substantive requirement. The majority's holding that it was formal — requiring the opinion to be sought and considered but not followed — sat uneasily with the constitutional principle that the judiciary must be insulated from political control, particularly since the Constitution had placed appointments outside ordinary legislative or executive power by requiring consultation with the Chief Justice at all. The dissenting and concurrent opinions preserved the view that consultation in this context should mean something closer to determinative weight on the Chief Justice's view, laying groundwork for later reconsideration.

## Long-term impact

The First Judges Case remained the governing law on judicial appointments for over a decade. During that period, significant tensions arose between successive governments and the Chief Justices over appointments to and transfers within the High Courts, with several controversies about judges being transferred to distant courts in apparent retaliation for judgments adverse to the government. These controversies accumulated into the pressure that produced the Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association case — the Second Judges Case — decided in October 1993 by a nine-judge bench that expressly overruled the First Judges Case.

In the Second Judges Case, the Court held that judicial independence required the Chief Justice's opinion — arrived at collegially — to be given primacy, not merely consideration, in appointments. "Consultation" was reread to mean "concurrence" in constitutional practice, inverting the First Judges Case holding. The Third Judges Case of 1998 further refined the collegium mechanism. The First Judges Case thus operates as the historical counterpoint that justifies the collegium: its reasoning is the cautionary example of what happens when appointment power rests unrestrained in the executive. The NJAC case of 2015, in striking down the constitutional amendment that would have given the executive a voice in a judicial appointments commission, again invoked the First Judges Case framework as the baseline that judicial independence principles had corrected.

Justice Bhagwati's expansive observations in the First Judges Case about the right of citizens to access justice and about public interest litigation have had a separate and enormous influence on Indian constitutional law, independently of the appointments holding that was overruled. In that sense the judgment is one of the rare cases that has had more long-term effect through its subsidiary reasoning than through its principal holding.
