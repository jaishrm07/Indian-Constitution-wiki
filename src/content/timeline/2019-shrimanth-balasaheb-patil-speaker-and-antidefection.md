The decision matters because it sits at the point where resignation strategy, Speaker control, and the Tenth Schedule can change legislative outcomes.

## What happened

In the context of contested political developments in Karnataka, a large number of MLAs submitted resignations to the Speaker, triggering parallel questions about the validity of those resignations and pending disqualification petitions filed under the Tenth Schedule. The Speaker declined to accept several resignations and simultaneously took up the disqualification proceedings, a sequencing that had significant implications for legislative arithmetic and trust votes. The Supreme Court, examining the Speaker's handling of both sets of proceedings, held that the Speaker must accept a voluntary and genuine resignation in accordance with Article 190(3)(b) and cannot hold the resignation process hostage to parallel disqualification proceedings.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment sharpened the constitutional relationship between the right of a member to resign from the House and the Speaker's power to disqualify under the Tenth Schedule, clarifying that these are distinct constitutional processes with different legal effects. It reinforced that the Speaker, when exercising Tenth Schedule jurisdiction, acts as a quasi-judicial authority and cannot use that jurisdiction to delay or defeat the separate exercise of a member's right to resign. The case also reaffirmed the Supreme Court's supervisory role over Speaker proceedings in defection matters, building on the framework established in Kihoto Hollohan.

## Its legacy

The decision became a key reference point in later defection and floor-crossing crises — including those in Maharashtra and Manipur — where the sequencing of resignations, disqualification petitions, and trust votes again became the central tactical and constitutional battleground. It prompted renewed calls for reforming the Tenth Schedule to remove the Speaker from adjudicating defection cases and substitute an independent tribunal.
