## What happened

The Constitution had, as part of the settlement under which former princely states acceded to India, guaranteed privy purses to their rulers under Article 291 and recognised their personal rights, privileges, dignities, and titles under Article 362. In 1970, President V.V. Giri issued an order derecognising all rulers, which the Supreme Court in Madhavrao Scindia v. Union of India struck down on the ground that derecognition required a formal constitutional amendment, not a presidential order.

Parliament then passed the Constitution (Twenty-Sixth Amendment) Act 1971 after the Congress party's general election landslide, fought in part on the slogan of abolishing privy purses. The amendment omitted Articles 291 and 362 and inserted Article 363A, which provided that no ruler shall be entitled to any privy purse, recognition, or privileges from the government of India.

## Constitutional significance

The episode illustrated that the Supreme Court's procedural gatekeeping — requiring amendment rather than executive order — did not protect the substantive entitlement once Parliament chose to use its amending power. The sequence demonstrated how formal constitutional rights, even those written in as part of specific accession settlements, could be extinguished through the amendment process. The amendment was also enacted shortly after the Twenty-Fourth Amendment had restored Parliament's amending power following Golaknath, demonstrating how quickly and consequentially that restored power could be deployed.

The amendment removed the last formal vestige of the colonial-era treaty arrangements through which the princely territories had been integrated into the republic, completing a transformation that had begun with the accession instruments in 1947 and 1948.

## Long-term impact

The abolition of privy purses completed the legal consolidation of the princely states into a constitutionally uniform India. The episode is frequently cited in constitutional debates about whether Parliament's amending power can extinguish rights or commitments written into the Constitution as part of specific political settlements — a question that resurfaced most acutely in the context of the abrogation of Article 370's special status for Jammu and Kashmir in 2019. In that later context, the Twenty-Sixth Amendment's precedent — Parliament can amend away constitutionally entrenched commitments made as part of territorial integration — was invoked in support of the abrogation's constitutional validity.
