## What happened

The Constitution (Ninety-Ninth Amendment) Bill was introduced in the Rajya Sabha in August 2014, passed by both Houses of Parliament in a matter of days, and subsequently ratified by more than half of the state legislatures. On receiving Presidential assent on 31 December 2014, it inserted Articles 124A, 124B, and 124C into the Constitution and came into force on 13 April 2015. The amendment established the National Judicial Appointments Commission as the body responsible for recommending appointments to the offices of the Chief Justice of India and other judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts, replacing the collegium system that had developed through the Second and Third Judges cases of 1993 and 1998. The NJAC Act, passed alongside the amendment, gave the Commission its operational structure: a six-member body comprising the Chief Justice, the two senior-most puisne judges of the Supreme Court, the Union Law Minister, and two eminent persons selected by a committee of the Prime Minister, the Chief Justice, and the Leader of Opposition. Any two members could block an appointment, giving the non-judicial members an effective veto. The amendment represented the most significant legislative challenge to judicial primacy in appointments since the collegium doctrine was established.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Ninety-Ninth Amendment used the amending power in Article 368 to modify the appointments procedure in Article 124, which had previously been interpreted as vesting final recommendation authority in the collegium of senior judges. The amendment acknowledged that Article 368 empowers Parliament to alter constitutional provisions dealing with the judiciary but tested whether this power extended to restructuring the body responsible for judicial appointments in ways that reduced judicial primacy. The inclusion of the Law Minister and eminent persons on the Commission raised constitutional questions about whether executive and lay participation in the appointments process violated the basic structure doctrine as articulated in Kesavananda Bharati, particularly its components of judicial independence and the separation of powers. Parliament's position was that the collegium system had no explicit constitutional text supporting it and that restoring legislative and executive participation in appointments was a legitimate exercise of constituent power. The speed with which the amendment was passed — with near-unanimous support across party lines — underscored the extent of dissatisfaction with the opacity and accountability of the collegium in both the legislature and among the public.

## Its aftermath

The Ninety-Ninth Amendment was challenged almost immediately after coming into force. In October 2015, a five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India, struck down both the amendment and the NJAC Act by a four-to-one majority. The Court held that judicial independence in appointments was part of the basic structure and that the NJAC's composition — in particular the veto available to non-judicial members — would compromise it. The lone dissenter held that the amendment should have been upheld and reforms to the collegium addressed separately. The episode remains the most direct confrontation between Parliament and the Supreme Court over the constitutional design of judicial appointments, and the collegium system that the amendment sought to replace continues to operate, now under heightened scrutiny from both the executive and the public.
