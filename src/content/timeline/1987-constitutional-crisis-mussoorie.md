## What happened

By the mid-1980s, India's constitutional framework for Centre-State relations was under severe strain. Article 356, which empowered the President to dismiss a State government and impose Central rule on a report that constitutional machinery in the State had broken down, had been used over eighty times since 1950 — far more frequently against opposition-governed States than against Congress-governed ones. Regional parties in Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Jammu and Kashmir, and Punjab had documented instances of Central interference in State affairs through partisan Governors, manipulated floor tests, and pre-emptive dismissals of elected governments. The dismissal of Farooq Abdullah's National Conference government in 1984 — widely condemned as constitutionally unjustified — had made Centre-State relations a live political and constitutional issue.

The Sarkaria Commission, appointed in June 1983 under Justice Ranjit Singh Sarkaria, conducted extensive consultations across 1984 to 1987, visiting every State and Union Territory, receiving thousands of memoranda, and examining witnesses from State governments, political parties, trade unions, and civil society. By early 1987, the Commission had completed its consultations and was consolidating findings. It submitted its final report, running to over eighteen hundred pages in two volumes, to the Union Government in January 1988. The Commission made 247 specific recommendations covering every major dimension of Centre-State relations — legislative competences under Schedule VII, financial and fiscal relations, the role of the Governor, emergency provisions, deployment of Central forces in States, and the Inter-State Council.

The Commission's most consequential constitutional recommendations addressed Article 356. It held that the provision had been systematically misused for political rather than constitutional purposes, and recommended that the Centre must attempt to resolve any breakdown of constitutional machinery through alternative means before invoking Article 356; that the Governor's report must be objective and not merely the product of Central political direction; that the State government must be given an opportunity to respond before dismissal; and that the State Assembly should not be dissolved before parliamentary approval of the proclamation. The Commission also recommended that the Inter-State Council under Article 263, which had been dormant since its constitutional inception, should be revived as an active deliberative forum for cooperative federalism.

## Constitutional significance

The Sarkaria Commission's work was significant not primarily as a piece of legal reasoning but as a sustained constitutional audit of the gap between the text of the Constitution's federalism provisions and their actual operation. Its method — empirical inquiry across all States, consultation with all political actors, and recommendations grounded in constitutional text and convention — gave its findings an authority that went beyond what any government White Paper or parliamentary committee could have achieved.

The Commission drew a principled distinction between the constitutional text of Article 356 — which on its face gave wide discretion to the Centre — and the constitutional conventions that should govern its exercise. It argued that the Governor's role, while formally advisory to the President, was constitutionally intended to be impartial and objective rather than an extension of Central political will; that the emergency provision was meant for genuine constitutional crises and not for the removal of politically inconvenient State governments; and that the cooperative federal spirit of the Constitution required the Centre to exhaust all remedial options before resorting to dismissal. These were constitutional arguments about how text should be read in light of the Constitution's overall structure and fundamental commitments — including federalism, parliamentary democracy, and representative government at the State level.

The Commission's recommendation that the Inter-State Council be constituted and made operative was implemented in 1990, when Prime Minister V.P. Singh established the Council under Article 263. The Council has met periodically since, though its practical influence on Centre-State disputes has been limited by the absence of binding decision-making power and by the political context in which it has operated.

## Long-term impact

The Sarkaria Commission's most significant constitutional legacy was its intellectual influence on SR Bommai v. Union of India (1994), the Supreme Court's landmark nine-judge bench decision that imposed constitutional discipline on Article 356. In Bommai, the Court drew on the same federal constitutional principles that the Commission had articulated — federalism as a basic feature of the Constitution, the justiciability of Article 356 proclamations, the requirement of a floor test before dismissal — and converted them from advisory recommendations into binding constitutional law. While the Court's reasoning in Bommai was independent and derived from the basic structure doctrine and prior constitutional jurisprudence, the Sarkaria Commission had prepared the conceptual ground and given the arguments systematic expression.

The Commission also established a template for constitutional review of Centre-State relations that was followed by the Punchhi Commission in 2007-2010, which updated the Sarkaria recommendations in light of developments over the intervening two decades. Both commissions together constitute the most comprehensive non-judicial examination of Indian constitutional federalism in practice. Their recommendations, though not legally binding, have shaped how political actors, courts, and constitutional scholars understand the obligations that flow from the Constitution's federal design — obligations that exist in the interstices between formally discretionary constitutional powers and the democratic and representative commitments that give those powers their legitimate purpose.
