## What happened

Shayara Bano's husband dissolved their marriage by pronouncing talaq three times in a single utterance — a form of Islamic divorce known as talaq-e-biddat or instantaneous triple talaq — which Muslim personal law as applied by courts had treated as valid and irrevocable. Shayara Bano petitioned the Supreme Court arguing the practice violated her fundamental rights. A five-judge Constitution Bench comprising judges from five different religious communities delivered a fragmented verdict in August 2017. Three of the five judges held the practice unconstitutional, though by differing routes: two held it was manifestly arbitrary and violated Article 14; a third held it was un-Islamic and therefore not protected under Article 25. The remaining two judges, in dissent, would have referred the matter to Parliament rather than strike it down. Following the judgment, Parliament enacted the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019, which criminalised the utterance of instantaneous triple talaq as a cognisable, non-bailable offence.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment raised foundational questions about the intersection of personal law, constitutional rights, and religious freedom. The majority's application of Article 14's arbitrariness standard to a personal law practice — rather than to state legislation — was significant: it confirmed that personal law practices applied through courts are subject to constitutional scrutiny. The case also tested the limits of Article 25's protection for religious practice, with the plurality finding that a practice condemned within the religion's own textual tradition cannot claim constitutional shelter as an essential religious practice. The subsequent criminalisation by the 2019 Act added a further constitutional dimension, with critics questioning whether a penal response to a civil matrimonial act was proportionate.

## Its constitutional legacy

The triple talaq case became a landmark in the long-running constitutional debate over a uniform civil code under Article 44. It demonstrated that the Supreme Court would apply Part III guarantees to personal law without waiting for codifying legislation, accelerating pressure for reform. The 2019 Act's criminal provisions remained contested, with opponents arguing that jailing a husband dissolves neither the marriage nor the constitutional tension between gender equality and religious autonomy that the judgment brought to the surface rather than resolved. The case is now regularly cited in discussions of constitutional morality, the essential practices doctrine, and the scope of gender equality under Articles 14 and 15.
