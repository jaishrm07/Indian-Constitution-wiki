## What happened

The Administrative Tribunals Act 1985 was enacted under Article 323A, which had been inserted into the Constitution by the Forty-Second Amendment in 1976. Article 323A empowered Parliament to create administrative tribunals for adjudication of service disputes involving public servants, and it included a provision — clause (2)(d) — enabling Parliament to exclude the jurisdiction of all courts, including the Supreme Court and High Courts, over such disputes, except for the Supreme Court's jurisdiction under Article 136. The Act accordingly established the Central Administrative Tribunal (CAT) and State Administrative Tribunals, and barred the jurisdiction of High Courts to hear service matters.

Article 323B, also inserted by the Forty-Second Amendment, permitted similar exclusion clauses for tribunals dealing with other specified subjects — including taxation, foreign exchange, company law, elections, and land reform. Various statutes created tribunals under this authority with similar jurisdiction-ousting clauses.

Employees and litigants aggrieved by CAT orders challenged the exclusion of High Court jurisdiction before the High Courts themselves and before the Supreme Court. The fundamental question — whether Parliament could, through an ordinary statute or through Article 323A and 323B clauses, take away the High Courts' power of judicial review under Articles 226 and 227 — had divided courts for over a decade. A seven-judge Constitution Bench was constituted to resolve the issue definitively.

The Bench, in its judgment delivered on 18 March 1997, held unanimously that the provisions in Articles 323A and 323B enabling exclusion of High Court jurisdiction to that extent were unconstitutional. Tribunals established under those articles could not be made the final repositories of judicial power, excluding High Court review entirely.

## Constitutional significance

The Court's reasoning was grounded squarely in the basic structure doctrine. Tracing the line from Kesavananda Bharati through Minerva Mills, the Bench held that the power of the superior courts — both the Supreme Court under Article 32 and the High Courts under Articles 226 and 227 — to review executive and legislative action for conformity with constitutional requirements is itself a feature of the basic structure. It is not merely a statutory or even an ordinary constitutional provision; it is an architectural element of the Constitution that no amendment procedure can destroy.

The Court drew a distinction between curtailing the original jurisdiction of courts in particular categories of dispute — which Parliament may do — and entirely ousting the supervisory and certiorari jurisdiction of the High Courts. The former involves routing adjudication through a specialist body better equipped to handle technical matters; the latter removes the constitutional safety valve through which unlawful executive action is checked. The distinction was crucial because the Forty-Second Amendment's exclusion clauses went to the second category: they attempted to create islands of executive and tribunal power immune from High Court scrutiny.

The Bench held, however, that tribunals could and should continue to function as effective first-tier adjudicative bodies. Their decisions remained subject to challenge before the Division Bench of the High Court exercising jurisdiction over the area from which the dispute arose, under Articles 226 and 227. Only after exhausting this remedy could parties approach the Supreme Court under Article 136. This restored a two-tier structure of judicial oversight while preserving the specialist expertise that the tribunal system was designed to provide.

## Long-term impact

L. Chandra Kumar restructured the entire Indian tribunal architecture. Every statutory tribunal created under Articles 323A and 323B — the Central Administrative Tribunal, State Administrative Tribunals, the Income Tax Appellate Tribunal, the Securities Appellate Tribunal, the National Green Tribunal, the National Company Law Tribunal, the Armed Forces Tribunal, and others — became subject to High Court oversight, with their decisions challengeable before the jurisdictionally competent High Court. The decision ended the period of legal uncertainty during which some litigants could bypass High Courts entirely by proceeding to the Supreme Court directly from tribunal orders.

The judgment also clarified that while Parliament may vest adjudicatory functions in tribunals — and may provide that only persons with judicial experience or technical expertise may serve on them — it cannot constitutionally confer on tribunals the status of final courts of record replacing the High Courts. This principle has been applied and extended in a series of later cases. In Union of India v. R. Gandhi (2010), the Supreme Court held that the National Company Law Tribunal established under the Companies Act could not entirely displace the High Courts' jurisdiction over company matters, applying L. Chandra Kumar's reasoning in a commercial law context.

The decision is also significant for its role in the debate over judicial independence and tribunal independence. The Court held that tribunal members must have security of tenure and conditions of service ensuring their independence from the executive, because a tribunal exercising judicial functions while remaining subservient to the executive for its continuance would not deliver justice. This reasoning was later extended in subsequent cases examining the composition, appointment process, and service conditions of various tribunals, leading to a series of Supreme Court interventions requiring Parliament to restructure tribunal appointment mechanisms.
