## What happened

The Constitution (Sixty-First Amendment) Act 1988 was passed by Parliament and received Presidential assent on 28 March 1988. It amended Article 326 of the Constitution — the provision governing elections to the House of the People and to the legislative assemblies of States — by substituting the qualifying age of twenty-one years with eighteen years. The amendment came into force in time for its provisions to be given effect through the Representation of the People Act, with revised electoral rolls prepared for the ninth general elections held in November 1989.

The impetus for the change had been building for several years. Rajiv Gandhi's government, which had come to power in 1984 with a massive mandate, positioned itself as oriented toward India's younger population. Advocates of the change argued that eighteen-to-twenty-one year olds were subject to the full obligations of citizenship — liability under criminal law, eligibility for military service, contractual capacity in many areas — yet were denied a voice in electing the governments that exercised authority over them. International comparison also played a role: by the late 1980s, most established democracies had already moved to an eighteen-year voting age, and the Twenty-Sixth Amendment to the United States Constitution, adopted in 1971, had provided a high-profile precedent.

The amendment passed without significant political opposition. The Representation of the People Act 1950 and the Representation of the People Act 1951 were amended in consequence to update electoral roll registration procedures and to permit the enrolment of voters down to the age of eighteen.

## Constitutional significance

Article 326 is the constitutional foundation of universal adult suffrage in India, and its text from 1950 onwards had already embodied a strong commitment to the broadest possible franchise. The original provision disqualified prospective voters only on grounds of non-residence, unsound mind, crime, and corrupt or illegal practice — not on grounds of property, education, or literacy. Reducing the qualifying age to eighteen was therefore consistent with Article 326's underlying constitutional logic: the franchise should be as inclusive as possible, and exclusions from it should require substantial justification.

The amendment also engaged with a structural tension in the constitutional scheme between civic rights and civic obligations. The Constitution confers rights and imposes obligations on citizens — and on persons generally — from birth. Specific constitutional and legal obligations, however, attach at particular ages: criminal responsibility attaches at different points under the Juvenile Justice Act; contractual capacity under the Indian Majority Act attaches at eighteen; military recruitment is available from seventeen. The decision to align voting eligibility with the majority threshold of eighteen years gave constitutional expression to the principle that those who bear adult obligations of citizenship should also enjoy the adult right of political participation.

## Long-term impact

The Sixty-First Amendment's most immediate impact was quantitative: approximately fifty million new voters became eligible to vote in the 1989 general elections, representing a significant expansion of the electorate. Given that India has one of the youngest populations of any major democracy — with a substantial proportion of the population under thirty — the sustained effect of the amendment has been to make the youth demographic politically significant in a way that has only grown over the decades since.

The amendment also left unresolved a structural asymmetry that remains in place: while the minimum voting age is eighteen, the minimum age for candidacy to the Lok Sabha is twenty-five and to the Rajya Sabha is thirty. This means that eighteen-to-twenty-four year olds can vote but cannot stand for Parliament, a gap that has generated periodic debate about whether the Constitution's treatment of youth political participation is fully coherent. Proposals to lower the candidacy age have been discussed but not enacted, and the question of whether Article 326's logic of maximum inclusive participation should extend to candidacy eligibility remains live in constitutional and electoral reform debates.

The amendment reinforced the broader constitutional commitment to universal franchise that the founding generation had embedded in the Constitution — a commitment that distinguished India from many post-colonial democracies that had imposed literacy or property qualifications on the vote, and that has remained one of the Constitution's most durable and least contested provisions.
