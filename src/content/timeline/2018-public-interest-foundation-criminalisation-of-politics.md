The case remains central because it links criminalisation of politics to democratic accountability while leaving broad new disqualification rules to Parliament.

## What happened

Public Interest Foundation and other petitioners approached the Supreme Court seeking a judicial order that would bar candidates with pending criminal charges from contesting elections, going beyond the existing disqualification framework in the Representation of the People Act. A five-judge Constitution Bench, led by the Chief Justice, declined to create a new disqualification by judicial direction, holding that such a step would be a legislative act which the Constitution assigns to Parliament under Articles 102 and 191. Instead, the Court mandated enhanced disclosure requirements, requiring candidates to publicise their criminal antecedents prominently through multiple channels, and directed political parties to provide reasons for fielding candidates with criminal records.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The bench drew a sharp line between what courts can require in the exercise of constitutional jurisdiction and what only Parliament can enact as law governing candidate eligibility. It reinforced the separation of powers constraint on judicial lawmaking in the electoral domain, even where the constitutional purpose — democratic accountability — was plain. The decision also developed the constitutional dimension of voters' right to information under Article 19(1)(a) as a tool for electoral transparency, building on the earlier disclosure directions in the Association for Democratic Reforms and PUCL cases.

## Its legacy

The case gave momentum to efforts by civil society and election watchdogs to pressure Parliament into legislating stiffer disqualification rules, but no comprehensive amendment followed. It also set up the follow-on directions the Supreme Court issued in 2020 requiring political parties to upload explanations for fielding candidates with criminal cases, turning the original judgment into an ongoing compliance exercise rather than a settled endpoint.
