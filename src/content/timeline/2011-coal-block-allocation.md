## What happened

Between 1993 and 2010 the Union Ministry of Coal allocated captive coal blocks to public sector undertakings and private companies through an inter-ministerial Screening Committee that evaluated applications on largely undisclosed criteria without competitive bidding. In August 2012 the CAG laid before Parliament a performance audit report estimating that private allottees received coal blocks at far below market value, with a notional under-recovery to the government calculated in excess of Rs 1.86 lakh crore. Advocate Manohar Lal Sharma filed a public interest writ petition before the Supreme Court. After examining allocation files, the Supreme Court in its judgment of 24 September 2014 held that the Screening Committee process was opaque, non-transparent, and arbitrary, and cancelled 214 of 218 allocations reviewed, directing allottees to pay additional levies and discontinue mining operations on a scheduled timeline. The CBI simultaneously registered first information reports against officials and corporate officers, including cases implicating senior political figures.

## Constitutional significance

The Supreme Court's analysis in Manohar Lal Sharma v The Principal Secretary drew on two separate parts of the Constitution. Under Article 14, the Court applied the arbitrariness strand of the equality guarantee, holding that the Screening Committee had no objective, disclosed criteria for ranking applicants and that the selection of successful applicants was therefore constitutionally unsustainable. Under Articles 39(b) and 39(c), which direct the State to ensure that the ownership and control of material resources of the community are distributed so as to subserve the common good and that the operation of the economic system does not result in the concentration of wealth, the Court reasoned that coal — a finite subterranean resource owned by the nation — carries a public trust character that imposes a duty of accountability on the State when it distributes extraction rights. While Articles 39(b) and (c) are Directive Principles not directly enforceable, the Court used them to interpret the content of the Article 14 obligation and to confirm that arbitrary allocation of a public resource at below-market terms to private parties violates the constitutional framework. The judgment expanded the public trust doctrine, originally articulated in the 2G natural resources reference, into the domain of mineral resources.

## Long-term impact

The coal block cancellation had immediate legislative consequences: Parliament enacted the Coal Mines (Special Provisions) Act 2015, which created a legal framework for re-auctioning the cancelled blocks and established that future allocations of coal mines must proceed through competitive auction or allotment with disclosed criteria. The constitutional doctrine developed in Manohar Lal Sharma — that allocation of natural resources is a constitutional act subject to Article 14 and informed by Articles 39(b)-(c) — has been applied in subsequent cases involving spectrum, port concessions, and land grants. The case also strengthened the institutional position of the CAG, demonstrating that a performance audit under Articles 148-151 could, through parliamentary and judicial processes, initiate the constitutional review and invalidation of executive allocation decisions made over nearly two decades.
