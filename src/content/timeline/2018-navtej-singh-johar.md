Navtej Singh Johar is one of the clearest examples of rights doctrine moving from formal equality toward lived dignity and autonomy.

## What happened

A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in a set of four concurring opinions, partially struck down Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code insofar as it criminalised consensual same-sex intimacy between adults. The petitioners included members of the LGBTQ community and others who challenged the provision as violating Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21. The Court expressly overruled the 2013 decision of the Supreme Court in Suresh Kumar Koushal, which had reinstated Section 377 after the Delhi High Court's earlier reading down. The bench held that historical and colonial legislation could not be permitted to override constitutional guarantees of dignity and identity.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment deployed the concept of constitutional morality — as distinct from popular or social morality — to hold that public sentiment cannot override the fundamental rights of minorities. It grounded the right to sexual orientation and identity firmly in Articles 14, 15, and 21, treating identity itself as a protected dimension of personhood. The decision also built directly on Puttaswamy's privacy framework and extended it to intimate life and autonomy, showing how constitutional doctrine evolves through the interlinkage of successive judgments. It confirmed that Article 15's prohibition on discrimination includes discrimination based on sexual orientation.

## Its legacy

Navtej Singh Johar set the constitutional floor for subsequent questions about LGBTQ recognition, including later petitions on same-sex marriage and adoption. Its emphasis on constitutional morality and non-discrimination continues to be cited in cases involving minoritarian rights, and its overruling of Koushal reinforced the Court's capacity to correct its own constitutional errors on rights questions when confronted with a changed doctrinal landscape.
