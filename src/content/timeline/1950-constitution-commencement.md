## What happened

On 26 January 1950, the Constitution of India came into force, replacing the Government of India Act 1935 as the supreme law of the land. The Constituent Assembly, which had deliberated since December 1946 under the presidency of Dr. Rajendra Prasad, dissolved itself and became the provisional Parliament. India ceased to be a dominion under the Crown and became a sovereign democratic republic. Dr. Prasad was sworn in as the first President of India. The date was chosen to mark the twentieth anniversary of the 1930 Purna Swaraj declaration, linking the new constitutional order to the longer freedom movement.

Article 395 formally repealed the Indian Independence Act 1947 and the Government of India Act 1935. Article 372 provided for the continuance of all laws then in force until altered, repealed, or amended. Article 1 declared India to be a Union of States, settling the federal character of the republic.

## Constitutional significance

The commencement of the Constitution immediately activated Part III, making fundamental rights enforceable by courts for the first time in Indian legal history. Article 13 declared that any pre-constitutional law inconsistent with Part III rights was void to the extent of its inconsistency, creating an immediate wave of litigation over colonial-era statutes. The simultaneous inauguration of a federal structure, an independent judiciary with power of judicial review, and a Bill of Rights created three interconnected axes of constitutional tension that courts, Parliament, and the executive would negotiate across subsequent decades.

The commencement also fixed the constitutional baseline. The validity of all executive action, legislation, and constitutional amendment from this date forward would be assessed by reference to the Constitution as it stood, and as subsequently interpreted. This made 26 January 1950 the indispensable reference point for any account of Indian constitutional development.

## Long-term impact

The choice of Republic Day as the annual national observance embedded constitutional consciousness in the civic calendar in a way rare among modern constitutions. Every subsequent amendment, judicial doctrine, and institutional crisis situated itself in relation to the founding text. The commencement simultaneously created the constitutional order and the conditions for its contestation: the juxtaposition of justiciable rights against a Parliament with amending power would generate the core conflicts of Indian constitutional law — from the first amendment's Ninth Schedule in 1951 through to the basic structure doctrine articulated in Kesavananda Bharati in 1973 and beyond.
