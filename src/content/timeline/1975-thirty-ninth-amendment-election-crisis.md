This was one of the clearest moments when election law, institutional legitimacy, and constitutional power collided. It also helped sharpen the later basic-structure debate over whether amendment power could be used to block review.

## What happened

The Constitution (Thirty-Ninth Amendment) Act was passed by Parliament in August 1975, after the Emergency had been proclaimed and opposition members had been detained. The amendment inserted Article 329A, which declared that elections to the offices of President, Vice-President, Prime Minister, and Speaker could not be challenged in any court, and that any such challenge pending before a court would stand abated. It simultaneously amended the Representation of the People Act to retrospectively alter the law on electoral offences, effectively removing the specific conduct that the Allahabad High Court had found to constitute corrupt practice in Indira Gandhi's case. The amendment was passed through Parliament in a single day, with the opposition having been decimated by arrests and the press under censorship.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Thirty-Ninth Amendment was the most naked use of the amending power to protect an individual officeholder in Indian constitutional history. By attempting to transfer disputes about prime ministerial elections to a newly constituted forum, making those elections unchallengeable, and retrospectively validating the specific election under challenge, the amendment attacked simultaneously the separation of powers, the rule of law, the principle of free and fair elections, and the concept of equality before the law. It presented the Supreme Court with the question of whether the basic structure doctrine, articulated just two years earlier in Kesavananda, could be invoked against an amendment that was transparently designed to circumvent judicial review.

## Its legacy

The Supreme Court's decision to strike down the key provisions of the Thirty-Ninth Amendment in the Indira election case was the first concrete validation of the basic structure doctrine as an operational limit on amendment power. The episode demonstrated that the doctrine was not merely academic but capable of constraining emergency-era constitutional manipulation, and it preserved at least a symbolic commitment to the principle that elections are subject to law rather than law being subject to elections. The Forty-Fourth Amendment subsequently removed Article 329A entirely, erasing this amendment from the constitutional text.
