The judgment became one of the main constitutional bridges between traditional speech doctrine and the regulation of digital communications infrastructure.

## What happened

Following the revocation of the special status of Jammu and Kashmir in August 2019, the government imposed an extensive communications shutdown, including suspension of internet services, telephone lines, and movement restrictions. Anuradha Bhasin, the executive editor of Kashmir Times, and others challenged these restrictions before the Supreme Court, arguing that they infringed the freedom of speech and expression and the freedom to carry on any trade or profession through digital means. A three-judge bench, deciding the case in January 2020, held that the internet is a protected medium under Article 19(1)(a) and (g), and that indefinite shutdowns without a publicly accessible legal order and periodic review are constitutionally impermissible.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment extended constitutional free speech protection explicitly to the internet as a medium, rejecting the idea that digital restrictions fall entirely outside Part III because they operate on communications infrastructure rather than speech content directly. It required that shutdown orders must be proportionate, time-limited, and subject to review, applying the proportionality standard that Puttaswamy had introduced into fundamental rights analysis. The Court also recognised that press freedom under Article 19(1)(a) encompasses the ability of journalists to access the internet as a necessary precondition for the exercise of that freedom.

## Its legacy

Anuradha Bhasin became the principal precedent for subsequent challenges to internet shutdowns across the country, which are among the most frequent in the world. However, critics noted that the practical relief afforded was limited, since the Court did not order immediate restoration of services, and the proportionality review mechanism it envisaged has not always been robustly applied in subsequent shutdown orders by state and Union governments.
