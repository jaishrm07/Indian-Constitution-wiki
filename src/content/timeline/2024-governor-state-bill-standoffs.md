## What happened

From 2022 onwards, several Opposition-governed states — including Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Punjab, and Telangana — reported that their Governors had withheld assent to bills passed by the respective state legislatures, in some cases for periods running into years, without returning the bills with objections or reserving them for the President's consideration as required by Article 200. The affected state governments filed writ petitions before the Supreme Court. In the Kerala Governor case, a bench of the Supreme Court in early 2024 held that the Governor was bound to act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers on matters within the state's legislative competence and could not create an indefinite pocket veto by taking no action on passed bills. In the Punjab Governor case, involving bills for which the Governor had declined to call a special session and had withheld assent, the Court similarly held that the Governor's discretion under Article 200 was bounded by the constitutional text, which provides only three options: assent, return for reconsideration, or reserve for the President. The Court's use of Article 142 to fashion direct remedies — in some instances treating bills as having received assent — marked a significant step in enforcing constitutional duties on Governors.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Governor-assent cases brought to a head a broader constitutional question about the nature of the gubernatorial office in India's parliamentary-federal framework. The Governor is appointed by the President on the advice of the Council of Ministers of the Union, creating a structural incentive for the office to act in the interests of the central government rather than as a neutral constitutional head of the state. The Supreme Court's intervention clarified that whatever the political context, the Governor's powers under Article 200 are constitutionally defined and cannot be converted into tools of executive obstruction. The decisions reinforced the principle that the constitutional design requires the Governor to facilitate, not frustrate, the functioning of an elected state government.

## Its constitutional legacy

Together with the 2025 Tamil Nadu judgment, the 2024 Governor rulings created a body of doctrine on Article 200 that had been largely undeveloped since the provision's enactment. They shifted the constitutional equilibrium by making it clear that the Governor's assent power is subject to judicial enforcement, and that courts will intervene to protect state legislative authority from constitutional head-level obstruction. The cases prompted renewed discussion about whether constitutional reform — including reconsidering the method of appointment of Governors — was necessary to address the structural tensions that had produced the standoffs.
