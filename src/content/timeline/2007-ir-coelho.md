I.R. Coelho matters because it makes the basic-structure doctrine concrete for laws that might otherwise claim immunity through constitutional drafting.

## What happened

The I.R. Coelho case arose from a challenge to Tamil Nadu land ceiling laws that had been placed in the Ninth Schedule. The petitioners argued that even though Parliament had the power to add laws to the Ninth Schedule under Article 31B — thereby granting them immunity from challenge on grounds of inconsistency with Fundamental Rights — that immunity could not extend to laws which, if enforced, would violate the basic structure of the Constitution. A nine-judge Constitution Bench decided the matter in January 2007. The Court held that any law inserted into the Ninth Schedule after April 24, 1973 — the date of the Kesavananda Bharati judgment — could be subjected to basic structure review. If such a law abridged or abrogated rights under Articles 14, 19, and 21 to a degree that amounted to destroying the golden triangle of fundamental freedoms, it was open to judicial invalidation despite its Ninth Schedule placement.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Before I.R. Coelho, the Ninth Schedule had come to function as a constitutional safe harbour: Parliament could shield controversial legislation from judicial scrutiny simply by including it in the schedule through a constitutional amendment. This practice had expanded enormously since the schedule was first used to protect agrarian land reform laws, and by 2007 it covered laws in domains far removed from its original purpose. The Court's ruling closed this loophole by ruling that Article 31B does not create a fundamental rights-free zone impervious to basic structure review, and that the schedule cannot be weaponised to bypass constitutional guarantees that form part of the Constitution's core identity.

## Its legacy

I.R. Coelho established that there is no constitutional drafting technique that can permanently foreclose basic structure review. The Ninth Schedule still exists and new additions to it can still give laws protection from ordinary fundamental rights challenges, but that protection is not unconditional. The judgment reinforced the systemic role of the basic structure doctrine as a limitation that operates against all forms of constitutional amendment, whether through direct changes to the text or through creative use of existing constitutional mechanisms to achieve the same result. It remains the leading authority on the limits of Article 31B.
