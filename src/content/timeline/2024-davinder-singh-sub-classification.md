The decision immediately reshaped reservation design debates by distinguishing constitutional lists from the internal tailoring of benefits.

## What happened

A seven-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in the case arising from a Punjab law that sub-classified Scheduled Castes for differential quota allocation, held by a majority of six to one that states have the constitutional power to sub-classify within the Scheduled Caste category for the purpose of targeted reservation benefits. The majority overruled the earlier five-judge bench decision in E.V. Chinnaiah, which had held that Scheduled Castes form a homogeneous class under Article 341 that cannot be sub-divided. The Court accepted that the empirical reality of unequal distribution of reservation benefits within the SC category — where some sub-groups have historically cornered most of the reserved seats and posts — provides a rational basis for sub-classification.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The decision resolved a significant tension between the constitutional text of Article 341, which treats the Presidential List of Scheduled Castes as an integrated statutory category, and the equality principle, which requires that state action address actual deprivation rather than assume all members of a reserved category share the same degree of disadvantage. The majority held that the Article 341 List defines the outer boundary of the class, but states retain flexibility in allocating benefits within that class through reasonable sub-classification backed by empirical data. It also introduced a requirement that sub-classification be based on evidence of inadequate representation, not merely political or administrative preference.

## Its legacy

Davinder Singh triggered legislative and policy responses across states examining whether to create sub-quotas within their SC reservation frameworks. It also re-opened academic and political debate about whether the same logic should apply to Scheduled Tribes, and whether the decision should lead to a broader reconsideration of how the State identifies and quantifies targeted backwardness for reservation purposes.
