Golaknath matters because it put amendment power itself inside constitutional controversy and set up the later Kesavananda settlement.

## What happened

The Golaknath family of Punjab challenged land-ceiling legislation that had been placed in the Ninth Schedule and challenged the validity of the First, Fourth, and Seventeenth Amendments. An eleven-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, by a six-to-five majority, overruled its earlier decisions in Shankari Prasad and Sajjan Singh, which had held that Parliament could amend any part of the Constitution including Part III. The majority, led by Chief Justice K. Subba Rao, held that a constitutional amendment is "law" within the meaning of Article 13, and that Parliament therefore cannot use the amending power under Article 368 to abridge or take away fundamental rights. The majority applied the doctrine of prospective overruling to avoid immediately invalidating the three amendments already in force.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Golaknath elevated fundamental rights to a category of constitutional provisions that the amending power could not touch, creating a hierarchy within the Constitution itself. This was a dramatic assertion of judicial authority over the formal text-amending process and directly contradicted the position that Parliament had a constituent power unlimited by Part III. The decision forced Parliament to confront the question of whether it could implement socialist land reform and nationalisation programmes within the constitutional framework as the Court had interpreted it, generating an acute tension between the constitutional directive principles, which favoured such programmes, and the court-enforced limits on amendment power.

## Its legacy

Golaknath directly provoked Parliament's constitutional response through the Twenty-Fourth, Twenty-Fifth, and Twenty-Ninth Amendments, all of which sought to restore and clarify Parliament's amending power. It set the stage for the thirteen-judge bench in Kesavananda Bharati, which effectively superseded Golaknath by holding that while Parliament could amend any part of the Constitution including fundamental rights, it could not destroy the basic structure. Golaknath thus served as the necessary catalyst for the most consequential constitutional judgment in Indian history.
