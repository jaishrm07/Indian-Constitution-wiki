## What happened

By the early 1980s, tensions between the Union Government and several State governments had reached an acute level. The Congress party's dominance at the Centre sat uneasily alongside the emergence of regional parties in Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, and other states. The misuse of Article 356 — under which the President could dismiss a State government and impose Central rule — was a recurring grievance: between 1950 and 1983, President's Rule had been imposed over eighty times, far more frequently in States governed by opposition parties than in those governed by Congress. Regional leaders, including N.T. Rama Rao of Telugu Desam, M.G. Ramachandran of AIADMK, and Farooq Abdullah of the National Conference, complained of systematic Central interference in State governance.

Against this background, the Union Government appointed the Sarkaria Commission on 9 June 1983. It was chaired by Justice Ranjit Singh Sarkaria, a retired judge of the Supreme Court, and included B. Sivaraman, a former IAS officer, and Dr. S.R. Sen, a former member of the Planning Commission. The Commission's mandate was broad — to examine and review the working of existing arrangements between the Union and the States and to recommend appropriate changes within the framework of the Constitution. The Commission toured all States and Union Territories, received thousands of memoranda from governments, political parties, trade unions, academics, and civil society organisations, and examined witnesses over a period of five years.

The Commission submitted its report in January 1988. Running to over eighteen hundred pages in two volumes, it examined every major aspect of Centre-State relations — the distribution of legislative powers, financial relations, the planning process, the role of the Governor, emergency provisions, deployment of Central forces in States, and the working of the Inter-State Council. It made 247 specific recommendations.

## Constitutional significance

The Commission's most consequential constitutional recommendations addressed the misuse of Article 356. It held that the provision had been frequently invoked for political rather than constitutional reasons, and recommended a series of procedural and substantive safeguards — that the Centre must attempt to resolve the breakdown of constitutional machinery in a State through alternative means before invoking Article 356; that the Governor must submit an objective and impartial report; that an opportunity should be given to the State government to respond before dismissal; and that the State Assembly should not be dissolved before the parliamentary vote approving the proclamation. These recommendations anticipated and informed the Supreme Court's own constitutional discipline over Article 356 established in S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994), though Bommai derived its reasoning from the basic structure doctrine independently of the Commission's recommendations.

On the allocation of legislative powers under Schedule VII, the Commission recommended that there should be no further erosion of the State List, that the Concurrent List should not be expanded unilaterally by the Centre, and that in areas of concurrent legislative competence the Centre should consult States before legislating on matters of primary State concern. It recommended that the Inter-State Council under Article 263 should be revived and made a functioning deliberative body for cooperative federalism, having been dormant for decades. The Council was eventually constituted in 1990 under V.P. Singh's government, partly in response to the Sarkaria recommendations.

The Commission also addressed the role of the Governor, identifying the practice of appointing politically partisan Governors and using them as agents of the Centre as a major source of dysfunction in Centre-State relations. It recommended that Governors should be eminent persons outside active politics, appointed in consultation with the Chief Minister of the State, and should act as constitutional heads facilitating communication between the Centre and the State rather than as instruments of Central oversight.

## Long-term impact

The Sarkaria Commission's report became the standard reference document for Centre-State relations debates in India for the following three decades. Its analysis of Article 356's misuse directly fed into the intellectual and legal arguments that produced Bommai, and several of the Commission's specific recommendations were reflected in the Court's judgment, particularly the requirement of a floor test and the principle of federalism as a basic feature of the Constitution.

A second commission — the Punchhi Commission, chaired by former Chief Justice M.M. Punchhi — was appointed in 2007 to update the Sarkaria Commission's work in light of developments since 1988. The Punchhi Commission submitted its report in 2010 and largely built upon the Sarkaria framework, recommending further safeguards around Governor appointments, stronger Inter-State Council mechanisms, and clearer boundaries between Central and State fiscal competences.

The Sarkaria Commission's legacy also lies in the institutional vocabulary it gave to federal disputes. Arguments about cooperative federalism, the proper use of Articles 256, 257, and 263, the meaning of "failure of constitutional machinery" under Article 356, and the Governor's discretionary role have all been conducted partly in the Commission's terms. Its work demonstrated that non-judicial bodies, through sustained consultative inquiry, could contribute to constitutional development even where formal constitutional amendment was politically impossible.
