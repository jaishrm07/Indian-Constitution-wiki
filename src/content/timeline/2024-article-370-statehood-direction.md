## What happened

Following the August 2019 Presidential Orders and the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act 2019 (which rendered Article 370 inoperative and bifurcated the state into two Union Territories), a large number of petitions were filed before the Supreme Court challenging the constitutional validity of the abrogation. The matters were referred to a five-judge Constitution Bench. The Bench heard extensive arguments over several months in 2023.

On 11 December 2023, the Constitution Bench delivered a unanimous judgment authored by Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud. The Court held that Article 370 was a temporary provision by its own terms and that the President had the constitutional power to render it inoperative through the mechanism used. It accepted the government's argument that the Jammu and Kashmir Constituent Assembly's recommendation — required by Article 370(3) — could be substituted by the concurrence of the State Legislature, which itself could be provided by the Governor acting under President's Rule. The Court upheld the bifurcation into Union Territories as a valid exercise of Article 3, while observing that reducing a state to a Union Territory is an extreme measure. It directed that statehood be restored as soon as possible, that an election be held in Jammu and Kashmir by September 2024, and appointed a former Chief Election Commissioner to oversee the process. Elections were held in September and October 2024, following which a state government was formed under the National Conference party.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment clarified that "temporary" provisions in the Constitution are subject to the President's power of modification or revocation within the textual framework those provisions themselves establish, without requiring a formal constitutional amendment under Article 368. This established an important principle: the Centre can use the mechanism of a temporary provision's own amendment clause to effectively extinguish it, provided it acts within the provision's procedural framework. The Court's acceptance that the Governor's concurrence during President's Rule was sufficient to satisfy Article 370(3)'s requirement meant that the procedural safeguards intended to protect Jammu and Kashmir's special status could be satisfied when the state had no elected government — a reading that some judges characterised as transforming emergency powers into a tool of permanent constitutional change.

## Its constitutional legacy

The Article 370 judgment resolved the immediate constitutional challenge but left several tensions in the federal structure visible. The Court's direction to restore statehood — while unprecedented as a judicial direction to the executive on territorial organisation — had the practical effect of ensuring a return to elected governance, with elections duly held. The judgment is now studied for its treatment of temporary constitutional provisions, the scope of Article 356 powers to alter a state's constitutional position, and the limits of judicial remedy under Article 142 when the Court disagrees with the political outcome of executive action but finds it technically within constitutional bounds.
