## What happened

In Arunachal Pradesh, a political crisis developed in late 2015 when a faction of the ruling Congress party sought to remove Speaker Nabam Rebia. The Governor, J.P. Rajkhowa, issued a notice advancing the date of the Assembly session without the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. When the Assembly met in the advanced session, events led to the displacement of the Speaker and ultimately the installation of a rival government under Kalikho Pul. President's Rule was briefly imposed and revoked. The ousted Congress government challenged the Governor's actions before the Gauhati High Court, which ruled in its favour, and the matter went to the Supreme Court.

A five-judge Constitution Bench delivered a unanimous judgment on 13 July 2016. It held that the Governor had no power to advance the date of the Assembly session on his own initiative without the aid and advice of the Chief Minister and Cabinet, as the power under Article 174 to summon the Assembly does not include the power to act contrary to ministerial advice in a matter within the Council of Ministers' constitutional domain. The Court quashed all the Governor's actions and restored the Congress government and the Speaker to their respective positions.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Nabam Rebia is the most extensive judicial examination of the scope of the Governor's discretion under Article 163. The Constitution Bench clarified that the Governor's "discretion" in constitutional practice is narrowly bounded: in most matters, including the summoning and prorogation of the Assembly, the Governor must act on ministerial advice. The judgment drew a clear line between genuine constitutional discretion — for example, in the appointment of the Chief Minister when no single party commands a majority — and the ordinary exercise of constitutional powers, which remains subject to the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. By quashing actions taken through the covert use of gubernatorial power, the Court showed that constitutional conventions on responsible government are justiciable and not merely political.

## Its constitutional legacy

Nabam Rebia is routinely cited alongside Bommai as the foundational pair of judgments on gubernatorial constitutional obligations. Its reasoning on Article 174 was applied in the Arunachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, and Maharashtra cases that followed. The judgment did not resolve all questions about gubernatorial discretion, but it significantly narrowed the space available to Governors to act against the advice of an elected ministry in matters ordinarily governed by constitutional convention. It remains the leading authority on the intersection of Article 163, the Speaker's powers, and the summoning of state legislatures.
