The NJAC judgment is the Supreme Court's most direct confrontation with a constitutional amendment in the modern era, and its most consequential statement on judicial independence as a non-negotiable structural feature.

## What happened

The Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association and other petitioners challenged the Ninety-Ninth Amendment, which had inserted Articles 124A, 124B, and 124C into the Constitution to establish the National Judicial Appointments Commission, and the NJAC Act that gave it operational shape. The NJAC was to consist of the Chief Justice of India, the two senior-most puisne judges of the Supreme Court, the Union Law Minister, and two eminent persons nominated through a joint process — replacing the collegium with a mixed body that gave the executive significant representation. A five-judge Constitution Bench, by a majority of four to one, struck down both the amendment and the Act in October 2015. The majority held that the NJAC's composition and the veto it effectively gave to non-judicial members violated the independence of the judiciary.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The NJAC judgment is significant on multiple levels. It held that judicial independence in the appointments process is part of the basic structure of the Constitution, and therefore cannot be abridged even by a constitutional amendment. The majority applied the Kesavananda-Minerva Mills framework to a structural provision — not an individual right — and found that giving the executive and eminent persons a decisive role in appointments crossed the constitutional line. The ruling also had the effect of reaffirming the Second Judges Case framework, despite widespread acknowledgment even within the majority that the collegium system had serious shortcomings in transparency and accountability.

## Its legacy

The NJAC judgment resolved the formal constitutional question in favour of judicial primacy, but it did not resolve the underlying institutional tensions. The Court acknowledged the need to improve the collegium's working and committed to developing a memorandum of procedure governing appointments — a process that has remained stalled. The decision deepened friction between the judiciary and the executive over appointments, and the collegium system continued to attract criticism for opacity. The NJAC case is now cited as the clearest example of the Court using the basic structure doctrine to protect its own institutional role, raising ongoing questions about the legitimacy and limits of that doctrine when the Court adjudicates on matters directly affecting its own composition.
