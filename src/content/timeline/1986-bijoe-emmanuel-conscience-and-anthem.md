Bijoe Emmanuel remains a leading conscience case because it treats respectful non-participation as constitutionally protected when the State tries to compel expression.

## What happened

Three children from the Jehovah's Witnesses community — students at a school in Kerala — stood in silence and respect when the national anthem was played but did not sing along, in keeping with the tenets of their faith. They were expelled from school. Their father challenged the expulsion before the Kerala High Court, which upheld it, and the matter reached the Supreme Court. A two-judge bench reversed the High Court, holding that the children's conduct — standing respectfully throughout — satisfied every reasonable expectation of civic respect for the anthem, and that compelling them to sing, in violation of their sincere religious belief, infringed their freedoms of conscience, expression, and religion guaranteed by Articles 19 and 25.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The case drew a sharp distinction between compelling expression and requiring respectful acknowledgment. The Court held that the State cannot conscript a citizen's voice or physical participation to convey a message that the citizen sincerely objects to on grounds of faith. This reasoning identified a constitutional principle — that silence, when not disruptive, cannot be treated as disrespect — and grounded it in the intersection of Article 19(1)(a) and Article 25. The judgment also clarified that the Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act requires actual disrespect, not mere non-participation.

## Its legacy

Bijoe Emmanuel is regularly cited in debates about compelled speech, patriotic rituals in public institutions, and the constitutional space for conscientious objection. The principles it established found renewed relevance when the Supreme Court later considered whether standing during the national anthem in cinema halls could be compelled. The case established that visible, respectful non-compliance rooted in sincere belief stands on firmer constitutional ground than the State's interest in uniformity of expressive participation.
