The reference remains foundational because it rejects the idea that legislative privilege is absolute while still preserving a real sphere of internal autonomy for the House.

## What happened

Keshav Singh was a citizen of Uttar Pradesh who was committed to custody by the Legislative Assembly for contempt of the House. He obtained a bail order from the Allahabad High Court, which the Assembly then treated as a fresh contempt, proceeding against the two judges who had made the bail order and the advocate who had moved the application. The judges of the Allahabad High Court responded collectively, declining to appear before the Assembly, and the President of India made a reference to the Supreme Court under Article 143 seeking its opinion on whether the Assembly had the power to commit judges of the High Court for contempt arising from judicial acts. A full bench of the Supreme Court gave its advisory opinion holding that the High Court's bail order was a judicial act protected from contempt proceedings by the House, and that legislative privilege does not authorise the legislature to punish judges for exercising constitutional judicial functions.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The reference addressed the foundational question of whether legislative privilege in India is absolute and beyond the reach of judicial review, or whether it exists within a constitutional system in which courts retain the authority to protect constitutional rights and independent judicial functions. The Supreme Court's opinion established that privilege is extensive but bounded — it covers proceedings in and internal to the House but cannot be used to coerce or punish courts exercising their constitutional jurisdiction. This placed the privileges of Indian legislatures on a different footing from the absolute privilege of the Westminster Parliament, because the Indian Constitution is the supreme law and the courts are its guardians.

## Its legacy

The Keshav Singh opinion became the foundational text for subsequent privilege jurisprudence in India, cited in every major case involving the boundary between legislative authority and judicial review. It was the reference point for Raja Ram Pal v. Hon'ble Speaker when the Supreme Court reviewed the expulsion of Members of Parliament, and for Sita Soren when the Court addressed the scope of immunity for legislative acts connected to corruption. Its core holding — that courts can review whether a claim of privilege is constitutionally valid even if they cannot review the merits of proceedings inside the House — remains the governing principle.
