## What happened

In November 2010 the CAG laid before Parliament a report under Article 151 estimating that the government had caused a notional revenue loss of up to Rs 1.76 lakh crore by allocating 2G spectrum licences in 2008 at 2001 prices on a first-come-first-served basis rather than through competitive auction. The then Telecom Minister A. Raja had advanced the cut-off date for applications without public notice and manipulated the queue to benefit chosen applicants. The Centre for Public Interest Litigation and Subramanian Swamy filed separate writ petitions in the Supreme Court challenging the allocations. The Court, in a judgment delivered on 2 February 2012, cancelled all 122 licences granted after January 2008. The Central Bureau of Investigation and the Enforcement Directorate pursued criminal and money-laundering proceedings against several ministers and corporate officials, and the matter occupied Parliamentary debates and joint parliamentary committee hearings through 2012 and 2013.

## Constitutional significance

The Supreme Court's cancellation order in Centre for Public Interest Litigation v Union of India rested on two constitutional grounds. First, the Court held that the grant of licences at artificially low prices through a manipulated queue violated Article 14 because it was arbitrary, non-transparent, and designed to favour particular applicants over others. The arbitrariness doctrine — that executive action which lacks intelligible differentia and is not rationally connected to a legitimate purpose violates the equality guarantee — was applied with full force to the allocation of a public resource. Second, the Court held that spectrum, as a public resource, could not be distributed by the State in a manner that amounted to a gift to private parties at the expense of the public revenue, importing the idea that natural resources carry a public trust dimension that limits executive discretion. The CAG's constitutional role under Articles 148 to 151, as the independent auditor of central government accounts whose reports go directly to Parliament rather than to the executive, was central to placing the scandal in the public domain. The 2G case became the clearest judicial statement that the constitutional guarantee of equality extends to the terms on which the State chooses to share public resources with private parties.

## Long-term impact

The 2G judgment prompted an immediate shift in government policy: the Union of India moved to spectrum auction as the mandatory method of allocation, and the Supreme Court's statement that natural resources must be allocated by a transparent, competitive process became the doctrinal starting point for the coal block allocation litigation that followed in 2014. The decision was subsequently qualified in subsequent cases, including Natural Resources Allocation (Presidential Reference) in 2012, where a Constitution Bench held that the Court had not mandated auction as the only constitutionally permissible method and that the executive retains policy discretion in resource allocation — but that any chosen method must be non-arbitrary and in the public interest. Together, the 2G cancellation and the Natural Resources Reference defined the outer boundary of Article 14 as applied to government allocation of scarce resources, a question that continues to arise in disputes over spectrum, coal, land, and port allocations.
