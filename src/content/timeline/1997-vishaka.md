Vishaka is the judgment that first gave constitutional force to the right against sexual harassment at work in India. It held that the absence of enacted law is not a reason to deny a constitutional right, and that international norms can supply the content of domestic rights where Parliament has not acted.

## What happened

Bhanwari Devi, a social worker in Rajasthan, was gang-raped in 1992 while attempting to prevent a child marriage. The failure of state institutions to prosecute her attackers adequately prompted a coalition of women's groups, operating under the name Vishaka, to file a public interest litigation. They asked the Supreme Court to lay down guidelines for protection against sexual harassment in the workplace, a subject on which no specific domestic legislation existed. A three-judge bench of the Supreme Court, in a judgment delivered in August 1997, accepted the petition and issued the Vishaka Guidelines — a set of binding directions addressed to all employers in India, requiring establishment of complaints committees, prohibition of harassment, and awareness measures.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Court reasoned that sexual harassment at the workplace violates the fundamental rights to equality under Article 14, non-discrimination under Article 15, freedom to practise any profession under Article 19(1)(g), and the right to life and personal dignity under Article 21. It then used Article 51(c) — the Directive Principle requiring the State to foster respect for international law — together with Article 253 (Parliament's power to legislate for treaty implementation), to incorporate the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) as an interpretive resource. The judgment explicitly held that in the absence of enacted domestic law, international conventions that are consistent with fundamental rights can be read into those rights to give them content. This was a significant methodological move: it used a Directive Principle as a gateway for international norms to enter fundamental rights adjudication.

## Its constitutional legacy

The Vishaka Guidelines operated as binding law for sixteen years until Parliament enacted the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act in 2013. The methodological legacy of the case — using international conventions to expand the content of fundamental rights — has influenced subsequent judgments across civil liberties, prisoners' rights, and environmental law. The case also established the model of the Supreme Court issuing detailed, legislative-quality directions in public interest litigation when Parliament has been inactive on a constitutional right, a model that has been both celebrated and contested in subsequent constitutional discourse.
