## What happened

Maneka Gandhi, a journalist and then-spouse of Sanjay Gandhi, had her passport impounded in July 1977 by the Ministry of External Affairs under the Passports Act 1967 in the "public interest," without any reasons being given. She challenged the impoundment before the Supreme Court, arguing that it violated her right to personal liberty under Article 21, her right to freedom of movement under Article 19(1)(d), and Article 14's equality guarantee. The case came before a seven-judge bench which handed down its decision on 25 January 1978.

The bench unanimously held that the impoundment was invalid. More significant than the outcome were the doctrinal principles the Court articulated. Justice P.N. Bhagwati, writing the leading opinion, rejected the narrow reading of Article 21 established in A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950) — that "procedure established by law" meant any procedure that a competent legislature had prescribed, however arbitrary. Instead, the Court held that the procedure depriving a person of life or personal liberty must be just, fair, and reasonable. A procedure that was unfair or arbitrary could not satisfy Article 21 even if it was technically authorised by law.

## Constitutional significance

Maneka Gandhi fundamentally restructured the relationship between the fundamental rights in Part III. The Gopalan decision had held that each fundamental right was a self-contained guarantee, so a law that fell within the ambit of Article 21 was not separately tested against Articles 14 or 19. The Maneka Gandhi bench overruled this approach and held that Articles 14, 19, and 21 together constitute a "golden triangle" — each interacts with and conditions the others. A law depriving a person of personal liberty must therefore comply with Article 14 (it must be non-arbitrary), Article 19 (it must be a reasonable restriction on the freedoms listed there), and Article 21 (the procedure must be just, fair, and reasonable). This triangular reading enormously expanded the substantive content of the fundamental rights guarantee.

The judgment also established that the right to travel abroad is a component of personal liberty under Article 21, and that natural justice — specifically the right to be heard before an adverse decision — is part of any fair procedure under Article 21.

## Long-term impact

Maneka Gandhi is the foundational decision for the post-1978 expansion of Article 21. The concept of just, fair, and reasonable procedure under Article 21 became the vehicle through which the Supreme Court progressively recognised rights that are not expressly mentioned in the Constitution: the right to live with dignity, the right to a clean environment, the right to speedy trial, the right to legal aid, the right to health, the right to privacy (formally recognised in Puttaswamy in 2017), and many others. The golden triangle reading also transformed Article 14 into a substantive protection against arbitrary state action rather than a merely procedural equality guarantee.

The timing of the judgment — delivered just days before the Forty-Fourth Amendment was enacted — meant that Maneka Gandhi and the Amendment together restructured the constitutional framework for personal liberty. The Amendment made Article 21 non-suspendable even during emergencies, and Maneka Gandhi gave Article 21 substantive content. Together they converted the right to life and personal liberty from one of the more modest fundamental rights into the broadest and most litigated constitutional provision in Indian law.
