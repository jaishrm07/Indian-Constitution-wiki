## What happened

The Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act 1958 — commonly known as AFSPA — was enacted by Parliament on 11 September 1958, receiving Presidential assent and coming into force shortly thereafter. The Act applied initially to the Naga Hills and Tuensang Area of what was then Assam, where an armed insurgency led by the Naga National Council had been under way since the early 1950s. The Act was later extended to cover other parts of the north-east, and a separate version — the Armed Forces (Jammu and Kashmir) Special Powers Act 1990 — was enacted for that state during the insurgency of the 1990s.

Under the Act, once an area is declared "disturbed" by the Central Government or a state governor under Section 3, commissioned officers and non-commissioned officers of the armed forces are empowered, among other things, to use force even to the causing of death against any person acting in contravention of law or order; to arrest without warrant any person who has committed a cognisable offence or is reasonably suspected of having done so; to enter and search premises without a warrant; and to destroy any shelter used by armed volunteers or unlawful assemblies. Section 6 provides that persons arrested under the Act must be handed over to the nearest police station, but crucially, Section 7 confers near-absolute immunity from prosecution on members of the armed forces acting under the Act, requiring prior sanction of the Central Government for any prosecution.

## Constitutional basis and controversy

The Act drew its legislative competence primarily from Entries 1 and 2 of List I (Union List) of the Seventh Schedule — the defence of India and naval, military, and air forces — as well as Article 355 of the Constitution, which imposes a duty on the Union to protect every state against external aggression and internal disturbance. Defenders of the Act argued that armed insurgencies in the north-east constituted precisely the kind of internal disturbance that Article 355 contemplated, and that the Union's duty justified conferring special powers on the armed forces to discharge that obligation.

Critics argued that AFSPA's provisions were incompatible with the protections of Part III: the power to shoot to kill without judicial authorisation conflicted with the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21; arrest without warrant for extended periods raised questions under Article 22; and the immunity from prosecution in Section 7 made the Act's provisions practically unreviewable and unaccountable. The question of whether fundamental rights are suspended in "disturbed areas" — or merely that the state is permitted to restrict them — became a persistent jurisprudential issue.

## Judicial treatment and ongoing constitutional debate

The Supreme Court addressed AFSPA's validity directly in Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v. Union of India (1997), upholding the Act as constitutionally valid but laying down twelve binding guidelines for its exercise, including the requirement that the power to use lethal force be exercised only as a last resort, that the armed forces assist the civil administration rather than supplant it, and that grievances be redressed through a designated authority. The Court also held that the declaration of an area as "disturbed" was not entirely immune from judicial review, though the standard of review was narrow.

AFSPA remained one of the most constitutionally contested statutes in Indian law through the subsequent decades. The Supreme Court in Extra Judicial Execution Victim Families Association v. Union of India (2016) held that the armed forces could not claim absolute immunity under AFSPA for deaths caused in the course of operations and that all such deaths — whether in alleged encounters or otherwise — required independent investigation. This judgment significantly narrowed the practical scope of Section 7's immunity but did not disturb the Act's overall validity. AFSPA's continued operation has been the subject of periodic review by parliamentary committees and human rights bodies, and remains a central reference point in constitutional debates about emergency powers, civil liberties, and the use of military force within national territory.
