The 86th Amendment converted what had for fifty years been a Directive Principle aspiration into an enforceable fundamental right, and the RTE Act then built the institutional architecture through which that right could be claimed in practice.

## What happened

Article 45 of the Constitution, as originally enacted, directed the State to endeavour to provide free and compulsory education for all children until they completed the age of fourteen within ten years of the Constitution coming into force. This deadline passed unmet in 1960. Decades of litigation, including the Supreme Court's ruling in Unni Krishnan v. State of Andhra Pradesh in 1993 — which held that the right to education up to the age of fourteen was implicit in Article 21 — underscored the constitutional importance of primary education while leaving its enforcement uncertain. In 2002 Parliament enacted the Constitution (86th Amendment) Act, which inserted Article 21A as a new fundamental right, amended Article 45 to confine the Directive Principle obligation to early childhood care for children below six, and inserted a new clause in Article 51A making it a fundamental duty of parents and guardians to provide educational opportunities to their children aged six to fourteen. The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009, enacted under Article 21A, created the detailed framework: neighbourhood schools, pupil-teacher ratios, infrastructure norms, no-detention policy, and private school reservation obligations under the twenty-five per cent RTE quota.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The shift of primary education from Part IV to Part III was constitutionally significant because rights under Part III are justiciable — they can be enforced by courts under Articles 32 and 226 — whereas Directive Principles under Part IV are not directly enforceable as individual entitlements. The insertion of Article 21A gave every child between the ages of six and fourteen an individual constitutional right that could in principle be enforced against the State. The 2002 amendment also acknowledged the Unni Krishnan jurisprudence linking education to dignity under Article 21, while giving it an explicit textual home. The RTE Act's twenty-five per cent quota obligation on unaided private schools was later upheld in Society for Unaided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012), though with an exemption for unaided minority institutions under Article 30.

## Its constitutional legacy

Article 21A and the RTE Act created an enforceable architecture for primary education that did not previously exist. Enrolment rates improved substantially in the decade after 2009. The quota obligation on private schools became a significant constitutional controversy, tested in the Society for Unaided Private Schools judgment. The scope of the right — including whether children below six or above fourteen have enforceable claims — has been the subject of continuing litigation and policy debate. The amendment also illustrates a recurring constitutional pattern: a Directive Principle aspiration, once given constitutional urgency by judicial recognition in the Part III context, eventually migrates into Part III through formal amendment.
