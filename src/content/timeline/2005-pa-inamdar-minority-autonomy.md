The case remains a major reference point for the constitutional line between institutional autonomy and state control in professional education.

## What happened

P.A. Inamdar and connected cases came before a seven-judge Constitution Bench to resolve ambiguities that remained after T.M.A. Pai Foundation. The central questions concerned whether the State could impose compulsory reservations on private unaided professional institutions — whether minority or non-minority — by directing them to admit candidates from reserved categories as a condition of recognition or affiliation. The Bench, deciding the matter in August 2005, held that private unaided institutions — both minority and non-minority — cannot be compelled to accept State-determined reservation quotas. The State's power of regulation extends to ensuring transparency and merit in admissions, but not to dictating who must be admitted on the basis of the State's social policy objectives.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

P.A. Inamdar drew a firm constitutional line between regulation — which is permissible — and imposition of social policy burdens on private institutions that receive no state aid — which is not. The Court reasoned that a private institution uses its own resources and that compelling it to implement the State's reservation scheme without compensation or control would violate the institutional autonomy protected by Articles 19(1)(g) and 30. The decision thus placed significant limits on how far the State could use private educational capacity to achieve public reservation goals, a holding with major practical consequences given the large share of professional college seats in private hands.

## Its legacy

The P.A. Inamdar ruling prompted an almost immediate constitutional response. Parliament enacted the Ninety-Third Amendment to the Constitution, inserting clause (5) into Article 15, which specifically enabled the State to make special provisions — including reservations — in private unaided educational institutions, except for minority institutions under Article 30. This amendment was challenged and upheld by the Supreme Court in the Ashoka Kumar Thakur case. P.A. Inamdar thus has the distinction of being a judgment whose central holding was overridden by constitutional amendment within months, though it continues to define the autonomy of private unaided minority institutions which remain outside the scope of Article 15(5).
