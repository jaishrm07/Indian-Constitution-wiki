## What happened

Sajjan Singh and other petitioners challenged the Constitution (Seventeenth Amendment) Act 1964, which had inserted forty-four state Acts — primarily land acquisition and tenancy legislation — into the Ninth Schedule to the Constitution. Entry in the Ninth Schedule, by virtue of Article 31B, placed these Acts beyond challenge under any of the fundamental rights in Part III. The challenge argued that the Seventeenth Amendment was invalid because Parliament could not use its amending power under Article 368 to insert laws into the Ninth Schedule in a manner that effectively nullified fundamental rights.

The Supreme Court, by a majority of three to two, dismissed the challenge and upheld the Seventeenth Amendment. Chief Justice P.B. Gajendragadkar, writing for the majority, applied the earlier decision in Shankari Prasad v. Union of India (1951) and held that the word "law" in Article 13(2) — which prohibits the state from making any law that abridges fundamental rights — did not include a constitutional amendment. Article 368 gave Parliament a plenary power to amend any provision of the Constitution, including provisions in Part III, and a constitutional amendment could not be invalidated on the ground that it curtailed fundamental rights.

## The dissents that foreshadowed Golaknath

The significance of Sajjan Singh lies as much in its two dissenting opinions as in the majority's holding. Justice J.R. Mudholkar dissented on the ground that the word "amendment" in Article 368 might not extend to the power to abridge or abolish fundamental rights, and raised the question of whether there might be certain "basic features" of the Constitution that were beyond the amending power. This was the first time a Supreme Court judge had articulated what would eventually become the basic structure doctrine. Justice M. Hidayatullah also dissented, questioning whether the amending power extended to removing the entire content of fundamental rights from judicial enforceability through the Ninth Schedule mechanism.

These dissents were the direct intellectual precursors of the majority opinion in Golaknath v. State of Punjab, decided just under three years later in February 1967, and of the basic structure doctrine fully articulated in Kesavananda Bharati in 1973. The pattern of a majority reaffirming Parliament's unlimited amending power over a dissent questioning that premise — which had also appeared in Shankari Prasad — was reversed in Golaknath, where the sceptical position became the majority view of an eleven-judge bench.

## Constitutional significance

Sajjan Singh is a transitional case in the history of Indian constitutional law. It represents the last affirmation by a majority of the Supreme Court of the position — established in Shankari Prasad and maintained consistently since 1951 — that Parliament's amending power was unlimited with respect to fundamental rights. It also illustrates the Ninth Schedule mechanism's continued expansion: the Seventeenth Amendment inserted more laws than any previous amendment to the Schedule, reflecting Parliament's desire to place an ever-larger body of agrarian reform legislation beyond judicial scrutiny.

The case also crystallised the functional tension between Articles 31A, 31B, and the Ninth Schedule on one side, and the enforceability of fundamental rights on the other. The majority's reasoning in Sajjan Singh treated the amending power as a sovereign constituent power that could be exercised without substantive limits, a position that the Supreme Court majority in Golaknath would repudiate in dramatic terms. Sajjan Singh thus stands at the end of one constitutional era and at the threshold of another, its dissenters having written the jurisprudential foundation on which the subsequent decade of constitutional transformation would be built.
