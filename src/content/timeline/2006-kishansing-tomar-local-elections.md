This is the leading local-government election case and a key authority on the timing and independence of State Election Commissions.

## What happened

The Kishansing Tomar case arose from the failure of Gujarat to hold timely elections to municipal bodies after the expiry of their terms. The matter came before the Supreme Court, which examined the constitutional obligation imposed by the Seventy-Fourth Amendment — specifically the provisions on mandatory periodic elections, the role of the State Election Commission, and the constitutional scheme requiring that local bodies not remain without elected representation for extended periods. The Court held in October 2006 that the State Election Commission has a constitutional duty to set the election schedule and conduct elections without delay, and that the state government cannot frustrate this obligation by withholding cooperation or administrative support.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Kishansing Tomar gave practical teeth to the constitutional guarantee of periodic elections in local bodies. Articles 243K and 243U require that the State Election Commission superintend, direct, and control local body elections and that fresh elections be held before the expiry of the term or, if a body is dissolved, within six months. The Court held that these provisions are mandatory and enforceable, not merely aspirational, and that the independence of the State Election Commission in conducting elections without executive interference is constitutionally protected on the same basis as the Election Commission of India's independence in national and state elections.

## Its legacy

The judgment became the standard reference for courts and litigants seeking to compel timely local body elections when state governments delayed them, as occurred repeatedly in states including Tamil Nadu, Rajasthan, and Madhya Pradesh. The principle that the constitutional mandate of periodicity cannot be defeated by executive inaction or administrative pretext — and that courts can and should intervene to secure elections — was confirmed and applied in subsequent decisions. Kishansing Tomar thus anchors the right to local democratic representation as an enforceable constitutional entitlement, not a matter of executive grace.
