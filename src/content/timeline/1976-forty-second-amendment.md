The Forty-Second Amendment is the high-water mark of executive ambition over the constitutional text. Understanding it is essential for understanding why Minerva Mills, the Forty-Fourth Amendment, and the basic structure doctrine matter.

## What happened

The Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act 1976 received the President's assent on 18 November 1976, during the period of Internal Emergency that had been proclaimed on 26 June 1975. It was the product of the Swaran Singh Committee's recommendations, which the Indira Gandhi government used as a blueprint for the most extensive single round of constitutional amendment India has seen. The amendment contained over fifty clauses modifying the Preamble, Articles, and Schedules. Parliament, whose term had been extended by one year under emergency powers, enacted it with the ruling party's overwhelming majority.

The changes fell into several broad categories. The Preamble was amended to insert the words "socialist" and "secular" between "sovereign" and "democratic," and "unity and integrity" was substituted for "unity" in its closing phrase. Ten subjects, including education, forests, weights and measures, and protection of wild animals, were moved from the State List to the Concurrent List, substantially centralising legislative competence. New Directive Principles under Articles 39A, 43A, and 48A were inserted relating to equal justice, workers' participation in management, and environmental protection. Article 31C was expanded to protect any law that claimed to implement any Directive Principle, removing the requirement that the law had to relate specifically to Articles 39(b) and (c). Fundamental duties of citizens were inserted through a new Part IVA comprising Article 51A.

The most constitutionally significant changes concerned the relationship between Parliament and the courts. New clauses were inserted into Article 368 to declare that there was no limitation on Parliament's constituent power and that no constitutional amendment, for any reason, could be questioned in any court. High Courts' power to issue writs of the kind enumerated in Article 226 was curtailed in relation to central laws. A new chapter on "Tribunals" was inserted under Articles 323A and 323B with provision for administrative and other tribunals to replace High Court jurisdiction. Provisions for special courts to try offences connected with the preservation of democracy were added. The period within which a proclamation under Article 356 required parliamentary approval was extended. The amendment also inserted Article 31D protecting laws related to anti-national activities from fundamental rights challenges.

## Constitutional significance

The Forty-Second Amendment was a systematic effort to alter the balance of the constitutional order in favour of Parliament — and more specifically the executive that commanded Parliament's majority. The amendment to Article 368 was its most aggressive move: by inserting provisions declaring that no constitutional amendment could ever be questioned in court, it attempted to eliminate the basic structure doctrine that the Supreme Court had announced in Kesavananda Bharati three years earlier. If the amendment had been allowed to stand, it would have effectively made Parliament the master of the Constitution rather than its creature. The expansion of Article 31C would have similarly placed any "socialist" legislation outside the reach of fundamental rights challenges, regardless of how it was framed.

The changes to the Concurrent List and the curtailment of High Court writ jurisdiction altered the federal structure and reduced the states' legislative autonomy in significant areas. The insertion of Fundamental Duties under Article 51A, while normatively defensible, was used at the time as a rhetorical counterweight to individual rights and as a justification for laws restricting civil liberties. The amendment thus operated on multiple planes simultaneously — altering the Preamble's identity-statement, shifting legislative competences, expanding directive principles, curtailing judicial power, and attempting to insulate itself from review.

## Long-term impact

The Forty-Second Amendment lasted in its most constitutionally controversial provisions only until the Supreme Court addressed them. In Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980), a five-judge Constitution Bench struck down the Article 368 clauses claiming unlimited amendment power, holding that they violated the basic structure of the Constitution. The Court held that limited amending power was itself a basic feature, and that Parliament could not bootstrap unlimited power by amending the amendment procedure. The expanded Article 31C was similarly struck down as going beyond what Kesavananda had permitted. The Forty-Fourth Amendment of 1978 had already reversed several other Emergency-era changes, removing the provision for special courts and restoring a degree of High Court jurisdiction.

The insertions that survived have had their own long trajectories. "Socialist" and "secular" in the Preamble have been the subject of ongoing litigation, with the Supreme Court in various cases reading both terms as confirming a constitutional commitment to economic equity and state neutrality in religion respectively. The Fundamental Duties provision has been used by courts as an interpretive tool in environmental and other cases, though it creates no independently enforceable rights. The addition of education to the Concurrent List facilitated the Right to Education Act 2009 and several subsequent central legislative interventions in schooling. The Forty-Second Amendment thus leaves a complex legacy: its most aggressive provisions were reversed, but its formal contributions to the constitutional text — including the Preamble's current wording, the fundamental duties, and the directive principles — remain.
