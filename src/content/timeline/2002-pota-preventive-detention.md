POTA illustrates the recurring tension in Indian constitutional law between the emergency and security powers of the state and the fundamental rights guarantees of Part III. It was the most contested preventive detention legislation since the Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act (TADA) lapsed in 1995, and its repeal in 2004 marked the end of a particular phase in India's counter-terrorism legal architecture.

## What happened

After TADA lapsed in 1995 following sustained criticism of its misuse, India had no central anti-terrorism statute for nearly six years. Following the attacks of 11 September 2001 in the United States and the attack on Parliament House in New Delhi on 13 December 2001, the National Democratic Alliance government promulgated the Prevention of Terrorism Ordinance in October 2001, which lapsed when the Rajya Sabha refused to pass the corresponding bill. The government then passed a Rajya Sabha resolution under Article 249 — which permits the Council of States to resolve that Parliament may legislate on a state-list subject in the national interest — to invoke Parliament's power over a subject otherwise in the State List. The Prevention of Terrorism Act, 2002, received presidential assent on 28 March 2002.

The Act defined terrorism broadly, allowed police custody for up to 30 days (extendable to 180 days before bail could be sought), made confessions before a police officer of the rank of Superintendent of Police or above admissible as evidence, and shifted the burden of proof in certain circumstances onto the accused. Special courts were established with procedures that reduced the accused's access to documents in the interest of witness protection. The Act applied throughout India and empowered both central and state governments to notify organisations as terrorist organisations.

## Constitutional significance

POTA tested the outer limits of Article 22's preventive detention safeguards and Article 21's guarantee of a fair procedure. Article 22 sets minimum procedural protections for persons arrested and detained — including the right to be informed of grounds of arrest, the right to consult a legal practitioner, and production before a magistrate within 24 hours. Preventive detention laws may derogate from some of these protections but are subject to the requirements in Article 22(4) to (7), including a maximum detention period without reference to an Advisory Board. POTA's extended custody and bail-denial provisions pushed against the boundaries of these safeguards.

The admissibility of police confessions was particularly controversial. Since the Code of Criminal Procedure bars confessions to police officers as evidence in ordinary criminal trials — a protection designed to prevent coercive interrogation — POTA's carve-out raised a direct Article 21 challenge. The Supreme Court in People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India upheld several provisions of POTA while reading down others, requiring the confession provisions to be applied strictly and insisting that courts scrutinise claims of coercion. The Court held that POTA did not, on its face, violate Articles 14, 19, 21, or 22, but acknowledged that its misuse could attract constitutional challenge in individual cases.

## Long-term impact

POTA was repealed by the United Progressive Alliance government in 2004, shortly after it came to power. The repeal was partly driven by evidence of serious misuse — POTA had been invoked against political opponents, minorities, and activists in multiple states. The constitutional and political controversy around POTA shaped the design of its successor legislation, the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act as amended in 2004 and 2008, which sought to address some of the due-process concerns while retaining extended custody and bail-denial provisions. The POTA episode reinforced the established constitutional principle — running from the A.K. Gopalan era through Maneka Gandhi to the present — that national security cannot serve as a blank cheque for executive detention, and that the courts must scrutinise the procedural fairness of each statutory scheme under Article 21.
