The simultaneous creation of three new states in November 2000 was the most substantial exercise of Parliament's Article 3 power since the linguistic reorganisation of 1956. It demonstrated both the constitutional flexibility of the federal structure and the limits of the affected states' ability to resist or shape the reorganisation.

## What happened

Demands for separate statehood in the tribal and underdeveloped regions of three large states had existed for decades. In Bihar, the Jharkhand movement — centred on the mineral-rich Chota Nagpur plateau and Santhal Parganas — had roots going back to the late colonial period, with activists arguing that the tribal and Adivasi communities of the region had been economically marginalised under a Bihari administration. In Uttar Pradesh, the hill districts of Garhwal and Kumaon had long sought separation from the plains-dominated government in Lucknow, producing a sustained Uttarakhand (or Uttaranchal) agitation that turned violent in 1994 with the Muzaffarnagar killings of protesters. In Madhya Pradesh, the Chhattisgarh movement drew on the distinct cultural and linguistic identity of the Chhattisgarhi-speaking communities and their sense of marginalisation within MP's political economy.

The National Democratic Alliance government under Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee moved the bills in 2000. The Madhya Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2000, carved out Chhattisgarh from the southeastern portion of Madhya Pradesh; it came into force on 1 November 2000. The Uttar Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2000, separated the hill and sub-Himalayan districts to form Uttaranchal (renamed Uttarakhand in 2007); it came into force on 9 November 2000. The Bihar Reorganisation Act, 2000, created Jharkhand from the southern districts of Bihar; it came into force on 15 November 2000. In each case, the affected state legislature was required under the proviso to Article 3 to express its views on the proposed bill within a time specified by the President, but its concurrence was not legally required.

## Constitutional significance

Article 3 of the Constitution confers on Parliament the power to form new states, increase or diminish the area of any state, alter its boundaries or name. The only procedural constraint in the proviso is that a bill affecting a state must be referred to the legislature of that state, which must be given an opportunity to express its views within a specified period — after which Parliament may proceed regardless of whether the state legislature concurred. This makes India's federal structure constitutionally asymmetric: the central legislature can unilaterally alter the territorial composition of the federation without the consent of the units concerned.

The 2000 reorganisations confirmed this asymmetry in practice. The Bihar legislature, in particular, was deeply reluctant to cede Jharkhand and passed a resolution opposing the bill; Parliament proceeded nonetheless. The Sarkaria Commission (1988) had recommended that Parliament should give due weight to state views on reorganisation, but this recommendation has no binding legal force under Article 3. Article 4 further provides that laws made under Articles 2 and 3 shall not be deemed to be amendments for purposes of Article 368, meaning that Parliament may reshape the federation through ordinary legislation without the special majority requirements for constitutional amendments.

The creation of Jharkhand also engaged Article 244 and the Fifth Schedule, which govern the administration of Scheduled Areas. Large parts of the new state fell within Scheduled Areas, and the reorganisation triggered questions about continuity of tribal self-governance rights, the application of the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act 1996 (PESA), and the administrative responsibilities of the newly constituted state government toward its tribal population.

## Long-term impact

The three new states quickly established their own constitutional trajectories. Uttarakhand became constitutionally notable for being one of the smaller states most frequently subject to political instability and central intervention — including the President's Rule imposition in 2016 that reached the Supreme Court and produced an important ruling on floor tests and constitutional governance. Jharkhand's governance challenges — including recurring political instability and questions about the administration of Scheduled Areas — gave continuing prominence to Fifth Schedule jurisprudence. Chhattisgarh became the site of Salwa Judum, a state-sponsored vigilante programme challenged before the Supreme Court in Nandini Sundar v. State of Chhattisgarh (2011), where the Court held that arming civilians to combat Naxalites violated Articles 14 and 21. The 2000 reorganisations remain the last major exercise of Article 3 powers, and the continuing demands for statehood from regions such as Vidarbha and Bodoland keep the constitutional question of India's federal reorganisation power politically live.
