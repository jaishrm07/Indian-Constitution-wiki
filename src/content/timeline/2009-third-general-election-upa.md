## What happened

The fifteenth general election was held in five phases between April and May 2009. The Indian National Congress-led United Progressive Alliance won 262 seats in a House of 543, with the Congress itself winning 206 seats — its best performance since 1991. The Bharatiya Janata Party-led National Democratic Alliance won 159 seats. The UPA formed a government with external support from smaller regional parties, giving the coalition a working majority without the formal Left Front support that had constrained the first UPA government between 2004 and 2008. Dr Manmohan Singh was sworn in for a second term as Prime Minister on 22 May 2009, the first Prime Minister since Jawaharlal Nehru to return to office after completing a full term. The relative clarity of the result meant that President Pratibha Patil had no occasion to exercise any discretion in the invitation to form a government.

## Constitutional significance

The constitutional dimensions of the 2009 result are best understood against the backdrop of the period from 1989 to 2004, during which no single party or pre-election coalition commanded a stable Lok Sabha majority and the office of the President became an active site of constitutional controversy. The conventions governing the President's discretion to invite a party leader, to impose conditions on that invitation, and to call a floor test had to be worked out in real time through episodes including the 1996 invitation to Atal Bihari Vajpayee, the sequence of coalition formations that followed, and the 1999 no-confidence vote. A more decisive electoral mandate in 2009 meant that the President's role in government formation reverted to its constitutional default: a ceremonial act following an obvious political result. Articles 74 and 75, which vest executive authority in the Council of Ministers and require ministerial advice to the President, function most smoothly when the Lok Sabha arithmetic is unambiguous. The 2009 result also forestalled the political conditions under which Article 356 proclamations tend to be deployed at the state level, since a governmentally stable Centre is less incentivised to destabilise states held by opposition parties.

## Long-term impact

The 2009 election became a reference point for scholars of Indian constitutionalism who argued that the parliamentary system's core assumptions — collective Cabinet responsibility, Prime Ministerial authority, and the supremacy of the elected executive over constitutional offices — are easier to sustain under majority or near-majority governments than under ideologically incoherent coalitions. The coalition period from 1989 to 2004 had produced a series of constitutional practices that stretched or tested the text of Articles 74, 75, and 356, including the use of the Sarkaria Commission report as guidance for President's rule and the Supreme Court's intervention in S.R. Bommai to impose judicial limits on Article 356 after years of its political misuse. The UPA's second term, operating with less internal coalition pressure than its first, demonstrated that constitutional conventions around stable government could be observed more easily when electoral outcomes were relatively clear — a lesson that bore on subsequent debates about electoral reform, coalition management, and the formal and informal rules governing presidential discretion in hung-Parliament situations.
