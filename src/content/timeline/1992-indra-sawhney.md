Indra Sawhney is the foundational constitutional text for reservation policy in India. Its doctrinal holdings on the ceiling, the creamy layer, and promotion reservations remain the starting point for every major backward-class dispute four decades on.

## What happened

The Mandal Commission, appointed under Article 340, had in 1980 recommended 27 per cent reservations in central government services for Other Backward Classes. When the V.P. Singh government announced implementation in August 1990, widespread litigation followed. Nine petitions were consolidated before a nine-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court. The Court delivered its judgment in November 1992. By a majority it upheld the 27 per cent OBC reservation as constitutionally valid under Article 16(4). It simultaneously struck down an office memorandum that had sought to extend reservations to economically weaker sections among forward castes, holding that economic criterion alone could not satisfy the constitutional test for backwardness under Article 16(4). The Court also imposed a ceiling of 50 per cent on total reservations in any cadre, to be exceeded only in rare and extraordinary circumstances, and held that the more prosperous sections — the "creamy layer" — of OBC groups must be excluded from reservation benefits.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment resolved a fundamental tension in the equality provisions. Article 16(1) guarantees equality of opportunity in public employment; Article 16(4) permits reservations for any backward class of citizens not adequately represented in State services. The Court clarified that these two clauses operate as a composite scheme, and that the permitted departure from formal equality under clause (4) cannot swallow the rule in clause (1) — hence the ceiling and the creamy layer. The majority also held that reservations in promotions were not covered by Article 16(4) as it then stood, a holding that prompted Parliament to enact the seventy-seventh and subsequent amendments to create Article 16(4A) and Article 16(4B) to restore such reservations.

## Its constitutional legacy

Indra Sawhney set the terms for all subsequent reservation litigation. Every major dispute — sub-classification within OBC groups, the Jat inclusion controversy, the Maratha reservation quota challenged in Jaishri Laxmanrao Patil, the 103rd Amendment creating EWS reservations — is resolved by direct engagement with this ruling. The creamy layer principle has been extended to SC and ST promotion reservations in M. Nagaraj and Jarnail Singh. The 50 per cent ceiling has been tested, explained, and maintained through Janhit Abhiyan. The judgment's doctrinal architecture has proved more durable than the political controversy that gave rise to it.
