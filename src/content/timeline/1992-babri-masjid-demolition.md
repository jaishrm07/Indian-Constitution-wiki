The Babri Masjid demolition is one of the defining constitutional crises of post-independence India. It tested the State's obligation to protect minority religious rights, the Centre's power to intervene in states under Article 355, and the reviewability of Presidential proclamations under Article 356.

## What happened

On 6 December 1992, a large assembly of kar sevaks — religious volunteers affiliated with the Ram Janmabhoomi movement — demolished the sixteenth-century Babri Masjid at the disputed site in Ayodhya, Uttar Pradesh, in breach of undertakings given to the Supreme Court by the State Government that the structure would be protected. The demolition triggered communal riots across India, killing over two thousand people. In the immediate aftermath, the Union Government under Prime Minister P.V. Narasimha Rao dissolved the BJP-governed state governments in Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Himachal Pradesh, imposing President's Rule under Article 356 on the grounds that constitutional governance had broken down in those states. The Central Bureau of Investigation registered criminal cases against political and religious leaders for their roles in the demolition.

## Constitutional significance

The events of 6 December 1992 raised foundational questions under Articles 25 and 26, which guarantee the freedom of conscience and the right to manage religious affairs. The demolition of a mosque by a private mob with the acquiescence — and in some assessments the active encouragement — of state authorities raised the question of whether the State had discharged its positive obligation to protect minority religious freedoms. The Supreme Court had previously held, in the Shirur Mutt line of cases, that religious freedom encompasses the right to maintain and use places of worship as a core aspect of religious practice. The demolition of an active mosque starkly exposed the gap between constitutional text and state action.

The imposition of President's Rule in four states simultaneously became the central constitutional controversy. The dismissed state governments challenged the proclamations before various High Courts. This litigation consolidated into S.R. Bommai v. Union of India, decided by a nine-judge Constitution Bench in March 1994. The Court used the opportunity not only to constrain future use of Article 356 by requiring judicial review of Presidential satisfaction, but also to hold — in a set of powerful concurring opinions — that secularism is a basic feature of the Constitution, and that a state government whose conduct is fundamentally anti-secular could in principle face dismissal under Article 356, subject to the same standards of judicial review.

## Long-term impact

The Babri Masjid demolition set in motion constitutional and legal consequences that took decades to resolve. The land title dispute proceeded to trial in the Allahabad High Court, whose 2010 judgment partitioning the disputed site was appealed to the Supreme Court. The Supreme Court's landmark judgment in M. Siddiq v. Mahant Suresh Das (2019) awarded the entire disputed site to the Ram Janmabhoomi deity and directed allotment of a separate five-acre plot for the mosque. Criminal proceedings against those accused of conspiracy and incitement in the demolition were revived after the Supreme Court set aside an earlier discharge order, and a Special CBI Court ultimately delivered its verdict in 2020. The events of 1992 also catalysed the Liberhan Commission inquiry, which submitted its report in 2009 after seventeen years of proceedings. The demolition accelerated judicial elaboration of secularism as a constitutional value with substantive, enforceable content — a doctrinal development that has shaped Indian constitutional law ever since.
