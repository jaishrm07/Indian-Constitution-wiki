## What happened

India's second general elections to the Lok Sabha and state legislative assemblies were held in February and March 1957. The Election Commission, now led by Chief Election Commissioner Sukumar Sen for the last time before his retirement, administered the polls across more than 500 constituencies. Approximately 193 million voters were enrolled under Article 326, an increase over the first general election, reflecting population growth and continued expansion of electoral rolls. The Indian National Congress won 371 of 494 Lok Sabha seats, securing a commanding majority that returned Jawaharlal Nehru as Prime Minister for a second term. Voter turnout was approximately 47 per cent.

Among the notable electoral results, B.R. Ambedkar, who had resigned from the Cabinet in 1951 and formed the Scheduled Castes Federation (later the Republican Party of India), contested from Bombay North but was defeated. His loss underscored the challenge that minority-based political formations faced within the first-past-the-post system under Article 81. The Communist Party of India made significant gains in Kerala, going on to form the first elected Communist state government in the world under E.M.S. Namboodiripad — a constitutional milestone in the operation of India's federal parliamentary design under Articles 163 and 164.

## Constitutional significance

The second general election was constitutionally significant as confirmation rather than inauguration. The first election in 1951–52 had demonstrated that universal adult franchise was administratively feasible; the second election demonstrated that the constitutional framework could produce peaceful transfers of mandate and institutional renewal. The formation of a Communist government in Kerala under the parliamentary system tested the constitutional scheme's flexibility: a state government holding views fundamentally at odds with Congress ideology nevertheless operated within the constitutional framework until its dismissal under Article 356 in 1959, which itself became a significant moment in centre–state constitutional relations.

The election also reinforced the institutional position of the Election Commission under Article 324 as an independent constitutional body capable of administering complex multi-phase elections without direction from the executive. The smooth repetition of the electoral process — on an even larger scale than 1952 — helped settle the question, which had been live in some quarters, of whether India's constitutional democracy was a temporary arrangement contingent on charismatic leadership rather than a self-sustaining institutional order.

## Long-term impact

The 1957 election's outcomes had direct consequences for constitutional development. The Communist government in Kerala demonstrated that the constitutional framework permitted significant ideological diversity in state governance, but also that Article 356 could be deployed by the Centre when politically convenient — a use that the Supreme Court would not scrutinise closely until S.R. Bommai v. Union of India in 1994. The election also deepened the Congress party's dominance in a manner that shaped constitutional politics for the following decade, with Parliament's large majorities enabling the series of constitutional amendments — from the Seventh to the Seventeenth — that characterised the period between 1956 and 1964.
