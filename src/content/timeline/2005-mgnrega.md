MGNREGA is the largest employment guarantee programme in the world and one of the most significant instances of parliamentary legislation giving concrete shape to rights that the Supreme Court had derived from Article 21. It transformed the relationship between the rural poor and the constitutional right to life by placing an individual legal entitlement to work at the centre of social welfare law.

## What happened

The National Rural Employment Guarantee Act was enacted on 7 September 2005 and came into force initially in two hundred districts on 2 February 2006, before being extended to all rural districts of India by 2008. The Act (later renamed the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act) created a framework under which every rural household is entitled to at least one hundred days of unskilled manual employment per financial year at the statutory minimum wage. The entitlement is demand-driven: the household applies for work, the State must provide employment within fifteen days of the application, and if it fails to do so it must pay an unemployment allowance. The programme is funded primarily by the Central Government, administered through gram panchayats, and subject to social audit requirements designed to ensure transparency and accountability.

The Act was a centrepiece of the United Progressive Alliance government's first term under Prime Minister Manmohan Singh and was driven in significant part by advocacy from economists and civil society organisations who had documented the failure of earlier rural public works programmes to reach the poorest households with reliable employment.

## Constitutional significance

The Act's constitutional importance operates on several levels. First, it gives statutory content to the right to livelihood that the Supreme Court had recognised as part of Article 21 in Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985), where the Court held that the right to life encompasses the right to means of livelihood, and that any law or executive action depriving a person of their livelihood must satisfy the requirements of Articles 21 and 14. MGNREGA translated this judicial recognition into a positive, justiciable entitlement: a rural household can now enforce the right to work not merely through the uncertain path of public interest litigation but through the Act's own grievance redressal and compensation mechanism.

Second, the Act provides legislative implementation of Directive Principles under Articles 41 and 43. Article 41 directs the State to make effective provision for securing the right to work within the limits of its economic capacity and development; Article 43 directs that the State endeavour to secure living wages and conditions of work ensuring a decent standard of life to workers. MGNREGA does not merely aspire to these goals but creates legally binding duties on the State at every administrative level — from the gram panchayat to the Union Government — that can be enforced in court. This is constitutionally significant because Directive Principles are ordinarily non-justiciable, and the legislature's conversion of an Article 41 obligation into an enforceable statutory right exemplifies the constitutional design of Part IV as a guide for legislative action.

Third, MGNREGA's social audit provisions and transparency requirements reinforce Article 19(1)(a) and the broader constitutional value of open government. The Act requires gram sabhas to conduct social audits of the programme's implementation and mandates public display of records, institutionalising a form of participatory accountability that complements the formal statutory right.

## Long-term impact

MGNREGA has generated an important body of constitutional litigation. The programme's linkage to Aadhaar-based wage payment systems raised Article 21 concerns when biometric failures led to payment denials — a contestation the Supreme Court addressed in the Puttaswamy right to privacy judgment (2017) and in subsequent cases on Aadhaar's application to welfare entitlements. The guarantee's enforceability has been tested before High Courts in cases where states failed to provide employment or pay unemployment allowances. The act demonstrated that a statutory right to livelihood, properly constructed with individual entitlements and compensation for breach, is not merely a policy tool but a constitutional instrument — and its design has influenced subsequent rights-based legislation including the National Food Security Act 2013.
