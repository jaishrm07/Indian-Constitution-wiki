This judgment remains central whenever constitutional debate turns to defections, Speaker authority, or legislative survival.

## What happened

After the Fifty-Second Amendment inserted the Tenth Schedule in 1985, its constitutional validity was immediately challenged on multiple grounds. Kihoto Hollohan and others argued that the schedule violated democratic principles, that vesting adjudicatory power in the Speaker was contrary to natural justice, and that certain provisions had not been ratified by state legislatures as required for constitutional amendments affecting the powers of states. A five-judge Constitution Bench decided the matter in 1992. The Court upheld the overall validity of the Tenth Schedule and confirmed Parliament's power to enact an anti-defection mechanism, but struck down paragraph 7, which had sought to exclude all judicial review, for want of the required state ratification.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Kihoto Hollohan resolved whether the anti-defection framework itself was constitutionally sound, affirming that Parliament could constitutionally tie legislative membership to party loyalty. By striking paragraph 7, the Court preserved the principle that constitutional adjudication by the Speaker is not wholly immune from review — courts could step in where there was patent illegality, perversity, or a breach of constitutional mandate. The ruling also confirmed that a member's right to vote according to conscience is modified by the Tenth Schedule, resolving a significant doctrinal tension between individual representative freedom and collective party discipline.

## Its legacy

The judgment defined the terms on which Speaker decisions on disqualification would be tested for decades. The continued practice of partisan Speakers delaying or favouring particular disqualification outcomes prompted the Supreme Court to revisit the question repeatedly, including in the Nabam Rebia case and later in the Shiv Sena dispute, with courts often finding that Kihoto's residual judicial review window needed to be kept open and meaningful. The case remains the essential constitutional authority for the architecture and limits of anti-defection law.
