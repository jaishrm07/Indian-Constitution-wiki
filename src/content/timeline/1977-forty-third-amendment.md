The Forty-Third Amendment is less celebrated than the Forty-Fourth but represents the first direct legislative reversal of Emergency-era constitutional engineering. It signals the constitutional system's capacity for self-correction when democratic control changes hands.

## What happened

The Constitution (Forty-Third Amendment) Act 1977 was enacted on 13 April 1977 by the Janata government that had come to power after the landslide general elections of March 1977 — the first elections held after the lifting of the Emergency proclaimed in June 1975. The Janata coalition, which included parties across the political spectrum united primarily by opposition to the Emergency, moved quickly to repair the constitutional damage inflicted by the Forty-Second Amendment. The Forty-Third Amendment was the first step; the more comprehensive Forty-Fourth Amendment followed in 1978.

The Forty-Third Amendment made two principal changes. First, it deleted the provisions inserted by the Forty-Second Amendment that had provided for the establishment of special courts to try offences relating to the preservation of democratic institutions and other matters. These courts had been established under Emergency conditions and were seen as instruments of political prosecution rather than ordinary justice. Their constitutional removal was a symbolic and practical statement that the new government would not use extra-ordinary judicial machinery against political opponents.

Second, the amendment deleted the clause inserted by the Forty-Second Amendment into Article 226 that had curtailed High Courts' power to declare central laws unconstitutional. Under the Forty-Second Amendment, a High Court hearing a challenge to a central law had been stripped of the power to declare that law unconstitutional unless the matter had first been referred to the Supreme Court. This had created a structural hierarchy that significantly weakened the High Courts as sites of constitutional review, requiring litigants to go through an additional procedural step that increased the time and cost of constitutional litigation. The Forty-Third Amendment restored the position to what it had been before 1976: High Courts could hear and decide constitutional challenges to central laws directly.

## Constitutional significance

The restoration of High Court review power was constitutionally significant because High Courts are the primary interface between citizens and constitutional rights across India's vast territory. Centralising constitutional review of central laws exclusively in the Supreme Court — even as a first-filter requirement — would have made constitutional remedies practically inaccessible for most litigants. The Forty-Second Amendment's curtailment had been understood, correctly, as part of a broader strategy to insulate central government action from judicial scrutiny at accessible forums.

The deletion of the special courts provision addressed a different but related concern: that the Emergency government had been constructing a parallel judicial architecture tailored to its political requirements. Constitutional democracy requires that the ordinary courts — High Courts and the Supreme Court — remain the exclusive repositories of judicial power in constitutional matters, with any departure requiring compelling justification. The Forty-Third Amendment's removal of the special courts clause signalled that the new government regarded the earlier provision as incompatible with that principle.

The amendment also mattered as a constitutional precedent. It demonstrated that Parliament's power to amend under Article 368 includes the power to undo what a previous Parliament had done — that amendments are not irreversible even when they have been constitutionally enacted. This reinforced the understanding that the Constitution is a living document subject to correction through democratic processes, not merely one capable of accumulating changes in a single direction.

## Long-term impact

The Forty-Third and Forty-Fourth Amendments together constitute the principal legislative response to the Emergency's constitutional lessons. By restoring High Court jurisdiction over central laws, the Forty-Third Amendment helped preserve the decentralised constitutional review structure that has been essential to the volume and accessibility of constitutional litigation in India. The fifty-plus High Courts across the country continue to be the first forum for most constitutional challenges, with appeals lying to the Supreme Court — a structure that would have been significantly impaired if the Forty-Second Amendment's centralisation had remained.

The removal of the special courts provision has particular resonance in retrospect. Later governments have periodically proposed similar extra-ordinary tribunals or courts for particular categories of cases, and the Forty-Third Amendment's rejection of that architecture under the Forty-Second Amendment has been invoked in debates about the proper institutional design of adjudication. The amendments enacted by the Janata government in 1977 and 1978 remain the clearest examples of the constitutional system using its amendment procedure to repair itself after democratic breakdown, and they have shaped subsequent thinking about the relationship between ordinary democratic politics, constitutional amendment, and judicial independence.
