## What happened

On the night of 25 to 26 June 1975, President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed, acting on the Cabinet advice of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, proclaimed a national emergency under Article 352 on the ground of internal disturbance. The immediate political context was the Allahabad High Court's judgment of 12 June 1975, which had set aside Indira Gandhi's election on grounds of corrupt practice, and the Supreme Court's conditional stay granted by Justice V.R. Krishna Iyer, which permitted her to remain Prime Minister but barred her from voting in Parliament pending appeal.

The proclamation suspended the right to move courts for enforcement of fundamental rights under Articles 14, 19, and 21 through an order under Article 359. Thousands of political opponents, journalists, and activists were detained under the Maintenance of Internal Security Act and other preventive detention laws. Press censorship was imposed. The Constitution was amended multiple times during the Emergency — the Thirty-Eighth, Thirty-Ninth, and Forty-Second Amendments — in attempts to entrench executive power, curtail judicial review, and immunise emergency governance from constitutional challenge.

## Constitutional significance

The Emergency exposed fundamental vulnerabilities in the Constitution's design. The Supreme Court, in ADM Jabalpur v. Shivakant Shukla (1976), held that during a declared emergency with fundamental rights suspended, no court could examine whether a detention was lawful or issue a writ of habeas corpus. Only Justice H.R. Khanna dissented, holding that the right to life could not be suspended. This decision, subsequently condemned as a failure of judicial independence and later formally repudiated by the Supreme Court in K.S. Puttaswamy (2017), demonstrated that formal constitutional protections depend for their effectiveness on the institutional courage of courts and the political culture of the ruling party.

The Emergency also demonstrated that the emergency provisions in Part XVIII were capable of being weaponised against constitutional democracy itself — the very opposite of their intended purpose as crisis management tools.

## Long-term impact

The Emergency's direct constitutional legacy was the Forty-Fourth Amendment of 1978, enacted by the Janata government that came to power after the Emergency's end. The amendment tightened the conditions for proclaiming an emergency, required written Cabinet approval before any proclamation, substituted "armed rebellion" for "internal disturbance" as a valid ground, and made suspension of Articles 20 and 21 impossible even during emergency. The Supreme Court's ADM Jabalpur decision was repudiated by the Court itself in K.S. Puttaswamy. The Emergency remains the defining stress test against which every subsequent discussion of constitutional resilience, judicial independence, and executive overreach in India is measured.
