The anti-defection framework is one of the Constitution’s clearest attempts to stabilise legislative majorities through text. It later generated an entire line of judicial interpretation around disqualification, review, and party discipline.

## What happened

The Fifty-Second Amendment to the Constitution was passed in early 1985 in response to widespread and destabilising floor-crossing by legislators across the country. The amendment inserted the Tenth Schedule into the Constitution and also amended Articles 102 and 191, which govern disqualification of members of Parliament and state legislatures respectively. The Tenth Schedule laid down that a legislator would be disqualified if they voluntarily gave up membership of the political party that fielded them, or if they voted or abstained contrary to the party’s direction without prior sanction. The Speaker or Chairman of the relevant house was designated as the authority to decide disqualification questions.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Before the amendment, the Constitution had no mechanism to punish defection as such; a legislator could switch sides without losing their seat. The Fifty-Second Amendment for the first time gave constitutional standing to party discipline within the legislature, creating a new category of member ineligibility tied directly to political conduct. The choice of the Speaker as adjudicator and the placement of the schedule within the constitutional text — rather than ordinary legislation — gave the framework an unusual structural character that courts had to interpret with care. The amendment raised fundamental questions about the tension between representative democracy, the freedom of conscience of an elected member, and the desire for stable government.

## Its legacy

The Fifty-Second Amendment provoked immediate constitutional litigation, culminating in the Kihoto Hollohan judgment in 1992, which upheld the schedule’s validity while preserving limited judicial review of Speaker decisions. Subsequent floors-crossing crises demonstrated that the framework, though robust, had gaps — particularly around mergers, split provisions, and the impartiality of partisan Speakers — leading to further amendment and continuing litigation. The Tenth Schedule today remains the primary constitutional text governing the relationship between legislative membership and party loyalty.
