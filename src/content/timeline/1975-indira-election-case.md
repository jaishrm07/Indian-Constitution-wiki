The decision made democratic legitimacy part of the Constitution's structural vocabulary. It remains a key bridge between amendment power and electoral accountability.

## What happened

In Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain, the Supreme Court heard an appeal against the Allahabad High Court judgment that had set aside Indira Gandhi's 1971 election from Rae Bareli constituency on grounds of corrupt practice involving the misuse of government machinery and officials. While the appeal was pending, Parliament enacted the Thirty-Ninth Amendment, which purported to place disputes concerning elections to the offices of President, Vice-President, Prime Minister, and Speaker beyond the jurisdiction of ordinary courts and to validate retrospectively the impugned election. The Constitution Bench, by majority, struck down the relevant clauses of the Thirty-Ninth Amendment as unconstitutional, holding that they violated the basic structure by destroying the principle of free and fair elections. Justice H.R. Khanna's concurrence, which contributed the free-and-fair-elections component of the basic structure, was particularly significant.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The case was the first direct application of the Kesavananda basic structure doctrine to strike down a constitutional amendment. By holding that free and fair elections are themselves part of the basic structure, the Court extended the doctrine beyond structural provisions about the separation of powers into the democratic process itself, giving the basic structure doctrine an explicitly normative and democratic content. The judgment also implicitly confirmed that no constitutional amendment could insulate an individual officeholder from judicial scrutiny of the electoral process through which they obtained power, preventing the basic structure doctrine from being turned against itself.

## Its legacy

The identification of free and fair elections as a basic structure element became foundational for subsequent electoral law jurisprudence and for the Court's willingness to scrutinise attempts to manipulate electoral rules through constitutional amendment. The case also had an immediate political consequence: the Emergency continued even after this partial judicial success, but the Court's willingness to strike down the Thirty-Ninth Amendment — in the face of extraordinary political pressure — preserved a measure of judicial credibility that proved important to the post-Emergency constitutional recovery.
