The Third Judges Case is the operational constitution of the collegium. Where the Second Judges Case (1993) established the principle of judicial primacy in appointments, the Third Judges Case converted that principle into detailed procedural rules that determined how the collegium would actually function for the next two decades.

## What happened

The Second Judges Case (Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India, 1993) had held that the Chief Justice of India's institutional opinion carried primacy over the executive's preference in the appointment of Supreme Court and High Court judges. However, ambiguities remained about the exact composition of the collegium — how many judges must the CJI consult, what happens when collegium members disagree, and what is the status of the executive's response to a collegium recommendation. Following a period of tension between the judiciary and the executive over specific appointments, the President of India made a reference to the Supreme Court under Article 143(1) posing nine specific questions about the operation of the collegium system. A nine-judge Constitution Bench answered the reference on 28 October 1998.

The Court held that for appointments to and transfers of Supreme Court judges, the collegium must consist of the Chief Justice of India and the four senior-most puisne judges of the Supreme Court. For appointments to High Courts, the collegium of the relevant High Court consists of the Chief Justice of that court and its two senior-most judges, with the Supreme Court collegium of five participating at the final stage. A recommendation by the collegium, once made, is binding on the executive; the executive may return it for reconsideration with reasons, but if the collegium reiterates its recommendation, the executive must act on it. The CJI must not send forward a recommendation if the two most senior judges of the Supreme Court are both opposed to it, introducing a check within the collegium itself.

## Constitutional significance

The advisory opinion under Article 143 has an unusual constitutional status: it is not a binding judgment in the technical sense — it does not decide a dispute between parties — yet the Court treated its contents as the authoritative gloss on Articles 124, 217, and 222. By extending the collegium from the CJI-plus-two framework of the Second Judges Case to CJI-plus-four, the Court provided a larger deliberative body for what are among the most consequential appointments in Indian governance. The binding effect given to reiterated collegium recommendations directly curtailed the executive's residual discretion in judicial appointments, reducing the President's and Cabinet's role to a formality after the collegium's decision is confirmed.

The judgment also addressed the position of judges who come from the High Court from which a nominee is being elevated, requiring the views of such judges to be obtained and considered — a safeguard against regional or personal biases in the collegium's deliberations. These procedural details, while not stated in the constitutional text, were treated as constitutional requirements derived from the structural principle of judicial independence.

## Long-term impact

The Third Judges Case, together with the Second Judges Case, governed judicial appointments until 2014. When Parliament enacted the Constitution (Ninety-Ninth Amendment) Act, 2014 and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act to replace the collegium with a six-member commission that included the Law Minister and two eminent persons, the Supreme Court — in Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015), popularly called the NJAC case — struck down both enactments as violating the basic structure of the Constitution. The majority opinion in that case expressly reaffirmed the Third Judges Case framework as constitutionally mandated. The collegium system, along with the Third Judges Case's procedural architecture, therefore continues to govern appointments. Persistent criticism of the system's opacity led the Supreme Court to publish collegium resolutions on its website from 2017 onwards, a transparency reform that operated within rather than against the Third Judges Case framework.
