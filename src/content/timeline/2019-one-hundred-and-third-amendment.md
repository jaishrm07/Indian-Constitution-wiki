This amendment expanded the reservation framework beyond the older social and educational backwardness model. It remains central to current equality and representation debates.

## What happened

The One Hundred and Third Amendment, passed by Parliament within two days and receiving Presidential assent on 12 January 2019, inserted clauses (6) into Articles 15 and 16 of the Constitution. Article 15(6) permitted up to ten percent reservation in educational institutions for economically weaker sections of citizens, defined by family income and other indicators, expressly excluding those already covered by existing backward class reservations. Article 16(6) extended the same logic to government employment. The amendment was enacted with exceptional speed, just ahead of the general elections, and broke from the six-decade tradition of limiting reservations to socially and educationally backward classes and Scheduled Castes and Tribes.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The amendment challenged two long-standing propositions. First, it departed from the principle settled in Indra Sawhney that economic criterion alone is not sufficient to characterise backwardness for reservation purposes. Second, it breached the fifty percent ceiling on reservations that Indra Sawhney had described as a constitutional norm, since EWS reservation was designed to operate above and beyond existing quotas. Whether these departures constituted an infringement of the basic structure became the central question in subsequent litigation, particularly in Janhit Abhiyan, where the Supreme Court ultimately upheld the amendment by a majority.

## Its legacy

The constitutional validity of the EWS amendment, confirmed in Janhit Abhiyan in 2022, has had lasting effects on how courts approach the relationship between equality provisions and affirmative action. The majority's acceptance that a purely economic criterion can ground reservation under the Constitution opened the door to new arguments about the flexibility of the equality code, while the dissenting opinions preserved the counter-position that the basic structure limits permissible models of affirmative action.
