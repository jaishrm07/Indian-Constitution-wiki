This decision remains the main reference point for privilege, expulsion, and the limited but real reach of judicial review into parliamentary proceedings.

## What happened

Raja Ram Pal and other Members of Parliament had been expelled from the Lok Sabha after a sting operation that captured them on video accepting cash for asking questions in Parliament — the cash-for-query scandal. The expelled members challenged their expulsion before the Supreme Court, arguing that Parliament's power to expel was limited and that the expulsion violated their fundamental rights and those of their constituencies. A five-judge Constitution Bench decided the matter in January 2007, upholding the expulsions but holding that parliamentary privilege and the power to expel were not completely beyond judicial review. Courts could intervene where there was illegality, unconstitutionality, or a breach of express constitutional provisions, even if they would not second-guess the merits of Parliament's internal disciplinary decisions.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Raja Ram Pal navigated the long-standing tension between parliamentary sovereignty in internal affairs and the Constitution's supremacy over all institutions including Parliament. Articles 105 and 122 protect parliamentary proceedings and internal management from being questioned in courts, but the Constitution Bench held that this protection is not absolute — it does not extend to acts that violate the Constitution itself or transgress express constitutional provisions. The Court's recognition that the judiciary retains a residual supervisory role, even if a narrow one, over whether Parliament acted in accordance with constitutional limits was a significant reaffirmation of constitutionalism over parliamentary absolutism.

## Its legacy

Raja Ram Pal is the governing authority on the expulsion power and on the scope of judicial review over parliamentary privilege in India. The judgment has been cited in subsequent disputes involving the actions of Speakers, expulsion of members, and the constitutional limits of internal parliamentary proceedings. It shaped the approach courts took in the anti-defection litigation following the Manipur and Maharashtra political crises, where the question of whether Speaker decisions and parliamentary action could be reviewed by courts arose in acute forms. The decision firmly established that privilege, however broad, cannot insulate conduct that is constitutionally impermissible.
