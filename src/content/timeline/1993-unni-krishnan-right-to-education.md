The decision did not settle the whole law of education, but it changed the constitutional expectation that basic education could remain only a directive aspiration.

## What happened

Unni Krishnan J.P. and other petitioners challenged fee structures and admission policies at private medical and engineering colleges in Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, and other states, arguing that these violated constitutional guarantees. The case reached a five-judge Constitution Bench, which decided it in February 1993. The Court held that the right to education up to the age of fourteen years was a fundamental right flowing from Article 21 read with Articles 41 and 45 of Part IV. Because life and personal liberty under Article 21 could only be meaningful if a citizen was educated, the State was constitutionally obliged to provide free education at least through the compulsory schooling years. Beyond fourteen, education remained a constitutional aspiration enforceable according to the State's economic capacity.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Unni Krishnan judgment was significant because it used the interpretive technique of reading Directive Principles into Article 21 to elevate a Part IV aspiration into a justiciable right. This approach — linking the right to life to the conditions necessary for a dignified human existence — extended a method that had already been used for other social rights and demonstrated that the boundary between justiciable and non-justiciable constitutional provisions was malleable. At the same time, the Court also laid down a scheme governing private educational institutions that generated controversy by curtailing their fee autonomy, portions of which were later revisited.

## Its legacy

Unni Krishnan directly led to the Eighty-Sixth Constitutional Amendment in 2002, which inserted Article 21A making the right to free and compulsory education for children aged six to fourteen a standalone fundamental right in Part III. The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 gave effect to that amendment. However, the educational scheme constructed by the Court in Unni Krishnan for private institutions was substantially overhauled by the T.M.A. Pai Foundation and P.A. Inamdar decisions, which reset the balance between institutional autonomy and state regulation.
