R.C. Cooper is the bridge between formal liberty and the more substantive fairness review that later cases would make central.

## What happened

Rustom Cavasjee Cooper, a shareholder and director of the Central Bank of India, challenged the Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act 1969, through which the government nationalised fourteen major private banks. The Supreme Court, by a ten-to-one majority, struck down the nationalisation law, holding that the compensation provisions were inadequate and that the legislation infringed the rights of shareholders and bank directors under Articles 14, 19, and 31. Crucially, the Court departed from the Gopalan-era approach of treating each fundamental rights article as a separate, self-contained guarantee, and instead assessed the real effect of the impugned legislation across multiple articles simultaneously.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Court's methodology of looking at the direct and inevitable effect of state action on the totality of a person's rights, rather than asking which single article applied, was a significant doctrinal shift. It meant that a law could not escape constitutional scrutiny under one article simply because it appeared to target a different area of law, if its actual effect swept across rights guaranteed by several articles. The decision also confirmed that the adequacy of compensation for acquisition of property was justiciable and that token or illusory compensation could not satisfy the constitutional standard, a holding that added teeth to Article 31's compensation requirement.

## Its legacy

R.C. Cooper's integrated rights methodology directly influenced the reasoning in Maneka Gandhi, where the Court used the same approach to hold that a passport impoundment order had to satisfy the standards of Articles 14, 19, and 21 collectively, not just the narrow procedural requirement of Article 21. The nationalisation itself was subsequently upheld through the Twenty-Fifth Amendment, which modified the compensation standard, but the methodological contribution of R.C. Cooper to substantive rights review proved more durable than the specific property rights outcome.
