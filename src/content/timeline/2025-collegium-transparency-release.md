This was not a formal amendment or judgment, but it materially changed the public visibility of a constitutionally significant appointments process.

## What happened

In May 2025, the Supreme Court collegium published a set of documents and data on the official Supreme Court website disclosing the status of pending judicial appointment recommendations, the timeline of recommendations made to the government, and instances where recommendations had been returned or were awaiting executive action. The release was not triggered by a court order or legislative mandate but represented an initiative by the collegium itself, following sustained public and parliamentary criticism of opacity in the appointments process. The disclosure included recommendations for High Court and Supreme Court appointments that had been pending with the executive for extended periods, some running to years.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The transparency release engaged the constitutional architecture of Articles 124 and 217 by making visible the gap between collegium recommendations and executive action — an area that had previously been opaque in formal institutional terms even if known in broad strokes. It reinforced the principle, underlying the Second and Third Judges Cases, that the appointments power is a shared constitutional function in which neither the executive nor the collegium can act unilaterally, and that delay or non-action on recommendations is a form of constitutional irregularity. The release also placed the burden of explanation on the government for pending recommendations, shifting the accountability dynamic.

## Its legacy

The 2025 transparency release set a precedent for collegium-level disclosure that subsequent compositions of the Court may continue or build upon. It also intensified debate about whether the collegium system, as it has evolved since the Second Judges Case, provides adequate accountability mechanisms, and whether a statutory or constitutional framework for judicial appointments — as proposed in the NJAC design — might better serve transparency goals if it were structured to preserve judicial independence.
