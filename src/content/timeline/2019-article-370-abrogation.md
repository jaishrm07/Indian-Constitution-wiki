## What happened

On 5 August 2019, the President issued The Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order, 2019 under Article 370(1), substituting the earlier 1954 Order and extending all provisions of the Constitution to Jammu and Kashmir. The same day, a resolution was moved in the Rajya Sabha under Article 370(3) recommending that Article 370 be rendered inoperative, and the President issued a further notification to that effect. Parliament simultaneously passed the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019, which divided the existing state into two Union Territories: Jammu and Kashmir (with a legislature) and Ladakh (without one). The concurrence of the Constituent Assembly of Jammu and Kashmir required by Article 370(3) was not obtained because that body had ceased to exist in 1956; the government treated the state legislature acting through the Governor as an equivalent. The entire process was conducted while the Jammu and Kashmir assembly was under President's Rule and the state was governed by the Governor acting on the advice of the Union Cabinet.

In December 2023, a five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in Dr Farooq Abdullah and connected matters, upheld the abrogation. The Court held that Article 370 was a temporary provision that the President had the power to render inoperative through the mechanism used, and that the exercise of power under Article 356 to act on behalf of the state legislature did not require the state assembly to be in session. The Court also held that the bifurcation of a state into Union Territories was a valid exercise of Article 3, though it directed that statehood be restored to Jammu and Kashmir as soon as possible and that elections be held by September 2024.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The abrogation raised fundamental questions about the limits of executive action under emergency provisions, the use of one constitutional article to modify or extinguish another, and whether Parliament could use the bifurcation power in Article 3 to permanently reduce a state to a Union Territory. The process of using the Governor's concurrence in lieu of a functioning state legislature — during a period of central rule — tested whether procedural safeguards built into Article 370 could be bypassed through the interaction of Presidential Rule and executive orders. The Constitution Bench's acceptance of the government's interpretation gave the Centre broad latitude to use temporary emergency mechanisms to achieve permanent constitutional change.

## Its constitutional legacy

The 2023 judgment settled the immediate constitutional challenge but left significant tensions unresolved. The direction to restore statehood became a live political and legal issue, with the Supreme Court monitoring compliance. The case deepened debate about the scope of the President's power under Article 370, the relationship between temporary and permanent provisions in the Constitution, and the constitutional status of federalism as a basic structure element when the Centre acts against a state under emergency powers. The bifurcation of Jammu and Kashmir into Union Territories without the consent of an elected state legislature remains the most far-reaching unilateral reorganisation of a state in independent India.
