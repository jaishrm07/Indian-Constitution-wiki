This judgment is the real starting point for the collegium era. Later fights over reform, transparency, and NJAC all operate in its shadow.

## What happened

The Second Judges Case — formally the Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association case — was decided by a nine-judge Constitution Bench in October 1993. It arose from a reference made after the First Judges Case of 1981 had held that the executive had primacy in judicial appointments, a position that had generated growing concern within the legal community about executive interference in judicial independence. The nine-judge bench overruled the First Judges Case and held that in the appointment of judges to the Supreme Court and High Courts, the opinion of the Chief Justice of India — formed after consultation with a collegium of the two senior-most judges — would have primacy. The President, acting on the advice of the Cabinet, was bound to act in accordance with that opinion except in very narrow circumstances.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment substantially rewrote how Articles 124 and 217 would be understood. The text of those articles gave the appointment power to the President acting in consultation with the Chief Justice of India, language the First Judges Case had read in favour of executive primacy. The nine-judge bench held that meaningful judicial independence — itself a basic structure element — required the judiciary's institutional view to prevail, and that "consultation" in this constitutional context must be read as "concurrence." This was a significant act of purposive constitutional interpretation that prioritised structural inferences over textual literalism.

## Its legacy

The Second Judges Case inaugurated the collegium system as it is known today. The Third Judges Case in 1998 expanded the collegium to five senior judges for Supreme Court appointments. The collegium system's lack of formal transparency and accountability became a persistent criticism, ultimately leading Parliament to pass the Ninety-Ninth Amendment creating the NJAC — which the Supreme Court then struck down in 2015 in a direct confrontation with the executive, reaffirming the Second Judges Case framework. The case remains the constitutional foundation of judicial appointments law in India.
