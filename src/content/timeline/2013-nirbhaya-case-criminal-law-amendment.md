## What happened

On the night of 16 December 2012, a twenty-three-year-old paramedical student was gang-raped and assaulted on a moving bus in South Delhi by six men. She died from her injuries on 29 December 2012 in a Singapore hospital. The attack triggered sustained mass protests in Delhi and across the country, with demonstrators demanding stronger legal protection against sexual violence and accountability for systemic police and institutional failures. The government constituted a Committee chaired by retired Chief Justice of India J.S. Verma, with Justice Leila Seth and Senior Advocate Gopal Subramaniam as members, to recommend amendments to criminal law. The Verma Committee, working with extraordinary speed, submitted its 630-page report on 23 January 2013 after receiving more than 80,000 submissions from the public. Its recommendations — covering the definition of rape, command responsibility, marital rape, sexual harassment at the workplace, and police accountability — formed the basis of the Criminal Law (Amendment) Act 2013, which received Presidential assent on 2 April 2013. The Act significantly broadened the definition of sexual assault, enhanced penalties, and introduced new offences including stalking and voyeurism.

## Constitutional significance

The constitutional dimensions of the Nirbhaya case and its legislative aftermath centre on Article 21's guarantee of the right to life and personal liberty and Article 15's prohibition of discrimination on grounds of sex. The Supreme Court's jurisprudence, beginning with Vishaka v State of Rajasthan in 1997, had already established that sexual harassment at the workplace violated the constitutional rights of women under Articles 14, 15, and 21, and that the State's failure to provide a legal framework was itself unconstitutional. The Verma Committee report went further, framing gender-based violence as a systemic constitutional failure requiring not only better laws but better implementation of existing laws, police accountability, and attitudinal reform in the criminal justice system. The report drew explicitly on constitutional text and international human rights standards to argue that the right to life and dignity under Article 21 is not merely a negative right against arbitrary state action but a positive guarantee that obliges the State to create conditions in which women can live free from violence. The 2013 Amendment Act's introduction of enhanced penalties and a wider definition of assault operationalised this constitutional reading, and subsequent Supreme Court judgments on sexual violence and institutional accountability have cited the constitutional framework the Verma Committee articulated.

## Long-term impact

The Criminal Law (Amendment) Act 2013 became the baseline for subsequent legislative and judicial developments in the law of sexual violence in India. The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act 2013 was enacted in the same parliamentary session, implementing the Vishaka directions in statutory form. The Verma Committee's recommendation on marital rape was not accepted in the 2013 legislation and remains a live constitutional question, with petitions pending before the courts arguing that the marital rape exception violates Articles 14, 15, and 21. The positive obligation reading of Article 21 — that the State must actively protect the right to life and dignity and cannot remain passive in the face of structural violence — has been reinforced in subsequent constitutional litigation on mob violence, custodial deaths, and the right of survivors to receive effective remedies, making the Nirbhaya case and the Verma Committee report a constitutional watershed in the treatment of gender-based violence as a matter of fundamental rights.
