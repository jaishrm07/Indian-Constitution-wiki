## What happened

The Electoral Bonds Scheme was notified by the Government of India on 2 January 2018 under Section 31 of the Reserve Bank of India Act and authorised through amendments to the Finance Act 2017, which modified the Companies Act 2013, the Income Tax Act 1961, the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act 2010, and the Representation of the People Act 1951. The amendments removed the earlier twenty-five-per-cent cap on corporate profits that had limited corporate donations, deleted the requirement to disclose the names of political donors in company accounts, and exempted electoral bond transactions from income tax disclosure requirements. Under the scheme, any eligible individual or entity could purchase bearer bonds from authorised branches of the State Bank of India in denominations up to one crore rupees, donate them to any registered political party, which could encash them within fifteen days. Neither the donor's identity nor the recipient party was required to be publicly disclosed. The Association for Democratic Reforms and other civil society organisations filed writ petitions challenging the scheme and the associated statutory amendments. The Supreme Court directed disclosure of bond data as an interim measure and ultimately struck down the scheme in full in February 2024.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Electoral Bonds Scheme tested the constitutional boundary between legitimate regulation of electoral finance and state-sanctioned opacity in democratic funding. Critics argued that the scheme, by preserving full records with the State Bank of India — a government-owned institution — while withholding them from the public, created an asymmetric arrangement in which the ruling party could access information about donors through administrative channels while the electorate was denied the same. The scheme intersected with the Supreme Court's developing doctrine — in Association for Democratic Reforms (2002) and PUCL (2003) — that voters have a constitutionally protected right to information about candidates and parties. The constitutional question was whether the right to informed voting extended to information about the financial relationships between large corporate donors and political parties in power.

## Its constitutional legacy

The scheme became the central illustration in public debate about the relationship between money, power, and democracy in India's constitutional order. Its eventual invalidation by a unanimous Supreme Court bench in 2024 affirmed that electoral transparency is not merely a statutory aspiration but a constitutional requirement under Article 19(1)(a). The subsequent court-ordered disclosure of bond data by the SBI produced significant public reporting on the linkage between political donations and regulatory and procurement decisions, reinforcing the constitutional importance of transparency that the scheme had systematically suppressed.
