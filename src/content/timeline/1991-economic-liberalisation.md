The 1991 reforms did not amend the Constitution but they changed the assumptions on which post-independence constitutional doctrine had been built. The tensions they created — between directive principles favouring socialised resource distribution and a market-oriented policy framework — have been worked out slowly through litigation and further amendment ever since.

## What happened

On 24 July 1991 Finance Minister Manmohan Singh presented the Union Budget that formally inaugurated a programme of economic liberalisation, initiated under Prime Minister P.V. Narasimha Rao's government in response to a severe balance-of-payments crisis. The reforms involved abolishing industrial licensing for most sectors, opening the economy to foreign direct investment, reducing import restrictions and tariffs, beginning disinvestment from public sector undertakings, and dismantling the permit-raj framework that had governed economic activity since the 1950s. None of these changes required a constitutional amendment. They were implemented through executive orders, licensing notifications, and ordinary legislation amending statutes such as the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act 1951 and the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act.

## Constitutional significance

The liberalisation programme created tensions with the constitutional framework that had shaped economic policy since 1950. Article 39(b) of the Directive Principles directs the State to ensure that material resources are distributed so as to best serve the common good; Article 39(c) directs that the economic system should not result in the concentration of wealth to the common detriment. These provisions had been used since the 1970s to justify nationalisation, land reform, and monopoly restrictions. The shift to a market economy did not repeal these directives but substantially changed how the government intended to fulfil them. Simultaneously, the expansion of private economic activity gave new practical importance to Article 19(1)(g)'s guarantee of the right to practise any profession or carry on any occupation, trade, or business, subject only to reasonable restrictions under Article 19(6). The constitutional question of whether directing principles could demand specific economic outcomes — socialisation of resources — versus merely directing policy goals became more contested. This culminated decades later in the Supreme Court's nine-judge bench reference on Article 39(b) in the Property Owners Association case.

## Long-term impact

The 1991 reforms shifted the centre of gravity of constitutional economic litigation from questions of nationalisation and redistribution toward questions of regulatory power, competition, and the outer limits of economic rights under Article 19(1)(g). The disinvestment programme raised new constitutional issues about state obligations to public-sector workers and the limits of executive power to transfer public assets without parliamentary authorisation. The tension between Directive Principles mandating resource distribution and a liberalised economy remains constitutionally live, surfacing in cases involving mining rights, natural resource auctions, and the scope of Article 39(b)'s community resources directive.
