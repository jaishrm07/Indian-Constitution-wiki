This amendment is important not just for the NCBC itself but for the federal consequences of defining backward classes. It set up the later debate over who can identify and notify OBC lists.

## What happened

The One Hundred and Second Amendment, which received the President's assent on 11 August 2018, inserted Articles 338B and 342A into the Constitution. Article 338B created the National Commission for Backward Classes as a constitutional body, while Article 342A empowered the President to notify the Central List of socially and educationally backward classes on the advice of the Commission. The amendment was intended to give the NCBC constitutional standing comparable to the National Commission for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. However, the language of Article 342A did not expressly preserve the pre-existing power of states to maintain their own OBC lists, generating immediate legal uncertainty about whether state governments had been divested of that function.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The constitutional ambiguity in Article 342A came to a head in Maratha Quota case proceedings, where the Supreme Court held that after the 102nd Amendment, only the President — and not state governments — could notify the list of socially and educationally backward classes for purposes of reservations. This reading effectively transferred a significant portion of state power over OBC identification to the Union, creating a major federal controversy. The amendment illustrated how a reform primarily aimed at institutional entrenchment of a Commission could produce unintended shifts in the balance of power between the Union and states on reservation policy.

## Its legacy

The controversy generated by the 102nd Amendment directly led to the One Hundred and Fifth Amendment in 2021, which was explicitly enacted to restore state power to identify and notify their own OBC lists. Together, the two amendments form the clearest recent example of constitutional amendment and judicial interpretation interacting to produce a significant federal correction in the domain of social justice policy.
