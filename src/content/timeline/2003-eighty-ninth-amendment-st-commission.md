The Eighty-Ninth Amendment reflects the long-running political and constitutional recognition that Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, while both subject to historical discrimination and given constitutional protections, face different socio-economic conditions, different forms of exclusion, and different legal regimes under the Constitution. A shared commission, the argument ran, could not adequately represent both communities' interests before the Union and the States.

## What happened

The original Article 338, inserted by the Sixty-Fifth Amendment in 1990, established a National Commission for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes as a constitutional body replacing the earlier statutory Special Officer for SCs and STs. The Commission had a Chairperson, a Vice-Chairperson, and three other members appointed by the President, with a duty to investigate and monitor all matters relating to the safeguards provided for SCs and STs under the Constitution or other laws, to inquire into specific complaints, and to participate in and advise on the planning process for socio-economic development of these communities. The Commission submitted annual reports to the President, who placed them before each House of Parliament. The arrangement combined the oversight of two constitutionally distinct categories of protected groups in a single body.

Demand for a separate tribal commission had been building through the 1990s, drawing strength from the constitutional recognition of tribal distinctiveness in Parts X and XIX, the Fifth and Sixth Schedules, and the enactment of PESA in 1996. The Eighty-Ninth Amendment received presidential assent on 28 January 2003. It amended Article 338 to remove all references to Scheduled Tribes, so that it now establishes only the National Commission for Scheduled Castes. The new Article 338A, structurally identical to the amended Article 338, establishes the National Commission for Scheduled Tribes with its own Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson, and members appointed by the President, with the same investigative, monitoring, and advisory functions directed specifically at the safeguards provided for Scheduled Tribes.

## Constitutional significance

The amendment reflects a constitutional judgment that the regulatory and advocacy needs of Scheduled Tribes — particularly in relation to land rights in Scheduled Areas, forest rights, customary governance under PESA, and the protections of the Fifth and Sixth Schedules — are sufficiently distinct from those of Scheduled Castes to warrant a dedicated constitutional institution. Scheduled Castes are defined by reference to untouchability and social exclusion within the Hindu caste hierarchy (and its extension to other religions by presidential orders), whereas Scheduled Tribes are defined by reference to primitive traits, geographical isolation, and distinctive culture, with a strong territorial dimension through the Scheduled Area framework. A single commission addressing both communities risked blurring these distinctions and diluting the specialised expertise needed to advise on each community's constitutional protections.

Article 338A gives the new National Commission for Scheduled Tribes the same constitutional status — appointment by the President, service conditions determined by the President, annual reports to Parliament via the President — as the Commission for Scheduled Castes, ensuring institutional parity between the two bodies.

## Long-term impact

The National Commission for Scheduled Tribes established under Article 338A has since been a significant body for monitoring compliance with tribal constitutional safeguards and for advising the government on policy relating to Scheduled Tribes. It has been involved in scrutinising forest rights, displacement caused by infrastructure and mining projects in Fifth Schedule areas, and the implementation of PESA. The bifurcation also set a precedent for specialised constitutional monitoring bodies: the Constitution (One Hundred and Second Amendment) Act, 2018 subsequently inserted Article 338B to establish a separate National Commission for Backward Classes with similar constitutional status, completing the move from a single SC/ST commission to three distinct constitutional commissions for the three principal categories of constitutionally protected groups. The Eighty-Ninth Amendment thus represents an important step in the institutional differentiation of the constitutional framework for affirmative action and social justice.
