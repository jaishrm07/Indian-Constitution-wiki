## What happened

The Travancore Devaswom Board and the priests of the Sabarimala temple in Kerala observed a centuries-old practice excluding women between the ages of ten and fifty — the age of menstruation — from entering the shrine of Lord Ayyappa, treating them as ritually impure. The Indian Young Lawyers Association challenged this exclusion as unconstitutional. A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court, in September 2018, ruled four to one that the exclusion was unconstitutional. The majority held that the restriction violated Article 25 (freedom of religion and right to worship) and Article 17 (prohibition of untouchability), finding that the treatment of menstruating women as impure was a form of social exclusion that Article 17 was designed to eradicate beyond caste. Justice Indu Malhotra, in dissent, held that the Court should not interfere with religious practices widely considered essential by devotees and that questions of essential religious practice should be left to the community concerned.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment expanded the constitutional understanding of Article 17 beyond its historical focus on caste-based untouchability to cover gender-based exclusions grounded in notions of bodily impurity. It also reaffirmed that the freedom of religion under Article 25 is subject to other fundamental rights, so that religious practices cannot shelter behind constitutional protection when they violate guarantees of equality and dignity. The majority's invocation of constitutional morality over popular morality — developed in Navtej Singh Johar the same year — confirmed that the Court would not treat majoritarian religious sentiment as a reason to withhold constitutional protection from affected individuals.

## Its constitutional legacy

The Sabarimala judgment triggered immediate and widespread protests in Kerala and produced a political and legal backlash. Several review petitions were filed, and a larger nine-judge Constitution Bench was subsequently constituted to reconsider broader questions about the essential practices doctrine and the conflict between individual rights and religious group autonomy — questions left unresolved by the fractured reasoning in Sabarimala itself. The case became the central exhibit in debates about whether the Court should make rights determinations in matters of deep religious significance, and the scope of the essential religious practices test as a mediating doctrine between Articles 25 and 14 remains unsettled.
