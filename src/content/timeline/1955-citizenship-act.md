## What happened

Articles 5 to 11 of the Constitution established citizenship at commencement and vested in Parliament under Article 11 the power to make any provision with respect to the acquisition and termination of citizenship and all other matters relating to citizenship. Parliament exercised this power by enacting the Citizenship Act 1955, which received Presidential assent on 30 December 1955.

The Act specified four modes of acquiring citizenship after the Constitution's commencement: by birth in India, by descent from an Indian citizen father, by registration for persons of Indian origin resident in India or married to Indian citizens, and by naturalisation for persons meeting a specified period of residence. It also provided for termination of citizenship by renunciation, termination, and deprivation.

## Constitutional significance

The Citizenship Act filled the statutory gap left by the Constitution's transitional provisions, which had addressed citizenship at commencement but left the ongoing regulation of citizenship to Parliament. By enacting the Act, Parliament defined the terms of civic membership in the republic and established the administrative machinery for determining and recording citizenship. The Act gave concrete legal effect to the Constitution's decision to vest exclusive citizenship — India does not permit dual citizenship — and its provisions on citizenship by birth and descent shaped the legal status of millions of persons of Indian origin across the world.

The Act also reflected the Constitution's framework of parliamentary supremacy over citizenship law: Article 11 gives Parliament wide and unrestricted power in this field, subject only to any other provision of the Constitution itself.

## Long-term impact

The Citizenship Act 1955 remained the foundational statute governing Indian citizenship for decades. Its amendment in 2003 introduced a significant change by removing the unqualified right to citizenship by birth and adding the requirement that neither parent be an illegal migrant. The Act's most contested amendment came through the Citizenship Amendment Act 2019, which added a pathway to citizenship by naturalisation for non-Muslim minorities from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, and Pakistan, generating constitutional challenges under Article 14 and a broad public controversy about the relationship between citizenship law and constitutional secularism. These later amendments and their constitutional challenges trace directly back to the framework Parliament established in 1955 under Article 11.
