The ruling remains a defining privilege case because it sits at the intersection of legislative immunity, criminal law, and the limits of constitutional accountability.

## What happened

P.V. Narasimha Rao, former Prime Minister and sitting member of Parliament, along with several other Members of Parliament, faced prosecution arising from the JMM bribery scandal in which members of the Jharkhand Mukti Morcha had allegedly been paid to vote against a no-confidence motion against the Narasimha Rao government in 1993. The Central Bureau of Investigation filed charges of criminal conspiracy and corruption. A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court decided by majority in April 1998 that Members of Parliament who accepted bribes and then voted as directed were protected from prosecution under Articles 105(1) and 105(2), because the speech and vote in Parliament was protected conduct and the alleged bribery was inextricably connected to that vote. However, two dissenters took a narrower view. Those who accepted bribes but did not vote as directed were held not to be protected.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The majority's reasoning in P.V. Narasimha Rao produced the troubling proposition that a legislator who is bribed to vote a certain way and does vote that way is immune from prosecution for that corruption, while a legislator who takes the bribe but reneges is not. This outcome flowed from a generous reading of Articles 105 and 194 as protecting not just speech and votes in themselves but also conduct antecedent to and causally linked to them. The case raised the fundamental question of whether parliamentary privilege was meant to shield fraud and corruption or only to ensure freedom of deliberation, and the majority's answer attracted lasting criticism.

## Its legacy

P.V. Narasimha Rao stood as binding authority for over two decades, effectively creating a constitutional immunity gap for vote-related corruption by legislators. The ruling was squarely reconsidered and overruled by a seven-judge Constitution Bench in 2024 in the Sita Soren case, which held that no immunity extends to bribery in connection with a vote or speech, reversing one of the most criticised constitutional holdings of the twentieth century. The arc from the 1998 ruling to its 2024 overruling illustrates how the Court's understanding of the proper scope of privilege evolved over a generation of democratic experience.
