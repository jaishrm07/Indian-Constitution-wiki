This judgment helped define how Indian constitutional law would talk about religious autonomy, regulation, and secular constitutional structure.

## What happened

The Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments, Madras brought proceedings concerning the administration and finances of the Shirur Mutt, a religious institution in the Udupi region. The matter raised questions about the extent to which the state could regulate and supervise the internal administration of a religious denomination under the Madras Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act. The Supreme Court, in a seven-judge bench decision, held that a religious denomination has the right under Article 26(b) to manage its own affairs in matters of religion, and that the state cannot interfere with what is genuinely a religious practice or management function. The Court also laid down the foundational test for distinguishing between matters of religion, which are protected, and secular activities associated with religion, which may be regulated.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment gave substantive content to Articles 25 and 26 by establishing the essential religious practices test: only those practices that are essential to a religion or denomination fall within the protected core of religious freedom. This test made it necessary for courts to assess the theological importance of contested practices, a role that would prove both powerful and controversial. The Court also clarified that the right of a denomination to manage its affairs in matters of religion is not absolute and must yield to laws made in the interest of public order, morality, and health, establishing the framework for balancing state regulation against denominational autonomy.

## Its legacy

The essential religious practices test articulated in Shirur Mutt became the dominant framework for religious freedom cases in India for decades, applied repeatedly in disputes over temple management, personal law, and religious instruction. The test has attracted sustained scholarly and judicial criticism for requiring courts to adjudicate internal religious questions, and its continued validity and scope was revisited in later cases including those concerning the Sabarimala temple. The judgment remains the foundational reference point for any analysis of Article 26's denomination rights.
