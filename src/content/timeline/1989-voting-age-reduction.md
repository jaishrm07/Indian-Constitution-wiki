Lowering the voting age was one of the most consequential franchise expansions in Indian electoral history after the original universal adult suffrage of 1950. It shifted the electorate's composition and made youth a politically salient demographic in a way that has deepened since.

## What happened

The Sixty-First Amendment to the Constitution was passed in March 1989 and came into force before the ninth general elections held later that year. It amended Article 326, which governs elections to the House of the People and to state legislative assemblies, by substituting the qualifying age of twenty-one years with eighteen years. The change was driven by the recognition that a large section of the population — particularly young people who had grown up after independence — was subject to the obligations of citizenship, including military service and criminal liability, but had no say in choosing their representatives. The amendment was broadly supported across parties and passed without significant opposition.

## Constitutional significance

Article 326 is the constitutional foundation of universal adult suffrage in India. Its original text already embodied a strong commitment to inclusive democratic participation: it disqualified voters only on grounds of non-residence, unsound mind, crime, and corrupt or illegal practice, with no property or literacy restrictions. Changing the qualifying age from twenty-one to eighteen was therefore consistent with the article's underlying logic of maximum inclusive franchise. The amendment required no corresponding change to any other constitutional provision, though the Representation of the People Act had to be amended to update electoral rolls and registration procedures for the new age cohort.

## Long-term impact

The 1989 amendment enfranchised approximately fifty million young voters who participated in the general elections of that year. The youth vote has since become a significant factor in Indian electoral politics, given that India has one of the youngest populations of any major democracy. The amendment reinforced the constitutional principle that the franchise should be as broad as possible consistent with the basic requirements of informed civic participation. It has also shaped subsequent constitutional debates about youth representation, the age of candidacy (which remains twenty-five for the Lok Sabha), and the relationship between civic rights and civic obligations for young citizens.
