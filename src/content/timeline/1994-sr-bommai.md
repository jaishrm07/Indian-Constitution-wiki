Bommai is the ruling that converted Article 356 from a near-unreviewable executive power into a provision subject to constitutional discipline. It transformed the politics of President's Rule and gave the basic structure doctrine its clearest expression in the federal domain.

## What happened

S.R. Bommai was Chief Minister of Karnataka when his government was dismissed under Article 356 in 1989, following defections engineered by political opponents. Similar dismissals of state governments — including the Bharatiya Janata Party governments in Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Himachal Pradesh after the Babri Masjid demolition in December 1992 — made the constitutional status of Article 356 an urgent question. A nine-judge Constitution Bench heard the consolidated matter and delivered its judgment in March 1994. The Court held, by a majority, that a Presidential proclamation under Article 356 is not immune from judicial review; courts may examine whether the material before the President was relevant and whether the satisfaction recorded was based on that material. The Court further held that the proper test of whether a government has lost majority support is a floor test in the legislature, and that dismissal before such a test is constitutionally suspect.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Before Bommai, the conventional understanding — reinforced by the Supreme Court's own earlier rulings — was that the satisfaction of the President under Article 356 was a political question beyond judicial scrutiny. Bommai overturned this. The Court held that federalism is a basic feature of the Constitution and that Article 356, however textually absolute it appears, must be read subject to that structural commitment. The requirement of a floor test operationalised this by providing an objective mechanism for testing majority support before executive action. The Court also, in a set of significant concurrences, held that secularism is a basic feature of the Constitution, and that a State government whose conduct is fundamentally anti-secular could, in principle, be dismissed — though the same standards of reviewability would apply to the exercise of that power.

## Its constitutional legacy

Bommai substantially reduced the frequency and partisan use of Article 356. The floor test requirement became the standard procedural check when the loyalty of a legislative majority is in dispute, and it has been invoked repeatedly in subsequent Speaker and Governor controversies. The Court's characterisation of federalism and secularism as basic features has been cited across a wide range of constitutional challenges. The judgment did not wholly prevent misuse of President's Rule, but it introduced meaningful judicial accountability into a provision that had previously operated almost entirely at executive discretion.
