This moment is essential for understanding how Indian constitutionalism extends below the state and national levels.

## What happened

The Seventy-Third and Seventy-Fourth Constitutional Amendments were passed by Parliament and received Presidential assent in April 1993, though their drafting and passage through Parliament was completed in December 1992. The Seventy-Third Amendment inserted Part IX into the Constitution, creating a constitutional framework for Panchayati Raj institutions in rural areas and adding the Eleventh Schedule listing subjects for potential devolution. The Seventy-Fourth Amendment inserted Part IXA, establishing a parallel framework for urban local bodies including municipalities and corporations, with the Twelfth Schedule. Both amendments required states to constitute State Finance Commissions and State Election Commissions, and mandated regular elections with reservations for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Before these amendments, local self-government in India rested entirely on state legislation without constitutional protection or guarantee. States could dissolve local bodies, delay elections indefinitely, and withhold meaningful functions and funds. The amendments transformed Panchayats and municipalities from creations of statute into constitutionally mandated institutions with express protections, mandatory elections, and enumerated functions, creating a third tier of government with a direct constitutional basis. This was a structural deepening of federalism and democratic decentralisation without parallel in the Constitution's earlier history.

## Its legacy

The amendments generated important litigation on the timing and conduct of local body elections, reservation in local institutions, and the extent of devolution required by the constitutional mandate. Cases like Kishansing Tomar and K. Krishna Murthy tested the boundaries of what the new provisions required courts to enforce. Despite the constitutional guarantee, real devolution of functions, funds, and functionaries to local bodies has remained uneven across states, making the promise of the Seventy-Third and Seventy-Fourth Amendments a continuing site of democratic accountability rather than a settled constitutional achievement.
