This ruling is the starting point for India's constitutional candidate-disclosure line because it treated voter knowledge as part of meaningful democratic choice.

## What happened

The Association for Democratic Reforms, an NGO, filed a writ petition before the Delhi High Court seeking a direction that the Election Commission of India require candidates to disclose their criminal antecedents, assets and liabilities, and educational qualifications at the time of filing nominations. The Delhi High Court allowed the petition and directed such disclosure. The Union of India challenged the ruling before the Supreme Court, arguing that Parliament alone had competence to prescribe such conditions through election law. A three-judge bench of the Supreme Court decided the matter in May 2002, upholding the direction. The Court held that the right to information — particularly information about those who seek to govern — is implicit in the freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) and in the constitutional design of free elections under Articles 324 and 326.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment was doctrinally significant because it treated the right to know about candidates not as a statutory creation but as a constitutional entailment of meaningful voting. By locating the right within Article 19 and the election provisions, the Court established that the Election Commission could issue directions requiring such disclosures under its Article 324 plenary power, without waiting for Parliament to amend the Representation of the People Act. This cut through years of legislative inaction on electoral reform and gave constitutional standing to the idea that informed voting is qualitatively different from mere voting.

## Its legacy

The Association for Democratic Reforms ruling immediately prompted the Election Commission to issue orders requiring disclosures. Parliament then responded with amendments to the Representation of the People Act that attempted to narrow the scope of required disclosure, leading directly to the PUCL case in 2003 where the Supreme Court struck down those restrictive amendments and confirmed the full scope of the constitutional disclosure right. The framework the two cases together created — requiring disclosures of criminal record, assets, liabilities, and educational qualifications in affidavits filed with nominations — has since become a standard feature of Indian electoral practice.
