The case is important because it sharpens the question of whether the Speaker may hold a disqualification matter in suspense while political control changes around it.

## What happened

Keisham Meghachandra Singh was a member of the Manipur Legislative Assembly against whom a disqualification petition had been pending before the Speaker under the Tenth Schedule for an extended period without decision. The Supreme Court, hearing the matter in January 2020, expressed serious concern at the prolonged delay and directed that Tenth Schedule disqualification proceedings must be decided within a reasonable time and ideally within three months of the petition being filed. The bench observed that allowing such proceedings to drift indefinitely was incompatible with the constitutional purpose of the anti-defection law, which was designed to bring prompt resolution to questions of membership arising from political defection.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment addressed a structural weakness in Tenth Schedule enforcement: the Speaker, who has a political interest in the outcome of defection proceedings, also holds the exclusive adjudicatory function and faces no hard time limit. By prescribing a three-month norm and holding that indefinite delay is constitutionally unjustifiable, the Court signalled that it would scrutinise Speaker inaction as a form of Tenth Schedule misuse. The case reinforced the principle, established in Kihoto Hollohan and developed in Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil, that the Speaker's quasi-judicial function is not insulated from constitutional review.

## Its legacy

Keisham Meghachandra Singh was cited repeatedly in subsequent defection-related litigation where Speakers were accused of sitting on disqualification petitions to protect legislative majorities. It contributed to a growing body of judicial direction on Tenth Schedule procedure and kept alive the debate about whether the Speaker should be replaced as the adjudicating authority by an independent body, a reform that the Election Commission and various Law Commission reports have also recommended.
