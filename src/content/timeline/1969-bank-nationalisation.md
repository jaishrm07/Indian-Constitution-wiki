## What happened

On 19 July 1969, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's government promulgated the Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Ordinance, nationalising fourteen major private commercial banks with combined deposits exceeding fifty crore rupees. The nationalisation was a centrepiece of Indira Gandhi's "Garibi Hatao" programme and was accompanied by the simultaneous abolition of privy purses, signalling a sharp leftward turn in government economic policy. The ordinance was subsequently replaced by the Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act 1969. Rustom Cavasjee Cooper, a shareholder and director of the Central Bank of India, challenged the measure in the Supreme Court, arguing that it violated his fundamental rights under Articles 14, 19, and 31 of the Constitution.

The Supreme Court, in a ten-to-one majority judgment delivered on 10 February 1970, struck down the nationalisation Act. The majority held that the compensation offered to the dispossessed shareholders was illusory, amounting to far less than the true value of the banks' undertakings, and that the Act violated the right to carry on business under Article 19(1)(g) and the property rights guaranteed under Article 31. Critically, the Court departed from the formal approach established in AK Gopalan, which had treated each fundamental rights article as an independent and self-contained code, and instead asked whether the real and inevitable effect of the impugned legislation infringed any of the rights guaranteed across multiple articles of Part III read together.

Following the Court's decision, Parliament enacted the Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act 1970 — the ordinance's legislative replacement — with revised compensation provisions. The government simultaneously prepared the Twenty-Fifth Amendment to the Constitution to modify the compensation standard in Article 31(2) and to insert Article 31C, insulating legislation implementing the Directive Principles on distribution of material resources from challenge under Articles 14 and 19.

## Constitutional significance

The RC Cooper judgment is constitutionally significant on two distinct levels. First, its substantive holding on property rights confirmed that courts would examine the adequacy of compensation for compulsory acquisition and not simply accept a statutory formula if its practical result was to deny meaningful compensation to owners. This gave enforceable content to the constitutional guarantee and put the government on notice that nationalisation programmes had to include genuine valuation mechanisms to survive constitutional scrutiny.

Second, and more enduringly, the Court's methodological shift to an integrated rights analysis — asking about the direct and inevitable effect on all of a person's fundamental rights rather than assessing each article in isolation — transformed Indian constitutional jurisprudence. This approach directly influenced the reasoning in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978), where the Court held that any state action affecting personal liberty had to satisfy the requirements of Articles 14, 19, and 21 simultaneously. The integrated analysis became the baseline method for fundamental rights adjudication that the Court has applied ever since.

The bank nationalisation controversy also demonstrated the limits of constitutional text as an instrument of economic policy management. The government had assumed that Parliament's political will to nationalise would suffice; the Supreme Court's intervention showed that fundamental rights guarantees imposed real constraints, and that circumventing those constraints required formal constitutional amendment — setting up the prolonged Parliament-judiciary conflict that culminated in Kesavananda Bharati in 1973.

## Long-term impact

The constitutional battle over bank nationalisation set in motion a chain of amendments and counter-judgments that restructured the relationship between property rights and state economic power. The Twenty-Fifth Amendment's insertion of Article 31C created a new constitutional hierarchy in which laws implementing specified Directive Principles could override Articles 14 and 19. The Forty-Second Amendment attempted to extend this immunity to all Directive Principles, but Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980) struck that extension down as violating the basic structure. The eventual outcome of this sequence was the Forty-Fourth Amendment of 1978, which removed the right to property from the fundamental rights chapter entirely and relocated it as a constitutional right under Article 300A, where it retains legal force but no longer commands the same tier of judicial protection as Part III guarantees.

The fourteen banks nationalised in 1969 — plus six more nationalised in 1980 — remain in public ownership and continue to form the backbone of India's public sector banking system. The RC Cooper methodology of integrated rights review remains one of the most cited propositions in Indian constitutional law, applied routinely in cases concerning deprivations of liberty, livelihood, and dignity.
