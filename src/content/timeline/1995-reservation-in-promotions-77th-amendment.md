The Seventy-Seventh Amendment is the opening move in a sustained constitutional back-and-forth between Parliament and the Supreme Court over whether and how reservation in promotions can be constitutionally secured. Every subsequent development in this area — M. Nagaraj, Jarnail Singh, B.K. Pavitra — operates in the space this amendment created.

## What happened

In November 1992 the Supreme Court, in its nine-judge bench decision in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India, upheld the Mandal Commission's fifty per cent ceiling on reservations but also held that the power under Article 16(4) to make reservations in favour of backward classes applied only to initial appointments and could not extend to promotions. The Court directed that any reservation already in force in promotions should be phased out within five years. This threatened to roll back decades of practice under which SC and ST employees had received reservation in promotions across central and state services. Parliament responded by passing the Seventy-Seventh Amendment in June 1995, which inserted a new clause 16(4A) into the Constitution expressly empowering the State to make provisions for reservation in matters of promotion to any class of posts in favour of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes which, in the State's opinion, are not adequately represented in the services under the State.

## Constitutional significance

The amendment is constitutionally significant for several reasons. It is an instance of Parliament using its constituent power under Article 368 to directly override or circumvent a Supreme Court interpretation — in this case, the Indra Sawhney court's holding on the limits of Article 16(4). This raises the perennial question of whether such targeted constitutional amendments are legitimate exercises of constituent power or attempts to subvert the Court's constitutional interpretation. The amendment also deepens the constitutional framework for affirmative action by creating a distinct textual basis for promotion reservations separate from the general backward-class clause in Article 16(4), suggesting that the Constitution now treats SC and ST employees as a distinct category for service reservation purposes. The adequacy of representation standard embedded in Article 16(4A) introduced a quantitative threshold question that courts would later develop into the "creamy layer" and "carry-forward" doctrines.

## Long-term impact

The Seventy-Seventh Amendment triggered the M. Nagaraj case (2006), in which a five-judge bench of the Supreme Court upheld Article 16(4A) as not violating the basic structure, but held that any actual reservation-in-promotion scheme must satisfy three conditions: the State must collect quantifiable data showing backwardness, inadequate representation, and overall administrative efficiency. These conditions were themselves contested and revisited in Jarnail Singh (2018) and B.K. Pavitra (2019), which progressively refined the framework. The amendment was the first of several constitutional changes in the reservation-in-promotions saga, followed by the Eighty-Second Amendment (2000) which re-inserted the relaxation of qualifying marks, and the Eighty-Fifth Amendment (2001) which added consequential seniority for promoted SC/ST employees.
