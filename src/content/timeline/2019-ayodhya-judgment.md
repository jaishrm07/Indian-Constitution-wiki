## What happened

The title dispute over the 2.77-acre plot at Ram Janmabhoomi-Babri Masjid in Ayodhya had been pending in the Allahabad High Court for decades. The High Court in 2010 divided the disputed land in three parts among the Sunni Waqf Board, the Nirmohi Akhara, and Ram Lalla Virajman (the deity as juridical person). All three parties appealed to the Supreme Court. A five-judge Constitution Bench headed by the Chief Justice heard the matter in a marathon hearing spanning forty days. On 9 November 2019 the Court delivered a unanimous judgment. It held that the Muslim parties had not established possessory title over the inner courtyard, and that the demolition of the Babri Masjid in 1992 was an illegal act that nevertheless could not be reversed by restoring title to the Sunni Waqf Board. The entire 2.77 acres was awarded to Ram Lalla Virajman. The Court directed the Central Government to create a trust to construct a temple and to allot an alternative five-acre plot within Ayodhya for a mosque. The Shri Ram Janmabhoomi Teertha Kshetra Trust was constituted and the Ram Mandir was consecrated in January 2024.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The Ayodhya judgment was constitutionally significant in several respects. The Court's characterisation of the 1992 demolition of the Babri Masjid as an "illegal act" while declining to restore possession to the Muslim parties highlighted the tension between the rule of law and the Court's exercise of equitable power under Article 142. The use of Article 142 to fashion a remedy that balanced competing claims — rather than strictly applying title law — was a notable exercise of the Court's extraordinary jurisdiction to do complete justice. The Court's treatment of the deity as a juridical person capable of holding and vindicating title also engaged constitutional questions about the relationship between religious institutions and property rights. The judgment consciously avoided ruling on whether the site was in fact the birthplace of Lord Ram, treating faith as a matter of belief rather than a justiciable fact.

## Its constitutional legacy

The Ayodhya judgment closed the longest-running and most politically charged property dispute in independent India. The Court's unanimous outcome, and its deliberate restraint in confining the ruling to property law, were widely noted as a conscious attempt to provide finality without making the judgment a constitutional endorsement of any religious claim. The award of alternative mosque land — an exercise of curial direction to the executive — was unusual. The judgment continues to be cited in debates about the Court's proper role when property disputes are entangled with communal history, the scope of Article 142, and the ability of courts to deliver constitutional finality in matters of profound public sensitivity.
