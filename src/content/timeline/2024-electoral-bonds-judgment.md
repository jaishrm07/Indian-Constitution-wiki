The judgment turned campaign-finance opacity into a constitutional transparency issue and made donor disclosure central to democratic accountability.

## What happened

A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court unanimously struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme introduced in 2018, which had allowed unlimited, anonymous corporate and individual donations to registered political parties through bearer bonds purchased from the State Bank of India. The Association for Democratic Reforms and other petitioners argued that the scheme, combined with amendments to the Companies Act and the Income Tax Act removing caps on corporate donations and disclosure requirements, unconstitutionally suppressed voters' right to information about political funding. The Court held that the scheme violated Article 19(1)(a), directed the SBI to cease issuing bonds, and ordered that the bank disclose to the Election Commission all data on bond purchasers and recipient parties, which was then published on the ECI's website.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment established that voters' right to information about the funding of political parties is a protected aspect of the freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a), and that the State cannot legitimise systemic opacity in political finance by invoking donor privacy. It applied a proportionality analysis and found that the scheme's anonymity design was neither minimally restrictive nor proportionate to the legitimate goal of preventing donor harassment, particularly since the government-owned bank that issued the bonds retained full records accessible to the executive. The ruling also has implications for the constitutional relationship between money, electoral competition, and democratic equality under Article 14.

## Its legacy

The Electoral Bonds judgment prompted wide public and scholarly debate about the adequacy of India's campaign finance disclosure framework. The subsequent disclosure of bond-purchaser data by the SBI, ordered by the Court, produced detailed reporting about the relationship between large corporate donations and regulatory or procurement outcomes, reinforcing the political significance of the constitutional transparency principle the Court had articulated.
