The Naz Foundation judgment by Chief Justice A.P. Shah and Justice S. Muralidhar of the Delhi High Court stands as one of the most significant constitutional decisions by a High Court in India's history. It introduced constitutional reasoning on sexual orientation and personal autonomy that rewrote the terms of the debate, even after the Supreme Court temporarily reversed it.

## What happened

Naz Foundation, an NGO working on HIV/AIDS prevention among men who have sex with men, filed a writ petition before the Delhi High Court in 2001, arguing that Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code — which criminalised "carnal intercourse against the order of nature" — was unconstitutional as applied to consensual sexual acts between adults in private. After years of procedural litigation including a Supreme Court direction to the High Court to hear the matter on merits, a two-judge bench of the Delhi High Court delivered its judgment on 2 July 2009. The Court held that Section 377, to the extent it applied to consensual adult sexual acts in private, violated the right to life and dignity under Article 21, the right to equality under Article 14, and the prohibition on discrimination under Article 15. The Court read the provision down — rather than striking it down entirely — preserving its application to non-consensual acts and acts involving minors.

## Constitutional significance

The judgment's constitutional reasoning was path-breaking in three respects. First, on Article 21, the Court held that the right to life and personal liberty encompasses the right to dignity and privacy, which necessarily includes the right of adults to make choices about their intimate life without interference by the criminal law. The Court drew on the Maneka Gandhi-to-Puttaswamy line of jurisprudence — in particular the expanding conception of liberty as encompassing personal autonomy — to hold that criminalising consensual adult intimacy based on the identity of the participants rather than the nature of harm inflicted failed the constitutional test of reasonableness.

Second, on Article 15, the Court held that the word "sex" in Article 15(1)'s prohibition on discrimination based on sex encompasses discrimination based on sexual orientation, because sexual orientation is inextricably linked to sex. This reading extended the anti-discrimination guarantee to a ground not explicitly named in the Constitution, using purposive interpretation to give effect to the provision's equality-protecting rationale. The Court also held that the mere fact that a majority disapproves of a minority group's conduct cannot constitute a legitimate constitutional justification for criminalisation — a significant affirmation of the counter-majoritarian function of fundamental rights.

Third, the Court engaged explicitly with the argument that cultural and religious morality justified Section 377. It held that constitutional morality — the values of dignity, liberty, and equality embedded in the Constitution — must prevail over social morality when the two conflict, particularly in the domain of personal autonomy.

## Long-term impact

The Supreme Court reversed the Naz Foundation judgment in Suresh Kumar Koushal v. Naz Foundation (2013), holding — by a two-judge bench — that Section 377 was not unconstitutional because it affected "a minuscule fraction" of the population, a reasoning widely criticised as analytically and constitutionally inadequate. The reversal returned LGBTQ+ persons to criminal jeopardy for five years. In 2018, a five-judge Constitution Bench in Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India unanimously struck down Section 377 as applied to consensual adult acts, expressly overruling Koushal and substantially adopting the reasoning of the Naz Foundation High Court. The Constitution Bench in Navtej Johar held that the right to privacy under Article 21 — as confirmed in the nine-judge Puttaswamy judgment of 2017 — protects sexual orientation as a core component of identity, and that historical criminalisation based on Victorian-era morality was unconstitutional. The Naz Foundation judgment is thus the direct constitutional ancestor of Navtej Johar and of the subsequent constitutional litigation on same-sex partnership rights.
