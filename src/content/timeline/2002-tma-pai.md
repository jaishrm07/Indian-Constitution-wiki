The judgment became a durable reference point for minority educational institutions, admissions policy, and the balance between autonomy and regulation.

## What happened

T.M.A. Pai Foundation and connected cases came before an eleven-judge Constitution Bench — the largest bench to consider a single matter in the Court's modern history — to settle persistent disputes about the scope of Articles 29 and 30 in the context of education. The litigation had been building since St. Stephen's and Unni Krishnan, and the bench was constituted to comprehensively restate the constitutional position on the rights of minority institutions, the power of state regulation, and the rights of non-minority private institutions. The Court's judgment in October 2002 held, among other things, that the unit for determining minority status is the state, not the country as a whole; that minority institutions have the right to admit students of their choice but not to the exclusion of merit; and that private unaided non-minority institutions also have a significant sphere of autonomy in administration.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

T.M.A. Pai substantially reorganised the constitutional law of educational institutions across several dimensions. It expanded autonomy for private unaided institutions — both minority and non-minority — over admissions and fee structures, while drawing clearer lines about when state regulation is permissible as ensuring standards versus when it crosses into controlling the institution. The judgment clarified that the Unni Krishnan scheme which had tightly regulated private professional college admissions was too restrictive, rebalancing the field toward institutional freedom. It also settled the geographic basis for minority determination, which had significant practical consequences for national institutions claiming minority status.

## Its legacy

T.M.A. Pai did not fully resolve all the litigation it sought to address. Uncertainties about fee regulation and compulsory quotas in aided minority institutions required a further reference, which produced the P.A. Inamdar ruling in 2005. Together, T.M.A. Pai and P.A. Inamdar define the modern constitutional settlement for private educational institutions, one that Parliament partially disturbed through the Ninety-Third Amendment and the Right to Education Act, generating yet another round of litigation about whether applying the RTE Act to minority and private unaided institutions was constitutional.
