## What happened

The Berubari Union was an area of approximately 8.7 square kilometres situated in the Jalpaiguri district of West Bengal. Under the Nehru–Noon Pact of 1958, India agreed to partition the Berubari Union, ceding the southern half to Pakistan while retaining the northern half. The implementation of the pact raised an immediate constitutional question: what legislative or constitutional vehicle was required for India to cede territory to a foreign state?

President Rajendra Prasad referred the question to the Supreme Court under Article 143(1), asking whether the implementation of the Nehru–Noon Pact required legislation by Parliament, whether such legislation had to be a constitutional amendment under Article 368, or whether an ordinary law under Article 3 would suffice. An eight-judge Constitution Bench delivered its advisory opinion on 14 March 1960. The Court held unanimously that the cession of Berubari Union to Pakistan was not achievable through ordinary legislation under Article 3 (which governs alteration of the boundaries of states) and required a constitutional amendment under Article 368.

## Constitutional reasoning

The Court's reasoning turned on the nature of territorial sovereignty under the Constitution. Article 1(3) defines the territory of India to include the territories of the states, union territories, and such other territories as may be acquired. The First Schedule to the Constitution specifically listed the territory of each state, including West Bengal. The Court held that ceding territory identified in the First Schedule necessarily required altering the Schedule, which could only be done by constitutional amendment. An ordinary law under Article 3 could reorganise or adjust state boundaries, but could not remove territory from the constitutional definition of "India" and transfer it to a foreign sovereign.

The advisory opinion established an important constitutional proposition: India's territorial integrity is embedded in the Constitution's text (Article 1 and the First Schedule) at a level that places it beyond the reach of ordinary parliamentary legislation. This was significant not only for the immediate question of Berubari but as a general principle about the relationship between the Union's treaty-making power and its constitutional obligations to the states and to the integrity of the territory defined in the Constitution.

## Consequences and the Ninth Amendment

Following the advisory opinion, Parliament enacted the Constitution (Ninth Amendment) Act 1960, which amended the First Schedule to implement the partition of the Berubari Union as agreed under the Nehru–Noon Pact. The amendment came into force on 28 December 1960. The Ninth Amendment is historically modest in its substantive content — it affected a small area and was driven by a specific diplomatic agreement — but its constitutional significance lies in the principle it confirms: that territorial cession requires formal constitutional amendment, embedding the process in the most demanding legislative procedure available rather than leaving it to executive treaty-making or ordinary legislation.

The Berubari opinion also illustrates the role of Article 143 advisory jurisdiction as a constitutional planning tool. By seeking the Court's opinion before attempting to implement the pact through legislation, the government was able to identify the correct constitutional instrument without enacting unconstitutional legislation. The Berubari principle was later invoked in subsequent territorial questions, including discussions about the status of territories transferred under later agreements with Bangladesh (resolved through the Hundredth Amendment in 2015), confirming the durability of the Court's 1960 holding.
