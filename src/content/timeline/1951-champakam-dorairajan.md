Champakam Dorairajan is the opening chapter of the modern reservation debate because it forced equality and representation to be written together more clearly.

## What happened

Srimathi Champakam Dorairajan, a Brahmin woman, was denied admission to a state medical college in Madras despite having higher marks than candidates admitted under a communal reservation scheme that divided seats among different religious and caste communities. She challenged the Communal Government Order that prescribed these reservations. The Supreme Court struck down the Communal Government Order as violating Article 15(1), which prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. The Court held that Article 29(2), which guarantees the right of citizens not to be denied admission to state-maintained educational institutions on those same grounds, applied directly and that the reservation order fell squarely within that prohibition.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

The judgment exposed a tension that the Constitution's framers had not fully resolved: Article 15(4) had not yet been inserted, meaning there was no express constitutional authorisation for the state to make special provision for backward classes in education. The Court's ruling that the equality norm overrode the reservation order created an immediate political impasse because several states had relied on communal reservations as a tool of social policy. The decision forced Parliament to confront squarely whether the Constitution as drafted was adequate to support affirmative action in education, or whether explicit textual authorisation was required.

## Its legacy

The direct legislative response was the First Amendment, which inserted Article 15(4) to authorise special provisions for socially and educationally backward classes and scheduled castes and tribes in education. Champakam Dorairajan thus triggered the first constitutional amendment dealing with reservations and set the template for a pattern in which adverse judicial decisions on affirmative action prompted textual amendments expanding the state's authority, a cycle that continued into the debates over the Mandal Commission and the 93rd Amendment.
