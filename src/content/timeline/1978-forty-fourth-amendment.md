## What happened

The Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act 1978 was enacted by the Janata government that had come to power after the 1977 general elections held following the end of the Emergency. The amendment had multiple components addressing different aspects of the Emergency-era constitutional damage.

On emergency powers, the amendment amended Article 352 to require that a Cabinet decision to advise the President to proclaim an emergency must be communicated in writing. It substituted "armed rebellion" for "internal disturbance" as a valid ground for proclamation, making the threshold substantially higher. It provided for mandatory review of any proclamation by Parliament and for revocation on a resolution passed by a simple majority in the Lok Sabha.

On fundamental rights during emergency, the amendment amended Article 359 to provide that the suspension of the right to move courts to enforce fundamental rights could never extend to Articles 20 and 21, making the right against ex post facto punishment, double jeopardy, self-incrimination, and the right to life and personal liberty non-derogable even during a proclaimed emergency.

The amendment also omitted Article 329A, which the Thirty-Ninth Amendment had inserted to protect the Prime Minister's election from judicial review, and reversed several other Forty-Second Amendment changes that had curtailed judicial power.

On property rights, the amendment removed Article 31 from Part III, ending the right to property as a fundamental right and relocating property protection to Article 300A as an ordinary constitutional right, resolving the long-running conflict between Parliament and the courts over land reform and nationalisation.

## Constitutional significance

The Forty-Fourth Amendment addressed the Emergency's constitutional lessons on two levels. First, it repaired the formal constitutional text by removing the provisions that the Emergency government had used or inserted to entrench its power. Second, and more importantly, it built new structural safeguards into the emergency provisions themselves — safeguards that had not existed in the original text and whose absence had enabled the Emergency's constitutional abuses.

The non-suspendability of Articles 20 and 21 directly responded to the ADM Jabalpur decision, ensuring that even if a future government suspended other fundamental rights during an emergency, it could never again legally extinguish the right to life and personal liberty or the protections against arbitrary criminal procedure.

## Long-term impact

The Forty-Fourth Amendment fundamentally changed the constitutional architecture of emergency powers. No emergency has been proclaimed at the national level since 1977, and the stronger procedural requirements — written Cabinet resolution, higher substantive threshold, mandatory parliamentary review — have raised the political and legal costs of proclamation substantially. The amendment's removal of property from the fundamental rights chapter resolved the property-rights-versus-land-reform conflict that had generated the First, Fourth, and Twenty-Fifth Amendments and multiple confrontations between Parliament and the Supreme Court, and it remains the constitutional settlement on property rights today.
