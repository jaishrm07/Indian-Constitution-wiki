Kargil is one of the few cases in Indian constitutional history where the emergency provisions of Part XVIII were genuinely tested by circumstances — and not invoked. The decision not to proclaim a formal Emergency, and the successful conduct of the conflict without one, is itself constitutionally informative.

## What happened

Between May and July 1999, Pakistani military forces and affiliated militants occupied strategic peaks in the Kargil district of Jammu and Kashmir. The Indian Army launched Operation Vijay to recapture the positions, culminating in the withdrawal of Pakistani forces by 26 July 1999, which is commemorated as Kargil Vijay Diwas. The conflict involved sustained conventional military operations including artillery exchanges, ground assault, and air strikes, and resulted in significant casualties on both sides. Throughout the conflict the government of Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee did not proclaim a National Emergency under Article 352, nor was President's Rule extended or modified under Article 356 in Jammu and Kashmir. The conflict was conducted entirely under the ordinary legal framework, including the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act and the Army Act, without resort to the emergency provisions of Part XVIII of the Constitution.

## Constitutional significance

Article 352 permits a proclamation of National Emergency when the security of India or any part thereof is threatened by war, external aggression, or armed rebellion. The Kargil War, involving armed intrusion by a foreign state's forces, would have met the constitutional threshold for a war-based emergency. However, proclaiming a National Emergency under Article 352 (as amended by the Forty-Fourth Amendment in 1978) requires a written cabinet recommendation, approval by both Houses of Parliament by special majority within one month, and carries significant consequences including the suspension of state governments' powers. These requirements, combined with the compressed timeframe of the conflict and the political risks of a formal emergency declaration, made proclamation practically and politically unsuitable. Article 355, which imposes a duty on the Union to protect states against external aggression and internal disturbance, provided a structural constitutional basis for the Union's military response without requiring formal emergency procedures.

## Long-term impact

The Kargil conflict reinforced a pattern in Indian constitutional practice where the formal emergency provisions, made more stringent by the Forty-Fourth Amendment to prevent abuse after 1975, are effectively unavailable for short or geographically limited conflicts. The broad executive powers available to the Union government under ordinary law — including the army's authority to conduct operations — proved sufficient for the conflict's duration. Kargil also prompted significant constitutional and policy reflection on civil-military relations, parliamentary oversight of defence decisions, and the accountability mechanisms available during armed conflict conducted without formal emergency law. The Kargil Review Committee's report, submitted in 2000, led to institutional reforms in defence governance without any constitutional amendment.
