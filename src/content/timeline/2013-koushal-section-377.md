## What happened

In July 2009, the Delhi High Court in Naz Foundation v. Government of NCT of Delhi had held that Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code, insofar as it criminalised consensual sexual acts between adults in private, violated Articles 14, 15, and 21 of the Constitution. The High Court read down the provision to exclude such acts from its ambit, producing the first judicial recognition of equal dignity and anti-discrimination protection for LGBTQ persons in Indian constitutional law. The government did not appeal, but a group of religious and social organisations — including Suresh Kumar Koushal — filed appeals in the Supreme Court. On 11 December 2013, a two-judge bench of the Supreme Court reversed the High Court judgment and restored Section 377 in full. The bench held that the High Court had erred in striking down a provision of Parliament's making on the basis that it affected a small fraction of the population, and that the question of whether to retain or amend Section 377 was for Parliament rather than the courts to decide. The judgment did not engage substantively with the fundamental rights arguments on Articles 14, 15, and 21 that the High Court had accepted.

## Why it mattered constitutionally

Koushal is an anomaly in the arc of Indian fundamental rights jurisprudence: a Supreme Court decision that withdrew constitutional protection already accorded by a High Court. Its holding that a law criminalising conduct could survive constitutional scrutiny simply because it affected a minority, and that the legislature rather than the courts was the appropriate forum for rights reform, was in sharp tension with the Court's own foundational decisions. The judgment was criticised for inverting the purpose of fundamental rights — which exist precisely to protect minorities against majoritarian legislation — and for a failure to engage with the equal protection and dignity framework developed in Maneka Gandhi, E.P. Royappa, and the Court's own precedents on Articles 14 and 21. The refusal to apply the doctrine of reading down, and the dismissal of the LGBTQ community's constitutional claims as concerning only a negligible fraction of the population, drew sustained academic and civil society criticism. The decision also raised questions about the institutional role of the Supreme Court when it declines to apply constitutional scrutiny to a penal provision that the government itself had chosen not to defend.

## Its overruling and legacy

The Koushal judgment was overruled five years later in Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018), where a five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously read down Section 377 to exclude consensual adult same-sex conduct and held that the provision, as originally applied, violated Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21. The bench in Navtej was explicit in its rejection of Koushal's reasoning, particularly its dismissal of the rights of sexual minorities as insufficiently numerous to attract constitutional protection. Koushal therefore stands as a significant negative precedent — a demonstration of constitutional retrogression and of the costs, measured in years of continued criminalisation, when a court declines to apply rights review to a discriminatory penal law. Its overruling also illustrated the remedial capacity of constitutional litigation and the role of a larger bench in correcting earlier misreadings of fundamental rights.
