accessNotes: |
  Five-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 26 September 2018 (majority 4:1). Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation (2019) 1 SCC 1. The majority (Dipak Misra CJ, A.K. Sikri, A.M. Khanwilkar, and Ashok Bhushan JJ) upheld the core of the Aadhaar Act, 2016 and held that biometric-based identification for welfare delivery does not violate the right to privacy established in Puttaswamy (2017), finding it proportionate and backed by law. However, the majority struck down Section 57, which permitted private entities including banks and telecom companies to use Aadhaar for authentication, holding that private use of Aadhaar data lacked sufficient statutory justification and was disproportionate. Justice D.Y. Chandrachud dissented on two grounds — first, that the Aadhaar Act was wrongly certified as a Money Bill under Article 110, since it contained provisions well beyond Money Bill subjects, and this misuse amounted to a fraud on the Constitution; second, that the Act failed the proportionality test because less intrusive alternatives existed. The dissent also warned of the surveillance potential of a centralised biometric database. The validity of the Speaker's Money Bill certification remains a contested constitutional question.
description: Five-judge Constitution Bench judgment (4:1) upholding the Aadhaar Act's use of biometric identification for welfare delivery while striking down provisions permitting private entities to use Aadhaar data. The ruling is notable for Justice Chandrachud's powerful dissent challenging both the Money Bill classification of the Act — arguing it constituted a fraud on the Constitution — and the majority's application of the proportionality framework to mass biometric surveillance.
