## The constitutional frame

Delimitation sits at the intersection of representation and federal design. Even when the technical language is about seats and constituencies, the constitutional effect is about how the Union maps population change into political power.

## What readers should watch

The core signals are whether Parliament, the Election Commission, or the states define delimitation as a one-time administrative exercise or as part of a broader representational settlement that also includes women's reservation.

## Chronology

- **1976 — 42nd Amendment**: Parliament froze the total number of seats in the Lok Sabha and in State Legislative Assemblies at their 1971 levels and suspended fresh delimitation of constituency boundaries until after the census of 2001, in order to remove any electoral disincentive to family planning programmes.
- **2002 — 84th Amendment**: The freeze on total seat numbers was extended until the first census after 2026, meaning the count of parliamentary and assembly seats cannot be altered by delimitation until that census is taken and published. Constituency boundaries, however, could be adjusted on the basis of 2001 census data.
- **2002–2008 — Delimitation Commission**: A Delimitation Commission constituted under the Delimitation Act 2002 completed a comprehensive readjustment of constituency boundaries across States based on 2001 census data, but without altering total seat numbers.
- **2021 census (deferred)**: The decennial census that was due in 2021 was postponed because of the COVID-19 pandemic and as of early 2026 had not been conducted, leaving the data foundation for any post-2026 delimitation absent.
- **2026 — freeze lapses**: The constitutional bar on increasing total Lok Sabha and assembly seats expires after 2026, creating the first legal window for a full delimitation — including potential changes to seat counts — once the pending census is completed and figures are published.
- **2023 — 106th Amendment intersection**: The Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 made the commencement of women's reservation contingent on the next delimitation after the relevant census, directly linking the two processes and raising the political stakes of the delimitation timeline.

## What is constitutionally at stake

Delimitation affects the ratio of voters to elected representatives and therefore the practical equality of the vote across constituencies and States; a fresh delimitation based on current population data would substantially redistribute seats from States that achieved lower population growth rates to States with higher growth. Southern States — which reduced fertility rates through sustained development investment and social policy over decades — fear that a seat recount under current population would significantly shrink their share of Lok Sabha seats relative to more populous northern States, altering the federal balance of parliamentary representation in a way those States regard as a penalty for complying with national population policy. This is a federalism tension as much as a democratic representation question, testing whether the constitutional framework for delimitation — which ties seats to population — adequately protects States that achieved the development goals the original freeze was designed to incentivise. The constitutional provisions at stake are Articles 81 and 170, which govern the composition of the House of the People and State Legislatures respectively, read alongside the Tenth Schedule of the Delimitation Act and the representation-equality principle that underlies Articles 324 and 329. An additional constitutional dimension arises from the interaction with the 106th Amendment: the longer delimitation is deferred to manage the north-south tension, the longer women's reservation remains inoperative, creating compounding pressure on Parliament to find a sequence that satisfies both federal fairness and gender representation commitments simultaneously.

## How to follow this issue

Monitor the census completion announcement from the Office of the Registrar General and Census Commissioner of India, which is the necessary precondition for any post-2026 delimitation, and watch for the official Gazette notification constituting a new Delimitation Commission under the Delimitation Act once census figures are finalised. Parliamentary statements — particularly in Question Hour and during budget debates — on the timeline and terms of reference for delimitation are the clearest signal of executive intent, and any proposal to separate the women's reservation commencement from delimitation would appear first in those forums. The Election Commission of India's website and the Sansad.in portal for parliamentary proceedings, supplemented by PRS Legislative Research, are the most reliable public sources for tracking both the administrative and legislative dimensions of this issue.

## Sources and how to verify

- **Constitution of India** (`constitution-of-india`): Articles 81, 82, 170, 171, 330, and 332 govern House composition, state representation, and delimitation authority.
- **Sansad** (`sansad`): Parliamentary proceedings and questions on census timeline and delimitation commission notification.
- **Election Commission of India** (`election-commission-of-india`): Delimitation Commission reports and constituency boundary notifications once delimitation is triggered.
- **PRS Legislative Research** (`prs-india`): Bill-level analysis of delimitation legislation and background papers on seat allocation formulas.
- **Vidhi Centre for Legal Policy** (`vidhi-centre-for-legal-policy`): Analysis of constitutional constraints on delimitation design and the north-south representation challenge.
- **UN World Population Prospects 2024** (`un-world-population-prospects-2024`): Comparable cross-country population and aging baselines that help contextualise population-change arguments, but should be used as background rather than as the legal authority for delimitation design.
