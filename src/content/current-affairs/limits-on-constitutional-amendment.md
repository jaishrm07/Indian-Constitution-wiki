## The constitutional frame

Article 368 creates the formal route for amendment, but formal validity does not end the inquiry. The deeper constitutional question is whether the proposed change preserves the document's identity as interpreted through doctrine.

## What readers should watch

The difficult cases are not routine textual updates. They are proposals that alter institutional relationships, democratic guarantees, judicial review, or the federal compact.

## Key constitutional provisions

Article 368 sets out the amendment procedure, distinguishing ordinary special-majority amendments from those requiring ratification by at least half the state legislatures, but it does not on its face impose any substantive ceiling on Parliament's power to amend. Kesavananda Bharati changed that in 1973 by holding, by a bare majority, that Parliament cannot use Article 368 to destroy or abrogate the Constitution's basic structure, even though the specific features of that structure were left for case-by-case elaboration. Minerva Mills reinforced the doctrine in 1980 by striking down clauses that sought to immunise amendments from judicial review, ruling that the power to amend is itself limited and that removing the courts' ability to enforce those limits would itself violate the basic structure. I.R. Coelho extended the doctrine to the Ninth Schedule, holding that even laws placed there after 1973 must be tested for compatibility with fundamental rights that form part of the basic structure. The working principle is that formal compliance with Article 368 is necessary but not sufficient: a court will still ask whether the amendment's substance undermines democracy, federalism, the separation of powers, or judicial review.
