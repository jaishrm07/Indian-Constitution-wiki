## The constitutional frame

Candidate disclosure is no longer a courtesy or a campaign preference. Once the Court treated voter knowledge as part of meaningful democratic choice, the publication of candidate data became part of the constitutional environment in which elections are held.

## What readers should watch

The key signals are whether affidavit information remains easy to inspect before polling, whether criminal-antecedent disclosure is standardized and complete, and whether qualification rules are enforced quickly enough for voters to rely on them.

## Chronology

- **2002:** Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India — the Supreme Court holds that voters have a fundamental right under Article 19(1)(a) to information about candidates, including criminal antecedents, assets, and liabilities, and directs the Election Commission to require such disclosures.
- **2003:** PUCL v. Union of India — the Court reiterates and expands disclosure requirements; candidates must disclose whether they have been convicted or have pending criminal charges framed by a court.
- **2005:** The Representation of the People Act amended to implement some disclosure requirements through statutory affidavit formats.
- **2013:** Lily Thomas v. Union of India — the Supreme Court holds that a conviction carrying two or more years of imprisonment results in immediate disqualification; a stay of conviction pending appeal does not prevent disqualification from taking effect.
- **2018:** Public Interest Foundation v. Union of India — the Court directs political parties to publish reasons for fielding candidates with serious criminal charges, moving obligations from candidates alone to the parties that select them.
- **2020:** The Supreme Court directs parties to publish criminal antecedents of candidates on their official websites and in newspapers within 48 hours of selection, and to submit compliance reports to the Election Commission.
- **2024:** Affidavit scrutiny by NGOs including the Association for Democratic Reforms reveals the percentage of candidates with criminal cases remains high; Enforcement Directorate and SFIO attachment orders against sitting legislators draw renewed attention to the enforcement gap.

## What is constitutionally at stake

The right to vote under Article 326 and the right to information under Article 19(1)(a) together require that voters have adequate information about candidates to exercise a meaningful franchise. The criminalisation-of-politics problem has two distinct constitutional threads: the disclosure question — do voters know before they vote — and the disqualification question — when does criminal conduct make a candidate constitutionally ineligible. The disclosure machinery now exists through successive Supreme Court orders, but its effectiveness depends on party compliance and the searchability of the Election Commission's affidavit infrastructure. The deeper and unresolved reform question is whether candidates under trial for serious offences should be barred from standing, a step that would require legislative action Parliament has so far not taken. The tension between the right to stand for election and the right of voters to an uncriminalised legislature remains the live constitutional fault line.

## How to follow this issue

Monitor the Election Commission's affidavit database and the myneta.info portal maintained by the Association for Democratic Reforms for candidate declaration compliance across general and state elections. Track Supreme Court cases on party disclosure obligations and any contempt or compliance proceedings arising from the 2018 and 2020 directions. Watch for private member or government bills on disqualification of under-trial candidates, and review Election Commission press notes issued at the start of each election phase for updates on the affidavit and KYC framework.

- **eSCR** (`escr`): Retrieve Association for Democratic Reforms (2002) 5 SCC 294, PUCL v. Union of India (2003) 4 SCC 399, Lily Thomas (2013) 7 SCC 653, and Public Interest Foundation (2019) 3 SCC 224 for the full doctrinal sequence.

- **ECI Phase One Press Note 2024** (`eci-phase-one-press-note-2024`): Election Commission data on candidates' declared criminal antecedents from the 2024 general election.
- **Law Commission of India** (`law-commission-of-india`): Earlier Law Commission reports on electoral disqualification and criminalisation of politics for reform context.
- **Vidhi Centre for Legal Policy** (`vidhi-centre-for-legal-policy`): Analysis of disclosure enforcement and reform options for under-trial candidate disqualification.
- **Supreme Court Observer** (`supreme-court-observer`): Monitoring of compliance proceedings on the 2018 and 2020 party-disclosure directions.
