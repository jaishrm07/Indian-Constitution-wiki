## The constitutional frame

Speech regulation questions are not limited to censorship alone. They also involve procedural fairness, transparency, overbreadth, and whether regulatory burdens chill lawful speech.

## What readers should watch

The most important legal questions are whether restrictions map onto constitutionally permitted grounds, whether they are proportionate, and whether affected persons have meaningful notice and remedy.

## Key constitutional provisions

Article 19(1)(a) guarantees every citizen the right to freedom of speech and expression, a protection the Supreme Court has extended to online expression including websites, social media, and digital journalism. Article 19(2) permits the state to impose restrictions on speech, but only by law and only on enumerated grounds — sovereignty, security, public order, decency, morality, contempt of court, defamation, and incitement — and any restriction must be reasonable in scope and proportionate in effect. Shreya Singhal struck down Section 66A of the Information Technology Act in 2015 because it criminalised speech far beyond any Article 19(2) ground, used terms too vague to give citizens fair notice, and had a chilling effect on lawful expression — establishing the baseline that digital speech restrictions must satisfy the same constitutional discipline as restrictions on any other form of speech. Subsequent platform-regulation frameworks, including rules on takedowns, grievance mechanisms, and content oversight, continue to be tested against that baseline: mandatory compliance architecture that coerces platforms into over-removal, or that lacks procedural safeguards for speakers, risks failing the reasonableness requirement even where the stated objective falls within Article 19(2).

## Sources and how to verify
- **Constitution of India** (`constitution-of-india`): Articles 19(1)(a) and 19(2) are the primary constitutional text; read them with the Shreya Singhal judgment's interpretation of the exhaustive restriction grounds.
- **eSCR** (`escr`): Retrieve Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015) 5 SCC 1 and Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India (2020) 3 SCC 637 for the foundational speech and internet doctrine.
- **e-Gazette of India** (`egazette-of-india`): Official IT Rules amendments and Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology notifications.
- **Sansad** (`sansad`): Parliamentary debates and questions on the IT Amendment Bills and Data Protection Bill deliberations.
- **PRS Legislative Research** (`prs-india`): Structured analysis of IT Rules 2021, IT Amendment Act 2023, and related digital regulation Bills.
- **Vidhi Centre for Legal Policy** (`vidhi-centre-for-legal-policy`): In-depth constitutional analysis of intermediary liability, content moderation, and proportionality.
- **Live Law** (`live-law`): Monitoring of active High Court and Supreme Court challenges to IT Rules and content regulation orders.
