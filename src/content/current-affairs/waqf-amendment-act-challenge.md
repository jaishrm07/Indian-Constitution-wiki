## The constitutional frame

Waqf is a permanent dedication of property by a Muslim for purposes recognised as pious, religious, or charitable by Islam. Statutory waqf boards — created under the Waqf Act 1954 and its successor legislation — are public statutory bodies charged with surveying, protecting, and managing properties that have been dedicated as waqf. The legal architecture sits at the intersection of statutory administration and religious institution.

Articles 25 and 26 form the core of the challenge. Article 25 guarantees freedom of conscience and the right freely to profess, practise, and propagate religion, subject to public order, morality, and health. Article 26 gives every religious denomination the right to manage its own affairs in matters of religion, to establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes, and to administer property in accordance with law. The essential-religious-practices doctrine — developed from Shirur Mutt (1954) through a long line of cases — asks whether the impugned practice or institution is essential to or integral to the religion; if it is, the State may not abolish or substantially alter it through legislation.

Article 30 gives minorities — whether based on religion or language — the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice. Although waqf boards are not educational institutions, the Article 30 argument in this litigation draws on the broader principle that minorities have a constitutionally protected sphere of institutional self-management.

## What the 2025 Act changed

The Waqf (Amendment) Act 2025 made several structural changes to the 1995 framework. State Waqf Boards, previously composed entirely of Muslims, were required to include two non-Muslim members. The Central Waqf Council was similarly opened to non-Muslim members. The definitional provisions for waqf by user — properties that had been treated as waqf on the basis of long-standing religious use even without a formal deed — were altered in ways that petitioners argue will result in many historically recognised waqf properties losing that status. District Collectors were empowered to determine, where a dispute arises, whether a property is government land or waqf; pending that determination, the property would be treated as government land.

## The petitions

Petitioners before the Supreme Court advanced arguments on several constitutional grounds. On Articles 25 and 26, they argued that waqf is a fundamental institution of Islamic religious practice and that a body charged with managing waqf properties must be composed of Muslims in order to give effect to the religious purposes of the endowment; non-Muslim members cannot be bound by or adequately understand the religious obligations that govern waqf administration. The imposition of non-Muslim members was said to fundamentally alter the denominational character of the institution.

On Article 14, petitioners argued that no comparable non-Muslim religious endowment board — whether a Hindu Charitable Endowments Board or a Sikh Gurdwara Prabandhak Committee — is required to include members of other religions; treating Muslim waqf boards alone in this manner lacked a rational basis and was discriminatory.

On the Collector's determination power, petitioners argued that giving a revenue officer authority to provisionally and determinatively characterise disputed properties as government land — with the practical consequence that the waqf board loses custody pending litigation — created an unequal procedural framework that pre-emptively dispossessed waqf of contested properties.

## The Union's defence

The Union government defended the Act on the ground that Waqf Boards are statutory creatures of Parliament and not purely religious bodies; their composition, powers, and procedures are therefore within Parliament's legislative competence and need not conform to religious requirements. Adding non-Muslim members was characterised as ensuring diversity of perspective in the administration of a public statutory body managing properties that often abut or interact with non-waqf properties. The definition changes were defended as necessary to curb fraudulent claims of waqf by user that had encroached on private and government land. The Collector's power was characterised as a procedural mechanism to resolve jurisdictional disputes efficiently.

## The Supreme Court's interim position

On 17 April 2025 the Court stayed the Collector's determination power. The bench noted that if properties were treated as government land and transferred or developed pending the final decision, that could not be undone — making the interim stay essential to preserve the subject matter. The Union gave assurances that no appointments to the Central Waqf Council or State Waqf Boards under the amended composition scheme would be made and no waqf — including waqf by user — would be de-notified or have its character changed until the next hearing. These assurances were recorded as undertakings by the Court.

## What to watch

The core question before the Constitution Bench is whether the statutory character of Waqf Boards is sufficient to fully displace the Articles 25 and 26 analysis. The Shirur Mutt line of cases draws a distinction between the right of a denomination to manage its own affairs in matters of religion — which the State cannot abrogate — and the regulation of secular or administrative aspects of a religious institution — which the State may undertake in the public interest. Whether waqf board composition is on the religious side or the administrative side of that line will be the central doctrinal question.

The Court's treatment of the Collector's determination power will also signal how the bench views the property-rights dimension of the challenge. Waqf properties are connected to Article 26's protection of religious denomination property; any permanent transfer of such property to government through an administrative procedure that bypasses the Waqf Tribunal raises procedural-due-process concerns under Article 21 read with Article 300A.
