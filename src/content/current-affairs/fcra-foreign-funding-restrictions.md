## The constitutional frame

Article 19(1)(c) guarantees every citizen the right to form associations or unions. Article 19(4) permits the State to impose reasonable restrictions on this right in the interests of public order or morality or the sovereignty and integrity of India. Article 19(2) is the equivalent restriction clause for freedom of speech and expression. Article 14 requires that any classificatory scheme treating organisations differently have an intelligible differentia bearing a rational nexus to the legislative object.

The FCRA challenge centred on whether restrictions on the mode of receiving and deploying foreign funding curtail the associational right itself — or merely regulate one source of funding — and whether that distinction is constitutionally meaningful when, for many organisations, foreign contributions are the only viable funding stream.

## What happened

The Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act 2010 already required civil-society organisations to register, maintain a designated FCRA bank account, and limit administrative expenditure. The Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Amendment Act 2020 introduced three principal changes that generated constitutional challenge.

First, the permissible administrative-overhead share was reduced from 50 percent to 20 percent of total foreign-contribution receipts. Second, organisations registered under the FCRA were prohibited from transferring received funds to any other person or organisation — eliminating sub-grants through which larger NGOs had channelled resources to smaller grassroots bodies. Third, all FCRA accounts were required to be maintained exclusively at the State Bank of India's New Delhi Main Branch, regardless of where an organisation was headquartered or operated.

Petitioners including Noel Harper and the Indian Social Action Forum challenged these amendments before the Supreme Court. The Union defended all three provisions on grounds of national security, prevention of money laundering, and the government's legitimate interest in ensuring that foreign funds do not influence domestic politics or policy.

## The Noel Harper judgment

On 6 April 2022 a three-judge bench of the Supreme Court dismissed all challenges and upheld the 2020 amendments. The Court held that receipt of foreign contributions is not a fundamental right under Article 19(1)(c); the right to form an association does not carry with it an absolute entitlement to be funded from abroad. Accordingly, the State's power to regulate the terms on which foreign money enters the country involves a lower level of scrutiny than a direct restriction on associational activity itself.

On the 20-percent overhead cap, the Court held that the limitation was reasonable and bore a rational nexus to the object of preventing foreign funds from being diverted to administrative costs at the expense of developmental or charitable purposes. On the sub-grant prohibition, the Court held that the government could legitimately insist on accountability at each registered entity rather than allowing chains of transfer that might obscure the ultimate use of funds. On the mandatory SBI New Delhi Main Branch requirement, the Court held that centralised banking facilitated easier audit and monitoring and was a regulatory convenience rather than an impermissible burden.

## The live controversy

Despite the judgment being final, the controversy it generated has not settled. The Court's reasoning has attracted sustained criticism for treating the regulatory burden on associations as categorically different from a restriction on associational rights, without adequately analysing whether, in practice, the cumulative effect of all three amendments substantially impairs the ability of an organisation to function.

The mandatory SBI New Delhi Main Branch condition has drawn particular criticism. For organisations based in southern or north-eastern India, routing every foreign-contribution transaction through a single branch in the capital imposes operational costs and delays disproportionate to any plausible monitoring benefit. Critics argue this is not a neutral administrative arrangement but a de facto gatekeeping mechanism that creates centralised visibility over which organisations receive foreign funds and how they spend them.

The cancellation of FCRA licences for Amnesty India and Oxfam India — which occurred through administrative orders issued by the Ministry of Home Affairs — sit outside the scope of the Noel Harper constitutional challenge but illustrate the practical stakes. Once a licence is cancelled, an organisation cannot receive any foreign contribution; for organisations that depend substantially on foreign institutional funding, cancellation is functionally equivalent to dissolution.

## What to watch

The Noel Harper judgment does not foreclose all further constitutional challenge. A direct challenge to a specific licence cancellation on Article 14 grounds — arguing that the Ministry targeted organisations based on the viewpoints they expressed — would raise a different question from the structural validity of the 2020 amendments. The distinction between upholding the statutory framework and immunising every administrative application of it from review remains open.

A future Constitution Bench reconsidering the scope of Article 19(1)(c) in a different context might revisit the proposition that funding conditions on associations attract no meaningful constitutional scrutiny. Whether any such reconsideration extends to the FCRA context will depend on how broadly the Court frames the question of associational freedom and its relationship to effective exercise of other Part III rights.
