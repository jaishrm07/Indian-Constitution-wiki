## The constitutional frame

The Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 amended Part XV and Part XVI of the Constitution by inserting Articles 330A, 332A, and 334A. Article 330A reserves not less than one-third of the total seats in the House of the People for women, including seats reserved for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. Article 332A makes the equivalent provision for State Legislative Assemblies. Article 334A, which is the operationally critical provision, states that the reservation shall come into effect after the first census taken after the commencement of the Act and after delimitation on the basis of that census — and that it shall cease to have effect fifteen years from the date it comes into force.

The structural consequence is that Articles 330A and 332A are constitutional text but cannot be exercised until Article 334A's commencement conditions are fulfilled. The commencement conditions depend entirely on two executive and legislative acts — the conduct of a census, and a delimitation exercise — for neither of which does the Constitution or the amending Act specify any deadline.

## The deferral mechanism and the constitutional challenge

The precise constitutional challenge is that Parliament has created a right that is unenforceable for an indeterminate period, and that the body in whose power it lies to remove the deferral — the Union government, which controls census scheduling and can constitute a Delimitation Commission — has no judicial obligation to act by any particular date. Critics argue that this is not a permissible staging mechanism but a structural nullification of the amendment. They contend that if a constitutional amendment can be inserted alongside a commencement condition that the executive can indefinitely delay simply by not conducting a census, the amendment does not in substance change the law, and the constitutional promise it makes is illusory.

The Union's likely response is that Parliament has broad discretion over the implementation architecture of constitutional amendments, and that the choice to link women's reservation to delimitation serves a coherent purpose: ensuring that reserved constituencies are drawn afresh rather than carved out of existing male-held constituencies, which would produce unequal constituency sizes. The argument is that the sequencing is rational even if inconvenient. The question for the Court is whether rationality of design is sufficient, or whether a rights guarantee requires a justiciable timetable.

## Why delimitation was chosen as the trigger

Parliament's stated rationale for linking women's reservation to delimitation was practical: under the existing Lok Sabha seat-count freeze (in place since the 42nd Amendment of 1976, extended by the 84th Amendment until after the census after 2026), there is no room to create one-third reserved seats by adding new constituencies. The reservation can only be created by designating existing seats as reserved for women — which requires constituency boundary readjustment to avoid gross disparities in voter-to-representative ratios. A full delimitation exercise was seen as the cleanest way to achieve this. The constitutional difficulty is that this rational administrative choice was embedded in the constitutional text itself, tying an equality provision to an administrative exercise that could take decades to occur.

## What has happened since 2023

The 2021 decennial census — which was already due before the amendment was enacted — has still not been conducted as of early 2026. The government has cited operational and administrative reasons for the delay. The constitutional freeze on increasing Lok Sabha seat numbers lapses after 2026, but the absence of census data means a Delimitation Commission cannot lawfully commence its work on seat reallocation. The One Nation One Election Joint Parliamentary Committee's recommendations, if implemented, would further complicate the delimitation timeline by seeking to synchronise assembly and parliamentary election cycles, which intersects directly with when and how a fresh delimitation could be carried out. The practical result is that the constitutional right inserted in 2023 is likely to remain inoperative until at least 2029–2030, and possibly until 2034–2035 if the delimitation exercise that follows the census is as protracted as previous exercises have been.

## What is constitutionally at stake

The deeper constitutional question is whether there is a category of constitutional provision — one that is textually enacted but structurally inoperative — that the courts can address. The basic structure doctrine, as applied in Kesavananda Bharati and Minerva Mills, holds that Parliament cannot use the amendment power to damage the Constitution's essential features. If an amendment inserts an equality provision and simultaneously makes it perpetually inoperative, the question is whether that architecture damages the equality and representation principles that the amendment purports to advance. No court has directly addressed this pattern, making the Constitution Bench hearing a potentially significant doctrinal moment. The Court must decide whether the concept of justiciable rights requires that rights inserted into the constitutional text must have some judicially enforceable path to becoming operative, or whether the timing of operationalisation is entirely a matter of legislative and executive discretion.

## How to follow this issue

The Supreme Court of India's case-status portal and cause lists are the primary sources for tracking the Constitution Bench petition. The Office of the Registrar General and Census Commissioner of India will publish official notices when the census is launched, which is the single most important practical development to watch for. Parliamentary proceedings — particularly Question Hour, standing committee reports, and any amendment Bill introduced to modify Article 334A — are available on Sansad.in. PRS Legislative Research and the Supreme Court Observer provide accessible analytical summaries of both the legislative history of the 106th Amendment and any judicial developments in the pending petition.
