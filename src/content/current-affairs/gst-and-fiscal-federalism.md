## The constitutional frame

Before 2016, Indian fiscal federalism divided taxing power along strict lines: Parliament taxed the manufacture of goods and services; States taxed the sale of goods within their territory. The 101st Amendment broke this boundary by inserting Article 246A, which gives both Parliament and every State legislature concurrent power to make laws with respect to goods and services tax. The Union retains exclusive power over inter-state supply under the proviso to Article 246A(1), exercised through the Integrated GST (IGST) framework, while Article 269A directs that tax on inter-state trade shall be levied and collected by the Union and then apportioned to States in the manner Parliament prescribes.

Article 279A constitutes the GST Council, composed of the Union Finance Minister as Chair and the Finance Ministers of all States and Union Territories. The Council makes recommendations on GST rates, exemptions, thresholds, model laws, and special provisions for certain States. Decisions require a three-fourths majority of the weighted votes cast, with the Union holding one-third of the total weight and all States together holding two-thirds. The voting formula effectively gives the Union a blocking minority: no recommendation can pass over Union opposition.

## What Mohit Minerals settled — and what it left open

In Union of India v. Mohit Minerals Pvt Ltd (2022), the Supreme Court held that Article 279A(1) did not transfer legislative power to the GST Council. The Council recommends; it does not legislate. Parliament and State legislatures each retain their independent competence under Article 246A and are not bound to implement every Council recommendation. The judgment resolved a doctrinal uncertainty — whether the GST Council had, in effect, displaced Parliament and the State legislatures as the primary law-making authority on GST — by confirming the continued sovereignty of elected legislatures.

What the judgment left open is the political and institutional question: when a State legislature diverges from a Council recommendation, what prevents the Union from using its blocking minority in the Council to deny the divergent State the benefits of uniform GST rules? The asymmetry between formal legal co-equality and practical fiscal dependence is the live tension.

## Key constitutional provisions

**Article 246A** creates concurrent GST jurisdiction. Both Parliament and every State legislature may make laws on goods and services tax imposed by the Union or the respective State. The proviso preserves Union exclusivity over inter-state supply. This is a significant departure from the Seventh Schedule structure, which otherwise governs the division of legislative competence through the Union, State, and Concurrent Lists under Articles 245 and 246.

**Article 269A** governs GST on inter-state trade. Tax on the supply of goods or services in the course of inter-state trade is levied by Parliament and is apportioned between the Union and States as Parliament by law provides. This ensures that States benefit from IGST revenue even though the Union collects it.

**Article 279A** constitutes the GST Council and sets out its composition, voting weights, and the subjects on which it recommends. The article does not use the word "binding"; it uses "recommendations." The Mohit Minerals court treated this textual choice as dispositive.

**Article 280** requires the President to constitute a Finance Commission every five years to make recommendations on the distribution between the Union and States of the net proceeds of taxes and on grants-in-aid. The Finance Commission's devolution recommendations operate alongside, not through, the GST framework. How the Fifteenth and Sixteenth Finance Commissions factor GST revenue into horizontal devolution formulas remains a live policy and constitutional question.

## The 101st Amendment as a federal bargain

States surrendered their VAT and entry-tax powers; Parliament surrendered Central Excise and Service Tax. Both sides accepted a constitutional constraint — the GST Council — as the forum for ongoing fiscal negotiation. In exchange, States received a guarantee: if their GST revenues fell below a projected growth trajectory of fourteen percent per annum over the first five years, the Union would compensate them from a cess levied on certain goods.

The compensation guarantee expired in June 2022. Several States claimed arrears; the Union disputed the amounts. The dispute reveals the limit of the constitutional structure: Article 279A creates a Council with recommendation power, not an arbitral or enforcement mechanism. Compensation disputes are political rather than justiciable in any direct sense, though an Article 131 suit between a State and the Union remains a theoretical avenue.

## Petroleum and Part XIII

Crude petroleum, high-speed diesel, petrol, natural gas, and aviation turbine fuel were constitutionally placed outside GST by the 101st Amendment until the GST Council recommends their inclusion. States continue to levy high VAT rates on these products. The exclusion creates a distortion: producers and transporters pay VAT on petroleum inputs but receive no corresponding GST input tax credit, raising effective costs throughout the economy.

The petroleum exclusion also has a Part XIII dimension. Article 301 declares that trade, commerce, and intercourse throughout India shall be free. Articles 302 to 305 govern Parliament's and State legislatures' power to restrict that freedom. High and varying State VAT rates on petroleum — which are the direct result of the GST exclusion — raise the question whether they constitute impediments to the freedom of trade guaranteed by Part XIII, and whether Parliament's choice not yet to include petroleum within GST is consistent with that guarantee.

## The federalism question on Council voting

The GST Council's voting weights have attracted sustained scholarly and political criticism. The Union's one-third weight means that no recommendation passes without Union agreement. States collectively hold two-thirds, but must act with near-unanimity to outvote the Centre. In practice, States with political alignment to the governing coalition at the Centre may not vote against the Union's preferred position, making the formal two-thirds State weight less than its face value suggests.

Critics argue that this structure inverts the federal spirit of Article 246A's concurrent jurisdiction: while the constitution text treats Parliament and State legislatures as co-equals in GST legislation, the Council structure makes the Union a de facto agenda-setter. The SR Bommai principle — that federalism is part of the basic structure — gives this critique constitutional weight, though no court has yet applied it directly to the Council's voting formula.
