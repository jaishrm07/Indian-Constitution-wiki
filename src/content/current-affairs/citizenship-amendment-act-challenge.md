## Constitutional question

The Citizenship Amendment Act 2019 amends the Citizenship Act 1955 to create an accelerated naturalisation pathway — reducing the qualifying period of residence from eleven years to five years — for members of six named religious communities (Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, Christian) who entered India from Pakistan, Bangladesh, or Afghanistan on or before 31 December 2014 and who allege religious persecution. Muslims from those three countries receive no equivalent benefit.

The constitutional challenge centres on three intersecting arguments.

**Article 14 — equality and intelligible differentia.** The State must show that the classification (religion of the immigrant) has an intelligible differentia and that the differentia bears a rational nexus to the object of the law (protection of religiously persecuted minorities). The Union's position is that the three source countries have Islam as the state religion, which makes non-Muslim minorities structurally vulnerable there; the persecutor-religion criterion therefore tracks the actual pattern of persecution. The petitioners' counter is that the criterion is simultaneously over-inclusive (not all non-Muslims in those countries are persecuted) and under-inclusive (persecuted Muslims, Ahmadis, Shia communities, and migrants from other countries are excluded), and that a law ostensibly about persecution should not use religious identity as a proxy.

**Articles 15 and 25 — discrimination and religious freedom.** Article 15(1) prohibits the State from discriminating against any citizen on grounds of religion. While citizenship acquisition precedes citizenship, the petitioners argue that the statutory regime that creates differential pathways to citizenship on the ground of religion is itself a form of state action indexed to religion, contrary to the secular structure of Part III. Article 25's guarantee of freedom of conscience and religious practice is engaged because the Act's operation signals that belonging to one religion — Islam — carries a constitutional disadvantage in the naturalization queue.

**Article 21 — life and liberty consequences.** The right against arbitrary deprivation of liberty has been read to cover statelessness and forced deportation since Maneka Gandhi (1978). Persons who might otherwise have acquired citizenship or regularized residence under earlier settlement schemes could, if the CAA is the exclusive available pathway and they do not qualify, face detention or deportation. The combination of CAA with a potential National Register of Citizens exercise sharpens this concern: those who cannot prove citizenship under NRC criteria and who are also excluded from the CAA safety net face the prospect of statelessness.

## What has happened

- **December 12, 2019**: The Citizenship Amendment Act receives Presidential assent and is published in the Gazette of India. Within days, widespread protests erupt across India, including at Shaheen Bagh in Delhi and university campuses, objecting to the Act's exclusion of Muslims and the anticipated combination with an NRC.
- **January 2020**: The Supreme Court issues notice to the Union on the petitions but declines to stay the Act. Hundreds of petitions are tagged and the matter is referred to a Constitution Bench.
- **2020–2023**: The Citizenship Amendment Rules — necessary to make the Act operational — are not notified. The Union repeatedly invokes the disruptions caused by COVID-19, and subsequently by the electoral schedule, as reasons for the delay in rule-making.
- **March 11, 2024**: The Ministry of Home Affairs notifies the Citizenship Amendment Rules 2024 in the Gazette of India, making the Act fully operational. The Rules specify the application procedure, documents required, district-level empowered committees, and the online portal for CAA citizenship applications.
- **2024 onwards**: Citizenship grants begin being processed through the CAA portal. The Constitution Bench, constituted of five judges, continues to have the petitions listed but final hearings have not been scheduled conclusively as of early 2026.

## Key provisions at stake

**Article 11** gives Parliament plenary power to regulate the right of citizenship by law. The Union relies on this power as the constitutional basis for the CAA's differential treatment, arguing that citizenship regulation is inherently a sovereign classification exercise in which wide legislative latitude is permissible.

**Article 14** requires that every law meet the twin tests of intelligible differentia and rational nexus. The Article 14 challenge to the CAA is the core of the litigation: the Constitution Bench must determine what standard of review applies when Parliament classifies by religion in a citizenship statute and whether the stated nexus — that persecutor-religion in the source country predicts victim-religion — is legally sufficient.

**Article 15(1)** prohibits discrimination by the State on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. The question is whether Article 15 binds Parliament in framing citizenship law, or whether Article 11 carves out citizenship as a domain insulated from Article 15's anti-discrimination command.

**Article 21** read with Maneka Gandhi's procedural due-process requirements demands that any deprivation of liberty — including threatened statelessness or detention under a future NRC exercise — be backed by a law that is fair, just, and reasonable. The CAA-NRC combination is the principal context in which Article 21 claims arise.

**Article 25** protects freedom of conscience and free profession of religion from state interference. The argument from Article 25 is that statutory architecture which treats religion as a disqualifying criterion for a benefit encroaches upon the constitutional prohibition on the State taking an interest in the citizen's religion.

## What to watch

The decisive question in the Constitution Bench is the standard of review. If the Court applies the conventional rational-basis intelligible-differentia test, the Union's argument that Islam is the state religion in all three source countries — and non-Muslims are therefore structurally disadvantaged — is likely to survive. If the Court imports a stricter scrutiny standard for religious classifications, modelled on the logic in Navtej Singh Johar (2018) which treated identity-based classification with heightened suspicion, the Union's defence becomes harder to sustain.

Equally important is whether the Court treats secularism as a constraint on Article 11 power. If the basic-structure doctrine extends to positive citizenship law — not merely to constitutional amendments — then Parliament's power under Article 11 may be limited by the prohibition on state-sponsored religious discrimination even in the domain of naturalization.

The CAA-NRC connection remains politically and constitutionally live. The Union has not formally announced a nationwide NRC exercise since the Assam NRC was completed in 2019. If a national NRC is announced or commenced, the interaction between the CAA (which provides a safe-harbour for non-Muslim illegal migrants from three countries) and the NRC (which requires all residents to prove citizenship) will sharpen the Article 21 and Article 14 issues already before the Bench.
