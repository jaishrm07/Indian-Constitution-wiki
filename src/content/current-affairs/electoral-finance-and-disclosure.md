## The constitutional frame

Electoral finance is not merely an accounting issue. Once political donations affect how elections are fought and how parties are funded, transparency becomes part of the constitutional environment in which voters exercise choice.

## What readers should watch

The key question is whether disclosure after the 2024 judgment becomes a durable reporting system or just a temporary fix. If the funding trail remains hard to inspect, the constitutional problem will continue even without electoral bonds.

## Chronology

- **October 2003** — The Election Commission publishes a detailed proposal for comprehensive political-finance disclosure, including expenditure reporting requirements and donor identification norms.
- **2013** — The Electoral Trust scheme is introduced, allowing companies to channel donations through registered trusts with partial disclosure obligations.
- **January 2018** — The Electoral Bonds scheme is introduced under the Finance Act; it allows any person or company to purchase bearer bonds from the State Bank of India in specified denominations, which can be redeemed by registered political parties — without the donor's identity appearing on the bond or in any public record.
- **February 2024** — A five-judge Constitution Bench in Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India unanimously strikes down the Electoral Bonds scheme; holds that anonymous electoral financing violates voters' right to information under Article 19(1)(a) because it conceals the link between corporate donors and the parties they fund.
- **March 2024** — The Supreme Court refuses SBI's application for an extension and directs it to furnish complete bond data, including alphanumeric identifiers, by 12 March 2024; the Election Commission is directed to publish the data, which goes live on 21 March 2024.
- **2025** — The Court continues to monitor compliance and enforcement; the legislature has not yet enacted a replacement disclosure regime.

## What is constitutionally at stake

The electoral bonds ruling established that the constitutional right to vote encompasses the right to know which interests finance the candidates and parties being voted upon, grounding this in the right to information under Article 19(1)(a). Anonymising the donor-party link is constitutionally impermissible because it deprives voters of information necessary for an informed electoral choice and allows undisclosed influence over political parties to flow unchecked. The post-ruling question is whether Parliament can design a replacement regime that protects legitimate donor safety from harassment or retaliation while maintaining the voter's right to know — a direct tension between the privacy interests of donors and the transparency demands of democratic accountability. If no durable replacement is enacted, political parties retain significant funding from sources that remain effectively untraceable under existing reporting obligations. The ruling also raises the question of whether the Election Commission's regulatory powers extend to prescribing disclosure norms in the absence of legislation.

## How to follow this issue

Monitor the Supreme Court registry for compliance orders arising from the Association for Democratic Reforms judgment and any contempt or enforcement proceedings. Track the Election Commission's disclosure portal for updated party contribution reports and any gaps in the bond redemption data. Watch for new legislation or subordinate regulations from the Union government proposing a replacement political-finance regime.
