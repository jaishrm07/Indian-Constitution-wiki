## The constitutional frame

Articles 124 and 217 vest the appointment of Supreme Court and High Court judges in the President, acting in consultation with the Chief Justice of India and such other judges as the President deems necessary. The constitutional text is spare; the practical system that operates under it has been shaped almost entirely by Supreme Court adjudication rather than parliamentary legislation. The live issues are not only who appoints judges, but how visible, timely, and contestable the appointment pipeline should be — and whether any reform can satisfy both the independence imperative the Court has declared to be basic structure and the accountability imperative that a democratic polity reasonably expects.

## Chronology

- **1981 — First Judges Case (S.P. Gupta v. Union of India):** An eleven-judge bench holds that "consultation" in Articles 124 and 217 means only that the President must seek and consider the Chief Justice's opinion; it does not bind the President to follow it. The executive retains final authority over judicial appointments, and the judgment is soon associated with the emergency-era pattern of preferential treatment for government-friendly judges and punitive transfers of independent ones.

- **1993 — Second Judges Case (SCAORA v. Union of India, (1993) 4 SCC 441):** A nine-judge bench overrules S.P. Gupta by 7–2. The majority holds that "consultation" carries primacy: the Chief Justice of India's institutional opinion on appointments and transfers must ordinarily prevail over executive preference. The CJI's opinion must be formed in consultation with the two senior-most puisne judges. The government may return a recommendation once for reconsideration; if the CJI reiterates it, the recommendation is binding on the President. This judgment creates the collegium system.

- **1998 — Third Judges Case (Special Reference No. 1 of 1998, (1999) 7 SCC 739):** The President refers nine questions to the Supreme Court under Article 143. The advisory opinion clarifies and expands the collegium: for Supreme Court appointments, the collegium is the CJI plus the four senior-most puisne judges; for High Court appointments the collegium is the CJI plus the two senior-most puisne judges. The government's one-time return power is confirmed; a reiterated collegium recommendation binds the executive regardless of the government's objections.

- **2014 — 99th Constitutional Amendment and NJAC Act:** Parliament, with near-unanimous support in both Houses and ratification by nearly every state legislature, inserts Articles 124A, 124B, and 124C into the Constitution. The 99th Amendment creates the National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC): the CJI (chair), the two senior-most Supreme Court judges, the Union Law Minister, and two eminent persons selected by a three-member committee. Any two members of the NJAC can veto a nomination.

- **2015 — Fifth Judges Case / NJAC Judgment (SCAORA v. Union of India, [2015] 13 SCR 974):** A five-judge Constitution Bench strikes down the 99th Amendment and the NJAC Act by 4–1, with Justice Chelameswar dissenting. The majority holds that judicial primacy in appointments is a component of the basic structure; the veto power of any two NJAC members — capable of allowing the Law Minister and one eminent person to block any nominee the judiciary favoured — makes judicial independence structurally dependent on executive forbearance. The Court acknowledges that the collegium is opaque, lacks accountability, and needs reform, but holds that the answer is collegium improvement, not structural replacement. The Court invites memoranda on reforming collegium practice and issues subsequent orders requiring publication of resolutions and reasons.

- **2017 — Publication of collegium resolutions begins:** The Supreme Court starts publishing collegium resolutions on its website, making nomination decisions and some reasoning publicly accessible for the first time. This represents a partial fulfilment of the NJAC judgment's implicit bargain — collegium survival in exchange for greater transparency.

- **2018 onwards — MoP deadlock:** The Memorandum of Procedure that was being renegotiated after the NJAC judgment is never finalised. The government and the Supreme Court collegium continue to disagree on which security and eligibility criteria the government may apply when processing collegium recommendations, and on what happens if the government simply sits on a recommendation without returning it formally. The absence of a finalised MoP means there is no agreed mechanism for resolving deadlocks.

- **2022 — In re: Filling of Vacancies in High Courts:** The Supreme Court takes up a suo motu proceeding to monitor the appointment of High Court judges, directing the government and High Court collegia to file status reports. The bench repeatedly notes that hundreds of posts across all 25 High Courts remain vacant, that the executive has been sitting on processed recommendations for months or years without either appointing or formally returning them, and that the resulting pendency backlog deprives litigants of their constitutional right of access to justice. The proceedings remain ongoing.

- **2023 — Anoop Baranwal and the ECI appointment model:** The Supreme Court, in Anoop Baranwal v. Union of India ((2023) 6 SCC 161), directs that Election Commission appointments be made by a committee of the Prime Minister, the Leader of Opposition, and the Chief Justice of India until Parliament legislates. Though the case concerns Article 324, it generates public discussion about whether a similarly structured committee could strengthen accountability in the judicial appointments process without repeating the NJAC's constitutional defects.

- **April 2025 — Asset disclosure:** The Supreme Court Full Court resolves that sitting judges' asset statements will be placed in the public domain, extending its own transparency obligations for the first time to personal financial disclosure.

- **May 2025 — Transparency release:** The Supreme Court announces that process-stage information for all ongoing High Court and Supreme Court appointments has been published online, covering the period from November 2022 to May 2025. This is the most detailed public disclosure of the appointment pipeline to date, showing at which stage — High Court collegium, State government, Intelligence Bureau, Union government, Supreme Court collegium, Department of Justice, Presidential notification — each pending file sits.

## What is constitutionally at stake

**The collegium and basic structure.** The NJAC judgment treats judicial primacy in the appointments process as a component of the basic structure, meaning Parliament cannot abolish it even by constitutional amendment. This extends the basic structure doctrine — which had previously protected the power of constitutional amendment review — into the internal governance of the appointing authority. Any future reform proposal must therefore either work within collegium primacy or argue persuasively that the relevant basic structure element is "judicial independence" rather than "collegium primacy," a distinction with potentially significant implications for reform design.

**The government's one-time return power and silent non-compliance.** The collegium system gives the executive one opportunity to return a recommendation with reasons; if the collegium reiterates the recommendation, the executive is constitutionally bound to proceed with the appointment. In practice, the government has repeatedly sat on both initial and reiterated recommendations for months or years without formally returning them. The In re Filling of Vacancies proceedings have highlighted this practice; the Supreme Court has noted that indefinite deferral is constitutionally impermissible, but enforcement mechanisms against a recalcitrant executive are limited.

**The unfinished Memorandum of Procedure.** The MoP is the administrative document that translates the constitutional framework into a step-by-step process: who initiates a recommendation, what security and integrity checks apply, how long each stage may take, and what happens if any actor fails to act within the stipulated time. Since the NJAC was struck down and the MoP renegotiation stalled, there is no agreed document specifying these details. The government has at various points insisted on broad security-vetting discretion that the collegium considers an indirect way of reasserting executive control. Until the MoP is finalised, every disagreement about a specific appointment can escalate into a constitutional confrontation with no agreed resolution procedure.

**Vacancy crisis and access to justice.** India's 25 High Courts have a combined sanctioned strength of over a thousand judges; at any given time several hundred posts are vacant. Combined with mounting case filings, this produces the pendency crisis that the National Judicial Data Grid tracks in real time. The constitutional dimension is that the right of access to justice — an aspect of Article 21 as interpreted in cases from Maneka Gandhi through Olga Tellis — is compromised when courts lack the judicial capacity to hear cases within a reasonable time. The appointment deadlock is therefore not only an institutional governance question but a fundamental rights question.

**Diversity and criteria.** The collegium has faced persistent criticism for producing a bench that is geographically narrow, drawn overwhelmingly from a small circle of senior advocates and High Court judges with metropolitan practice, and underrepresenting women and communities from disadvantaged backgrounds. The absence of published criteria means it is difficult to evaluate whether recommendations are made on principle or on preference. Transparency of resolutions is a partial remedy, but without stated criteria, publication of decisions does not tell observers why candidates were preferred or rejected.

**The Anoop Baranwal analogy.** The ECI appointments model — a committee with representation for the judiciary, the executive, and the parliamentary opposition — is now a live alternative paradigm for constitutional appointments. Supporters of judicial appointments reform have argued that a similarly structured committee, excluding any executive veto but including non-judicial voices in the recommendation process, might preserve the independence requirement of the NJAC judgment while improving accountability. This argument has not yet been tested in litigation, but it frames the policy discussion about what "collegium reform" could constitutionally look like.

**Article 124(2) and parliamentary legislation.** Article 124(2) provides that the President shall appoint Supreme Court judges "in consultation with such of the Judges of the Supreme Court and of the High Courts in the States as the President may deem necessary." Parliament has never legislated a comprehensive appointments framework under this provision. The NJAC judgment held that any such legislation must preserve judicial independence, but did not foreclose legislation altogether. The question of whether Parliament can legislate criteria, timelines, a secretariat, or a structured process that stops short of breaking judicial primacy remains open and is the central terrain for any future reform debate.

## How to follow this issue

Monitor the In re Filling of Vacancies proceedings in the Supreme Court for judicial interventions on specific delay patterns and government non-compliance. Watch the Supreme Court's collegium portal for the frequency and completeness of resolution updates after the May 2025 disclosure. Track Department of Justice Gazette notifications and appointment orders to measure the lag between each collegium recommendation and the resulting notification. Consult the National Judicial Data Grid for vacancy figures disaggregated by court. Follow Vidhi Centre and Supreme Court Observer for detailed analysis of individual appointment timelines and reform proposals. Watch for any government statement on MoP finalisation and for any parliamentary bill on judicial appointments process.

## Sources and how to verify

- **Constitution of India** (`constitution-of-india`): Articles 124, 124A–C, 217, and 222 are the textual basis. Articles 124A–C remain on the statute book as inserted by the 99th Amendment even though they are inoperative following the NJAC judgment; reading them alongside the operative articles illustrates the precise scope of what Parliament attempted and what the Court struck down.
- **Second Judges Case judgment (1993)** (`second-judges-case-judgment-1993`): The foundational text for collegium primacy. Retrieve via eSCR using citation (1993) 4 SCC 441. Read the majority reasoning on the meaning of "consultation" in Articles 124 and 217.
- **NJAC judgment (2015)** (`escr`): Retrieve SCAORA v. Union of India [2015] 13 SCR 974, also reported at (2016) 5 SCC 1. Justice Khehar's majority opinion identifies judicial primacy as basic structure; Justice Lokur's concurrence acknowledges collegium flaws; Justice Chelameswar's dissent argues that accountability and independence are not mutually exclusive.
- **Collegium Resolutions** (`collegium-resolutions`): Official Supreme Court collegium resolution publications since 2017, now available on the SC website and updated following the May 2025 transparency release.
- **Supreme Court Collegium Transparency 2025** (`supreme-court-collegium-transparency-2025`): The May 2025 release covering appointment process-stage information and High Court appointment data from November 2022 to May 2025.
- **Department of Justice / Appointment Orders** (`department-of-justice`, `department-of-justice-appointments`, `judges-appointments-orders`): Official appointment notifications and Gazette entries for each judicial appointment, enabling calculation of the lag between collegium recommendation and formal appointment.
- **National Judicial Data Grid** (`national-judicial-data-grid`): Real-time vacancy statistics and pendency data for the Supreme Court and all High Courts. The vacancy and case-pendency figures are the empirical core of the "vacancy crisis" argument.
- **eSCR** (`escr`): Primary portal for all Supreme Court judgments cited here, including the First, Second, Third, and Fifth Judges Cases and the In re Filling of Vacancies orders.
- **Supreme Court Observer / Vidhi Centre** (`supreme-court-observer`, `vidhi-centre-for-legal-policy`): Structured tracking of appointment timelines, analysis of individual collegium decisions, and in-depth reform proposals. Essential for understanding the gap between formal process and observed practice.
- **Live Law / PRS India** (`live-law`, `prs-india`): Real-time coverage of court proceedings in the vacancy monitoring case and analysis of any legislative proposals touching judicial appointments.
