## Why this topic matters

The Constitution does not treat equality as a purely abstract guarantee. It also uses representational devices, reservation, and tailored inclusion mechanisms to address structural disadvantage inside legislatures and public institutions. Equality in Indian constitutional law is simultaneously a negative right — the state cannot discriminate — and a positive commitment — the state must sometimes act to correct inherited hierarchies. The tension between these two dimensions runs through every major equality case and every major reservation dispute. Part XVI of the Constitution makes provision for reservation of seats in the Lok Sabha and state legislative assemblies for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, reflecting the founders' view that formal electoral equality is not sufficient to secure political voice for historically marginalised communities. Understanding this topic requires holding together the anti-discrimination strand of equality and the affirmative representation strand simultaneously, since the Constitution deploys both and requires courts to reconcile them. The 106th Amendment reserving one-third of legislative seats for women, combined with its conditioned commencement linked to delimitation, shows that equality as a constitutional commitment continues to evolve and is never fully settled.

## How to read the topic

Move between Articles 14 to 16 and the representation provisions in Part XVI. Then read the amendment history that added or refined reservation design, especially when current disputes concern sub-classification, legislative reservation, or the interaction between equality doctrine and democratic representation. Article 14 is the general equality guarantee; Articles 15 and 16 are its specific applications to discrimination and public employment; Article 17 abolishes untouchability as a practice. Part XVI provisions on reserved seats operate alongside, not instead of, the Part III equality guarantees, so the two chapters must be read in conjunction rather than as alternatives. The Ninth Schedule and its jurisprudence matter here too, since several reservation statutes have been placed in that schedule to protect them from judicial review, and the Supreme Court in I.R. Coelho held that Ninth Schedule laws enacted after Kesavananda Bharati remain subject to basic structure review. Reading the equality code as a whole — rather than article by article — reveals the Constitution's commitment to substantive equality as a foundational value, one that cannot be reduced either to formal non-discrimination or to unlimited affirmative action.

## How equality works in constitutional law

Article 14 prohibits the state from denying any person equality before law or equal protection of laws, and the Supreme Court has read into it a prohibition on arbitrary state action through the reasonable classification test: the state may differentiate between groups provided the classification is intelligible and has a rational nexus to the object of the law. Article 15 goes further by prohibiting discrimination specifically on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth, while Articles 15(3), 15(4), and 15(5) make explicit that the state may take special measures for women, children, and socially and educationally backward classes. Article 16 guarantees equal opportunity in matters of public employment, and Articles 16(4), 16(4A), and 16(4B) enable affirmative action for inadequately represented groups in state services, including in matters of promotion. Article 17 abolishes untouchability and makes its practice an offence, directly targeting the social structure that has most comprehensively denied equality across Indian history. The equality code is thus both protective — shielding individuals from discriminatory state action — and enabling, permitting and in some cases requiring the state to act affirmatively to secure substantive equality for groups that formal neutrality would leave behind. The post-Maneka Gandhi reading of Article 21 has also enlarged the equality code indirectly, since the Court has held that the right to life and personal liberty must be consistent with the equality guarantee, meaning arbitrary or discriminatory state action that affects life or liberty is doubly unconstitutional.

## Key cases

- [E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu](../cases/e-p-royappa) introduced the anti-arbitrariness doctrine as a substantive strand of Article 14, holding that equality is antithetical to arbitrariness and that wherever the state acts arbitrarily it violates the constitutional guarantee regardless of whether any formal classification or differential treatment is present. By extending Article 14 beyond its earlier role of policing legislative classifications into a general discipline on all state action — including individualised executive decisions — Royappa gave Article 14 the breadth it needed to reach forms of official caprice and mala fide conduct that the traditional nexus test could not address, and its anti-arbitrariness principle became one of the foundations for subsequent equality and due process reasoning in Maneka Gandhi and beyond.

- [State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan](../cases/champakam-dorairajan) revealed the early tension between formal equality and educational access. The Supreme Court struck down a communal Government Order allocating seats in professional colleges by religion and caste as inconsistent with Article 15(1), but the response — the First Constitutional Amendment inserting Article 15(4) — showed that equality and affirmative action were understood from the outset as requiring simultaneous constitutional accommodation rather than sequential resolution. The case set the pattern for the next seventy years: a judicial ruling on equality, followed by a legislative response that modifies or adds to the constitutional text itself, followed by further judicial interpretation of the amended text.

- [Indra Sawhney v. Union of India](../cases/indra-sawhney) set the structural limits of affirmative action within the equality framework. By upholding the 27% OBC reservation while excluding the creamy layer and imposing the 50% ceiling, the Court treated equality and reservation not as opposing values but as constitutionally calibrated instruments that must be kept in balance. The exclusion of the creamy layer was crucial — it confirmed that reservation is a remedy for social and educational backwardness, not simply for belonging to a numerically large or politically influential group. The case remains the most comprehensive judicial statement on the constitutional architecture of reservations, and its holdings have constrained state governments from expanding reservation beyond fifty percent in most circumstances.

- [Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India](../cases/navtej-singh-johar), decided in 2018, extended the equality guarantee to protect identity itself. Striking down Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code insofar as it criminalised consensual same-sex intimacy, the Court held that Article 14 protects substantive equality — the equal worth and dignity of all persons — not just formal sameness of treatment. The judgment confirmed that the equality code must be read alongside Article 21 and the transformative commitments of the Constitution, and it explicitly acknowledged that the Constitution protects those who have been historically subjected to social discrimination even when that discrimination was encoded in law. The case marks the furthest point yet reached in the Court's recognition that equality requires the state to affirmatively dismantle legal structures that stigmatise or harm particular groups.

- [State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh](../cases/state-of-punjab-v-davinder-singh) confirmed that sub-classification within Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe categories is compatible with Articles 14 and 16. The seven-judge Constitution Bench held that treating all members of a scheduled list as a homogeneous group when their actual conditions of backwardness differ substantially would itself produce inequality; states therefore have authority, backed by empirical evidence, to create internal priorities within reserved categories to direct the benefit to the most disadvantaged sub-groups. The decision requires states to gather and rely on empirical evidence to justify sub-classification, which introduces a new dimension of constitutional review: courts will now examine not just whether a classification is legally permissible but whether the factual predicate for it is adequately established.

- [I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu](../cases/ir-coelho) is significant within this topic because it established that placing a reservation statute in the Ninth Schedule does not insulate it from constitutional review if the law violates the basic structure of the Constitution, including the equality provisions of Part III. The decision means that Parliament cannot use the Ninth Schedule as a device to place any reservation law beyond judicial scrutiny, and that the equality guarantee retains its bite even against statutes that Parliament has explicitly tried to protect.

- [K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India](../cases/k-krishna-murthy) addressed reservation for Other Backward Classes in local body elections, holding that reservations in Panchayati Raj institutions must comply with constitutional requirements of empirical justification and cannot be imposed in a manner that leaves the reserved categories without meaningful political representation in practice.

## Current significance

Equality and representation are live constitutional issues on several major fronts. Sub-classification within SC and ST reservation categories, following Davinder Singh, raises questions about how states must gather and use empirical evidence to justify internal distinctions, and how courts will review that evidence — these developments are tracked on the [reservation and sub-classification](../current-affairs/reservation-and-sub-classification) desk. Women's reservation in legislative bodies — the 106th Amendment reserving one-third of legislative seats — has been enacted but its commencement is linked to delimitation, creating a constitutional design where one equality commitment is conditioned on a separately delayed process; this is tracked on the [women's reservation and delimitation](../current-affairs/women-reservation-and-delimitation) desk.

The delimitation question itself deserves particular attention as an equality issue in its own right, and not merely as a precondition for women's reservation. Electoral representation is fundamentally an equality question: the equal weight of a citizen's vote, the equal access to representation of communities in different parts of the country, and the equal dignity of electors in less-populous or recently under-counted regions are all constitutional concerns that sit within the equality guarantee. When constituency boundaries are drawn or redrawn, the distributional consequences — which communities gain seats, which lose them, how the relative weight of votes in different states changes — are equality consequences in the deepest sense. The freeze on delimitation until 2026 based on the 1971 census created a known and acknowledged inequality between states that had controlled population growth (and therefore saw their relative seat share decline) and states that had not. The political and constitutional stakes of revisiting that freeze, and the interaction between delimitation, women's reservation commencement, and the rights of different regional communities to proportionate legislative voice, are all tracked on the [delimitation and representation](../current-affairs/delimitation-and-representation) desk.
