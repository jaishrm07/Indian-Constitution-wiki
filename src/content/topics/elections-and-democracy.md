## Why this topic matters

Democratic legitimacy in India is not self-executing. It depends on a constitutional framework that guarantees universal adult franchise, protects the right to vote as an aspect of constitutional rights, requires timely elections, and ensures that voters have enough information about candidates to make meaningful choices. These guarantees are spread across Part XV of the Constitution, the Fundamental Rights provisions in Parts III, and decades of judicial interpretation that have converted formal electoral rights into enforceable constitutional obligations. The relationship between elections and democracy is therefore a legal question as much as a political one, and the courts have been called upon repeatedly to define where democratic duty begins and electoral discretion ends.

The institutional stakes extend beyond any single election. Decisions about candidate disclosure, criminal disqualification, electoral finance, and the timing of elections shape the quality of representative democracy across all three tiers of government — Parliament, state legislatures, and local bodies. When the Supreme Court held in ADR v. Union of India (2002) that voters have a fundamental right to information about candidates, it did not merely add a disclosure requirement: it embedded voter information as part of the constitutional architecture of elections, making every subsequent attempt to restrict or dilute disclosure a rights question. Similarly, when the Court struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme in 2024 on the grounds that it violated voters' right to information under Article 19(1)(a), it confirmed that electoral finance is not just a policy question but a constitutional one with fundamental rights dimensions.

Understanding this topic is also essential for readers following current debates about electoral reform. The ONOE (One Nation, One Election) proposal, the post-Electoral Bonds debate about campaign finance regulation, the use of delimitation to reshape representation, and the question of reservation in legislatures all converge on the constitutional framework studied here. None of these debates can be engaged with seriously without understanding the constitutional foundations of democratic participation, the judicial doctrines that enforce them, and the continuing tension between Parliament's power to regulate elections and the Court's role in policing the constitutional minimum.

## Constitutional provisions

**Articles 324 to 329** are the core of Part XV, creating the institutional and legal framework for elections. Article 324 vests the superintendence, direction, and control of elections in the Election Commission. Article 325 prohibits electoral rolls that discriminate on grounds of religion, race, caste, or sex. Article 326 establishes adult suffrage for elections to the House of the People and Legislative Assemblies, and was amended by the Sixty-First Amendment in 1989 to lower the voting age from 21 to 18. Article 327 allows Parliament to make law with respect to elections to Parliament and state legislatures. Article 328 gives state legislatures the same power for their own elections subject to central law. Article 329 bars courts from entertaining election disputes except by way of election petition and prohibits interference with ongoing electoral processes.

**Article 19(1)(a)** guarantees freedom of speech and expression and has been interpreted by the Supreme Court to include the right to know — specifically, the right of voters to receive information about candidates contesting elections. This interpretation, developed in ADR 2002 and PUCL 2003, makes candidate disclosure an aspect of a fundamental right rather than merely a statutory requirement, and it grounds the Court's scrutiny of laws that restrict electoral information.

**Article 21** (right to life and personal liberty) has been invoked in the context of free and fair elections as a constitutional right that encompasses the right to effective participation in the democratic process. While Article 21's role in election law is less developed than Article 19's, it has featured in arguments about the quality of franchise and the constitutional obligation to hold elections on time, connecting the right to timely democratic renewal to the broader framework of constitutional liberties.

**Article 14** (equality before law and equal protection) has been deployed in election cases to challenge discriminatory or arbitrary electoral rules, to question the rationality of classification in candidate eligibility requirements, and to ground arguments about equal access to the electoral process. It operates across the entire field of election law as a check on arbitrary legislative or administrative distinctions.

## How courts have interpreted it

The Supreme Court's most sustained engagement with elections-as-democracy came in Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (2002), where the Court held that voters have a constitutionally protected right, rooted in Article 19(1)(a)'s freedom to receive information, to know the criminal antecedents, assets, liabilities, and educational qualifications of candidates. The Election Commission was directed to require disclosure by notification, and Parliament's attempt to legislatively override this direction through Section 33B of the Representation of the People Act was struck down in PUCL v. Union of India (2003) as violating the fundamental right to information. Together these two cases established the principle that democratic participation requires informed choice, that informed choice is a constitutional right, and that Parliament cannot legislate it away. That principle has since been extended, refined, and invoked in every subsequent round of candidate disclosure litigation.

The Anoop Baranwal v. Union of India judgment of 2023 addressed a structural dimension of electoral democracy: the independence of the institution that conducts elections. The Court held that the existing practice of Executive-only appointments to the Election Commission was constitutionally inadequate to protect the Commission's independence, and directed an interim collegium-style mechanism — involving the Prime Minister, the Leader of the Opposition, and the Chief Justice of India — until Parliament legislated a proper appointment process. The judgment grounded institutional independence in the right to free and fair elections as a basic structural element of the Constitution, making election administration itself a constitutional-rights question. Parliament subsequently enacted the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023, which replaced the collegium with a committee that excluded the Chief Justice — a substitution immediately challenged before the Court.

The Electoral Bonds Scheme case — Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (2024) — extended the democracy-and-disclosure framework into electoral finance. A five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously struck down the scheme on the grounds that it violated voters' right to information under Article 19(1)(a) by making political donations anonymous to the public while allowing the government access to donor information through the State Bank of India. The judgment held that electoral finance transparency is constitutionally necessary because voters cannot assess conflicts of interest between donors and government policy without knowing who funds political parties. The ruling also held that the right to political privacy of donors must be balanced against the public's right to know, and that the scheme failed that balance. The case marks the Court's deepest engagement to date with the constitutional dimensions of campaign finance.

## Key cases (detailed)

**Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (2002)** is the origin point of the judicial doctrine on voter information rights. The Delhi High Court had directed the Election Commission to require disclosure of candidates' criminal records, assets, and educational qualifications before the Commission had any statutory authority to do so. The Supreme Court upheld the High Court's direction, grounding it in Article 19(1)(a)'s right to receive information and the constitutional right to free and fair elections. The Court reasoned that the right to vote, already understood as a constitutional right, implies the right to vote on an informed basis, and that meaningful franchise requires information about who is seeking the voter's mandate. The judgment converted disclosure from a political preference into a legal obligation, reshaped the Commission's pre-election notification practice, and triggered the legislative response that produced PUCL in 2003.

**People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India (2003)** arose directly from Parliament's attempt to reverse ADR by inserting Section 33B into the Representation of the People Act, which would have negated the Election Commission's disclosure-related notifications. The Supreme Court struck down Section 33B as unconstitutional, confirming that Parliament cannot legislate away the voter's fundamental right to information about candidates. The Court held that the right identified in ADR 2002 was rooted in Article 19(1)(a) and was not merely a judicial preference that legislation could displace. PUCL therefore established the key constitutional principle that the right-to-know framework is legislative-proof within the bounds of fundamental rights, and that disclosure requirements derive their authority from the Constitution, not from parliamentary grace.

**Anoop Baranwal v. Union of India (2023)** is the leading case on the constitutional requirement of Election Commission independence. A five-judge Constitution Bench held that the appointment process for Election Commissioners must be structured to insulate the Commission from executive dominance, and laid down an interim mechanism involving the Prime Minister, Leader of Opposition, and Chief Justice pending legislation. The Court's reasoning drew on free and fair elections as a basic structural element of the Constitution — an element that requires not just neutral rules on paper but an institutional design capable of producing neutral administration in practice. The judgment triggered the 2023 legislation on Election Commissioner appointments, which in turn generated fresh constitutional litigation over whether the exclusion of the Chief Justice from the appointment committee satisfied the constitutional standard the Court had articulated.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (Electoral Bonds) (2024)** is the most recent landmark in this line of cases. A five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme, holding that anonymous political donations violate voters' constitutionally protected right to information about political finance. The Court held that the scheme impaired a critical dimension of electoral democracy — the ability of voters to identify potential conflicts of interest between political donors and government decision-making — and that this impairment could not be justified by the asserted privacy interests of donors or the government's interest in reducing cash donations. The direction to the State Bank of India and Election Commission to disclose bond data produced extensive reporting on the correlation between bond purchases and government contracts or regulatory decisions, demonstrating the practical constitutional stakes of the decision.

## Live issues and current significance

The post-Electoral Bonds landscape is the most active current site of democratic finance reform. The Supreme Court's 2024 ruling invalidated the scheme but did not prescribe a replacement, leaving Parliament to design a constitutionally compliant system for regulating political donations. The Law Commission's 2015 recommendations on electoral finance — mandatory disclosure above threshold amounts, state funding of elections, caps on party expenditure — remain unimplemented. Any new scheme will face the constitutional test established in 2024: does it adequately protect voters' right to know who funds political parties, while balancing any legitimate privacy interest? The tension between that constitutional requirement and political parties' preferences for donor confidentiality means that electoral finance reform is unlikely to produce a settled legal framework quickly.

The One Nation, One Election (ONOE) proposal raises distinct constitutional questions about the right to timely democratic renewal. Simultaneous elections to Parliament and all state assemblies would require constitutional amendments affecting the fixed terms of state legislatures and potentially the right of state electorates to hold their own representatives accountable on their own electoral cycles. Critics argue that ONOE could be used to avoid by-elections, reduce the frequency of democratic accountability in states, and effectively extend the terms of incumbents through coordinated timing. The constitutional challenge would likely focus on whether mandatory synchronisation of election cycles violates the structural autonomy of state legislatures and the democratic rights of state electorates under Part XV and the federal provisions of the Constitution.

Delimitation — the periodic redrawing of constituency boundaries — is a live constitutional issue that connects elections to representation in a direct way. The last delimitation was completed in 2008 on the basis of 2001 Census data. The Constitution requires delimitation on the basis of each Census, but a constitutional freeze on altering state-wise seat allocations in the Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha was extended to 2026 (now effectively to 2031 under the 106th Amendment). When the freeze lifts and delimitation proceeds on the basis of 2031 Census data, southern states — which have lower population growth than northern states due to more successful implementation of family planning — face a reduction in their proportional representation in Parliament. The constitutional dimensions of this coming delimitation, its interaction with the women's reservation requirement in the 106th Amendment (which is also conditioned on delimitation), and its effect on federal representation make it one of the most consequential democratic-constitutional events on the near horizon.
