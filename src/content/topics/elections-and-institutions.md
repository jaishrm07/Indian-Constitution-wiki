## Why this topic matters

Elections are the mechanism through which constitutional democracy renews its legitimacy, and the institutions that conduct them — the Election Commission of India, State Election Commissions, and the courts — are therefore constitutional actors of the first order. The quality of democratic governance depends not only on who wins elections but on whether the process of holding them is administratively sound, institutionally independent, and legally enforceable. The Constitution's designers understood this, which is why Part XV does not merely list electoral rights but creates an independent constitutional body (Article 324), shields electoral processes from judicial interference mid-stream (Article 329), and gives Parliament and state legislatures the power to make electoral law under Articles 327 and 328 subject to constitutional constraints. The institutional design of elections is as much a part of constitutional architecture as the design of Parliament or the Supreme Court.

The institutional dimension of elections becomes most visible when it is contested. When the Election Commission's independence from executive influence is compromised by the manner of its appointments, the entire scaffolding of free and fair elections is put at risk. When State Election Commissions are starved of resources, denied cooperation, or bypassed by state governments that delay local body elections, the constitutional guarantee of devolved democratic governance is undermined at its base. When election petitions are delayed for years and disqualified candidates continue to hold office during the litigation, the constitutional logic of accountability — that voters' verdict must take effect promptly — is frustrated. These are not peripheral problems: they are constitutional crises in miniature, and they recur with enough regularity to make the institutional design of elections one of the most practically important areas of constitutional law.

Readers who understand this topic will be equipped to engage with the full range of current debates about electoral integrity: the post-Anoop Baranwal litigation on Election Commission appointments, the independence of State Election Commissions in the face of political pressure to delay local body polls, the application of anti-defection law to legislative majorities, and the constitutional consequences of delayed election petitions. Each of these debates rests on a specific provision of the constitutional institutional framework, and each can only be properly understood by reading that provision together with the judicial doctrine that has developed around it.

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 324** is the institutional cornerstone of Indian election law. It vests the superintendence, direction, and control of elections to Parliament, state legislatures, the President, and the Vice President in the Election Commission of India — a constitutional body that is independent of the executive. The Commission's powers under Article 324 are plenary in the sense that the Court has repeatedly held they extend to all matters necessary to ensure free and fair elections, including powers not expressly enumerated in the Article. The ECI's independence is protected by the conditions of service provisions in Article 324(5), which make the Chief Election Commissioner removable only by the same process as a Supreme Court judge.

**Article 325** requires a single general electoral roll for each territorial constituency, prohibiting any discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, or sex. This ensures that the franchise is not fragmented into communal registers, a foundational guarantee of democratic equality that was a deliberate departure from the pre-independence communal electorate system.

**Article 326** establishes adult suffrage — every citizen above the prescribed age (currently 18 since the Sixty-First Amendment in 1989) who is not otherwise disqualified is entitled to be registered as a voter for elections to Parliament and state legislative assemblies.

**Article 327** enables Parliament to make law with respect to all matters relating to elections to Parliament or state legislatures, including preparation of electoral rolls, delimitation of constituencies, and all other matters necessary for securing the due constitution of such Houses. This is the source of Parliament's authority to enact the Representation of the People Act, 1950 and 1951, and all subsequent election-related legislation.

**Article 328** gives state legislatures the corresponding power to legislate on election matters for their own houses, subject to the provisions of the Constitution and any central law made under Article 327. It is the source of the legislative authority of states over their own electoral rolls, bye-elections, and related procedures.

**Article 329** contains two important restrictions: it bars any court from questioning the validity of a law relating to delimitation of constituencies or allotment of seats, and it requires that election disputes be raised only by way of election petition after the election has been concluded. The second restriction — no court interference in ongoing elections — has been interpreted to mean that challenges to the electoral process must wait until the poll is complete, preserving continuity in the conduct of elections.

**Article 243K** requires every state to have a State Election Commission, with the State Election Commissioner enjoying the same security of tenure and conditions of service as a High Court judge. This provision extends the institutional design of Article 324 — an independent constitutional body insulated from executive removal — to the local body electoral sphere.

**Article 243ZA** applies Article 243K's State Election Commission framework to urban local bodies (municipalities and similar entities), ensuring that elections to both panchayats and municipalities are under an independent institutional umbrella at the state level.

## How courts have interpreted it

The foundational judicial statement on the scope of Article 324 came in Mohinder Singh Gill v. Chief Election Commissioner (1978), where the Supreme Court held that the Election Commission's powers under Article 324 are residual and plenary — not limited to what the statute expressly confers, but extending to everything necessary to ensure free and fair elections where statute is silent or inadequate. The Court held that Article 324 is not a mere housekeeping provision but a conferral of constitutional authority designed to make electoral integrity enforceable even in situations that Parliament's legislation has not anticipated. This doctrine of plenary residual power has been consistently followed and has been the basis for the Election Commission's authority to issue the Model Code of Conduct, countermand elections where the process has been compromised, and direct the disclosure of candidate information in the absence of statutory requirement — the direction that triggered the ADR litigation.

The anti-defection cases — Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992), Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker (2019), and Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker (2020) — form a connected line of institutional cases about the integrity of legislative majorities and the constitutional role of the Speaker in enforcing the Tenth Schedule. In Kihoto Hollohan, the Constitution Bench upheld the Tenth Schedule against a basic structure challenge, holding that the anti-defection provisions protect the mandate of the electorate against post-election floor-crossing, but also held that Speaker's decisions under the Schedule are subject to judicial review for constitutional violations. Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil extended this to require that disqualification proceedings be decided expeditiously and cannot be indefinitely delayed by a Speaker to protect a government's majority. Keisham Meghachandra Singh went further, holding that where a Speaker has a conflict of interest in deciding disqualification petitions, the court may itself decide the matter or set a tight time limit for the Speaker to act. Together these cases establish the institutional principle that the anti-defection mechanism must actually work in the time-sensitive context of legislative majorities, and that courts will enforce this against Speakers who weaponise delay.

The Anoop Baranwal v. Union of India (2023) judgment represents the Court's most direct intervention in the institutional design of the Election Commission itself. A five-judge Constitution Bench held that the existing practice of purely executive appointment of Election Commissioners — with the President acting on the advice of the Prime Minister — was inadequate to protect the Commission's constitutional independence. The Court directed that until Parliament legislated, appointments would be made by a committee comprising the Prime Minister, the Leader of the Opposition, and the Chief Justice of India. The judgment grounded institutional independence not in any express constitutional provision but in the constitutional logic of Article 324: if the Commission is meant to be independent of the executive in conducting elections, it cannot be constituted solely at the executive's discretion. Parliament responded with the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023, which created a three-member appointment committee but replaced the Chief Justice with a Cabinet Minister chosen by the Prime Minister — a substitution immediately challenged before the Court.

## Key cases (detailed)

**Mohinder Singh Gill v. Chief Election Commissioner (1978)** established the doctrine of plenary residual power under Article 324 and remains the starting point for understanding what the Election Commission can and cannot do. The case arose from the countermanding of a Lok Sabha by-election after the death of a candidate, where the Commission acted under its own authority rather than under any specific statutory provision. The Supreme Court upheld the Commission's authority, holding that Article 324 itself — not merely enabling legislation — is the source of the Commission's power to take all steps necessary for free and fair elections. The judgment drew a sharp distinction between the Commission's residual constitutional authority and the statutory framework that regulates ordinary electoral matters, holding that the Constitution does not leave a vacuum when statute is silent. This doctrine is the basis on which the Commission has exercised a wide range of supervisory powers not explicitly provided for in the Representation of the People Act.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** is the foundational anti-defection case, decided by a Constitution Bench that upheld the Tenth Schedule in principle while subjecting it to judicial review in constitutional-violation cases. The Court held that the Tenth Schedule serves the legitimate constitutional purpose of protecting the voter's mandate against post-election desertion, and that it does not violate free speech or any other fundamental right because elected representatives have no constitutional right to vote against the party on whose ticket they were elected. Crucially, the Court held that Speaker's decisions under the Schedule are not immune from judicial review: where a Speaker's order disqualifying or declining to disqualify a member violates a constitutional provision, courts may intervene. This holding established the constitutional conditions under which the anti-defection mechanism operates and set the stage for subsequent cases about Speaker delay and bias.

**Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2019)** arose from the Karnataka political crisis in which the Speaker delayed deciding disqualification petitions for rebel MLAs who had resigned and whose defections would have toppled the government. The Supreme Court held that the Speaker's power to decide disqualification petitions must be exercised expeditiously and cannot be weaponised by delay to protect a particular government's majority or to serve partisan ends. The Court set a strong presumption against indefinite delay and confirmed that the Court can judicially review not just the final decision but the failure to decide within a reasonable time. The judgment directly addressed the structural problem identified since Kihoto Hollohan: that Speakers, who are themselves party members, have an inherent conflict of interest in deciding anti-defection cases involving members of the governing coalition.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** is the most direct judicial intervention in Speaker proceedings on anti-defection to date. The Supreme Court held that where a Speaker has demonstrated bias, conflict of interest, or has unreasonably delayed a disqualification decision for three or more years, the Court may either decide the disqualification itself or impose a short non-extendable deadline for the Speaker to act, with the understanding that the Court will decide if the deadline is not met. The Court explicitly recognised the structural deficiency in the Tenth Schedule's design — assigning disqualification to the Speaker, who is a party politician — and called on Parliament to consider establishing an independent tribunal. The judgment represents the apex of judicial impatience with the institutional failure of the anti-defection mechanism and marks the Court's willingness to substitute its own judgment for a delinquent Speaker's.

## Live issues and current significance

The independence of State Election Commissions is the most actively contested institutional question in sub-national electoral democracy. Article 243K creates SECs with the same constitutional protection as High Court judges, but state governments have in practice curtailed SEC independence through budget constraints, administrative non-cooperation, and legislative manoeuvres that shift control of election timing back to the state cabinet. The constitutional obligation under Articles 243E and 243U to hold panchayat and municipal elections within five years of the expiry of the term — and the obligation under Articles 243K and 243ZA to hold them under SEC supervision — have been enforced through litigation, but state governments have found ways to delay elections by delimitation exercises, reservation re-ordering exercises, and OBC reservation-related litigation. The Supreme Court has intervened repeatedly to direct elections be held, but compliance has been inconsistent, raising questions about the effectiveness of constitutional obligation without effective enforcement mechanisms.

The Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023 has generated fresh constitutional litigation on ECI independence. The Act, enacted in response to Anoop Baranwal, substituted the Chief Justice with a Cabinet Minister as the third member of the appointment committee, effectively restoring a 2:1 executive majority in appointments. Petitioners have challenged this as a violation of the constitutional standard of independence articulated in the judgment, and the matter is pending before the Supreme Court. The outcome will determine whether the Court accepts Parliament's legislative judgment about institutional independence or holds that the Constitution's text and structure impose a stronger non-executive component in ECI appointments.

The Tenth Schedule's structural deficiency — the Speaker's conflict of interest in deciding anti-defection cases — remains unaddressed by legislation notwithstanding the Supreme Court's repeated calls in Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil and Keisham Meghachandra Singh for an independent tribunal. Political incentives make reform unlikely: the party in power at the time of any particular disqualification dispute benefits from a compliant Speaker, and the party that loses that dispute typically lacks the legislative numbers to change the law. The result is a constitutional mechanism that exists on paper but delivers inconsistent results in practice, with courts providing the only real enforcement and even that enforcement being limited by the separation of powers to directions rather than direct decisions in all but the most extreme cases.
