## Why this topic matters

The National Capital Territory of Delhi occupies a unique and contested position in India's constitutional architecture. It is neither a full state nor a plain Union Territory: Article 239AA, inserted by the Sixty-Ninth Amendment in 1991, gave Delhi a Legislative Assembly, a Council of Ministers responsible to that Assembly, and a Lieutenant Governor who in most respects is expected to act on the aid and advice of the elected government. This hybrid design was a deliberate constitutional choice, reflecting Delhi's dual role as the seat of the Union government and as a community with its own democratic aspirations. The tension built into that choice has produced more constitutional litigation than almost any other question in Indian federalism.

The institutional stakes are high because Delhi is not a peripheral jurisdiction. It houses Parliament, the Supreme Court, the central executive, and diplomatic missions. The question of who actually governs the city — who controls its bureaucracy, its land, its police — is therefore simultaneously a question about the working of Indian democracy and about the practical capacity of the Union to secure its own capital. Every dispute about the Lieutenant Governor's discretion, officer postings, or legislative competence is also a dispute about the boundary between Union dominance and democratic self-governance.

Readers need to understand this topic because it condenses the broader tensions of asymmetric federalism into a single, closely watched constitutional unit. The arc from the 2018 Constitution Bench judgment through the 2023 Services judgment and then Parliament's near-immediate legislative reversal of that judgment illustrates the full constitutional cycle: litigation, judicial articulation of principle, and then legislative response. That cycle is itself a constitutional phenomenon worth studying carefully, particularly because the 106th Amendment that Parliament enacted in response to the Services judgment has itself been challenged on basic structure grounds, keeping the issue alive before the Court.

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 239AA** is the master provision for Delhi's governance. Inserted by the Sixty-Ninth Amendment, it creates Delhi's Legislative Assembly, vests executive power in the elected Council of Ministers for subjects within the Assembly's legislative competence, and defines the limited domains — public order, police, and land — where the Union retains full authority. The Article also sets out the LG's role, confirming that in matters of difference the LG may refer the question to the President but must otherwise act on aid and advice.

**Article 239AB** empowers the President, on the LG's report or otherwise, to suspend the operations of Article 239AA in whole or in part when governance of the NCT cannot be carried on in accordance with the Article's provisions. It is the emergency provision for Delhi, analogous in structure to Article 356 for states, and raises comparable questions about justiciability and objective necessity.

**Article 3** authorises Parliament to form new states or alter the boundaries of existing states and Union Territories, and is the source of Parliament's authority to legislate on Delhi's constitutional arrangements. It was deployed as one justification for the Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi (Amendment) Act, 2023 and the 106th Amendment, both of which purported to settle the services question Parliament's own way after the Supreme Court had ruled the other way.

## How courts have interpreted it

The foundational judicial statement on Delhi governance came in the 2018 Constitution Bench judgment in Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India, where five judges unanimously held that the Lieutenant Governor is bound by the aid and advice of the elected Council of Ministers on all matters within the Delhi Assembly's legislative competence, with no residual discretion to disagree and act independently. The Court's reasoning was grounded in constitutional purpose: Article 239AA was enacted to give Delhi's residents democratic self-governance, and that purpose would be defeated if the LG could function as a parallel executive. The Court drew on the familiar aid-and-advice doctrine — deployed in Articles 74 and 163 for the President and Governors — to hold that the LG's role is that of a constitutional head, not an independent power centre. This left the three reserved subjects (public order, police, land) under Union authority but subjected everything else to the logic of responsible, accountable government.

The 2023 Services judgment — again a unanimous five-judge Constitution Bench — resolved the one major question the 2018 ruling had expressly left open: which government controls the transfer and posting of the higher bureaucracy in Delhi's departments. The Court held that services, other than those relating to the three reserved subjects, fall within the elected government's executive domain, and the LG is bound to act on the elected government's advice on officer postings. The reasoning was direct: if the elected government cannot control the officers who implement its policies, democratic accountability is reduced to a fiction. Parliament's response was swift and constitutionally significant — the GNCTD (Amendment) Act, 2023 and the 106th Constitutional Amendment were enacted within weeks of the judgment, vesting services control in the Lieutenant Governor by statute and by constitutional amendment simultaneously.

The Tamil Nadu Governor case of 2025, while not a Delhi case, bears on this topic because it refined the Supreme Court's doctrine on when Governors and Lieutenant Governors may withhold or delay action on Bills passed by elected legislatures. The Court held that inaction is not a constitutional option and that a Governor who neither assents to a Bill, returns it, nor refers it to the President within a reasonable time acts unconstitutionally. That reasoning reinforces the principle, first established for Delhi in 2018, that unelected constitutional heads cannot use inaction as a tool to frustrate elected governments, and it is likely to be deployed in any future Delhi governance litigation.

## Key cases (detailed)

**Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018)** is the foundational Constitution Bench ruling on Delhi's governance structure. Five judges, speaking through four separate but concurring opinions, held that the LG has no independent discretion in matters within the Delhi Assembly's legislative field and must act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. The judgment settled the basic architecture: the Union controls public order, police, and land; the elected government controls everything else. The Court emphasised that Article 239AA must be read in light of its purpose — extending democratic accountability to Delhi's residents — and that any reading which made the LG a dominant executive would frustrate that purpose. The judgment also identified the proper mechanism for genuine disagreement: the LG may refer matters to the President under the proviso to Article 239AA(4), but cannot simply act unilaterally.

**Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (Services) (2023)** addressed the services question that the 2018 judgment had flagged but not decided. A unanimous five-judge Bench held that services — excluding the three reserved subjects — form part of the executive power of the elected government and that the LG is constitutionally required to act on the advice of the Council of Ministers on postings and transfers. The Court rejected the Union's argument that services fell within a residual category under Article 239AA's proviso, holding that such an interpretation would hollow out the elected government's executive authority. The judgment stands as the high-water mark of judicial support for elected governance in Delhi, precisely because Parliament reversed it so quickly.

**Government of NCT of Delhi (Amendment) Act, 2023 and the 106th Constitutional Amendment** are not cases but constitutional and legislative acts that complete the story. Within weeks of the Services judgment, Parliament enacted both an ordinary statute — giving service matters to the LG — and a constitutional amendment to Article 239AA inserting a new clause (3A) to the same effect. The elected Delhi government challenged both on basic structure grounds, arguing that the amendment destroys the democratic self-governance element of Article 239AA which the Court had identified as a constitutional value. That challenge is pending before the Court and represents the live frontier of this topic.

**Tamil Nadu Government v. Governor of Tamil Nadu (2025)** is the most recent Supreme Court pronouncement on the constitutional obligations of Governors and Lieutenant Governors in their dealings with elected governments. The Court held that a Governor who fails to act on Bills within a reasonable time acts unconstitutionally, and that the Court may direct the deemed-assent of Bills unlawfully withheld. While the case arose from Tamil Nadu, its reasoning about the constitutional obligations of unelected constitutional heads directly applies to the LG's relationship with the Delhi government and adds a further judicial layer to the ongoing Delhi governance dispute.

## Live issues and current significance

The most consequential live issue is the constitutional challenge to the 106th Amendment and the GNCTD (Amendment) Act, 2023. The Delhi government's petition argues that the Amendment, by reversing a Supreme Court ruling on the elected government's executive domain, destroys a basic structural feature of Article 239AA — namely, the democratic self-governance that the Constitution Bench in 2018 identified as its animating purpose. If the Court accepts this argument, it would be the first time a constitutional amendment to a Union Territory provision is struck down on basic structure grounds, with significant implications for Parliament's power to use the amendment procedure to legislatively override judicial interpretation of democratic rights. The case also raises the structural question of whether the basic structure doctrine — which has hitherto been applied to amendments affecting states — extends with equal force to Union Territories with special legislative assemblies.

Beyond the pending litigation, Delhi governance raises a broader systemic question about asymmetric federalism. The constitutional design of partial statehood — an elected assembly and responsible government, but without full state status or control over the full range of subjects — is not unique to Delhi. Puducherry has a similar structure under Article 239A. The principles developed in Delhi cases therefore have potential application across all special-category Union Territories. The 2023 legislative reversal of a judicial ruling on elected-government power sets a precedent that parties in any asymmetric jurisdiction can invoke: elected governments in partial-state territories now know that Parliament can legislatively override a judicial decision protecting their executive domain by ordinary legislation if the relevant constitutional provision permits, or by constitutional amendment otherwise.

The financial and administrative dimensions of the services dispute have not been resolved by the constitutional amendment alone. Bureaucratic loyalty, policy implementation, and accountability chains remain contested in practice even after the legal question appears settled on paper. Delhi governance therefore continues to be watched as a real-time test of whether democratic accountability in the asymmetric model can be made to function in the face of structural Union dominance — a question that the pending basic structure challenge before the Supreme Court may ultimately decide.
