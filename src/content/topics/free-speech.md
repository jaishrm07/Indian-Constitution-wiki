## Why this topic matters

Speech questions now arise across protest, media regulation, internet governance, hate speech, defamation, and intermediary liability. That makes free speech one of the Constitution's most publicly visible themes. The constitutional guarantee in Article 19(1)(a) — the right to freedom of speech and expression — is not merely a negative liberty against censorship; it is the foundation of political participation, press freedom, artistic expression, and the circulation of dissenting opinion in a democracy. Every time a sedition case is registered, a website is blocked, an internet shutdown ordered, or a journalist prosecuted, Article 19 is at the centre of the constitutional question.

## How to read the topic

Readers should begin with Article 19, then move to proportionality, procedural fairness, and the institutional role of courts in protecting public discourse. The structure of the article is unusual: Article 19(1)(a) guarantees the right in broad terms, and Article 19(2) then specifies the grounds on which the State may impose restrictions. Reading Article 19 well therefore requires understanding both what the guarantee covers and what the restriction grounds actually permit — including how courts have read the word "reasonable" as a constitutional standard that imposes discipline on legislative choices. The later evolution of proportionality doctrine, especially after Puttaswamy, has made the restriction analysis considerably more demanding than it once was.

## How Article 19(2) restrictions work

Article 19(2) contains an exhaustive list of grounds on which the State may impose restrictions on free speech: sovereignty and integrity of India, security of the State, friendly relations with foreign States, public order, decency or morality, contempt of court, defamation, and incitement to an offence. These grounds are exhaustive, not illustrative — a law that restricts speech on a ground not in this list cannot be saved by Article 19(2) no matter how compelling the State's interest. The restriction must also be reasonable, a word the Supreme Court has read to impose both substantive and procedural constraints: the restriction must be proportionate to the harm sought to be prevented, must not be vague or overbroad, and must leave a meaningful core of protected expression intact. A law that arms the executive with wide discretion to suppress speech without clear standards or procedural safeguards will fail the reasonableness test even if the nominal ground for restriction is enumerated in Article 19(2). Since the Puttaswamy privacy ruling, courts have applied a more structured proportionality analysis — asking whether the restriction pursues a legitimate aim, whether it is rationally connected to that aim, whether less restrictive means are available, and whether the restriction's impact on the right is proportionate to the benefit obtained — to free speech restrictions as well as privacy interferences, unifying the constitutional standard across the two rights.

## Key cases

- [Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras](../cases/romesh-thappar) is the Supreme Court's first major free speech ruling, decided within months of the Constitution coming into force. The Court struck down a state law banning the circulation of a journal on public safety grounds, holding that those grounds were wider than the constitutionally permitted categories in Article 19(2), which are exhaustive and cannot be supplemented by Parliament or state legislatures. The overbreadth reasoning — that a law capturing both protected and unprotected speech is facially void — directly prompted the First Constitutional Amendment of 1951 that added further restriction grounds to Article 19(2).
- [Shreya Singhal v. Union of India](../cases/shreya-singhal) struck down Section 66A of the Information Technology Act as unconstitutional, holding that its vague and open-ended language — criminalising speech that caused "annoyance" or "inconvenience" — failed the reasonableness standard under Article 19(2) and that its chilling effect on protected speech could not be cured by reading it down.
- [Anuradha Bhasin v. Union of India](../cases/anuradha-bhasin) held that freedom of the press and freedom to carry on an internet-dependent occupation are protected under Articles 19(1)(a) and 19(1)(g), and that orders imposing an internet shutdown must satisfy proportionality, must be of limited duration, must be publicly notified, and must be subject to judicial review.
- [Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala](../cases/bijoe-emmanuel) held that freedom of conscience under Article 19(1)(a) and Article 25 includes the right not to speak — protecting students who declined to sing the national anthem on religious grounds from expulsion — establishing that the State cannot compel affirmation of a belief.
- [Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India](../cases/navtej-singh-johar) recognised that criminalising identity suppresses expression: a law that makes it an offence to be who one is necessarily chills all self-expression by that person, so the Article 19 and Article 21 violations were intertwined and mutually reinforcing.

## Current significance

Platform regulation, internet shutdowns, and the legal frameworks governing digital intermediaries have made Article 19 central to contemporary policy debates. The government's power to direct platforms to take down content, its use of emergency orders under the Information Technology Act to block entire services, and the ongoing litigation over new intermediary liability rules all raise questions about whether the regulatory architecture satisfies the constitutional standards set in Shreya Singhal and Anuradha Bhasin. The live desk on [online speech and platform regulation](../current-affairs/online-speech-and-platform-regulation) tracks these developments, including new legislation, judicial challenges, and the doctrinal evolution of proportionality as the governing constitutional test for speech restrictions in the digital environment.
