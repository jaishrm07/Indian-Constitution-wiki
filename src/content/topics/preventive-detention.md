## Why this topic matters

Preventive detention is the point at which constitutional liberty encounters its sharpest test. Unlike criminal detention, which responds to a completed act and carries the procedural protections of a trial, preventive detention confines a person on the basis of what the state predicts they might do. The Constitution permits this — Article 22 is one of the relatively few provisions that explicitly authorises detention without trial — but surrounds it with safeguards intended to prevent executive arbitrariness. Whether those safeguards are real, operational, and judicially enforceable is the central question this topic addresses.

The framers were conscious they were building a permanent exception into a rights chapter. The Constituent Assembly debates show that preventive detention was accepted reluctantly, on the understanding that post-Partition security conditions were exceptional and that the procedural protections of Article 22 would be genuine constraints. That understanding has been strained repeatedly: during the Emergency of 1975-77, when the Supreme Court infamously held that the right to approach courts for habeas corpus could be suspended; in the decades of anti-terrorism legislation from TADA through POTA to UAPA; and in the routine use of state-level preventive detention laws against journalists, activists, and political opponents. The constitutional adequacy of preventive detention law is therefore never merely an abstract doctrinal question.

Understanding this topic also requires understanding the relationship between Article 22 and the broader liberty provisions of Article 21. For two decades after 1950, the Supreme Court treated Article 22 as a self-contained code for detention: if the procedural requirements of Article 22 were met, no further scrutiny under Article 21 was available. Maneka Gandhi (1978) changed this by holding that Articles 14, 19, and 21 must be read together and that any procedure depriving liberty — including preventive detention — must be fair, just, and reasonable. This shift from formalism to substantive review is the defining development in the field, though its implications for deferential advisory board review remain contested.

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 22** is the central provision, operating in two distinct modes. Clauses (1) and (2) provide ordinary protections for persons arrested: the right to be informed of the grounds of arrest, the right to consult a legal practitioner of choice, and the right to be produced before the nearest magistrate within twenty-four hours. Clauses (3) through (7) then carve out a separate regime for preventive detention: the right to be informed of the grounds of detention (subject to a public interest exception permitting the withholding of specific facts), the right to make a representation against the order to an advisory board, and a requirement that cases be placed before an advisory board within five weeks. Critically, the right to consult a lawyer does not apply to preventive detainees.

**Article 33** empowers Parliament to modify by law the rights conferred by Part III in their application to members of the armed forces and the police, to the extent necessary to ensure proper discharge of their duties and maintenance of discipline.

**Article 34** enables Parliament to indemnify any person in respect of acts done during any area where martial law was in force and to validate any sentence, punishment, forfeiture, or other act done or purported to be done under martial law.

**The National Security Act 1980 (NSA)** is the principal central preventive detention statute, permitting detention for up to twelve months to prevent persons from acting in any manner prejudicial to the defence of India, national security, public order, or the maintenance of essential services. NSA detentions do not require any trial and do not require the authorities to disclose all the grounds on which the subjective satisfaction of the detaining authority is based.

**The Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act 1967 (UAPA)** as amended significantly in 2008, 2012, and 2019 — operates differently from classical preventive detention but has overlapping constitutional implications. UAPA allows extended pre-trial custody of up to 180 days, severely restricts bail (the court must be satisfied that there are no prima facie grounds for believing the accusation is true), and enables the designation of individuals as terrorists. The Act's bail provisions effectively shift the presumption of innocence in a manner that the Supreme Court has assessed in several significant cases.

## How courts have interpreted it

The Supreme Court's jurisprudence on preventive detention divides into three broad phases. The first phase, running from 1950 to 1977, was characterised by formalistic review: courts would examine whether the statutory grounds for detention were specified, whether the detaining authority's subjective satisfaction was genuine, and whether the procedural requirements of Article 22 had been met, but would not enquire into the substantive adequacy of the grounds or their proportionality to the liberty cost. A.K. Gopalan (1950) set the tone by treating Article 22 as exhaustive and declining to read it alongside Article 19's freedom guarantees. ADM Jabalpur (1976) represented the catastrophic endpoint of this approach: the Court held that the right to life and liberty could be suspended wholesale during an Emergency, leaving detainees with no judicial recourse.

The second phase began with Maneka Gandhi (1978) and the repudiation of ADM Jabalpur's methodology. The Court held that all fundamental rights are mutually reinforcing and that any deprivation of liberty — however authorised — must satisfy the requirements of Article 21: the procedure must be prescribed by law, and that law must be fair, just, and reasonable. This standard, combined with the Court's increasing willingness to scrutinise the grounds disclosed in detention orders, produced a more exacting review regime through the 1980s and 1990s. Cases like Haradhan Saha, Frances Coralie Mullin, and Hussainara Khatoon established that the subjective satisfaction of the detaining authority is itself subject to review for relevance, non-application of mind, and arbitrariness.

The third phase, from the TADA era onward, has been marked by tension between the Maneka Gandhi standard and legislative choices that restrict judicial scrutiny. UAPA's reverse-burden bail standard has been interpreted by the Supreme Court in NIA v. Zahoor Ahmad Shah Watali (2019) in a way that critics argue substantially narrows bail courts' ability to assess evidence. The question of whether UAPA's bail restrictions are compatible with Article 21 after Maneka Gandhi — and after the Supreme Court's own ruling in Arnab Goswami (2020) affirming robust bail review — remains contested and has generated inconsistent outcomes across High Courts and bench compositions in the Supreme Court itself.

## Key cases (detailed)

**A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950) and its overruling by Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)** — These two cases define the arc of preventive detention jurisprudence. In Gopalan, a six-judge majority held that Article 22 was a self-contained code for detention and that a detenu was entitled only to challenge compliance with Article 22's specific procedural requirements — not to invoke the broader protections of Articles 19 and 21. The Court rejected the argument that the word "law" in Article 21 required the procedure to be fair or reasonable; enacted legislation was sufficient. Twenty-eight years later, Maneka Gandhi unanimously repudiated this approach. A seven-judge bench held that Articles 14, 19, and 21 are not watertight compartments but a seamless web, that deprivation of liberty must comply with all three, and that any procedure authorising such deprivation must satisfy an objective standard of fairness. While Maneka Gandhi arose in the context of passport confiscation rather than preventive detention, its logic extends directly to the detention field and has been applied in every subsequent liberty case.

**ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976) — the Habeas Corpus Case** — A five-judge majority held, during the Emergency proclaimed in 1975, that the right to move a court for habeas corpus was suspended and that no person could challenge their detention in any court during the Emergency, even to allege that the detention was wholly illegal. Justice H.R. Khanna alone dissented, holding that even during an Emergency some residual right to approach courts for protection against illegal state action survived. The majority judgment is now widely acknowledged — including by several subsequent Supreme Court judges in Puttaswamy (2017) — as a profound constitutional error and the Court's gravest failure in its institutional history. Justice Khanna, who was passed over for Chief Justice after his dissent, came to be celebrated as a symbol of judicial courage. The ADM Jabalpur majority approach was formally interred when the forty-fourth Constitutional Amendment in 1978 amended Article 359 to provide that the right under Article 21 cannot be suspended even during an Emergency.

**Kartar Singh v. State of Punjab (1994)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act (TADA) against constitutional challenge, while simultaneously reading down several of its more extreme provisions and emphasising that the safeguards in the Act must be applied strictly. The Court held that the act of terrorism has no nexus with the exercise of fundamental rights under Articles 19 and 21 and that Parliament could legitimately create a special criminal regime for terrorism-related offences. However, the bench also warned against abuse and directed that confessions recorded before police officers — admissible under TADA but not under ordinary law — be given to a judicial magistrate for review. Kartar Singh established that security legislation requiring extended detention and departure from ordinary criminal procedure could be upheld, but only when accompanied by real judicial oversight. That oversight condition has been the subject of continuing controversy in the UAPA era.

**Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) — the Privacy Judgment and its treatment of ADM Jabalpur** — Although primarily a privacy case, Puttaswamy's nine-judge bench delivered the formal constitutional interment of ADM Jabalpur. Every judge on the bench wrote separately to condemn the majority opinion in ADM Jabalpur. Justices Nariman and Chandrachud were most explicit: they held that ADM Jabalpur was per incuriam, contrary to the basic structure, and should be treated as overruled. Justice Chandrachud further observed that the majority's capitulation to executive power in 1976 had permanently damaged the Court's institutional standing and that the judiciary had an obligation to acknowledge and correct its own errors. While Puttaswamy's overruling of ADM Jabalpur is technically obiter — the case was not about detention — it is treated as authoritative by subsequent courts and has bolstered the argument that Article 21 rights can never be fully suspended.

## Live issues and current significance

UAPA bail jurisprudence is the most immediate live issue. The Supreme Court's 2019 ruling in Watali that bail courts must not examine the merits of the prosecution's case in detail has been widely applied to deny bail in UAPA cases involving journalists, academics, and civil society activists — several of whom have spent years in pre-trial custody. The Court has in subsequent cases (Thwaha Fasal, Union of India v. K.A. Najeeb) signalled that prolonged detention without trial engages Article 21 and may require bail even in UAPA cases, but the operative test remains contested and inconsistently applied. A Constitution Bench reference on the constitutional validity of UAPA's bail standard under Article 21, if and when it is made, would be among the most consequential liberty judgments of the decade.

State preventive detention laws — including the Goondas Acts of various states, the Maharashtra Control of Organised Crime Act, and the Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act — continue to produce substantial habeas corpus litigation in High Courts. The grounds of challenge typically raise the same core issues: whether the detaining authority genuinely applied its mind to the specific material against the detenu, whether the grounds of detention were communicated with sufficient particularity to enable a meaningful representation, and whether the advisory board's review was more than a formality. High Courts have regularly quashed detention orders on these grounds, but the structural incentive for the executive to use preventive detention broadly — especially near elections or during industrial and agricultural unrest — has not diminished.

The intersection of preventive detention with the rights of undertrial prisoners raises a question the Court identified but has not fully resolved: when pre-trial detention under ordinary criminal law becomes so prolonged that it amounts functionally to punitive detention without conviction, does the constitutional framework for preventive detention — with its safeguards — apply? The Bhima Regeo case, involving academics and activists held for years under UAPA on contested evidence, brought this question to public attention and into sustained judicial scrutiny, with outcomes that continue to divide the Court.
