## Why this topic matters

The Constitution does not treat democratic representation as exclusively national or state-based. The Seventy-Third and Seventy-Fourth Amendments, enacted in 1992 and inserting Parts IX and IXA into the Constitution, made local self-government a constitutional matter for the first time in India's post-independence history. Before these amendments, panchayats and municipalities were creatures of state legislation, subject to arbitrary suspension, manipulation, and supersession at state government discretion. After 1992, the constitutional framework required elections to be held, mandated independent State Election Commissions to conduct those elections, required reservation for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women at the ward and chair levels, and directed the devolution of functions and finances to local bodies through the Eleventh and Twelfth Schedule subjects. Local self-government is therefore not merely an administrative question but a constitutional entitlement of democratic participation at the third tier.

The institutional stakes of this topic extend to how the constitutional guarantee actually operates in practice. The gap between the text of Parts IX and IXA and the reality of local government in most Indian states is among the largest in the entire Constitution. States have formally complied with the amendment requirements while using delimitation exercises, reservation re-ordering, and non-devolution of functions to hollow out local autonomy. State Election Commissions are constitutionally independent in form but are often starved of resources, overridden by court orders obtained by states to delay elections, or simply ignored when they direct elections to be held. Finance Commissions are appointed but their recommendations are partially implemented or delayed. The constitutional floor that Parts IX and IXA establish has been tested in courts repeatedly, and the trajectory of that litigation reveals both the strength of the constitutional commitment and the institutional resistance it faces.

Readers who engage with this topic will understand why local democracy matters constitutionally — not merely as a good-governance preference but as a structural requirement that the Constitution itself enforces. The OBC reservation litigation, the timely-elections cases, the debate about women's reservation at local level, and the ongoing question of fiscal devolution all flow from the constitutional design of Parts IX and IXA. Without this foundation, debates about village panchayats, city corporations, and State Election Commissions seem like administrative minutiae; with it, they are arguments about constitutional compliance and democratic rights.

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 40** in the Directive Principles requires the State to organise village panchayats and endow them with such powers and authority as may be necessary to enable them to function as units of self-government. It was the pre-1992 constitutional basis for panchayati raj, but being a Directive Principle it did not create enforceable obligations and its implementation varied enormously across states.

**Articles 243 to 243O** (Part IX) constitute the Panchayats chapter introduced by the Seventy-Third Amendment. Article 243B requires every state to constitute Panchayats at village, intermediate, and district levels. Article 243C governs composition, with direct election to all seats. Article 243D mandates reservation of seats for SCs and STs in proportion to their population, and not less than one-third of seats and offices of Chairpersons for women. Article 243E requires elections to constitute Panchayats before the expiry of their five-year term. Article 243G requires states to devolve powers and responsibilities for the preparation of economic development plans and matters listed in the Eleventh Schedule. Article 243K establishes the State Election Commission, with the State Election Commissioner enjoying the same security of tenure as a High Court judge, responsible for superintendence, direction, and control of panchayat elections.

**Articles 243P to 243ZG** (Part IXA) extend the same constitutional framework to Municipalities, introduced by the Seventy-Fourth Amendment. Article 243Q requires the constitution of Nagar Panchayats, Municipal Councils, and Municipal Corporations depending on size. Article 243T requires reservation for SCs, STs, and women in municipalities on the same pattern as Part IX. Article 243U requires that municipal elections be held before expiry of the five-year term. Article 243W requires devolution of functions listed in the Twelfth Schedule to municipalities. Article 243ZA applies Article 243K's State Election Commission framework to municipalities, confirming that the same institutional protection of electoral independence applies to urban local body elections.

**Articles 243H and 243X** require each state to constitute a Finance Commission every five years to review the financial position of panchayats and municipalities respectively and make recommendations on the distribution of state taxes, grants, and the measures needed to improve their financial position. These provisions are the constitutional foundation for fiscal devolution to local bodies and are the basis on which courts have held that the obligation to constitute Finance Commissions is mandatory.

**The Eleventh Schedule** lists twenty-nine subjects including agriculture, land improvement, rural housing, drinking water, roads, education, health, and poverty alleviation that state legislatures are expected to devolve to panchayats under Article 243G. **The Twelfth Schedule** lists eighteen subjects including urban planning, regulation of land use, public health, slum improvement, and urban poverty alleviation that are expected to be devolved to municipalities under Article 243W. The permissive framing of Articles 243G and 243W — using "may" rather than "shall" for specific devolution — has allowed states to maintain nominal Schedule compliance while withholding substantive function transfer.

## How courts have interpreted it

The constitutional obligation to hold timely local body elections — established by Articles 243E and 243U — has been the most litigated aspect of Parts IX and IXA. Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of the City of Ahmedabad (2006) is the leading case establishing that Articles 243E and 243U are mandatory, not directory, and that state governments cannot delay elections beyond the five-year term by any administrative mechanism, including delimitation or reservation revision exercises. The Court held that the constitutional requirement of timely elections reflects the democratic right of citizens to elect their local representatives on schedule and that this right cannot be sacrificed to administrative convenience. Subsequent cases built on Tomar to hold that courts can direct the holding of elections within specific time frames when states delay, and that states cannot use delimitation or reservation-revision processes to produce indefinite intervals without elected local bodies.

The OBC reservation question — which has generated the most complex recent litigation in this field — centres on whether and under what conditions backward class quotas may be added to the SC/ST and women's reservations mandated by Articles 243D and 243T. Suresh Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra (2004) and K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010) established that OBC reservation at local body level is constitutionally permissible under Articles 243D(6) and 243T(6) but must be based on empirical data justifying both the fact of backwardness and the quantum of reservation, and that the fifty percent ceiling on total reservation must be observed. Vikas Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021) crystallised these requirements into the "triple test": a dedicated commission must inquire into backwardness, its recommendations must rest on empirical data, and the total reservation must not exceed fifty percent of seats in any local body. The Court struck down Maharashtra's OBC reservation notification for the 2021 elections because the state had not fulfilled these conditions, directing elections to proceed without OBC reservation and galvanising state-wide compliance efforts.

The finance question — whether local bodies receive adequate resources to discharge their constitutionally expected functions — has received less direct judicial attention than election timing or reservation, but it is constitutionally grounded in the State Finance Commission framework of Articles 243H and 243X. Courts have held that the obligation to constitute Finance Commissions is mandatory and justiciable. The adequacy of actual devolution — whether local bodies receive funds sufficient for their Schedule functions — remains primarily a matter of political accountability and Comptroller and Auditor General oversight rather than direct judicial supervision, making this the area where constitutional aspiration and administrative reality diverge most consistently.

## Key cases (detailed)

**Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of the City of Ahmedabad (2006)** established the constitutional principle that Articles 243E and 243U make timely elections mandatory. The Supreme Court held that the right to elect local body representatives at constitutionally required intervals is a democratic right that belongs to citizens, not a discretionary privilege that states may withhold. The Court directed elections to proceed within a fixed time frame and held that delimitation and reservation revision exercises cannot be used as reasons to postpone elections indefinitely. The judgment has been cited in dozens of subsequent cases where states delayed panchayat or municipal elections and remains the foundational precedent for court-directed local body elections across India.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** addressed OBC reservation in local body elections and held that while constitutionally permissible, it must be based on contemporaneous empirical data about the social and educational backwardness of OBCs in local self-government and must not cause total reservations to exceed fifty percent. The judgment established the evidentiary requirements for OBC reservation and was the direct precursor to the triple-test framework. It also addressed the women's reservation obligation under Articles 243D and 243T, holding that the constitutional minimum of one-third reservation for women at ward and chairperson levels is mandatory and that states that have raised the requirement to fifty percent have done so within constitutional authority.

**Vikas Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021)** is the most important recent case on local body reservations. A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the constitutional validity of OBC reservation provisions while holding that the triple test — dedicated commission inquiry, empirical data, fifty percent ceiling — must be met before any OBC reservation notification can be issued. The Court struck down Maharashtra's OBC reservation order for the 2021 local body elections because the state had not established the required commission or gathered the required data, and directed elections to proceed without OBC reservation. The judgment galvanised state governments across India to set up OBC empirical data commissions but compliance has been uneven and the Supreme Court has had to supervise implementation through subsequent orders in multiple states.

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra (2004)** is the earlier foundational case on OBC local body reservation, where the Supreme Court first held that OBC reservation at the local body level is constitutionally valid but requires empirical justification and cannot cause total reservations to exceed fifty percent. The case laid the doctrinal groundwork that K. Krishna Murthy and Vikas Gawali subsequently developed, and established the principle that OBC reservation in local bodies — unlike SC/ST reservation, which is proportional and mandatory — is a policy choice that requires constitutional justification in the form of backward-class data specific to the local governmental context.

## Live issues and current significance

The OBC triple-test requirement from Vikas Gawali continues to drive the most significant pattern of constitutional non-compliance in local government: delayed elections pending OBC data commissions. After the 2021 judgment, states across India faced situations where local body terms expired but elections were held without OBC reservation because the data commissions had not yet reported. State governments in some instances chose to delay elections rather than hold them without OBC reservation, producing extended periods of unelected administrator-appointed local governance. The Supreme Court has had to supervise compliance with election-directing orders, issuing increasingly firm directions that elections must be held within fixed time frames regardless of whether OBC data commissions have completed their work. The fundamental tension between the right to timely elections and the political imperative of elections with OBC reservation remains unresolved and continues to generate litigation in state High Courts and the Supreme Court.

Urban local body finance is a structural weakness in the constitutional design that has become more pressing as India urbanises rapidly. The Fifteenth Finance Commission recommended direct grants to local bodies, improved own-source revenue collection, and greater integration of municipal finance with state budgets. But the implementation of Finance Commission recommendations varies enormously by state, and most urban local bodies — particularly in smaller and medium-sized cities — lack the fiscal capacity to exercise the functions listed in the Twelfth Schedule. The constitutional obligation of Article 243X to constitute Finance Commissions is framed in a way that requires their establishment but not the implementation of their recommendations. Reform proposals focus on making own-source revenue collection a constitutional obligation, strengthening property tax as the principal urban fiscal tool, and conditioning central transfers on actual devolution of Schedule functions rather than nominal compliance.

The women's reservation in local bodies — where the constitutional minimum of one-third has been implemented and many states have raised it to fifty percent — has produced an extensive record of political participation and representation at the third tier. The rotation of reserved wards across election cycles, however, has been criticised for preventing women from building lasting electoral careers in specific constituencies, limiting the long-term political development that reservation is meant to foster. This debate connects to the women's reservation law enacted for Parliament and state legislatures in 2023 (the 106th Amendment), whose implementation is itself conditional on delimitation — making the local body women's reservation framework a constitutional testing ground for how larger reservations-in-legislatures schemes actually operate in practice.
