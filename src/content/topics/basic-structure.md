## Why this topic matters

Without the doctrine, constitutional amendment could become constitutional replacement. The doctrine keeps Article 368 powerful but bounded, ensuring that Parliament's constituent power cannot be used to dismantle the very framework that authorises Parliament itself to exist. It is the Court's answer to the question of whether any constitutional provision can be weaponised against the Constitution as a whole — and the answer the Court gave in 1973 was no. Without this limit, a transient parliamentary majority could amend away judicial independence, federalism, or fundamental rights, making the Constitution a tool of whoever holds power rather than a structure that constrains power.

## How to read the topic

The doctrine is best approached through Kesavananda Bharati and the later cases that rely on it when disputes arise over institutional design, democracy, federalism, and rights. Readers should trace the arc from Golaknath (where the Court held that rights cannot be abridged by amendment) through Kesavananda Bharati (where the Court permitted amendment but forbade destruction of basic structure) and onwards to Minerva Mills (where Parliament's attempt to remove the Court's power to review amendments was itself struck down). Each case refines what "basic structure" means by testing it against a specific amendment, which is why the doctrine is best understood as a line of cases rather than a single ruling.

## The doctrine and its limits

The basic structure doctrine does not consist of a fixed statutory list. It is instead a set of constitutional principles derived from the Constitution's overall identity — a constellation of features whose presence together makes the document recognisably a democratic, rights-respecting, federal constitution. Democratic governance, federalism, separation of powers, judicial review, the fundamental rights guaranteed in Part III, and secularism are all well-established candidates; so are the rule of law, free and fair elections, and the independence of the Election Commission. The doctrine operates as a ceiling on constitutional amendment: Parliament can alter structures, redistribute powers, re-configure rights, and even repeal individual provisions so long as it does not destroy any of these essential characteristics. An amendment that abolishes judicial review, removes fundamental rights wholesale, converts India into a unitary state, or permanently entrenches one party in office would cross the ceiling and be void. The principal difficulty in enforcing the doctrine is that no authoritative text enumerates basic structure elements: the Court identifies them case by case, which creates uncertainty about what future amendments might cross the line. Critics argue that this gives unelected judges a veto over democratic choices; defenders argue that it is the only structural guarantee against constitutional suicide, and that the Court has exercised the power with restraint, striking down only a handful of amendments since 1973.

## Key cases

- [Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala](../cases/kesavananda-bharati) is the foundational ruling, where a thirteen-judge bench held by a narrow majority that Parliament's power to amend under Article 368 does not extend to altering the basic structure or essential features of the Constitution.
- [Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India](../cases/minerva-mills) struck down clauses inserted by the 42nd Amendment that purported to make Parliament's amending power absolute and to immunise Directive Principles from judicial review, establishing that Parliament cannot enlarge its own constituent power beyond what the Constitution grants.
- [Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain](../cases/indira-nehru-gandhi-v-raj-narain) applied the doctrine to an election dispute, holding that the 39th Amendment's attempt to exclude certain election disputes from judicial scrutiny was void because free and fair elections constitute a basic structural element of the Constitution.
- [I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu](../cases/ir-coelho) extended the doctrine to the Ninth Schedule, holding that laws placed in the Ninth Schedule after 24 April 1973 (the date of Kesavananda Bharati) can be judicially reviewed if they violate fundamental rights that form part of the basic structure, closing the route of using the Schedule as a permanent safe harbour for rights-violating legislation.

## Current significance

Parliament retains the formal power to amend every provision of the Constitution under Article 368, but the basic structure doctrine means that power is not self-validating. Every significant constitutional amendment now faces the implicit question of whether it alters a structural element rather than merely modifying a provision. This question is active today: proposals to extend the amending power to judicial appointments, to overturn collegium-related rulings by amendment, or to restructure the division of powers between Centre and states all raise basic structure arguments.
