## Why this topic matters

Privacy entered Indian constitutional law not through an explicit textual provision but through judicial interpretation of existing rights. The Supreme Court's recognition in 2017 that privacy is a fundamental right implicit in Article 21 and in the structure of Part III resolved a question that had been contested since the Constitution's commencement. The significance of that recognition extends far beyond doctrine: it provided a constitutional foundation for challenging biometric surveillance, data-harvesting by state and private actors, criminalisation of consensual adult relationships, and mandatory digital identity systems. Privacy jurisprudence now touches nearly every domain of governance in which technology mediates the relationship between the individual and the state.

The constitutional status of privacy was uncertain for decades because the Supreme Court's early judgments — particularly M.P. Sharma (1954) and Kharak Singh (1962) — held, or appeared to hold, that the Constitution contained no right to privacy. These decisions prevented courts from engaging substantively with a growing category of rights claims involving information, bodily autonomy, and freedom from surveillance. The consequence was a doctrinal gap that Parliament and the executive exploited: identity schemes, surveillance infrastructure, and data-collection programmes were built without constitutional scrutiny. Puttaswamy (2017) closed that gap, but the task of working out what proportionality means in the context of large-scale digital governance — including welfare delivery systems, biometric databases, and law-enforcement surveillance — remains unfinished.

Privacy's intersection with other fundamental rights compounds its importance. The decriminalisation of same-sex relations in Navtej Singh Johar (2018) rested substantially on privacy as a dimension of dignity and autonomy under Article 21. The challenge to mandatory Aadhaar linkage of bank accounts and mobile SIM cards engaged privacy, equality, and the separation of legislative powers through the Money Bill controversy. And the protection of personal information from both state and private actors is the constitutional foundation of India's data protection framework, even though the statutory architecture under the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 remains to be tested against the constitutional standard of proportionality.

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 19(1)(a)** guarantees freedom of speech and expression, which the Supreme Court has read as including a right to communicate anonymously in some contexts and a right not to have the content of private communications disclosed — dimensions that directly engage informational privacy and protection against surveillance of communications.

**Article 19(1)(d)** guarantees freedom of movement throughout the territory of India. Location tracking and movement surveillance engage this right alongside Article 21's privacy component when the state monitors the movements of individuals without legal authorisation.

**Article 21** guarantees that no person shall be deprived of life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law. The Supreme Court has held that "life" in Article 21 extends to a life of dignity, and that privacy — in its informational, bodily, and spatial dimensions — is a necessary condition for living with dignity. Any state infringement of privacy must therefore be authorised by law, must serve a legitimate state aim, and must be necessary and proportionate.

**Article 20(3)** protects every accused person from being compelled to be a witness against themselves. The Supreme Court has connected this protection to privacy and bodily integrity, holding in Selvi v. State of Karnataka (2010) that narcoanalysis, brain-mapping, and polygraph testing without consent violate Article 20(3) and Article 21.

**Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) — Puttaswamy I** established privacy as a fundamental right, overruling M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh. Although it is a judicial decision rather than a textual provision, its authority is equivalent to constitutional text for the purposes of privacy analysis. The nine-judge bench identified multiple dimensions of privacy — personal autonomy, informational privacy, privacy of choice, and dignity — and held that each is protected under Article 21 and is subject to limitation only by law that satisfies a three-part test: there must be a legitimate state aim, the means must be rationally connected to the aim, and the means must be the least restrictive available (proportionality).

**The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023** is the primary statutory framework governing personal data processing by state and private actors. The Act establishes data principals' rights (to access, correct, and erase data), obligations on data fiduciaries, and a Data Protection Board. Its relationship to Puttaswamy's constitutional proportionality standard, particularly regarding the scope of government exemptions and the independence of the regulatory body, will be tested as implementing rules are notified and challenged.

## How courts have interpreted it

Before 2017 privacy doctrine in India was a patchwork. The Supreme Court had recognised aspects of privacy — the right not to have one's home surveilled without warrant in Kharak Singh (1962), the right not to be compelled to self-incriminate under Article 20(3), the bodily integrity dimension of Article 21 — without assembling these into a unified right. High Courts had developed privacy protections for telephonic communications, medical records, and sexual identity in their individual capacities, creating a rich but doctrinally fragmented body of law. The absence of a foundational Supreme Court precedent recognising privacy as a fundamental right meant that each privacy claim had to be argued from first principles, and that the executive could resist such claims by invoking M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh.

Puttaswamy I (2017) effected a comprehensive doctrinal consolidation. The nine-judge bench did not merely recognise privacy — it mapped its constitutional genealogy, identified its component dimensions, articulated a proportionality framework for assessing government intrusions, and explicitly overruled the earlier decisions that had denied its existence. The judgment is encyclopaedic in scope and has been characterised as a constitutional moment — a decision that reoriented not just one field of law but the entire framework within which the relationship between the individual and the information-state would be understood. Subsequent decisions have drawn on specific dimensions of the Puttaswamy I framework: Navtej Singh Johar used the privacy of sexual identity; Puttaswamy II (Aadhaar, 2018) applied the proportionality standard to biometric identity; and the Selvi narcoanalysis case, though predating Puttaswamy I, was reconsidered in its light.

The proportionality framework articulated in Puttaswamy I has not yet been applied by the Court in a comprehensive manner to state surveillance infrastructure. Cases involving centralised monitoring systems, metadata collection, facial recognition programmes, and geofencing have been filed but not finally decided. The constitutional question of whether bulk collection of personal information — even without targeted analysis of any individual's data — constitutes an infringement of privacy rights remains open. Lower courts have begun to engage with this question, and the Supreme Court's eventual engagement with mass-surveillance proportionality will be one of the defining constitutional developments of the coming decade.

## Key cases (detailed)

**Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India — Puttaswamy I (2017)** — A retired High Court judge, K.S. Puttaswamy, filed a writ petition challenging the constitutional validity of the Aadhaar programme. The Supreme Court constituted a nine-judge bench to decide the threshold question of whether the Constitution of India recognises a fundamental right to privacy. All nine judges held that it does. The bench overruled M.P. Sharma (1954) and Kharak Singh (1962) insofar as they held that no such right existed. The six separate opinions — by Justices Khehar, Nariman, Chandrachud, Bobde, Sapre, and Chelameswar — converged on the proposition that privacy is intrinsic to human dignity and liberty, that it is protected under Articles 14, 19, and 21 read together, and that the state may infringe privacy only when it can show that the infringement is authorised by law, serves a legitimate aim, is rationally connected to that aim, and is necessary and proportionate. Justice Chandrachud's opinion added a fourth requirement: procedural guarantees against abuse. The judgment is the constitutional foundation of all subsequent privacy, data protection, and surveillance litigation in India.

**Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India — Puttaswamy II — Aadhaar (2018)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench applied the Puttaswamy I proportionality standard to the Aadhaar Act 2016 and the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) scheme. The majority of four judges upheld the core Aadhaar framework — biometric enrollment and authentication for state welfare — as a proportionate means of ensuring that benefits reach genuine beneficiaries and of reducing fraud. The majority also upheld the classification of the Aadhaar Act as a Money Bill under Article 110. It struck down Section 57, which had permitted private entities — including telecom companies and banks — to use Aadhaar authentication for commercial purposes, on the ground that private-sector use of state-held biometric data without consent and without a legitimate state aim was disproportionate. Justice Chandrachud dissented comprehensively: he held that the Money Bill classification was constitutionally improper; that mandatory biometric seeding into welfare databases created an aggregable surveillance architecture that no welfare justification could support; and that the majority's proportionality analysis was insufficiently rigorous about the surveillance risks of centralised biometric identity infrastructure.

**Zulfiquar Ahman Khan v. Union of India (2024)** — A Division Bench of the Delhi High Court, in proceedings challenging the mandatory biometric registration of individuals under various government schemes, applied the Puttaswamy proportionality framework to the specific risks of biometric data storage and linkage. The Court held that biometric data — unlike demographic data — is uniquely identifying, immutable, and irreplaceable once compromised, and that its mandatory collection requires not only a statutory basis but also robust security standards, mandatory breach notification, and an independent data protection authority with real enforcement powers. The judgment is significant because it applied Puttaswamy I's proportionality to biometric data at the level of programme-specific scrutiny rather than at the level of constitutional principle, and because it anticipated the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023's regulatory framework while holding that the Act's government exemptions must be read narrowly to be consistent with constitutional obligations.

**Selvi v. State of Karnataka (2010)** — A three-judge bench of the Supreme Court held that compulsory administration of narcoanalysis, brain-mapping (P300 or BEOS), and polygraph tests violates Articles 20(3) and 21. The Court held that Article 20(3)'s protection against self-incrimination extends to testimonial acts even where they are coerced through means other than direct physical compulsion, and that the right to mental privacy — the right not to have the contents of one's mind extracted under compulsion — is a dimension of the right to privacy under Article 21. The judgment drew explicitly on the idea of mental privacy as a protected dimension of selfhood and linked it to the integrity of the criminal justice process.

## Live issues and current significance

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 is the most immediate live issue. The Act provides a statutory framework for data protection but has been criticised for the breadth of its government exemption clause (Section 17), which permits the central government to exempt any government instrumentality from the Act's obligations on grounds of national security and sovereignty. Critics argue that this exemption, if given the width its text suggests, would effectively immunise the state's own data-processing activities — the largest category of potential privacy violations in India — from regulatory scrutiny, contrary to the Puttaswamy I requirement that state infringements of privacy be proportionate and subject to procedural safeguards. The constitutionality of Section 17 and the independence of the Data Protection Board as a regulatory authority are expected to be tested in litigation once the Act's implementation rules are notified.

The Supreme Court's referral in Rojer Mathew on Money Bill classification, if decided against the government, could invalidate the Aadhaar Act's passage and reopen the constitutional status of the entire Aadhaar framework. This would not automatically dismantle the programme — Parliament could re-pass the Act through ordinary legislative procedure — but it would require fresh legislative consideration and potentially a fresh constitutional challenge. The pending reference therefore has direct practical significance for the hundreds of millions of Indians enrolled in Aadhaar-linked welfare schemes.

Surveillance law reform is a structural gap in India's constitutional and statutory architecture. India has no comprehensive legal framework governing interception of communications, data collection by intelligence agencies, or facial recognition deployment by law enforcement. The Information Technology Act's interception provisions (Section 69) have been challenged as constitutionally inadequate, and the Supreme Court has noted in passing judgments that the absence of a judicial-warrant requirement for interception is constitutionally troubling. The People's Union for Civil Liberties telephone-tapping case (1997) established some procedural safeguards by court order, but these remain insufficient against modern surveillance capabilities. A constitutional challenge to bulk surveillance infrastructure — encompassing NATGRID, CCTNS, and facial recognition databases — has been filed and is pending. The outcome of that litigation will determine whether Puttaswamy I's proportionality framework has any operational force against the Indian surveillance state.
