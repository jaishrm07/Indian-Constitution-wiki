## Why this topic matters

Emergency powers occupy the most consequential and most dangerous corner of constitutional law. The provisions in Part XVIII — Articles 352 to 360 — are designed for extraordinary circumstances: external aggression, armed rebellion, constitutional breakdown in a state, and financial crisis. But because they allow the normal constitutional architecture to be suspended, concentrated in the Centre, or overridden entirely, they have also been the provisions most vulnerable to political misuse. India's constitutional history with emergency powers is not theoretical: the national Emergency of 1975 to 1977 remains the most serious rupture in Indian democratic history, and the imposition of President's Rule under Article 356 in states has been wielded as a partisan tool dozens of times since 1950. Understanding these provisions requires reading the text alongside that history, and understanding how the courts have attempted — sometimes successfully, sometimes catastrophically — to discipline their use.

The institutional stakes are federal as well as rights-related. Articles 352 and 356 are principally federal provisions: a National Emergency under Article 352 effectively converts India into a unitary state for its duration, vesting Parliament with plenary legislative authority over state subjects and enabling the Central executive to give directions to states. Article 356 goes further in a targeted way, removing the elected government of a specific state and replacing it with Union authority through the Governor. Both provisions therefore strike at the heart of India's federal design — the distribution of power between Centre and states — as well as at the democratic accountability of elected state governments. The judicialisation of these provisions, particularly through S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994), has been one of the most significant judicial contributions to Indian federalism.

Readers should understand this topic not only because of its dramatic history but because its practical significance is ongoing. President's Rule continues to be invoked, Governor-state conflicts continue to test the boundaries of Article 356, and questions about emergency governance — raised sharply by the COVID-19 pandemic response — continue to reveal gaps in the constitutional framework. The fundamental question that Part XVIII poses is whether a democracy can arm itself with extraordinary powers sufficient to survive genuine crises without making those powers so available that they become instruments of ordinary politics. India's constitutional experience over seventy-five years provides rich, complex, and still-unresolved material for thinking about that question.

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 352** authorises the President to proclaim a National Emergency on the ground that the security of India or any part thereof is threatened by war, external aggression, or armed rebellion. After the 44th Amendment of 1978, a proclamation requires the written advice of the Cabinet (not just the Prime Minister), must be approved by both Houses of Parliament by a special majority within one month, and must be renewed every six months. Each House of Parliament may also pass a resolution by simple majority requiring the President to revoke the proclamation, giving the opposition a mechanism to dissolve an Emergency that has lost legislative support.

**Article 353** sets out the consequences of a National Emergency: Parliament acquires power to legislate on State List subjects, the Union executive can give binding directions to states on any subject, and the provisions of Article 352 relating to the Union's authority over states are extended for the duration. The effect is a formal constitutional shift from a federal to a quasi-unitary structure.

**Article 354** permits the President during a National Emergency to modify the provisions of Part XII relating to the distribution of revenues between the Union and states, enabling Central authority to redirect financial flows that would normally go to states.

**Article 355** imposes a duty on the Union to protect every state against external aggression and internal disturbance and to ensure that the government of every state is carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution. It is both the duty that justifies Article 356 and a limit on it: the power to intervene under Article 356 must be exercised for the purpose stated in Article 355, not for partisan advantage.

**Article 356** is the most frequently litigated and most politically contested emergency provision. It empowers the President, on the satisfaction that the government of a state cannot be carried on in accordance with the Constitution, to assume to the President the functions of the state government, suspend the Legislative Assembly, and have Parliament exercise the state legislature's functions. It is triggered on the Governor's report or otherwise, meaning it can technically be invoked without a formal Governor's report. The S.R. Bommai judgment radically changed its operation by making every proclamation justiciable and requiring an objective material basis.

**Article 357** provides for the exercise of Parliament's legislative authority in relation to a state under Article 356 proclamation, and for the delegation of legislative power to the President and through the President to other authorities.

**Article 358** provides that Article 19 fundamental rights are automatically suspended during a National Emergency if the proclamation is on the ground of war or external aggression — but not on the ground of armed rebellion, a distinction introduced by the 44th Amendment.

**Article 359** permits the President to suspend the right to move courts for enforcement of fundamental rights during a National Emergency, but after the 44th Amendment the rights under Articles 20 and 21 cannot be suspended under any circumstances. This is the direct legislative response to ADM Jabalpur, which had allowed even Articles 20 and 21 to be overridden.

**Article 360** authorises a Financial Emergency when the financial stability of India or any part thereof is threatened. It has never been invoked.

## How courts have interpreted it

The judicial history of emergency powers divides sharply into two eras: before and after S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994). The pre-Bommai era was defined by ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976), where a four-judge majority of the Supreme Court held that during a National Emergency proclaimed under Article 352, individuals detained under preventive detention orders have no locus to approach any court for enforcement of Article 21. The ruling effectively suspended the right to life during the 1975 Emergency and is widely regarded as the Supreme Court's worst judgment: Justice H.R. Khanna dissented alone, holding that Article 21 could not be completely abrogated even during an Emergency, and paid for his dissent with supersession in the appointment of the next Chief Justice. ADM Jabalpur was formally overruled in K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017), where a nine-judge Bench held that the right to life and liberty under Article 21 is part of the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be suspended even during an Emergency, vindicating Justice Khanna's dissent four decades later.

S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) is the transformative judgment on Article 356. A nine-judge Constitution Bench held that Presidential proclamations under Article 356 are subject to full judicial review, that the Court can examine whether objective material existed to justify the President's satisfaction, and that the floor test — a test of majority on the floor of the Assembly — is the constitutionally proper method of determining whether a government commands the confidence of the legislature. Critically, the Court also held that secularism is part of the basic structure of the Constitution, and that a state government that follows an anti-secular path thereby provides objective material for the President's satisfaction under Article 356. The Bommai judgment converted Article 356 from a near-unreviewable political tool into a judicially accountable power, though it did not end political misuse: it merely required that misuse be better disguised or justified with objective material.

The COVID-19 pandemic's governance revealed a different dimension of emergency powers: the absence of any pandemic-specific emergency provision in Part XVIII. The Union and state governments relied on the National Disaster Management Act, 2005 and the Epidemic Diseases Act, 1897 — neither of which is a constitutional provision — to exercise emergency-like powers. This exposed a gap in the constitutional design: Part XVIII addresses war, armed rebellion, constitutional breakdown, and financial crisis, but not biological or ecological disasters. The pandemic response generated litigation about lockdowns, vaccine policy, and the treatment of migrant workers that tested constitutional rights under the ordinary framework rather than the emergency framework, raising questions about whether the constitutional design is adequate for the crises most likely to arise in the twenty-first century.

## Key cases (detailed)

**ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976)** is the nadir of Indian constitutional jurisprudence and a case that every student of emergency powers must understand precisely because of how badly it went wrong. The question before a five-judge Bench was whether, during the 1975 National Emergency, a person detained under MISA could approach a High Court under Article 226 for a writ of habeas corpus. Four judges — Ray CJ, Beg, Chandrachud, and Mathew JJ — held that they could not: the suspension of Article 21 under Article 359 meant there was no right to approach a court for enforcement of liberty, and the executive's authority during an Emergency was unchallengeable. Justice H.R. Khanna dissented, holding that even without Article 21 there is a common law right to liberty and that the executive cannot detain a person without legal authority. The majority ruling gave the Emergency government judicial cover for arbitrary detention. The 44th Amendment partially repaired the constitutional design by making Articles 20 and 21 non-suspendable; Puttaswamy 2017 completed the repair by establishing these rights as basic structure, formally overruling ADM Jabalpur's constitutional reasoning.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** reshaped Article 356 from a political convenience into a legally constrained power. The case arose from a challenge to the dismissal of several state governments, and a nine-judge Constitution Bench issued nine separate opinions that collectively established: Article 356 proclamations are subject to judicial review on the ground of mala fide exercise or absence of objective material; the floor test is the constitutionally preferred method of establishing majority; a state government should be given the opportunity to demonstrate its majority before being dismissed; secularism is a basic structure element and its violation provides objective material for Article 356; and Parliament must approve the proclamation for it to continue beyond two months. The judgment created a legal framework that constrains the power and enables affected governments to seek judicial redress, even if it has not ended political misuse entirely.

**K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)** formally overruled ADM Jabalpur's constitutional reasoning as part of its holding that the right to privacy is a fundamental right under Article 21. The nine-judge Bench's reasoning on the inviolability of Article 21 during an Emergency is most fully developed in the concurring opinions of Nariman, Chandrachud, and Kaul JJ, all of whom explicitly held that Article 21 cannot be suspended even during an Emergency proclaimed under Article 352, that any such suspension would violate the basic structure, and that Justice H.R. Khanna's lone dissent in ADM Jabalpur was correct as a matter of constitutional principle. Puttaswamy effectively completed the 44th Amendment's project of making life and liberty emergency-proof, establishing that the 1976 majority's reasoning is constitutionally void.

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India (1980)** struck down clauses of the 42nd Amendment that had attempted to make National Emergency and Article 356 proclamations immune from judicial review and had sought to remove the courts' power to examine whether objective material existed. The Court held that these clauses violated the basic structure by destroying judicial review. The judgment confirmed that even Parliament's constituent power cannot be used to make emergency provisions unchallengeable, establishing the principle that any constitutional amendment that removes the courts' ability to review emergency proclamations is void.

## Live issues and current significance

President's Rule under Article 356 remains a live constitutional tool. Since S.R. Bommai, impositions of Article 356 have been challenged in court and occasionally stayed, but the political incentives to use the provision against opposition-governed states have not disappeared. The pattern of Governor's reports, delayed floor tests, and contested proclamations recurs whenever a state government faces a political crisis. The constitutional question of what counts as sufficient objective material — and whether the Bommai floor-test requirement has been properly followed — arises in each instance. Recent impositions have generated High Court and Supreme Court proceedings that continue to refine the Bommai doctrine on objective satisfaction and reviewability.

The COVID-19 experience exposed the constitutional inadequacy of India's emergency framework for public health crises. The Union's reliance on the NDMA and Epidemic Diseases Act — statutory rather than constitutional emergency powers — meant that the ordinary federal division of powers remained technically in place even as the Union exercised effective central coordination authority. The constitutional questions about the legality of lockdowns, curfews, vaccine sequencing, and the treatment of migrant labour were litigated under Articles 14, 19, and 21 without the emergency framework that Part XVIII would have provided. This experience has renewed debate about whether the Constitution needs a public health emergency provision and what the constitutional safeguards for such a provision would need to be, given the risk that any new emergency clause could be misused as Article 356 was before Bommai.

The relationship between Governor's powers under Article 356 and the Governor's ordinary relationship with an elected state government — including the power to summon or prorogue the Assembly, withhold assent to Bills, and invite leaders to form governments after hung election results — has generated a series of constitutional cases. The Nabam Rebia judgment, the Tamil Nadu Governor assent case, and several state-level High Court rulings have incrementally developed a body of law holding that Governors cannot use their discretionary powers to frustrate elected state governments. This jurisprudence applies the Bommai principle — that Union constitutional officers must act on objective constitutional grounds, not partisan ones — to the Governor's ordinary role, and it represents the most active zone of constitutional litigation at the intersection of emergency powers, federalism, and responsible government.
