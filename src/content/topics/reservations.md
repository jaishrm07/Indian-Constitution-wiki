## Why this topic matters

Reservations are a constitutionally authorised departure from formal equality, designed to achieve substantive equality for groups that have faced historic and structural disadvantage. OBC, SC, ST, and EWS reservations each rest on different constitutional bases: SC and ST reservations have been part of the constitutional framework since the original Constitution of 1949, OBC reservations were enabled by the First Amendment in 1951, and EWS reservation was added by the 103rd Amendment in 2019 as the only category defined purely by economic criteria rather than social backwardness. The 50% ceiling on total reservations, established in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992), remains the most contested numerical limit in Indian constitutional law. Reservation debates repeatedly test what equality means in a deeply unequal society, exposing persistent tensions between formal neutrality, representational justice, and constitutional structure.

## Constitutional foundations

The reservation framework rests on a cluster of articles and amendments spread across Parts III and XVI of the Constitution.

**Article 15(4)** was inserted by the First Constitutional Amendment Act, 1951, and permits the State to make special provision for the advancement of any socially and educationally backward classes of citizens or for SC and ST communities, as a defined exception to the non-discrimination guarantee in Article 15(1) and (2). It was the direct legislative response to Champakam Dorairajan (1951), which had struck down a caste-based communal reservation in college admissions.

**Article 15(5)** was inserted by the Constitution (Ninety-Third Amendment) Act, 2005, extending the State's power to make special provision for admission of backward classes, SCs, and STs into private unaided educational institutions (other than minority institutions protected by Article 30). The 93rd Amendment simultaneously inserted Article 15(6) through the 103rd Amendment (see below) and was upheld by the Supreme Court in Ashoka Kumar Thakur v. Union of India (2008) subject to the exclusion of the creamy layer in OBC admissions.

**Article 16(4)** is the foundational employment reservation clause, authorising the State to make provision for reservation of appointments or posts in favour of any backward class of citizens that is not adequately represented in the State services. It has been part of the Constitution since 1950. The Court has consistently held that Article 16(4) is not an exception to the equality norm under Article 16(1) but a facet of that norm — a recognition that adequate representation is itself a component of equality.

**Article 16(4A)** was inserted by the Constitution (Seventy-Seventh Amendment) Act, 1995, in direct response to the Indra Sawhney holding that reservations in promotions are not constitutionally compelled. It permits the State to make provision for reservation in promotions in favour of SC and ST employees who are not adequately represented in the services. The phrase "with consequential seniority" was later added by the 85th Amendment (2001), preserving the seniority benefits that flow from reserved promotions.

**Article 16(4B)** was inserted by the Constitution (Eighty-First Amendment) Act, 2000, in response to the judicial rule that unfilled reserved vacancies cannot be indefinitely carried forward. It permits the State to treat the backlog of unfilled SC and ST reserved vacancies as a separate bank not subject to the 50% ceiling in any given recruitment year.

**Article 16(6)** was inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and Third Amendment) Act, 2019, to enable up to 10% reservation in public employment for Economically Weaker Sections among citizens not covered by existing SC, ST, or OBC reservation schemes. It is read alongside Article 15(6), inserted by the same amendment, which enables EWS reservation in educational institutions.

**Article 46** is a Directive Principle directing the State to promote with special care the educational and economic interests of SC, ST, and other weaker sections and to protect them from social injustice. While not directly enforceable, it supplies the interpretive context in which the reservation provisions are read and has been cited by courts to justify a purposive construction of the equality articles.

**Amendment history in brief:** The First Amendment (1951) inserted Article 15(4) after Champakam Dorairajan. The 77th Amendment (1995) inserted Article 16(4A) after Indra Sawhney. The 81st Amendment (2000) inserted Article 16(4B). The 85th Amendment (2001) added "with consequential seniority" to Article 16(4A). The 93rd Amendment (2005) inserted Article 15(5) for reservations in private educational institutions. The 103rd Amendment (2019) inserted Articles 15(6) and 16(6) for EWS reservation.

## The evolution of doctrine

**Champakam Dorairajan (1951)** was the first reservation case to reach the Supreme Court. The Court struck down the Communal Government Order of Madras, which allocated college seats by caste and religion, holding that it violated Article 15(1)'s prohibition on discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. The judgment also held that Article 46's directive principle could not override an express fundamental rights guarantee. Parliament's immediate response — the First Amendment inserting Article 15(4) — set the pattern for the legislative-judicial dialogue that has defined reservation law ever since.

**M.R. Balaji v. State of Mysore (1963)** was the first major case to confront the outer limits of reservation. The Court held that reservation could not exceed 50% of available seats or posts in any year, reasoning that reservation above this level would convert an exception into the rule and damage the principle of open competition that equality of opportunity requires. This 50% ceiling, though then stated as a guiding principle rather than an absolute rule, became the foundational numerical constraint in reservation law and was subsequently consolidated in Indra Sawhney.

**Devadasan v. Union of India (1964)** addressed the carry-forward rule, under which unfilled reserved vacancies were carried over to subsequent recruitment years and added to the current year's quota. The Court struck down a rule that produced a situation where 68% of posts in a year were reserved, holding that carry-forward could not be used in a way that frustrated non-reserved candidates in any particular year to an extent exceeding the 50% ceiling. The 81st Amendment (2000), which inserted Article 16(4B), later constitutionalised a version of the carry-forward principle by treating backlog vacancies as a separate bank.

**Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992)** is the foundational authority. A nine-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Mandal Commission's recommendation of 27% OBC reservation in central government services but imposed three structural constraints that continue to govern the field: (1) total reservations cannot ordinarily exceed 50% of available posts in any year; (2) the creamy layer among OBCs — those who by virtue of economic and social advancement no longer need the remedy — must be excluded from OBC reservation benefits; and (3) reservations in promotions are not constitutionally required or guaranteed by Article 16(4) and must be separately authorised if a state wishes to provide them. The judgment also established the distinction between SC and ST reservation (whose beneficiary groups are determined by Presidential notification and are not open to state-level redefinition) and OBC reservation (where the state retains power to identify backward classes). Indra Sawhney remains the principal reference for the architecture of the entire reservation system.

**M. Nagaraj v. Union of India (2006)** addressed the constitutional validity of the 77th and 81st Amendments. A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) as valid but held that they are enabling provisions, not self-executing commands. Before a state can exercise the powers they confer, it must satisfy three preconditions: (1) collect quantifiable data demonstrating inadequacy of SC and ST representation in the service category in question; (2) collect quantifiable data demonstrating the continuing backwardness of those communities; and (3) be satisfied that reservation will not adversely affect overall administrative efficiency under Article 335. The 50% ceiling continues to apply. The creamy layer question for SCs and STs in the promotions context was left open.

**Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018)** partially modified Nagaraj. A five-judge bench held that the requirement to collect separate data on continuing backwardness of SCs and STs was inconsistent with the constitutional scheme under Articles 341 and 342, which conclusively determine the identity of those communities. The backwardness condition was overruled to that extent. The inadequacy of representation condition (with quantifiable data) and the efficiency condition under Article 335 survive. The bench also resolved the creamy layer question that Nagaraj had deferred: the creamy layer concept does apply to SCs and STs for the purpose of reservation in promotions, extending the Indra Sawhney logic into the SC and ST domain in this specific context.

**B.K. Pavitra v. Union of India (II) (2019)** tested Karnataka's compliance with the Nagaraj-Jarnail framework after an earlier Karnataka scheme had been struck down. A five-judge bench upheld the Karnataka Reservation in Promotions Act 2018, holding that outcome-based representational data — showing the actual distribution of SC and ST employees across service levels relative to population proportion — is constitutionally adequate quantifiable evidence of inadequacy of representation. This case provides the most detailed practical guidance on what state data collection for reservation in promotions must look like.

**State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024)** is the most recent major structural development. A seven-judge Constitution Bench, by a 6:1 majority, held that states may sub-classify within the Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe lists — that is, create internal priorities or sub-quotas so that the most disadvantaged sub-groups receive a larger share of reserved seats. The Court overruled E.V. Chinnaiah (2004), which had held that the SC list forms a homogeneous class incapable of internal differentiation. Sub-classification is now permissible provided the state has empirical justification for the internal distinction. Justice Bela Trivedi dissented on the ground that sub-classification effectively creates a new class within the constitutionally defined scheduled category, undermining the integrity of Presidential notification.

**Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (2022)** addressed the constitutional validity of the 103rd Amendment introducing EWS reservation. A five-judge bench, by a 3:2 majority, upheld Articles 15(6) and 16(6). The majority held that EWS reservation does not violate the basic structure even though it breaches the 50% ceiling, reasoning that the ceiling is a judicially developed rule applicable to Articles 16(4) and 16(4A) and does not bind a new, separately enacted constitutional provision. The majority also held that excluding SC, ST, and OBC persons from EWS reservation is not discriminatory because those communities are already covered by other reservation schemes. The dissent (Justices Ravindra Bhat and Hima Kohli) argued that the exclusion of SC/ST/OBC persons on the basis of class, and the sole reliance on economic criteria, introduced a discriminatory premise inconsistent with the basic structure.

**Ashoka Kumar Thakur v. Union of India (2008)** upheld the 93rd Amendment inserting Article 15(5) for OBC reservation in private unaided educational institutions. The Court held the amendment valid subject to the exclusion of the creamy layer from OBC reservation in educational admissions and protected the autonomy of minority institutions under Article 30. The case confirmed that the 93rd Amendment's reach does not extend to minority institutions.

## Key constitutional questions

**The 50% ceiling and basic structure.** The ceiling was established by judicial interpretation in M.R. Balaji (1963) and consolidated in Indra Sawhney (1992). It has never been removed by constitutional amendment; the 103rd Amendment's EWS reservation was instead housed in new provisions (Articles 15(6) and 16(6)) that the Court in Janhit Abhiyan treated as outside the ceiling's scope. Whether the ceiling itself forms part of the basic structure — such that Parliament could not override it even by amendment — has not been directly resolved and remains the deepest structural question in reservation law.

**Creamy layer applicability to SCs and STs.** The creamy layer doctrine, developed in Indra Sawhney for OBCs, was expressly held not applicable to SC and ST reservation in general services in that case. Jarnail Singh (2018) held that the creamy layer does apply to SCs and STs in the specific context of reservation in promotions. Whether this logic extends to the broader context of SC and ST reservation in direct recruitment remains open, and the question is periodically raised in litigation.

**Sub-classification within SCs and OBCs.** Before Davinder Singh (2024), the Court in E.V. Chinnaiah had treated the SC list as a homogeneous class that could not be internally differentiated. Davinder Singh overruled that position for SCs and STs. Sub-classification within OBCs has been practiced by states for longer and was not the direct subject of Davinder Singh, but the reasoning of the majority is broadly applicable. The conditions for sub-classification — empirical justification and proportionality — now frame how states must legislate.

**Reservation in promotions.** The promotion reservation debate has been the most heavily litigated strand in the field. Indra Sawhney held it unavailable under Article 16(4). The 77th Amendment re-enabled it via Article 16(4A). Nagaraj imposed a three-condition framework. Jarnail Singh removed one condition. B.K. Pavitra (II) confirmed what adequate compliance looks like. The question of how to define and implement the creamy layer exclusion for SCs and STs in promotions, mandated by Jarnail Singh, remains an open implementation challenge.

**EWS reservation and the economic criterion.** The 103rd Amendment introduced the first reservation category based entirely on economic criteria, excluding members of SC, ST, and OBC communities. Janhit Abhiyan upheld it by a narrow majority. The dissent's objection — that using economic backwardness as the sole criterion while excluding socially backward communities constitutes a form of class discrimination — continues to generate academic and political debate. The question of what data justifies the income threshold (currently Rs 8 lakh per annum) has not been judicially settled.

## Key cases

**[Indra Sawhney v. Union of India](../cases/indra-sawhney) (1992)** is the foundational nine-judge bench decision. It upheld 27% OBC reservation, imposed the 50% ceiling, established the creamy layer exclusion for OBCs, and held that reservation in promotions requires separate constitutional authorisation. Every subsequent reservation case is written in dialogue with this decision.

**[M. Nagaraj v. Union of India](../cases/m-nagaraj) (2006)** upheld the 77th and 81st Constitutional Amendments but subjected state exercise of the promotion reservation power to three preconditions: quantifiable data on inadequacy of representation, quantifiable data on continuing backwardness, and no adverse effect on efficiency under Article 335. The framework shaped over a decade of promotion reservation litigation.

**[Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta](../cases/jarnail-singh) (2018)** is the governing authority on reservation in promotions for SCs and STs. It removed the backwardness condition from Nagaraj, retained the representation and efficiency conditions, and held that the creamy layer concept applies to SCs and STs for the purposes of promotional reservation.

**[B.K. Pavitra v. Union of India (II)](../cases/b-k-pavitra) (2019)** upheld Karnataka's 2018 Reservation in Promotions Act as the first successful state compliance with the post-Jarnail Singh framework, confirming that outcome-based representational data satisfies the quantifiable data requirement.

**[State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh](../cases/state-of-punjab-v-davinder-singh) (2024)** — seven-judge bench, 6:1 majority — held that states may sub-classify within the SC and ST lists, overruling E.V. Chinnaiah (2004). States now have constitutional authority to create internal sub-quotas provided they demonstrate empirical justification for the internal distinction.

**[Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India](../cases/janhit-abhiyan) (2022)** upheld the 103rd Amendment's EWS reservation by a 3:2 majority, holding that Articles 15(6) and 16(6) are constitutionally valid and that EWS reservation does not damage the basic structure, even though it crosses the 50% ceiling applicable under the earlier reservation provisions.

**[Ashoka Kumar Thakur v. Union of India](../cases/ashoka-kumar-thakur) (2008)** upheld the 93rd Amendment enabling OBC reservation in private unaided educational institutions, with the creamy layer exclusion applied to educational admissions and minority institutions protected.

## Live issues

**Sub-classification implementation after Davinder Singh.** The 2024 judgment permits sub-classification but sets standards for it: there must be empirical justification for the internal distinction, and the sub-quota must be proportionate to the degree of underrepresentation of the sub-group. Several states — Punjab, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu among them — are either already implementing or framing sub-classification schemes for SC and ST lists. Litigation testing the adequacy of those schemes under the Davinder Singh standards is expected in the near term.

**State sub-classification schemes for OBCs.** Sub-classification within OBC lists has a longer history than within SC lists and was not the direct subject of Davinder Singh. Multiple states maintain sub-OBC quotas for the Most Backward Classes or Denotified Tribes. The doctrinal consolidation following Davinder Singh may lead to renewed challenges to these schemes and to fresh demands for similar internal prioritisation in states that do not yet have them.

**OBC municipal reservation and the triple test.** The Supreme Court in Vikas Kishanrao Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021) held that states cannot notify OBC reservation in local body elections without satisfying a triple test: constituting a dedicated commission to conduct contemporaneous empirical inquiry into backwardness and representation, acting on the commission's recommendations, and not exceeding the 50% ceiling. Several states have had their OBC municipal reservation struck down for failure to satisfy this test, and the litigation over fresh commission reports and their adequacy is ongoing.

**EWS data review.** The 10% EWS reservation rests on an income threshold of Rs 8 lakh per annum that has not been revised since the 103rd Amendment came into force in 2019. There is no constitutionally mandated mechanism for periodic review of this threshold, and the data basis for the threshold has been questioned. Whether the threshold is adequate or arbitrary is a question that may return to litigation if fresh data on economic distribution makes the classification harder to defend.

**Bihar caste survey and OBC enumeration.** The Bihar government's state-level caste survey, whose results were published in 2023, has renewed calls for a national-level caste census to update the empirical basis of OBC reservation. The central government's OBC reservation is still premised in significant part on the Mandal Commission's 1980 data. The question of whether outdated data can continue to sustain a 27% OBC quota — and what legal obligations flow from newly available enumeration data — is likely to generate both administrative and judicial activity in coming years.

## Current significance

Reservation law is in active evolution on multiple fronts simultaneously. The sub-classification question is moving from doctrine to implementation. The promotion reservation framework is operating under the Nagaraj-Jarnail-Pavitra sequence but generating fresh litigation on creamy layer exclusion criteria. EWS reservation has been judicially upheld but remains politically and empirically contested. The Bihar caste survey has added urgency to the question of whether current OBC identification data is constitutionally adequate. Against this background, the trajectory of Indian reservation law continues to be shaped by the iterative dialogue between the legislature, the executive, and the Court that has characterised this field since 1951.
