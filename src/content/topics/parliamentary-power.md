## Why this topic matters

Parliament is central to constitutional change, but its authority is not self-defining. Questions about amendment power, procedure, and structural limits shape some of the most important constitutional conflicts in Indian public law. Parliament's claimed sovereignty over its internal proceedings, combined with the Court's insistence that constitutional guarantees apply even inside those proceedings, creates a persistent tension that no single judgment has fully resolved. The discovery in Sita Soren that parliamentary privilege does not protect the pre-vote bribe reopened questions about the scope of immunity that many assumed had been settled since 1998. Meanwhile, structural reforms like One Nation One Election raise foundational questions about whether Parliament can, by ordinary or special majority, alter the terms of its own existence and the conditions for its dissolution.

## How to read the topic

Start with the constitutional position of Parliament and then move to Article 368. The most important reading path is through amendment-era cases and the debate over whether constituent power can be broad without being unlimited. Articles 105 and 194 are equally important entry points for understanding privilege — not because they define privilege exhaustively, but because their silences have been filled by evolving judicial interpretation. The privilege cases and the amendment-power cases are ultimately about the same constitutional question: whether Parliament can insulate its decisions from external review by claiming that they belong to a domain the Constitution has reserved for legislative self-governance. Reading Raja Ram Pal alongside Keshav Singh Reference and then Sita Soren traces the progressive narrowing of that claimed immunity.

## What parliamentary power covers

Parliamentary power under the Constitution includes legislative power (Articles 245–254 and Schedule VII), constituent power (Article 368), financial power (Articles 265–292), and the power of each House over its own procedure, privileges, and discipline (Articles 105, 118, 122). The legislative power is distributed between Parliament and state legislatures through three lists, but Parliament's residuary power under Entry 97 of the Union List and its override in cases of inter-state interest gives it a structurally superior position in the federation. The constituent power under Article 368 allows Parliament to alter almost any part of the Constitution by a special majority, subject to the basic structure doctrine established in Kesavananda Bharati — a judicially imposed outer limit that Parliament has contested but has not been able to override. The financial power, exercised through the annual budget and the Consolidated Fund, is backed by Article 265's requirement that no tax be levied or collected except by authority of law, making Parliament the gatekeeper for all public revenue. The privilege power under Articles 105 and 194 is the most contested — it protects legislative speech and votes from judicial scrutiny but does not shield pre-legislative corruption (Sita Soren, 2024).

## Key cases

- [Raja Ram Pal v. Hon'ble Speaker, Lok Sabha](../cases/raja-ram-pal) established that the expulsion of members by the House is judicially reviewable and that parliamentary privilege does not give the House unlimited authority to punish or exclude its own members without procedural fairness, even though expulsion is an internal disciplinary matter.

- [P.V. Narasimha Rao v. State (CBI/SPE)](../cases/pv-narasimha-rao) was for more than two decades the leading authority on the scope of parliamentary privilege in relation to votes, holding that the immunity under Article 105(2) extended to a member who received a bribe to vote in a particular way — a position the Supreme Court has now significantly modified by Sita Soren, which overruled the Narasimha Rao majority on this point.

- [Sita Soren v. Union of India](../cases/sita-soren) held, by a seven-judge Constitution Bench in 2024, that bribery antecedent to a legislative vote or speech is not protected by parliamentary privilege under Articles 105 and 194, overruling the earlier position and holding that the privilege is confined to acts within the House that are genuinely part of the legislative process, not to the inducements or corrupt bargains that precede them.

- [Keshav Singh Reference](../cases/keshav-singh-reference) is the foundational case on the limits of parliamentary privilege in relation to the High Courts, establishing that High Court judges exercising their constitutional jurisdiction to grant bail to a person committed for contempt by the House are not themselves in contempt of the House, and that privilege cannot be invoked to exclude the constitutional jurisdiction of courts.

- [Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu](../cases/kihoto-hollohan) remains relevant where parliamentary discipline overlaps with constitutional control, since it confirmed that Speaker decisions — including on disqualification — are subject to judicial review despite the general principle that courts do not enter the legislative chamber.

## Current significance

Parliamentary power is currently under pressure on two distinct fronts. On the privilege front, the Supreme Court's decision in Sita Soren has immediate implications for the prosecution of legislators accused of bribery in relation to votes and speeches, including cases that were previously shielded by the Narasimha Rao position; the live constitutional and policy questions that follow from that decision are tracked on the [legislative privilege and bribery prosecution](../current-affairs/legislative-privilege-and-bribery-prosecution) desk. On the structural front, the [One Nation One Election](../current-affairs/one-nation-one-election) proposal raises foundational questions about whether Parliament can, by constitutional amendment, synchronise Lok Sabha and state assembly elections, effectively curtailing state legislatures' natural terms and altering the conditions under which the Lok Sabha itself can be dissolved — questions that go to the heart of parliamentary sovereignty, federalism, and the basic structure doctrine simultaneously. The continuing tension between Parliament's amending power, its privilege claims, and the Court's insistence that constitutional structure still sets outer limits remains the defining dynamic of this area of public law.
