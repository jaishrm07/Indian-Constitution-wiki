## Why this topic matters

The Indian Constitution was drafted in the shadow of Partition, and its framers were acutely conscious that the new republic would be home to hundreds of millions of people whose languages, faiths, and cultural identities diverged sharply. Minority rights are therefore not a peripheral concession to difference but a structural commitment to pluralism built directly into the fundamental rights chapter. Without enforceable guarantees of cultural autonomy and educational independence, formal political equality would ring hollow for communities whose identities are expressed through institutions, languages, and practices that a majoritarian state could otherwise suppress or assimilate.

The constitutional scheme for minorities operates at three levels. The anti-discrimination provisions in Articles 15 and 16 protect individual members of minority communities from state action that disadvantages them on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. Articles 25 to 28 protect the freedom of religion for every person, including the right to profess, practise, and propagate religion and to manage religious affairs. Articles 29 and 30 then add a specifically minority-protective layer: the right to conserve a distinct language, script, or culture, and — most consequentially — the right of minorities to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice. Article 350A requires the state to provide instruction in the mother tongue at the primary stage, and Article 350B creates the office of Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities.

What makes this topic constitutionally interesting is the persistent tension between these autonomy rights and the state's power to regulate in the public interest. Every major case in this field has had to calibrate how much the state may intervene in minority institutions before intervention becomes destruction of the very identity the Constitution seeks to protect. That calibration exercise is ongoing, and the Supreme Court's 2024 ruling on Aligarh Muslim University's minority status has reopened fundamental questions about who qualifies as a minority institution and what the constitutional consequences of that status are.

## Constitutional provisions

**Articles 25–28** guarantee freedom of conscience and the right to profess, practise, and propagate religion; protect religious denominations' right to manage their own affairs and maintain institutions; and prohibit religious instruction in wholly state-funded educational institutions, while permitting it in institutions recognised by or aided by the state with consent-based opt-outs.

**Article 29** protects the right of any section of citizens having a distinct language, script, or culture to conserve the same, and separately prohibits denial of admission to any state-maintained or state-aided institution on grounds of religion, race, caste, or language.

**Article 30** grants religious and linguistic minorities the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice, and prohibits the state from discriminating against minority institutions in granting aid on the ground that the institution is under minority management.

**Article 350A** places a duty on every state and local authority to provide adequate facilities for instruction in the mother tongue at the primary stage of education to children belonging to linguistic minority groups.

**Article 350B** provides for a Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities appointed by the President to investigate all matters relating to the safeguards provided for linguistic minorities and to report to the President.

## How courts have interpreted it

The Supreme Court's early jurisprudence on minority rights focused on delineating the concept of a religious denomination — the entity that holds rights under Article 26 to manage its own affairs in matters of religion. In Shirur Mutt (1954) the Court held that a denomination must have a common faith, a common organisation, and a distinctive name, and that the state cannot use its regulatory power to effectively take over the internal management of a denominational institution under the guise of secular administration. This doctrine protected genuine autonomy but left open difficult questions about where secular management ends and religious practice begins.

The educational rights field was transformed by TMA Pai (2002), in which an eleven-judge Constitution Bench reconsidered the entire architecture of Article 30. The Court held that minorities are to be determined state-wise and not nationally, clarified that unaided minority institutions have a near-absolute right to administer their affairs including admissions and fee structure, and confirmed that aided institutions may be subjected to greater regulation but only up to the point at which they retain their minority character. The Court attempted to balance access and autonomy, but left a series of sub-questions — about the precise scope of state admission quotas in aided institutions, about the ability to compel reservation compliance — that subsequent benches have continued to refine.

The AMU minority status litigation culminating in the 2024 judgment represents a fresh and consequential chapter. A seven-judge Constitution Bench by a 4:3 majority overruled the 1967 Azeez Basha decision and held that an institution established by a minority does not lose its minority character simply because it was incorporated by a central statute. The majority reasoned that the test for minority status must look at who established the institution and for what purpose, not merely at the legal form through which it was created. The dissent warned that this reasoning could destabilise the principle that central universities belong to the nation as a whole and cannot be treated as denominational properties. The AMU case will now return to a regular bench for application of the new test, making it one of the most consequential pending matters in education law.

## Key cases (detailed)

**Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments, Madras v. Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar of Sri Shirur Mutt (1954)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held that a Hindu math — a religious institution with a specific religious head and doctrinal lineage — was a religious denomination entitled to manage its own affairs under Article 26(b). The Court struck down provisions of the Madras Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act that effectively transferred management of the math to a state-appointed commissioner. The judgment established the essentials of a religious denomination and articulated the distinction between matters of religion (exclusively within denominational autonomy) and matters of secular administration associated with a religious institution (open to state regulation). Shirur Mutt remains the foundational case for every challenge to state takeover of temples, mutts, and religious trusts.

**TMA Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — An eleven-judge Constitution Bench fundamentally reconsidered Article 30's scope in response to decades of litigation over state regulation of private educational institutions. The majority held that minority status is to be determined state-wise rather than nationally; that the right to administer includes the right to choose faculty and students subject only to minimal regulatory oversight; that unaided institutions have broad autonomy over admissions and fees but cannot run purely commercially; and that state-imposed quotas, including for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, cannot extend to minority unaided institutions without their consent. The judgment generated a series of follow-on references, including PA Inamdar (2005), and remains the governing constitutional framework for educational institution disputes.

**Aligarh Muslim University v. Naresh Agarwal (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench by a 4:3 majority overruled S. Azeez Basha v. Union of India (1967), which had held that AMU could not claim minority institution status because it was established by a central statute rather than by the Muslim minority community directly. The majority, led by Chief Justice Chandrachud, held that the correct test for minority status under Article 30 is substantive — whether the institution was founded by a minority community to serve minority educational interests — and not formal. The legislative incorporation of a minority-founded institution does not strip it of its constitutional identity. The three-judge dissent, authored by Justice Suri Aggarwal, held that a central university created by Parliament and funded by the Union cannot be treated as a minority institution without undermining the principle that such universities must serve all citizens equally. The case has been remitted for application of the new test on the specific facts of AMU's establishment.

**Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala (1986)** — Three children belonging to Jehovah's Witnesses refused to sing the national anthem at school on the ground that their faith prohibited them from participating in a ritual of that kind, though they stood respectfully during its singing. The Supreme Court held that their expulsion from school violated Articles 19(1)(a) and 25. The judgment is important for minority rights because it recognized that freedom of religion includes the right to act in accordance with sincere religious conviction even when that behaviour is publicly visible, and that the state cannot compel participation in a national ritual at the cost of excluding those whose conscience forbids it.

## Live issues and current significance

The AMU minority status case is the most immediately consequential pending matter. Once the case returns to a regular bench for application of the 4:3 majority's test, the Court will have to decide whether, on the facts of AMU's historical establishment, the university qualifies as a minority institution — with the practical consequence that it may then reserve up to fifty percent of its seats for Muslim students. The implications extend beyond AMU: the judgment's reasoning applies to Banaras Hindu University and other institutions established by communities for community purposes, raising questions that will take years of litigation to settle.

The intersection of minority rights with property law has become prominent through the Waqf (Amendment) Act 2025, which alters the governance and adjudication framework for waqf properties. Critics have argued that the amendments undermine the ability of Muslim religious institutions to manage their own affairs in matters of religion under Article 26 and to administer educational and charitable institutions under Article 30. Constitutional challenges to the amendments are pending before the Supreme Court. How the Court navigates the boundary between legitimate regulatory reform and constitutionally impermissible interference with minority institutional autonomy will be a defining test of contemporary minority rights doctrine.

Language-minority rights under Articles 29, 350A, and 350B remain structurally underenforced. The Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities continues to document states' non-compliance with mother-tongue instruction obligations, and the constitutional duty under Article 350A has never been the subject of decisive Supreme Court enforcement. As India's school system undergoes structural reform under the National Education Policy 2020 — which recommends but does not mandate mother-tongue instruction — the gap between constitutional obligation and administrative practice in this field deserves sustained attention.
