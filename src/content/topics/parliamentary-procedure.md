## Why this topic matters

Many constitutional crises arrive dressed as procedural disputes. A Speaker's refusal to schedule a trust vote, a delayed disqualification decision on anti-defection grounds, a rushed passage of legislation through a Money Bill route that bypasses the upper house — each of these is formally a matter of procedure, yet each can determine who holds power, which laws are valid, and whether democratic accountability can be enforced at all. Parliamentary procedure is therefore not a technical appendage to constitutional law but one of its most politically consequential domains.

The Constitution assigns extensive authority to the legislature to regulate its own affairs. Article 118 allows each House to make rules for the conduct of its business. Article 122 (for Parliament) and Article 212 (for state legislatures) bar courts from questioning proceedings on the ground of any alleged irregularity in procedure. Article 105 grants members of Parliament freedom of speech and immunity from proceedings in courts in respect of anything said or any vote given in Parliament. These provisions reflect the Westminster principle of parliamentary sovereignty over internal proceedings. But Indian constitutionalism departs from pure Westminster by subjecting legislative power to fundamental rights and basic structure review, which means the line between unreviewable internal procedure and judicially correctable constitutional violation is constantly contested.

The Tenth Schedule, added in 1985, turned anti-defection into a constitutional obligation enforced by the Speaker but reviewable by courts. This innovation created a structural conflict of interest: the Speaker, who is a member of the ruling party, adjudicates disqualification petitions against members who have broken from that party. The Supreme Court has progressively limited the Speaker's discretion — first by holding that disqualification orders are subject to judicial review, then by directing that pending petitions must be decided within reasonable time, and most recently by suggesting that the Election Commission rather than the Speaker should be the disqualification authority. These developments make parliamentary procedure one of the most active fronts in contemporary constitutional litigation.

## Constitutional provisions

**Articles 107–116** govern the legislative process in Parliament, covering the introduction and passage of bills, joint sittings to resolve deadlocks between the Houses, the special procedure for Money Bills under Article 110, and Presidential assent and reservation under Articles 111 and 117.

**Articles 196–212** replicate the legislative process framework for state legislatures, covering similar ground for bicameral and unicameral state assemblies, and establishing Article 212 as the state-level equivalent of Article 122 on the non-justiciability of procedural irregularity.

**Article 78** imposes duties on the Prime Minister to communicate to the President all decisions of the Council of Ministers, information respecting the administration of the Union, and proposals for legislation, providing the constitutional bridge between executive government and legislative initiative.

**Article 88** grants ministers and the Attorney General the right to speak and take part in proceedings of either House or any joint sitting, though without the right to vote, ensuring executive participation in legislative deliberation.

**Article 177** is the state-level equivalent of Article 88, giving ministers and the Advocate General the right to participate in state legislative proceedings.

**The Tenth Schedule** (added by the Fifty-Second Amendment, 1985) provides for the disqualification of members of Parliament and state legislatures on grounds of defection, defining voluntary giving up of party membership and voting or abstaining contrary to party direction as disqualifying conduct, subject to a merger exception.

**Articles 105 and 194** guarantee freedom of speech in Parliament and state legislatures respectively and provide immunity from court proceedings for anything said or any vote cast in the legislature, while also empowering each House to define and punish contempt.

## How courts have interpreted it

The Supreme Court has navigated parliamentary procedure through a doctrine of limited but real judicial review. The foundational principle, drawn from Article 122 and confirmed in cases from Keshav Singh (1965) onward, is that courts will not question the internal procedural regularity of legislative proceedings — how a bill was moved, how votes were conducted, whether debates followed standing orders. But this deference has a constitutional floor: it does not extend to legislative action that is constitutionally invalid, that violates fundamental rights, or that constitutes a colourable exercise of power. The distinction between unreviewable procedural irregularity and reviewable constitutional violation has required judicial calibration in each new factual context.

On anti-defection, the Court's evolution has been particularly marked. Early cases held that the Speaker's Tenth Schedule decisions were final and unreviewable. Kihoto Hollohan (1992) changed this by holding that since the Speaker acts as a tribunal deciding civil rights (membership of the house), the decision is subject to judicial review on grounds of constitutional validity, natural justice, and jurisdictional excess — though the scope of review is narrow and courts will not re-appreciate the merits. Subsequent cases progressively tightened this: Raja Ram Pal (2007) confirmed that even expulsion orders are judicially reviewable; Nabam Rebia (2016) held that a Speaker facing a notice of removal cannot continue to adjudicate disqualification petitions; and Sita Soren (2024) resolved a question left open since Narasimha Rao about whether the immunity in Articles 105 and 194 extends to bribery taken in connection with a legislative act.

The Money Bill route has become a separate site of procedural controversy. Article 110's definition of a Money Bill is exhaustive, but the Speaker's certification that a bill is a Money Bill is effectively unreviewable by the upper house. The Supreme Court in Rojer Mathew (2019) referred to a larger bench the question whether Finance Act amendments passed as Money Bills were constitutionally valid, and that reference remains pending. The constitutional stakes are high: if Money Bill misclassification can be judicially corrected, several major legislative changes — including Aadhaar and amendments to the Foreign Contribution Regulation Act — could be invalidated.

## Key cases (detailed)

**Raja Ram Pal v. Hon'ble Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the expulsion of MPs who had been filmed accepting cash in exchange for asking parliamentary questions, while simultaneously confirming that such disciplinary action is subject to constitutional judicial review. The Court held that Article 122, which bars courts from questioning proceedings on grounds of irregular procedure, does not shield Parliament's disciplinary jurisdiction from review for constitutional compliance. The Bench clarified that courts retain jurisdiction to examine whether Parliament's exercise of its power to expel a member satisfies the constitutional minimum of natural justice, is not infected by mala fides, and does not amount to a violation of fundamental rights. Raja Ram Pal is significant because it decisively rejected the argument that parliamentary privilege in India operates as a constitutional black hole equivalent to its Westminster counterpart.

**Sita Soren v. Union of India (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench unanimously overruled PV Narasimha Rao v. State (1998) on the scope of immunity under Articles 105(2) and 194(2). The Court held that the constitutional immunity for members of Parliament and state legislatures in respect of a vote given in the House does not extend to bribes taken in connection with that vote. The majority reasoned that extending immunity to bribe-taking would transform a provision designed to protect legislative freedom of speech and deliberation into a protection for corruption, which could not have been the framers' intention. The decision settles a question that had paralysed prosecutions for legislative corruption for over two decades and significantly strengthens the constitutional accountability of elected representatives.

**Nabam Rebia and Bamang Felix v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench held that a Speaker against whom a notice of motion for removal has been given under Article 179(c) loses the authority to proceed with disqualification petitions under the Tenth Schedule until the motion is disposed of. The Court reasoned that a Speaker who may imminently lose office has an inherent conflict of interest in deciding whether members who support the motion should be disqualified. The judgment arose from the Arunachal Pradesh political crisis and effectively curbed the practice of Speakers using disqualification powers as a political weapon against opponents seeking their removal. The decision is regularly applied in state political crises where floor-test disputes and anti-defection proceedings occur simultaneously.

**Powers, Privileges and Immunities of State Legislatures Reference (Keshav Singh Reference, 1965)** — A twenty-eight-judge bench — the largest in Indian constitutional history — resolved a constitutional confrontation between the Uttar Pradesh Legislative Assembly and the Allahabad High Court. Keshav Singh, a private individual, had been committed to prison for breach of privilege by the Assembly. When the High Court issued an interim order in habeas corpus proceedings, the Assembly passed a resolution requiring the judges concerned to appear before it for contempt. The President made a reference to the Supreme Court under Article 143. The Court held that legislative privilege in India, unlike the absolute privilege of the Westminster Parliament, cannot override the High Court's jurisdiction under Article 226 to examine the legality of the detention of a private individual. The judgment established that in India the Constitution is supreme and that privilege claims, however genuine, must yield to constitutional provisions protecting personal liberty and judicial review.

## Live issues and current significance

The pending reference in Rojer Mathew on Money Bill misclassification is the most structurally important unresolved question in parliamentary procedure. If the Constitution Bench holds that the Speaker's Article 110 certification is justiciable and that a misclassified bill is void, the constitutional implications for legislation passed since 2016 using the Money Bill route will be enormous. Conversely, if the Court holds the certification unreviewable, it will have effectively endorsed a mechanism that allows the government of the day to bypass the Rajya Sabha on any legislation it chooses to route through a Finance Act.

The Anti-Defection Law and the role of the Speaker continue to generate acute constitutional tension. In the Maharashtra and Manipur political crises of 2022-2023, the Supreme Court directed the Speaker to decide pending disqualification petitions within specified timeframes, reinforcing the principle that inaction is itself a reviewable constitutional failure. The Court also reiterated, in the Maharashtra case, that Speakers cannot indefinitely defer disqualification proceedings when a government's political survival depends on the outcome — a structural conflict of interest the Constitution did not fully anticipate when it assigned the Speaker adjudicatory power under the Tenth Schedule. Whether India should move disqualification decisions to an independent tribunal — as recommended by successive law commissions and the Dinesh Goswami Committee — remains a live legislative reform question.

The One Nation One Election proposal, which would synchronise Lok Sabha and all state legislative assembly elections to a single cycle, raises deep questions about parliamentary procedure and federal democratic accountability. Critics argue the proposal would require constitutional amendments affecting the fundamental right to periodic elections and potentially constrain the floor-test mechanisms that hold minority governments accountable between election cycles. The proposal's constitutional feasibility and compatibility with basic structure doctrine will require careful procedural analysis if it advances legislatively.
