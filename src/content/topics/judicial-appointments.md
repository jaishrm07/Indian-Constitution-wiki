## Why this topic matters

Appointment design is not just an administrative question. It shapes whether courts can remain institutionally independent while still being publicly accountable and constitutionally grounded. A judiciary that depends on the executive for appointments is structurally vulnerable to political pressure; a judiciary that appoints itself without any external accountability mechanism faces legitimacy questions of a different kind. India's constitutional history of judicial appointments is the story of repeated attempts to strike that balance — through constitutional text, judicial interpretation, legislative reform, and now transparency measures — and no settlement has yet commanded full institutional agreement. The quality of justice delivered by Indian courts, and the confidence that litigants and citizens place in those courts, depends substantially on whether the appointment process produces judges of the right quality, in sufficient numbers, and without political capture.

## How to read the topic

Start with Articles 124 and 217, then move to the Second Judges Case and the NJAC judgment. After that, use the current-affairs desk to follow how vacancies, transfers, and executive delay keep re-testing the structure those cases built. Article 124(2) provides that Supreme Court judges are appointed by the President after consultation with such judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts as the President may deem necessary; the word "consultation" was the pivot on which the entire collegium jurisprudence turned. Article 217(1) makes similar provision for High Court judges. Reading these provisions alongside the three judges cases — the First Judges Case (1981), the Second Judges Case (1993), and the Third Judges Case (1998 advisory opinion) — shows how the same constitutional text generated progressively stronger judicial control over the appointment process.

## How the collegium system works

The collegium for Supreme Court appointments consists of the Chief Justice of India and the four most senior puisne judges of the Supreme Court; their collective recommendation to the government carries constitutional weight that the executive cannot simply override. For High Court appointments, the process begins with the High Court collegium — the Chief Justice of the relevant High Court and the two most senior judges — whose recommendation is transmitted to the Governor, the state government, the Chief Justice of India, and finally to the Union government. The government may return a recommendation to the collegium with its objections, but if the collegium reiterates the recommendation after considering those objections, the government is constitutionally bound to act on it and make the appointment. The collegium's internal process — how it evaluates candidates, what criteria it applies, what role informal networks play — is not governed by statute or by any published written procedure, which is the principal reason why transparency is contested: there is no legal framework requiring the collegium to give reasons, consult stakeholders, or disclose deliberations. Since 2021, the Supreme Court collegium has begun publishing resolutions explaining its recommendations on its website, a significant if partial step toward transparency, but the criteria applied and the weight given to different considerations remain largely opaque to the public.

## Key cases

- [Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (Second Judges Case)](../cases/second-judges-case), decided in 1993, is the foundational authority for the collegium system. The nine-judge bench, by a 7:2 majority, overruled the First Judges Case (1981) and held that "consultation" in Articles 124(2) and 217(1) means "concurrence": the opinion of the Chief Justice of India, formed in consultation with the two most senior judges of the Supreme Court, is binding on the President. This transferred effective control over judicial appointments from the executive to the judiciary, establishing the collegium as the de facto appointing authority.

- The Third Judges Case (1998) arose as a Presidential Reference under Article 143. The Supreme Court, in its advisory opinion, refined the collegium's composition: it clarified that "consultation" with judges for Supreme Court appointments means consultation with the Chief Justice and the four (not two) most senior judges, and that a recommendation by the collegium must reflect the consensus of that body, not just the view of the Chief Justice. This advisory opinion fixed the collegium's structure as it currently operates.

- [Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (NJAC)](../cases/supreme-court-advocates-on-record-association-njac), decided in 2015 by a five-judge Constitution Bench, struck down the 99th Constitutional Amendment and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act. The NJAC would have replaced the collegium with a six-member commission comprising the Chief Justice, the two most senior judges, the Union Law Minister, and two eminent persons chosen by a committee including the Prime Minister, the Chief Justice, and the Leader of the Opposition. The Court, by a 4:1 majority, held that the inclusion of the Law Minister and the veto that any two members could exercise threatened judicial independence as an element of the basic structure; the majority found the entire scheme unconstitutional and restored the collegium system.

## Current significance

The [judicial appointments and collegium](../current-affairs/judicial-appointments-and-collegium) desk tracks this topic in real time across several distinct dimensions. On transparency, it follows the Supreme Court collegium's practice, begun in 2021 and expanded since, of publishing its resolutions — including the reasons for recommending or reiterating recommendations — online; this practice represents a significant institutional response to long-standing criticism of the collegium's opacity, but the scope and consistency of disclosure remain contested. On vacancy management, the desk tracks the current vacancy levels in the Supreme Court and across the twenty-five High Courts, where persistent vacancies mean that judges are carrying case loads far above sanctioned strength and pendency continues to grow; as of 2025, High Court vacancies have regularly exceeded 30% of sanctioned strength, a chronic condition that each round of collegium recommendations addresses only partially. On executive processing, the desk covers the gap between collegium recommendations and formal appointments — cases where the government holds a recommendation beyond the period within which constitutional conventions would require action, and the institutional consequences when that delay is indefinite. Reform proposals under discussion include a statutory framework for the collegium's procedures, enhanced disclosure requirements, a formal role for a broader consultation body short of an executive veto, and measures to accelerate the processing of recommendations — all of which raise questions about where the constitutional boundary between legitimate transparency reform and impermissible executive re-entry into appointments lies.
