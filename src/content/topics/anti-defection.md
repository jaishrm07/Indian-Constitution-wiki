## Why this topic matters

This topic repeatedly appears when governments are fragile, party lines split, or the timing of floor tests becomes politically decisive. The Tenth Schedule was inserted by the 52nd Constitutional Amendment in 1985 after decades of political instability caused by legislators changing sides, often for personal or financial advantage rather than genuine political conviction. It represents one of the Constitution's most direct attempts to discipline the behaviour of elected representatives by subordinating individual legislative choice to collective party discipline. The stakes are high in both directions: too little enforcement leaves legislatures vulnerable to manufactured majorities, while too much enforcement can suppress legitimate dissent and concentrate power in the hands of party leadership. Every significant state-level political crisis since 1985 has involved some application or avoidance of the Tenth Schedule, making this among the most practically consequential parts of the Constitution in day-to-day politics.

## How to read the topic

Readers should connect the Tenth Schedule, the logic of floor tests, and the case law on legislative disqualification and institutional authority. The Tenth Schedule does not stand alone — it must be read alongside the Governor's role in summoning and proroguing the Assembly, the Speaker's quasi-judicial function, and the Supreme Court's authority to review both. The constitutional text sets out who can be disqualified and for what, but the practical operation of the law has been shaped much more by judicial decisions than by the Schedule's sparse language. Kihoto Hollohan is the entry point for understanding why Speaker decisions are reviewable despite the general principle of non-interference in legislative proceedings. Later decisions build on Kihoto Hollohan to impose procedural and temporal requirements that the Schedule itself does not spell out.

## How the Tenth Schedule works

The Tenth Schedule creates disqualification for three types of conduct: voluntarily giving up party membership, voting or abstaining contrary to party direction, and (for nominated members) joining a political party after six months. The exception for mergers requires two-thirds of the legislative party to agree, which means a split short of that threshold is treated not as a legitimate political realignment but as defection subject to disqualification. The presiding officer — Speaker or Chairman — adjudicates disqualification petitions but is required by Kihoto Hollohan to decide within a reasonable time and cannot hold petitions in suspense indefinitely. Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil clarified that a member's resignation before the petition is decided renders the petition infructuous but also confirmed that the Speaker must act within a reasonable time once a petition is filed. Keisham Meghachandra Singh went further, holding that courts can impose a specific three-month deadline on a Speaker who has allowed a petition to languish, and that judicial intervention is appropriate when the presiding officer's inaction itself becomes a form of constitutional failure.

## Key cases

- [Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu](../cases/kihoto-hollohan) upheld the Tenth Schedule as constitutionally valid and held that Speaker decisions on disqualification are not immune from judicial review — they may be challenged on grounds of natural justice and mala fides, though courts will not intervene before the Speaker has decided except in extraordinary circumstances.

- [Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker](../cases/nabam-rebia) held that the Governor cannot manipulate the Assembly calendar — proroguing the House or accelerating its session — to influence the outcome of an anti-defection proceeding or a floor test, and that doing so amounts to interference with the constitutional authority of the House itself.

- [Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Hon'ble Speaker](../cases/shrimanth-balasaheb-patil) held that a member's resignation before the Speaker has decided a pending disqualification petition renders the petition infructuous, but the Court also made clear that the Speaker must decide within a reasonable time and cannot defer decision indefinitely as a political strategy.

- [Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Hon'ble Speaker](../cases/keisham-meghachandra-singh) is the most recent significant decision, holding that courts can impose a mandatory three-month timeline within which a Speaker must decide a disqualification petition, and that prolonged inaction by the presiding officer is itself a constitutional wrong that courts are empowered to remedy.

- [P.V. Narasimha Rao v. State (CBI/SPE)](../cases/pv-narasimha-rao) touched the intersection of legislative immunity and voting conduct, though this position on the privilege dimension of votes has since been substantially modified by Sita Soren.

## Current significance

Anti-defection law remains one of the most litigated areas of Indian constitutional law, with new petitions arising almost every time a government faces a confidence vote. The [anti-defection and floor tests](../current-affairs/anti-defection-and-floor-tests) desk tracks the current state of play across the country, including the recent Maharashtra political crisis, the Manipur Speaker delay issues, and the recurring tension between gubernatorial discretion and the Tenth Schedule's operation. Anti-defection also intersects with parliamentary privilege in important ways: a member's vote in the House is at the core of legislative privilege under Articles 105 and 194, yet the Tenth Schedule makes that same vote the basis for disqualification, and the bribery-for-vote question arising from Sita Soren raises further questions about whether the privilege that protects votes also shields the inducements that precede them — issues explored on the [legislative privilege and bribery prosecution](../current-affairs/legislative-privilege-and-bribery-prosecution) desk. The reform question — whether the Speaker is the right adjudicator for disqualification disputes — remains open, with proposals for an independent tribunal that have never been enacted.
