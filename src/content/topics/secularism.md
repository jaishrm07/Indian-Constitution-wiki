## Why this topic matters

Secularism in the Indian Constitution does not mean the absence of religion from public life — it means the state's equal treatment of all religions, its refusal to privilege any religion as official, and its insistence that citizenship is independent of faith. This understanding of secularism is both more modest and more demanding than the strict separation model associated with French laicite: more modest because it permits state recognition of and engagement with religious institutions, and more demanding because it requires positive neutrality — the state must treat all religious communities evenhandedly, not merely ignore them. This distinctive Indian constitutionalism of religion has generated a rich and contested body of doctrine.

The word "secular" was inserted into the Preamble by the Forty-Second Amendment in 1976. But the constitutional provisions giving secularism its operative content predate the Amendment: Articles 14 and 15 prohibit discrimination on grounds of religion; Articles 25 to 28 guarantee individual freedom of conscience and religious practice while limiting the state's capacity to impose or preference any religion; and Article 44 directs the state to work toward a uniform civil code. The relationship between these provisions — between individual religious freedom and state secular neutrality, between minority institutional autonomy and equal treatment, between reform of religious practices and non-interference with religion — has generated the most contentious constitutional litigation of the past three decades.

Secularism is also a basic structure principle. The Supreme Court in Bommai (1994) held that secularism is part of the unamendable constitutional identity of India, which has two consequences: Parliament cannot abrogate secularism by constitutional amendment, and a state government that systematically violates secularism — by aligning state power with the religion of the majority — loses its constitutional legitimacy and may be subject to President's Rule under Article 356. This basic structure status elevates secularism from a policy preference to a constitutional threshold condition, making it relevant not only to rights adjudication but to the structural integrity of federal governance.

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 15** prohibits the state from discriminating against any citizen on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. This provision grounds the constitutional equality dimension of secularism and prevents the state from singling out any religious community — whether majority or minority — for differential treatment in law.

**Articles 25–28** form the constitutional core of religious freedom and state neutrality. Article 25 guarantees freedom of conscience and the right to profess, practise, and propagate religion, subject to public order, morality, and health, and subject to laws relating to the regulation of secular activities associated with religious practice. Article 26 grants religious denominations the right to establish and maintain institutions and manage their own affairs in matters of religion. Article 27 prohibits compelling any person to pay taxes for the promotion or maintenance of any particular religion. Article 28 prohibits religious instruction in educational institutions wholly maintained out of state funds and provides opt-out rights in aided institutions.

**Article 44** contains the directive to the state to endeavour to secure a Uniform Civil Code for citizens throughout the territory of India. This provision sits in tension with the autonomy guarantees of Articles 25 and 26 and has been a constitutional flashpoint in discussions about whether the state can reform personal law without violating religious freedom. The Supreme Court has repeatedly noted Parliament's failure to act on Article 44 while declining to issue mandamus directing legislation.

**The Preamble's reference to secular** — inserted by the Forty-Second Amendment 1976 — declares India to be a Sovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic. The Supreme Court has held in Bommai and subsequent cases that this preambular declaration reflects the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be amended to remove the secular character of the state.

## How courts have interpreted it

The Supreme Court has approached secularism through two intersecting lines of doctrine. The first concerns individual religious freedom and its limits: when may the state regulate religious practices under the public order and morality exceptions of Article 25? The Court developed the "essential religious practices" test to distinguish protected religious practices from secular activities associated with religion that the state may regulate. Only those practices that are essential to the identity of the religion — not merely incidental or popular — attract the protection of Article 26(b) against state regulation. This test has been applied (and critiqued) in cases from Shirur Mutt to Sabarimala: it enables the state to reform exploitative or discriminatory practices while leaving the core of religious identity protected.

The second line of doctrine concerns state neutrality: what does equal treatment of all religions require? Bommai (1994) established that the state may not act as a partisan of any religion, and that systematic majoritarian religious identification by a state government is a ground for invoking President's Rule. The Court in Aruna Roy (2002) further held that secularism does not require ignorance of religion — teaching about all religions in schools is consistent with secularism and may even serve it — but that any state engagement with religion must be even-handed across communities. The boundaries of even-handedness have been contested in cases involving state takeover of Hindu temple trusts, differential regulation of personal law, and financial support for religious institutions.

Triple talaq presented a different dimension: the Court in Shayara Bano (2017) held that the practice of instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) was unconstitutional, but the five-judge bench fragmented 3:2 on the grounds. The plurality of three judges held that triple talaq was unconstitutional because it was manifestly arbitrary under Article 14; the two-judge minority would have gone further and examined it under the essential practices doctrine. The fragmentation meant that the constitutional reasoning was inconclusive, and Parliament subsequently abolished triple talaq by statute through the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019. Shayara Bano illustrates the difficulty of the Court's position at the intersection of religious freedom, gender equality, and secularism: any intervention in personal law can simultaneously be framed as protecting women's equality and as constituting state interference with religious practice.

## Key cases (detailed)

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench, in a case arising from the dismissal of BJP-led state governments following the demolition of the Babri Masjid, held that secularism is a basic structure principle of the Indian Constitution. The Court held that a state government whose actions are contrary to the constitutional mandate of secularism — one that systematically uses state power to advance the interests of one religious community over others — forfeits its constitutional legitimacy and may be validly dismissed under Article 356. The judgment was equally important for federalism (it significantly restricted the misuse of President's Rule) and for secularism (it established a constitutional sanction for secular violations at the state level). Bommai remains the foundational text for the argument that secularism is not merely a policy goal but an enforceable structural feature of the Indian republic.

**Aruna Roy v. Union of India (2002)** — The petitioners challenged the National Curriculum Framework for School Education, which proposed teaching of religious texts and values across religions in public schools, arguing that this violated the prohibition on religious instruction in state-funded institutions under Article 28. The Supreme Court rejected the challenge, holding that Article 28 prohibits denominational religious instruction — teaching pupils to adopt the faith of a particular religion — but does not prohibit teaching about religions, their histories, philosophies, and ethical frameworks. The Court held that education about all religions, conducted neutrally, is consistent with and may serve the constitutional value of secularism by promoting inter-religious understanding. Aruna Roy is the leading case on the constitutional distinction between religious instruction (prohibited in state schools) and religious education (constitutionally permissible).

**Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017) — Triple Talaq Case** — A five-judge Constitution Bench held, by a 3:2 majority, that the practice of instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) under Muslim personal law was unconstitutional and void. The plurality of three judges (Justices Nariman, Lalit, and Joseph) held that triple talaq was manifestly arbitrary under Article 14 because it allowed a husband to dissolve a marriage capriciously, without any opportunity for the wife to be heard, in a single impulsive utterance. Justice Kurian Joseph concurred in the result but held that triple talaq was bad in Islamic law itself, making constitutional invalidation unnecessary. The two-judge minority (Chief Justice Khehar and Justice Nazeer) held that triple talaq was protected as an essential religious practice of one section of the Muslim community and that the Court should not invalidate it but should direct Parliament to legislate. The judgment is constitutionally significant for its engagement with the scope of Article 14's arbitrariness doctrine, the essential practices test, and the intersection of gender equality with religious autonomy — even though the fractured reasoning left the doctrinal framework unsettled.

**Commissioner of Police v. Acharya Jagdishwarananda Avadhuta (2004) — The Ananda Margis Tandava Dance Case** — The Supreme Court considered whether the performance of Tandava dance with skull and trident — practiced by Ananda Margis — was an essential religious practice protected under Article 25. The Court held that a practice can only be considered essential to a religion if it has been a part of the religion since its inception and if without it the religion itself would be profoundly altered. Applying this test, the Court held that Tandava dance was not an essential practice of the Ananda Marga faith as constituted at its origin, and that police restrictions on public performances carrying dangerous implements were therefore valid regulatory action under the public order exception of Article 25(1). The case is a sharp illustration of how the essential practices test operates, of its controversial character — courts are in effect deciding questions of religious theology — and of the critique that it enables judicial interference in the internal definition of a religious tradition.

## Live issues and current significance

The Waqf (Amendment) Act 2025, simultaneously a property, minority rights, and secularism controversy, raises the question of whether the state can alter the governance and adjudication framework of an exclusively Muslim religious institution in ways that it has not applied to Hindu, Sikh, or Christian equivalents. Petitioners argue that the amendments violate Article 14's equality requirement as applied to religious institutions, Article 26's protection of denominational management, and Article 15's prohibition on religious discrimination. The government argues that the amendments address governance failures in waqf administration and serve legitimate public interest purposes. How the Supreme Court resolves the proportionality and equality dimensions of these challenges will significantly define the contemporary scope of constitutional secularism.

The Uniform Civil Code remains constitutionally unresolved. Several states — notably Uttarakhand in 2024 — have enacted or proposed state-level uniform civil codes governing personal law for their residents. Whether a state legislature has the competence to enact a uniform civil code (given that personal law is a concurrent list subject), whether such legislation is consistent with the religious freedom guarantees of Articles 25 and 26, and whether Article 44's directive creates a corresponding duty on courts to push toward uniformity — all remain open constitutional questions. Any serious state-level implementation will generate litigation that will reach the Supreme Court.

The essential religious practices doctrine continues to be the most internally contested area of secularism jurisprudence. The Sabarimala litigation — in which a five-judge bench 4:1 held that the exclusion of women of menstruating age from the Sabarimala temple was unconstitutional, but a later nine-judge review reference has not yet been decided — exposed deep divisions about whether courts should be in the business of adjudicating which religious practices are "essential." The review reference, which considers whether the essential practices test is the appropriate framework for resolving conflicts between individual religious autonomy and intra-community equality, remains pending and will likely reshape the entire secularism field when decided.
