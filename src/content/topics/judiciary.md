## Why this topic matters

The Constitution relies on courts not only to resolve disputes but also to protect constitutional structure. Rights enforcement, writ jurisdiction, and judicial review all sit inside this institutional role. Without a judiciary empowered to check executive and legislative overreach, the Constitution's textual guarantees would be unenforceable — something the Emergency period (1975–77) demonstrated when the Supreme Court upheld the suspension of fundamental rights in ADM Jabalpur and was widely seen as abdicating that protective role. The judiciary is not a passive interpreter but an active constitutional institution: it tells Parliament what it cannot do, tells the executive how it must act, and in doing so maintains the Constitution as supreme law rather than a political programme. Understanding the judiciary as a topic therefore means understanding both its powers and its institutional obligations.

## How to read the topic

This topic links Article 32, due process under Article 21, and the cases that define constitutional interpretation at moments of institutional strain. Readers should begin with the constitutional provisions that create and empower the courts — Articles 124 to 147 for the Supreme Court, Articles 214 to 231 for High Courts — and then read the cases that have tested and confirmed the judiciary's position within the constitutional order. The distinction between original, appellate, and advisory jurisdiction at the Supreme Court matters for understanding why certain questions come before it directly and others on appeal. The writ jurisdiction provisions — Articles 32 and 226 — are the mechanism by which rights become practically enforceable, and the cases on judicial review explain why the courts cannot be stripped of that power.

## Constitutional structure of the judiciary

The Supreme Court, established under Articles 124 to 147, is the apex court with original jurisdiction over disputes between the Union and states or between states, appellate jurisdiction over constitutional and other matters, and advisory jurisdiction when the President seeks its opinion on questions of law or fact. High Courts, established under Articles 214 to 231, exercise superintendence over all courts and tribunals within their respective territories, and their supervisory jurisdiction under Article 227 makes them the institutional backbone of the judicial system below the Supreme Court. Article 226 empowers High Courts and Article 32 empowers the Supreme Court to issue writs — including habeas corpus, mandamus, certiorari, prohibition, and quo warranto — for the enforcement of fundamental rights and for any other purpose, making writ jurisdiction the practical bridge between constitutional text and individual rights. Judicial review — the power of courts to strike down legislation and executive action that conflicts with the Constitution — is not expressly stated in a single provision but was confirmed as a necessary implication of constitutional supremacy in Kesavananda Bharati and identified as an element of the basic structure of the Constitution. The independence of the judiciary from executive and legislative control is secured through provisions on the security of tenure of judges, the non-reducibility of judicial salaries, and the collegium system for appointments — all of which are designed to ensure that courts can decide cases without fear of political consequence.

## Key cases

- [Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala](../cases/kesavananda-bharati), decided in 1973 by a thirteen-judge bench, is the foundational authority for judicial review as an element of the basic structure of the Constitution. The Court held that while Parliament has wide amending power under Article 368, it cannot use that power to destroy the Constitution's essential features, of which judicial review and the rule of law are central. The judgment is the constitutional charter of the modern Supreme Court's institutional role.

- [Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India](../cases/minerva-mills), decided in 1980, directly applied Kesavananda to strike down clauses of the 42nd Amendment that had sought to exclude judicial review of constitutional amendments and to give Directive Principles absolute primacy over fundamental rights. The Court held that a Parliament that can limit the court's power to review its own amendments has effectively made itself the final arbiter of the Constitution — a position inconsistent with constitutional supremacy.

- [Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (Second Judges Case)](../cases/second-judges-case), decided in 1993, established the collegium system by holding that the opinion of the Chief Justice of India, formed in consultation with the two most senior judges, is binding on the executive in matters of Supreme Court appointments and transfers. This fundamentally altered the balance of power over judicial appointments in favour of the judiciary itself, and laid the institutional groundwork for the collegium as it now functions.

- [Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (NJAC)](../cases/supreme-court-advocates-on-record-association-njac), decided in 2015, struck down the Constitution (Ninety-ninth Amendment) Act and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act. The Court held that the NJAC — which would have given the executive a role in judicial appointments through a six-member commission — threatened judicial independence and therefore violated the basic structure of the Constitution. The judgment reaffirmed that the collegium system, whatever its shortcomings, was constitutionally preferable to any arrangement in which the executive could block or veto judicial appointments.

- [L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India](../cases/l-chandra-kumar), decided by a seven-judge Constitution Bench in 1997, held that the power of judicial review exercised by High Courts under Article 226 and by the Supreme Court under Article 32 is an element of the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be excluded by legislation. The case arose from statutes that had created administrative tribunals and barred High Court jurisdiction over their decisions; the Court read down those exclusion clauses as unconstitutional, holding that every tribunal established by Parliament must remain subject to High Court supervisory writ jurisdiction, with appeal thereafter to the Supreme Court. L. Chandra Kumar is the constitutional ceiling on Parliament's power to reorganise adjudicatory institutions in ways that displace regular courts from their supervisory role.

- The cases concerning governors and Speakers withholding constitutional action — including the Nabam Rebia line of cases on the Speaker's authority during a floor test, and Tamil Nadu's governor-versus-state government disputes — show courts enforcing constitutional obligations on constitutional functionaries. These cases confirm that the judiciary's supervisory role extends to constitutional office-holders who delay, obstruct, or refuse to perform functions that the Constitution mandates.

## Current significance

The judiciary topic is inseparable from the ongoing debate about how judges are appointed, how vacancies are managed, and whether the collegium system can deliver both independence and accountability. The [judicial appointments and collegium](../current-affairs/judicial-appointments-and-collegium) desk tracks collegium resolutions, the pace of executive processing of recommendations, the scale of vacancies in the Supreme Court and High Courts, and the transparency measures the Supreme Court has begun taking by publishing collegium decisions. These are not merely administrative questions: vacancy levels directly affect case pendency and the practical availability of justice, while the pace of appointment and the degree of transparency in the collegium's reasoning bear directly on whether judicial independence is real or formal.
