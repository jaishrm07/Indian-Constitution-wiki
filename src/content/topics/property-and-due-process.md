## Why this topic matters

Property's demotion from a fundamental right (Articles 19(1)(f) and 31) to a constitutional right (Article 300A) by the Forty-Fourth Amendment in 1978 is one of the most consequential structural changes in the history of India's Constitution. But constitutional protection of property did not disappear — it was reconfigured. Article 300A provides that no person shall be deprived of property save by authority of law, and the Supreme Court has progressively elaborated what "authority of law" requires: not merely statutory authorisation but legality, procedural fairness, and in some contexts, compensation. The field sits at the intersection of individual rights, distributive justice, and state power over land and resources.

The history of property rights in the Constitution is inseparable from the history of land reform. The first generation of constitutional amendments — the First, Fourth, and Seventeenth Amendments — were enacted specifically to shield land acquisition and land-ceiling laws from judicial challenge. Articles 31A, 31B, and 31C were added to protect laws relating to land reform, industries in the public interest, and directive principles from fundamental rights challenges. The Ninth Schedule was created as a repository for laws placed beyond judicial review. This constitutional architecture was itself challenged in Kesavananda Bharati (1973) and Waman Rao (1981), which held that even Ninth Schedule laws are not immune from basic structure review and that constitutional amendments cannot abrogate fundamental rights to the point of destroying the basic structure. The resulting framework — in which property is protected but not absolutely, and in which Parliament's redistributive power is acknowledged but not unlimited — continues to generate litigation.

The practical contemporary relevance of property and due process runs across several domains: land acquisition for infrastructure and urban development (where compensation adequacy and procedural compliance under the Land Acquisition Act 2013 are regularly litigated); dispossession of urban informal settlements (where Article 21 due process requirements constrain demolition without notice and hearing); digital and intangible assets (where the state's regulatory power over economically significant interests is growing faster than the constitutional framework has developed); and the Waqf property controversy (where the intersection of property, minority rights, and institutional governance raises all of these questions simultaneously).

## Constitutional provisions

**Article 300A** (inserted by the Forty-Fourth Amendment in 1978) provides that no person shall be deprived of their property save by authority of law. The provision does not require compensation, but the Supreme Court has held that deprivation without compensation in cases of compulsory acquisition may nonetheless violate Article 300A if the compensation is so inadequate as to be illusory or if the deprivation is in substance a taking without legal authority.

**Article 19(1)(f) — deleted** originally guaranteed the right to acquire, hold, and dispose of property as a fundamental right, subject to reasonable restrictions. Its deletion by the Forty-Fourth Amendment removed the direct fundamental right to property but did not eliminate constitutional protection, which was relocated to Article 300A and continues through the due process requirements of Article 21.

**Article 31 — deleted** originally provided for compulsory acquisition of property with payment of compensation. It was deleted by the Forty-Fourth Amendment. Its legacy survives through Articles 31A, 31B, and 31C, which protect specific categories of property-related legislation from challenge.

**Article 31A** protects laws providing for the acquisition by the state of estates and extinguishment or modification of rights in them, and related agrarian reform measures, from challenge on grounds of Articles 14 and 19 — but not (after Kesavananda) from basic structure review.

**Article 31B** declares that the laws specified in the Ninth Schedule shall not be deemed void on the ground of inconsistency with fundamental rights. After IR Coelho (2007) this protection is conditioned on the laws not abridging the basic structure.

**Article 31C** protects laws implementing the directive principles in Articles 39(b) and 39(c) — securing equitable distribution of the material resources of the community and preventing concentration of wealth — from challenge on grounds of Articles 14 and 19. The Supreme Court in a 2024 Constitution Bench judgment on Article 39(b) considered whether "material resources of the community" could be interpreted to include privately held resources for redistribution purposes.

## How courts have interpreted it

The Supreme Court's early property jurisprudence was dominated by the conflict between fundamental rights and Parliament's redistributive ambitions. The Court in State of West Bengal v. Bela Banerjee (1954) and then in RC Cooper (1970) insisted that compensation for compulsory acquisition must be genuine and not illusory. Parliament responded with constitutional amendments. The Court responded by developing the basic structure doctrine in Kesavananda Bharati (1973), which held that even a constitutional amendment cannot destroy the essential features of the Constitution — a doctrine that was then deployed, in Waman Rao (1981) and subsequent cases, to subject Ninth Schedule laws to review. This cycle of legislative action and judicial response established the dynamic that continues to characterise Indian property law.

After the Forty-Fourth Amendment relocated property from fundamental rights to Article 300A, the Court developed the concept of due process of law as the principal vehicle for protecting individuals against arbitrary state action. Maneka Gandhi's principle that any procedure depriving a person of a constitutional right must be fair, just, and reasonable was applied to property deprivations through a line of cases holding that demolition of homes, encroachment removal, and summary dispossession must be preceded by notice, an opportunity to be heard, and reasoned orders. The Olga Tellis line of cases added the dimension of livelihood: the constitutional right to life includes the right to the means of livelihood, and the state cannot destroy those means without meeting the procedural requirements of Article 21.

The most recent wave of property jurisprudence has addressed the intersection of community rights, material resources, and redistribution. The Constitution Bench in Property Owners Association v. State of Maharashtra (2024) revisited whether Article 39(b)'s reference to "material resources of the community" encompasses privately held property for redistribution purposes, and whether the state could acquire private property under that directive without full compensation. Justice Chandrachud's plurality opinion held that not all privately held resources automatically fall within Article 39(b)'s ambit, requiring a case-by-case assessment of the nature, context, and purpose of the resource — a significant departure from the sweeping redistributive reading associated with Justice Krishna Iyer's earlier opinions.

## Key cases (detailed)

**R.C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970) — The Bank Nationalisation Case** — An eleven-judge Constitution Bench struck down the Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Ordinance 1969 by a 10:1 majority, invalidating the nationalisation of fourteen major private banks. The government had argued that the compensation formula was a matter entirely within Parliament's discretion and that the courts could not substitute their judgment on the adequacy of compensation for that of the legislature. The Court rejected this formalism. It held that the right to property under Article 31 required genuine compensation — not a formula designed to make meaningful challenge impossible — and that constitutional review must examine the actual effect of state action on rights rather than its stated purpose. The judgment introduced the principle of substance over form into Indian constitutional law: the Court would look past the legislative label to assess whether a law's real operation violated constitutional rights. RC Cooper also held that the right to carry on business under Article 19(1)(g) was separately infringed by the nationalisation, demonstrating that property-related state action implicates multiple constitutional rights simultaneously.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The Kesavananda case, while principally known for establishing the basic structure doctrine, was triggered by a property dispute: a religious head's challenge to Kerala's land reform legislation that threatened properties held in trust by his mutt. The thirteen-judge Constitution Bench's fractured 7:6 majority on the basic structure doctrine had immediate property law consequences. It held that Articles 31A, 31B, and 31C — which had been added specifically to shield property-related legislation from rights review — were not themselves beyond constitutional scrutiny and could not be used to destroy the basic structure. The decision limited Parliament's capacity to use constitutional amendments as a mechanism for evading judicial review of acquisitive legislation, and it preserved the constitutional minimum of equality and fairness even within the redistributive framework.

**Indira Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) — The Mandal Case** — Although principally an equality and reservation case, Indira Sawhney's treatment of property-adjacent questions — particularly the exclusion of the "creamy layer" from OBC reservations and the constitutional limits on the use of poverty-based criteria in state classification — has shaped due process analysis in social welfare contexts. The Court's methodology of examining whether state classifications are genuine and non-arbitrary, rather than merely formal, extended the substantive review principles of RC Cooper into the domain of social rights. The case is also relevant to property because it confirmed that the state cannot impose burdens on property rights — even indirectly through reservation schemes affecting private institutions — without demonstrating a proportionate public interest.

**Bangalore Medical Trust v. B.S. Muddappa (1991)** — A public park in Bangalore was de-notified by the state government and the land was allotted to a private medical trust for construction of a nursing home. Residents challenged the conversion under the municipal planning statute and under Article 21. The Supreme Court held that public parks are not private property available for allotment at the state's discretion, that citizens have a constitutional interest in public spaces established by statute, and that the conversion of a dedicated public amenity into a private facility — without following the statutory procedure for de-notification and without public participation — was illegal. The judgment is important for property and due process because it extended the concept of public trust to urban land use, held that arbitrary deprivation of community access to public land engages constitutional guarantees, and established that procedural fairness requirements apply to the state's management of public property as well as to the acquisition of private property.

## Live issues and current significance

The Waqf (Amendment) Act 2025 has placed property and minority rights at the centre of a major constitutional dispute. The Act alters the procedure for determining whether a property is waqf — including provisions allowing non-Muslim members on Waqf Boards and permitting the Collector to survey waqf properties — and changes the forum for adjudication of waqf property disputes. Petitioners have argued that these changes enable the state to dispossess Muslim religious and charitable institutions of their properties without the procedural protections that Article 300A and Article 21 require, and that they constitute discrimination on the basis of religion contrary to Articles 14 and 15. The Supreme Court has admitted the petitions and the constitutional questions are pending.

Land acquisition for infrastructure — highways, railways, data centre parks, semiconductor facilities, and renewable energy installations — continues to generate a high volume of constitutional litigation. Challenges arise on two fronts: compensation adequacy (whether market-value determinations under the 2013 Act are genuine) and procedural compliance (whether social impact assessments, hearings, and consent requirements are substantively followed or reduced to formality). The Supreme Court has repeatedly held that the right to be heard before dispossession is not waivable by legislative design and must be a real opportunity rather than a notional one, but enforcement of this principle at the district and High Court level remains uneven.

The question of whether privately held digital assets — cryptocurrency holdings, domain names, intellectual property rights — are "property" for the purposes of Article 300A, and what procedural standards apply when the state regulates or forfeits them, is entirely undeveloped in Indian constitutional jurisprudence. As the regulatory framework for virtual digital assets evolves and as enforcement actions involving asset freezing and forfeiture become more common, the absence of a constitutional framework for the property rights of digital asset holders will increasingly require judicial attention.
