## Why this topic matters

Federalism is not just a question of legislative lists. It also appears in governor-state conflict, finance, emergency powers, ratification requirements, and the political incentives that shape central-state relations. India's federal arrangement is constitutionally Union-dominant yet politically negotiated: the Constitution gives the Centre decisive tools — emergency proclamations under Articles 352 to 356, residuary power under Article 248, overriding authority on Concurrent List subjects — but decades of competitive politics and Supreme Court supervision have rebalanced the practice. Understanding federalism therefore requires reading text and constitutional convention together, watching how high-level principles operate under real political pressure.

## How to read the topic

Readers should move between Articles dealing with emergency intervention, amendment procedure, and institutional accountability. Case law becomes especially important because it disciplines the use of broad Union powers, converting textually wide grants into justiciable standards. The treatment of the Governor as a constitutional office, the finance commission relationship, and the conditions for President's Rule are all sites where the written Constitution and the political Constitution meet. Readers should also consult Schedule VII alongside Part XI to see how the division of legislative competence interacts with the conflict-resolution rules in Articles 246 and 251 to 254.

## The federal tension in practice

Indian federalism is formally cooperative but structurally Union-dominant. The Constitution does not use the word "federal" anywhere; it creates a Union of states where the Centre holds residuary power, can reorganise states unilaterally under Article 3, and can assume legislative and executive functions of a state during emergency. Schedule VII distributes subjects across three lists: the Union List (List I, currently 100 entries) covers defence, foreign affairs, banking, and communications; the State List (List II, currently 61 entries) covers law and order, agriculture, public health, and land; and the Concurrent List (List III, currently 52 entries) covers criminal law, education, forests, and several economic subjects where both Parliament and state legislatures may legislate. Article 246 assigns exclusive legislative competence for each list, but where Parliament and a state legislature make inconsistent laws on a Concurrent List subject, Article 254 gives the Central law precedence unless the state law has received Presidential assent. Articles 248 to 250 extend Parliament's reach further: Parliament alone legislates on residuary matters, may legislate on state subjects if the Rajya Sabha passes a resolution under Article 249, and has full legislative authority over states during a national emergency. The practical result is a system where states have constitutionally guaranteed domains but the Union holds decisive trump cards that courts are asked to police.

## Key cases

- [S.R. Bommai v. Union of India](../cases/sr-bommai) is the landmark ruling that made President's Rule under Article 356 judicially reviewable, required a floor test before recommending dismissal, and firmly established that the power to dismiss a state government is not absolute political discretion.
- [Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker](../cases/nabam-rebia) held that a Governor cannot summon or advance a session of the Assembly for the purpose of defeating a no-confidence motion against a Speaker who has issued disqualification notices, confining the Governor to a largely ceremonial role in the internal life of the legislature.
- [Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India](../cases/nct-delhi) applied federalism principles to a Union Territory, holding that elected governments of Union Territories exercise real executive power and that LG decisions must generally reflect the aid and advice of the elected Council of Ministers, thereby extending the logic of responsible government even to asymmetric federal units.
- [Tamil Nadu Government v. Governor of Tamil Nadu](../cases/tamil-nadu-governor-assent) raised the constitutional question of whether a Governor may withhold assent from Bills indefinitely, with the Supreme Court holding that the Governor must act within reasonable time and cannot use the assent power as a tool to frustrate the elected government's legislative programme.

## Current significance

Governor-state conflict has become one of the most active federalism questions in contemporary India. Opposition-ruled states have repeatedly faced Governors who delay assent to Bills, refuse to summon sessions, or withhold constitutional cooperation in ways that generate institutional deadlock. The live desk on [governor-state standoffs](../current-affairs/governor-state-standoffs) documents these disputes and their constitutional stakes, while the [governor assent and bill silence](../current-affairs/governor-assent-and-bill-silence) desk tracks the specific constitutional question of what happens when a Governor neither signs nor returns a Bill — an issue the Supreme Court addressed directly in the Tamil Nadu Governor assent litigation. Together these desks illustrate how federalism's textual design is stress-tested whenever the elected government at the Centre and an elected government in a state are from different political formations.
