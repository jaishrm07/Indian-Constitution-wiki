## Why this topic matters

Fundamental Rights are the most visible way many people encounter the Constitution. They shape debates on speech, protest, privacy, criminal procedure, welfare exclusion, censorship, and state coercion. Part III is unique among the Constitution's structures because it places binding obligations on government — not merely aspirational goals — and backs them with a specific constitutional remedy. Every time a citizen is arbitrarily detained, denied equal treatment, gagged, or subjected to unreasonable surveillance, Part III is the constitutional vocabulary through which that grievance can be formulated and litigated.

## How to read the topic

The rights chapter is best read relationally. Article 14 deals with arbitrariness and equality, Article 19 deals with civic freedoms, Article 21 governs liberty and dignity, and Article 32 converts these rights into enforceable remedies. The Supreme Court has insisted since Maneka Gandhi that these articles are not water-tight silos: a law that restricts one right will often be tested under multiple articles simultaneously, and the content of each right is partly defined by its relationships with the others. Readers should also pay close attention to Article 13, which makes the rights chapter self-executing by voiding any law — pre-constitutional or post-constitutional — that is inconsistent with Part III, and to the definition of "State" in Article 12, which governs which actors are bound by the rights guarantee.

## How fundamental rights work

The rights in Part III are enforceable against the State, which Article 12 defines broadly to include the Government and Parliament of India, the Governments and legislatures of every state, and all local or other authorities within India or under the control of the Government of India. This definition has been read expansively by courts to include statutory bodies, heavily funded or controlled private entities, and in some contexts public sector undertakings — ensuring that the rights framework reaches the full range of government-linked power rather than only the most narrowly defined executive action. Some fundamental rights also apply, directly or indirectly, against private parties: Article 17 abolishes untouchability and is enforceable against any person, Article 23 prohibits forced labour and trafficking regardless of who the perpetrator is, and the courts have recognised that certain horizontally-applicable obligations flow from constitutional values even without explicit textual direction. Article 13 is the enforcement hinge of Part III: it declares void any law made before or after the Constitution's commencement to the extent it is inconsistent with Part III rights, which means inconsistent legislation is not merely voidable but automatically of no effect. Crucially, Article 32 makes the right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of Part III rights itself a fundamental right that cannot be suspended except during an emergency, treating judicial remedy not as a procedural option but as a substantive constitutional guarantee — the right that protects all other rights.

## Key cases

- [Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India](../cases/maneka-gandhi) held that Articles 14, 19, and 21 must be read together rather than in isolation, so a law that deprives a person of liberty must satisfy the requirements of all three articles — it must be fair, just, and reasonable, not merely procedurally authorised.
- [Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala](../cases/kesavananda-bharati) established that the fundamental rights guaranteed in Part III form part of the basic structure of the Constitution, meaning Parliament can regulate or limit them by amendment but cannot abrogate or destroy them wholesale.
- [Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India](../cases/puttaswamy) unanimously recognised the right to privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21, overruling earlier decisions and establishing that informational autonomy, bodily integrity, and freedom from surveillance are constitutionally protected dimensions of individual liberty.
- [Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India](../cases/navtej-singh-johar) read dignity as a constitutional value that animates the whole of Part III, holding that Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code violated Articles 14, 19, and 21 by criminalising intimate conduct between consenting adults and by denying a segment of the population equal moral worth.
- [E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu](../cases/e-p-royappa) introduced the anti-arbitrariness doctrine as a component of Article 14, holding that equality is antithetical to arbitrariness and that wherever the state acts arbitrarily it violates the Constitution regardless of whether any formal classification or differential treatment is involved. The ruling extended Article 14 from its earlier role of policing legislative classifications into a general substantive discipline on all state action, including individualised executive decisions such as service transfers and administrative orders.
- [Romesh Thappar v. State of Madras](../cases/romesh-thappar) struck down an overbroad pre-censorship law in 1950, one of the Supreme Court's earliest fundamental rights rulings, and established that the grounds on which the state may restrict speech are exhaustively defined in Article 19(2) — the legislature cannot add new grounds by statute. The Court held that a law capable of reaching speech beyond the constitutionally authorised categories is facially void and cannot be saved by pointing to a permissible application in the particular case.
- [L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India](../cases/l-chandra-kumar) held that the power of judicial review vested in High Courts under Article 226 and in the Supreme Court under Article 32 is part of the basic structure of the Constitution and therefore cannot be excluded by legislation. The ruling placed a constitutional ceiling on Parliament's power to create statutory tribunals that displace superior court jurisdiction, ensuring that access to judicial review of state action remains a constitutional guarantee rather than a privilege Parliament can withdraw.

## Current significance

The most contested frontier of fundamental rights today lies at the intersection of digital technology and state power. Surveillance systems, mandatory biometric databases, platform-level content regulation, and data collection by the state and private actors all raise Article 21 privacy questions that the Puttaswamy judgment has made cognisable but not yet fully resolved. The live desk on [data protection and privacy](../current-affairs/data-protection-and-privacy) tracks the legislative and judicial developments around India's data protection framework and its constitutional foundations. The desk on [online speech and platform regulation](../current-affairs/online-speech-and-platform-regulation) covers the Article 19 dimensions of how the state regulates digital intermediaries and what constitutional constraints bind both the government and the platforms it seeks to control.
