## What this Part contains

Part IV runs from Articles 36 to 51 and sets out the Directive Principles of State Policy, which are constitutional injunctions to the state to strive towards social and economic justice, the welfare of the people, and a just social order. Unlike the fundamental rights in Part III, the Directives are expressly declared by Article 37 to be non-justiciable — they cannot be directly enforced in a court of law — but the same article imposes a constitutional duty on the state to apply them in making laws. The Part covers a wide range of policy areas: equal pay for equal work, free and compulsory education, living wages, the organisation of village panchayats, and international peace obligations, among others. The relationship between Part III and Part IV has been one of the most contested questions in Indian constitutional history, resolved by the basic structure doctrine which treats both Parts as complementary rather than hierarchically opposed.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 37** — States that the Directive Principles are fundamental in the governance of the country and that it shall be the duty of the state to apply these principles in making laws, while also clarifying that they are not enforceable by any court.
- **Article 38** — Directs the state to secure a social order in which social, economic, and political justice shall inform all institutions of national life, and to minimise inequalities in income and status.
- **Article 39** — Contains specific economic directives including the right of all citizens to an adequate livelihood, equal pay for equal work, and the obligation to prevent the concentration of wealth and means of production.
- **Article 39A** — Directs the state to ensure that the operation of the legal system promotes justice on a basis of equal opportunity, and in particular to provide free legal aid to ensure that opportunities for securing justice are not denied to any citizen by reason of economic or other disabilities.
- **Article 44** — Directs the state to endeavour to secure for citizens a uniform civil code throughout the territory of India, a provision that has remained politically and legally contested since 1950.

## Key cases

**State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan** — The Supreme Court held that a state reservation scheme for professional admissions could not be sustained under the Directive Principles when it violated the fundamental right under Article 29(2), prompting the First Constitutional Amendment which inserted Article 15(4) to enable such reservations, illustrating how the tension between Part III and Part IV has repeatedly driven constitutional amendment.

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India** — The Court struck down provisions of the Forty-Second Amendment that had purported to give the Directive Principles primacy over fundamental rights, holding that the balance between Part III and Part IV is part of the Constitution’s basic structure and cannot be destroyed by amendment.

**Unni Krishnan v. State of Andhra Pradesh** — Before the Eighty-Sixth Amendment, the Court read the right to free and compulsory education for children up to the age of fourteen into Article 21 through Article 45 of Part IV, demonstrating the technique of importing Directive Principles into justiciable rights through purposive constitutional interpretation.

## Current significance

Debates over a uniform civil code, triggered periodically by Law Commission consultations and political pronouncements, keep Article 44 at the centre of contested constitutional politics about the relationship between religious personal laws and state-imposed uniformity. Article 39A’s directive on legal aid feeds directly into ongoing litigation and policy work around access to justice, including the constitutional validity of fee restrictions in legal proceedings. The National Food Security Act and related welfare legislation are regularly assessed for their conformity with Articles 39 and 47, with courts asked whether the state’s failure to implement Directive Principles can be remedied through public interest litigation.
