## Why this Part matters

Fiscal power is constitutional power. Part XII explains how revenue, apportionment, and financial authority are organised across the Union and the states.

## How to read it

Readers should connect this Part with Article 269A, Article 279A, the GST framework, and wider debates about fiscal federalism and coordinated national markets.

## What this Part contains

Part XII covers Articles 264 to 300A and is the constitutional home of fiscal federalism, government finance, and property rights. It establishes the Consolidated Funds and Contingency Funds for both the Union and the states, regulates the sharing of taxes between the Centre and states through Finance Commissions constituted under Article 280, and addresses subjects ranging from the distribution of GST revenues (Article 269A) to the immunity of Union property from state taxation (Article 285). The Part also contains Article 300A, which protects the right to property against deprivation except by authority of law — a provision inserted after the right to property was removed from Part III by the Forty-Fourth Amendment and which remains a significant constitutional guarantee in matters of acquisition and requisition.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 265** — Prohibits the levy or collection of any tax except by authority of law, making every tax legally contestable if not grounded in valid legislation, a foundational provision for tax litigation.
- **Article 280** — Establishes the Finance Commission as a constitutional body to recommend the distribution of net tax proceeds between the Union and the states and the principles governing grants-in-aid, giving vertical and horizontal fiscal equity a constitutional anchor.
- **Article 286** — Imposes restrictions on the power of states to impose taxes on the sale or purchase of goods, including a prohibition on taxing transactions outside the state or in the course of import or export, which shaped Indian indirect tax law for decades before GST.
- **Article 300A** — Provides that no person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law, functioning as a post-Forty-Fourth Amendment constitutional protection that courts have used to impose a requirement of fair compensation and lawful procedure.
- **Article 293** — Governs borrowing by states, requiring prior consent of the Union where a state has outstanding loans from the Centre, a provision that anchors fiscal discipline obligations on state governments.

## Key cases

**Synthetics and Chemicals Ltd. v. State of Uttar Pradesh** — The Supreme Court examined the scope of state taxing power under the constitutional distribution of fiscal competence, illustrating how the inter-play between state and union entries in the Seventh Schedule determines the validity of state tax laws.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India** — The Court addressed the constitutional status of Finance Commission recommendations and the Union's discretion in implementing them, engaging the question of whether fiscal transfers under Part XII create enforceable constitutional obligations or remain discretionary executive acts.

**Jilubhai Nanbhai Khachar v. State of Gujarat** — The Supreme Court held that Article 300A does not confer a fundamental right to property but does require deprivation of property to be backed by valid law, and that such law must not be arbitrary or violate other constitutional guarantees, setting an important standard for property-taking under the post-Forty-Fourth Amendment regime.

## Current significance

States' complaints about the inadequacy of their share of central tax revenues, the conditionalities attached to Finance Commission grants, and the declining flexibility of state fiscal management remain at the centre of fiscal federalism debates directly grounded in Part XII. The GST Council's decision-making processes and the extent to which the Union can unilaterally modify the GST framework — touching Articles 269A and 279A — are subjects of ongoing constitutional scrutiny. Acquisition of property for public and private projects under Article 300A continues to generate litigation about fair compensation and the meaning of "authority of law."
