## What this Part contains

Part XIVA was inserted by the Forty-Second Constitutional Amendment in 1976 and contains Articles 323A and 323B, which empower Parliament and state legislatures to establish administrative tribunals for adjudication of disputes relating to recruitment and conditions of service of public servants and other specified matters. Article 323A enables Parliament to create a Central Administrative Tribunal and State Administrative Tribunals to take over the jurisdiction of High Courts and lower courts in service disputes, while Article 323B allows tribunals to be set up for a wider range of subjects including labour disputes, land acquisition, industrial and labour disputes, elections, and food and other essential commodities. The constitutional validity of the exclusion of High Court writ jurisdiction by these tribunals was hotly contested and ultimately settled by the Supreme Court in a landmark ruling.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 323A** — Authorises Parliament to provide by law for adjudication of disputes relating to recruitment and conditions of service of persons appointed to public services by administrative tribunals, and to exclude the jurisdiction of all courts except the Supreme Court under Article 136 in such matters.
- **Article 323B** — Empowers Parliament and state legislatures to provide by law for adjudication by tribunals of disputes, complaints, or offences with respect to a list of matters including taxation, foreign exchange, import and export, industrial and labour disputes, and elections, with a power to exclude ordinary court jurisdiction.

## Key cases

**L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench struck down the provisions of the Administrative Tribunals Act that excluded High Court jurisdiction, holding that the power of High Courts to exercise judicial review under Articles 226 and 227 is part of the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be excluded even by a constitutional amendment; tribunals were consequently held to be courts of first instance subject to High Court supervisory jurisdiction.

**S.P. Sampath Kumar v. Union of India** — An earlier five-judge bench had upheld the Administrative Tribunals Act in this case, finding that the tribunal system was a constitutionally valid substitute for High Court jurisdiction in service matters, a holding that was effectively overruled by L. Chandra Kumar.

**Union of India v. R. Gandhi (NCLT case)** — While addressing tribunals under company law, the Court substantially reiterated and applied the principles from L. Chandra Kumar to hold that tribunal members must possess qualifications comparable to the courts they replace, and that the essential judicial character of tribunals must be preserved.

## Current significance

The design and staffing of tribunals across multiple sectors — tax, environment, competition, consumer disputes, and company law — continues to generate constitutional challenges about the adequacy of tribunal procedures, the independence of tribunal members, and the validity of limitations on High Court jurisdiction. The Supreme Court has repeatedly struck down or modified tribunal appointment rules that gave the executive excessive control over selections, engaging directly with Part XIVA’s constitutional framework. The question of whether the National Company Law Appellate Tribunal, the National Green Tribunal, and other post-2000 specialised tribunals satisfy the constitutional standards articulated under Part XIVA remains an active area of constitutional oversight.
