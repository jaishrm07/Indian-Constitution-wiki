## What this Part contains

Part VII, as originally enacted, contained Article 238 which applied the provisions of Part VI (relating to the states) to the states specified in Part B of the First Schedule — former princely states such as Hyderabad, Mysore, Saurashtra, Travancore-Cochin, and others that had acceded to India. It was a transitional mechanism to bring the governance structures of these former princely territories under the same constitutional framework as British Indian provinces. The States Reorganisation Act 1956 and the Seventh Constitutional Amendment, which reorganised states on linguistic lines and abolished the distinction between Part A, Part B, and Part C states, rendered Part VII redundant. Article 238 was repealed and Part VII was omitted by the Seventh Amendment, which restructured the First Schedule to list only one category of states. The Part therefore exists in constitutional history as an important record of how India absorbed the princely order into its democratic constitutional framework.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 238 (repealed)** — Applied Part VI to Part B states with such exceptions and modifications as the President might by order specify, enabling a degree of flexibility in the transition period; it was deleted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956 following the reorganisation of states on linguistic lines.

## Key cases

**Madhao Rao Scindia v. Union of India (Privy Purses case)** — Although primarily concerning the privy purses paid to rulers of former princely states and the validity of the Presidential order derecognising them, this landmark case engaged the constitutional status of the former Part B states and the terms on which they had been integrated into the Indian Union, eventually resolved through the Twenty-Sixth Amendment which extinguished the privy purses by constitutional amendment.

**State of Saurashtra v. Memon Haji Ismail Haji** — The Court addressed jurisdictional questions arising from the integration of a former Part B state into the constitutional order, affirming that the courts of such states became fully integrated into the Indian judicial hierarchy upon the commencement of Part VII and its subsequent reorganisation.

## Current significance

Part VII has no direct operational significance today as it was deleted in 1956. Its historical importance lies in demonstrating how the Constitution managed the incorporation of several hundred princely states into the federal order, a process that shaped the initial architecture of federalism. The doctrines developed around the privy purses, instrument of accession, and merger agreements during the Part VII era continue to be cited in cases about the constitutional treatment of erstwhile rulers and their properties.
