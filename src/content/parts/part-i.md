## What this Part contains

Part I spans Articles 1 to 4 and establishes the foundational architecture of India as a sovereign constitutional entity. Article 1 declares India to be a Union of States and gives the country its official name, while Articles 2 to 4 vest Parliament with the power to admit new states, form new states by bifurcation or merger, and alter the names and boundaries of existing states. The central constitutional function of this Part is to define the territorial scope of the Republic and to vest in Parliament an ongoing, flexible authority over the composition of the Union — authority that has been exercised dozens of times since 1950. Crucially, the Part establishes that India is a Union rather than a federation, a deliberate textual choice that signals the indestructibility of the Union even when individual states are reorganised.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 1** — Declares India to be a Union of States and specifies that the territory of India comprises the states, union territories, and such other territories as may be acquired; the use of "Union" rather than "Federation" was deliberate to foreclose the right of secession.
- **Article 2** — Empowers Parliament to admit new states into the Union or establish new states on such terms and conditions as it thinks fit, a provision used when Sikkim acceded in 1975.
- **Article 3** — Authorises Parliament, by law, to form new states, increase or diminish the area of any state, and alter the name or boundary of any state, subject only to obtaining the views (not consent) of the affected state legislature.
- **Article 4** — Makes clear that laws made under Articles 2 and 3 are not constitutional amendments within Article 368, enabling Parliament to reorganise territory through ordinary legislation.

## Key cases

**Berubari Union case** — The Supreme Court held that ceding Indian territory to a foreign power pursuant to a treaty requires a constitutional amendment under Article 368 and cannot be accomplished by ordinary legislation alone, establishing a critical limit on executive treaty-making power in relation to territorial sovereignty.

**In re: Reorganisation of States** — The Court affirmed that Parliament’s power under Article 3 to form and alter states is plenary and that the opinion of the state legislature, while mandatory to obtain, is not binding on Parliament, clarifying the asymmetric relationship between the Union and the constituent states in territorial matters.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India** — While primarily an Article 356 case, the Court’s observations on the indestructibility of the Union under Article 1 confirmed that India’s federal structure operates within an overarching unitary frame where the centre holds ultimate authority over the existence of states themselves.

## Current significance

The delimitation of constituencies that follows state reorganisation keeps Article 3’s power politically live, and proposals to carve new states out of existing ones — including recurring demands in several large states — regularly revive the question of how much weight Parliament must give to a state legislature’s objection. Article 1’s "other territories" clause gains renewed relevance whenever questions arise about the constitutional treatment of acquired land or contested border enclaves. The abrogation of Article 370 and the conversion of Jammu and Kashmir into two union territories in 2019 directly engaged Articles 3 and 4 and generated ongoing litigation about the limits of Part I’s reorganisation power.
