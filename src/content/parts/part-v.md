## Why this Part matters

Part V is essential for understanding the constitutional structure of the Union, including how central institutions interact and how disputes involving States can reach the Supreme Court directly.

## How to read it

Readers should connect this Part to constitutional litigation, parliamentary structure, federal disputes, and the institutional design of central authority.

## What this Part contains

Part V runs from Articles 52 to 151 and is the largest and most institutionally dense Part in the Constitution, encompassing the President and Vice-President, the executive arm of the Union government, both Houses of Parliament, the Comptroller and Auditor-General, and the entire chapter on the Supreme Court of India. It establishes the President as the constitutional head of the Union executive, defines the Prime Minister's cabinet system of government, and vests Parliament with the power to make laws across a wide range of subjects. The Supreme Court chapter within this Part grants the Court original, appellate, advisory, and extraordinary jurisdictions, and Article 141 makes the Court's declarations of law binding on all courts within India. Read together, the provisions of Part V create the institutional skeleton of central governance in which authority is distributed but the constitutional text anchors the Supreme Court as the final arbiter.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 74** — Provides that the President shall act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister, establishing the constitutional basis for parliamentary and cabinet government at the Union level.
- **Article 105** — Confers on Members of Parliament freedom of speech in either House and immunity from court proceedings in respect of anything said or done in Parliament, constituting the privilege that protects legislative deliberation.
- **Article 123** — Empowers the President to promulgate ordinances when Parliament is not in session, a power that has been extensively used and judicially reviewed for its limits.
- **Article 136** — Grants the Supreme Court a discretionary special leave to appeal from any judgment, decree, or order of any court or tribunal in the territory of India, making it the ultimate court of appeal in all matters.
- **Article 142** — Authorises the Supreme Court to make any order necessary for doing complete justice in any cause pending before it, a provision that has been used for sweeping remedial actions in public interest litigation.

## Key cases

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India** — The nine-judge bench elaborated the limits on Presidential proclamation under Article 356 but its observations about parliamentary government, cabinet accountability, and Article 74 also shaped the understanding of executive power within Part V.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala** — The Court's reasoning drew heavily on the Supreme Court's constitutional position under Part V, holding that Article 32 and the Court's role as guardian of the Constitution form part of the unamendable basic structure.

**In re: Presidential Reference on Election Commission** — The Court's advisory opinion on whether the Chief Election Commissioner could be removed by a simple majority directly engaged Articles 148 and related provisions on constitutional office holders within Part V, clarifying the scope of Presidential and Parliamentary powers over independent institutions.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (NJAC case)** — The Court struck down the Ninety-Ninth Amendment and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act on the ground that they damaged judicial independence and the separation of powers embedded in Part V's chapter on the Supreme Court, reaffirming the collegium system for judicial appointments.

## Current significance

The debate over the collegium system of judicial appointments versus a more participatory commission remains politically unresolved, keeping Articles 124 to 127 under sustained attention. Ordinance-making power under Article 123 continues to generate controversy whenever Parliament is prorogued and major policy ordinances are issued in quick succession. The scope of Article 142 — complete justice jurisdiction — is under active judicial and academic scrutiny, with questions about whether it can be used to override substantive law rather than merely fill procedural gaps.
