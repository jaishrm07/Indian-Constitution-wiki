## What this Part contains

Part XVI spans Articles 330 to 342A and contains the special provisions that the Constitution makes for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, Anglo-Indians (now removed), backward classes, and minorities. It reserves seats in the Lok Sabha and state legislative assemblies for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in proportion to their population (Articles 330 and 332), requires the President and Governors to designate castes and tribes for each state as SCs and STs, establishes National Commissions for Scheduled Castes (Article 338) and Scheduled Tribes (Article 338A) and the National Commission for Backward Classes (Article 338B), and empowers the President to appoint Commissions to investigate the condition of backward classes. The One Hundred and Second Amendment inserted Article 342A giving the President and Parliament the power to specify Socially and Educationally Backward Classes, centralising at the Union level a function previously exercised by states.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 330** — Reserves seats in the House of the People for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, apportioned according to the ratio of their population to the total population in each state.
- **Article 334** — Originally set the reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes for a period of ten years from commencement; this period has been extended repeatedly by constitutional amendment, most recently by the One Hundred and Fourth Amendment extending it to 2030, with the One Hundred and sixth Amendment in 2023 extending it to 2034.
- **Article 338** — Establishes the National Commission for Scheduled Castes as a constitutional body with powers of a civil court to investigate and monitor safeguards for SCs.
- **Article 341** — Empowers the President to specify the castes, races, or tribes deemed to be Scheduled Castes in each state or union territory, with Parliament having the power to include or exclude any caste from the Presidential notification by law.
- **Article 342A** — Inserted by the One Hundred and Second Amendment, this article vests in the President the power to specify the Socially and Educationally Backward Classes for each state, with Parliament having the power to include or exclude entries, raising questions about whether states retain concurrent power to maintain their own OBC lists.

## Key cases

**State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan** — The Supreme Court held that reservations in educational institutions could not be based on religion, race, caste, or community in a way that violated Article 29(2), prompting the First Amendment which inserted Article 15(4), and thereby setting off the constitutional history of reservation law that Part XVI’s articles reflect.

**Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (Mandal case)** — The Supreme Court upheld reservations for Other Backward Classes under the Mandal Commission recommendations but struck down reservations for the economically advanced sections among them, capped total reservations at fifty percent except in extraordinary circumstances, and excluded the creamy layer from OBC reservations, a ruling that directly shaped how Part XVI’s provisions interact with the equality guarantees of Part III.

**Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta** — The Court held that the concept of inadequate representation for sub-groups within SCs and STs for reservation in promotions must be established by quantifiable data collected by the state, and that sub-classification within the reserved category requires fresh empirical justification, a ruling that generates continuing litigation about how Articles 335 and 341 operate together.

## Current significance

The constitutionality of reserving seats for women in Parliament and state assemblies — mandated by the One Hundred and Sixth Amendment Act 2023 but operative only after delimitation following the next census — is a live constitutional and political question engaging Articles 330A and 332A. The Supreme Court’s ongoing reference on whether sub-classification within Scheduled Caste reservations is permissible under Articles 341 and 342 directly touches the core of Part XVI. The One Hundred and Third Amendment’s introduction of economic reservations for Economically Weaker Sections in open category seats was upheld by a five-judge bench in 2022 but its interaction with the fifty percent ceiling remains contested.
