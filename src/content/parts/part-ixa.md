## Why this Part matters

Part IXA recognises that urban governance requires institutional design, representation, and devolution within the constitutional order. It is a major reason municipal structure can be debated in constitutional terms.

## How to read it

Readers should connect this Part with Article 243W, the Twelfth Schedule, and the Seventy-Fourth Amendment. That cluster explains how urban self-government is built into the constitutional map.

## What this Part contains

Part IXA spans Articles 243P to 243ZG and was inserted by the Seventy-Fourth Constitutional Amendment Act 1992, coming into force alongside Part IX in 1993. It applies the principles of local self-government to urban areas by constitutionalising Municipalities, Nagar Panchayats, Municipal Councils, and Municipal Corporations across a graduated scale based on the size and character of urban settlements. The Part requires states to hold regular elections to municipal bodies through state election commissions, reserve seats for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women, and constitute ward committees in cities of three lakhs or more. Article 243W makes the devolution of powers over the eighteen subjects in the Twelfth Schedule dependent on state legislation, creating the same structural gap between constitutional aspiration and practical devolution that characterises Part IX.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 243Q** — Mandates the constitution of Nagar Panchayats for transitional areas, Municipal Councils for smaller urban areas, and Municipal Corporations for larger urban areas, requiring states to maintain a constitutionally recognised municipal structure.
- **Article 243R** — Sets out the composition of Municipalities and requires that all seats be filled by persons chosen by direct election from the wards, with reservation for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women.
- **Article 243T** — Mandates reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in proportion to their population, and ensures that not less than one-third of the total seats are reserved for women.
- **Article 243W** — Enables state legislatures to endow Municipalities with powers for governance, economic development, and social justice over the subjects in the Twelfth Schedule, mirroring the devolution mechanism in Article 243G for Panchayats.
- **Article 243ZD** — Mandates the constitution of District Planning Committees to consolidate plans prepared by Panchayats and Municipalities in each district, creating a constitutional mechanism for integrated local planning.

## Key cases

**Ramachandran v. Union of India** — The Court examined the constitutional obligation to hold municipal elections within the constitutionally mandated timeframe, affirming that the five-year term under Article 243U is mandatory and that states cannot indefinitely defer municipal elections by extending the tenure of outgoing bodies or by administrative fiat.

**Rajasthan Urban Infrastructure Development Project cases** — Courts have repeatedly considered whether development authorities and parastatal bodies that bypass elected municipal corporations violate the constitutional scheme of Part IXA, given that Article 243W vests urban functions in the constitutionally mandated municipal structure.

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Solid Waste Management)** — The Supreme Court's orders on solid waste management in urban areas drew on the constitutional framework of Part IXA to hold that municipalities bear constitutionally anchored responsibilities for environmental management within their jurisdictions under the Twelfth Schedule.

## Current significance

The persistent marginalisation of elected municipal bodies by state governments — through creation of parallel development authorities, withholding of devolved functions, and delayed elections — remains the central governance challenge addressed by Part IXA. The constitutional requirement of ward committees in large cities and District Planning Committees under Article 243ZD is widely observed in the breach, generating litigation about the enforceability of these constitutional mandates. Urban flooding, solid waste, and infrastructure planning disputes regularly invoke the devolution framework of Part IXA to argue that bypassing municipalities is constitutionally impermissible.
