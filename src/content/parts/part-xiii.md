## What this Part contains

Part XIII spans Articles 301 to 307 and protects the freedom of trade, commerce, and intercourse throughout the territory of India. Article 301 guarantees that trade, commerce, and intercourse shall be free, creating a constitutional common market whose contours have been litigated extensively. Parliament may impose restrictions in the public interest under Article 302, but state legislatures may impose restrictions only if the restrictions are non-discriminatory between states and do not prefer one state over others, subject to an exception for laws that are declared essential by Parliament. Article 304 allows states to impose taxes on goods imported from other states on the same terms as goods manufactured locally, and to impose reasonable restrictions on trade for public interest with Presidential sanction. The Part’s central constitutional function is to prevent balkanisation of the Indian market through state barriers to inter-state commerce while leaving room for legitimate public interest regulation.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 301** — Declares that trade, commerce, and intercourse throughout the territory of India shall be free, establishing the constitutional common market principle that serves as the baseline against which state and central trade restrictions are measured.
- **Article 302** — Empowers Parliament to impose restrictions on the freedom of trade, commerce, or intercourse between one state and another or within any part of the territory of India if it is in the public interest to do so.
- **Article 303** — Prohibits Parliament and state legislatures from giving preference to one state over another or making any discrimination between states in matters of trade, except where Parliament declares that doing so is necessary to deal with a scarcity of goods.
- **Article 304** — Permits states to impose taxes on goods imported from other states on equal terms with locally produced goods, and to impose reasonable restrictions on freedom of trade for public interest, subject to prior Presidential sanction for such restrictions.
- **Article 307** — Empowers Parliament to appoint a body for the purpose of carrying out the purposes of Articles 301 to 304, the provision that would authorise a permanent inter-state trade commission though none has been constituted.

## Key cases

**Atiabari Tea Co. Ltd. v. State of Assam** — The Supreme Court held that taxes can directly and immediately impede trade and commerce and thus violate Article 301, establishing that fiscal measures are not automatically immune from Part XIII scrutiny merely because they are taxes rather than regulatory restrictions.

**Automobile Transport (Rajasthan) Ltd. v. State of Rajasthan** — A seven-judge bench overruled the earlier broad reading of Article 301 and held that compensatory taxes — levies charged for services rendered to the taxed trade — do not violate the freedom of trade and commerce, introducing the compensatory tax doctrine that long shaped indirect tax jurisprudence.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd. v. State of Haryana** — The Supreme Court reconsidered the compensatory tax doctrine and held that entry taxes on goods entering a local area must clear a higher test to qualify as compensatory, with each element of the levy needing to have a direct nexus to services rendered, a ruling that restructured the testing of state levies on inter-state trade.

## Current significance

The GST regime’s replacement of most state entry taxes and octroi levies has reduced but not eliminated Part XIII litigation, with disputes now focusing on whether particular GST provisions discriminate between states or impose unreasonable trade barriers. State restrictions on liquor trade and agricultural produce that affect inter-state commerce continue to generate Article 301 challenges. The constitutional validity of state policies requiring local procurement or purchase preference for state-manufactured goods — a form of economic nationalism — is regularly contested under Part XIII.
