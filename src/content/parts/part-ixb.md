## What this Part contains

Part IXB was inserted by the Ninety-Seventh Constitutional Amendment Act 2011 and covers Articles 243ZH to 243ZT. It gives co-operative societies a constitutional footing by imposing minimum standards for democratic governance, regular elections, audit, and accountability within the co-operative sector — a sector that had long been criticised for functioning as a patronage network under political control rather than as a member-owned institution. The Part requires states to enact laws ensuring free and fair elections to co-operative societies, fixing the term of elected boards, mandating independent audits, and protecting minority shareholders. Unlike Parts IX and IXA, Part IXB does not mandate devolution of specific powers but instead sets constitutional minimum standards that all state co-operative society laws must meet.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 243ZI** — Empowers state legislatures to make laws for the incorporation, regulation, and winding up of co-operative societies, subject to the minimum requirements laid down in Part IXB.
- **Article 243ZJ** — Fixes the number of directors of a co-operative society at a minimum of one and a maximum of twenty-one, including one seat for Scheduled Castes or Scheduled Tribes and two seats for women, introducing constitutional minimums for board composition.
- **Article 243ZK** — Mandates that election of directors be held by the State Election Commission to ensure independent and free elections to co-operative society boards.
- **Article 243ZL** — Fixes the term of elected boards at five years and requires fresh elections before the expiry of the term, preventing the indefinite supersession that had been common practice in the sector.
- **Article 243ZT** — Requires that existing state laws on co-operative societies be brought into conformity with Part IXB within one year of the Amendment’s commencement.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. State of Maharashtra (Multi-State Co-operative Societies cases)** — Litigation arising from the Centre’s assertion of regulatory authority over multi-state co-operative societies engaged Articles 243ZH and 243ZI, raising questions about whether Part IXB’s framework applies to co-operatives that straddle state boundaries and are regulated by central legislation.

**Supreme Court on Ninety-Seventh Amendment** — In a significant ruling, the Supreme Court struck down Part IXB to the extent it applied to co-operative societies within state jurisdiction, holding that the Amendment was passed without ratification by the required number of state legislatures as required by the proviso to Article 368(2) for amendments affecting the legislative lists, leaving the Part operative only for multi-state co-operatives.

## Current significance

The Supreme Court’s partial invalidation of Part IXB meant that the constitutional governance standards for co-operative societies apply only in a limited domain, and the broader reform agenda for the sector continues to be pursued through ordinary legislation at state level. The Ministry of Co-operation established in 2021 and the Multi-State Co-operative Societies (Amendment) Act 2023 have given renewed attention to the constitutional framework of Part IXB and whether it provides a sufficient constitutional anchor for central regulation of the sector. Questions about the democratic accountability of large co-operative banks, including their interaction with the Reserve Bank of India’s regulatory powers, keep Part IXB’s provisions about elections and audit in practical relevance.
