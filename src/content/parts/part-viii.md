## Why this Part matters

The constitutional treatment of Union Territories reveals how India manages asymmetric governance arrangements inside a federal structure.

## How to read it

Readers should use this Part when following disputes over Delhi, Lieutenant Governor powers, and the allocation of legislative or executive authority in Union Territories.

## What this Part contains

Part VIII covers Articles 239 to 242 and governs Union Territories, which are administered directly by the Union rather than by elected state governments following the ordinary Part VI model. Article 239 vests administration of each Union Territory in the President, who acts through an Administrator appointed by him. The Part accommodates significant diversity: some Union Territories have no legislature at all, while others, most notably Delhi and Puducherry, have been granted legislative assemblies with limited powers under Articles 239A and 239AA respectively. Article 239AA, inserted by the Sixty-Ninth Amendment, established the National Capital Territory of Delhi as a distinct constitutional category with a legislative assembly but with significant powers reserved for the Lieutenant Governor and the Union, and it has been the epicentre of high-stakes constitutional litigation over the relationship between an elected government and an appointed administrator.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 239** — Vests administration of every Union Territory in the President, who may appoint a Lieutenant Governor or other administrator to act as his agent, making Union Territories constitutionally distinct from states governed by Part VI.
- **Article 239A** — Permits Parliament to create a legislature or a council of ministers for certain Union Territories, providing the constitutional basis for the Pondicherry Legislative Assembly before Article 239AA was enacted.
- **Article 239AA** — Establishes a special status for the National Capital Territory of Delhi, granting it a Legislative Assembly with law-making powers over all subjects except public order, police, and land, while also creating a potential for conflict between the elected government and the Lieutenant Governor.
- **Article 239AB** — Allows the President to suspend the constitutional machinery in a Union Territory with a legislature, analogous to (but distinct from) Article 356 for states, if the government of the territory cannot be carried on in accordance with the constitutional provisions.
- **Article 240** — Grants the President the power to make regulations for the peace, progress, and good government of certain Union Territories, a power that substitutes for the legislative function of Parliament in those territories.

## Key cases

**Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench held that in matters within the legislative competence of the Delhi Assembly, the Lieutenant Governor is bound by the aid and advice of the elected Council of Ministers and has no independent decision-making authority, marking a significant assertion of the primacy of elected government.

**Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2023)** — The Supreme Court further held that the Delhi government had exclusive control over the services (civil servants) working for Delhi, except for matters relating to public order, police, and land; this ruling was subsequently overturned by Parliament through the Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi (Amendment) Act 2023 and the constitutional validity of that amendment remains pending before the Court.

**Madho Singh v. Gram Panchayat** — The Court considered the extent of the President's regulation-making power under Article 240 and held that regulations made for Union Territories have the force of law and take precedence over conflicting state-like legislation in territories without a full legislature.

## Current significance

The constitutional status of Delhi and the balance of power between the Lieutenant Governor, the elected government, and the Union government remain unresolved following Parliament's legislative reversal of the 2023 Supreme Court ruling on services. The constitutional treatment of Jammu and Kashmir as a Union Territory since 2019, and whether Article 3 read with Part VIII permitted such a downgrade from statehood, is the subject of ongoing judicial and political debate. Puducherry's legislature has also generated disputes about the scope of Article 239A that mirror, at a smaller scale, the Delhi governance controversies.
