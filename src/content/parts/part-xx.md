## Why this Part matters

Amendment power decides how stable the constitutional order is and how change can legitimately happen. In India, that question is inseparable from the basic structure doctrine.

## How to read it

Part XX should be read together with landmark amendment cases and the history of amendments that attempted deep institutional redesign.

## What this Part contains

Part XX consists of a single article — Article 368 — which provides the exclusive mechanism for amending the Constitution of India. Article 368 distinguishes between amendments that require a special majority of Parliament (two-thirds of members present and voting and a majority of the total membership of each House), and amendments touching certain specially entrenched provisions — such as the federal structure, representation in Parliament, and the amendment procedure itself — which additionally require ratification by at least half the state legislatures. Despite giving Parliament a seemingly plenary power to "amend by way of addition, variation or repeal any provision of this Constitution," the Supreme Court has read into Article 368 the judicially created basic structure doctrine which holds that certain essential features of the constitutional order cannot be abrogated or destroyed even by a duly passed constitutional amendment.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 368** — Provides the sole mechanism for constitutional amendment, setting out the procedural requirements of special majority and, for certain provisions, state ratification; it also declares that no constitutional amendment can be called in question in any court of law on the ground that it violates any provision of the Constitution — a clause that the Supreme Court has held cannot insulate amendments from review for violation of the basic structure.

## Key cases

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala** — A thirteen-judge bench held by a 7-6 majority that Article 368 does not give Parliament the power to amend the Constitution so as to destroy its basic structure or essential features, creating the foundational doctrine that limits the formally unlimited amendment power and has governed Indian constitutional law ever since.

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India** — The Court struck down provisions of the Forty-Second Amendment that had purported to give Parliament unlimited amending power and had immunised constitutional amendments from judicial review, holding that these provisions themselves violated the basic structure by removing the constitutional limitations on Article 368.

**I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu** — A nine-judge bench held that laws placed in the Ninth Schedule after 24 April 1973 (the date of the Kesavananda judgment) are subject to judicial review if they damage the basic structure, even though the Ninth Schedule is itself a constitutional text; this ruling extended the logic of Article 368's limits to amendments that shield ordinary legislation from fundamental rights scrutiny.

## Current significance

Each new constitutional amendment is assessed by constitutional scholars, litigants, and courts for compatibility with the basic structure, making Article 368 a continuously relevant provision rather than a procedural artifact. Proposed amendments to electoral laws, reservation frameworks, and judicial appointment procedures are regularly challenged in public interest petitions on basic structure grounds. The pending challenges to the One Hundred and First (GST), One Hundred and Third (EWS reservations), Ninety-Ninth (NJAC), and One Hundred and Sixth (Women's reservation) Amendments all engage the basic structure doctrine derived from the interpretation of Article 368.
