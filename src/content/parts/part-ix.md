## Why this Part matters

Part IX shifts local democracy from the margins of governance into the constitutional text. It makes the structure of Panchayats a constitutional subject and ties rural devolution to democratic design.

## How to read it

Readers should connect this Part with Article 243G, the Eleventh Schedule, and the Seventy-Third Amendment. Together they explain how powers can move below the state level while still remaining constitutionally structured.

## What this Part contains

Part IX spans Articles 243 to 243O and was inserted by the Seventy-Third Constitutional Amendment Act 1992, which came into force in April 1993. It constitutionalises the three-tier system of Panchayati Raj institutions — Gram Panchayats at the village level, intermediate Panchayats, and Zila Parishads at the district level — and mandates regular elections to these bodies through independent state election commissions. The Part requires states to devolve powers over the twenty-nine subjects listed in the Eleventh Schedule to Panchayats, though it leaves the extent and manner of devolution to each state legislature. Crucially, Part IX also mandates reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women in Panchayat bodies, establishing a constitutional floor for political representation at the grassroots level.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 243B** — Mandates the constitution of Panchayats at the village, intermediate, and district levels in every state, making the three-tier structure a constitutional requirement rather than a statutory choice.
- **Article 243C** — Sets out the composition of Panchayats, including the requirement of direct elections to seats and the basis for determining the number of seats.
- **Article 243D** — Requires reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in proportion to their population, and mandates that not less than one-third of all seats be reserved for women.
- **Article 243E** — Fixes the duration of Panchayats at five years and requires elections to be held before the expiry of that term, preventing indefinite supersession of elected local bodies.
- **Article 243G** — Empowers state legislatures to endow Panchayats with such powers and authority as may be necessary to enable them to function as institutions of self-government, and to implement schemes for economic development and social justice.

## Key cases

**K. Krishnamurthy v. Union of India** — The Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of reservations in excess of fifty percent in Panchayat elections for backward classes, holding that the constitutional scheme of Part IX permits states to provide additional reservations beyond the general ceiling applicable to other spheres, subject to the triple test of empirical data, non-exclusion, and overall ceiling limits.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v. Pradhan Sangh Kshettra Samiti** — The Court held that the requirement of gram sabha consultation under state Panchayati Raj laws derived constitutional importance from Part IX, and that bypassing elected panchayat structures for government schemes undermined the constitutional design of devolved self-government.

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh** — The Court addressed the constitutional obligation of states to hold Panchayat elections without delay, affirming that the five-year term under Article 243E and the obligation to hold elections before expiry are mandatory constitutional commands, not merely directory provisions.

## Current significance

The implementation gap between the constitutional promise of devolution under Article 243G and the actual transfer of functions, funds, and functionaries to Panchayats by state governments remains one of the most persistently discussed governance failures in Indian constitutional practice. The constitutional validity of reservation policies for Other Backward Classes in Panchayat elections, subject to the triple test laid down by the Supreme Court, continues to generate state-level litigation. Proposals to strengthen gram sabha powers and to give Panchayats direct budgetary authority are regularly framed in constitutional terms by invoking Part IX's vision of self-government.
