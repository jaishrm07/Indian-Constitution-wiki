## What this Part contains

Part IVA consists solely of Article 51A, which was inserted by the Forty-Second Constitutional Amendment in 1976 and contains a list of eleven fundamental duties that every citizen of India is obligated to perform. The duties range from abiding by the Constitution and respecting national symbols to protecting the natural environment and developing scientific temper. Unlike the fundamental rights in Part III, fundamental duties are not directly enforceable through writ jurisdiction; however, courts have increasingly used them as interpretive tools to construe the scope of legislative restrictions and to impose obligations on citizens in public interest litigation. A further duty — to provide opportunities for education to his child or ward between the age of six and fourteen years — was added by the Eighty-Sixth Amendment in 2002 alongside the Right to Education.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 51A** — Enumerates eleven fundamental duties of every citizen including to uphold the Constitution and its ideals, to promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood, to protect the natural environment, and to strive towards excellence in all spheres; the duties are non-justiciable but carry interpretive constitutional weight.

## Key cases

**AIIMS Students’ Union v. AIIMS** — The Supreme Court used Article 51A(j) — the duty to strive towards excellence — as a constitutional basis to hold that merit-based selection in academic institutions serves a constitutional purpose, illustrating how fundamental duties can inform the reasonableness of legislative and institutional choices.

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (environmental series)** — The Court repeatedly cited Article 51A(g) — the duty to protect and improve the natural environment — to justify wide-ranging environmental orders and to hold that citizens and industries alike bear constitutional obligations to the environment, not merely statutory ones.

**Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala** — While the case turned on Article 19(1)(a) and Article 25, the Court noted that standing silently during the national anthem could not be penalised as a violation of Article 51A(a) since Article 51A imposes duties but does not permit the state to punish non-compliance with them in the absence of a supporting law.

## Current significance

The debate over whether fundamental duties should be made justiciable — a proposal periodically canvassed by the Law Commission and policy bodies — keeps Part IVA in constitutional discussion. Courts have increasingly cited Article 51A in environmental litigation to hold polluting entities to elevated standards of care, and the framing of civic education policy regularly invokes the constitutional source of the duties listed here. The relationship between Article 51A(c) — the duty to protect sovereignty and integrity — and laws against sedition and dissent remains a live doctrinal question.
