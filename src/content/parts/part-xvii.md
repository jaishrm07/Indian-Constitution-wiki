## What this Part contains

Part XVII covers Articles 343 to 351 and governs the official languages of the Union and the states. Article 343 designates Hindi in Devanagari script as the official language of the Union and provides that English shall continue to be used for official purposes for fifteen years after the Constitution’s commencement, with Parliament empowered to extend its use thereafter. Articles 345 to 347 deal with the official languages of states, including the power of states to adopt their own official languages and the Union’s obligation to use a state’s language in communications to it. Article 348 requires proceedings in the Supreme Court and High Courts and authenticated texts of central and state laws to be in English until Parliament provides otherwise. Article 350 protects the right to address the Union or any state for redress of grievances in any language used in India.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 343** — Designates Hindi in Devanagari script as the official language of the Union, provides for continued use of English for fifteen years, and empowers Parliament to provide for the continued use of English and the use of numerals for official Union purposes thereafter.
- **Article 344** — Requires the President to constitute a Commission at the end of five years from commencement and thereafter to review the progressive use of Hindi for Union official purposes, setting up a periodic constitutional review mechanism for language policy.
- **Article 347** — Enables the President to recognise a language of a state for official purposes if a substantial proportion of the state’s population demands that the language spoken by them be recognised, a provision that has been engaged in several state language disputes.
- **Article 350A** — Directs states and local authorities to provide adequate facilities for instruction in the mother tongue at the primary stage of education for children belonging to linguistic minority groups, a constitutional guarantee for linguistic minority education.
- **Article 351** — Issues a directive to the Union to promote the spread of Hindi, to develop it so that it may serve as a medium of expression for all elements of India’s composite culture, and to secure its enrichment by drawing on the forms, style, and expressions used in other Indian languages.

## Key cases

**D.A.V. College v. State of Punjab** — The Supreme Court upheld the right of linguistic minorities under Article 30 to establish educational institutions that use their language as the medium of instruction, engaging the intersection between Part XVII’s language provisions and the minority rights in Part III.

**State of Madras v. V.G. Row** — Language laws and their relation to freedom of speech and the rights of linguistic minorities were considered in early cases that explored how Articles 19 and 350 interact, establishing that language restrictions are subject to the fundamental rights framework.

**TMA Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka** — While primarily concerning minority educational institutions under Article 30, the Court’s analysis of linguistic minorities directly drew on the Article 350A guarantee of mother-tongue instruction and shaped the understanding of linguistic minority rights under Part XVII.

## Current significance

The persistent demand by several southern states and linguistic communities for the equal status of regional languages alongside Hindi in Union government operations, public examinations, and higher education keeps Part XVII’s balance between Article 343’s Hindi policy and the protection of other Indian languages a live political and constitutional issue. The constitutional obligation under Article 350A to provide primary education in the mother tongue continues to generate litigation about the rights of linguistic minority children in states where the official language differs from their home language. Parliamentary and judicial proceedings conducted in English when regional-language parties or litigants seek to use their languages engage the practical limits of Part XVII’s language framework.
