## Why this Part matters

Part III turns constitutional values into enforceable guarantees. It is the doctrinal home of equality, speech, personal liberty, and remedies against unconstitutional state action.

## How to read it

This Part is strongest when read relationally. Article 14 controls arbitrariness, Article 19 structures civic freedom, Article 21 protects liberty and dignity, and Article 32 provides remedies.

## What this Part contains

Part III runs from Articles 12 to 35 and constitutes India's Bill of Rights, making it the most heavily litigated portion of the entire Constitution. It guarantees six broad categories of rights — equality before law, freedom of speech and other civic liberties, protection against exploitation, freedom of religion, cultural and educational rights, and the right to constitutional remedies — and subjects each to a carefully structured scheme of permissible restrictions. Article 12 defines "State" for the purposes of Part III, extending the reach of fundamental rights beyond Parliament and government departments to statutory bodies, corporations controlled by the state, and instrumentalities exercising public functions. The Part also contains its own internal enforcement mechanism through Article 32, which the Supreme Court has described as itself a fundamental right and the soul of the Constitution.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 14** — Guarantees equality before the law and equal protection of the laws to all persons, and has been extended by the Supreme Court into a general prohibition on arbitrary state action through the doctrine of non-arbitrariness.
- **Article 19** — Confers six freedoms including speech and expression, peaceful assembly, movement, and profession, each subject to reasonable restrictions that the state must justify under the sub-clauses.
- **Article 21** — Protects the right to life and personal liberty against deprivation except by procedure established by law; judicial interpretation has read into it a vast range of unenumerated rights including privacy, livelihood, health, a clean environment, and dignity.
- **Article 32** — Grants the right to move the Supreme Court directly for the enforcement of fundamental rights and empowers the Court to issue writs including habeas corpus, mandamus, and certiorari.
- **Article 13** — Declares that laws inconsistent with or in derogation of fundamental rights are void, giving Part III its superior constitutional status over ordinary legislation and even constitutional amendments insofar as they damage the basic structure.

## Key cases

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala** — The Supreme Court's thirteen-judge bench held that while Parliament can amend any provision of the Constitution under Article 368, it cannot destroy the basic structure of the Constitution, thereby limiting the power to curtail fundamental rights through amendment and entrenching Part III's core content against complete abrogation.

**Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India** — The Court overruled an earlier narrow reading of Article 21 and held that the procedure established by law for depriving a person of life or liberty must be fair, just, and reasonable, linking Articles 14, 19, and 21 into a golden triangle of mutually reinforcing protections.

**K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India** — A nine-judge bench unanimously recognised the right to privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21 with dimensions that encompass informational privacy, bodily autonomy, and dignitary protection, overruling earlier decisions and setting the framework for data protection and surveillance regulation.

**Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India** — The Court read down Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code, holding that criminalising consensual same-sex conduct violated Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21 and that sexual orientation is a protected characteristic under the Constitution's equality and dignity guarantees.

## Current significance

Surveillance and data protection law, internet shutdowns, and the constitutionality of laws regulating online speech continue to generate major Article 19 and 21 litigation. The Supreme Court's ongoing reference on sub-classification within Scheduled Caste reservations and the constitutionality of economic reservation touch directly on Articles 14, 15, and 16. Challenges to the legality of prolonged preventive detention under special laws and the scope of bail rights keep Article 21's procedural dimensions under close judicial scrutiny.
