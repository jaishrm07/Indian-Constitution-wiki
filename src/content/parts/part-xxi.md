## What this Part contains

Part XXI spans Articles 369 to 392 and is one of the most constitutionally significant and structurally heterogeneous Parts of the Constitution. It contains, under the heading "Temporary, Transitional and Special Provisions," a diverse set of articles covering: temporary parliamentary powers that have since lapsed (Articles 369 to 374); special provisions for individual states reflecting unique historical, political, or socio-economic circumstances (Articles 371 to 371J); the continuation of existing laws (Articles 372 and 372A); and the exercise of emergency and transitional powers in the early years of the Republic. The most politically prominent provision is Article 370, which gave Jammu and Kashmir a special status within the Union — a status that was effectively abrogated by Presidential Order in 2019 and subsequently affirmed by the Supreme Court in December 2023. Articles 371 to 371J contain special arrangements negotiated for states including Maharashtra and Gujarat, Nagaland, Assam, Manipur, Andhra Pradesh and Telangana, Sikkim, Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, and Karnataka.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 370** — Provided that only Article 1 and Article 370 itself applied automatically to Jammu and Kashmir, with all other provisions applicable only with the concurrence or consultation of the state’s Constituent Assembly or government; this article was effectively hollowed out by a Presidential Order in 2019 and the state’s statehood was simultaneously bifurcated into two Union Territories.
- **Article 371A** — Grants Nagaland special protections preventing Parliament from legislating on matters relating to the religious or social practices, customary law, and procedure, and land ownership of the Nagas, unless the Nagaland Legislative Assembly so decides.
- **Article 371F** — Contains special provisions for Sikkim, which acceded to India in 1975, including protections for the rights and interests of the Sikkim subject and the continuation of applicable laws in force.
- **Article 372** — Continues all laws in force in the territory of India immediately before the commencement of the Constitution until altered, repealed, or amended by a competent legislature, the foundational continuity provision for all pre-constitutional law.
- **Article 392** — Empowers the President to remove difficulties in the initial years of the Constitution’s operation, a sunset provision that permitted executive action to smooth the constitutional transition.

## Key cases

**In Re: Article 370 of the Constitution (Jammu and Kashmir case)** — The Supreme Court upheld the Presidential Orders of August 2019 that had effectively abrogated Article 370 and bifurcated Jammu and Kashmir into two Union Territories, holding that the President’s power under Article 370(3) to declare that Article 370 ceases to operate could be exercised without the Constituent Assembly’s recommendation since the Constituent Assembly had ceased to exist, but also directed the restoration of statehood to Jammu and Kashmir.

**Prem Nath Kaul v. State of Jammu and Kashmir** — The Supreme Court held in an early judgment that the legal framework of the then-princely state of Jammu and Kashmir, including land reform laws, was valid and that India’s constitutional relationship with the state under Article 370 was a unique arrangement that did not bring all central laws automatically into force.

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India** — In examining the continuity of pre-constitutional laws under Article 372, the Court affirmed that existing legislation carries constitutional force until amended or repealed, and that Parliament’s and state legislatures’ power over such laws is limited to the boundaries fixed by the Constitution.

## Current significance

The constitutional and political consequences of the effective abrogation of Article 370 and the bifurcation of Jammu and Kashmir remain live, with the Supreme Court’s direction to restore statehood yet to be implemented and a second round of litigation anticipated. The special provisions for individual states in Articles 371 to 371J periodically become subjects of political demand — for extension to other states or for renegotiation of their terms — engaging Part XXI in ongoing federal politics. The Sixth Schedule protections and the special provisions for north-eastern states interact with Part XXI’s architecture to create a complex map of differential constitutional treatment across the Indian Union.
