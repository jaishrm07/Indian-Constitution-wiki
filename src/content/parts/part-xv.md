## Why this Part matters

Electoral administration is constitutionally foundational. This Part ensures that elections are governed through a constitutional, not merely statutory, framework.

## How to read it

Readers should connect this Part to institutional independence, representation, delimitation, and the constitutional role of the Election Commission.

## What this Part contains

Part XV spans Articles 324 to 329A and constitutes the constitutional framework for elections to Parliament and state legislatures. Article 324 is the pivotal provision, vesting superintendence, direction, and control of all elections in the Election Commission of India and giving the Commission a residual power to fill gaps in election law. Articles 325 and 326 establish universal adult suffrage on the basis of a single general electoral roll with no discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, or sex. Parliament is empowered by Articles 327 and 328 to make provisions for elections, with Article 329 creating a bar on courts from questioning the validity of any law relating to delimitation or from interfering with ongoing election processes except through an election petition heard by a High Court. The constitutional status of the Election Commission and its three-member structure were significantly affected by the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act 2023, whose constitutional validity was challenged and remains under judicial consideration.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 324** — Vests the superintendence, direction, and control of the preparation of electoral rolls and the conduct of all elections to Parliament and state legislatures in the Election Commission, and empowers the Commission to give necessary directions in matters not covered by law.
- **Article 325** — Prohibits exclusion from the electoral roll of any person on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, or any of them, establishing a constitutional guarantee of non-discriminatory voter registration.
- **Article 326** — Guarantees universal adult suffrage, providing that elections shall be on the basis of adult suffrage — that is, every person who is a citizen and has attained the age of eighteen years shall be entitled to vote unless disqualified.
- **Article 327** — Empowers Parliament to make provision with respect to all matters relating to or in connection with elections to either House of Parliament or to the House or either House of the Legislature of a state.
- **Article 329** — Bars courts from calling in question any law relating to the delimitation of constituencies and requires that doubts and disputes arising out of or in connection with elections be decided only through the election petition mechanism.

## Key cases

**Election Commission of India v. Saka Venkata Rao** — The Court affirmed the width of Article 324's residual power, holding that the Election Commission can fill gaps in election law and act to ensure free and fair elections in situations the legislative draftsman did not anticipate, establishing the Commission's plenary character as a constitutional body.

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain** — The Supreme Court struck down the Thirty-Ninth Amendment which purported to oust judicial review of the Prime Minister's election, holding that free and fair elections are part of the basic structure and that the constitutional bar on election disputes in Article 329 cannot be extended to immunise the Prime Minister from judicial scrutiny through a constitutional amendment.

**Anoop Baranwal v. Union of India** — The Court ruled that Election Commissioners should be appointed by a committee comprising the Prime Minister, the Leader of the Opposition, and the Chief Justice of India pending a parliamentary law, directly engaging the constitutional independence of the Election Commission under Article 324; this ruling was effectively superseded by the 2023 Act which replaced the Chief Justice with a Cabinet Minister in the selection committee, and the constitutional validity of that Act is under challenge.

## Current significance

The appointment process for Election Commissioners established by the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act 2023 — which removed the Chief Justice from the selection committee — is under challenge before the Supreme Court as potentially compromising the constitutional independence required by Article 324. Proposed simultaneous elections to Parliament and state assemblies raise constitutional questions under Articles 327 and 328 about whether such synchronisation is permissible without amending the fixed five-year terms of legislatures. Voter data privacy, electoral bond financing, and the use of electoral rolls in welfare delivery schemes continue to generate litigation touching Part XV.
