## Why this Part matters

Federalism is not only a matter of political negotiation. Part XI supplies the constitutional architecture that decides who can legislate, administer, and coordinate across overlapping areas of governance.

## How to read it

Readers should connect this Part with the Seventh Schedule, fiscal federalism, and the case law that polices central overreach or clarifies legislative competence.

## What this Part contains

Part XI spans Articles 245 to 263 and is divided into two chapters — legislative relations (Articles 245 to 255) and administrative relations (Articles 256 to 263). The legislative relations chapter distributes law-making power between Parliament and state legislatures through the three lists of the Seventh Schedule: the Union List, the State List, and the Concurrent List, with Article 246 as the master provision. Parliament has residuary powers under Article 248 and can legislate on State List subjects in the national interest under Article 249 or in an emergency under Article 250. The administrative relations chapter imposes obligations on states to comply with Central laws and gives Parliament the power to extend Union executive powers to states by agreement, creating a collaborative but hierarchically structured federal administrative system.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 245** — Draws the territorial extent of parliamentary and state legislative power, establishing that Parliament may make laws for the whole or any part of the territory of India while state legislatures may make laws only for the state.
- **Article 246** — Assigns law-making powers according to the three-list Seventh Schedule, with Parliament supreme on the Union List, both Parliament and state legislatures competent on the Concurrent List, and states primarily competent on the State List.
- **Article 249** — Enables Parliament to legislate on a State List subject if the Rajya Sabha passes a resolution by a two-thirds majority declaring it necessary in the national interest, a provision that allows central expansion into state subjects without constitutional amendment.
- **Article 254** — Provides that in case of repugnancy between a state law and a central law on a Concurrent List subject, the central law shall prevail, but a state law reserved for Presidential assent may prevail over a central law for that state.
- **Article 263** — Authorises the President to establish an Inter-State Council for inquiring into and advising upon disputes between states or for investigating and discussing subjects of common interest, the provision under which the Inter-State Council was constituted.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India** — The Supreme Court rejected the West Bengal government's challenge to central acquisition of state-owned land, affirming the supremacy of Parliament's legislative power over mines and minerals and establishing that the Union's paramountcy in certain fields is absolute.

**ITC Ltd. v. Agricultural Produce Market Committee** — The Court elaborated the doctrine of pith and substance for resolving apparent conflicts between Union and State List entries, holding that the true character of a law must be examined to determine which list it falls under and that incidental encroachment on another list does not invalidate the law.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India** — While focused on Article 356, the Court's extensive analysis of Indian federalism drew on Part XI to characterise India as quasi-federal with a strong unitary pull, an analysis that continues to shape how the Union-State legislative and administrative relationship is understood.

## Current significance

The GST framework, implemented through Article 246A (inserted by the One Hundred and First Amendment), has reshaped the Union-State financial legislative relationship and generated ongoing disputes before the GST Council and courts about the boundaries of central and state taxing power. Climate legislation and environmental regulation frequently engage Article 246 and the Concurrent List to determine whether central environmental laws override state land-use and forest laws. Disputes about central schemes imposing conditions on state expenditure and the validity of centrally sponsored scheme conditionalities regularly raise questions about the limits of the Union's administrative authority under Part XI.
