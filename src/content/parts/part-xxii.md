## What this Part contains

Part XXII is the concluding Part of the Constitution, spanning Articles 393 to 395, and deals with the Constitution’s short title, date of commencement, authoritative text in Hindi, and repeals. Article 393 gives the document its name — the Constitution of India — while Article 394 establishes that the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950, a date chosen to coincide with the anniversary of the 1930 Declaration of Purna Swaraj. Article 394A, inserted by the Fifty-Eighth Amendment in 1987, provides that the President shall cause to be published under his authority a translation of the Constitution in the Hindi language, giving that translation authoritative status and ensuring that the constitutional text is accessible in both official languages of the Union. Article 395 repeals the Indian Independence Act 1947 and the Government of India Act 1935, thereby formally dissolving the legal continuity from colonial constitutional instruments to the new sovereign Constitution.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 393** — Gives the document its official name "the Constitution of India," a provision of symbolic constitutional significance establishing the text’s identity and self-referential status.
- **Article 394** — Declares the commencement date of the Constitution as 26 January 1950 and specifies which articles came into force earlier on 26 November 1949, the date of adoption.
- **Article 394A** — Authorises the President to publish an authoritative Hindi translation of the Constitution and to authorise translations in other languages specified in the Eighth Schedule, with the Devanagari version prevailing in case of doubt.
- **Article 395** — Repeals the Indian Independence Act 1947 and the Government of India Act 1935 along with enactments amending or supplementing that Act, completing the constitutional break from the colonial legal order.

## Key cases

**In Re: The Berubari Union case** — The Supreme Court considered the difference between Articles that came into force on 26 November 1949 and those that came into force on 26 January 1950 under Article 394, with implications for the legal status of territorial arrangements made in the interim period; the Court also addressed the commencement question in the context of whether the territory-cession treaty fell within the Constitution’s scope from 1950 or from an earlier date.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala** — The preamble to the Constitution, though not a Part, was considered alongside Part XXII’s declaration that the Constitution was "adopted, enacted and given to ourselves" by the people of India; the Court held that the preamble is part of the Constitution and its core values — sovereignty, democracy, republic — form elements of the basic structure.

**R.C. Poudyal v. Union of India** — The Court examined the constitutional commencement provisions and transitional arrangements when assessing the constitutional position of Sikkim, which became part of India after 26 January 1950, and the extent to which the commencement articles of Part XXII determined the scope of the Constitution’s initial application.

## Current significance

Part XXII carries less live doctrinal significance than most other Parts, but the authoritative Hindi text under Article 394A becomes significant whenever a constitutional provision’s meaning differs between the English and Hindi versions, requiring a resolution of which text governs. The repeal of the Government of India Act 1935 effected by Article 395 is occasionally revisited in historical and academic contexts to examine whether any colonial-era legal concepts were impliedly continued by the new constitutional order. The date of constitutional commencement in Article 394 is the anchor for calculating the transitional periods referenced across the constitutional text, making it a point of reference in cases involving time-limited constitutional provisions.
