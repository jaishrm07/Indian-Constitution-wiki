## Why this Part matters

Emergency powers can be constitutionally necessary and democratically dangerous at the same time. Their design and later judicial limits therefore deserve more scrutiny than ordinary provisions.

## How to read it

Readers should treat this Part not just as emergency text but as a recurring test of institutional restraint, federalism, and judicial review.

## What this Part contains

Part XVIII covers Articles 352 to 360 and provides three distinct emergency regimes for situations of national, state, and financial emergency. Article 352 governs national emergencies proclaimed on grounds of war, external aggression, or armed rebellion (previously "internal disturbance"), under which Parliament acquires plenary power to legislate on all subjects including the State List and fundamental rights under Articles 19 can be suspended. Article 356 empowers the President to impose President's Rule in a state if the state's constitutional machinery has failed, a provision that was used repeatedly and sometimes abusively before the Bommai judgment imposed judicial limits on it. Article 360 provides for a financial emergency when the financial stability or credit of India or any part thereof is threatened. The Forty-Fourth Amendment tightened Article 352 by requiring a Cabinet resolution and parliamentary approval by special majority, and explicitly protecting Articles 20 and 21 from suspension even during national emergencies.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 352** — Empowers the President to proclaim a national emergency on the ground of an imminent threat to the security of India by war, external aggression, or armed rebellion, with the consequence that Parliament can legislate on all subjects and fundamental rights under Article 19 stand suspended.
- **Article 356** — Enables the President to assume the functions of the state government if the President is satisfied, on the receipt of a Governor's report or otherwise, that the government of the state cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution.
- **Article 358** — Provides that during a national emergency, Article 19 rights are automatically suspended and no challenge to laws enacted during the emergency on the ground of Article 19 can be maintained.
- **Article 359** — Empowers the President, during a national emergency, to declare that the right to move any court for enforcement of fundamental rights other than Articles 20 and 21 shall remain suspended for the period of the emergency.
- **Article 360** — Provides for a proclamation of financial emergency when the financial stability or credit of India or any part thereof is threatened, enabling the Centre to direct states in financial matters and reduce salaries of all persons serving the Union and the states.

## Key cases

**A.D.M. Jabalpur v. Shiv Kant Shukla (Habeas Corpus case)** — During the 1975-77 Emergency, a majority of the Supreme Court held that the right to move for habeas corpus was suspended under the Presidential Order issued under Article 359, a ruling widely condemned as a capitulation to executive overreach and a primary driver of the Forty-Fourth Amendment's protection of Articles 20 and 21.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench held that the proclamation of President's Rule under Article 356 is justiciable, that the floor test is the only legitimate test of a government's majority, and that a state assembly cannot be dissolved before Parliament has approved the proclamation, fundamentally reforming the use of Article 356 and signalling that emergency powers are within the Constitution's control.

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India** — The Court struck down the Forty-Second Amendment's provision giving Article 356 proclamations immunity from judicial review, reaffirming that judicial oversight of emergency powers is part of the basic structure that cannot be abrogated by constitutional amendment.

## Current significance

The imposition of President's Rule in states — and the question of when a Governor's report to the President provides adequate constitutional grounds for it — continues to arise whenever coalition governments face floor test crises or ruling parties change in state elections. Internet shutdowns, which have been argued to fall within Article 352-related security powers, are increasingly challenged under Part III and scrutinised for whether they constitute disguised emergency measures. The constitutional limits on ordinance-making and executive power during declared emergencies, and the relationship between emergency powers and fundamental rights protections post-Forty-Fourth Amendment, remain unsettled at the edges.
