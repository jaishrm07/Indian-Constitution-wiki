## Why this Part matters

Part VI shows that Indian constitutionalism is not only national in practice. State institutions, including High Courts, carry much of the Constitution's day-to-day force.

## How to read it

This Part should be read together with federalism, writ jurisdiction, and the constitutional relationship between Union and State institutions.

## What this Part contains

Part VI runs from Articles 152 to 237 and mirrors the structure of Part V at the state level, providing a comprehensive constitutional framework for the state executive (Governor, Chief Minister, and Council of Ministers), state legislatures (both unicameral and bicameral), state finances, and High Courts. The Governor's role, defined in Articles 153 to 161, has been one of the most contested areas of constitutional practice, touching on the appointment of Chief Ministers, reservation of bills for Presidential assent, and the invocation of Article 356 — all of which arise from the relationship between Articles 163 and 164. The High Court chapter in Articles 214 to 232 grants each state court supervisory jurisdiction over all subordinate courts and tribunals within its territory, and Article 226 confers a writ jurisdiction that is in some ways wider than the Supreme Court's under Article 32 because it extends to rights beyond the fundamental rights listed in Part III.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 163** — Provides that there shall be a Council of Ministers to aid and advise the Governor, except in functions where the Governor is required to act in his discretion, setting up an enduring tension between gubernatorial discretion and cabinet government.
- **Article 200** — Governs the Governor's power to assent to, withhold assent from, or reserve a bill for Presidential consideration; the withholding of assent and indefinite reservation of bills has generated sustained litigation and political controversy.
- **Article 213** — Empowers the Governor to promulgate ordinances when the state legislature is not in session, a power that mirrors the President's ordinance-making authority under Article 123 and is subject to similar doctrinal limits.
- **Article 226** — Grants every High Court the power to issue writs, orders, and directions for the enforcement of fundamental rights and for any other purpose, giving state courts an expansive remedial jurisdiction that drives a large portion of constitutional litigation in India.
- **Article 227** — Confers superintendence over all courts and tribunals within the state's territorial jurisdiction on the High Court, a supervisory power that has been used to correct jurisdictional errors and ensure procedural compliance throughout the subordinate judiciary.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab** — The Supreme Court held that the Governor must in virtually all matters act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers and that the grant of discretionary powers to the Governor must be construed narrowly, reinforcing the principle of parliamentary government at the state level.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh** — The Court quashed a Governor's order summoning the Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly out of turn, holding that the Governor cannot act independently of ministerial advice on questions concerning the timing of Assembly sessions and that such unilateral action violates the constitutional scheme of Part VI.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India** — In assessing the validity of simultaneous proclamations of President's Rule across multiple states, the Court laid down that the Governor's report recommending Article 356 action must be based on relevant material and that the satisfaction of the President is justiciable, a principle that deeply shaped the interpretation of gubernatorial and Presidential power under Part VI.

## Current significance

Disputes between Governors and state governments over assent to bills, appointment of Vice-Chancellors, and the timing of floor tests have produced a stream of constitutional litigation that directly engages Articles 163, 200, and 213. The Supreme Court has heard and decided multiple cases arising from Governors reserving state bills for Presidential assent for extended periods, and the question of whether such reservation can be made in perpetuity remains unsettled. High Court writ jurisdiction under Article 226 faces persistent questions about its scope in relation to arbitral awards, tax administration actions, and private disputes involving state instrumentalities.
