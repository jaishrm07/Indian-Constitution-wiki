## Editorial status

This Part page is maintained as a structural hub for the constitutional text. It is designed to keep every Article in the Part connected to doctrine, institutions, amendments, and live constitutional disputes as the corpus expands.

## Reading note

Use this page to move from the Constitution’s high-level structure to the individual Articles that operationalise it.

## What this Part contains

Part XIX spans Articles 361 to 367 and contains a miscellaneous collection of provisions that do not fit neatly elsewhere in the constitutional scheme. Article 361 grants the President and Governors immunity from court proceedings for acts done or purported to be done in the exercise of their official powers. Articles 361A and 361B were added to protect persons from prosecution for publishing substantially true reports of parliamentary proceedings and to disqualify legislators who hold offices of profit. Article 363 bars courts from entertaining disputes arising from treaties and agreements with princely rulers concluded before commencement, a provision that interacts with the integration of the princely order. Article 366 is the Constitution’s definitional section — a long article that defines key terms used throughout the document — while Article 367 provides general rules for interpretation of the constitutional text including the rule that the General Clauses Act applies unless the context otherwise requires.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 361** — Grants the President and each Governor immunity from court proceedings while in office for official acts and from personal presence or process of court during their term, though it does not exempt them from parliamentary accountability.
- **Article 363** — Bars courts from entertaining disputes relating to rights and obligations arising from treaties, agreements, covenants, or other instruments with Rulers of Indian States, placing this class of disputes beyond judicial cognisance.
- **Article 365** — Provides that where a state fails to comply with or give effect to any provisions of the Constitution or any direction given by the Union, it shall be lawful for the President to hold that a situation has arisen in which the government of the state cannot be carried on in accordance with the Constitution.
- **Article 366** — Contains the Constitution’s master definitions, including the definitions of "law," "State," "Union territory," "Scheduled Castes," and "Scheduled Tribes," terms that operate throughout the constitutional text.
- **Article 367** — Provides interpretive rules including that references to Acts of Parliament or Governor-General’s Order are to be construed accordingly and that the General Clauses Act 1897 applies to the interpretation of the Constitution.

## Key cases

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India** — The Supreme Court addressed whether Article 361’s immunity to the Governor extends to acts done in an official capacity that are motivated by mala fides, holding that the immunity is absolute for official acts but that constitutional review of the legal consequences of those acts is not thereby excluded.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India** — In the context of Article 365, the Court confirmed that this article does not by itself trigger Article 356 but must be read alongside it, and that non-compliance with Union directions is a fact that the President can consider when forming the satisfaction required for President’s Rule.

**State of J&K v. A.R. Zakki** — The Court analysed the definitional reach of Article 366 in the context of identifying what constitutes a "law" for the purposes of determining constitutional validity under Article 13, illustrating how the miscellaneous provisions of Part XIX carry significant interpretive weight throughout the Constitution.

## Current significance

Article 361’s immunity for Governors has attracted renewed scrutiny in cases where Governors are accused of acting with political motivation against elected state governments, with courts asked to determine whether acts framed as official are truly within the immunity’s scope. The scope of "law" as defined in Article 366 is at issue in debates about whether executive orders, constitutional customs, and administrative circulars can be challenged under Part III. Article 367’s interpretive provisions are engaged whenever courts must determine whether particular constitutional terms should be given their general legal meaning under the General Clauses Act or a contextual constitutional meaning.
