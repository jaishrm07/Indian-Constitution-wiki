## What this Part contains

Part II spans Articles 5 to 11 and deals exclusively with citizenship at the commencement of the Constitution on 26 January 1950. The provisions address persons domiciled in India, persons who migrated from Pakistan (both ways), persons of Indian origin residing abroad, and the continuing validity of citizenship acquired before commencement. Unlike most other Parts, Part II is essentially transitional — it does not create an ongoing statutory citizenship code but instead sets out who was a citizen on day one and then delegates to Parliament, through Article 11, the plenary power to regulate acquisition and termination of citizenship by legislation. The Citizenship Act 1955 and its subsequent amendments, including the deeply contested Citizenship Amendment Act 2019, derive their constitutional authority from Article 11.

## Key constitutional provisions

- **Article 5** — Grants citizenship to persons domiciled in India at commencement, provided they or at least one parent were born in India, or had been ordinarily resident for at least five years.
- **Article 6** — Deals with persons who migrated to India from Pakistan, distinguishing those whose parents or grandparents were born in undivided India and conditioning citizenship on registration where migration occurred after 19 July 1948.
- **Article 9** — Declares that no person shall be a citizen of India if they have voluntarily acquired the citizenship of any foreign state, establishing the general rule against dual nationality.
- **Article 10** — Guarantees that every person who is or is deemed to be a citizen continues to be a citizen subject to any law made by Parliament.
- **Article 11** — Vests Parliament with full power to make any provision with respect to the acquisition and termination of citizenship and all other matters relating to citizenship, effectively making this Part a constitutional foundation for ordinary legislation.

## Key cases

**Izhar Ahmad Khan v. Union of India** — The Supreme Court examined the interaction between Article 9 and the Citizenship Act, affirming that voluntary acquisition of foreign nationality operates automatically to terminate Indian citizenship without any formal state action.

**Pradeep Jain v. Union of India** — The Court addressed citizenship and domicile in the context of professional admissions, reinforcing that the constitutional text of Part II ties citizenship closely to territorial connection and that Parliament’s power under Article 11 is wide enough to define residence and domicile for citizenship purposes.

**Citizenship Amendment Act cases (pending)** — Multiple petitions before the Supreme Court challenge the Citizenship Amendment Act 2019 on the ground that its religion-based classification for expedited citizenship violates Articles 14 and 15 read with Part II’s underlying premise that citizenship determination must be secular and non-discriminatory.

## Current significance

The Citizenship Amendment Act 2019, which provides an expedited pathway to Indian citizenship for non-Muslim refugees from three neighbouring countries, continues to be the subject of major litigation testing whether religion can be a permissible classification criterion under Article 11’s delegated power. Stateless persons held in long-term detention pending deportation orders raise questions about whether the right to be treated as a citizen under Article 10, once established, can be administratively undone. The National Register of Citizens process in Assam has sharpened debates about evidentiary standards for proving citizenship under Articles 5 and 6.
