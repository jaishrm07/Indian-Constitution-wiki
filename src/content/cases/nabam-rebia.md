## Background

In late 2015, Arunachal Pradesh was engulfed in a political crisis when a faction of rebel Congress MLAs, with support from the BJP, sought to remove Speaker Nabam Rebia by tabling a notice of resolution against him. The Governor, Jyoti Prasad Rajkhowa, took a series of unilateral actions: he advanced the date of the Assembly session by over a month without the advice of the Council of Ministers, and he altered the agenda of the session to include the motion against the Speaker as the first item of business. The elected Congress government challenged these actions as unconstitutional intrusions into the domain of responsible government. President's Rule was subsequently imposed, which was itself challenged before the Supreme Court. A five-judge Constitution Bench was constituted to address the constitutional questions raised by the Governor's conduct and the events surrounding the collapse of the state government.

## The legal question in detail

The primary constitutional question was whether the Governor of a State can exercise the power under Article 174 — to summon, prorogue, or dissolve the Assembly — at personal discretion, or whether that power must be exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers as required by Article 163. A subsidiary question concerned whether the Governor could alter the agenda of a session once summoned, and whether doing so to enable a motion against the Speaker was constitutionally permissible. The case required the court to draw the line between the Governor's genuine discretionary functions, such as the appointment of a Chief Minister where no single party commands a majority, and powers that the text and structure of the Constitution require to be exercised on ministerial advice.

## What the court decided

The Constitution Bench unanimously held that the Governor's power under Article 174 to summon and prorogue the Legislature is exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers and is not a matter of the Governor's personal discretion. The Governor could not advance the session date or alter the business agenda without a valid recommendation from the elected government, and the actions taken in Arunachal Pradesh were accordingly held to be unconstitutional. The Court restored the status quo ante, reinstating the position of the Speaker and setting aside the crisis-era executive actions including the Presidential Proclamation under Article 356. The judgment reaffirmed that the Governor is a constitutional head whose formal powers operate through the filter of cabinet responsibility, and that the office may not be used as an instrument of central intervention in state politics. The ruling is a direct application of the principle that it is elected governments, not appointed Governors, that control the legislative calendar of a State.

## Why the case remains central

Whenever a Governor's formal power is invoked during a state-level political crisis, Nabam Rebia becomes one of the first cases to read. Its authority extends beyond Arunachal Pradesh and establishes a firm rule that Article 174 cannot be weaponised to destabilise a sitting government.

## Long-term impact

The judgment strengthened the idea that constitutional offices must operate within democratic structure, not around it. It has since been cited in challenges arising in Maharashtra, Goa, and other states where Governors have been accused of exercising discretion in favour of one political faction over another.
