## Background

The National Capital Territory of Delhi occupies a unique and contested position in Indian constitutional law. It is not a full State, but unlike other Union Territories it has a directly elected Legislative Assembly and a Council of Ministers responsible to that Assembly. This special framework was introduced by the 69th Constitutional Amendment, which inserted Article 239AA into the Constitution with effect from 1992. The Article granted Delhi an elected legislature with power over List II (State List) subjects except three: public order, police, and land, which were reserved for the Union. A Lieutenant Governor was designated as Delhi's administrator, appointed by the President and exercising authority in the name of the Union.

From the early 2000s onward, and with particular intensity after the Aam Aadmi Party formed the government in 2015, a serious constitutional dispute emerged over the practical relationship between the elected Delhi government and the LG. The LG began referring a large volume of decisions taken by the Council of Ministers to the President for his consideration, effectively stalling governance. The elected government took the position that under Article 239AA read with Article 74 (the aid-and-advice requirement at the Union level, which by analogy applies to delegated administrators), the LG was a constitutional figurehead obliged to act on ministerial advice, not an independent executive authority. The LG and the Union of India maintained the opposite: that the LG represented the President and could exercise broad independent discretion, and that Delhi's special status meant the elected government's authority was far more constrained than that of a State government. The constitutional impasse eventually produced a reference to a five-judge Constitution Bench.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was the proper constitutional relationship between the LG and Delhi's elected Council of Ministers under Article 239AA. More specifically: when Article 239AA(4) states that in certain cases the LG may refer a matter to the President where he disagrees with the Council of Ministers, is that a limited exception or a broad independent power? Does the elected government require the LG's concurrence before acting, or is it entitled to act on its own on all matters within the Assembly's competence, informing but not seeking approval from the LG? The case required the Court to decide whether the purpose of Article 239AA was to establish genuine representative government in Delhi — analogous, with appropriate modifications, to a State government — or whether it was primarily an instrument for Union control with a democratic facade. Connected questions included whether the "aid and advice" principle familiar from Articles 74 and 163 applied by analogy to the LG's relationship with the Delhi Council of Ministers, and whether constitutional offices can be used to paralyse elected governance.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench delivered a unanimous judgment authored by Chief Justice Dipak Misra, with concurrences from Justices A.K. Sikri, A.M. Khanwilkar, D.Y. Chandrachud, and Ashok Bhushan. The Court held that Article 239AA must be read in light of its democratic purpose. The insertion of an elected Assembly and a responsible Council of Ministers into Delhi's governance framework was intended to deliver representative government to Delhi's citizens, not merely to create an advisory body subordinate to an unelected LG. The Court laid down that the LG is bound by the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers for all matters falling within the Delhi Assembly's legislative competence. He cannot act independently on those subjects. His role is analogous to that of a Governor under Article 163 — a constitutional head who formally receives decisions, may communicate concerns to the government, and in cases of genuine disagreement may refer the matter to the President under Article 239AA(4), but who may not in the ordinary course substitute his own judgment for that of the elected government. The Court also gave guidance on the three reserved subjects — police, public order, and land — which remain with the Centre and are therefore not within the elected Delhi government's domain. The practical takeaway was that for the bulk of governance — health, education, public services, water, infrastructure — the elected government was constitutionally entitled to govern without requiring the LG's concurrence at each step. The LG could express reservations and refer matters to the President in genuine cases of disagreement, but this was meant to be an exceptional mechanism, not the normal operating mode.

## The 2023 services sequel

The 2018 judgment left open one significant question: which government — Delhi's elected Council of Ministers or the Union through the LG — controlled transfers and postings of the higher bureaucracy (Group A officers) who actually administered Delhi's day-to-day governance. This question returned to the Constitution Bench in 2023, culminating in the judgment in Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (Services). The five-judge Bench held in May 2023 that "services" (excluding police, public order, and land) were within the elected government's domain and that the LG was bound by its advice on transfer and posting of officers. Parliament responded swiftly by passing the Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi (Amendment) Act, 2023, which reversed the judgment and legislatively vested control over services in the LG. The constitutional validity of this legislative reversal remains a live question.

## Why the case remains central

The 2018 judgment is the foundational statement on Article 239AA's constitutional purpose and architecture. Its reasoning on representative government, the limits of LG discretion, the analogy with aid-and-advice doctrine, and the interpretation of Article 239AA(4) continues to govern the analysis of Delhi's constitutional status, even after the legislative developments of 2023. Every serious engagement with Delhi governance — whether by practitioners, courts, or students — must begin with this judgment.

## Long-term impact

The judgment has wider significance beyond Delhi. It contributed to the evolving jurisprudence on the relationship between appointed constitutional officers (Governors, Administrators) and elected governments, a question of intense practical relevance across multiple States. Its statement that constitutional offices may not be used to obstruct democratic governance has been cited in challenges to Governor conduct in States including Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Punjab. It also established that constitutional provisions creating hybrid governance frameworks must be interpreted in light of their democratic purpose, not construed in favour of the appointed authority.
