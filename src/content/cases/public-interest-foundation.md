## Background

Despite the Association for Democratic Reforms judgment of 2002 and the PUCL ruling of 2003 requiring candidates to disclose criminal antecedents, financial assets, and educational qualifications in their nomination affidavits, the share of MPs and MLAs with declared pending criminal cases continued to grow with each successive election cycle. Public Interest Foundation and other petitioners returned to the Supreme Court seeking directions compelling political parties to explain why they had chosen candidates with serious criminal antecedents, and ultimately asking the Court to bar such candidates from standing altogether. The case brought together the earlier disclosure regime and the larger question of whether the judiciary could itself construct a disqualification rule that Parliament had declined to enact.

## The legal question in detail

The primary legal question was whether political parties' selection of candidates with serious criminal charges could be regulated by court-directed disclosure obligations, and what remedies were available short of a judicial bar on such candidates — which would require altering the statutory qualifications in the Representation of the People Act, a task the Court acknowledged belonged to Parliament. The petitioners argued that the right to vote under Article 326, when read with Articles 19(1)(a) and 21, implies a right to vote for candidates whose backgrounds are fully known and who are not facing grave criminal charges. The state and the Election Commission argued that disqualification criteria are a matter for Parliament and that judicial creation of new bars would violate the separation of powers.

## What the court decided

A Constitution Bench held that while the Court could not itself bar criminally charged candidates from contesting elections — creating such a bar would require legislative action amending the Representation of the People Act — there was constitutional space to impose stringent disclosure obligations on political parties that went beyond the existing nominee-affidavit regime. The Court directed political parties to publish on their websites, in local and national newspapers, and on social media the reasons for selecting each candidate with a pending criminal case involving a sentence of two years or more, within forty-eight hours of that selection being announced. Parties were required to file compliance affidavits with the Election Commission, and non-compliance was to be treated as contempt of court. The Court expressed the hope — and a degree of expectation — that Parliament would legislate to bar candidates facing serious charges from standing for office, but it was clear that the Court could not substitute its judgment for Parliament's on the content of disqualification law.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment is the bridge between voter-information cases and disqualification doctrine. It accepts that criminalisation of politics is a constitutional problem that the existing disclosure framework has not solved, but it leaves the architecture of disqualification to the legislature unless the Constitution itself says otherwise. The Court's unusual step of addressing its hopes to Parliament directly underscores the institutional tension the case exposes.

## Long-term impact

The case still anchors later debates about candidate affidavits, criminal antecedents, and the limits of judicially crafted eligibility rules in a representative democracy. Compliance monitoring before the Supreme Court has continued through successive election cycles, keeping the issue alive in constitutional litigation and in political discourse about electoral reform.
