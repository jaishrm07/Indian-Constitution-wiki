## Background

The immediate context for this litigation was the conduct of Tamil Nadu Governor R.N. Ravi, who declined to act on ten bills passed by the Tamil Nadu Legislative Assembly. The bills covered subjects within the State Legislature's competence — several related to universities, higher education regulation, and local governance — and had been presented to the Governor for assent in the normal way. Instead of assenting, returning the bills with objections as Article 200 permits, or reserving them for the President's consideration under Article 201, the Governor simply held them. He neither accepted nor rejected nor referred; he sat on them for months and in some cases for over a year. The state government of Tamil Nadu took the position that this conduct was constitutionally impermissible and filed a writ petition before the Supreme Court seeking directions. The dispute crystallised a recurring friction between elected state governments and Governors appointed by the Union — a friction that had also surfaced in Kerala, Punjab, Telangana, and other States where Governors had withheld action on state legislation for extended periods.

The case came before the Supreme Court as a constitutional question of the first order: whether the powers conferred on a Governor by Articles 200 and 201 are subject to any time discipline, whether judicial review extends to gubernatorial inaction, and whether a court can treat a bill as having received assent when a Governor has indefinitely failed to act. The Constitution Bench was assembled to resolve these questions with finality.

## The legal question in detail

Articles 200 and 201 of the Constitution describe the options available to a Governor when a bill passed by the State Legislature is presented for assent. Under Article 200, the Governor may assent, withhold assent, or reserve the bill for the President's consideration. If the Governor withholds assent and returns the bill to the Legislature with a message, and the Legislature repasses it with or without amendments, Article 200 further provides that the Governor shall give assent to the bill. Under Article 201, if a bill is reserved for the President, the President may assent, withhold assent, or direct the Governor to return it to the Legislature.

The constitutional problem exposed by the Tamil Nadu dispute was that neither Article 200 nor Article 201 specifies any time limit within which the Governor must make a choice. The absence of an express deadline had been exploited to convert the assent power into a pocket veto: by neither assenting nor formally acting, a Governor could neutralise state legislation for as long as the political standoff lasted. The questions for the Court were: whether the absence of an explicit time limit meant there was no constitutional obligation to act promptly; whether the Governor's inaction was a "decision" amenable to judicial review; whether the Court under Article 142 could supply the remedy of deemed assent if an unreasonable period of inaction passed; and whether the power to reserve bills for the President could itself be exercised after the Legislature had already repassed the bill following the Governor's withholding — in other words, whether serial reservation was constitutionally permissible.

## What the court decided

Justice J.B. Pardiwala, writing for the Constitution Bench, held that the constitutional scheme of Articles 200 and 201 is premised on timely action. A Governor who pockets a bill — who neither assents, withholds, nor reserves — violates the constitutional framework because the scheme contemplates a choice, not silence. The Court drew on the structure of Article 200 itself: it provides a complete procedural cycle (assent, withhold and return, reserve) that presupposes the Governor will act. Open-ended inaction is not one of the options the Article makes available.

The Court then addressed the remedy. It held that when a Governor fails to act on a bill within a reasonable period, courts can apply the doctrine of deemed assent: treating the bill as having received assent as a matter of constitutional law, exercising the Court's power under Article 142 to do complete justice. The three-month period was identified as the outer constitutional limit within which a Governor must act on a bill after it is presented. Inaction beyond three months without a constitutionally valid reason would attract the deemed-assent remedy.

The Court also addressed the specific question of bills that had been repassed by the Legislature after the Governor withheld assent. It held that once the Legislature repasses a bill under Article 200, the Governor's role is ministerial: he is constitutionally obliged to give assent. He cannot at that stage reserve the repassed bill for the President's consideration, because that option was available before the return, not after the Legislature has exercised its right to repass. The Court exercised Article 142 powers to declare that the ten withheld Tamil Nadu bills had received deemed assent, effectively converting the Governor's prolonged inaction into a legal nullity.

## Why the case remains central

The case converted a recurrent political standoff into a doctrinal question about constitutional time, federal balance, and the meaning of assent powers. For the first time, the Supreme Court gave precise content to the temporal obligations that structure of Articles 200 and 201 implies. The three-month limit is not merely a guideline — it is the outer boundary of permissible gubernatorial inaction, enforceable through the deemed-assent doctrine. The ruling also reinforced the principle, established in S.R. Bommai and carried forward through a series of Governor-discretion cases, that constitutional offices are not sites of unchecked personal judgment. A Governor who attempts to pocket state legislation now acts against a settled constitutional backdrop.

The judgment is also important for its use of Article 142. By deploying that power to declare bills as having received deemed assent, the Court demonstrated that constitutional inaction — not merely unconstitutional action — is justiciable and remediable. This extends the reach of judicial review into a zone that had previously seemed unreachable.

## Long-term impact

Future disputes over Governors, state bills, and Presidential reservation will now be measured against this ruling's insistence that constitutional silence is not neutral. State governments across the country have already cited the judgment in correspondence with their respective Governors, and the three-month limit has entered constitutional practice as a hard outer boundary. The deemed-assent doctrine, though developed under Article 142 for these facts, sets the precedent for similar remedies in future legislative-executive standoffs at the state level. The case has also sharpened the doctrinal question — not resolved here — of whether the President's power under Article 201 to withhold assent is subject to the same constitutional discipline, a question likely to arise in future litigation.
