## Background

Following the Second Judges Case (1993), the collegium — the CJI and the four senior-most Supreme Court judges — had operated as the effective appointing authority for the higher judiciary for over two decades. Criticism of the collegium mounted over time: appointments were made with minimal transparency, the process was perceived as self-selecting and insular, and the collegium had no mechanism for accepting public input or explaining rejections. The 99th Constitutional Amendment (2014) replaced the collegium with a National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC), consisting of the CJI, the two senior-most Supreme Court judges, the Union Law Minister, and two eminent persons chosen by a committee of the CJI, the Prime Minister, and the Leader of the Opposition. Parliament and nearly every state legislature ratified the amendment within months. The Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association and others challenged the amendment immediately, arguing that it violated the basic structure of the Constitution by destroying judicial independence.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether the 99th Amendment violated the basic structure doctrine by altering the composition of the appointing authority in a manner that compromised judicial independence. The challengers argued that giving the Law Minister a formal role — along with two lay eminent persons who could between them veto any appointment — created structural conditions under which the executive could block nominees the judiciary favoured. The government countered that the collegium had operated without accountability for twenty years and that the NJAC introduced democratic legitimacy without removing judicial participation. The court also had to decide whether judicial independence in the appointments sphere was itself a basic structure element, a question that the Second Judges Case had left partly open.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench struck down both the 99th Amendment and the NJAC Act, 2014 by a majority of four to one, with Justice Chelameswar dissenting. The majority held that judicial independence, including the primacy of the judiciary in appointments, is a component of the basic structure and cannot be abolished even by a constitutional amendment. The veto power of any two NJAC members — which could allow the Law Minister and one eminent person to block a judicial nominee — was identified as a structural defect capable of subordinating judicial preferences to executive will. The Court acknowledged that the collegium system was opaque, suffered from lack of accountability, and needed improvement, but held that the proper response was collegium reform rather than structural replacement. Following the judgment, the Court invited memoranda on improvements to the collegium's working, and subsequent orders sought to introduce greater transparency through the publication of minutes and the creation of a formal secretariat.

## Why the case remains central

This is the constitutional anchor for every modern dispute about how judges are selected and how much control elected branches may exercise over that process. By striking down a duly enacted and ratified constitutional amendment, the judgment illustrates both the power and the contested legitimacy of the basic structure doctrine.

## Long-term impact

Current debates on collegium reform, transparency, and judicial accountability still operate within the doctrinal frame established here. The judgment's implicit bargain — that the collegium survives, but must become more open — remains only partly fulfilled, and periodic political friction over specific appointments keeps this case at the centre of constitutional discourse.
