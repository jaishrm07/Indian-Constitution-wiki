## Background

A private medical college in Andhra Pradesh was charging capitation fees — large one-time payments demanded from students seeking admission, effectively allowing wealthy applicants to buy their way into professional education. J.P. Unni Krishnan challenged this practice and the broader question of whether private unaided educational institutions could operate outside any constitutional framework of accountability. The case also raised a deeper structural question that had not been definitively resolved: whether the right to education was a fundamental right implied by the Constitution, decades before the 86th Amendment explicitly inserted it as Article 21A in 2002. The petitioners argued that if Article 21 protected a right to life and personal liberty with sufficient breadth to encompass human dignity, it must also encompass access to the education necessary to make that dignity real. This argument required the Court to read Article 21 together with the Directive Principles in Articles 41 and 45, which directed the state to provide free and compulsory education for children up to the age of fourteen within a specified period.

## The legal question in detail

The primary constitutional question was whether the right to education, at least at the elementary level, was a fundamental right derivable from Article 21 read together with Directive Principles under Articles 41 and 45, which directed the state to secure the right to education and to provide free and compulsory education for children. A secondary question was whether private educational institutions engaged in commercialisation — charging capitation fees and other exorbitant amounts — could continue operating without constitutional constraint, or whether the state had both the power and obligation to regulate them. The case also raised the broader methodological question of how far the Court could use Directive Principles as interpretive tools to expand the content of fundamental rights, a question with implications well beyond education. The government's position was complicated by the fact that it had not fulfilled its own obligation under Article 45 to provide free elementary education, making the state simultaneously a defender of regulatory authority over private institutions and a defaulter on its own constitutional obligation.

## What the court decided

A five-judge bench held that the right to education up to the age of 14 is a fundamental right flowing from Article 21 read with Article 45, establishing for the first time a judicially enforceable constitutional entitlement to elementary education ahead of any textual amendment. The judgment struck down capitation fees as exploitative and inconsistent with the constitutional character of education, holding that education cannot be treated as a commodity to be allocated to those who can pay the most. The Court also laid down a detailed "scheme" governing admissions and fees for private educational institutions, dividing seats between government nominees and the institution's own selections and prescribing a framework for what charges could be levied. That scheme was later substantially modified — and partly overruled — by the eleven-judge bench in T.M.A. Pai Foundation, which found the Unni Krishnan framework too restrictive of private institutional autonomy. However, the core recognition of education as a constitutionally protected right survived this correction and provided the doctrinal foundation that eventually led Parliament to pass the 86th Amendment in 2002, inserting Article 21A and making elementary education a textual fundamental right.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment is an important bridge between rights and directive principles, demonstrating how the Court used interpretive creativity to extend constitutional protection to social interests that the original text did not explicitly guarantee as rights. It shows how social rights arguments entered constitutional doctrine through judicial interpretation before the text was amended to reflect them, illustrating the dynamic relationship between adjudication and constitutional development. The case also raised the still-contested question of how far the state can regulate private educational enterprise when education serves both individual opportunity and public good.

## Long-term impact

Modern debates over school access, state obligations, and Article 21A make more sense when read against Unni Krishnan's attempt to constitutionalise education through interpretive reasoning rather than amendment. The judgment established the principle that the state cannot treat education as a sphere of unconstrained private commerce, even if the precise regulatory framework it created was subsequently revised. Its long-term significance lies less in the specific scheme it prescribed — much of which was overruled — and more in the constitutional proposition it established: that education is not a luxury or a privilege but a right that the Constitution protects because it is inseparable from the dignified life that Article 21 is meant to secure.
