## Background

The Shirur Mutt was a religious institution of the Madhva sect in South India. The Madras Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act, 1951 empowered the state government to appoint a Commissioner to supervise Hindu religious institutions and endowments, and gave him wide authority over the management, finances, and administration of mutts, temples, and their associated properties. The head of the Shirur Mutt, Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar, challenged the Act as an unconstitutional interference with religious freedom, arguing that the powers conferred on the Commissioner — including control over the mutt's funds, the power to remove functionaries, and the authority to approve expenditure — invaded the protected domain of religious denomination and management guaranteed by Articles 25 and 26. The case reached a seven-judge Constitution Bench, which was asked to lay down the constitutional framework for distinguishing state regulation of religion from state administration of religion.

## The legal question

The constitutional question was how to draw the boundary between the state's power to regulate the secular aspects of religious institutions and the constitutional protection given to a religious denomination's right to manage its own affairs in matters of religion under Article 26(b). Article 25 guarantees the freedom to profess, practise, and propagate religion, subject to public order, morality, health, and other provisions of the Constitution; Article 26 protects every religious denomination's right to manage its own affairs in matters of religion. The central difficulty was identifying what counted as a "matter of religion" — a question the Constitution left undefined. A subsidiary question was whether the state could appoint a Commissioner with effectively controlling authority over a religious institution's finances and administration without crossing from regulation into management, which Article 26 reserved exclusively to the denomination.

## What the court decided

The Constitution Bench held that Articles 25 and 26 protect not only core doctrinal beliefs but also practices that are regarded by the denomination itself as essential parts of its religion, giving rise to what became known as the "essential religious practices" doctrine. The Court held that whether a practice is essential to a religion must be determined primarily by reference to the doctrines and tenets of the religion itself, and courts must look to scriptural authority and the beliefs of the community rather than impose an external secular assessment. On the facts, the Court struck down several provisions of the Madras Act that gave the Commissioner powers of actual management over the religious affairs of the Mutt — including control over the internal organisation and the appointment and removal of religious functionaries — as unconstitutional infringement of Article 26(b). The Court drew a distinction between the state regulating the secular administration of religious property and endowments (permissible) and the state controlling how a denomination organises its religious life and chooses its own religious managers (not permissible). This religion-versus-secular-administration line became the foundational doctrinal framework for all subsequent cases involving state regulation of religious institutions.

## Significance

Shirur Mutt is the originating source of the essential religious practices doctrine, the most important and most contested analytical tool in Indian religious freedom law. By holding that courts must assess which practices are essential to a religion in order to determine the scope of constitutional protection, the judgment gave courts a significant role in defining religion — a role that has attracted sustained scholarly criticism and produced difficult subsequent decisions. The religion-versus-secular-administration distinction it drew has been applied in cases involving temple administration, waqf management, and the authority of denominational schools. Almost every subsequent Article 25 and 26 case begins by invoking Shirur Mutt's framework, even when it ultimately departs from it.

## Why the case remains central

Most serious arguments about religious denomination, protected religious affairs, and state regulation still pass through this case in some form.

## Long-term impact

Even where later judgments qualify or contest parts of its reasoning — particularly the judicial determination of which practices are "essential" to a religion — Shirur Mutt continues to structure the vocabulary of religion cases in constitutional law. It was applied and elaborated in cases involving the Ananda Marga, the Dargah Khwaja Saheb, and the Sabarimala Temple, among many others. The Sabarimala reference judgment explicitly debated whether the essential practices doctrine articulated here should be retained, modified, or abandoned, illustrating how much the case's framework still commands engagement even among those who question its methodology.
