## Background

Kesavananda Bharati, the head of the Edneer Mutt in Kerala, challenged state land reform laws that threatened the mutt's properties under Articles 25 and 26, which protect religious freedom and the right to manage religious affairs. Kerala placed the impugned laws in the Ninth Schedule through the 29th Constitutional Amendment, shielding them from challenge under Article 13. The case arose in the shadow of the Supreme Court's 1967 ruling in Golaknath v. State of Punjab, which had held that Fundamental Rights were wholly outside Parliament's amending power — a holding that created immediate constitutional turmoil. Parliament responded by enacting the 24th Amendment (asserting plenary amending power under Article 368), the 25th Amendment (restricting property rights further and inserting Article 31C to shield Directive Principle-implementing laws from rights challenge), and the 29th Amendment (adding the Kerala land reform laws to the Ninth Schedule). The Union and State governments needed a definitive answer on whether constituent power was absolute. A 13-judge Constitution Bench — the largest in the history of the Supreme Court of India — was assembled specifically to reconsider Golaknath and resolve the contest between Parliament's sovereignty and constitutional limits. The hearing lasted five months and produced eleven separate opinions running to nearly 700 pages.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether Article 368 conferred unlimited power on Parliament to amend any provision of the Constitution, including the Fundamental Rights in Part III. The Union of India argued that Articles 368(1) and 368(2), read with the 24th Amendment, gave Parliament plenary constituent power: the power to amend being sovereign, no provision was beyond its reach. The petitioners countered that certain core features of the Constitution were inherent limits on amendatory power — Parliament could alter the text but could not use that power to destroy the identity of the Constitution itself. A subsidiary question was whether the 24th, 25th, and 29th Amendments were validly enacted, and whether Article 31C (which shielded laws implementing Directive Principles from rights challenge) was constitutionally permissible. Deeper still lay the question of the nature of the Indian Constitution itself: was it a sovereign grant capable of unlimited revision, or did it derive from "We, the People" and thus carry inbuilt structural commitments that no temporary parliamentary majority could dissolve?

## What the court decided

By a 7-6 majority, the Court overruled Golaknath's blanket prohibition on amending Fundamental Rights, confirming that Parliament does have broad amending power under Article 368. However, the majority held that this power does not extend to altering or destroying the "basic structure or framework" of the Constitution. Chief Justice Sikri's statement — endorsed by the majority — identified features such as supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, secular character, separation of powers, and federal character as elements of basic structure. Justice H.R. Khanna's concurrence was the critical swing vote that made the 7-6 majority possible; his formulation — that the amending power cannot be used to abrogate or take away the Constitution itself — proved particularly influential in subsequent cases. The 24th Amendment was upheld in full. Article 31C was partially upheld: a law implementing Directive Principles under Articles 39(b) and (c) was protected, but Parliament could not expand Article 31C to cover all Directive Principles. The narrowest common holding, signed by nine judges on the final day of the hearing, was that amendments may be made to any provision but cannot damage or destroy the Constitution's basic structure. The elements of basic structure were not exhaustively defined — the majority left that question to develop case by case — but the principle of limitation was firmly established.

## Why the case remains central

Almost every major debate on constitutional amendment, institutional design, or judicial review in India eventually returns to Kesavananda Bharati. The case defines the outer boundary of amendment power and explains why Parliament — however large its majority — cannot legally dismantle constitutional democracy through formal amendment. It is cited in virtually every subsequent constitution bench ruling that involves limits on state power. The doctrine it created has since been applied to protect judicial independence, free and fair elections, federalism, and the rule of law from legislative and executive encroachment.

## Long-term impact

The case created the enduring compromise that constitutional change remains possible but is not unlimited, a balance that has shaped every significant amendment dispute since 1973. It provided the theoretical foundation for striking down the 42nd Amendment's self-entrenchment clauses in Minerva Mills (1980) and for subjecting Ninth Schedule immunities to review in I.R. Coelho (2007). That compromise has also shaped disputes involving federalism, democratic accountability, judicial independence, and the scope of rights protection across five decades of constitutional litigation. The basic structure doctrine originating here has influenced constitutional jurisprudence in several other South and Southeast Asian jurisdictions.
