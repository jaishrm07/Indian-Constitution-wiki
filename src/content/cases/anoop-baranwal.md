## Background

Since independence, Election Commissioners had been appointed entirely through executive discretion: the President acts on the advice of the Union Cabinet, with no requirement of consultation outside the government, no parliamentary confirmation, and no formal criteria. Several petitioners — including retired civil servants and election-law scholars — argued that this arrangement was constitutionally inadequate because the Election Commission's independence from the executive is essential to its function. Article 324(2) expressly provides that the Election Commissioners shall be appointed by the President "subject to the provisions of any law made in that behalf by Parliament", but Parliament had never enacted such a law, leaving the field entirely to executive appointment by convention. The petitioners argued that the vacuum itself was a constitutional problem, because an EC whose members owe their appointment entirely to the ruling government cannot credibly superintend elections involving that government.

## The legal question in detail

The principal legal question was whether the absence of an independent statutory appointment mechanism for Election Commissioners violated the constitutional requirement of institutional independence implicit in Article 324, read alongside the basic structure doctrine. A secondary question was whether the Supreme Court could, in the absence of parliamentary legislation, itself direct an appointment process as an interim constitutional measure. The case also required the Court to address the structural comparison with the appointment of the Chief Justice of India and senior judges, to examine whether executive-only appointment was constitutionally comparable to appointment processes for other constitutional authorities, and to consider what institutional safeguards best protected the EC's impartiality.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that an unconstrained executive appointment process for Election Commissioners was constitutionally inadequate, since the EC's independence is an essential feature of Article 324 and, by extension, of the electoral democracy that forms part of the Constitution's basic structure. The Court held that the President must act on the advice of a committee rather than solely on Cabinet advice, and directed that, until Parliament enacted a law under Article 324(2), appointments would be made on the recommendation of a three-member committee comprising the Prime Minister, the Leader of Opposition in Lok Sabha, and the Chief Justice of India. The Court grounded this direction in the power to fill constitutional interstices, citing the precedent of the collegium's emergence in the judges' cases. Parliament responded swiftly by enacting the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023, which constituted a selection committee with the Prime Minister, the Leader of Opposition, and a Union Cabinet minister nominated by the Prime Minister — replacing the Chief Justice of India with a government nominee. That law is now the subject of a fresh constitutional challenge on the ground that it does not satisfy the independence standard the Court articulated in Anoop Baranwal.

## Why the case matters now

This case forced appointment design into the constitutional foreground. Once Parliament legislated, the central question became whether the enacted model answers the independence concerns the Court identified or merely changes their form. The exclusion of the Chief Justice from the committee and the replacement by a Cabinet minister has intensified the perception that the government retains effective control over EC appointments, and the pending challenge tests whether the Court's judgment can be given legislative effect through a law that changes the composition the Court had directed.

## Long-term impact

The judgment links electoral administration to broader constitutional ideas about impartial institutions, separation of powers, and safeguards against partisan control. It extends the logic of Mohinder Singh Gill — that the Election Commission is a constitutional authority, not a mere creature of statute — into the domain of how that authority's members are chosen. The case has also opened a wider jurisprudential question about whether other constitutional appointments made entirely through executive discretion are similarly vulnerable to challenge on independence grounds.
