## Background

In 1964, the Uttar Pradesh Legislative Assembly passed a resolution holding Keshav Singh, a private citizen, in contempt for publishing and distributing a pamphlet critical of an MLA, and sentenced him to brief imprisonment. Singh filed a habeas corpus petition in the Allahabad High Court, which admitted it and granted him bail. The UP Assembly thereupon passed a fresh resolution summoning the two judges who had granted bail — along with his advocate — to appear before it for breach of legislative privilege. The two judges and the Chief Justice of the High Court secured an interim stay of the summons from a larger bench of the same court, deepening the confrontation. The resulting standoff between a High Court and a State Legislature was so serious that the President of India referred the constitutional questions to the Supreme Court under Article 143.

## The legal question in detail

The reference raised two intertwined questions: whether a State Legislature's privilege power under Article 194 extends to punishing private citizens for contempt of the House by imprisonment, and whether High Courts retain jurisdiction under Article 226 to entertain habeas corpus petitions challenging such legislative detention. The legislature argued that its power to punish for contempt was a necessary incident of its dignity and functioning, historically analogous to the contempt powers of the House of Commons, and that courts had no business scrutinising that power. The petitioners countered that personal liberty under the Constitution cannot be extinguished by a legislative resolution unsupported by law and unreviewable by courts.

## What the court decided

A full bench of the Supreme Court — twenty-eight judges sitting together, the largest bench ever assembled in India — held that High Courts do have jurisdiction under Article 226 to inquire into the legality of imprisonment ordered by a legislature in the exercise of privilege. Legislative privilege does not override the fundamental right to personal liberty, and the courts' power to examine whether the deprivation of liberty is lawful cannot be ousted by a privilege claim. The judgment drew a careful distinction between the internal workings of a legislature — procedural matters and in-House discipline — where courts defer, and the detention of individuals outside the chamber, where judicial review under Article 226 remains fully available. The Court also addressed the specific confrontation by clarifying that neither the High Court judges nor the Chief Justice had breached privilege by entertaining the petition and granting the stay.

## Why the reference remains central

The opinion remains the classic statement that privilege is real, but not absolute. Courts may not interfere with mere procedural irregularity inside the House, yet they can and must examine illegality and constitutional breach where individual liberty is at stake. Every subsequent Indian case on the boundary between legislative autonomy and judicial authority traces its framework back to this reference.

## Long-term impact

Later privilege cases repeatedly return to this reference when drawing the line between internal legislative autonomy and judicial review, including modern disputes over discipline, contempt, and constitutional limits. The twenty-eight-judge bench itself stands as a constitutional moment: the sheer size of the bench signalled the gravity with which the Court treated the separation-of-powers conflict, and its resolution has kept that conflict from recurring in the same acute form.
