## Background

The First Judges Case (S.P. Gupta v. Union of India, 1981) had interpreted "consultation" in Articles 124 and 217 to mean that the executive retained final authority over judicial appointments, treating the Chief Justice's opinion as only one of many inputs. This opened the door to sustained executive influence over the higher judiciary through selective appointments and punitive transfers during the 1980s. Judicial independence visibly eroded as judges known to be government-friendly received preferential treatment, and several transfers were widely read as retaliatory. By the early 1990s, the bench and bar shared a strong sense that the constitutional scheme had been distorted. In 1993, through a Presidential Reference and a cluster of writ petitions, the Supreme Court agreed to reconsider the entire constitutional framework for judicial appointments.

## The legal question in detail

The core question was how Articles 124 and 217 — which require the President to consult the Chief Justice of India — should be interpreted when the executive and the judiciary are in disagreement over who should be appointed or transferred. Specifically, the court had to decide whether "consultation" implied that the CJI's view was merely advisory and the executive could disregard it, or whether it meant that the CJI's institutional opinion must carry primacy such that the government could not act contrary to it. A secondary question was how many judges' views must inform the CJI's opinion, given that a single individual's preference could be arbitrary or mistaken. The case therefore required the court to balance constitutional text, original intent, comparative models, and the structural imperative of judicial independence.

## What the court decided

A nine-judge bench overruled S.P. Gupta by a 7-2 majority and held that the word "consultation" in Articles 124 and 217 must be read as conferring primacy on the Chief Justice of India's institutional opinion in all matters of judicial appointments and transfers to and within the higher judiciary. The CJI's opinion for this purpose must be formed in consultation with the two senior-most judges of the Supreme Court, ensuring the opinion is collegiate rather than personal. The Union government is bound to act in accordance with the CJI's opinion and may return a recommendation only once for reconsideration; if the CJI reiterates the recommendation, it is binding on the President. This effectively created the collegium system as the operative constitutional practice, though the precise composition and procedures of the collegium remained to be fully worked out. The Third Judges Case (Special Reference No. 1 of 1998) subsequently clarified that the collegium for Supreme Court appointments must comprise the CJI and the four senior-most puisne judges.

## Why the case remains central

This is the modern starting point for India's judge-appointments system. It changed the constitutional meaning of "consultation" in a way that still structures every appointments debate, and every reform proposal must either work within the framework it created or justify departing from it.

## Long-term impact

Arguments about delay, transparency, diversity, and institutional balance all operate in the shadow of the Second Judges Case. The collegium system it inaugurated was tested most directly in 2015–16 when Parliament enacted the NJAC — a challenge the Supreme Court ultimately rejected while simultaneously acknowledging that the collegium model was far from perfect.
