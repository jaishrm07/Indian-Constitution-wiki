## Background

Section 66A of the Information Technology Act, 2000 penalised the transmission of "grossly offensive," "menacing," or otherwise objectionable messages through a computer or communication device with up to three years' imprisonment. It had been widely used to arrest individuals for social media posts criticising politicians and governments, turning an ostensibly cybercrime provision into a tool for suppressing political dissent. Shreya Singhal, a law student, filed a writ petition challenging the provision after two women in Mumbai were arrested for a Facebook post about the shutdown of the city following the death of Bal Thackeray. The arrests sparked national outrage and focused attention on how vague the statutory language was and how easily it could be weaponised by authorities.

## The legal question in detail

The primary question was whether Section 66A of the IT Act was a constitutionally valid restriction on speech under Article 19(1)(a), given that it used vague terms like "grossly offensive," "menacing," and "causing annoyance" that did not correspond to any of the permissible grounds of restriction enumerated in Article 19(2). A related question was whether the vagueness of the provision created an unconstitutional chilling effect on legitimate speech, since citizens could not predict with any confidence whether their online expression would attract prosecution. The Court also had to consider whether Section 69A — which provided a different and more structured mechanism for blocking online content — and the intermediary liability rules were constitutionally valid.

## What the court decided

A two-judge bench unanimously struck down Section 66A in its entirety as unconstitutional and void. The Court held that the terms used in Section 66A — grossly offensive, menacing, causing annoyance, inconvenience, danger, obstruction — did not fall within any of the eight permissible grounds under Article 19(2), which are exhaustive and not illustrative. Because the provision could reach speech that is fully protected by the Constitution, it was not saved by any of the grounds in Article 19(2) and therefore could not stand. The Court upheld Section 69A and the intermediary liability framework subject to safeguards, drawing a sharp constitutional distinction between targeted content blocking with procedural protections and blanket criminalisation of vague categories of speech. The judgment also clarified the distinction between discussion, advocacy, and incitement, holding that only the last category may be restricted under the "public order" ground.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment translated free speech principles into the online sphere and remains essential for understanding the constitutional limits of digital speech regulation. By insisting that the grounds in Article 19(2) are exhaustive, the Court closed off the possibility of restricting speech through provisions that rely on vague terms like "offensive" or "annoying" without a nexus to a recognised constitutional harm. The decision also established that the medium — the internet — does not diminish the constitutional protection otherwise available to speech. Every subsequent attempt to regulate online speech in India must reckon with the boundaries Shreya Singhal drew.

## Long-term impact

As the state continues to regulate online content and intermediary liability through successive amendments to the IT Act and new IT Rules, Shreya Singhal remains the most immediate constitutional reference point. The judgment is routinely cited in challenges to take-down orders, content moderation mandates, and new categories of prescribed online speech. Its insistence on the exhaustiveness of Article 19(2) grounds has become a hard doctrinal limit that any new speech regulation must navigate. The case also catalysed a broader public conversation about the abuse of criminal law to suppress political criticism online.
