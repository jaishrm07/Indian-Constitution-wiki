## Background

The case arose from the gang rape of Bhanwari Devi, a social worker in Rajasthan employed by the state's Women Development Programme, who had attempted to prevent a child marriage. Despite her complaint, the trial court acquitted the accused. Women's rights groups and activists, led by an organisation called Vishaka, filed a public interest litigation under Article 32 seeking enforcement of the fundamental rights of working women and the formulation of guidelines to prevent sexual harassment at the workplace. The petition was not about the specific crime against Bhanwari Devi but about the structural absence of any legal framework that addressed the pervasive problem of workplace sexual harassment affecting women across all sectors of employment. At the time, India had no statute specifically addressing sexual harassment at work, leaving women dependent on fragmented tort law and scattered provisions of the Indian Penal Code that did not squarely address the workplace context.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether the absence of a specific statute on workplace sexual harassment meant that women had no justiciable right against such harassment, or whether existing fundamental rights — to equality, to work in a dignified environment, and to personal liberty — independently required the state and employers to prevent and redress harassment. A related question was whether the Court, when faced with a legislative vacuum that allowed a constitutional right to remain unprotected in practice, could fill that vacuum by issuing guidelines with the force of law until Parliament legislated. The petitioners relied on Articles 14, 15, 19(1)(g), and 21, arguing that sexual harassment is a form of sex discrimination that violates all these provisions simultaneously, and that international conventions — particularly CEDAW — informed the content of these obligations. The state argued that the matter was one for legislative action.

## What the court decided

A three-judge bench led by Chief Justice Verma held that sexual harassment of women at the workplace constitutes a violation of fundamental rights under Articles 14, 15, 19(1)(g), and 21. The Court held that the right to work in a safe environment free from sexual harassment flows directly from these provisions, and that the state's failure to enact protective legislation could not be used to defeat the underlying constitutional right. Exercising its jurisdiction under Article 32 and drawing on the state's obligations under CEDAW and the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women as applicable gender equality norms, the Court formulated the Vishaka Guidelines. These guidelines defined sexual harassment, required every employer to constitute a complaints committee, laid down a complaints procedure, and imposed duties of awareness and preventive action on employers. The Court expressly declared that the guidelines would have the force of law and be binding on all employers — public and private — until appropriate legislation was enacted.

## Why the case remains central

Vishaka matters because it demonstrates that constitutional rights can generate positive institutional obligations that the judiciary can enforce in the absence of legislation. The judgment was a significant use of Article 32 not merely to strike down or restrain but to create a framework — a rare exercise of what is sometimes called the Court's remedial jurisdiction. It also established that international human rights treaties ratified by India, even when not enacted into domestic law, can inform and shape the content of fundamental rights, giving Indian constitutional doctrine a more cosmopolitan dimension. The case is a foundation text for arguments that equality under Articles 14 and 15 encompasses freedom from a hostile working environment, not merely formal non-discrimination in hiring or terms of employment.

## Long-term impact

The Vishaka Guidelines operated as binding law for sixteen years and shaped employer practice and institutional design across the public and private sectors. They were eventually superseded by the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 — the POSH Act — which codified and expanded the guidelines into statutory form. The POSH Act's architecture owes a direct debt to the structure the Court designed in Vishaka. The judgment's reasoning on the court's power to fill legislative vacuums in the protection of fundamental rights has been relied upon in subsequent public interest litigation cases across a range of rights. The case also contributed to the development of equality jurisprudence that treats substantive rather than merely formal equality as the constitutional standard.
