## Background

Francis Coralie Mullin, a British national, was detained under the Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974 (COFEPOSA). During her detention at Tihar Jail, the authorities imposed restrictions on her ability to meet her family members — including her infant daughter — and to consult her legal adviser. She petitioned the Supreme Court under Article 32, contending that these restrictions violated her rights under Articles 21 and 22. The case arose in the immediate aftermath of Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978), which had dramatically reoriented the Court's understanding of Article 21 by requiring that any procedure depriving a person of life or liberty be fair, just, and reasonable. The question before the Court was whether the constitutional guarantee of life and liberty extended to the conditions and quality of detention, and whether a detenu retained rights that could not be removed simply by virtue of being in lawful custody.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21 was limited to freedom from physical death and restraint, or whether it encompassed a broader entitlement to live with human dignity and to the minimum conditions necessary for such dignity. A connected question was whether a detainee held under preventive detention legislation retained the right to meet family members and consult a legal adviser, and if so, whether the restrictions imposed by jail authorities on such meetings had any legal sanction. The petitioner argued that the restrictions went beyond what COFEPOSA authorised and that, in any event, a blanket curtailment of contact with family and counsel was incompatible with the constitutional concept of life. The state argued that persons in lawful preventive detention could be subject to reasonable administrative regulations, and that the restrictions were justified in the interests of prison management.

## What the court decided

Justice Bhagwati, writing for the Court, held that the right to life in Article 21 cannot be read narrowly as a right merely to exist as an animal — it comprehends the right to live with human dignity and to the minimum necessities of life, including adequate nutrition, clothing, shelter, and the facilities necessary for reading, writing, and expressing oneself. The Court held that any act of the state that denies these minimum conditions constitutes a deprivation of life within the meaning of Article 21, subject to the requirement that the deprivation be pursuant to a fair and reasonable procedure. On the specific facts, the Court found that the restrictions preventing the petitioner from meeting her infant daughter and consulting her lawyer went beyond what the detention order and the applicable statute authorised, and were therefore without legal basis. The Court directed that the petitioner be permitted to meet her family members and her legal adviser subject only to reasonable security conditions, and made clear that the right to consult counsel is an integral part of the right to personal liberty that cannot be denied to any detainee without clear statutory authority.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment matters because it translates the post-Maneka Gandhi interpretive method into a substantive right with a qualitative dimension. By holding that Article 21 protects not just the fact of life but the dignity of life, the Court opened the way for a long line of cases that derived positive entitlements — to health, livelihood, education, a clean environment, and personal development — from the text of a provision that had originally been read as purely a protection against arbitrary state interference. The case also contributed to the developing jurisprudence on the rights of detainees and prisoners, establishing that custodial status does not extinguish fundamental rights except to the extent strictly authorised by law. The reasoning that human dignity is the animating core of Article 21 became foundational to Indian constitutional rights doctrine.

## Long-term impact

The dignity-based reading of Article 21 established in Francis Coralie Mullin was carried forward and deepened in Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985), which extended the right to livelihood, and in a series of prisoner rights cases that imposed constitutional standards on prison conditions. The case also anticipated the Court's later treatment of socio-economic rights as implicit in the constitutional guarantee of life, a doctrinal trajectory that culminated in decisions recognising rights to food, clean water, and health care. The principle that a detainee retains the right to consult legal counsel is now firmly embedded in both constitutional and statutory law. Every subsequent invocation of the dignity dimension of Article 21 owes something to the foundational reasoning of this case.
