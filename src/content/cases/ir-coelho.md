## Background

I.R. Coelho, a senior civil servant and constitutional scholar, filed a writ petition challenging the practice of placing laws in the Ninth Schedule as a device to immunise them from fundamental rights scrutiny under Article 31B. The Ninth Schedule, introduced by the First Amendment in 1951, was originally intended as a limited protective measure for land reform legislation, but over subsequent decades Parliament extended it to cover a wide range of economic and social laws — including laws that restricted rights under Articles 14, 19, and 21 — that had been struck down or were under challenge in the courts. After Kesavananda Bharati (1973) established that constitutional amendments themselves are subject to basic structure review, the question arose whether the same doctrine applied to laws added to the Ninth Schedule through post-1973 constitutional amendments. The case was referred to a nine-judge bench to give a definitive answer on the scope of Article 31B and its relationship with the basic structure doctrine.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether the immunity conferred by Article 31B — which declares that Ninth Schedule laws shall not be deemed void on the ground of inconsistency with Fundamental Rights — survives the basic structure doctrine when applied to laws inserted after 24 April 1973 (the date of the Kesavananda judgment). The Union of India argued that Article 31B’s terms are clear and absolute: once a law is placed in the Ninth Schedule by constitutional amendment, it is beyond challenge under Part III, and the courts have no jurisdiction to re-examine it. The petitioners argued that if a constitutional amendment adding a law to the Ninth Schedule itself damages the basic structure — for example, by annihilating the right to equality or gutting Article 21 — then Kesavananda requires the court to intervene, regardless of the textual immunity that Article 31B appears to confer.

## What the court decided

A nine-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that laws inserted into the Ninth Schedule after 24 April 1973 do not enjoy absolute immunity from fundamental rights challenge and are subject to basic structure review. The Court reasoned that the post-Kesavananda constitutional order requires all constitutional amendments — including those adding laws to the Ninth Schedule — to be tested against the basic structure, and Article 31B cannot be read to override that requirement without itself violating the doctrine. The Court developed a "rights test": if the direct and inevitable effect of a Ninth Schedule law or the amendment placing it there is to abrogate or nullify rights guaranteed by Articles 14, 19, 20, or 21, or to damage the basic structure, that law and the amendment are void pro tanto. Laws placed in the Ninth Schedule before 24 April 1973 retain their immunity, providing a clear temporal line. The judgment closed the most significant remaining route by which Parliament could otherwise have circumvented Kesavananda through selective Schedule manipulation.

## Why the case remains central

I.R. Coelho closes the loophole that could otherwise have turned the Ninth Schedule into an ever-expanding safe harbour for laws that undermine constitutional democracy while remaining textually immune from challenge. It is the culmination of the basic structure doctrine’s development across three and a half decades — from Golaknath’s first assertion of limits, through Kesavananda’s articulation of the doctrine, and Minerva Mills’ defence of it against self-entrenchment, to this final confirmation that constitutional form cannot override constitutional substance. The decision is also significant for establishing the temporal bright line of 24 April 1973, giving clarity to decades of accumulated Ninth Schedule legislation. It remains the governing authority on the scope of Article 31B in modern constitutional practice.

## Doctrinal line

The case makes the basic structure doctrine operational in a concrete and practically significant setting: laws that are explicitly shielded by constitutional text still cannot damage the Constitution’s core identity, because the amendment creating that shield is itself subject to review. This reasoning extends the logic of Minerva Mills — that Parliament cannot use amendment power to entrench itself against review — to the specific technique of schedule placement. The Court’s "rights test" provided a workable standard that goes beyond abstract invocations of basic structure to ask whether the practical effect of a law destroys justiciable rights that are themselves basic structure elements. This approach has influenced how subsequent courts assess constitutional validity when formal protective mechanisms are invoked.

## Long-term impact

Any serious study of constitutional immunities, amendment design, or the tension between legislative protection of socio-economic policy and rights protection now has to account for this decision. The judgment has direct implications for affirmative action laws, land reform legislation, and economic regulation that have been placed in the Ninth Schedule since 1973, requiring re-evaluation of their constitutional standing if they nullify rights under Articles 14, 19, or 21. It also reinforces the broader principle — running across Kesavananda, Minerva Mills, and this judgment — that no constitutional device, however formally crafted, can permanently insulate government action from the Constitution’s core requirements.
