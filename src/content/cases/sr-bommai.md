## Background

The immediate trigger for the Bommai litigation was the dismissal of S.R. Bommai's Congress-supported Janata Dal government in Karnataka in 1989, when the Governor reported to the President that the government had lost majority — without ever allowing a floor test. A broader wave of Article 356 dismissals followed the Babri Masjid demolition in December 1992, when the Union government used President's Rule to remove BJP governments in Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Himachal Pradesh. These dismissals were challenged in multiple High Courts and consolidated before the Supreme Court. The sheer scale of partisan Article 356 use — the provision had been invoked over ninety times since 1950 — made the case one of the most consequential federalism disputes in Indian constitutional history. A nine-judge Constitution Bench was assembled to resolve the question authoritatively.

## The legal question in detail

The primary constitutional question was whether a Presidential proclamation under Article 356 — which suspended the elected state government and imposed Union rule — was a purely political act beyond judicial scrutiny, or whether courts could examine the factual and constitutional basis on which it was issued. A subsidiary but equally important question was whether the Governor's personal assessment of a government's majority was a sufficient basis for dismissal, or whether a floor test in the state legislature was constitutionally required before any dismissal. The petitioners argued that treating Article 356 as non-justiciable gave the Union an unreviewable power to destabilise opposition-ruled states, striking at the federal character of the Constitution. The government contended that the satisfaction of the President was subjective and personal, placing it beyond the reach of courts in the way executive privilege traditionally operated.

## What the court decided

All nine judges agreed that Article 356 proclamations are subject to judicial review; the court could examine whether the proclamation was based on relevant material, was free from mala fides, and was not issued on irrelevant or extraneous grounds. The majority held that the floor of the state legislature — not the Governor's private correspondence or assessment — is the constitutionally proper place to test whether a government commands a majority, and that dismissal without affording this opportunity was constitutionally improper. The Court struck down the proclamations against Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Himachal Pradesh as constitutionally unsustainable, while upholding the Karnataka dismissal on its particular facts. Justice B.P. Jeevan Reddy, writing for a plurality, declared that secularism and federalism are basic structure elements, so Article 356 cannot be wielded in ways that corrode either. Although the judgments ran to hundreds of pages and there was no single majority opinion on every point, the core propositions on reviewability and the floor test commanded clear majority support. The decision fundamentally altered the political calculus of Centre-State relations by removing the expectation of judicial passivity.

## Why the case remains central

Bommai is the key interpretive brake on a constitutional provision that historically invited partisan abuse, converting a raw emergency power into one meaningfully constrained by judicial and procedural reason. Its floor-test requirement has become a practical constitutional norm: governors who attempt to dismiss governments without a floor test do so against an established constitutional backdrop. The case also cemented federalism as a basic structure element, meaning Parliament cannot amend the Constitution in ways that destroy the federal balance. Every major political controversy involving gubernatorial action against a state government — from Arunachal Pradesh in 2016 to Maharashtra in 2019 — is argued and decided against the framework Bommai established.

## Long-term impact

Modern arguments about governors, assembly majorities, and Union intervention in state politics are difficult to understand without Bommai. The decision gave state governments a constitutional tool to resist politically motivated dismissals and compelled the Union government to articulate factual grounds before invoking Article 356. It also contributed to the practical decline of President's Rule as a routine political instrument: the provision is now invoked far less frequently and with greater constitutional caution. Bommai's federalism reasoning fed directly into later decisions on state autonomy, fiscal federalism, and the limits of concurrent legislative power.
