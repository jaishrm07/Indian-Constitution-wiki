## Background

The Punjab Vidhan Sabha elections of 1969 were marred by widespread booth-capturing, electoral violence, and serious irregularities across several constituencies. The Election Commission, exercising its superintendence function, took the extraordinary step of cancelling polling in over thirteen constituencies and ordering a fresh poll. Mohinder Singh Gill, one of the affected candidates, moved the courts to challenge whether the EC possessed any constitutional power to countermand elections beyond what Parliament had expressly authorised in the Representation of the People Act. The case raised a fundamental question about the constitutional architecture of election administration — whether the EC was merely a statutory creature or an independent constitutional authority with powers sourced directly from Article 324.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was the scope of the Election Commission's power under Article 324. Did Article 324 confer only those powers that Parliament had chosen to confer through electoral legislation, or did it create a residual plenary authority that the EC could exercise in the gaps that statutory law had not yet filled? If Parliament had not enacted a provision covering a particular contingency — such as mid-election cancellations due to violence — could the EC act anyway, or was it disabled until Parliament legislated? The case also asked how the EC should exercise any such residual power, and what safeguards or constraints existed on that exercise.

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court held that Article 324 is a plenary provision that vests full superintendence, direction, and control of elections in the Election Commission, not as a mere agent of Parliament but as an independent constitutional authority. The EC's powers are not confined to what Parliament has expressly authorised in the Representation of the People Act; where Parliament has not legislated, the EC can fill the gap by drawing on the residual power conferred directly by Article 324. This power must be exercised in accordance with the constitutional scheme and general principles of law, not arbitrarily. On the facts, the EC's decision to countermand polling in the affected constituencies because of serious irregularities was upheld as a lawful exercise of this constitutional authority. The judgment also addressed the question of judicial restraint, holding that courts should be cautious about intervening mid-election and should ordinarily allow the electoral process to reach its statutory challenge stage before scrutinising disputed decisions.

## Why the case remains central

The case is one of the most cited starting points for understanding why election administration is treated as a constitutionally sensitive institutional domain. It establishes that Article 324 is a self-executing grant of constitutional power, not merely a peg on which Parliament hangs statutory authority, and that distinction continues to shape how the EC's decisions are assessed by courts. Any argument that the EC has exceeded its powers — or, conversely, that it has shirked a constitutional duty — must still reckon with the framework Mohinder Singh Gill laid down.

## Long-term impact

Modern election cases still return to this judgment when they ask whether courts should intervene immediately or allow the electoral process to continue into the statutory challenge stage. The residual-power doctrine recognised here has been invoked to support the EC's authority over matters like Model Code enforcement, electronic voting machine design, and election schedule adjustments. The judgment is also frequently cited alongside Anoop Baranwal when courts and scholars analyse what institutional independence for the Election Commission really demands.
