## Background

Sita Soren was a Member of the Jharkhand Legislative Assembly belonging to the Jharkhand Mukti Morcha (JMM). She was accused of accepting a bribe from an independent candidate to cast her vote in his favour during a Rajya Sabha election held in 2012. A criminal complaint under the Prevention of Corruption Act was registered against her. She moved the Jharkhand High Court to quash the prosecution, relying on the parliamentary privilege provisions in Articles 105(2) and 194(2) of the Constitution, which provide that a member cannot be subject to any proceedings in any court in respect of anything said or any vote given in the legislature.

The High Court declined to quash the prosecution. Soren appealed to the Supreme Court, and the case raised afresh the question that the five-judge bench in P.V. Narasimha Rao v. State (CBI/SPE) (1998) had addressed in the context of the 1993 JMM bribery scandal — whether the privilege provisions of the Constitution extend to shield legislators from prosecution for bribery connected to their legislative votes.

The significance of the referral to a seven-judge bench was explicit: the 1998 decision in P.V. Narasimha Rao had produced a deeply controversial 3:2 majority holding that MPs who voted as they had been bribed to vote were immune from prosecution under Article 105(2), since any prosecution would require inquiry into their vote. The two-judge minority had taken the opposite view. The conflict between the majority's broad reading of privilege and the intuition that corruption in the legislature cannot attract constitutional immunity had never been resolved. The seven-judge bench in Sita Soren provided that resolution.

## The legal question in detail

The question can be broken into two related but distinct parts.

The first is textual. Articles 105(2) and 194(2) each provide that no member of Parliament (or a state legislature, respectively) shall be liable to any proceedings in any court in respect of anything said or any vote given by him in Parliament or the legislature or any committee thereof. The question is whether this immunity covers only the speech or vote as such, or whether it also extends to the entire transaction — including the bribe — that produces the speech or vote. If the bribery agreement is a "necessary step" to the vote, one could argue (as the 1998 majority did) that prosecuting the agreement amounts to questioning the vote.

The second is purposive. Privilege under Articles 105(2) and 194(2) derives historically from the constitutional necessity of protecting legislators from executive and judicial interference in the exercise of their legislative functions. The privilege exists so that a member can speak freely in debate, vote without fear of legal retaliation, and participate fully in parliamentary proceedings without being hauled before a court for what was said in the House. Does that rationale extend to a corrupt bargain made outside the House for personal gain?

In P.V. Narasimha Rao (1998), the majority had held that since the prosecution of the bribe-taking MPs would necessarily scrutinise how they voted, and since the vote was protected, the prosecution could not be maintained. It treated the bribe and the vote as so intertwined that protection of the latter required protection of the former. The minority had disagreed, reasoning that the offence of bribery is complete on acceptance of the illegal gratification and does not depend on anything subsequently said or done in the House. Whether or not the member ultimately voted as promised was irrelevant to the completed offence.

The seven-judge bench in Sita Soren was asked to decide which approach was correct and, if necessary, to overrule the 1998 majority.

## What the court decided

The seven-judge Constitution Bench, speaking through the Chief Justice (with the remaining six judges concurring), unanimously overruled P.V. Narasimha Rao v. State.

**The privilege does not extend to bribery.** The Court held that Articles 105(2) and 194(2) protect the act of speaking or voting in the legislature — the exercise of the legislative function inside the House. They do not protect every act that has some connection to what is eventually said or voted. Bribery is an offence against the public interest that is complete the moment the illegal gratification is accepted. It involves a transaction outside the House, between a private person and the legislator, for the legislator's personal benefit. That transaction does not become part of the legislative process by reason of the fact that the legislator later exercises a legislative function.

**The 1998 majority reasoning was flawed.** The Court rejected the "necessary step" logic of the 1998 majority. The fact that a prosecution for bribery might incidentally require proof that the legislator voted in a particular way does not convert the prosecution into proceedings in respect of the vote. The prosecution is in respect of the corrupt agreement, not the vote. The Court distinguished between a proceeding that questions the validity or propriety of a legislative act — which privilege protects against — and a proceeding that prosecutes a prior criminal transaction — which privilege does not reach.

**Privilege protects independence, not impunity.** The Court emphasised the constitutional purpose of privilege. Articles 105(2) and 194(2) are designed to protect the freedom and independence of legislative proceedings from external legal interference. They are not designed to create a special zone of criminal impunity for legislators in their personal capacities. A reading that shields bribery from prosecution would invert the purpose of privilege: instead of protecting the legislature from external pressure, it would protect corruption within the legislature from accountability.

**The offence is complete before the House meets.** Consistent with the minority's reasoning in 1998, the Court held that the offence under the Prevention of Corruption Act is complete at the moment the bribe is accepted. Whether the legislator performed the promised act — voted as directed, spoke as directed — is not an element of the offence and need not be proved. Accordingly, no inquiry into what was said or voted in the House is necessary to sustain the prosecution, and the privilege clause cannot be invoked to block it.

## Why the case remains central

Sita Soren marks a significant doctrinal correction. For twenty-six years, the Narasimha Rao majority had stood as a barrier to the prosecution of legislators who took bribes and then voted as directed. The case had attracted sustained criticism from jurists, reformers, and the Law Commission, who argued that extending privilege to the antecedent bribery act rewarded corruption with constitutional protection and undermined democratic accountability.

By drawing a clear line — privilege covers the legislative act, not the corrupt bargain — the Court reaffirmed that constitutional protections are purposive. They protect the function the framers sought to secure, no more. This reasoning has wider relevance: it sets a template for how to interpret broad privilege and immunity clauses without allowing them to become instruments for insulating representatives from accountability.

The case also connects to the ongoing jurisprudence on anti-defection and privilege. Courts have had to reckon with the limits of privilege in the context of the Tenth Schedule, where legislators face disqualification for voting against party direction. Sita Soren reinforces the principle that privilege provisions are to be read in light of their purposes and do not override other constitutional mechanisms — anti-defection, criminal law, or otherwise — that discipline legislative conduct.

## Long-term impact

Sita Soren is now the mandatory starting point for any analysis of Articles 105(2) and 194(2) in the context of criminal liability. Its practical consequences are significant.

First, it removes the principal shield that legislators had used to block prosecution for cash-for-vote corruption. State and central prosecuting agencies can now proceed against legislators accused of bribery connected to legislative votes without having to contend with the 1998 privilege precedent.

Second, it reopens questions about prosecutions that may have been impeded by the 1998 rule. Whether the decision applies retroactively to pending cases, and what its effect is on proceedings stayed pending this outcome, is likely to generate further litigation.

Third, the Court's reasoning about the purposive limits of privilege has implications beyond bribery. It reinforces that courts may look to the purpose of privilege provisions when deciding whether a particular type of proceedings is excluded — a principle with application to questions about legislative contempt powers, member accountability, and the interface between parliamentary proceedings and judicial review.

Fourth, Justice Gavai's observations on the institutional dimension of privilege — that the Houses of the legislature have their own disciplinary mechanisms, and that privilege is not a substitute for those mechanisms in cases of corruption — may influence future cases about the relationship between internal parliamentary discipline and external legal accountability.

Reading Sita Soren together with P.V. Narasimha Rao (1998) and the earlier privilege cases — Keshav Singh (1965), Raja Ram Pal (2007) — provides the full arc of privilege doctrine.
