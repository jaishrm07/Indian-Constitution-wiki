## Background

On 25 June 1975, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi proclaimed an internal Emergency under Article 352, and a Presidential order under Article 359 suspended the right to move courts for enforcement of fundamental rights under Articles 14, 21, and 22. Tens of thousands of political opponents and activists were detained under the Maintenance of Internal Security Act (MISA) and similar laws, often without charges or reasons. Detenues filed habeas corpus petitions in various High Courts, and several — including Allahabad, Bombay, Delhi, Madhya Pradesh, and Karnataka — ruled in favour of the detenues, holding that courts retained the power to examine whether a detention was authorised by law even if the substantive right was suspended. The government appealed to the Supreme Court, arguing that the Presidential order had completely ousted judicial jurisdiction over any challenge to detention during the Emergency. The case therefore required the Court to answer one of the most fundamental questions a constitutional court can face: whether the rule of law survives even when its formal textual anchors have been suspended.

## The legal question in detail

The precise constitutional question was whether, during an Emergency when Article 359 had suspended the right to move courts to enforce Article 21, a detenu could still petition a High Court under Article 226 or the Supreme Court under Article 32 to challenge the legality of detention. The detenues argued that even without Article 21, courts possessed an inherent power to issue habeas corpus, and that MISA itself required the detention to be "in accordance with law" — so a detention authorised by no law at all remained challengeable regardless of the Presidential order. The government's position was that the Presidential order was comprehensive and that any judicial examination of detention during the Emergency would be an impermissible interference with the executive's exercise of emergency powers. The Court was thus forced to choose between two visions of constitutionalism: one in which rights existed only as positive textual grants that could be suspended, and one in which the judiciary retained a residual protective role regardless of executive proclamation.

## What the court decided

By a 4-1 majority, the Supreme Court held that no person had any locus standi to move any court to challenge any executive action during the Emergency while the Presidential order under Article 359 was in force, and that the right to personal liberty was entirely a creature of Article 21 — which had been suspended. Chief Justice A.N. Ray and Justices M.H. Beg, Y.V. Chandrachud, and P.N. Bhagwati comprised the majority; all four later became Chief Justices of India. Justice H.R. Khanna, in a dissent widely regarded as one of the most courageous acts in Indian judicial history, held that the right to life and personal liberty is not solely derived from Article 21 but is a natural and common law right anterior to the Constitution itself, and that courts retained the power to examine whether a detention had any legal basis whatsoever. Khanna J noted that accepting the majority position would mean that a government could order the killing of citizens without any legal authority and no court could interfere. Justice Chandrachud later publicly acknowledged regret for his majority vote. The case was effectively overruled by the 44th Constitutional Amendment in 1978, which made the right to life under Articles 20 and 21 non-suspendable even during an Emergency.

## Why the case remains central

The case is studied less as a constitutional success and more as a cautionary example of what happens when judicial independence yields to executive authority at a moment of democratic crisis. It illustrates the structural vulnerability of rights that exist only as positive textual grants, since a sufficiently determined government can suspend the text while leaving the underlying injustice intact. Justice Khanna's dissent has become a canonical document in Indian constitutional thought — an example of what principled adjudication looks like when the personal and institutional costs are highest. The case also prompted direct constitutional amendment: the 44th Amendment's removal of Articles 20 and 21 from the list of suspendable rights is the constitutional system's own acknowledgment that ADM Jabalpur identified a fatal design flaw.

## Long-term impact

Later liberty jurisprudence and post-Emergency constitutional reflection often define themselves against ADM Jabalpur, and the case has become the reference point for every scholarly and judicial argument about the proper limits of executive power during emergencies. The Maneka Gandhi judgment of 1978 can be read in part as a collective act of atonement by some of the same judges, particularly Justice Bhagwati, who had joined the ADM Jabalpur majority. The Supreme Court's unanimous 2017 privacy judgment in K.S. Puttaswamy expressly recognised ADM Jabalpur as wrongly decided, formally interring it after four decades. The case continues to be invoked whenever courts are asked to examine the limits of emergency powers, national security detention, and the question of whether any rights survive in conditions of declared crisis.
