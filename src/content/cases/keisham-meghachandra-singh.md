## Background

Keisham Meghachandra Singh was a sitting member of the Manipur Legislative Assembly. A disqualification petition was filed against him under the Tenth Schedule on the ground that he had voluntarily given up the membership of his political party. More than a year passed without the Speaker of the Manipur Legislative Assembly deciding the petition. In the interim, the political landscape in Manipur had shifted and the Speaker's inaction operated to protect the sitting member from the consequences of the Tenth Schedule.

Singh petitioned the Supreme Court seeking a writ of mandamus directing the Speaker to decide the petition within a fixed time. The respondent Speaker raised the constitutional objection that proceedings in the legislature are protected from judicial interference under Article 212, which bars courts from inquiring into the proceedings of a legislature on the ground of any alleged irregularity in procedure. The Speaker also relied on the exclusionary clause in paragraph 6 of the Tenth Schedule — inserted and upheld in Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992) — which bars courts from questioning decisions of the Speaker in disqualification matters except under limited grounds of judicial review.

The case came to the Supreme Court as a companion to Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2020) 2 SCC 595, which was decided on the same day and had already established the broader framework for judicial oversight of Speaker delay. Keisham Meghachandra Singh became the vehicle for specifying what "reasonable time" means in numerical terms.

## The legal question in detail

The core question was whether the Speaker's obligation to decide disqualification petitions — an obligation recognised in Kihoto Hollohan and made explicit in Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil — could be enforced through mandamus, or whether Articles 212 and the Tenth Schedule's exclusionary clause together placed Speaker inaction beyond the reach of courts.

A subsidiary question was what standard of timeliness courts should apply when assessing whether a Speaker has acted within a reasonable time. "Reasonable time" is inherently context-dependent; the petitioner pressed the Court to crystallise it into a specific outer limit so that the obligation would be practically enforceable rather than merely theoretical.

There was also the deeper structural question of whether mandamus to compel a Speaker to decide — as distinct from mandamus to decide in a particular way — was constitutionally permissible. The Tenth Schedule expressly reserves the merits of disqualification decisions to the Speaker; nothing in Article 212 or the Tenth Schedule, however, expressly insulates the Speaker from an obligation to act at all within a defined period.

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court held that a Speaker's failure to decide a disqualification petition within a reasonable time is justiciable and that mandamus will lie to compel the Speaker to act.

On the Article 212 objection, the Court held that mandamus compelling a Speaker to perform a constitutional duty — deciding a petition — is not an inquiry into the regularity of proceedings within the meaning of Article 212. Article 212 protects the internal procedural autonomy of legislatures; it does not protect a Speaker's deliberate or negligent omission to exercise the jurisdiction that the Constitution has expressly conferred on the Speaker under the Tenth Schedule. These are legally distinct questions.

On the exclusionary clause in the Tenth Schedule, the Court reaffirmed the Kihoto Hollohan position that judicial review of the merits of a Speaker's decision is limited. But the exclusionary clause applies to a decision already made — it does not license indefinite delay in making any decision at all. A Speaker who has simply not acted has not yet made a decision within the meaning of the exclusionary clause; there is accordingly no decision to protect from review.

On timeliness, the Court set the outer limit at three months. If a Speaker fails to decide a disqualification petition within three months of it being filed, without justification, the Speaker can be directed by a court to do so within a further specific period. The Court acknowledged that three months is a norm rather than an absolute bar — circumstances such as legislative sessions, election periods, or genuine complexity may affect the calculation — but it is the benchmark against which delay will be measured.

## Why the case remains central

The case addressed the most persistent mechanism through which Speakers had historically neutralised the Tenth Schedule: not deciding. The practical effect of a Speaker sitting on a petition is that the member continues to participate in the House, vote in divisions, and benefit from the party whose whip he or she has violated. A Speaker aligned with the ruling party has every incentive to delay a petition against its own members and every incentive to decide quickly against members of the opposition. Before this case, that asymmetry had no legally enforceable corrective.

The three-month norm converts an abstract obligation into a measurable standard. It also creates a litigation trigger: once three months pass without a decision, a party can go to court with a clear benchmark already established rather than having to argue from first principles that the delay has become unreasonable. This lowers the cost of enforcing the Tenth Schedule and removes the Speaker's ability to use procedural silence as a permanent political tool.

The Article 212 analysis is independently important. The Court's distinction between protecting a legislature's internal procedures (which Article 212 covers) and a Speaker's failure to perform a constitutionally mandated duty (which it does not) clarifies that legislative privilege does not create a zone of executive-style inaction immune from constitutional accountability.

## Long-term impact

The three-month norm has been cited in virtually every subsequent anti-defection proceeding in which a Speaker has delayed. In the Maharashtra political crisis of 2022-2023, the Supreme Court relied on the Keisham Meghachandra Singh framework in directing the Speakers of the Maharashtra Legislative Assembly and Legislative Council to decide pending Tenth Schedule petitions within specified timeframes. The norm has similarly been invoked in proceedings before the High Courts of various states.

The case has also shifted the balance of bargaining in political crises. Legislators considering defection or rebel action now know that a Speaker cannot provide indefinite protection through procedural delay; the three-month clock runs regardless of the political convenience of not deciding. This has made the Tenth Schedule a marginally more credible deterrent than it was before 2020, though the structural problem — that the Speaker adjudicating defection is drawn from the same political pool as the parties before him or her — remains unresolved and continues to generate litigation.

The companion relationship between Keisham Meghachandra Singh and Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil means the two cases are routinely cited together. Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil defines the outer limits of what a Speaker can order by way of punishment; Keisham Meghachandra Singh defines the timeframe within which that order must be made. Together they constitute the current doctrinal framework governing Speaker conduct in Tenth Schedule proceedings.
