## Background

P.V. Narasimha Rao's government survived a no-confidence motion in 1993 by a narrow majority that depended on a cluster of Jharkhand Mukti Morcha MPs voting against the motion. It later emerged that those MPs had accepted bribes to cast their votes in the government's favour. Criminal prosecution was launched against the bribe-takers and several of Rao's associates. The accused MPs invoked Articles 105(2) and 194(2) to argue that their votes — even if obtained by bribery — could not be questioned in any court. The case thus raised a direct conflict between parliamentary privilege and criminal accountability.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether Articles 105(2) and 194(2), which protect legislators from proceedings in any court in respect of anything said or any vote given in Parliament, extended to shield them from criminal prosecution for accepting a bribe to cast that vote. The privilege clauses use broad language — "in respect of" any vote — and the accused argued that a prosecution for the act of voting, however motivated, fell squarely within that protection. The state and the CBI countered that the bribery offence was complete at the moment money changed hands and did not require any inquiry into the vote itself. The question had significant consequences: an affirmative answer would effectively immunise cash-for-vote corruption from prosecution as long as the MP voted as instructed.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench, by a majority of three to two, held that MPs who actually voted as directed by the bribe were protected by parliamentary privilege under Article 105(2), because the prosecution necessarily required scrutiny of the vote. The majority reasoned that the privilege attached to the act of voting without qualification, and any proceeding that made the vote the subject of inquiry fell within its scope. Only MPs who took the bribe but did not vote as promised lost the protection, since in those cases the vote was not itself under scrutiny. The minority judges dissented, arguing that bribery is complete before the House sits and that privilege cannot sanitise a corrupt bargain. This majority position was squarely overruled by a seven-judge bench in Sita Soren v. Union of India (2024), which held that bribery antecedent to a vote is not protected by privilege regardless of how the vote was ultimately cast.

## Why the case remains central

The case is still the starting point for discussions of privilege, bribery, and the line between legislative immunity and criminal accountability. It illustrates how broadly worded constitutional protections can produce outcomes that cut against democratic intuitions. Although the specific holding on bribery immunity has now been overruled, the reasoning about the scope of Article 105(2) continues to inform privilege doctrine across a wider range of situations.

## Long-term impact

Later rulings, most decisively Sita Soren (2024), have reversed the bribery-immunity holding, but the case remains unavoidable in any serious privilege timeline. The confrontation between Articles 105(2) and the integrity of democratic processes that this case dramatised is still the frame within which courts and commentators analyse legislative immunity claims.
