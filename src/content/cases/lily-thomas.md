## Background

Section 8(4) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 had for decades allowed a Member of Parliament or a State Legislature who was convicted of an offence carrying a sentence of two years or more to continue in office so long as an appeal against the conviction was pending in a higher court. This created a significant asymmetry: an ordinary citizen convicted of the same offence would face immediate legal consequences, but a sitting legislator could remain in the legislature for years while exhausting appellate remedies. Lily Thomas, a practising lawyer, filed a public interest writ challenging this provision as unconstitutional. The challenge was grounded in the argument that Parliament had exceeded its authority under Articles 102 and 191, which allow Parliament to prescribe disqualifications but do not authorise Parliament to suspend a disqualification that has already been incurred.

## The legal question in detail

The central legal question was whether Section 8(4) was constitutionally valid. Articles 102 and 191 empower Parliament to legislate on disqualifications for membership of Parliament and state legislatures. The question was whether this power included the further power to defer the operation of a disqualification — effectively suspending a constitutional consequence that had already been triggered — pending the outcome of an appeal. A related question was whether the Constitution's scheme of disqualification, which differentiates between grounds that can be contested before the Speaker and grounds that operate automatically by law, left room for a statutory saving clause of the kind Section 8(4) provided.

## What the court decided

A two-judge bench of the Supreme Court unanimously struck down Section 8(4) as unconstitutional. The Court held that Articles 102 and 191 grant Parliament the power to prescribe disqualifications but do not grant any power to suspend or defer a disqualification once it has been incurred. Once a person is convicted and sentenced to two or more years of imprisonment, the disqualification under Section 8 takes effect immediately by operation of law, and Parliament cannot legislate away that consequence. The filing of an appeal does not reverse the conviction; it only creates the possibility that the conviction may later be set aside. A stay of conviction granted by a court in an appeal is a different matter and may affect the operation of disqualification, but Section 8(4)'s blanket suspension of disqualification for all convicted legislators pending any appeal went further than the Constitution permitted. The judgment was immediately consequential: several sitting legislators including Rashid Masood and Lalu Prasad Yadav were convicted around the same time, and the ruling meant they were required to vacate their seats without the protection the repealed provision would have offered.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment closes the gap between conviction and constitutional disqualification, making criminal accountability in legislatures immediate rather than delayed. It dismantled a provision that had effectively insulated convicted politicians from the consequences of their criminal records for as long as the judicial process could be extended. The case is the foundational authority for the proposition that Parliament cannot use its law-making power to weaken constitutional disqualification mechanisms in its own favour.

## Long-term impact

It remains a baseline case whenever the criminalisation of politics or qualification rules for legislators are examined. The ruling directly accelerated subsequent efforts — largely driven by the Association for Democratic Reforms and similar organisations — to require fuller criminal-record disclosure in election affidavits and to improve the speed of trial court proceedings in cases involving sitting legislators. The judgment also raised questions about the constitutional status of stays of conviction granted by appellate courts, a question that continues to generate litigation distinct from the original Section 8(4) challenge.
