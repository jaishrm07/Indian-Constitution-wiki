## Background

The Ahmedabad Municipal Corporation's elected term had expired without fresh elections being called, leaving the city governed by an administrator rather than an elected body. Kishansing Tomar challenged the delay, arguing that the 74th Amendment, which inserted Part IX-A into the Constitution, imposed a positive and mandatory duty to hold municipal elections before the expiry of the five-year term under Article 243U. The state government and the State Election Commission had allowed administrative and data-collection exercises to drift, effectively treating the constitutional timetable as aspirational rather than binding. The case therefore tested whether constitutional provisions on local democracy could be enforced as hard obligations through judicial mandamus or whether they were merely directory guidelines leaving large discretion with the executive.

## The legal question in detail

The core legal question was whether Article 243U's requirement that municipal elections be completed before the expiry of the five-year term created an enforceable constitutional obligation or merely a directory instruction. If it was a mandatory obligation, courts could issue a writ of mandamus directing the State Election Commission to proceed with elections regardless of administrative inconvenience or pending delimitation exercises. The case also asked whether the State Election Commission's independence under Article 243K — modelled on the Election Commission's independence under Article 324 — meant it could be compelled by the executive to delay elections, or whether it was under a constitutional duty to resist such pressure and proceed on time.

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court held that Article 243U creates a mandatory constitutional duty and that the expiry of a municipal body's five-year term without fresh elections being held is a constitutional breach, not a mere irregularity. Courts have jurisdiction to issue mandamus directing the State Election Commission to hold elections without further delay, and no administrative exercise — including ward delimitation or OBC data collection — can justify indefinitely postponing the poll. The State Election Commission, as an independent constitutional authority, is obligated to act and cannot shelter behind executive inaction or executive instruction. The judgment drew a direct parallel between the constitutional protection of electoral continuity in Article 243U and the protection of parliamentary and assembly terms in Part XV, treating local elections as part of the same constitutional commitment to periodic democratic renewal. The practical effect was to place timely local elections on the same constitutional footing as national and state elections and to give affected citizens a clear judicial remedy when that timetable is breached.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment makes local democracy a constitutional timetable, not a matter left open-ended by political convenience. By treating delay as a constitutional violation remediable by mandamus rather than a matter of administrative discretion, the Court gave Part IX-A its teeth. The case is especially significant because local governments are the tier of democratic governance most frequently subjected to delayed elections, and Kishansing Tomar remains the primary authority litigants cite when seeking court intervention.

## Long-term impact

It is the key case for delayed municipal elections, State Election Commission independence, and the enforcement of Part IX-A. States that have sought to postpone local elections — often using ongoing delimitation or OBC reservation data-collection as a justification — routinely face petitions citing this judgment, including in the prolonged litigation over Maharashtra and Madhya Pradesh local body elections in the 2020s. The case also intersects with K. Krishna Murthy, because the triple-test requirement for OBC reservations has itself become a reason states give for electoral delay, creating a tension the courts continue to manage.
