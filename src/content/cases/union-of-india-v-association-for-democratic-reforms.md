## Background

Association for Democratic Reforms, a civil society organisation, filed a public interest litigation challenging the absence of any legal requirement for candidates contesting elections to disclose their criminal records, financial assets and liabilities, and educational qualifications to voters before polling. At the time, neither the Representation of the People Act nor the Election Commission's forms required such disclosure, meaning voters had no official means of accessing this information about the people seeking to represent them. The Delhi High Court ruled in favour of disclosure, directing the Election Commission to require candidates to furnish this information. The Union of India challenged that judgment before the Supreme Court, arguing that such a requirement could only be introduced by Parliament through legislation and could not be imposed by judicial direction.

## The legal question

The central question was whether voters possess a constitutionally protected right to information about candidates seeking their votes, and if so, whether that right could be enforced through judicial directions to the Election Commission in the absence of specific legislation. The Court had to address whether the right of free speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a), read with the constitutional framework for elections under Articles 324 and 326, generates an enforceable right to material information without which electoral choice is not genuinely free. A subsidiary question was whether the Election Commission had constitutional authority under Article 324 to require disclosures that Parliament had not explicitly mandated.

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court upheld the Delhi High Court's direction and held that voters have a fundamental right to know relevant information about candidates as part of their right to free and fair elections and freedom of speech under Article 19(1)(a). The Court reasoned that the right to vote is meaningful only if voters possess sufficient information to make an informed choice, and that information about a candidate's criminal background, wealth, and qualifications is directly relevant to that choice. Relying on its earlier holding in Mohinder Singh Gill that Article 324 gives the Election Commission residual constitutional authority to fill gaps in electoral law, the Court held that the EC could and should require disclosure through its forms. The directions were accordingly issued requiring candidates to file affidavits disclosing criminal antecedents, assets, liabilities, and educational qualifications before each election. The decision was subsequently confirmed and elaborated in People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India.

## Significance

This case is the foundational precedent for candidate-disclosure requirements in Indian electoral law. It constitutionalized the idea that electoral transparency is not a legislative favour to voters but a constitutional right, grounded in the premise that voting without adequate information is not genuine democratic participation. The affidavit requirements introduced following this case transformed the availability of candidate information in India and led to the creation of public databases of candidate declarations. The case is also a key precedent for the Election Commission's use of its residual Article 324 authority to fill regulatory gaps that Parliament has not addressed.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment constitutionalized the idea that a voter cannot make a meaningful choice without basic information about candidates. Later affidavit rules, disclosure formats, and Election Commission portals all sit in its shadow.

## Long-term impact

This remains the opening case for candidate transparency, the right to know in elections, and every later argument that disclosure is part of constitutional democracy rather than campaign policy.
