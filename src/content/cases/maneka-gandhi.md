## Background

Maneka Gandhi, daughter-in-law of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, had her passport impounded in July 1977 without reasons being assigned, under the Passports Act, 1967, which permitted the government to retain passports in the "public interest." She challenged the impoundment, arguing that the denial of the right to travel abroad and the refusal to give reasons violated Article 21's guarantee of personal liberty and the audi alteram partem principle of natural justice. The case arrived at the Supreme Court in the immediate aftermath of the Emergency (1975-77) and the Court's deeply criticised capitulation in ADM Jabalpur, giving it a particular political and moral urgency. The bench of seven judges saw in the case an opportunity to restore the Court's rights jurisprudence and correct the doctrinal damage of A.K. Gopalan's compartmentalised reading of Part III. The political context — the government attempting to restrict the travel of a prominent family member — also sharpened public attention on the case.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether the "procedure established by law" in Article 21 meant any procedure validly enacted by Parliament, however arbitrary or unfair, or whether that procedure had to satisfy higher standards of reasonableness and fairness. A related question was whether Articles 14, 19, and 21 operated as watertight, mutually exclusive compartments — as A.K. Gopalan had held — or whether a law depriving liberty had to satisfy the requirements of all three simultaneously. The petitioner argued that even if the Passports Act was formally valid, its application without reasons and without any opportunity to be heard was arbitrary under Article 14 and unreasonable under Article 19(5), and that these failures tainted the procedure under Article 21. The government argued that once a law was duly enacted, its application was a purely executive matter beyond the reach of due process-style scrutiny.

## What the court decided

Justice Bhagwati, writing for the seven-judge bench, held that the expression "procedure established by law" in Article 21 cannot be read to permit any procedure however unjust — the procedure must itself be fair, just, and reasonable. The Court expressly overruled the A.K. Gopalan holding that Articles 19, 21, and 22 operate as independent silos: a law that deprives a person of liberty must satisfy the non-arbitrariness requirement of Article 14 and the reasonableness requirement of Article 19 simultaneously, creating an interlocking triangular test. The right to travel abroad was held to be part of personal liberty within Article 21, rejecting the government's submission that only domestic movement counted. The Court further held that the principles of natural justice — including the right to be heard and the right to reasons — are embedded within the concept of fair procedure under Article 21. Although the Court ultimately directed the government to give reasons and hear Maneka Gandhi rather than immediately releasing her passport, the doctrinal transformation was permanent and far-reaching.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment is a turning point in Indian rights jurisprudence because it rejected the narrow positivism of A.K. Gopalan and moved toward a substantive conception of liberty that treats procedural fairness as constitutionally compelled rather than legislatively granted. By fusing Articles 14, 19, and 21 into a single evaluative framework, it made constitutional review of liberty-restricting laws both broader and more demanding. The case also demonstrated that courts can use procedural rights cases to recalibrate the constitutional order after periods of executive overreach. Every subsequent expansion of Article 21 — whether to privacy, health, livelihood, dignity, or education — draws on the interpretive method Maneka Gandhi established.

## Long-term impact

Later privacy, dignity, travel, and procedural fairness cases build on the doctrinal opening created by Maneka Gandhi, and the case is the essential bridge between the narrow early Constitution jurisprudence and the expansive rights culture of modern Indian constitutional law. The 2017 privacy judgment in K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India, which unanimously recognised a constitutional right to privacy, explicitly traces its lineage through Maneka Gandhi's interlocking-rights methodology. The case also influenced the development of administrative law: the principle that executive action affecting liberty must be accompanied by reasons is now treated as a basic requirement of lawful governance. Maneka Gandhi changed how lawyers, judges, and citizens understand the relationship between law, procedure, and human freedom.
