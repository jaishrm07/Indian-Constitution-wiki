## Background

Champakam Dorairajan, a Brahmin woman from Madras, applied for admission to a government medical college and was denied a seat despite being academically qualified, because the state government had established communal reservation rosters that allocated seats to specific communities in fixed proportions based on religion and caste. Under this scheme, seats were distributed among Brahmins, non-Brahmin Hindus, Backward Hindus, Harijans, Anglo-Indians and Indian Christians, Muslims, and other communities in specified numbers regardless of merit. She argued that this scheme violated her fundamental rights under Article 15(1), which prohibited discrimination by the state on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth, and Article 29(2), which specifically prohibited denial of admission to state-maintained or state-aided educational institutions on those grounds. The state defended the scheme as a legitimate exercise of its authority to ensure that all communities received representation in professional education. This was among the earliest fundamental rights cases to reach the Supreme Court after the Constitution came into force, and it forced an immediate confrontation between formal equality and social policy.

## The legal question in detail

The central legal question was whether communal government orders fixing category-wise seats in state educational institutions were constitutionally valid under the original text of Articles 15 and 29, which prohibited discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth, and language. The state argued that the Directive Principles of State Policy — in particular Article 46, which directed the state to promote the educational and economic interests of weaker sections — provided a constitutional basis for preferential allocation of seats. The deeper question was whether Directive Principles could be used to limit or override Fundamental Rights, or whether the Fundamental Rights chapter represented absolute constraints on state action that the Directive Principles could not displace. The Court was thus asked to determine the constitutional hierarchy between Part III and Part IV, a question that would recur across many later cases.

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court held that the communal reservation order violated Article 29(2), which prohibited denial of admission to state-maintained or state-aided educational institutions on grounds of religion, race, caste, or language. The Court ruled that Directive Principles under Part IV of the Constitution could not override Fundamental Rights guaranteed under Part III, establishing a clear hierarchy between the two parts in cases of direct conflict. The state’s reliance on Article 46 was rejected on the ground that it could guide legislative policy but could not authorise the state to act in direct contravention of explicit constitutional rights. This decision prompted Parliament to pass the First Constitutional Amendment in 1951, which inserted Article 15(4) into the Constitution — explicitly enabling the state to make special provisions for the advancement of socially and educationally backward classes and scheduled castes and scheduled tribes. That insertion transformed the constitutional architecture of affirmative action in education, providing a textual foundation that the original Constitution had not included and that all subsequent reservation jurisprudence has had to engage.

## Why the case remains central

The decision is the constitutional starting point for the reservation story in education. It made clear that state classifications in admissions could not be treated as ordinary policy choices exempt from fundamental rights scrutiny, and that the government bore the burden of pointing to a clear constitutional authorisation whenever it allocated public educational resources on the basis of community identity. By exposing the gap between the original constitutional text and the demands of social justice policy, the case also demonstrated how constitutional litigation can reshape constitutional text itself.

## Doctrinal line

The immediate constitutional consequence was the First Amendment and the later framing of Article 15(4), which allowed the Constitution to speak more clearly about substantive equality and the state’s power to address historical disadvantage. The case thus occupies a unique position in Indian constitutional history: it is one of the very few judicial decisions that directly caused a constitutional amendment, and the amendment it caused fundamentally altered the trajectory of equality law. All subsequent debates about the scope of reservations, the identification of backward classes, and the ceiling on reservations trace their doctrinal starting point to this intervention.

## Long-term impact

Every modern argument about educational reservations has to account for this case’s insistence that equality provisions mean something concrete at the institutional level and cannot be simply set aside by reference to social objectives. The case established that constitutional design required explicit text if the state was to depart from the formal equality principle, a lesson that lawmakers absorbed and that shaped constitutional amendments for decades. It also opened the larger question of how to reconcile equality and affirmative action within a single constitutional framework — a question that Indra Sawhney, Ashoka Kumar Thakur, and many other landmark cases have continued to grapple with.
