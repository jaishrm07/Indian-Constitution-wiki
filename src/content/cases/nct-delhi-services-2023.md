## Background

The 2018 Constitution Bench judgment in Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India resolved the general framework question: the LG is bound by the aid and advice of the elected Council of Ministers for all matters within the Delhi Assembly's legislative competence. However, the 2018 judgment expressly left open one critical question — control over the services, meaning the transfers, postings, and service conditions of the higher bureaucracy (IAS and DANICS officers) who actually implement the decisions of Delhi's elected government.

This was not an abstract question. The officers who ran Delhi's health, education, water, and public works departments were transferred by the Centre through the LG, even when those departments were formally under the Delhi government's executive authority. The elected government found that it could not effectively govern if its own administrative machinery was controlled by an authority not accountable to it. The LG and the Union of India maintained that services fell within the Union List and that the 2018 judgment's reasoning did not extend to the appointments framework.

The question was referred back to a five-judge Constitution Bench, which heard it over a prolonged period. The bench delivered its unanimous judgment in May 2023.

## The legal question in detail

The core question was how to read the entries in the Seventh Schedule in relation to Delhi's hybrid constitutional status. The Union List includes "Union services" (Entry 70). The State List includes "state public services" (Entry 41). The concurrent list contains an entry on matters of service regulation that overlaps with both. Given that Delhi is not a full State, does Entry 41 of the State List apply to it at all? And if Group A cadre officers in Delhi are recruited and ultimately controlled by the Union, does the elected Delhi government have any executive power over their postings and transfers within its own departments? The deeper constitutional issue was whether effective democratic governance requires that the executive machinery administering the competences of the elected government should be answerable to that government. If the officers posted in, say, the health department could be transferred away by the LG without the elected government's concurrence, the constitutional right to govern health — formally within the Assembly's competence — was hollow in practice.

## What the court decided

The five-judge Bench, in a unanimous judgment by Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud, held that services — excluding those relating to police, public order, and land — fall within the executive domain of the elected Delhi government. The Court reasoned from the democratic principle articulated in 2018: representative government requires that those who are constitutionally accountable to the electorate must also control the machinery through which they govern. An elected government that commands a majority in the Assembly but cannot transfer officers in its own departments is not a government in any meaningful sense. The triple test of accountability — that the elected government should control those who are answerable to it — ran through the Court's reasoning. The LG's power to refer and the Union's authority over the three reserved subjects were left undisturbed. For all other matters, including transfers and postings of officers working in Delhi's administration on non-reserved subjects, the LG was bound to act on the advice of the elected Council of Ministers.

## Parliament's legislative reversal

The judgment's practical life was short. Within weeks of the decision, Parliament enacted two measures designed to reverse it. The first was the Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi (Amendment) Act, 2023, which amended the GNCTD Act, 1991, to create a new "National Capital Civil Service Authority" controlling the transfer and posting of Group A officers in Delhi. The Authority comprises the Chief Minister and two senior civil servants, but decisions are taken by majority — meaning the two bureaucrats can outvote the Chief Minister — and the LG is the appellate authority who has the final word. The second measure was the 106th Constitutional Amendment to Article 239AA, which expressly vested legislative power over services in Parliament, removing any ambiguity about Parliament's competence to override the elected Delhi government on the services question. The constitutional validity of both measures was challenged and remains under judicial scrutiny.

## Why the case remains central

The 2023 Services judgment, even after legislative reversal, is constitutionally important for two reasons. First, its reasoning on effective representative governance — that formal constitutional authority over a subject must carry with it real administrative control — is a substantial contribution to the jurisprudence on Article 239AA and by extension to the broader question of what meaningful democratic self-governance requires. Second, the legislative response it provoked raised an unresolved constitutional question: can Parliament, through ordinary legislation and constitutional amendment, eliminate the effective executive authority of an elected government on matters the Constitution has placed within that government's domain, without violating the basic structure? The elected Delhi government's challenge to the 106th Amendment on basic structure grounds is pending before the Court.

## Long-term impact

The judgment and its legislative reversal together illustrate the limits of judicial resolution of structural power disputes when Parliament retains the authority to respond through legislation and constitutional amendment. The episode is likely to become a canonical case study in the relationship between constitutional adjudication and parliamentary override in India's federal architecture. It also illustrates the difficulty of creating genuine representative government for Union Territories through constitutional amendment while the Union retains the instruments of administrative control.
