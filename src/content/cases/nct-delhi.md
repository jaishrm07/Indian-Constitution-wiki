## Background

Delhi's constitutional status is unusual in the Indian federal system. It is not a full State, but unlike most Union Territories it has a directly elected Legislative Assembly and a Council of Ministers accountable to that Assembly. This hybrid arrangement was created by the 69th Constitutional Amendment, which inserted Article 239AA into the Constitution with effect from 1992. The Article gave Delhi an elected legislature with power to make laws on matters in the State List and Concurrent List, subject to three exceptions — public order, police, and land — which remained with the Union. A Lieutenant Governor, appointed by the President, was designated as Delhi's administrator.

The practical relationship between the elected Council of Ministers and the LG deteriorated sharply after 2015, when the Aam Aadmi Party formed the Delhi government with a commanding majority in the Assembly. The LG began referring a substantial volume of decisions made by the elected government to the President for consideration, effectively putting routine governance on hold. The elected government maintained that the LG was a constitutional figurehead bound to act on ministerial advice; the LG and the Union of India maintained that the LG had broad independent discretion and that Delhi's special status meant the elected government's authority was severely constrained. The impasse generated a reference to a five-judge Constitution Bench.

## The legal question in detail

The central constitutional question was the scope and content of the LG's authority under Article 239AA. Three issues arose with particular force. First, does Article 239AA(4) — which allows the LG to refer a matter to the President where he disagrees with the Council of Ministers — create a general and independent discretionary power, or is it a limited safety valve to be used sparingly in cases of genuine constitutional concern? Second, does the principle of aid and advice, which structures the relationship between elected governments and constitutional heads under Articles 74 and 163, apply by analogy to the relationship between the LG and the Delhi Council of Ministers? Third, can a constitutional office-holder use procedural mechanisms such as referral and concurrence-seeking to obstruct the day-to-day governance of the elected executive?

Underlying these technical questions was a deeper constitutional issue: whether Article 239AA was designed to give Delhi a genuine representative government — limited in scope but real in its domain — or whether it was principally a mechanism for Union control dressed in democratic clothing. The answer to that prior question determined how every subsequent textual issue was to be resolved.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench delivered a unanimous judgment authored by Chief Justice Dipak Misra, joined by Justices A.K. Sikri, A.M. Khanwilkar, D.Y. Chandrachud, and Ashok Bhushan. The Court held that Article 239AA must be interpreted in accordance with its democratic purpose. The insertion of an elected Assembly and a responsible Council of Ministers was meant to deliver genuine representative government to Delhi's citizens, not to create a formal democracy subject to effective Union control through an unelected LG. The Court drew on the structural analogy between the LG's constitutional position and that of a Governor under Part VI: both are constitutional heads who must ordinarily act on the aid and advice of the elected executive and may use their independent powers only in the specific circumstances the Constitution defines. The Article 239AA(4) referral mechanism was therefore an exceptional safety valve, not a routine instrument.

The practical holding was that for all matters within the Delhi Assembly's legislative competence — health, education, water supply, public services, infrastructure, and similar subjects — the LG was bound by the Council of Ministers' advice and could not substitute his independent judgment. The three reserved subjects (police, public order, land) remained with the Centre and were unaffected. For those reserved subjects, the LG acted as the Union's representative with genuine independent authority; for everything else within the Assembly's competence, the elected government governed.

The Court also addressed the question of day-to-day governance: it held that the elected government did not need the LG's concurrence before implementing its decisions, and that the LG's proper course on disagreement was the limited referral mechanism under Article 239AA(4), not routine obstruction. Constitutional offices, the Court stated, may not be deployed in ways that paralyse elected governance.

## Why the case remains central

The case gave constitutional language to the tension between Union control and democratic self-government in the capital. It is the foundational statement on Article 239AA's constitutional architecture and purpose, and its reasoning on the limits of LG discretion, the applicability of aid-and-advice doctrine, and the impropriety of using constitutional offices to obstruct governance continues to govern the analysis of Delhi's status. The judgment has been cited in constitutional challenges to Governor conduct in States including Maharashtra, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Punjab, extending its significance well beyond Delhi itself. The Court's holding that constitutional silence on the LG's authority should be resolved in favour of democratic governance has become a general principle that informs the relationship between elected governments and appointed constitutional heads across the country.

The case should be read alongside its two sequels. In 2023, a further Constitution Bench held in the Services case that control over transfer and posting of the higher bureaucracy (Group A officers) fell within the elected Delhi government's domain — a ruling Parliament then reversed through the 106th Constitutional Amendment and the GNCTD (Amendment) Act, 2023. The constitutional validity of that legislative reversal remains pending before the Court.

## Long-term impact

Most serious constitutional disputes about Delhi governance still return to the reasoning in this judgment. The 2018 ruling set the constitutional baseline against which every subsequent development — the 2023 Services judgment, Parliament's legislative response, the pending challenge to the 106th Amendment — has been measured. Beyond Delhi, the judgment contributed to an emerging body of doctrine limiting the ways in which appointed constitutional officers can use procedural mechanisms to obstruct elected governments, a doctrine with practical relevance wherever Governors have been in confrontation with state cabinets. The Court's insistence that representative government is the constitutional default for the subjects within the Assembly's competence, not a privilege that requires the LG's ongoing concurrence, has reshaped the constitutional conversation about hybrid governance frameworks more broadly.
