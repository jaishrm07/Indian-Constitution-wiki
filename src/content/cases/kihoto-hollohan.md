## Background

The Tenth Schedule was inserted into the Constitution by the 52nd Amendment in 1985, in direct response to the political instability of the 1970s and early 1980s, during which large-scale defections — sometimes called "aaya ram gaya ram" politics — destabilised governments at both the national and state levels. The Schedule disqualified a legislator who voluntarily gave up party membership or voted contrary to party directions, while allowing mergers of at least two-thirds of a party's legislators as an exception. The Speaker of each House was designated as the sole authority to decide disqualification questions, with paragraph 6 of the Schedule making the Speaker's decision final and beyond judicial review. The constitutional validity of the Tenth Schedule was challenged almost immediately in petitions that raised overlapping grounds: that it curtailed the freedom of speech of members, that it assigned judicial functions to a political officer (the Speaker), and that the amendment required ratification by state legislatures because it affected the constitutional relationship between MPs and their constituents.

## The legal question in detail

Three distinct but connected legal questions were before the court. First, whether the Tenth Schedule violated Articles 105 and 194 by curtailing the freedom of speech of legislators and the right to vote according to conscience. Second, whether paragraph 6's finality clause was valid, given that it purported to exclude judicial review of decisions by the Speaker — an authority who is not independent of the party in power. Third, whether the 52nd Amendment required ratification by at least half the state legislatures under the proviso to Article 368(2), on the ground that by altering disqualification rules it affected the composition of legislative bodies and thereby changed the scope of legislative power enjoyed by state assemblies.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Tenth Schedule by a majority of three to two, holding that the law furthered democracy and constitutional values by curbing the unprincipled floor-crossing that had corroded the stability and legitimacy of legislative bodies. The majority held that the Schedule did not violate the freedom of speech of legislators because the Constitution itself, through Article 102, permits Parliament to add to the grounds of disqualification, and the Schedule fell within that power. On the question of ratification, the majority held that the amendment did not alter the representation of states in Parliament and therefore did not require state ratification under Article 368(2). Most significantly, the majority struck down paragraph 6's finality clause to the extent that it excluded judicial review: the Court held that while the Speaker's decision was entitled to deference, the High Courts and the Supreme Court could review it for violation of natural justice, jurisdictional error, or mala fides. This judicial check on the Speaker's disqualification power has remained one of the most actively litigated aspects of constitutional law in the decades since.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment shaped one of the most consequential frameworks governing legislative discipline and political survival in India. By preserving judicial review while otherwise sustaining the Speaker's role, Kihoto Hollohan created a doctrinal tension that reappears in every subsequent disqualification dispute.

## Long-term impact

Whenever legislatures face defections, disqualification disputes, or government collapse, Kihoto Hollohan returns as the doctrinal anchor. The court's insistence on limited judicial review has been the basis for interventions in Manipur, Goa, Maharashtra, and other states where the Speaker's impartiality has been in question — and the unresolved problem of a partisan Speaker adjudicating disqualification petitions continues to prompt calls for institutional reform.
