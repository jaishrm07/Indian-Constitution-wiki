## Background

Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's election from the Rae Bareli constituency in 1971 was set aside by the Allahabad High Court in June 1975 on grounds of electoral malpractice under the Representation of the People Act, including misuse of government machinery during the campaign. Rather than step down pending appeal, the government filed an appeal to the Supreme Court and simultaneously promulgated the 39th Constitutional Amendment, which placed the elections of the Prime Minister, President, Vice-President, and Speaker of the Lok Sabha entirely beyond judicial review. The Amendment operated retroactively, purporting to validate the Prime Minister's election regardless of what any court had found or might find. The Emergency was declared the following day, creating an atmosphere in which the constitutional stakes of the litigation were unusually high. The case thus placed the Supreme Court in the position of ruling on both the validity of a constitutional amendment and the integrity of democratic institutions while a formal state of emergency was in force.

## The legal question in detail

The primary question was whether the 39th Amendment, by removing the Prime Minister's election from judicial scrutiny with retroactive effect, was constitutionally valid. This required the Court to assess whether free and fair elections and judicial review of those elections were features so fundamental to the constitutional order that they could not be destroyed even by Parliament acting through the amendment power under Article 368. A secondary question arose from Kesavananda Bharati (1973): if Parliament cannot destroy the basic structure of the Constitution, did an amendment that immunised a specific election from legal challenge cross that line. The case also required the Court to determine the interaction between Article 329A — the provision introduced by the 39th Amendment — and ordinary constitutional provisions governing elections and the rule of law. The answer turned on whether democratic legitimacy, in the sense of elections subject to impartial adjudication, was a structural value rather than a mere statutory convenience.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench struck down the operative clause of the 39th Amendment that removed the Prime Minister's election from judicial review, holding that free and fair elections are an element of the basic structure that Parliament cannot destroy through the amendment power. The Court applied the Kesavananda doctrine, but this was the first time it was used to actually invalidate a specific constitutional amendment, giving the basic structure doctrine operational force rather than merely theoretical existence. Indira Gandhi's election was accordingly restored to challenge under ordinary law, though the judgment also upheld other parts of the 39th Amendment that had independent justification. The ruling made clear that the amendment power is not a mechanism by which incumbents can entrench themselves against the ordinary accountability processes of a constitutional democracy. Justice Krishna Iyer's concurring reasoning on democratic legitimacy has been widely cited in subsequent electoral jurisprudence.

## Why the case remains central

The election case matters because it linked democratic legitimacy to constitutional structure rather than leaving it entirely to Parliament to define. The Court treated electoral fairness and judicial review of elections as structural commitments — features that give the Constitution its democratic character — and refused to accept that a formal amendment could simply redesignate them as matters of legislative discretion. This was the moment when basic structure shifted from a principle announced in Kesavananda to a doctrine with real consequences for constitutional amendments.

## Long-term impact

Later disputes over amendment power, electoral design, and institutional insulation still return to this case's warning against constitutional self-protection by incumbents. The judgment established that no branch of government can use its formal authority to place itself beyond the reach of the constitutional framework that grants that authority in the first place. It remains a foundational reference point whenever questions arise about the relationship between democratic accountability and the limits of constitutional amendment.
