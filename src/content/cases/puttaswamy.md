## Background

Retired Justice K.S. Puttaswamy challenged the government's Aadhaar scheme, which required biometric enrollment and linked welfare benefits to biometric authentication. A preliminary question arose: did the Constitution guarantee a fundamental right to privacy at all? Two earlier Constitution Bench decisions — M.P. Sharma (1954) and Kharak Singh (1963) — had held there was no such right. The government argued those precedents barred any privacy claim, making it unnecessary to examine the constitutional validity of Aadhaar on its merits. Given the significance of the question, the matter was referred to a nine-judge Constitution Bench.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether the right to privacy is a constitutionally protected fundamental right under the Indian Constitution, and if so, whether it is absolute or subject to limitations. The Court also had to determine whether privacy could be located within the existing text of Part III — particularly Articles 14, 19, and 21 — or whether it required independent textual recognition. A subsidiary question was whether any right to privacy, once recognised, could be weighed against compelling state interests such as efficient welfare delivery and national security. The answers would determine the constitutional fate not only of Aadhaar but of the entire ecosystem of state surveillance, biometric databases, and data collection that the government was building.

## What the court decided

A nine-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that privacy is a fundamental right protected under Articles 14, 19, and 21. All earlier judgments denying a constitutional right to privacy — most importantly M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh — were expressly overruled. The Court held that privacy encompasses bodily integrity, personal autonomy, informational self-determination, and dignity, and that these dimensions are integral to what it means to be a free individual under the Constitution. While the right is not absolute, any state interference must satisfy a triple test: legality (the interference must be authorised by law), legitimate aim (there must be a compelling state interest), and proportionality (the means used must be proportionate to the ends sought). Six separate concurring opinions were delivered, each elaborating the scope and philosophical underpinnings of the right, making the judgment one of the most richly reasoned in the Court's history.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment gave constitutional language to modern concerns about technology, identity systems, bodily autonomy, and informational control. By tying privacy to dignity and liberty under Article 21, it transformed privacy from a common-law interest into a justiciable constitutional guarantee capable of checking state power. It also provided the doctrinal foundation for later challenges to Aadhaar on the merits and for the long-running debate about a comprehensive data protection law. Every argument about surveillance, genetic privacy, reproductive autonomy, or behavioural profiling by the state now begins with Puttaswamy.

## Long-term impact

Later disputes over data protection, surveillance, and platform governance often operate in the shadow of Puttaswamy. The triple test articulated by the Court has become the standard framework for evaluating whether any state restriction on a fundamental right is constitutionally justified, influencing analysis well beyond privacy. The judgment also set the stage for Navtej Singh Johar (2018), where the Court relied heavily on the privacy and dignity reasoning of Puttaswamy to decriminalise consensual same-sex conduct. It remains the most important constitutional privacy judgment in Indian legal history.
