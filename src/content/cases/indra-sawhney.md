## Background

The case arose from the Mandal Commission report of 1980, which had recommended 27% reservation in central government employment for Other Backward Classes (OBCs) — a category distinct from the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes who already had reservations. For nearly a decade, successive governments declined to implement the recommendation. In August 1990, Prime Minister V.P. Singh's government issued an Office Memorandum implementing the Mandal recommendation, triggering nationwide protests and the filing of writ petitions challenging the order. The case was eventually referred to a nine-judge Constitution Bench, one of the largest in the history of the Supreme Court of India, to resolve the deep constitutional questions about the scope and limits of reservation under Articles 14, 15, and 16.

The constitutional backdrop was complex. The Court had previously upheld the general power to make reservations in State of Kerala v. N.M. Thomas (1976), and the existence of SC/ST reservations was well-established. But the Mandal implementation raised new questions: What made a community an "Other Backward Class"? Could economic criteria alone determine backwardness? Was there a ceiling on the total proportion of posts that could be reserved? Did reservation extend to promotions, or only initial appointments? Could the more affluent sections of a backward class be excluded from reservation benefits? The bench grappled with all of these simultaneously.

## The legal question in detail

The petitioners attacked the 27% OBC reservation on multiple grounds. They argued that backwardness under Article 16(4) had to be based on social and educational criteria — not merely economic status — and that many of the Mandal Commission's communities were not genuinely backward. They contended that caste alone could not be the basis for classifying "backward classes" under Article 16(4), which uses the phrase "backward class of citizens" rather than the word "caste." They argued that existing SC/ST reservations of 22.5% plus the new 27% OBC reservation would together reach 49.5%, approaching the point at which reservations would swallow the rule of merit-based appointment entirely. They also questioned whether any of the Mandal communities qualified as constitutionally backward under an objective test.

The government argued that the Mandal Commission's identification of backward communities was the product of extensive social investigation, that caste in India was deeply correlated with social and economic backwardness, and that Article 16(4) permitted reservations for any identifiable backward class, not just communities already on the official SC/ST lists.

## What the court decided

The nine-judge bench, by a 6-3 majority on several key propositions, delivered the following holdings that together constitute the constitutional framework for OBC reservation:

**Backwardness and Article 16(4):** The Court held that "backward class of citizens" in Article 16(4) is a distinct concept from the poverty-line definition and from the SC/ST categories. Caste is a relevant but not the sole criterion for identifying backwardness — a community may be socially and educationally backward without falling on the SC/ST lists. The test for backwardness under Article 16(4) requires social and educational disadvantage, not merely economic deprivation.

**The 50% ceiling:** The Court held, by majority, that total reservations under Articles 16(4) and 16(4A) cannot ordinarily exceed 50% of the total available posts or seats. This ceiling applies cumulatively across SC, ST, and OBC reservations. The Court acknowledged that exceptional circumstances — such as the special treatment of remote areas or particularly disadvantaged communities — might justify exceeding the ceiling, but set the ceiling as the general rule. This holding was based on the Court's reading that Article 16(1) guarantees equality of opportunity and that systematic exclusion of the unreserved categories from half or more of all posts would destroy the essence of that guarantee.

**The creamy layer exclusion:** The Court held that the more advanced and affluent sections of an OBC community — the "creamy layer" — must be excluded from OBC reservation in employment. The rationale was that reservation is a remedy for disadvantage, and the better-off segments of a backward community no longer need the remedy; allowing them to continue benefiting from reservation after they have advanced would divert the remedy from those who genuinely need it and would violate the principle that Article 16(4) is an exception to, not a negation of, Article 16(1). The creamy layer exclusion was held not to apply to SC/ST reservations, where social stigma and untouchability-based discrimination warranted full group-based protection.

**Reservations in promotions:** The majority held that Article 16(4) as originally enacted did not permit reservations in promotions — it authorised reservations only at the stage of initial appointment. This holding was directly overruled by the Constitution (Seventy-Seventh Amendment) Act 1995, which inserted Article 16(4A) specifically authorising reservation in promotions for SC/ST employees in government posts, subject to the Court's further conditions set in subsequent cases.

**Carry-forward of vacancies:** The Court limited the carry-forward rule for unfilled reserved vacancies to two years, and held that bunching of carry-forward vacancies could not be used to exceed the 50% ceiling in any single recruitment year.

## Why the case remains central

Almost every significant reservation dispute since 1992 — whether about the quantum of reservation, eligibility for reservation, sub-classification within reserved categories, extension of reservation to promoted posts, or entirely new categories of reservation like EWS — has had to engage with Indra Sawhney as its constitutional anchor. The 50% ceiling remains the most contested numerical rule in Indian constitutional law: the Court reaffirmed it in M. Nagaraj (2006) and Jarnail Singh (2018) while in Janhit Abhiyan (2022) the majority held by 3:2 that the new Article 16(6) enabling EWS reservation was constitutionally distinct from Article 16(4) and therefore not constrained by the Indra Sawhney ceiling. The creamy layer doctrine has been extended and refined repeatedly. The promotions question spawned a line of cases from Ajit Singh v. State of Punjab (I and II) through M. Nagaraj to Jarnail Singh. The sub-classification question, finally settled in State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024), had Indra Sawhney as the starting point of the constitutional argument.

## Long-term impact

Indra Sawhney changed the constitutional landscape of reservation in three structural ways. First, it moved the debate from whether reservation was permissible to what conditions must govern it: the 50% ceiling and the creamy layer exclusion became durable institutional constraints that subsequent governments could not easily dismantle. Second, it separated the OBC category from the SC/ST category in doctrinal terms, creating two tracks with different rules for creamy layer, promotions, and ceiling calculations. Third, it prompted Parliament to respond with constitutional amendments — the 77th, 81st, and 82nd Amendments — specifically designed to restore reservation in promotions for SC/ST employees in areas where the Court had restricted it, illustrating how the case became the template for a sustained dialogue between judicial limits and legislative responses that continues to the present.

## How to find the judgment

The primary text is available on the **eSCR portal** at [scr.sci.gov.in](https://scr.sci.gov.in/) using the citation **1992 Supp (3) SCC 217**. The Supreme Court portal at [sci.gov.in](https://www.sci.gov.in/) also hosts the judgment. This is a nine-judge bench decision with multiple separate opinions running to hundreds of pages; the five key doctrinal holdings summarised above — 50% ceiling, creamy layer, backwardness test, no promotions, carry-forward limits — are the operative rules to locate and read. The Indra Sawhney follow-up (Indra Sawhney II, 2000) further clarified the promotions question and its interaction with the 77th Amendment.
