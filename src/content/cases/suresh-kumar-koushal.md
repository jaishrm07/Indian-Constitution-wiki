## Background

Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code, 1860, a provision of colonial vintage, criminalised "carnal intercourse against the order of nature with any man, woman or animal" — language that had been used by courts and police to prosecute consensual same-sex conduct between adults. In 2009, a Division Bench of the Delhi High Court in Naz Foundation v. Government of NCT of Delhi held that Section 377, insofar as it criminalised consensual same-sex conduct between adults in private, violated Articles 14, 15, and 21 of the Constitution, and read the provision down accordingly. The decision was widely welcomed by civil society and legal scholars. Several persons and religious organisations who had not been parties to the High Court proceedings filed special leave petitions before the Supreme Court challenging the High Court's ruling. Naz Foundation and the petitioners who had succeeded before the High Court sought to defend the judgment.

## The legal question in detail

The questions before the Supreme Court were whether the Delhi High Court had correctly applied constitutional doctrine in reading down Section 377, and whether the arguments from Articles 14, 15, and 21 supported the conclusion that the provision was unconstitutional as applied to consensual adult conduct. The Court also had to determine whether it was appropriate for the judiciary to effect what amounted to a significant change in criminal law when Parliament had not chosen to act. The appellants challenging the High Court judgment argued that Section 377 was a neutral provision targeting unnatural acts and not any particular group, that the High Court had exceeded its jurisdiction, and that any reform of the penal law was a matter for the legislature. Naz Foundation and the respondents defended the High Court's reasoning and argued that the mere criminalisation of identity was constitutionally untenable regardless of enforcement patterns.

## What the court decided

A two-judge bench comprising Justices Singhvi and Mukhopadhaya set aside the Delhi High Court's judgment. The Court held that Section 377 does not, on its face, discriminate against any particular group or community and that the High Court had incorrectly relied on a reading of the provision that focused on its disproportionate application rather than its text. The Court reasoned that the LGBTQ+ community constituted a "minuscule minority" of the population and that the statute's continued existence on the books could not, by that fact alone, be characterised as a violation of fundamental rights. It further held that the responsibility for deciding whether to amend or repeal a penal provision rested with Parliament, and that the courts should not substitute their judgment for that of the legislature on a question of social policy. The Court reinstated Section 377 in its unamended form, with the result that consensual same-sex conduct between adults was once again criminal in law.

## Why the case remains central

Koushal matters not for any doctrinal contribution of its own but as a cautionary illustration of constitutional regression: a judgment that narrowed rights protection by deferring to legislative inaction on a question of fundamental rights. The Court's reliance on the minority status of the affected group as a reason for restraint was directly and emphatically repudiated in Navtej Singh Johar, which held that the size of the affected population is irrelevant to the scope of constitutional protection — a minority's rights do not shrink because it is a minority. The case is also a reminder that constitutional adjudication is not always progressive and that rights gained can be judicially withdrawn, making vigilance about the composition and disposition of constitutional benches a matter of ongoing public importance.

## Overruling and subsequent history

In 2016, a five-judge Constitution Bench admitted curative petitions challenging Koushal and in 2018, in Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India, a different five-judge bench unanimously overruled it. The Navtej bench held that Koushal's reasoning was constitutionally flawed in every material respect: the counter-majoritarian role of the Court requires it to protect minorities precisely because popular majorities may not, the criminalisation of identity-constitutive conduct offends the dignity guarantee of Article 21, and constitutional morality must prevail over popular morality. The overruling was explicit and comprehensive. Koushal now survives only as a negative reference in constitutional discourse.

## Long-term impact

The reversal of Koushal in Navtej Singh Johar has reshaped the constitutional landscape on personal autonomy and anti-discrimination. Koushal's logic — that the courts should defer to Parliament on questions involving socially contested issues affecting minorities — has been firmly rejected, and the experience of its overruling has reinforced the principle that an affected group's small numbers cannot be used to dilute constitutional protection. The episode also informed subsequent thinking about the conditions under which the Supreme Court entertains curative petitions to revisit its own decisions on constitutional questions.
