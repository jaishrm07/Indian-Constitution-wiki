## Background

This case had a substantial prior history. The Supreme Court in B.K. Pavitra v. Union of India (I) (2017) had struck down an earlier Karnataka reservation in promotions scheme on the ground that the state had failed to collect adequate quantifiable data on the inadequacy of representation of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, as required by M. Nagaraj. Following that ruling, the Karnataka government commissioned a fresh survey — conducted by a retired High Court judge — that examined representation data across all departments and grade levels. On the basis of that survey, the state enacted the Karnataka Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Reservation of Appointment by Direct Recruitment to the Posts in the State Civil Services) Act 2018. The new Act was challenged before the Supreme Court by petitioners who contended that the survey methodology was flawed and that the state still had not satisfied the constitutional preconditions. The matter came before a five-judge Constitution Bench.

## The legal question in detail

The core question was whether Karnataka's fresh survey and the legislation premised upon it satisfied the inadequacy of representation condition under the Nagaraj-Jarnail framework. The petitioners argued that the survey's methodology was defective — that it relied on representational outcomes and administrative data rather than direct evidence of backwardness — and that using outcome data rather than backwardness data was constitutionally impermissible. The state argued that following Jarnail Singh's elimination of the separate backwardness condition, the constitutional obligation was to demonstrate inadequacy of representation with evidence, and that outcome-based data showing underrepresentation at various service levels was precisely the kind of quantifiable evidence the Constitution required. The Court also had to consider whether the 50 per cent ceiling was observed and whether Article 335 had been satisfied.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the 2018 Karnataka Act. The Court held that Karnataka had satisfied the constitutional preconditions for reservation in promotions. On the data question, the Court accepted that outcome data — showing the actual representation of SC and ST persons at different levels of state service relative to their population proportion and expected representation — is a constitutionally adequate basis for demonstrating inadequacy of representation. The Court rejected the petitioners' contention that Nagaraj required a more specific or direct methodology, holding that the constitutional requirement is for quantifiable evidence of underrepresentation rather than a particular form of data collection. The survey commissioned by Karnataka, while not perfect, was sufficient to meet the constitutional standard. The Court also found that the fifty per cent ceiling under Indra Sawhney had been respected and that the state had considered Article 335 efficiency requirements. Applying the post-Jarnail Singh framework, the Court confirmed that Karnataka was not required to separately demonstrate continuing backwardness of SCs and STs, since that question is resolved by Presidential notification.

## Why the case remains central

B.K. Pavitra (II) is the most detailed Supreme Court examination of what constitutes constitutionally adequate data for reservation in promotions. While M. Nagaraj and Jarnail Singh establish the preconditions in principle, this judgment shows what compliance looks like in practice. It confirms that outcome-based representational data is a valid form of evidence, provides guidance on the level of granularity required in state surveys, and demonstrates the full process that a state must follow — fresh survey, legislative action, and judicial scrutiny — to implement reservation in promotions lawfully after a previous scheme has been struck down.

## Doctrinal line

B.K. Pavitra (II) completes the doctrinal cycle begun by Indra Sawhney and developed through Nagaraj and Jarnail Singh. It shows that the reservation in promotions framework is a genuinely two-stage constitutional process: the amendments enable state action, and independent judicial review then examines whether the enabling conditions have been met. The Court's acceptance of outcome data as adequate evidence also suggests a pragmatic reading of the quantifiable data requirement — it is not an obstacle to reservation in promotions but a discipline requiring the state to act on real evidence. The case also confirmed that the Jarnail Singh modification (eliminating the backwardness condition) operates straightforwardly in subsequent litigation without requiring re-argument.

## Long-term impact

B.K. Pavitra (II) has become a reference point for other states designing or defending reservation in promotion schemes. Its acceptance of a survey-based, outcome-driven methodology has influenced how state governments structure their own data collection exercises. The case also illustrates the iterative quality of constitutional litigation in this field: a state may lose one round (B.K. Pavitra I), reform its approach, and succeed in a second round (B.K. Pavitra II), provided it conducts itself in accordance with the evolving constitutional standard. This iterative pattern reinforces the Court's underlying message that reservation in promotions is constitutionally available but not constitutionally automatic — it requires periodic empirical justification.
