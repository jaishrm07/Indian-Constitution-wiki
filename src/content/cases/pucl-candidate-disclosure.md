## Background

In 2002, the Supreme Court in Union of India v. Association for Democratic Reforms held that voters have a constitutional right under Article 19(1)(a) to know basic information about candidates — criminal antecedents, assets and liabilities, and educational qualifications — and directed the Election Commission to require disclosure through nomination affidavits. The judgment was welcomed by electoral reform advocates and threatened incumbent political parties whose candidates might have records they preferred to keep private.

Parliament responded by enacting the Representation of the People (Amendment) Act, 2002, which amended Section 33B of the Representation of the People Act, 1951. The amendment effectively nullified the Supreme Court's directions: it provided that candidates were not required to disclose any information other than what the Act itself explicitly mandated, and the Act did not mandate the disclosures the Court had directed. By inserting Section 33B, Parliament was in substance overriding a judicial direction interpreting fundamental constitutional rights through ordinary legislation — a direct challenge to the Court's power to enforce the constitutional right to information.

PUCL and other petitioners challenged the validity of this amendment before the Supreme Court. The case therefore presented a constitutional confrontation between parliamentary authority to legislate on electoral procedure and the Supreme Court's interpretation of fundamental rights as requiring candidate disclosure to make democratic choice meaningful.

## The legal question in detail

The primary legal question was whether Parliament, by enacting Section 33B, had validly circumscribed the constitutional right to voter information that the Court had recognised in the 2002 ADR ruling, or whether that provision was unconstitutional because it abridged fundamental rights under Article 19(1)(a) and Article 21 by denying voters information essential to an informed electoral choice. The respondents argued that Parliament had plenary authority over the conduct of elections under Article 327 and that the regulation of nomination procedure — including what information candidates must furnish — was a matter of legislative policy, not constitutional compulsion. The petitioners argued that the right to know, once grounded in the fundamental rights provisions of Part III, could not be legislatively extinguished by an ordinary Act of Parliament, especially one whose evident purpose was to immunise candidates from disclosure requirements they found inconvenient.

A subsidiary question was the scope of the Election Commission's independent authority under Article 324. The 2002 ruling had relied partly on Article 324's grant of superintendence and control over elections to the EC as the constitutional basis for directing disclosure in the absence of parliamentary action. The PUCL case raised the question whether Parliament could foreclose that independent constitutional authority by enacting a statute that occupied the field and excluded the EC's supplementary power.

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court struck down Section 33B of the Representation of the People Act as unconstitutional. The Court held that the voter's right to know about candidates is not a creature of statute that Parliament can retract at will; it is part of the fundamental right to free speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a), as interpreted in the context of representative democracy. An electoral choice made in ignorance of a candidate's criminal history, financial position, or educational background is not a genuinely free choice; the right to vote for a representative, when read with Article 19(1)(a), implies a right to the information without which that choice is not meaningful. Parliament cannot enact legislation that abridges a fundamental right, and Section 33B did exactly that by depriving voters of information the Constitution obliged candidates to provide.

The Court reaffirmed the Election Commission's authority under Article 324 to require disclosures as part of its superintendence over elections, and directed the EC to issue revised instructions requiring candidates to furnish information about pending criminal cases, assets and liabilities, and educational qualifications as part of their nomination papers. The directions were framed to be self-executing through the EC's administrative machinery, without requiring fresh parliamentary action.

## Why the case remains central

The case constitutionalized the idea that a voter cannot make a meaningful choice without basic information about candidates. More specifically, it established that the right to candidate information is not merely a statutory benefit subject to legislative revision but a constitutional entitlement rooted in Article 19(1)(a) and the requirements of genuine democratic participation. The judgment also resolved a structural tension: it confirmed that Parliament cannot use ordinary legislation to nullify judicial interpretations of fundamental rights simply by asserting legislative authority over the same subject matter.

The affidavit-disclosure regime that flows from the 2002 ADR judgment and this ruling has become a foundational feature of Indian electoral law. The Election Commission's affidavit portal — which allows any voter to examine a candidate's declared criminal record, assets, liabilities, and qualifications before polling — is a direct institutional product of these two decisions. Every later debate about candidate affidavits, under-disclosure, and the constitutional response to criminalization of politics takes this case as its starting point.

## Long-term impact

The PUCL ruling anchors a line of later disclosure and transparency cases in the electoral domain. Public Interest Foundation v. Union of India (2019) 3 SCC 224 built on the same foundations when petitioners returned to Court arguing that disclosure was insufficient and seeking a blanket judicial bar on candidates with serious criminal charges. The Supreme Court in that case declined to impose a disqualification rule — noting that the content of disqualification law is for Parliament — but directed parties to publish reasons for fielding candidates with pending criminal cases, using the same Article 19(1)(a) rationale this case had cemented. The constitutional architecture of candidate transparency, from the 2002 ADR judgment through PUCL 2003 and Public Interest Foundation 2018, represents the most developed body of doctrine on the relationship between free speech, democratic participation, and the obligations of candidates in Indian constitutional law.
