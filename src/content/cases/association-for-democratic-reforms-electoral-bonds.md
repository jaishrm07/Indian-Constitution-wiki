## Background

The Electoral Bonds Scheme was introduced by the Union government in 2018 as a mechanism for political funding. Under the scheme, donors could purchase special bearer instruments — electoral bonds — from designated branches of the State Bank of India and donate them to registered political parties, which could encash them at the bank. The scheme was designed so that the identity of donors would not be disclosed to the public, though the SBI would hold records accessible to the government. Related amendments to the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, the Companies Act, and the Representation of the People Act removed earlier restrictions on corporate political donations and the obligation to declare donor identity in party accounts. Association for Democratic Reforms and other petitioners challenged the scheme before the Supreme Court, arguing that anonymous political funding on this scale was incompatible with constitutional democracy. The case was eventually referred to a five-judge Constitution Bench.

## The legal question

The primary constitutional question was whether voters, as rights-holders under Article 19(1)(a), have a constitutionally protected right to information about who funds political parties, and whether anonymous electoral bonds violated that right. The Court also had to address whether the amendments removing disclosure requirements from corporate and individual donation law were constitutionally valid. A subsidiary question was whether the scheme violated Article 14 by creating an arbitrary and disproportionate restriction on the public's ability to know the source of funds that shape electoral competition. The Court needed to decide whether transparency in political funding is a constitutional value or merely a legislative policy option that Parliament may freely adjust.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench, speaking unanimously through Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud, struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme and the related statutory amendments. The Court held that the right to information under Article 19(1)(a) extends to information about political funding, because voters cannot make meaningful electoral choices without knowing whose financial interests lie behind political parties. The anonymity guaranteed by the scheme was therefore a restriction on voters' fundamental right to information, and the Union government had not demonstrated any compelling justification that could proportionately outweigh this restriction. The Court also found that the removal of disclosure obligations in the Companies Act and FCRA was not justified. The State Bank of India was directed to submit full records of electoral bonds purchased and encashed to the Election Commission, which was directed to publish the information on its website. The scheme was declared unconstitutional.

## Significance

The Electoral Bonds judgment is the most significant Indian constitutional ruling on political finance since the voter information cases of the early 2000s. It firmly established that campaign finance transparency is a constitutional requirement grounded in freedom of expression and democratic legitimacy, not merely a statutory policy choice. By applying proportionality analysis to the government's claimed justification for donor anonymity — chiefly, the protection of donors from political retaliation — the Court set a standard that any future political-finance scheme will need to satisfy. The disclosure of SBI records and their publication by the Election Commission following the judgment produced the first systematic public accounting of large-scale political donations in Indian electoral history.

## Why the case matters now

The decision reset the constitutional baseline for political-finance transparency. It makes clear that party funding cannot be insulated from public scrutiny if that secrecy weakens democratic choice.

## Long-term impact

Future campaign-finance reform will now be judged against this ruling's insistence that voter information is a constitutional value, not a policy afterthought.
