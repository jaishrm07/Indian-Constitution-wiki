## Background

Henry and William Golaknath held agricultural land in Punjab well in excess of the ceilings permitted by the Punjab Security of Land Tenures Act, 1953, a law amended and extended through the First and Seventeenth Amendments to place it in the Ninth Schedule. The Golaknaths argued that the constitutional amendments enabling this land reform legislation abridged their fundamental rights to property and equality and were therefore void. The case required an 11-judge bench to revisit two earlier decisions — Shankari Prasad v. Union of India (1951) and Sajjan Singh v. State of Rajasthan (1965) — which had both upheld Parliament's power to amend fundamental rights on the basis that Article 368 exercised constituent power, not ordinary legislative power, and was thus outside Article 13's restrictions. The political context was charged: land reform and economic redistribution were central government priorities, and the question of whether Parliament could amend away property rights had been contested since the First Amendment.

## The legal question in detail

The precise constitutional question was whether a constitutional amendment is "law" within the meaning of Article 13(2), which prohibits the State from making any law that takes away or abridges Fundamental Rights. If an amendment is "law" under Article 13, it would be void to the extent it infringes Part III rights. The Union of India argued, following Shankari Prasad and Sajjan Singh, that constituent power exercised under Article 368 was categorically different from ordinary legislative power and therefore not caught by Article 13(2). The Golaknaths argued that the word "law" in Article 13 had no natural reason to exclude amendments — the rights conferred by the Constitution should be enforceable against Parliament acting in any capacity. A secondary question was whether the Court could overrule its own two-bench decisions and, if it did, what the temporal effect of that overruling should be.

## What the court decided

By a 6-5 majority on the 11-judge bench, Chief Justice Subba Rao held that a constitutional amendment is "law" under Article 13(2) and that Parliament therefore has no power to enact a constitutional amendment that takes away or abridges Fundamental Rights. The majority drew on the idea that Fundamental Rights were a transcendental, inalienable part of the Constitution — echoing arguments made by Dr B.R. Ambedkar — and that Parliament acting under Article 368 did not possess some superior constituent power separate from ordinary legislative power. To avoid retrospective disruption of the many amendments and statutes already in place, the majority applied the doctrine of prospective overruling: the ruling applied from the date of the decision onwards, saving the First, Fourth, and Seventeenth Amendments from immediate invalidity. The practical consequence was immediate and severe — Parliament could no longer amend Part III — and the government responded by enacting the 24th, 25th, and 29th Amendments specifically to restore its amending power, all of which were then challenged in Kesavananda Bharati.

## Why the case remains central

Golaknath is the decisive moment when the Court treated amendment power as a constitutional problem requiring substantive legal resolution, rather than a political question to be left to Parliament's judgment. It broke decisively with the earlier consensus in Shankari Prasad and Sajjan Singh, converting the relationship between Article 13 and Article 368 into one of the defining questions of Indian constitutional law. Even though Kesavananda Bharati later overruled Golaknath's specific holding, the underlying concern — that Parliament should not be able to amend away the Constitution's core commitments — animated the basic structure doctrine that Kesavananda developed. The case demonstrates how land reform disputes, which appear factually narrow, can produce constitutional rulings of the highest order.

## Doctrinal line

The case pushed the Constitution toward the Kesavananda settlement by insisting that formal amendment procedure cannot be treated as a blank cheque for unlimited constitutional replacement, even when the amendment method set out in Article 368 is scrupulously followed. By treating amendment power as bounded by Part III, Golaknath forced the Court and Parliament into a confrontation that eventually produced the basic structure doctrine — a more nuanced solution than the Golaknath majority's outright prohibition. The doctrine of prospective overruling deployed here was itself constitutionally novel in India and has since become an accepted tool in the Court's jurisprudence. The intellectual genealogy of the basic structure doctrine runs directly through Golaknath.

## Long-term impact

Golaknath's immediate doctrinal force was narrowed when Kesavananda overruled it in 1973, but its influence survives in every serious discussion of amendment limits, rights protection, and the nature of constituent power. The three amendments Parliament enacted to reverse Golaknath — the 24th, 25th, and 29th — were themselves the subject of the Kesavananda litigation, making Golaknath the direct cause of the most important constitutional case in Indian history. The decision also established that the Court would treat the accumulation of Ninth Schedule entries as a justiciable matter, a thread that ran forward to I.R. Coelho forty years later. Scholars of comparative constitutional law routinely cite Golaknath alongside global decisions on unamendable constitutional cores.
