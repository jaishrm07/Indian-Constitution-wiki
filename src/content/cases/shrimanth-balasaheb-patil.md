## Background

In July 2019, Karnataka's fragile coalition government led by Chief Minister H.D. Kumaraswamy — a post-2018 election arrangement between the Indian National Congress and the Janata Dal (Secular) — collapsed when fifteen rebel MLAs, drawn mostly from the BJP and JD(S) ranks, submitted resignations to the Speaker and then walked out of their respective party meetings. The rebels travelled to Mumbai and refused to return. Without their support, the Kumaraswamy government could not survive a floor test. Speaker K.R. Ramesh Kumar initially declined to accept the resignations, raising questions about whether they were genuine and voluntary under Article 190(3)(b). Disqualification petitions were simultaneously filed against the rebels under the Tenth Schedule of the Constitution, which bars members from voting or abstaining contrary to party directions.

The Speaker eventually accepted some resignations, rejected others on voluntariness grounds, and then disqualified all fifteen MLAs under the Tenth Schedule — in effect ruling that even those who had resigned remained disqualifiable for conduct predating the acceptance of their resignations. Crucially, the Speaker also ordered that the disqualified members were barred from contesting elections for the remainder of the Fifteenth Karnataka Legislative Assembly's term. The disqualified members petitioned the Supreme Court under Article 32, arguing that the disqualification was improper and that the additional bar on contesting was ultra vires.

The Supreme Court heard the matter while B.S. Yediyurappa (BJP) had already formed a new government following Kumaraswamy's resignation. The Court constituted a three-judge bench and heard the matter as a matter of constitutional importance touching the Tenth Schedule, the Speaker's jurisdiction, and the limits of judicial review of anti-defection proceedings.

## The legal question in detail

Four distinct legal questions were before the Court. First, did the Speaker have the constitutional authority under the Tenth Schedule to go beyond disqualification from the House and impose an additional disability — a bar on contesting elections for the remainder of the assembly term? The Tenth Schedule provides that a member shall be "disqualified for being a member" of the House; nothing in its text speaks to future electability.

Second, did the acceptance of a member's resignation under Article 190(3)(b) before the disqualification petition was decided have the effect of rendering the petition infructuous? If a member was no longer a member of the House because the resignation was accepted, could the disqualification machinery — which operates on "members" — still reach them?

Third, were the resignations tendered by the rebel MLAs voluntary and genuine under the proviso to Article 190(3)(b), which allows the Speaker to reject a resignation if satisfied it is not voluntary or genuine? The Speaker's inquiry into voluntariness was itself challenged as improperly conducted.

Fourth, following Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992) and Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker (2016), to what extent can courts intervene in ongoing disqualification proceedings, and what standard of review applies?

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court, in a judgment delivered by a three-judge bench, upheld the disqualification of the rebel MLAs but struck down the additional order barring them from contesting elections for the rest of the assembly term.

On the debarment question — the most constitutionally significant holding — the Court ruled that the Speaker's power under the Tenth Schedule is limited to disqualifying a member from being a member of the House. The Schedule confers no power to impose the additional punishment of preventing a disqualified member from standing in fresh elections. That additional disability, if it is to exist at all, must be found in some other constitutional provision or statute; it cannot be read into the Tenth Schedule by implication. The debarment order was accordingly set aside.

On the resignation question, the Court held that where a Speaker accepts a resignation as genuine and voluntary under Article 190(3)(b), the member ceases to be a member of the House from the moment of acceptance. A disqualification petition under the Tenth Schedule can only be maintained against a sitting member; once the member is no longer in the House, the petition becomes infructuous. The Court found that some of the rebel MLAs whose resignations had been accepted fell into this category.

On the reasonableness-of-time obligation, the Court confirmed that Speakers are not free to sit indefinitely on disqualification petitions. Drawing on the line from Kihoto Hollohan, it held that the Speaker must decide petitions within a reasonable time, and that courts retain the power to issue directions prescribing a timeline when the Speaker delays unreasonably. This branch of the ruling was developed further in the companion case Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur (2020) 2 SCC 598, which set the outer limit at three months.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment arose from a textbook illustration of how anti-defection proceedings and voluntary resignations can be deployed simultaneously in a political crisis. The rebels' strategy — resign to deprive the government of numbers while also triggering an acceptance-of-resignation argument that neutralises any disqualification petition — forced the Court to map the exact boundary between Article 190 and the Tenth Schedule. That mapping has since been cited in every subsequent state-level political crisis involving a combination of resignations and defection proceedings.

The holding that the Speaker's punitive power is exhausted once disqualification from the House is ordered also resolves a question that had been left open after Kihoto Hollohan. Speakers who are aligned with the party engineering a defection might otherwise use the debarment weapon as an additional deterrent or negotiating chip. The Court's ruling closes that avenue.

More broadly, the case is the sharpest post-Kihoto Hollohan statement of the principle that the Tenth Schedule creates a defined adjudicatory jurisdiction for the Speaker and that this jurisdiction cannot be expanded by executive-style orders issued by the Speaker on his or her own motion.

## Long-term impact

The ruling's immediate consequence was that the disqualified Karnataka MLAs — though barred from sitting in the Fifteenth Assembly — were free to contest the by-elections that followed, and most of them won, entering the new Yediyurappa government. This outcome was controversial in some quarters, but it confirmed the Court's reading that the Tenth Schedule does not extend to post-disqualification electoral disability.

The three-part framework the Court applied — (1) the Speaker's power is limited to what the Tenth Schedule expressly confers; (2) voluntary resignation under Article 190(3)(b) is a separate track from disqualification; (3) courts can supervise the timeliness of Speaker decisions — became the analytical template for subsequent cases including the Maharashtra political crisis of 2022 (Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary) and the Manipur proceedings in Keisham Meghachandra Singh.

The ruling also put legislative draftsmen on notice that if Parliament wishes to extend anti-defection consequences to post-disqualification electoral contests, it must do so by explicit constitutional amendment, not through Speaker orders. The Tenth Schedule has not been amended in this direction since 2019.

The Supreme Court portal at [sci.gov.in](https://www.sci.gov.in/) also hosts the full judgment. The operative holdings on the debarment question and the resignation-disqualification sequence are found in the main judgment; read alongside the companion case Keisham Meghachandra Singh **(2020) 2 SCC 598** — decided on the same day — for the full picture on Speaker delay obligations. Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu **(1992) Supp (2) SCC 651** remains the constitutional foundation for the entire line and should be read first for the doctrinal background on the Tenth Schedule's exclusionary clause and judicial review.
