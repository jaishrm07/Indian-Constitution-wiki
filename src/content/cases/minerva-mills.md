## Background

Minerva Mills Ltd was a textile company in Karnataka whose management was taken over by the government under the Sick Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1974, a law placed in the Ninth Schedule. The broader constitutional challenge arose from the 42nd Amendment (1976), enacted during the Emergency, which inserted clauses into Article 368 declaring that constitutional amendments could not be called into question in any court on any ground — effectively attempting to place Parliament's constituent power beyond judicial review. The same amendment also inserted Article 31C in expanded form, directing courts to give precedence to Directive Principles over Fundamental Rights. After the Emergency ended and the Janata government's 44th Amendment (1978) partially restored some fundamental rights protections, the 42nd Amendment's amending-power provisions remained on the books and were directly challenged in this litigation.

## The legal question in detail

The primary question was whether Parliament could, through Articles 368(4) and (5) inserted by the 42nd Amendment, immunise its own exercise of amending power from judicial scrutiny. The Union argued that the constituent power under Article 368 was inherently unlimited and that Parliament was entitled to define the scope and effect of its own amendments. The petitioners argued that removing judicial review of amendments was itself an assault on the basic structure, because limited government and the rule of law — including the Court's power to review the Constitution's fidelity to its own identity — were basic structure elements. A connected question was whether the expanded Article 31C, which prioritised Directive Principles over all rights in Part III, was constitutionally valid.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench, speaking through Chief Justice Chandrachud, struck down Articles 368(4) and (5) as unconstitutional on the ground that Parliament cannot convert its limited amending power into an unlimited one — to do so would be to destroy the very limitation that Kesavananda identified as basic structure. The Court reasoned that the Constitution itself is the source of Parliament's power; Parliament cannot use a derivative power to remove the source's constraints. On Article 31C, the Court partially struck it down, holding that the harmony and balance between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles is itself a basic structure element, and that neither Part can completely override the other. Judicial review — particularly the Court's power to determine whether an amendment damages basic structure — was confirmed as a non-negotiable feature of constitutional identity.

## Why the case remains central

Minerva Mills is one of the clearest post-Kesavananda statements that amendment power is inherently limited and that constitutional equilibrium between rights and directive principles is a structural imperative, not a policy choice. The decision is especially significant because it was decided in a direct confrontation with a Parliament that had explicitly tried to nullify Kesavananda's logic through formal amendment — and it held that such self-entrenchment is constitutionally impermissible. It remains the foundational precedent for the proposition that judicial review of constitutional amendments cannot be legislated away. The case also established that the balance between Parts III and IV of the Constitution is itself judicially enforceable.

## Long-term impact

The case helps explain why constitutional change in India remains judicially reviewable even when it follows formal amendment procedure, and why Parliament's procedural compliance with Article 368 is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a valid amendment. Minerva Mills' treatment of harmony between rights and directive principles has been repeatedly invoked in cases involving property, social welfare legislation, and affirmative action, requiring courts to evaluate whether legislative choices destroy rather than calibrate that balance. Together with Kesavananda and I.R. Coelho, it forms the trilogy that defines the scope and limits of the basic structure doctrine as applied to constitutional amendments specifically.
