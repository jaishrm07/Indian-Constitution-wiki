## Background

The TMA Pai Foundation judgment of 2002 had recognised that private unaided educational institutions possess a right under Article 19(1)(g) to administer their affairs, including admission of students, subject to reasonable state regulation to ensure standards and prevent exploitation. Despite that ruling, the state of Maharashtra continued to insist that private unaided professional colleges must participate in state-run centralised admissions through a Common Entrance Test and implement government quota schemes reserving seats for particular categories of students. P.A. Inamdar's case consolidated multiple petitions from private professional colleges in Maharashtra and other states challenging this insistence. The dispute arose partly because TMA Pai itself had left some ambiguity about whether minority and non-minority institutions stood on identical footing regarding admissions autonomy.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether, after TMA Pai, states could compel private unaided professional institutions — both minority institutions protected by Article 30 and non-minority institutions relying on Article 19(1)(g) — to participate in government-run common entrance tests and to implement government quota schemes reserving seats for state-nominated candidates. The institutions argued that compelled participation in state quota systems converted their seats into state-allocated seats, which was an unconstitutional interference with their autonomy. The states responded that regulating admissions through a centralised test served legitimate objectives of merit, transparency, and social justice, and that TMA Pai did not immunise institutions from socially purposive regulation.

## What the court decided

A seven-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that private unaided professional institutions — both minority and non-minority — cannot be compelled by the state to implement government quota schemes or to surrender their admissions to the state's reservation policy. The Court affirmed that the state's interest in regulating academic standards, ensuring merit, and preventing capitation fees is legitimate, and that regulation directed at those ends is permissible. However, forcing a private unaided institution to fill a percentage of its seats with state-nominated candidates crosses from regulation into compelled expropriation of institutional autonomy in violation of Article 19(1)(g) and, for minority institutions, Article 30. The Court drew a line between the state's power to conduct counselling and maintain a merit list — which the state can require institutions to consult — and a state power to compel seat allocation, which it cannot. The state was left free to operate its own quota system for seats in state-funded or state-aided institutions.

## Why the case remains central

It is the key case for the line between permissible state regulation and impermissible compelled quota policy in private professional education. The distinction between regulation for standards and anti-exploitation purposes on the one hand, and forced admission quota implementation on the other, is P.A. Inamdar's enduring doctrinal contribution to education law.

## Long-term impact

The judgment still shapes disputes about admissions autonomy, fee regulation, and the extent of permissible state control over private colleges. It also set the stage for the Ninety-Third Constitutional Amendment and the Supreme Court's subsequent review of the OBC-in-private-colleges question in Ashoka Kumar Thakur (2008), illustrating how a judicial ruling can itself provoke a constitutional amendment in response.
