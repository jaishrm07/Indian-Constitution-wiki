## Background

The Banking Companies (Acquisition and Transfer of Undertakings) Act, 1969 nationalised 14 major commercial banks owned by private shareholders, transferring their ownership to the Union government. Compensation was fixed by Parliament on its own terms rather than determined by reference to market value or an independent tribunal, meaning shareholders had no realistic mechanism to challenge the adequacy of what they received. R.C. Cooper, a shareholder in one of the nationalised banks, challenged the Act arguing that the compensation offered was illusory. He contended that the nationalisation violated his fundamental rights under Articles 14, 19, and 31, and that the government could not rely on a formal classification of the law to insulate it from substantive rights scrutiny. The case reached an eleven-judge Constitution Bench, making it one of the most significant constitutional adjudications of the post-independence era.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether the bank nationalisation law was a valid exercise of Parliament's power under Article 31, and whether illusory or grossly inadequate compensation could satisfy the constitutional requirement that property not be acquired without fair compensation. A deeper methodological question ran beneath this: could the Court look at the real effect of a law on the rights of individuals rather than confining itself to the formal category in which the legislature had placed the law. The government argued that since the law was a valid nationalisation measure, the compensation terms were a matter of legislative policy not subject to judicial override. Cooper's argument was that the practical effect of stripping shareholders of property for nominal consideration was constitutionally indistinguishable from confiscation. The Court was thus being asked to choose between formalism — judging state action by its stated purpose — and a more searching inquiry that examined what the action actually did to constitutional rights.

## What the court decided

An eleven-judge Constitution Bench struck down the nationalisation law by a 10-1 majority, representing one of the clearest judicial rebukes of executive economic policy in India's constitutional history. The Court held that the "object and effect" of a law, not merely its formal classification or stated purpose, determined which fundamental rights it engaged and how they had to be assessed. The compensation provisions were held to be illusory because the government had fixed its own compensation terms rather than allowing independent determination, converting a constitutional obligation into a formal gesture. This violated Article 31's requirement that compensation be genuine rather than nominal. The case overruled the earlier approach — followed under the Bank Nationalisation Ordinance — of treating compensation as entirely at Parliament's discretion, and established that compensation cannot be reduced to a constitutional fiction. The ruling led directly to the 25th Amendment (1971) and subsequent efforts to shield nationalisation laws from judicial scrutiny, eventually leading to the property-rights changes introduced under the 44th Amendment.

## Why the case remains central

This case matters because it refused to let the state defend constitutional action by choosing the right label. The Court asked what the law actually did to rights holders, and insisted that formal compliance with constitutional categories was not enough if the practical effect was to nullify the rights those categories were meant to protect. The move from form to substance in constitutional review is one of the enduring contributions of this judgment.

## Doctrinal line

That effect-based approach became one of the foundations for later fairness and dignity reasoning, including the wider turn in Maneka Gandhi. Courts applying R.C. Cooper's logic look past the legislature's stated rationale to examine the concrete impact of state action on the persons whose rights are at stake. This made the case a template for a more rigorous, rights-protective style of judicial review across multiple domains.

## Long-term impact

The case remains important in economic-rights debates, compensation disputes, and any argument where state power is defended through formal wording rather than practical consequences. It stands as an early and forceful statement that constitutional rights impose genuine constraints on the state rather than procedural hoops that a determined legislature can design around. The subsequent political and legislative reactions to the ruling — culminating in the deletion of the right to property from Part III — demonstrate how much the judgment disturbed the assumption that Parliament was the final judge of what counted as adequate treatment of citizens.
