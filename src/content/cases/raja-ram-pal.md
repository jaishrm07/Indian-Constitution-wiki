## Background

In 2005, a sting operation conducted by a television channel showed eleven MPs accepting cash in return for agreeing to raise questions in Parliament. The Lok Sabha expelled ten of those members after a report from the Ethics Committee. The expelled members challenged the expulsion as unconstitutional, arguing that neither the Constitution nor the Rules of Procedure expressly authorised expulsion as a punishment and that the power, if it existed at all, could not be exercised in a manner that eliminated elected representatives without adequate process. The case consolidated multiple writ petitions before a Constitution Bench.

## The legal question in detail

The core question was whether Parliament's power to expel its own members was a constitutional privilege power immune from judicial review, or whether Article 105 did not extend to expulsion and therefore courts could review such expulsions for compliance with constitutional standards, including natural justice. The expelled members argued that expulsion removed a constituency's representation in Parliament, engaging both their individual rights and the democratic rights of their voters. Parliament countered that every legislature must be master of its own house and that judicial interference with a disciplinary resolution would undermine the separation of powers. The case also asked the Court to define the scope of Article 122, which bars courts from inquiring into proceedings of Parliament on grounds of irregularity.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench held that Parliament does have the power to expel members as part of its privilege and disciplinary authority under Article 105(3), drawing on the historical practice of legislatures and the necessity of maintaining the integrity of the House. However, the Court firmly rejected the claim that this power is wholly immune from judicial review: courts can examine whether an expulsion was carried out in conformity with constitutional standards, including procedural fairness and natural justice, even while respecting Parliament's autonomy over its internal affairs. Article 122 shields mere procedural irregularity, not constitutional violations or patent illegality. Applying that standard to the facts, the Court upheld the specific expulsions, finding that the procedure followed — referral to the Ethics Committee, consideration by the House — met the constitutional minimum. The judgment thus simultaneously affirmed legislative privilege and subjected it to a constitutional collar.

## Why the case remains central

The case matters because it resists two overstatements at once: that Parliament is just another litigant body, and that privilege places it fully beyond the Constitution. By locating the power to expel within Article 105(3) while simultaneously subjecting its exercise to judicial review for constitutional compliance, the Court charted a middle course that preserves institutional dignity without creating a zone of impunity.

## Long-term impact

Questions about expulsion, discipline, privilege, and institutional self-policing still rely on Raja Ram Pal's calibrated view of judicial review. Its framework — defer to Parliament on internal proceedings, review for constitutional compliance at the edges — has become the standard template for courts navigating the separation of powers in legislative-privilege disputes.
