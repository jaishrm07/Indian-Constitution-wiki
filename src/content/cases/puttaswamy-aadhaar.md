## Background

This case is entirely distinct from the earlier Puttaswamy judgment of 2017. The 2017 nine-judge bench ruled on a preliminary constitutional question: does the right to privacy exist as a fundamental right under the Indian Constitution? It answered that question unanimously in the affirmative, overruling M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh. That ruling said nothing about whether Aadhaar was constitutionally valid; it merely established the legal framework within which the Aadhaar challenge would have to be resolved on the merits. This 2018 judgment is the merits ruling — the substantive constitutional adjudication of the Aadhaar architecture itself.

The Aadhaar scheme was initially introduced through executive notifications and later codified in the Aadhaar (Targeted Delivery of Financial and Other Subsidies, Benefits and Services) Act, 2016 (the Aadhaar Act). The Act created a centralised biometric database — the Central Identities Data Repository (CIDR) — administered by the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI). Every resident was enrolled by collecting ten fingerprints, two iris scans, and a photograph, generating a 12-digit Aadhaar number. The Act made Aadhaar authentication compulsory for receiving subsidies and welfare benefits under schemes funded from the Consolidated Fund of India. Linkage requirements were subsequently extended to filing income tax returns, opening bank accounts, and obtaining mobile phone SIM cards, and Section 57 of the Act permitted private entities to use Aadhaar for authentication purposes.

The constitutional challenges raised before a five-judge Constitution Bench were wide-ranging: that the Act violated the right to privacy established in the 2017 judgment; that compulsory biometric enrollment violated bodily integrity; that the exclusion of genuine beneficiaries from welfare due to authentication failures was unconstitutional; that Section 57's private use provisions enabled commercial surveillance; and that the Aadhaar Act had been unconstitutionally introduced as a Money Bill under Article 110, bypassing the Rajya Sabha and thereby violating the bicameral legislative process.

## The legal question in detail

The Bench had to address several distinct constitutional questions. First, whether compulsory biometric enrollment and its linkage to welfare entitlements was proportionate under the privacy framework articulated in the 2017 judgment, which required legality, legitimate aim, and proportionality. Second, whether the exclusion of beneficiaries due to authentication failures violated the right to life and the right to food and welfare under Article 21. Third, whether Section 57, permitting private use of Aadhaar data, was constitutionally permissible given the greater risks of commercial surveillance compared with state welfare delivery. Fourth, and perhaps most structurally significant, whether the Speaker of the Lok Sabha had validly certified the Aadhaar Act as a Money Bill under Article 110 — a classification that allows a bill to be passed by the Lok Sabha alone, with the Rajya Sabha having only a recommendatory role. The Aadhaar Act contained many provisions that arguably had nothing to do with the Consolidated Fund of India, which is the defining criterion of a Money Bill under Article 110.

## What the court decided

The five-judge Bench divided 4:1. The majority comprised Chief Justice Dipak Misra, Justice A.M. Khanwilkar, Justice A.K. Sikri (who wrote the lead opinion), and Justice Ashok Bhushan. Justice D.Y. Chandrachud dissented in full.

The majority upheld the Aadhaar Act's core framework as constitutional. It held that Aadhaar serves a legitimate state interest in ensuring targeted delivery of subsidies and benefits, reducing leakage and ghost beneficiaries, and preventing impersonation. The biometric architecture, the majority reasoned, was proportionate to this aim: the data collected was minimal (fingerprints, iris, photograph), the CIDR does not store biometric data post-authentication (it stores only a hash), and the security architecture provides adequate safeguards. The right to food and welfare of genuine beneficiaries, the Court held, is served — not harmed — by an accurate identity system. Aadhaar therefore survived the proportionality challenge as applied to welfare delivery. The majority also upheld the Money Bill certification.

However, the majority struck down Section 57 of the Act, which permitted private entities — telecom companies, banks, and others — to use Aadhaar authentication for their own purposes. The Court held that private use of Aadhaar creates an entirely different risk profile: it enables commercial profiling, data aggregation, and surveillance without the welfare justification that saves the state's own use. Section 57 was accordingly severed and struck down. The Court also read down the requirement to link Aadhaar to mobile SIM cards and to income tax PAN numbers (for those not seeking government benefits), narrowing the scope of mandatory seeding.

Justice Chandrachud's dissent is among the most significant solo dissents in the Supreme Court's modern history. He held, first, that the Aadhaar Act was not a Money Bill within the meaning of Article 110 because many of its provisions — those creating the UIDAI, governing enrolment, permitting private use, creating offences — had no connection to the Consolidated Fund of India. Introducing a law of such constitutional significance through the Money Bill route, he held, deprived the Rajya Sabha of its legitimate role and was an abuse of the constitutional process. The entire Aadhaar Act, on this ground, was unconstitutional. Second, he held that mandatory seeding of Aadhaar with other databases — particularly private ones — created a permanent surveillance infrastructure that violated the right to privacy in a manner no welfare objective could justify. The architecture enabled the profiling of individuals across contexts, allowing the State and private actors to build a comprehensive picture of a person's movements, transactions, and choices. This, he held, was disproportionate. Third, Chandrachud J. held that authentication failures, which excluded real beneficiaries from welfare, were not a marginal implementation problem but a systemic constitutional deficiency.

## Why the case remains central

Puttaswamy (Aadhaar) is the foundational case for anyone analysing the constitutional legality of national identity systems, compulsory biometric enrollment, welfare conditionality, and the private use of state-issued digital identity. It is also indispensable for the debate about Money Bill abuse: the question of whether Parliament can route legislation of general significance through the Money Bill procedure simply to bypass the Rajya Sabha has arisen in connection with the Finance Acts of 2017 and 2019 as well, and Chandrachud J.'s dissent in Puttaswamy (Aadhaar) is the most thorough judicial treatment of the constitutional limits on that practice. The dissent's reasoning on proportionality — that not all state interests justify biometric infrastructure — has become highly influential even though it did not command a majority.

## Long-term impact

The judgment produced two lasting effects. First, it constrained the expansion of Aadhaar's mandatory scope, particularly for private entities, and set limits on seeding with non-welfare databases. Second, and more durably, Justice Chandrachud's dissent on the Money Bill question has animated challenges to several Finance Act amendments that amended non-financial legislation under the cover of the annual appropriations bill, and the constitutional question he raised — whether Article 110 certification is justiciable — remains unresolved. The Aadhaar ecosystem continues to expand through subordinate legislation and executive policy, and the constitutional questions opened by this judgment — especially on aggregation, surveillance, and proportionality — remain live as India builds out its digital public infrastructure.
