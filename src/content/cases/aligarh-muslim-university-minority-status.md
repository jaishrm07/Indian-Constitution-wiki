## Background

The Aligarh Muslim University (AMU) was founded by Sir Syed Ahmad Khan, a Muslim reformer, who established the Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental College at Aligarh in 1875. The institution grew out of a Muslim community initiative and was intended to provide modern education to the Muslim community while preserving Islamic learning. It was incorporated as a university under the Aligarh Muslim University Act, 1920 — a central statute. In 1967, a five-judge Constitution Bench in S. Azeez Basha v. Union of India held that because AMU was created by a parliamentary statute and was not established by the Muslim community in the direct sense, it could not claim minority institution status under Article 30. This ruling led to decades of legislative and judicial dispute over AMU's character, and in 1981 an amendment to the AMU Act purported to declare the university a minority institution — an amendment whose validity was itself contested. Naresh Agarwal challenged reservations that AMU provided for Muslim candidates in faculty appointments on the ground that a non-minority institution could not maintain such preferences. The Supreme Court referred the question to a seven-judge bench to reconsider Azeez Basha, given how fundamental the issue was to minority educational rights.

## The legal question

The central question was whether the test articulated in S. Azeez Basha — that a minority institution must be one that a minority community actually established, and that an institution created by parliamentary statute is not so established — correctly stated the constitutional position under Article 30. More precisely, the bench was asked to determine what "established" means in Article 30(1)'s guarantee that "all minorities... shall have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice." The subsidiary questions were: whether a minority community's founding initiative and continued identification with an institution are sufficient to constitute establishment even if the formal legal vehicle for the institution is a central statute; what test courts should apply to determine whether an institution qualifies as a minority institution; and whether the factual question of AMU's status could be resolved at the level of the constitutional bench or required remand to a regular bench for evidence.

## What the court decided

The seven-judge bench, by a majority of 4:3, overruled S. Azeez Basha. The majority held that the fact of statutory incorporation does not, by itself, divest an institution of minority character. The correct approach is to look at the genesis of the institution — who conceived it, who funded it, who built it, and for what community purpose — rather than at the legal form in which it was ultimately organised. The majority articulated a three-part test: (1) whether the institution was established by a member or members of a minority community; (2) whether it was established for the benefit of a minority community; and (3) whether it has been administered continuously as an institution reflecting the minority community's purpose and identity. An institution that satisfies these criteria retains its minority character under Article 30 even if it later obtained a statutory charter or received state funds. The majority acknowledged that where an institution is incorporated under a statute that itself transforms the institution's character — by, for example, transferring control to the government — the question may be more complex, but that is a factual inquiry. On the specific question of AMU, the majority found that the historical and factual record raised substantial questions about whether the founding of AMU satisfied the three-part test, and remanded the matter to a regular bench for determination of those factual questions rather than resolving them at the Constitution Bench level. The three dissenting judges would have maintained Azeez Basha or applied a narrower version of the majority's framework.

## Significance

The judgment is the most significant ruling on Article 30 since T.M.A. Pai Foundation (2002). By overruling a precedent that had stood for more than five decades, the Court acknowledged that Azeez Basha had placed an unjustifiably restrictive interpretation on minority educational rights. The three-part test provides a more principled and community-centred framework for assessing minority institution claims, consistent with the Constitution's pluralist commitments. The remand to a regular bench means that AMU's actual minority status remains contested and will require further litigation, leaving the practical implications of the ruling for AMU itself unresolved. More broadly, the ruling has implications for any minority-founded institution that was given statutory form — potentially reopening minority-status questions for other universities and institutions that had assumed Azeez Basha foreclosed such claims.
