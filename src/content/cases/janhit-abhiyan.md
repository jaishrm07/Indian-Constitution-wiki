## Background

The One Hundred and Third Constitutional Amendment, enacted in January 2019, inserted Articles 15(6) and 16(6) into the Constitution, permitting the state to make special provision for the advancement of economically weaker sections of citizens in education and public employment, up to ten per cent of seats or posts. Crucially, the amendment excluded from the EWS category persons already covered by existing reservation provisions for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and Other Backward Classes. A large number of petitions challenged the amendment before the Supreme Court on various grounds, including that creating a reservation based purely on economic criteria violated the equality code and the basic structure of the Constitution. Janhit Abhiyan, a public interest organisation, was among the petitioners. The case was referred to a five-judge Constitution Bench.

## The legal question

The principal constitutional question was whether the 103rd Amendment, by creating a reservation category defined solely by economic weakness and expressly excluding SC, ST, and OBC communities from it, violated the equality code under Articles 14 to 16 or damaged the basic structure of the Constitution. The petitioners argued that the exclusion of SC, ST, and OBC persons from EWS benefits amounted to discrimination within a social welfare scheme, and that using economic criteria as the sole basis for reservation departed fundamentally from the constitutional reservation framework, which is grounded in social and educational backwardness. The Court also had to consider whether the fifty per cent ceiling on reservations, established in Indra Sawhney, was a basic structure element that the amendment had broken.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the 103rd Amendment by a majority of three to two. The majority held that the EWS reservation is a distinct constitutional category addressing economic deprivation among those not already covered by existing reservation schemes, and that its exclusion of SC, ST, and OBC persons does not violate equality norms because those communities have their own constitutional protections. The majority further held that the amendment did not breach the fifty per cent ceiling problem in a basic structure sense, as that ceiling pertained to existing reservation categories and the EWS quota represented a separately justified category. Justices Ravindra Bhat and Bela Trivedi dissented, with Justice Bhat writing extensively that the exclusion of SC, ST, and OBC persons from EWS reservation was unconstitutional because it created a hierarchy within disadvantaged communities that the equality code cannot support, and that it violated the basic structure of the Constitution as it had evolved in equality jurisprudence.

## Significance

Janhit Abhiyan is a landmark in Indian reservation law because it validated the use of purely economic criteria as the basis for a constitutional reservation category for the first time. Before this case, Indian reservation doctrine was anchored in social and educational backwardness, with economic criteria treated as subsidiary or as a sub-classification tool; the majority's acceptance of EWS as a freestanding category represents a significant doctrinal shift. The decision will shape legislative and constitutional debates about affirmative action design for years, particularly the question of whether further economically defined categories can be created. The strong dissent by Justice Ravindra Bhat, which argued that the amendment violated equality at its core, ensures the case will remain contested ground in constitutional scholarship.

## Why the case remains central

Janhit Abhiyan matters because it widened the constitutional space for reservation design. It shifted the reservation debate beyond backward-class doctrine toward the status of economic criteria.

## Long-term impact

The decision is now unavoidable in any debate over reservation architecture, representational justice, and how equality doctrine absorbs new classifications.
