## Background

The Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes are identified by Presidential notification under Articles 341 and 342 of the Constitution. Once a caste or tribe is notified by the President, the entire community is entitled to the constitutional protections and reservations prescribed for the SC/ST category. The constitutional design, as originally understood, treated the scheduled list as a single composite group deserving uniform protection.

In practice, however, the scheduled communities are not socially or historically uniform. Within the large number of communities included in the scheduled list, some sub-groups have historically faced deeper and more entrenched exclusion — for example, the Valmikis and Mazhabi Sikhs in Punjab, who worked predominantly as sanitation workers and were subject to a level of social degradation even within the broader Dalit community. Other communities on the scheduled list had made measurable social and educational progress and were better placed to compete for reserved posts and seats. State governments observed that reserved posts meant for SCs were disproportionately captured by the relatively more advanced communities within the category, leaving the most disadvantaged sub-groups with negligible representation even after decades of reservation.

Several states — Punjab being the most prominent — responded by enacting legislation that created sub-classifications within the SC reservation, earmarking a portion of the reserved quota for communities at the bottom of the social hierarchy within the scheduled list. Punjab's Punjab Scheduled Castes and Backward Classes (Reservation in Services) Act, 2006 provided that if the first preference within the reserved SC quota went to Valmikis and Mazhabi Sikhs, who were identified as more disadvantaged within the category.

The constitutional validity of this sub-classification was challenged in court. In 2004, the Supreme Court in E.V. Chinnaiah v. State of Andhra Pradesh had held that the SC category, once constituted by Presidential notification, formed a homogenous class in constitutional terms. Since the Presidential list under Article 341 was sacrosanct and Parliament alone could add or remove communities from it, no state legislature could create internal distinctions within the list. Sub-classification, the bench reasoned, was in effect a tampering with the Presidential notification — an impermissible re-ordering of a constitutionally defined category by the state. This holding blocked sub-classification across the country.

A Constitution Bench reconsidered E.V. Chinnaiah in the context of Punjab's legislation and referred the question to a seven-judge bench, acknowledging that the issue was of exceptional constitutional importance and that the Chinnaiah ruling had produced practical inequities that the constitutional text did not compel.

## The legal question in detail

The constitutional question in Davinder Singh turned on three intersecting problems.

**The homogeneity premise.** E.V. Chinnaiah rested on the premise that SCs form a homogenous class. The question was whether this premise was constitutionally mandated or merely assumed. Articles 341 and 342 authorise the President to specify the communities included in the scheduled lists; they say nothing about the internal characteristics of those communities or whether they share a uniform degree of disadvantage. The petitioners challenging sub-classification argued that the Presidential list, by including a community, treats all its members as equally disadvantaged and equally entitled to the benefit of reservation; the states countered that the Presidential list is a catalogue, not a constitutional declaration of homogeneity.

**The power to sub-classify.** The states argued that the power to make reservations under Article 16(4) (and by extension Article 15(4)) includes the power to ensure that the reservation actually reaches those for whom it is designed. If some communities within the SC list consistently capture most of the reserved posts while others remain wholly unrepresented, the state is entitled to use empirical data to make a rational sub-classification — not to create a new class outside the Presidential list, but to make the existing reservation more equitable. The counter-argument was that sub-classification effectively removes some SC communities from the benefit of reservation in proportion, which only Parliament can do by amending the Presidential list.

**The relationship with Indra Sawhney.** The nine-judge bench in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992) had held that the creamy layer principle — which excludes the affluent segments of a backward class from reservation — does not apply to SCs and STs. E.V. Chinnaiah had relied in part on this distinction: SCs and STs were treated as a single undifferentiated group precisely because Indra Sawhney had declined to apply the creamy layer to them. The question in Davinder Singh was whether sub-classification — distinguishing more disadvantaged from less disadvantaged sub-groups within the SC list — was conceptually different from the creamy layer, or whether it was a back-door route to the same outcome.

## What the court decided

The seven-judge bench, by a 6:1 majority, held that states have constitutional authority to sub-classify within Scheduled Castes and overruled E.V. Chinnaiah.

**The SC category is not constitutionally homogenous.** The majority held that the inclusion of a community in the Presidential list under Article 341 does not entail a constitutional declaration that all listed communities are equally disadvantaged. The Presidential list operates as a legal designation that brings communities within the protective umbrella of reservation; it does not preclude inquiry into the relative levels of disadvantage among communities within the list. Social reality — as the Court observed — demonstrates that there is wide variation in the levels of representation, education, and social advancement among different communities within the scheduled list. The homogeneity premise in E.V. Chinnaiah was based on a legal fiction that the Constitution does not require.

**Sub-classification is within state power.** The majority held that the state's power under Article 16(4) to make provision for reservation in favour of backward classes includes the power to sub-classify within the reserved category to ensure that the most disadvantaged communities receive adequate benefit. This power must be exercised on the basis of empirical data: the state must demonstrate, with quantifiable material, that a particular sub-group within the SC category is inadequately represented despite the overall SC reservation. Sub-classification without such data would be arbitrary and vulnerable to challenge under Article 14.

**Sub-classification does not tamper with the Presidential list.** The majority carefully distinguished sub-classification from tampering with the content of the Presidential list. Sub-classification does not add or remove any community from the scheduled list; it does not declare any listed community unentitled to reservation. It merely makes the existing reserved quota more equitable in its internal distribution. The Presidential list retains its exclusivity: only Parliament can amend it, and sub-classification does not affect that exclusivity.

**Overruling E.V. Chinnaiah.** The majority held that E.V. Chinnaiah was wrongly decided and was overruled to the extent it held that the SC category is a constitutionally homogenous class incapable of internal sub-classification.

**The creamy layer question (Justice Gavai's concurrence).** In a significant separate concurrence, Justice B.R. Gavai held that the creamy layer principle — which Indra Sawhney had applied to OBCs and declined to apply to SCs/STs — should also apply to SCs and STs in the context of reservation in promotions. He reasoned that the rationale for excluding the creamy layer from reservation — that reservation is a remedy for disadvantage, and those who are no longer disadvantaged should not indefinitely claim the benefit — applies with equal force to economically and socially advanced members of the SC/ST communities. This observation was a concurring addition and was not part of the majority's operative holding, but it introduces a doctrinal development that is likely to generate further litigation and legislative attention.

**The dissent (Justice Bela Trivedi).** Justice Trivedi dissented in full. She held that the Presidential list under Article 341 is a sacrosanct constitutional category that admits no internal sub-division by the state. The list is made by the President after consultation with the governor of the concerned state and can be amended only by Parliament; allowing state legislatures to create sub-classifications effectively allows them to re-order the Presidential list without going through the constitutional process. She also argued that sub-classification, if permitted, would fragment the SC community, create intra-community competition for reservation, and undermine the solidarity that the constitutional protection was designed to foster.

## Why the case remains central

Davinder Singh resolves a structural tension in reservation doctrine that had existed since E.V. Chinnaiah was decided in 2004. The practical problem — that some communities within the SC list consistently occupy a disproportionate share of reserved benefits while others remain perpetually underrepresented — is real and widespread. The constitutional tools for addressing it were blocked by the homogeneity premise of Chinnaiah. By removing that premise, the Court opens the door to state-level measures directed at intra-category equity.

At a deeper level, the case reflects a broader evolution in the Court's equality jurisprudence. The formal equality model, which treats all members of a legally defined category as identical, has given way to a more substantive model that recognises gradations of disadvantage and permits remedial measures calibrated to actual conditions. Davinder Singh extends this approach inside the SC category — a space that, until now, had been treated as a constitutional constant.

The case also raises important questions about the relationship between the judiciary and the constitutional amendment process. Both the majority and the dissent engage with the fact that Parliament has not acted to address intra-category inequity despite decades of evidence. The majority's willingness to permit state-level sub-classification without a constitutional amendment reflects a view that the existing text is sufficient; the dissent sees this as usurping a role reserved for the constituent power.

## Long-term impact

Several threads of future litigation and policy flow from Davinder Singh.

**The creamy layer for SCs/STs.** Justice Gavai's concurrence on extending the creamy layer to SCs and STs in promotions will be the most contested legacy of the judgment. It was not the majority's holding, but it was a reasoned concurrence from a senior judge on a seven-judge bench. Governments will have to decide whether to act on it; individuals who believe they are affected will litigate it. The question whether Indra Sawhney's exclusion of SCs/STs from the creamy layer doctrine is still good law — after Davinder Singh — is likely to come before a Constitution Bench.

**Data requirements.** The majority's insistence that sub-classification must be backed by empirical data on inadequate representation sets a constitutional discipline for how states proceed. States that enact sub-classification legislation without adequate data support will face Article 14 challenges. The collection, quality, and judicial review of such data will become a new front of reservation litigation.

**Uniformity across states.** Different states have different histories of intra-SC inequality. The ruling enables state-specific responses, which may produce a patchwork of sub-classification regimes that vary in the communities benefited and the data standards applied. The Court has provided a constitutional framework but not a uniform national policy, leaving significant room for legislative diversity and conflict.

**Relationship with Indra Sawhney.** The 50% ceiling on total reservations affirmed in Indra Sawhney remains undisturbed. Sub-classification operates within the existing SC reservation quantum; it does not expand the total reservation percentage. But the interplay between sub-classification, the creamy layer extension proposed by Justice Gavai, and the Indra Sawhney framework will require careful working-out in subsequent cases.
