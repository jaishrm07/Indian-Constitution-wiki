## Background

The revocation of Jammu and Kashmir's special status under Article 370 in August 2019 was accompanied by the longest internet shutdown in any democracy in recorded history, lasting over a year in parts of the region. Telephone lines, mobile internet, and broadband services were suspended through orders issued under Section 144 of the Code of Criminal Procedure and the Temporary Suspension of Telecom Services (Public Emergency or Public Safety) Rules, 2017. Journalist Anuradha Bhasin, executive editor of the Kashmir Times, petitioned the Supreme Court challenging the shutdown as an unconstitutional restriction on freedom of the press and freedom of speech that prevented her newspaper from functioning. The petition highlighted a structural problem: the shutdown orders had not been published, making it impossible for affected persons to know the legal basis for the restriction, let alone challenge it.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether prolonged internet shutdowns ordered under Section 144 CrPC and the Telecom Suspension Rules, 2017 were constitutionally valid restrictions on freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) and the freedom to carry on trade or profession under Article 19(1)(g), or whether they failed the proportionality standard under Article 19(2) and Article 19(6). A subsidiary but practically critical question was whether orders issued under these executive powers needed to be published so that they were accessible to the persons affected and could be subjected to judicial challenge. The Court also had to address whether indefinite and blanket shutdowns — as opposed to narrowly tailored and time-limited restrictions — were constitutionally permissible.

## What the court decided

The Court held that the internet is a medium through which the right to freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) and the right to carry on trade or profession under Article 19(1)(g) are exercised, and that any restriction on internet access must therefore satisfy the proportionality standard under Articles 19(2) and 19(6). Indefinite and blanket shutdowns are constitutionally impermissible; restrictions must be limited to what is necessary for the specific purpose claimed and must be subject to periodic review. The Court required the government to publish all shutdown orders so that affected persons could access and challenge them, and to review ongoing orders to ensure they remain necessary. However, the Court declined to immediately strike down the orders before it or order restoration of services, instead directing review by a high-level committee. The judgment thus set a prospective framework — grounding internet access in fundamental rights and imposing procedural obligations — without granting the full immediate relief sought by the petitioner.

## Why the case remains central

Any constitutional debate about internet shutdowns, digital public discourse, or the procedural legality of communication restrictions now runs through this judgment. By locating internet access within the scope of Articles 19(1)(a) and 19(1)(g), the Court made clear that executive restrictions on digital communications are constitutional restrictions subject to the same standards of legality, necessity, and proportionality as other speech restrictions. The insistence on publication of orders was also a significant administrative law contribution, establishing that restrictions affecting fundamental rights cannot be kept opaque from the people they bind.

## Long-term impact

The case reinforced the idea that digital restrictions are still constitutional restrictions and cannot evade ordinary legality and review merely because they operate through communications infrastructure. India continues to lead the world in the number of internet shutdowns ordered annually, and Anuradha Bhasin is routinely invoked — though unevenly applied — in challenges to those orders. The proportionality and publication requirements set out in the judgment have provided a legal vocabulary for civil society, journalists, and courts to scrutinise shutdown decisions. The case also raised unresolved questions about whether the framework it created is sufficient to deter or remedy the harm of prolonged shutdowns, given that the Court did not strike down the orders it reviewed.
