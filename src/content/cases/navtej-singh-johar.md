## Background

Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code criminalised "carnal intercourse against the order of nature" — a provision inherited from colonial law — and had been used to prosecute consensual same-sex conduct between adults. The Delhi High Court had read down Section 377 in Naz Foundation (2009) to exclude consensual adult homosexual acts, a decision widely welcomed by civil society. However, a two-judge Supreme Court bench in Suresh Kumar Koushal (2013) overturned that decision and restored the criminalisation, reasoning that the LGBTQ+ community was a "minuscule minority" and that it was for Parliament, not the courts, to change the law. Five curative petitions were filed challenging Koushal, and a five-judge Constitution Bench took up the matter afresh in the wake of the Puttaswamy privacy judgment.

## The legal question in detail

The central question was whether Section 377, insofar as it criminalised consensual same-sex conduct between adults in private, was unconstitutional as violating Articles 14 (equality before law), 15 (non-discrimination on grounds including sex), 19 (freedom of expression and identity), and 21 (dignity and privacy). A subsidiary question was whether the Koushal judgment — which had given deference to popular morality and legislative inaction — should be overruled. The Court also had to determine whether "constitutional morality," which reflects the transformative values of the Constitution itself, could override "popular morality," which may reflect prevailing social prejudices against minorities.

## What the court decided

A unanimous five-judge bench held that Section 377, to the extent it criminalises consensual sexual conduct between adults in private, is unconstitutional. The Court expressly overruled Koushal, holding that the mere size of an affected group cannot determine the scope of fundamental rights. All four concurring opinions grounded the decision in constitutional dignity, autonomy, and the principle that criminalising identity — rather than harmful conduct — cannot survive constitutional scrutiny. The Court held that constitutional morality must prevail over popular morality, and that the Constitution's transformative character demands inclusion and equal citizenship for LGBTQ+ persons. Justice Indu Malhotra's concurrence went furthest in acknowledging historical injustice, holding that history owes an apology to sexual minorities for the wrong done by the colonial-era law.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment matters because it extends constitutional protection to a historically stigmatised class through the language of dignity, autonomy, and equal citizenship. It firmly established that constitutional morality — not public sentiment or parliamentary silence — is the proper metric for testing whether a law is compatible with fundamental rights. By overruling Koushal, the Court also signalled a more robust conception of judicial review as a counter-majoritarian guarantee for minorities. The decision is now a foundational text for any constitutional argument about the rights of marginalised communities.

## Doctrinal line

It ties together equality, liberty, expression, and privacy in a way that makes Indian rights doctrine more internally coherent than a compartmentalised reading would allow. The concurring opinions collectively demonstrate that Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21 are not isolated provisions but mutually reinforcing guarantees that together protect the full range of individual personhood. The Court's reliance on Puttaswamy's privacy and dignity framework shows how a ruling in one area of constitutional law can restructure doctrine in adjacent areas.

## Long-term impact

Navtej Singh Johar now informs constitutional arguments about bodily autonomy, anti-discrimination, and the limits of moral policing by the state. Its affirmation of constitutional morality over popular morality has been cited in subsequent cases involving minority rights, religious freedom, and personal law reform. The judgment also opened the door to further litigation on marriage equality and adoption rights for same-sex couples, debates that continue to develop in Indian courts.
