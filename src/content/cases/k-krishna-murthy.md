## Background

Following the 73rd and 74th Amendments, several states introduced reservations for Other Backward Classes in panchayats and municipalities under the enabling provisions in Articles 243D and 243T. These reservations were challenged on the ground that they exceeded constitutionally permissible limits, particularly given the Supreme Court's holding in Indra Sawhney that aggregate reservations should not ordinarily exceed 50% of available seats. In many local bodies, the combined reservations for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, OBCs, and women were pushing total reserved seats well above that ceiling. The case required the Court to examine whether the constitutional amendments that authorised local-body reservations had also imported the equality constraints that govern reservations in other contexts.

## The legal question in detail

The central legal question was what constitutional limits apply to OBC reservations specifically in local self-government bodies, as distinct from reservations in public employment or state legislature seats. The petitioners argued that OBC reservations could not be imposed mechanically by carrying over legislative lists without fresh empirical inquiry into whether those classes were backward and inadequately represented in the specific context of local governance. The case also asked whether the aggregate reservation ceiling from Indra Sawhney — generally understood as 50% — applied in the local-body context, and whether courts could strike down reservation orders that exceeded it or that lacked adequate empirical grounding.

## What the court decided

The Supreme Court held that OBC reservations in local bodies must satisfy a triple test before they can be implemented. First, a dedicated commission must be constituted to conduct a contemporaneous empirical inquiry into the backwardness of OBC communities in the specific context of local self-government. Second, the commission must collect data on the inadequacy of OBC representation in local bodies, not merely rely on general backwardness indicators used for employment or legislative reservations. Third, the total reservations across all categories — SC, ST, OBC, and women — must not exceed 50% of the seats in a given local body. Where these conditions are not met, the reservation order is constitutionally invalid. The Court distinguished local-body reservations from employment reservations and from legislative reservations, holding that the participatory democratic character of local government calls for a context-specific justification that cannot be derived simply from pre-existing lists. This triple-test framework has subsequently been reiterated and applied in a series of cases as states have attempted to notify OBC reservation orders.

## Why the case remains central

The judgment shows that constitutional decentralisation is not just about creating institutions but about structuring who gets represented inside them. By requiring a dedicated empirical exercise before OBC reservations can be notified, the Court made the quality of evidence — not simply the political salience of backwardness — the constitutional standard. The triple-test doctrine is now a significant constraint on the political calendar for local elections, because states that wish to reserve seats for OBCs must complete the commission process first.

## Long-term impact

State rules on local body reservation, ward design, and democratic inclusion still have to fit within the framework laid down in this case. The judgment has produced extensive subsequent litigation: states have constituted commissions, had their reports challenged as insufficiently empirical, and faced court orders restraining elections or reservations. The tension between K. Krishna Murthy's triple test and Kishansing Tomar's mandate for timely elections has become one of the defining structural problems in Indian local-body election law, with courts repeatedly asked to balance the two.
