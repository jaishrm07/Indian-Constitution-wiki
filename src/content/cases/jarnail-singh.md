## Background

Following the 2006 judgment in M. Nagaraj v. Union of India, states seeking to implement reservation in promotions for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes were required to satisfy three preconditions: quantifiable data on inadequacy of representation, quantifiable data on continuing backwardness of the group, and no adverse impact on overall administrative efficiency. The backwardness condition proved particularly burdensome and contested in practice, because SCs and STs derive their constitutional status from inclusion in Presidential lists made under Articles 341 and 342 — lists that are treated as conclusive determinations of social disadvantage — and many states argued that requiring fresh data on backwardness was inconsistent with the constitutional logic of that listing process. Multiple service matters involving this question were referred to a five-judge Constitution Bench, which was also asked to revisit M. Nagaraj on the question of whether the creamy layer concept — developed in the OBC context by Indra Sawhney — applies to SCs and STs receiving reservation in promotions.

## The legal question in detail

The principal question was whether M. Nagaraj's backwardness condition was correctly decided. If SCs and STs are designated by Presidential notification under Articles 341 and 342, the argument ran, their backwardness is constitutionally established and cannot be relitigated by state-level data collection exercises. Requiring states to produce fresh evidence of backwardness effectively reopens a determination that the Constitution places in the hands of the President, not the states or the courts. A subsidiary question was whether the creamy layer principle — which in the OBC context excludes the more affluent and advantaged members of backward classes from reservation benefits — should apply to SCs and STs when reservation in promotions is at stake, given that M. Nagaraj had deliberately left that question open.

## What the court decided

A five-judge Constitution Bench held that the requirement in M. Nagaraj that states collect quantifiable data on the continuing backwardness of SCs and STs is inconsistent with the constitutional scheme governing those communities and must be overruled to that extent. The Court reasoned that Presidential listing under Articles 341 and 342 is itself a conclusive constitutional determination of backwardness, which cannot be questioned or reconsidered through subsequent state-level data collection. Requiring states to re-establish what the Constitution has already settled creates an impossible and constitutionally anomalous burden. However, the Court preserved the other two Nagaraj conditions: states must still collect and present quantifiable data demonstrating inadequacy of representation of SCs and STs in the relevant service category, and the reservation must not adversely affect overall administrative efficiency under Article 335. The fifty per cent ceiling established in Indra Sawhney also continues to apply. On the creamy layer question, the bench held — applying constitutional equality logic — that the creamy layer concept does apply to SCs and STs in the context of reservation in promotions: individuals from those communities who have achieved a level of economic and social advancement comparable to general category citizens should not continue to receive promotional reservation benefits that are constitutionally premised on addressing ongoing disadvantage.

## Why the case remains central

Jarnail Singh is the current governing authority on the law of reservation in promotions. It refines M. Nagaraj's three-condition framework into a two-condition framework for SCs and STs: inadequacy of representation (with quantifiable data) and maintenance of efficiency (Article 335), while eliminating the backwardness condition that had generated the most litigation. The creamy layer holding for SCs and STs in promotions is also significant constitutional development, extending the logic of Indra Sawhney into a domain from which it had previously been held at arm's length.

## Doctrinal line

Jarnail Singh demonstrates that constitutional doctrine on reservation is not static — it evolves through engagement between legislative and judicial understandings of the equality code. The partial reversal of M. Nagaraj on the backwardness condition illustrates the Court's willingness to revisit its own prior readings when those readings are shown to be inconsistent with the broader constitutional scheme. At the same time, the retention of the inadequacy of representation condition shows that the Nagaraj framework's core insight — that affirmative action in promotions requires state justification — survives. The creamy layer extension represents convergence of the SC/ST and OBC reservation doctrines at the level of a shared equality principle: reservation benefits should reach those who most need them, and not be captured by the relatively advantaged within a designated group.

## Long-term impact

Jarnail Singh remains the governing standard for state governments designing or defending reservation in promotion schemes for SCs and STs. States must demonstrate inadequacy of representation with quantifiable data and must show that efficiency is maintained, but need not separately establish backwardness. The creamy layer holding has become part of the constitutional discourse on whether further legislative or judicial clarification is needed to define the criteria for creamy layer exclusion within SC and ST communities in the promotions context — a question that remains under consideration in subsequent litigation. The decision is also relevant to the broader reservation jurisprudence in B.K. Pavitra v. Union of India (II) (2019), which tested the adequacy of Karnataka's data-gathering exercise under the Nagaraj-Jarnail framework.
