## Background

Three siblings — children of the Emmanuel family, members of the Jehovah's Witnesses faith — attended a government-aided school in Kerala. Their faith teaches that the national anthem should be respected but not sung, as singing would amount to a form of worship inconsistent with their religious belief that only God should be worshipped through song. The children stood in respectful silence each day when the anthem was played but did not sing. Following a complaint, they were expelled from school for violating the Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act, 1971, which penalises deliberate disrespect of the national anthem. The Kerala High Court dismissed the children's challenge, holding that the expulsion was justified. The family appealed to the Supreme Court.

## The legal question

The central question was whether compelling schoolchildren to sing the national anthem, on pain of expulsion, when their sincere religious belief permitted them to stand respectfully but not to sing, violated Articles 19(1)(a) (freedom of expression, including the right not to speak) and 25 (freedom of conscience and religion). The Court also had to consider whether standing silently during the anthem constituted "disrespect" within the meaning of the 1971 Act, or whether criminal and disciplinary liability required some positive act of contempt.

## What the court decided

A two-judge bench of the Supreme Court allowed the appeal and directed the reinstatement of the children. The Court held that the right to freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) protects not only the right to speak but also the right not to speak — the right to silence. Compelling the children to sing was therefore an infringement of that right. The Court further held that Article 25 protects sincerely held religious conscience, and that there was no evidence the children had acted with any intent to show disrespect; standing in silence is not contempt of the national anthem. The expulsion was set aside as unconstitutional. The judgment was unanimous.

## Significance

Bijoe Emmanuel remains the leading Indian authority for the proposition that compelled expression — forcing someone to affirm something against their conscience — violates constitutional freedom. It established that constitutional secularism is not the erasure of religious difference but the protection of space for conscientious dissent within the public sphere. The case is regularly cited in debates about compelled patriotic observances, school discipline, and the constitutional limits of state-enforced symbolic conformity. It also confirmed that the size or unpopularity of a religious minority does not diminish the constitutional protection owed to its members.

## Why the case remains central

The case is a clear statement that constitutional secularism includes space for conscientious difference, not just formal neutrality.

## Long-term impact

It still appears whenever the Constitution Atlas explains compelled expression, school discipline, and religious conscience in public institutions.
