## Why this Schedule matters

Oaths and affirmations are not ceremonial extras. They are the constitutional threshold for holding office and for accepting the duty to act within the Constitution's limits.

## How to read it

Read this Schedule as the Constitution's language of fidelity. It sits behind ministers, legislators, judges, and other public officers by specifying the form in which they publicly commit to constitutional responsibility.

## What this Schedule contains

The Third Schedule sets out twelve distinct forms of oath or affirmation, covering Union ministers, candidates for election to Parliament, members of Parliament, Supreme Court and High Court judges, the Comptroller and Auditor-General, state ministers, state legislative members, and members of the Delhi Legislative Assembly. Each form is tailored to the precise nature of the duty being undertaken — a Union minister swears to bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution and to discharge duties faithfully, while a judge additionally swears to perform duties without fear or favour, affection or ill-will. The constitutional purpose of prescribing exact forms is to create a uniform, legally identifiable standard that courts can measure conduct against and that signals the moment at which the Constitution's authority attaches to the office-holder.

## Constitutional function

The Schedule is activated by specific Articles that require office-holders to subscribe to the relevant oath or affirmation before assuming their functions. Article 75(4) requires ministers to be sworn in according to the Third Schedule, Article 99 does the same for members of Parliament, and Article 124(6) requires Supreme Court judges to take the oath set out here. This structure means that the oath is a constitutional precondition to the valid exercise of office, not merely a protocol. Deviation from the prescribed form has been argued in litigation as grounds for questioning the validity of acts taken before proper administration of the oath.

## Key cases

In **Har Sharan Verma v. Tribhuvan Narain Singh**, the Supreme Court addressed whether a Chief Minister who had not yet faced a floor test could validly exercise executive authority, touching on the moment at which the oath creates constitutional capacity — a line of reasoning directly linked to the Third Schedule.

In **Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India**, the Court examined the conditions under which a Governor's satisfaction before recommending President's Rule can be constitutionally valid, raising by implication questions about whether officials acting before formal oath-taking are acting under constitutional authority.

The **S.R. Bommai v. Union of India** judgment discussed constitutional obligation and legitimate expectations of office-holders, reinforcing the idea that the oath of office embodies a commitment to constitutional norms that can inform judicial scrutiny of ministerial conduct.

## Current significance

Disputes about the timing of oath ceremonies — particularly when Governors delay administering oaths to elected ministers in states where the political relationship is adversarial — have made the Third Schedule a live point of constitutional friction. The Supreme Court has on several occasions been asked to compel oath ceremonies, bringing the Schedule into immediate contact with questions about Governor's discretion and the right of elected governments to assume office promptly. The form of the oath for Delhi's ministers, which references Article 239AA, also remains part of ongoing debates about the constitutional status of Delhi as a Union Territory with partial self-governance.
