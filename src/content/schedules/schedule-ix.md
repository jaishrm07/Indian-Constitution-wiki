## Why this Schedule matters

The Ninth Schedule sits at the centre of the relationship between reform legislation and constitutional review. It began as a protective device, but later became part of the basic-structure debate.

## How to read it

Read it with Articles 13, 31A, 31B, and 368, then follow the case law that limits or explains how much protection the Schedule can actually provide. It is a schedule about insulation, but not complete immunity.

## What this Schedule contains

The Ninth Schedule was inserted by the First Constitutional Amendment in 1951 along with Article 31B, which provides that none of the Acts and Regulations specified in the Schedule shall be deemed void on the ground that they are inconsistent with or take away or abridge any rights conferred by Part III. The Schedule began with thirteen Acts, mostly land reform statutes from state legislatures that had been struck down for violating the fundamental right to property under the original Article 19(1)(f) and Article 31. Over subsequent decades Parliament used the Schedule as a repository for laws it wanted to protect from judicial challenge, eventually growing to include 284 Acts and regulations covering land reform, nationalisation, reservation laws, and other subjects. The mechanism Article 31B creates is a categorical bar on fundamental rights challenges for any law placed in the Schedule, regardless of the nature of the right infringed.

## The constitutional controversy

The Ninth Schedule was Parliament's attempt to create a zone of constitutional immunity for legislation it considered socially necessary but judicially vulnerable. The core tension is that Article 31B purports to remove all fundamental-rights challenges to laws listed in the Schedule, yet Article 368 itself is subject to the basic structure doctrine established in Kesavananda Bharati — meaning the amending power used to add laws to the Schedule is not unlimited. The Supreme Court confronted this directly in I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007), where a nine-judge bench held that laws inserted into the Ninth Schedule after 24 April 1973 — the date Kesavananda was decided — are not immune from review if they violate the basic structure, particularly by abridging rights under Articles 14, 19, and 21. The 24 April 1973 cutoff is constitutionally significant because it marks the moment the amending power was judicially bounded: insertions made before that date were treated as part of the pre-Kesavananda constitutional settlement, while post-1973 insertions carry no such grandfathered immunity. The result is a two-tier Ninth Schedule — the original land-reform core enjoys near-complete protection, while the later accretions, including several reservation statutes, remain open to basic structure challenge.

## Key laws placed in the Ninth Schedule

- **Bihar Land Reforms Act 1950 and similar zamindari abolition statutes**: The first thirteen entries in the Schedule, inserted by the First Amendment 1951, were state zamindari abolition laws that courts had struck down under the original property rights in Articles 19(1)(f) and 31. Parliament placed them in the Schedule to make land redistribution politically irreversible.

- **The Mysore Land Reforms Act 1961 (and successive state land ceiling acts)**: A range of state land-ceiling laws fixing maximum landholding limits were added through the Seventeenth and subsequent amendments because landlords challenged them as violations of the right to property; the Schedule allowed Parliament to override the judicial invalidations and push land redistribution forward.

- **Nationalisation laws (banks and industries)**: Statutes nationalising major commercial banks and coal mines were inserted following constitutional challenges to the government's acquisition terms, allowing Parliament to protect nationalisation from compensation-related challenges under Article 31 before the right to property was removed from Part III in 1978.

- **Tamil Nadu Backward Classes, Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Reservation of Seats in Educational Institutions and of Appointments or Posts in the Services under the State) Act 1993**: Inserted by the 76th Amendment 1994, this statute providing 69 percent reservation in Tamil Nadu was placed in the Schedule after the Supreme Court's Indra Sawhney ruling imposed a 50 percent ceiling on reservations, making this one of the most legally contested post-1973 inclusions directly vulnerable under I.R. Coelho.

- **Maharashtra, Gujarat, and other state agricultural land tenure statutes**: Dozens of state tenancy and agricultural land reform laws were progressively added to extend protection to legislation where courts had found compensation provisions inadequate or procedural protections insufficient.

- **Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act 1976**: This central statute imposing ceilings on urban landholdings was placed in the Schedule to foreclose challenges to its acquisition and compensation provisions, reflecting Parliament's use of the Schedule well beyond its original agrarian focus.

## Sources and how to verify

- The text of the Ninth Schedule and its full list of 284 Acts can be found in the official Constitution of India published by the Ministry of Law and Justice and maintained at the e-Gazette and the Legislative Department portal.
- The I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu judgment (2007) 2 SCC 1 is available in full on the Supreme Court of India's official judgment portal and provides the authoritative statement of the post-1973 immunity limit.
- Academic commentary and the evolution of the Schedule across its many amendments can be traced through the Parliament Digital Library's constitutional amendment records, which index the numbered amendments and the Acts they inserted.

## Constitutional function

Article 31B is the Schedule's operative provision, and it works in conjunction with Article 31A's general protection for property acquisition laws. The key constitutional tension is between Article 31B's protection from fundamental rights challenges and Article 368's limits on the amendment power — specifically the basic structure doctrine. The Schedule is amended by inserting laws through a constitutional amendment under Article 368, but those amendments are themselves subject to the basic structure limitation. This creates the legal question whether Parliament can immunise a law from judicial review simply by placing it in the Ninth Schedule, or whether that protection has its own constitutional limits.

## Key cases

In **Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala**, the Supreme Court established the basic structure doctrine and thereby created the outer limit within which Ninth Schedule protections must operate, holding that Parliament's amending power, including the power to add to the Ninth Schedule, cannot destroy the Constitution's basic structure.

In **Waman Rao v. Union of India**, the Court drew a distinction between laws inserted in the Ninth Schedule before the Kesavananda judgment (24 April 1973) and those inserted after, holding that pre-Kesavananda inclusions enjoyed full protection while post-Kesavananda inclusions could be challenged if they violated the basic structure.

In **I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu**, a nine-judge bench comprehensively settled the issue by holding that the Ninth Schedule does not provide absolute immunity: any law included after 24 April 1973 that abridges the rights in Articles 14, 19, and 21 can be struck down if it violates the basic structure, thereby permanently limiting the Schedule's protective scope.

## Current significance

The I.R. Coelho ruling has created a practical ceiling on Parliament's use of the Ninth Schedule as a device to shield controversial legislation — particularly reservation laws — from judicial challenge. Several state reservation statutes that exceed the 50 percent ceiling set in Indra Sawhney have been inserted into the Ninth Schedule, and the question of whether post-1973 inclusions that affect equality rights survive under the I.R. Coelho framework continues to be litigated. The Schedule has also become a lens for examining the boundary between transformative constitutional amendment and constitutional destruction.
