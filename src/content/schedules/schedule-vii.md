## Why this Schedule matters

Readers often meet federalism through headline conflict, but the underlying structure often begins here. The Seventh Schedule distributes law-making power and therefore shapes constitutional disputes over centralisation and autonomy.

## How to read it

The Schedule matters both in routine governance and in moments of tension when the Union and states disagree over institutional power, national policy, or regulatory reach.

## The three Lists and their constitutional purpose

List I, the Union List, confers exclusive legislative competence on Parliament over ninety-seven entries covering subjects of national concern — defence, foreign affairs, atomic energy, railways, banking, and interstate trade among them. List II, the State List, gives state legislatures exclusive authority over sixty-six entries covering subjects of local and regional significance — public order, police, land, agriculture, and public health. List III, the Concurrent List, enumerates forty-seven entries — including criminal law, family law, education, forests, and economic and social planning — on which both Parliament and state legislatures may legislate, but where a Union law prevails over an inconsistent state law by virtue of Article 254. Article 248 gives Parliament residuary power to legislate on any matter not enumerated in any of the three Lists, including the power to impose any tax not mentioned elsewhere. Together, these four constitutional provisions — Articles 246, 248, 254, and the Schedule itself — form the complete framework of legislative allocation in the Indian constitutional order.

## How the Lists work in practice

When a law is challenged as beyond the competence of the legislature that enacted it, courts apply the pith and substance doctrine: they look to the true character, dominant purpose, and subject matter of the law as a whole rather than its incidental effect on a subject in another List. If a law substantially relates to an entry in the legislature's own List, it is valid even if it incidentally trenches on another List. Where entries in different Lists genuinely overlap — which they do frequently because the framers deliberately used broad language — courts read the entries generously and try to assign the matter to the List whose entry is most directly and substantially engaged. On the Concurrent List, Article 254(1) provides that if a state law is repugnant to a Parliament law on the same subject, the Parliament law prevails and the state law is void to the extent of repugnancy; Article 254(2) carves out an exception allowing a state law to operate if the President gives assent to it, creating a mechanism for state variation within the concurrent domain.

## Key entries that have generated constitutional litigation

- **Union List Entry 1 (Defence)** — Entry 1 covers defence of India and every part of the armed forces. It has been litigated in the context of whether state laws touching on cantonments, land near defence establishments, or security forces deployed for internal security could conflict with Union legislation, and courts have read its scope broadly to protect exclusive Parliamentary authority.

- **Union List Entry 66 (Coordination of university standards)** — Entry 66 empowers Parliament to coordinate and determine standards for higher education and scientific and technical institutions. It has been central to disputes between the University Grants Commission Act and state university statutes, particularly over affiliation conditions, minimum qualification requirements for teachers, and the extent to which Parliament's coordinating power displaces the state's general competence over education under Concurrent List Entry 25.

- **Union List Entry 97 read with Article 248 (Residuary power)** — The residuary entry has become the constitutional basis for Parliament's regulation of entirely new categories of economic activity. It was invoked to support Union legislation on the internet and digital telecommunications before those fields were expressly brought within regulatory frameworks, and it remains the fallback when an emerging subject resists easy assignment to any enumerated entry.

- **Concurrent List Entry 25 (Education)** — Education was moved from the State List to the Concurrent List by the Forty-Second Amendment in 1976. Its current placement has been the source of sustained tension between Union regulatory frameworks — particularly the Right to Education Act, 2009 and UGC standards — and state governments' claims that the character of day-to-day educational administration belongs to them. The entry has also featured in disputes over medium of instruction, minority institution autonomy, and the regulation of private unaided schools.

- **Concurrent List Entry 17A (Forests) and Entry 17B (Protection of wild animals and birds)** — Forests and wildlife protection were shifted to the Concurrent List by the Forty-Second Amendment. These entries intersect with State List Entry 21 (fishing and fisheries), Union treaty obligations under international environmental conventions, and state forest-based livelihoods legislation, generating disputes about which government's framework governs when they conflict.

- **State List Entry 6 (Public health and sanitation)** — Entry 6 gives states authority over public health, sanitation, and hospitals. It has been in tension with Union legislation on food safety, drugs and pharmaceuticals (Union List Entry 19), and epidemic disease management, particularly when a Union law creates standards that states must administer but may not wish to modify.

- **State List Entry 18 (Land)** — Land, land tenure, and transfer of land are core State List subjects, yet they have been the site of persistent overlap with Union legislation on land acquisition (operating under Concurrent List Entry 42 on acquisition and requisitioning of property), tribal land protections under the Fifth Schedule, and Union land in cantonment areas.

- **State List Entry 62 (Taxes on luxuries, entertainment, amusements)** — Entry 62 has generated significant litigation over whether state entertainment taxes and levies on cable, broadcasting, and online streaming services fall within the entry's scope or whether Parliament's power over broadcasting (Union List Entry 31) or the GST framework displaces the field.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India (1963)** — The Supreme Court addressed whether Parliament had competence to acquire state-owned land, considering whether the Union's legislative authority could extend to compulsory acquisition of a state's own property. The Court held by majority that Indian federalism is not of the coordinate kind: the Constitution does not create fully sovereign units at the state level, and the Union's acquisition power under Entry 42 of the Concurrent List extended even to state property. The dissent by Justice Subba Rao remains influential for its vision of stronger state constitutional immunity and is cited whenever state autonomy arguments are pressed in List-allocation disputes.

**Prafulla Kumar Mukherjee v. Bank of Commerce Ltd (1947)** — The Privy Council, applying the pith and substance doctrine, held that a Bengal law regulating money-lending was valid even though it incidentally touched on promissory notes, a federal subject. The principle — that a law must be assessed in its true character and dominant purpose, not by its incidental effects — passed directly into post-Constitution Supreme Court jurisprudence and remains the primary analytical tool for resolving List allocation disputes. It is cited in virtually every legislative competence challenge involving an apparent cross-List subject matter.

**Synthetics and Chemicals Ltd v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1990)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held that State List Entry 8 on intoxicating liquors does not extend to industrial alcohol, which is an industrial product falling under Union List Entry 52 (industries) and Entry 84 (duties of excise on certain goods). The judgment drew a firm line between potable and industrial alcohol for the purpose of distributing regulatory and revenue authority, with significant practical consequences for state excise revenues. The case illustrates how courts must read the same physical substance through the lens of its constitutional character — looking to the dominant regulatory purpose — rather than its physical description alone.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — While primarily a case on Article 356, the nine-judge bench's reasoning was grounded in the federal structure protected by the Seventh Schedule and held that federalism is a basic structure element. The Court treated the distribution of legislative power across the three Lists as a constitutional commitment that the Union cannot hollow out through either ordinary legislation or the emergency power. Justice Jeevan Reddy's plurality opinion made explicit that the Schedule's architecture is constitutionally entrenched, shaping how all subsequent centre-state disputes are framed.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd v. State of Haryana (2016)** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench comprehensively reconsidered the taxation of interstate trade under Article 301 and the entries in the three Lists conferring tax powers, overruling a restrictive reading of State List Entry 52 and clarifying the test for when a compensatory tax is constitutionally permissible. The Court rationalised the constitutional law governing tax on goods and addressed how List-based tax entries interact with the Part XIII freedom-of-trade framework. The judgment is the leading modern authority on fiscal federalism and the interplay between the tax entries in the Seventh Schedule and the free movement of goods across state borders.

## Current significance

The most structurally significant recent development was the Goods and Services Tax reform: because GST required both Parliament and state legislatures to have simultaneous power to levy tax on the same transaction — a configuration the original Lists did not provide — the Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act, 2016 inserted Article 246A, which creates a concurrent taxing power outside the Schedule's List structure for GST purposes, while also amending List II to remove states' exclusive VAT and sales-tax entries. Online gaming and virtual digital assets have produced fresh List-allocation disputes, with questions about whether regulation of skill-based online games falls under Union List Entry 34 (betting and gambling), Union List Entry 97 (residuary), or state entries, with different states taking different legislative positions that courts are now being asked to reconcile. The movement of education to the Concurrent List continues to generate friction over the National Education Policy's implementation and the Right to Education Act's obligations for private unaided schools, where Union minimum standards for teacher qualifications have been contested by states as displacing their legitimate administrative competence. The Seventh Schedule thus remains the first constitutional reference point for any legislative competence dispute and the structural foundation beneath most centre-state conflicts in India.

## Sources and how to verify

- **Constitution of India** (`constitution-of-india`): The Schedule itself is the primary text; read Lists I, II, and III alongside Articles 245, 246, 248, 249, 250, 252, 253, and 254 to understand the full legislative competence architecture. The Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976 made the most significant structural changes to the Lists — including the transfer of education and forests to the Concurrent List — and should be read alongside the original text.
- **eSCR** (`escr`): Search the Supreme Court Reports for cases on "legislative competence," "pith and substance," "Seventh Schedule," and individual entry numbers to retrieve the primary judicial texts. Key cases include Synthetics and Chemicals Ltd v. State of UP (1990) 1 SCC 613, a seven-judge bench ruling on the scope of the liquor entry, and Jindal Stainless Ltd v. State of Haryana (2016) 11 SCC 1, a nine-judge bench ruling on the fiscal federalism entries.
- **PRS Legislative Research** (`prs-india`): PRS publishes legislative briefs on bills that raise competence questions — including the GST amendment bills, the Right to Education Act, and online gaming regulation proposals — and tracks state legislative responses to Union legislation on concurrent subjects, making it the most accessible starting point for understanding live List-allocation disputes.
- **Vidhi Centre for Legal Policy** (`vidhi-centre-for-legal-policy`): Vidhi has published research on the GST constitutional framework, online gaming regulation, and the distribution of regulatory authority over emerging sectors, providing doctrinal analysis grounded in the Schedule's List structure and identifying where judicial clarification remains pending.
