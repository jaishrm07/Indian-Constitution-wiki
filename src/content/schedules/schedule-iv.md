## Why this Schedule matters

The Rajya Sabha is supposed to represent the states in the federal structure. The Fourth Schedule gives that idea concrete numerical form.

## How to read it

Read the Schedule with the Articles that create Parliament and the state legislatures. It shows how the Constitution distributes federal voice, and why state reorganisation can have consequences beyond geography.

## What this Schedule contains

The Fourth Schedule is a single allocation table assigning a specified number of seats in the Rajya Sabha to each State and Union Territory. The total number of elected seats cannot exceed 238, with the remaining twelve seats filled by Presidential nomination under Article 80(1)(a). The allocation is not proportional in a strict mathematical sense — smaller states receive a larger share relative to their population in order to ensure every state has meaningful federal representation. The mechanism works in conjunction with Article 80, which requires members of the Rajya Sabha to be elected by the elected members of each State's Legislative Assembly using the single transferable vote system.

## Constitutional function

Article 80(2) explicitly requires that the allocation of seats to States shall be in accordance with the Fourth Schedule, making the Schedule a direct operative provision rather than a reference document. Article 4 provides that a law made under Article 3 reorganising States may also amend the Fourth Schedule without going through the full Article 368 amendment procedure, which is the specific constitutional mechanism that allows seat reallocations to follow reorganisation efficiently. This creates a deliberate asymmetry — boundary changes can cascade into Rajya Sabha representation changes without a full constitutional amendment.

## Key cases

In **Kuldip Nayar v. Union of India**, the Supreme Court upheld the Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act provisions relevant to Rajya Sabha elections and addressed questions about open balloting, residency requirements, and the nature of state representation in the upper house — all of which bear on how the Fourth Schedule's allocations translate into actual political outcomes.

In the **Rajya Sabha Seat Allocation** challenges following the reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir in 2019, legal questions arose about how two new Union Territories would be represented in the Council of States, requiring a fresh reading of the Fourth Schedule alongside Articles 3 and 4.

The **National Capital Territory of Delhi v. Union of India** litigation, though primarily about executive power, implicated the broader question of how a Union Territory's unique representation in Parliament (including its Rajya Sabha seats) shapes its constitutional position relative to a full State.

## Current significance

The demand for statehood from Jammu and Kashmir, combined with proposals for statehood for Delhi, keeps the Fourth Schedule under active political negotiation because full statehood would alter both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha seat counts. The representation of newly carved Union Territories through nominated rather than elected routes is increasingly contested on grounds of democratic legitimacy. Delimitation and the prospect of seat reallocation following the next census also raise questions about whether the current Schedule's allocation, last substantially revised in 2003, adequately reflects contemporary demographic and political realities.
