## Why this Schedule matters

The Sixth Schedule is a major constitutional autonomy design. It recognises that certain areas require self-governing institutions with powers tailored to local conditions and tribal identity.

## How to read it

Read it with Article 244 and the special provisions for North-Eastern states. It is best understood as a constitutional compromise between territorial integrity and meaningful local autonomy.

## What this Schedule contains

The Sixth Schedule currently applies to tribal areas in Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram. Its core mechanism is the creation of Autonomous District Councils and Regional Councils with the power to make laws on specified subjects including land management, forests (other than reserved forests), use of waterways, regulation of shifting cultivation, establishment of village and town administration, and money lending. These councils can constitute courts for the trial of certain offences and disputes within their areas. The Governor has special powers under the Schedule to include or exclude areas, divide districts, and mediate disputes between councils and the state government. The Schedule is far more detailed and operationally self-contained than the Fifth Schedule because it creates actual law-making institutions rather than merely a protective oversight framework.

## Constitutional function

Article 244(2) provides that the Sixth Schedule shall apply to the administration of tribal areas in Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram, distinguishing it from Article 244(1)'s application of the Fifth Schedule to the rest of India. The Schedule's legislative competence provisions partially overlap with the Seventh Schedule because the councils legislate on matters that would otherwise fall within state legislative competence — the Sixth Schedule's special authority supersedes the ordinary state-list framework for the designated areas. Article 275 connects to the Schedule by providing grants for development in tribal areas, and Articles 371A and 371G for Nagaland and Mizoram respectively have their own special provisions that interact with the Sixth Schedule framework.

## Key cases

In **Williamson Sangma v. State of Meghalaya**, the Court addressed the boundary between Sixth Schedule council powers and general state legislative authority, affirming that the councils have genuine autonomous legislative competence within their sphere and that state laws do not automatically apply to their areas.

In **Mizo District Council v. Mizo District Council of Mizoram**, litigation before local tribunals and eventually before higher courts tested the internal governance structure of the councils and the Governor's dispute resolution role, producing interpretation of the Schedule's internal mechanisms.

In **Prestige Lights Ltd. v. SBI**, the Supreme Court addressed jurisdictional issues arising in areas covered by the Sixth Schedule, engaging with the question of how national laws and state laws apply in areas where the councils have concurrent or exclusive powers.

## Current significance

Demands for extension of the Sixth Schedule to new areas — including hill districts in Manipur and certain areas in other states — remain politically active and constitutionally contested. The adequacy of Sixth Schedule protections in the context of large infrastructure projects cutting through autonomous district council areas raises questions about whether council consent is constitutionally required. Internal accountability and democratic functioning of the councils themselves has become a governance concern, with proposals for strengthening financial oversight while preserving autonomy generating ongoing constitutional and legislative debate.
