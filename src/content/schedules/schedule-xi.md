## Why this Schedule matters

Local self-government is a constitutional question, not only an administrative one. The Eleventh Schedule helps frame how power may move below the state level.

## How to read it

Readers should see it as part of a longer constitutional attempt to institutionalise democratic participation closer to citizens.

## What this Schedule contains

The Eleventh Schedule, inserted by the Seventy-Third Constitutional Amendment in 1992, lists twenty-nine subjects that state governments may devolve to Panchayati Raj institutions. These subjects include agriculture, land improvement, minor irrigation, animal husbandry, fisheries, social forestry, minor forest produce, small-scale industries, khadi village and cottage industries, rural housing, drinking water, roads, bridges, rural electrification, non-conventional energy sources, poverty alleviation programmes, education including primary and secondary schools, cultural activities, markets and fairs, health and sanitation, maintenance of community assets, and others. The Schedule does not mandate devolution — it creates a menu of subjects from which states may choose to transfer functions. The constitutional purpose is to institutionalise a third tier of government by specifying the subject-matter range within which Panchayats can be given powers and responsibilities.

## Constitutional function

Article 243G provides that state legislatures may endow Panchayats with powers and authority to enable them to function as institutions of self-government, and that such laws may entrust to Panchayats the implementation of schemes for economic development and social justice in relation to matters listed in the Eleventh Schedule. The Schedule is therefore permissive rather than mandatory — the state legislature controls the extent of actual devolution, a design choice that has led to widely varying implementation across states. The Schedule connects to the broader Seventy-Third Amendment framework including Articles 243A through 243O, which together create mandatory elections, reservations for women and Scheduled Castes and Tribes, and State Finance Commissions.

## Key cases

In **State of Rajasthan v. Basant Nahata**, the Supreme Court addressed the scope of state legislative power to define the functions of Panchayats within the framework established by the Seventy-Third Amendment and the Eleventh Schedule, clarifying that states retain significant discretion in determining the actual transfer of functions.

In **Gram Sabha challenges** in various High Courts, courts have addressed whether a Gram Sabha's consent is required before the state alienates land within a panchayat area, connecting the Eleventh Schedule's land-related entries to the broader constitutional question of grassroots consent in development decisions.

The **PESA litigation** in Scheduled Areas raised the relationship between the Eleventh Schedule subjects and tribal communities' rights under the Fifth Schedule, showing how the two special protection frameworks interact in areas where both apply.

## Current significance

The persistent gap between the constitutional promise of decentralisation and the reality of states' reluctance to genuinely devolve functions, funds, and functionaries continues to make the Eleventh Schedule a live governance debate. State Finance Commissions tasked with recommending fiscal devolution to Panchayats often lack independence, and Panchayats in most states remain dependent on state grants for almost all of their resources despite the Eleventh Schedule's breadth. The question of whether the constitutional mandate of democratic local self-government implies a justiciable right to adequate devolution is an open and contested one.
