## Why this Schedule matters

This Schedule is about institutional independence. It helps ensure that constitutional offices are not reduced to ordinary bargaining over pay or status.

## How to read it

Read it alongside the Articles that create the offices themselves. The Schedule gives those offices their financial and ceremonial framework, while the Articles explain their powers and duties.

## What this Schedule contains

The Second Schedule is organised into five parts, each covering a distinct class of constitutional office-holder. Part A covers the President and Governors, Part B was repealed when the Rajpramukhs of princely states were abolished, Part C covers the Speaker and Deputy Speaker of the Lok Sabha and their state equivalents, Part D covers the judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts, and Part E covers the Comptroller and Auditor-General. For each office the Schedule specifies salary, residence, sumptuary allowances, leave entitlements, and pension arrangements. The underlying constitutional purpose is to remove these matters from day-to-day legislative bargaining so that holders of independent constitutional offices cannot be pressured by adjustments to their pay.

## Constitutional function

The Schedule operates as the financial annex to the Articles that create and describe each office. Article 59 directs readers to this Schedule for the President's emoluments, Article 158 does the same for Governors, Article 221 for High Court judges, and Article 148 for the Comptroller and Auditor-General. The specific link between each Article and the relevant Part of the Schedule means that any alteration to emoluments must pass through the legislative process, providing a degree of transparency and procedural protection that a purely executive fixing of salaries would lack.

## Key cases

In **P. Sambamurthy v. State of Andhra Pradesh**, the Supreme Court emphasised that security of tenure and conditions of service — including financial independence — are indispensable safeguards for judicial independence, lending constitutional weight to the salary protection that the Second Schedule embodies.

In **Union of India v. Sankalchand Himatlal Sheth**, the Court recognised that the independence of judges from executive pressure depends in part on constitutional guarantees about service conditions, reinforcing why matters such as those in the Second Schedule cannot be altered to their disadvantage after appointment.

The **Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India** litigation (the judges' transfer cases) confirmed that the financial and service security provided to judges under the Constitution, including through the Second Schedule, are part of the broader architecture of judicial independence that the executive cannot unilaterally modify.

## Current significance

Recurring debates about whether judicial salaries have kept pace with the private bar re-open questions about whether Parliament should revise the Second Schedule more frequently. The Governor's emoluments listed in the Schedule have also drawn commentary in the context of disputes between Governors and elected state governments, because the office's financial independence from the state legislature is one element of its constitutional insulation. The question of whether the President's emoluments should be subject to consolidated fund accountability without parliamentary vote remains a structural topic in public finance discussions.
