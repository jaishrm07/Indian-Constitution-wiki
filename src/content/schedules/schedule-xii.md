## Why this Schedule matters

Urban governance is often discussed as administration, but the Constitution also shapes its institutional framework. The Twelfth Schedule reflects that constitutional ambition.

## How to read it

The Schedule is useful for understanding how urban governance, democratic accountability, and local institutional design fit within the broader constitutional framework.

## What this Schedule contains

The Twelfth Schedule, inserted by the Seventy-Fourth Constitutional Amendment in 1992, lists eighteen functions that may be assigned to municipalities. These include urban planning and regulation of land use, construction of buildings, roads and bridges, public health and sanitation, solid waste management, water supply for domestic commercial and industrial purposes, public amenities including street lighting and parking, slum improvement, urban poverty alleviation, provision of urban amenities and facilities such as parks, gardens and playgrounds, promotion of cultural educational and aesthetic aspects, burials and cremation grounds, cattle pounds, regulation of slaughter houses, tanneries and objectionable trades, safeguarding the interests of weaker sections, provision of urban amenities, and maintenance of vital statistics including registration of births and deaths. Like the Eleventh Schedule, the Twelfth Schedule is a permissive menu — it lists the range of subjects that may be devolved, but actual devolution depends on state legislation.

## Constitutional function

Article 243W provides that state legislatures may endow municipalities with powers to perform functions and implement schemes in relation to matters listed in the Twelfth Schedule. The Schedule operates within the broader Seventy-Fourth Amendment framework — Articles 243P through 243ZG — which establishes ward committees, mandates elections, requires reservations, creates State Finance Commissions for municipalities, and in certain designated cities establishes Metropolitan Planning Committees under Article 243ZE. The Schedule's permissive structure means that its constitutional ambition can be effectively nullified by state governments that choose not to devolve functions, making the gap between constitutional text and actual urban governance a persistent structural problem.

## Key cases

In **Ahmedabad Municipal Corporation v. Nawab Khan Gulab Khan**, the Supreme Court addressed the constitutional obligations of municipalities in relation to housing rights and urban poor, indirectly engaging with the scope of municipal functions listed in the Twelfth Schedule.

In **M.C. Mehta v. Union of India** (the Taj Trapezium case and related environmental matters), courts examining municipal obligations in relation to pollution control and public health engaged with the scope of functions assigned to urban local bodies, touching on Twelfth Schedule entries relating to public health and sanitation.

The **Delhi Slum Demolition** litigation before the Supreme Court and the Delhi High Court tested the constitutional limits of municipal and state government action in areas touching on slum improvement — one of the Twelfth Schedule's listed functions — requiring courts to balance demolition powers against rights of urban residents.

## Current significance

India's rapid urbanisation has made the adequacy of the Twelfth Schedule's devolution framework — and states' actual compliance with it — a pressing constitutional and governance question. Metropolitan cities like Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru, and Chennai have complex multi-agency governance structures that fragment the functions listed in the Twelfth Schedule across parastatals, development authorities, and municipal corporations, often leaving elected municipal bodies with limited real authority. The constitutional status of Metropolitan Planning Committees under Article 243ZE and the question of whether the Twelfth Schedule requires genuine transfer of planning and development functions rather than nominal listing remain unresolved.
