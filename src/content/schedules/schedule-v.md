## Why this Schedule matters

The Fifth Schedule shows that the Constitution does not use one uniform administrative model everywhere. It creates a special framework where tribal protection and land-based governance need stronger constitutional attention.

## How to read it

Read it alongside Articles 244, 339, and 46. The Schedule sits between welfare, territorial administration, and protective governance, making it central to constitutional discussions about tribal rights and state responsibility.

## What this Schedule contains

The Fifth Schedule has five numbered paragraphs. Paragraph 1 defines the executive power over Scheduled Areas. Paragraph 2 provides for Tribal Advisory Councils consisting mainly of Scheduled Tribe representatives in the state legislatures, mandated to advise on matters concerning welfare and advancement of Scheduled Tribes. Paragraph 3 requires the Governor to submit an annual report to the President on the administration of Scheduled Areas. Paragraph 4 is the most legally significant — it empowers the Governor to direct that any Act of Parliament or the state legislature shall not apply to a Scheduled Area, or shall apply with specified modifications, giving the Governor a unique power of legislative modification in these areas. Paragraph 5 empowers the President to declare any area as a Scheduled Area and alter boundaries, making the constitutional protection mobile in response to changing conditions.

## How the Schedule operates

The Fifth Schedule is not self-executing: it operates through a set of interlocking powers that the Governor activates on behalf of the State. The Governor's power under Paragraph 4 to direct that a central or state law shall not apply, or shall apply only with modifications, in a Scheduled Area is the Schedule's sharpest protective tool — it allows the executive to effectively suspend or tailor legislation that would otherwise apply uniformly across a State, making Scheduled Areas constitutionally distinct legislative spaces. The Tribes Advisory Council, constituted under Paragraph 2, must contain members drawn largely from Scheduled Tribe representatives in the state legislature; it advises the Governor on all matters affecting the welfare and advancement of Scheduled Tribes in the State, giving tribal communities a formal channel into the executive decision-making that shapes their lives. Under Paragraph 3 the Governor must submit an annual report to the President on the administration of Scheduled Areas, creating a reporting loop that runs directly to the Union government and sits alongside Article 339's power for the Union to issue directions to States on tribal welfare. "Scheduled Area" status itself is declared by the President under Paragraph 5, which also authorises alteration, increase, decrease, incorporation, or exclusion of any area from Schedule status by order — meaning the geographic scope of constitutional protection can be changed without amending the Constitution itself, a flexibility that has generated controversy when industrially valuable areas are removed from Schedule coverage.

## PESA and the Fifth Schedule

The Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act 1996 (PESA) was Parliament's attempt to give the Fifth Schedule's promise of self-governance institutional form at the village level by extending a modified version of the Panchayati Raj system to Scheduled Areas. The central innovation of PESA is the constitutional primacy it assigns to the gram sabha — the assembly of all adult voters in a village — which must be consulted before land acquisition, before the grant of prospecting licences or mining leases, and before displacing persons from Scheduled Areas, creating a consent requirement that sits above normal executive decision-making. PESA also requires state legislatures in Scheduled Areas to ensure that the gram sabha is consulted before grant of minor forest produce rights and that customary law and traditions are preserved, obligations that place binding constraints on how state law can operate within Schedule territory. The gap between PESA's text and its implementation is, however, vast: most states have either failed to enact conforming legislation, enacted laws that dilute the gram sabha's powers, or allowed executive agencies to bypass consent requirements in practice, making the law a persistent subject of constitutional litigation and civil society advocacy.

## Constitutional function

The Schedule operates through Article 244(1), which declares that the provisions of the Fifth Schedule shall apply to the administration of Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes in any State other than those covered by the Sixth Schedule. The Governor's special powers under Paragraph 4 are an exception to the general structure of Article 200 and federal legislative distribution, meaning that normal legislative supremacy is deliberately modified in Scheduled Areas to give the executive a protective override. Article 339 further empowers the Union to give directions to States regarding the welfare of Scheduled Tribes, completing a triangle of superintendence between the President, the Union government, and the Governor.

## Key cases

In **Samatha v. State of Andhra Pradesh** (1997), the Supreme Court held by a 2:1 majority that the Government of Andhra Pradesh could not lease tribal lands in Scheduled Areas to non-tribal private companies for mining under the Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, because such a lease would amount to transfer of tribal land to non-tribals in contravention of state land transfer regulations and the trust obligation the State owes under the Fifth Schedule. The Court went further to hold that the entire income from mining and forest produce in Scheduled Areas should be used for protection and development of Scheduled Tribes, and that industries set up in such areas must be owned by tribal cooperatives or the State government. Samatha is constitutionally significant because it read the Fifth Schedule as imposing a positive obligation of trusteeship on the State rather than merely granting a discretionary power, and the ruling remains contested — particularly whether it applies to leases to government-owned corporations, a question that later courts have narrowed.

In **Bhuri Nath v. State of Jammu and Kashmir** (1997), the Supreme Court examined the scope of the Governor's special powers under the Fifth Schedule and affirmed that the overriding purpose of the Schedule is the protection and advancement of tribal welfare, meaning that when the Governor exercises the Paragraph 4 power to extend, modify, or exclude legislation, that discretion must be exercised with tribal interests as the paramount consideration. The case clarified that the Governor acting under the Fifth Schedule does not act as the constitutional head of the State bound by the Council of Ministers' advice, but rather exercises a constitutional trust power that is directly accountable to the President and the purposes the Schedule was designed to serve — a distinction with significant implications for when tribal communities can challenge the Governor's exercise or non-exercise of these powers.

In **State of Andhra Pradesh v. Lavu Narayana Swamy**, the Court examined whether protective legislation enacted within the Fifth Schedule framework, particularly reservations and land-related protections, had to satisfy the non-discrimination requirements of Part III in the same way as other legislation. The case sits within the broader tension between Articles 14, 15, and 16 on the one hand and the Constitution's asymmetric protection regimes for tribal communities on the other, illustrating that the Fifth Schedule does not create a complete exemption from Part III scrutiny but rather must be read as part of a larger constitutional scheme in which special protections for historically disadvantaged groups are constitutionally valid provided they do not violate the basic structure.

## Current significance

Mining and forest rights conflicts in Scheduled Areas are among the most active constitutional flashpoints in India today: large infrastructure and extractive-industry projects in mineral-rich states like Jharkhand, Odisha, and Chhattisgarh repeatedly raise questions about whether Samatha's trust obligation has been respected, whether gram sabha consent under PESA was genuinely obtained, and whether Forest Rights Act 2006 entitlements were settled before displacement. The displacement and rehabilitation of tribal communities from Scheduled Areas for mining, dam, and highway projects has generated sustained litigation challenging the adequacy of consent processes and the constitutionality of land acquisition procedures that bypass Fifth Schedule protections. The PESA implementation gap is itself a major constitutional problem: tribal communities in Scheduled States such as Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Himachal Pradesh, and Andhra Pradesh continue to find that state-level panchayat laws either contradict PESA's requirements or fail to vest gram sabhas with the powers the Act mandates, and the Union government has not consistently used Article 339 directions to compel conformity. Tribal rights litigation before the Supreme Court and High Courts increasingly combines constitutional arguments under the Fifth Schedule with statutory arguments under PESA and the Forest Rights Act, producing a layered jurisprudence in which the constitutional promise of the Schedule is tested against the practical reach of executive and legislative implementation.
