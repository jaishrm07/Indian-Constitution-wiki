## Why this Schedule matters

The First Schedule is the Constitution's territorial index. It identifies the units that make up the Union and gives legal shape to the distinction between States and Union Territories.

## How to read it

Read this Schedule with Articles 1 to 4, because territorial identity, admission of new States, reorganisation, and constitutional updating all work together. It is the simplest territorial list in the Constitution, but one of the most important.

## What this Schedule contains

The First Schedule is divided into two parts: Part I lists the twenty-eight States along with the territorial description of each, and Part II lists the eight Union Territories. The Schedule does not permanently fix these territories but provides the baseline reference point that Parliament updates by amendment whenever reorganisation occurs. Its constitutional purpose is to give legal precision to the otherwise general claim in Article 1 that India is a Union of States. The mechanism is straightforward — any change to the number or boundaries of States or Union Territories must amend this Schedule, thereby requiring Parliament to act formally rather than through executive order alone.

## Constitutional function

Article 1 declares India a Union of States and directs readers to this Schedule for the actual list, making the Schedule inseparable from the founding territorial declaration. Articles 2, 3, and 4 authorise Parliament to admit new States, form new States by alteration of existing boundaries, and update the Schedule accordingly, establishing a clear link between the Schedule and the amendment-by-ordinary-law mechanism under Article 4. The Schedule is therefore an operative annex rather than a static table — every major reorganisation since 1956 has required its amendment.

## Key cases

In **Berubari Union and Exchange of Enclaves**, the Supreme Court held that the cession of Indian territory to another country could not be accomplished by executive agreement alone and required a constitutional amendment, which necessarily meant amending the First Schedule. The ruling established that the Schedule has genuine constitutional weight and cannot be bypassed.

In **State of Rajasthan v. Union of India**, the Court recognised that the territorial framework set out in Articles 1 to 4 and the First Schedule expresses a deliberate constitutional choice to make India's Union indissoluble, distinguishing it from federations where member units retain a right of secession.

The reorganisation cases arising out of the **States Reorganisation Act 1956** confirmed that Parliament's power under Article 3 is wide enough to diminish, merge, or dissolve existing States and that no State has a constitutional right to its territory, reinforcing that the First Schedule is a modifiable instrument of Union policy.

## Current significance

The delimitation cycle and the demand for statehood from several Union Territories — most prominently Jammu and Kashmir — keep the First Schedule under active political and legal scrutiny. The reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir into two Union Territories in 2019 through the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act was immediately challenged in the Supreme Court, raising fresh questions about the limits of Article 3 when applied to a State created by accession. Proposed statehood for Delhi continues to generate debate about whether the constitutional distinction between a State and a Union Territory is one of democratic self-governance or merely of administrative convenience.
