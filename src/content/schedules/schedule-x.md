## Why this Schedule matters

The anti-defection framework shapes how representative institutions function in practice. It can stabilise governments, but it also raises questions about debate, dissent, and leadership control inside legislatures.

## How to read it

This Schedule is best read as part of constitutional practice rather than abstract design. It affects coalition politics, floor tests, and disputes over control of the legislative process.

## What this Schedule contains

The Tenth Schedule was inserted by the Fifty-Second Constitutional Amendment in 1985 in response to widespread legislative instability caused by legislators switching parties after election. The Schedule has seven paragraphs. Paragraph 2 is the core provision: it disqualifies a member of Parliament or a state legislature if they voluntarily give up membership of the political party on whose ticket they were elected, or if they vote or abstain contrary to any direction issued by their party without prior permission. Paragraph 4 originally created an exception for mergers of parties where at least two-thirds of a legislature party joined another party, and Paragraph 3 created an exception for splits, but the latter was deleted by the Ninety-First Constitutional Amendment in 2003, making it harder to protect defections dressed as splits. Paragraph 6 makes the decision on disqualification a matter for the Speaker or Chairman of the house.

## Constitutional function

The Schedule operates through Articles 102(2) and 191(2), which provide that a person shall be disqualified from membership of Parliament or a state legislature if they are disqualified under the Tenth Schedule. The Speaker's role as adjudicator under Paragraph 6 has generated the most significant constitutional controversy, because Speakers are themselves party members and their decisions are not easily reviewable. The Schedule interacts with Article 179 (removal of the Speaker), Article 212 (non-justiciability of legislative proceedings), and the broader question under Article 32 and 226 of how far courts can look behind a Speaker's disqualification ruling.

## Key cases

In **Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu**, the Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of the Tenth Schedule but held that the Speaker's disqualification decision is subject to judicial review on grounds of mala fides or perversity, and that the provision purporting to bar judicial review was itself unconstitutional.

In **Balchandra L. Jarkiholi v. B.S. Yediyurappa**, the Court addressed the timing of a Speaker's decision on disqualification petitions, holding that undue delay in deciding such petitions can itself be subject to judicial oversight, a ruling that became important in Karnataka and Manipur defection disputes.

In **Nabam Rebia and Bamang Felix v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly**, the Supreme Court held that a Speaker who faces a notice of motion for their own removal cannot preside over disqualification proceedings, significantly limiting the Speaker's authority in precisely the situations where it is most often abused.

## Current significance

The Tenth Schedule is at the centre of several ongoing constitutional disputes including the Maharashtra political crisis, where the Supreme Court in **Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary** considered at length whether a faction of a political party could constitute the original party for anti-defection purposes and what role the Election Commission plays in recognising the "real" party. The question of whether the Speaker can be a fair adjudicator when they belong to one of the parties in the dispute remains unresolved and is a persistent structural critique of the Schedule's design. Calls for reform — including transferring adjudication authority to an independent tribunal — are regularly made by constitutional scholars and law commissions.
