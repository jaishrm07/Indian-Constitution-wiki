## Why it matters

Article 54 reflects one of the Constituent Assembly's most carefully reasoned design choices — that India's head of state should carry a mandate that spans both the Union and the States. A purely parliamentary election would have made the President a creature of whichever party commanded Parliament, importing the partisan dynamics of parliamentary confidence into the presidential office. A direct popular election would have created an independently elected executive with its own democratic mandate, threatening the primacy of parliamentary government that the framers considered essential for a diverse, newly independent nation. The electoral college represents a deliberate middle path: the President's mandate derives from elected representatives collectively, without creating a rival centre of popular authority.

The exclusion of nominated members from the electoral college is constitutionally principled. Nominated members of Parliament — whether the twelve Rajya Sabha members nominated under Article 80 for their expertise, or the two Anglo-Indian Lok Sabha members nominated under the original Article 331 — represent presidential or gubernatorial discretion rather than popular election. Including them would compromise the principle that the President's mandate must derive entirely from elected representatives. The exclusion of members of State Legislative Councils (upper houses of State legislatures) reflects the same logic: Councils are not directly elected bodies in the same sense as Assemblies, and their indirect electoral pedigree was considered insufficient for participation in choosing the national head of state.

The federal character of Article 54's electoral college is its most significant structural contribution. By giving State Assemblies a direct role in electing the President, the Constitution makes the head of the Union simultaneously accountable — through the electoral process — to the federal units. This design distinguishes India from purely unitary systems where the national head of state is chosen by central authorities alone. The States are stakeholders in the Presidency itself, not merely subjects of Union executive authority. The practical consequence is that which coalition controls which State Assemblies directly influences who can be elected President, making State elections relevant to national executive power in a way that goes beyond ordinary federalism.

The Explanation extending the electoral college to Delhi and Pondicherry reflects the Constitution's capacity for evolutionary adaptation. When these Union territories were given elected assemblies — Delhi through the 69th Amendment's insertion of Article 239AA, and Pondicherry through statute — the 70th Amendment (1992) and the accompanying Explanation ensured their MLAs participated in the presidential electoral college without requiring a formal amendment to Article 54's main text. This has generated ongoing debate about whether the inclusion of Delhi's legislators is constitutionally consistent given Delhi's contested status as neither a full State nor an ordinary Union territory.

The broader significance of the electoral college design is that it ties every presidential election to the current distribution of political power across Parliament and the States. Unlike a constitutional monarchy where succession is predetermined, or a directly elected presidency where popular votes directly determine the outcome, India's presidential elections are exercises in federal coalition arithmetic. The outcome tests not merely which candidate is preferred but how effectively political alliances hold across Parliament and State Assemblies simultaneously. This was dramatically illustrated in 1969 and again in 2022, when the presidential elections became flashpoints for precisely these federal coalition dynamics.

## Reading the provision

The provision is architecturally compact. It names the electoral college but deliberately leaves the mechanics of vote-weighting and the counting method to Article 55, which immediately follows. Article 54 is concerned only with membership — who may vote — while Article 55 addresses how votes are counted and weighted. This division of labour between the two provisions reflects careful drafting: constitutional design at the level of institutional membership is separated from technical electoral mechanics, which can be further regulated by Parliament under Article 71(3) through the Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952.

The words "elected members" carry significant constitutional weight. They perform two exclusions simultaneously: they exclude nominated members of both Parliament and State legislatures, and they exclude members of State Legislative Councils who, while members of a legislature, are not members of the Legislative Assembly. The result is an electoral college composed exclusively of persons who won their seats through direct popular election — a principled democratic requirement for the body that chooses the nation's highest constitutional office.

The Explanation's scope has practical significance. The inclusion of Delhi's elected MLAs means that the strength and party composition of the Delhi Assembly affects presidential election calculations. When Delhi's Assembly is under President's Rule or otherwise non-functional, those seats disappear from the college, shifting the balance between Parliament and remaining State Assemblies. Similarly, the reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir from a State into two Union territories in 2019 — with Jammu and Kashmir's elected Assembly suspended — temporarily removed an entire State's MLA contribution from the electoral college, illustrating how Article 3's power to alter States can have downstream effects on Article 54.

## Key cases

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court's seven-judge bench examined the constitutional nature of the presidential office created by Articles 53 through 55. The Court held that the President is a constitutional head who exercises formal powers only on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers, and that the indirect electoral process of Article 54 does not vest independent political authority in the President. The elaborate electoral architecture reinforces rather than undermines the parliamentary character of the office: a President elected by Parliament and the States still governs through the Cabinet, not personally.

**In re Presidential Poll, Special Reference No. 1 of 1974** — The Supreme Court, in its advisory jurisdiction under Article 143, confirmed that vacancies in the electoral college at the time of the presidential election do not vitiate the election. The electoral college is composed of whoever holds elected seats at the time of the poll — not the theoretical full complement. This principle was later given express constitutional sanction in Article 71(4), which bars challenges to presidential elections on the ground of vacancies in the electoral college.

**V.V. Giri v D.S. Dora (1970)** — Following the 1969 presidential election in which Varahagiri Venkata Giri narrowly defeated the Congress party's official candidate Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy through second-preference transfers in the single transferable vote count, an election petition was filed challenging the outcome. The Supreme Court upheld Giri's election, working through the mechanics of the Article 54 electoral college and the Article 55 counting procedure. The case confirmed that the STV system can produce outcomes that diverge from first-preference totals, and that the Article 54 framework fully accommodates such outcomes.

**Presidential Election 2022 — Draupadi Murmu** — The election of Draupadi Murmu as India's fifteenth President, and the first from a Scheduled Tribe community, illustrated how Article 54's federal electoral college can produce outcomes that cross ordinary party lines. The NDA's majority in Parliament and in a majority of State Assemblies sufficed numerically, but Murmu also secured cross-voting from opposition MLAs in several States, demonstrating how the federal dimensions of the electoral college create political incentives for candidates with broader symbolic appeal than any single coalition's parliamentary arithmetic would require.

**NCT of Delhi v Union of India (2018 and 2023)** — The Supreme Court's decisions on the constitutional status of the Delhi government engaged the question of whether Delhi's elected legislators, whose participation in the Article 54 electoral college flows from the Explanation, are part of an autonomous elected government for other constitutional purposes. The cases highlighted the unresolved tension between Delhi's electoral college participation — which implies democratic legitimacy — and its limited constitutional autonomy in executive matters under Article 239AA.

**Presidential Election 1969 — Political Significance** — The 1969 presidential election between the Congress's official nominee Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy and the independent candidate V.V. Giri — who stood with the covert support of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi — was fought through the federal electoral college of Article 54. Giri's victory, achieved through second-preference votes from a fractured Congress and opposition support, split the Indian National Congress and exposed how the federal electoral college could become the arena for intra-party power struggles. The election is a watershed moment in the constitutional history of Article 54, demonstrating that the electoral college's federal composition gives internal party dissidents genuine power when State Assembly allegiances diverge from Parliamentary leadership.

## Current significance

The electoral college model remains the live architecture within which every presidential election is fought. The 1971 census freeze governing the MLA vote-value formula under Article 55 means that the composition of the electoral college reflects demographic realities now more than fifty years old. As delimitation and a fresh census move toward implementation, there is increasing attention to how a recalibrated formula might redistribute effective weight among State MLAs — particularly between the more populous northern States and the more stable-population southern States. Southern States, which have controlled population growth and correspondingly maintained smaller assembly-to-population ratios, may find their MLAs' relative vote value declining when fresh census figures are applied.

The extension of the electoral college to Delhi's Legislative Assembly has added a layer of complexity given the ongoing constitutional disputes about Delhi's governance. When Delhi's Assembly has faced suspension or President's Rule, its contribution to the electoral college disappears, subtly shifting the balance. The constitutional status of Jammu and Kashmir — which lost its State Legislature and was bifurcated into two Union territories in 2019 — also affected the electoral college composition, raising structural questions about the relationship between Article 54 and Article 3's power to alter State boundaries and whether such alterations should require constitutional amendment when they affect the federal electoral college.

The deeper political significance of Article 54 is that it makes the Presidency a genuine prize in coalition politics without making it a directly politicised office. A President elected by a particular coalition of Parliamentary and State Assembly majorities carries the implicit mandate of that coalition, shaping the relationship between the President and the Council of Ministers in moments of constitutional tension — notably around the formation of governments after inconclusive elections, the timing of Lok Sabha dissolutions, and the exercise of discretionary presidential powers. Understanding Article 54's electoral mechanics is therefore essential to understanding the constitutional politics of the executive branch as a whole.
