  146. Officers and servants and the expenses of the Supreme Court.—

  (1) Appointments of officers and servants of the Supreme Court shall be made by the Chief Justice of India or such other Judge or officer of the Court as he may direct:

  Provided that the President may by rule require that in such cases as may be specified in the rule, no person not already attached to the Court shall be appointed to any office connected with the Court, save after consultation with the Union Public Service Commission.

  (2) Subject to the provisions of any law made by Parliament, the conditions of service of officers and servants of the Supreme Court shall be such as may be prescribed by rules made by the Chief Justice of India or by some other Judge or officer of the Court authorised by the Chief Justice of India to make rules for the purpose:

  Provided that the rules made under this clause shall, so far as they relate to salaries, allowances, leave or pensions, require the approval of the President.

  (3) The administrative expenses of the Supreme Court, including all salaries, allowances and pensions payable to or in respect of the officers and servants of the Court, shall be charged upon the Consolidated Fund of India, and any fees or other moneys taken by the Court shall form part of that Fund.

## Why it matters

Article 146 governs the staffing, service conditions, and financing of the Supreme Court's administrative apparatus. The three clauses work together to create a self-governing institutional structure: the Chief Justice controls appointments, makes service-condition rules, and the Court's expenses are charged on the Consolidated Fund so that they flow automatically without being subject to annual parliamentary appropriation. This design is not merely administrative convenience — it is a structural component of the judiciary's independence from the political branches.

The significance of administrative independence is easy to underestimate. A court whose registry staff are appointed by the executive, whose service conditions are set by the executive, and whose budget must be voted through Parliament every year is a court that the executive can pressure through bureaucratic levers without touching the judges themselves. Slowing the processing of petitions, creating administrative friction for litigants in disfavoured cases, or using budget threats to discourage expensive litigation are all mechanisms that administrative dependence would enable. Article 146 closes these channels by vesting administrative control in the Chief Justice.

The charging of court expenses on the Consolidated Fund deserves particular attention. Under Article 112, certain expenditures are "charged" on the Consolidated Fund rather than "voted" — meaning they are automatically appropriated without needing to be passed by Parliament in the Demands for Grants process. This places the Supreme Court's finances in the same category as the salaries of the President, the judges themselves under Article 125, the Comptroller and Auditor-General, and the Election Commission. The list reflects the Constitution's judgment about which institutions must be insulated from annual parliamentary financial pressure in order to perform their constitutional functions. The Supreme Court's inclusion reflects the framers' commitment to a court that is beyond financial intimidation.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) places the power to appoint court officers and servants in the Chief Justice personally, with the option to delegate to another judge or officer. The word "shall" makes the Chief Justice's role mandatory — appointments must flow through the Chief Justice or someone the Chief Justice designates. The proviso carves out a residual executive check: the President may, by rule, require that for specified categories of external appointments (those not already attached to the Court), the UPSC must be consulted. This proviso reflects the framers' concern that purely internal appointment processes, without any outside check, could become nepotistic. The UPSC consultation requirement preserves a merit-testing mechanism for fresh entrants to the court staff without giving the executive any power to approve or veto individual appointments.

Clause (2) governs service conditions — the rules about pay, promotion, leave, discipline, and retirement for court staff. The Chief Justice (or an authorised officer) makes these rules, subject to two constraints: any law Parliament has made, and presidential approval for rules relating to financial matters (salaries, allowances, leave, pensions). The structure reflects a careful division. The Chief Justice controls the non-financial terms of employment — matters of discipline, posting, duties, and working conditions — without needing the executive's approval. For financial matters, which have budget implications across the wider civil service, presidential approval is required. This prevents the court registry from setting salary scales entirely out of line with the rest of the government's pay structure, while still keeping the executive out of the day-to-day management of court administration.

Clause (3) performs two functions. First, it charges the administrative expenses of the Court — including all salaries, allowances, and pensions of staff — on the Consolidated Fund. Second, it provides that fees and other moneys taken by the Court form part of the Consolidated Fund. The second function is significant: the Court cannot retain court fees as a form of independent revenue. All receipts go to the Consolidated Fund and all expenditures are charged against it. This prevents both a scenario in which the Court is starved of funds through budgetary pressure and a scenario in which the Court accumulates independent financial resources outside public accountability structures. The Comptroller and Auditor-General audits Consolidated Fund expenditures, meaning that Supreme Court administrative spending is subject to the CAG's scrutiny, which is reported to Parliament.

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Employees' Welfare Association v Union of India (1989)** — The Court addressed the scope of the Chief Justice's rule-making power under Clause (2) and the relationship between court administration rules and parliamentary legislation. The judgment held that the Chief Justice's administrative authority over court staff is a constitutional grant that Parliament cannot divest without amending Article 146 itself. This settled that the administrative autonomy conferred by Article 146 is constitutionally entrenched, not a statutory creation subject to ordinary legislative modification.

**S P Gupta v Union of India (1981) — First Judges Case** — The case touched on aspects of judicial administration and confirmed that the constitutional framework for court finances and staffing under Article 146 is part of the broader architecture securing judicial independence. The Court's analysis of administrative independence as a component of the overall constitutional design of the judiciary was later developed and deepened in the Second and Third Judges Cases.

**Central Public Information Officer, Supreme Court of India v Subhash Chandra Agarwal (2019)** — A Constitution Bench held that the Chief Justice of India holds a public office and that information held by the Chief Justice's office is subject to the Right to Information Act 2005. This decision brought Article 146's framework for court administration into direct tension with transparency obligations. The Court held that while Article 146 grants administrative autonomy, that autonomy does not exempt court administrative functions from public accountability under the RTI Act. The decision required the Supreme Court's registry — which operates under the Chief Justice's authority under Article 146 — to respond to RTI applications concerning its administrative functions.

**In re: Cognisance for Extension of Limitation (2020)** — During the COVID-19 pandemic, the Court exercised suo motu power to extend limitation periods across all courts in India. While the judgment rested primarily on Article 142, the Court's administrative instructions issued alongside it — including directions for virtual hearings and registry procedures — demonstrated the Chief Justice's broad Article 146 authority to govern court operations in exceptional circumstances.

## Current significance

Article 146 has come into focus with growing debate about the Supreme Court's administrative opacity. Questions about Right to Information Act applicability to court administrative matters, and about the accountability of court registries, intersect directly with Article 146's grant of self-governance to the judiciary. The 2019 Constitution Bench ruling in the Subhash Chandra Agarwal case — that the Supreme Court is subject to the RTI Act for information held by the Chief Justice's office — brought Article 146's administrative framework into public scrutiny. The ruling clarified that administrative independence under Article 146 and accountability under the RTI Act are not incompatible: the Chief Justice controls appointments and conditions of service, but those exercises of administrative power remain subject to transparency obligations.

The charging of court expenses on the Consolidated Fund also means that the court's budgetary requirements are presented to the Finance Ministry for inclusion in the Union Budget but are not subject to parliamentary vote. This creates a situation in which the Supreme Court communicates its financial requirements through administrative channels to the executive, which then includes them in the budget presented to Parliament. The absence of a formal mechanism for the Court to make its budgetary case independently — without the executive as an intermediary — has been noted by commentators as a structural gap, though no constitutional amendment has been proposed to address it. The CAG's audit of Consolidated Fund expenditures provides an ex post accountability mechanism, but it operates after money has been spent rather than as a prior authorization process.

The relationship between Article 146 and the broader question of judicial accountability is an ongoing constitutional conversation. The self-governing structure created by Article 146 — Chief Justice controls appointments and service conditions, expenses charged on the Consolidated Fund — was designed to protect the judiciary from executive pressure. But the same structure insulates court administration from public scrutiny in ways that have generated legitimate criticism. The resolution reached in the RTI judgment — administrative autonomy and transparency as complementary rather than competing values — represents the current constitutional equilibrium, though debates about the extent and limits of that equilibrium continue.
