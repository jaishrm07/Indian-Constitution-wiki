officialText: |-
  243T. Reservation of seats.—(1) Seats shall be reserved for the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes in every Municipality and the number of seats so reserved shall bear, as nearly as may be, the same proportion to the total number of seats to be filled by direct election in that Municipality as the population of the Scheduled Castes in the Municipal area or of the Scheduled Tribes in the Municipal area bears to the total population of that area and such seats may be allotted by rotation to different constituencies in a Municipality.

  (2) Not less than one-third of the total number of seats reserved under clause (1) shall be reserved for women belonging to the Scheduled Castes or, as the case may be, the Scheduled Tribes.

  (3) Not less than one-third (including the number of seats reserved for women belonging to the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes) of the total number of seats to be filled by direct election in every Municipality shall be reserved for women and such seats may be allotted by rotation to different constituencies in a Municipality.

  (4) The offices of Chairpersons in the Municipalities shall be reserved for the Scheduled Castes, the Scheduled Tribes and women in such manner as the Legislature of a State may, by law, provide.

  (5) The reservation of seats under clauses (1) and (2) and the reservation of offices of Chairpersons (other than the reservation for women) under clause (4) shall cease to have effect on the expiration of the period specified in article 334.

  (6) Nothing in this Part shall prevent the Legislature of a State from making any provision for reservation of seats in any Municipality or offices of Chairpersons in the Municipalities in favour of backward class of citizens.

## Why it matters

Article 243T embeds affirmative representation directly into the constitutional design of urban local government. The Seventy-Fourth Amendment's architects recognised that reservations at the parliamentary and assembly level would not automatically translate into meaningful representation at the closest point of governance to citizens. Clauses (1) through (3) therefore create mandatory, population-proportionate reservations for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes and a floor of one-third reservation for women across all directly elected seats. Clause (6) enables — but does not compel — States to extend reservations to Other Backward Classes. The discretionary OBC provision has generated the most litigation, because OBC reservation in local bodies is not self-executing: it requires empirical backing and must conform to the overall 50 per cent ceiling established by the Supreme Court for reservations generally.

## How courts use it

The SC/ST reservations under clauses (1) and (2) are mandatory and population-proportionate; courts treat them as self-executing once census data is available and will issue mandamus to compel notification if a State government fails to notify reserved wards. The women's reservation under clause (3) is similarly mandatory and courts have struck down State electoral notifications that fell below the one-third floor. Clause (6) OBC reservations are discretionary and have been the subject of the most contested litigation. From Suresh Mahajan (2004) onward, the Supreme Court has held that States cannot simply notify OBC reservation by legislative or executive fiat — they must first satisfy the triple-test: (a) constitute a dedicated Backward Classes Commission or equivalent body; (b) undertake a contemporaneous empirical investigation into the political inadequacy of OBC representation in local bodies; and (c) ensure that aggregate SC, ST, and OBC reservation does not exceed 50 per cent of total seats in any municipality. Failure to satisfy any of these conditions renders the OBC reservation notification constitutionally invalid.

## Key cases

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004) — Triple test for OBC reservation** — A Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court laid down that States cannot extend reservation to OBCs in local body elections without first establishing the political backwardness of OBCs through an inquiry by a commission, collecting data on the inadequacy of OBC representation in local bodies, and ensuring the aggregate reservation cap of 50 per cent is respected. The Court struck down M.P.'s OBC reservation notification because it lacked this empirical foundation. This case is the foundational authority on Article 243T(6).

**Vikas Kishanrao Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021) — Triple test reaffirmed and tightened** — The Supreme Court unanimously reaffirmed and elaborated the Suresh Mahajan triple test. The Court held that the inquiry commission must be a dedicated body specifically constituted for the purpose and cannot be satisfied by prior general OBC commission reports. Contemporaneous data is required — historical or stale data does not suffice. The Court struck down Maharashtra's OBC reservation in local bodies and stayed municipal elections in several districts until the State complied, creating a significant political and electoral controversy.

**Maharashtra Rajya Panchayat v. Union of India (2022) — Election cannot await compliance indefinitely** — Dealing with the fallout from Gawali 2021, the Supreme Court held that while the triple test is mandatory before OBC reservation can be notified, the right of all other candidates to contest elections is not suspended indefinitely. The Court directed that elections must be held without OBC reservation if States fail to complete the triple-test process within a reasonable period, preventing the reservation dispute from becoming a tool for indefinitely deferring urban local body elections.

## Current significance

Article 243T has been at the centre of prolonged political and constitutional controversy. The Gawali 2021 ruling caused municipal elections in Maharashtra to be delayed for several years while the State struggled to satisfy the triple-test conditions. Similar delays occurred in Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, and Rajasthan. The requirement for a dedicated empirical inquiry has proved institutionally demanding — States must constitute a commission, identify representative data, and quantify the gap between OBC population and OBC representation in local bodies, all before notifying reservation.

The women's reservation under clause (3) has recently been given fresh constitutional emphasis by the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act 2023, which extended one-third reservation to Parliament and State assemblies under Article 330A and 332A. Several States have already moved to increase women's reservation in urban local bodies beyond the constitutional floor of one-third, and the Supreme Court has generally sustained these higher quotas as permissible under clause (3)'s use of "not less than." The interaction between the OBC quota and the women's quota — particularly for women belonging to OBCs — continues to raise contested questions about how the 50 per cent aggregate cap is calculated when multiple categories overlap.
