## What this Article did

Article 390 was a transitional financial provision in the original Constitution (1950) that addressed money received, raised, or expenditure incurred between the commencement of the Constitution and 31 March 1950 — that is, the money and expenditure during the period from 26 January 1950 to the end of the financial year 1949-50. It has been omitted.

The Indian financial year runs from 1 April to 31 March. The Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950, part-way through the financial year 1949-50. This created a constitutional question: money that was received, raised, or spent between 26 January 1950 and 31 March 1950 was technically governed by the new Constitution, but the budget authorisations and appropriations for that financial year had been made under the pre-constitutional framework.

Article 390 resolved this by providing that the money and expenditure for this period were to be treated as having been received, raised, or incurred under the appropriate authority, validating pre-constitutional financial arrangements for the remainder of the transitional financial year.

## Why it was omitted

Article 390 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. The financial year 1949-50 ended on 31 March 1950 — just over two months after the Constitution's commencement — and from 1 April 1950 all financial arrangements were fully governed by the new constitutional framework under Articles 112 to 117. By 1956, this provision had been spent for six years.

## Historical context

The constitutional provisions for government finance — the Consolidated Fund of India, the Contingency Fund, the Annual Financial Statement, and the Appropriation Bill requirements under Articles 112-117 — created a new financial framework from 26 January 1950. But the financial year that was already in progress had been planned and authorised under the pre-constitutional framework. Article 390 bridged this gap by validating the financial transactions of the transitional period.

This was a technically necessary provision because Article 266 of the Constitution creates the Consolidated Fund of India, and Article 267 the Contingency Fund, and expenditure from these funds requires parliamentary authorisation. For the period 26 January to 31 March 1950, Article 390 provided the transitional constitutional sanction for existing financial authorisations.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional or financial case arising from the transitional financial arrangements under Article 390 is recorded. The provision worked smoothly in practice.

## Current significance

Article 390 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in providing constitutional sanction for government financial transactions during the brief but legally complex overlap period between the beginning of the new Constitution and the end of the first financial year. It is a minor but precise example of the meticulous attention to practical transition management that characterises Part XXI.

Financial continuity was particularly important in the context of 1950. India was still managing the economic and administrative aftermath of partition, which had disrupted revenue collection, altered the boundaries of fiscal units, and created new demands on public expenditure. The period from 26 January to 31 March 1950 was also the period when the new budget for 1950-51 was being prepared and presented to the provisional Parliament. Article 390 ensured that the government could continue spending and receiving revenues without a constitutional gap while this first budget was being formulated and approved under the new constitutional framework.

The practical significance of this provision should not be understated. The Government of India's expenditure in the months immediately after 26 January 1950 covered salaries for millions of civil servants, military expenditure, development projects, and the running costs of the entire machinery of government. Without Article 390, each of these expenditures in the transitional period could potentially have been challenged as lacking constitutional sanction. The provision eliminated this vulnerability entirely by validating financial transactions during the brief overlap period.
