## Why it matters

Article 305 performs two distinct saving functions within Part XIII's architecture. The first saving clause protects laws that existed at the time of the Constitution's commencement from immediate invalidity under Articles 301 and 303. Without this, the Constitution's trading-freedom guarantees would have automatically invalidated a large body of colonial and pre-constitutional legislation that restricted inter-state commerce — creating legal chaos on day one. A sweep of that magnitude would have disrupted supply chains, cancelled existing licences, and paralysed regulatory regimes governing food, raw materials, and public services. The President's retained power to direct that any particular existing law should not receive this protection gives the executive a graduated tool to phase out legacy trade restrictions on a case-by-case basis rather than having all of them wiped out simultaneously or preserved indefinitely.

The second saving clause, inserted and then modified by the Constitution (Fourth Amendment) Act 1955, protects State-monopoly laws from Article 301's freedom guarantee. The linkage is to Article 19(6)(ii), which permits Parliament and State legislatures to create monopolies in trade and industry held or managed by the State, as a restriction on the right under Article 19(1)(g). Article 305 ensures that once a legislature validly creates such a monopoly under Article 19(6)(ii), that monopoly regime is not simultaneously undermined by the trade-freedom guarantee of Article 301. This prevents an internal constitutional contradiction: if a State monopolises coal distribution as a matter of economic policy under Article 19(6)(ii), it would be absurd for a trader to simultaneously claim under Article 301 the right to distribute coal in competition with the state monopoly. Article 305 forecloses that contradiction by explicitly saving the monopoly from Part XIII challenge.

The 1955 Fourth Amendment, which rewrote this article, was part of a broader constitutional adjustment to permit more expansive nationalisation and state enterprise without triggering Part III or Part XIII challenges. The amendment introduced the specific reference to Article 19(6)(ii) as the anchor for the monopoly saving, replacing the broader original language. This choice — anchoring the monopoly saving in a Part III provision rather than leaving it free-standing — means that the scope of the Article 305 monopoly saving tracks any judicial interpretation of Article 19(6)(ii) and cannot extend beyond it.

The practical importance of Article 305 extends to the design of State-sector dominance in major industries. States that have created statutory monopolies in road transport, liquor retail, mining, and power distribution rely on Articles 19(6)(ii) and 305 together when those monopolies are challenged on trade-freedom grounds. The article is therefore not a relic but an active constitutional backstop for mixed-economy policies at the State level.

## Reading the provision

The "existing laws" saving in Article 305 has a precise temporal meaning. "Existing law" is defined in Article 366(10) as a law in force immediately before the commencement of the Constitution on 26 January 1950. The saving therefore covers all pre-constitutional legislation — colonial Acts, provincial ordinances, regulation orders — that restrict trade, commerce, or intercourse. Such laws are immune from challenge under Articles 301 and 303 unless the President has specifically directed that the saving shall not apply. The President's power to withdraw the saving is an executive ordering mechanism, not a legislative one: it does not require Parliament's involvement and can be exercised selectively. Courts have confirmed that once the President makes such a direction, the pre-constitutional law becomes exposed to full Part XIII scrutiny and can be struck down if it fails that scrutiny.

The monopoly saving clause operates differently. It applies to any law — pre-constitutional or post-constitutional — that creates the type of State monopoly authorised by Article 19(6)(ii). For such laws, Article 301 does not apply at all; they are simply outside the reach of Part XIII's free-trade guarantee. The critical limiting principle is that the monopoly must be genuine: a private cartel cannot shelter under Article 305 by calling itself a State enterprise. Courts have required that the entity managing the monopoly must be "the State" within the meaning of Article 12, and the monopoly must be structured as a State activity rather than a licensed private-sector dominant position.

The relationship between Article 305 and Article 304 is important for State legislatures. A State that enacts a law restricting trade under Article 304 must satisfy the public-interest and non-discrimination requirements of that article. A State that instead nationalises a sector outright and creates a State monopoly under Article 19(6)(ii) can invoke Article 305 as a complete shield against Part XIII challenge. This structural difference gives legislatures an incentive to choose nationalisation over regulation whenever they wish to control a sector, since nationalisation offers stronger constitutional protection against the Article 301 freedom.

## Key cases

**State of Bombay v. F.N. Balsara (1951)** — An early post-constitutional case testing the limits of the existing-laws saving. The Court considered the Bombay Prohibition Act, which predated the Constitution, and assessed whether the Article 305 saving fully insulated it from challenge. The judgment established that the saving protects existing laws from Articles 301 and 303 but does not insulate them from challenge under Part III fundamental rights provisions — Article 305's saving is limited to Part XIII, not the Constitution as a whole.

**Saghir Ahmad v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1954)** — A challenge to a UP law creating a State monopoly in road transport. The Supreme Court struck down the law as then framed because it did not meet the requirements of what is now Article 19(6)(ii). The decision prompted the Constitution (First Amendment) Act 1951, which inserted Article 19(6)(ii) to explicitly permit State trading and business monopolies, and the subsequent Fourth Amendment 1955, which brought Article 305 into its current form to save such monopoly laws from Article 301.

**Akadasi Padhan v. State of Orissa (1963)** — The Court upheld a State monopoly in the trade of kendu leaves, confirming that Article 19(6)(ii) permits monopolies in specific commodities and that Article 305 saves such monopoly legislation from Part XIII challenge. The judgment identified the twin conditions for Article 305's monopoly saving to apply: the enterprise must be State-owned or State-managed, and the law must fall within the scope of Article 19(6)(ii).

**State of Karnataka v. Hansa Corporation (1980)** — The Court examined whether a State's preference for local suppliers in government procurement — effectively creating a soft monopoly for local industry — could be saved under Article 305 or justified under Article 304. The judgment clarified that the Article 305 saving for monopolies does not extend to preferential procurement regimes that stop short of full nationalisation; such regimes must independently satisfy Article 304's requirements.

**Excel Wear v. Union of India (1979)** — In the nationalisation context, the Court considered whether Parliament's creation of a Central public sector undertaking with exclusive rights in a sector constituted a valid exercise of the Article 302 restriction power or was better understood as an Article 305-type State monopoly saving. The judgment explored the interaction between the two provisions and confirmed that Parliamentary nationalisation can draw on both bases.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. M/s Kaveri Rice Mill (1984)** — The Court dealt with a State procurement monopoly for rice, holding that the Article 305 saving applies to laws that genuinely nationalise or give the State a monopoly in a commodity or service, but not to laws that merely reserve a right of first purchase or give the State a price-control power — those latter restrictions must satisfy Article 304's requirements.

## Current significance

Article 305 has limited but continuing practical relevance in two areas. First, in states that maintain government monopolies over liquor retail and distribution: since alcohol is excluded from GST, state-created liquor monopolies continue to restrict inter-state trade in alcohol, and Article 305 is frequently invoked alongside Article 304 to defend those regimes against constitutional challenge. Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and several other States operate State-owned liquor retail chains that function as de facto monopolies; these are routinely defended on Article 19(6)(ii) and Article 305 grounds when private traders challenge them under Part XIII.

Second, the article retains relevance in legacy cess and levy structures that date from before 1950 and have never been reformed. Such levies, if they impede trade, may be shielded by Article 305's existing-laws save unless the President has specifically withdrawn that protection. The power sector provides another active example: State Electricity Boards and their successors (State DISCOMs) operate under frameworks partly rooted in pre-constitutional provincial legislation, and Article 305 is cited when challenges are made to their distribution monopolies.

The broader trend is that Article 305's saving function has become less significant over time as most pre-constitutional trade restrictions have either been repealed, replaced, or subjected to specific Part XIII scrutiny by the Supreme Court. Following the Jindal Stainless (2017) reformulation of the compensatory-tax doctrine, many State levies that previously escaped Article 301 scrutiny on the "compensatory tax" ground now face proportionality review. Article 305 thus operates as a residual constitutional backstop rather than as an active source of contested litigation, but its structural role — protecting State monopoly decisions from the free-trade guarantee — remains constitutionally significant whenever States choose nationalisation over market regulation as their policy instrument.
