## Why it matters

Article 243Z provides the constitutional authority for the entire framework of municipal financial accountability in India. Municipalities in major Indian cities manage budgets running into thousands of crores: Mumbai's Municipal Corporation (MCGM) has an annual budget exceeding Rs. 50,000 crore, while Bengaluru, Delhi, Chennai, and Hyderabad each manage tens of thousands of crores annually across their various urban bodies. The constitutional requirement under Article 243Z that states enact law on accounts maintenance and audit is the foundation on which this financial accountability rests.

The article's textual brevity — a single sentence, identical in structure to Article 243J for Panchayats — reflects a choice to leave the technical architecture of municipal accounts and audit entirely to state legislatures rather than constitutionalising specific standards. This gives states flexibility to adapt accounting frameworks to the variety of municipal bodies they govern: the financial complexity of a large metropolitan municipal corporation bears no resemblance to that of a small-town Nagar Panchayat. States have generally enacted municipal accounts provisions through their municipal acts, designating either the state's own Accountant General, the Directorate of Local Fund Audit, or (for bodies receiving substantial central funding) the CAG as the audit authority.

A major reform initiative in Indian municipal finance has been the transition from single-entry cash-based accounting to double-entry accrual-based accounting, following the National Municipal Accounts Manual issued by the Ministry of Urban Development in 2004. This reform — which has been implemented in varying degrees across states — is premised on Article 243Z's framework: it requires state legislative or administrative action to change the accounts maintenance provisions of municipal law, precisely the kind of provision that Article 243Z authorises states to make.

## How courts use it

Courts have used Article 243Z principally to establish that municipal financial records are public documents subject to transparency requirements and available for inspection under the Right to Information Act. High Courts have rejected arguments by municipalities that their financial accounts are internal administrative documents; they are constitutional records of public institutions, and their maintenance and audit are constitutional obligations.

Courts have also used Article 243Z when addressing CAG audit findings about municipal accounts. Where the CAG has flagged significant irregularities — misappropriation, ghost works, inflated bills — High Courts have directed municipalities to respond to and take corrective action on audit objections, treating the audit as a constitutional process rather than a merely administrative one. Municipalities that have refused to comply with audit requirements or have placed obstacles in the way of auditors have been held to be in violation of the constitutional framework under Article 243Z.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor-General of India v. Municipal Corporation of Delhi (various years)** — CAG audit reports on Delhi's municipal bodies have regularly been placed before the Delhi Assembly and have generated High Court and Supreme Court proceedings regarding specific audit objections. The constitutional basis for the CAG's audit role in municipal finance derives from the DPC Act read with Article 243Z, and courts have treated CAG audit reports on municipal bodies as carrying significant constitutional weight in accountability proceedings.

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (1987 onwards)** — The Supreme Court's ongoing environmental governance cases involving municipal bodies have repeatedly engaged with the financial management of municipal corporations, particularly in the context of infrastructure investment and maintenance. The Court's directions for financial accountability and transparent reporting in municipal infrastructure have relied on the constitutional framework of Article 243Z as the basis for requiring public financial transparency from municipal bodies.

**State of West Bengal v. Calcutta Municipal Corporation (2001)** — The Calcutta High Court addressed the accounts and audit obligations of the Kolkata Municipal Corporation, holding that the state government's failure to constitute an adequate audit mechanism for the Corporation's accounts was a default under the constitutional framework of Part IXA. The case reinforced that Article 243Z's permissive language ("may, by law") does not give states a choice about whether to have an accounts and audit framework — it is a constitutional obligation that must be discharged through state legislation.

## Current significance

The transition to accrual accounting — which would vastly improve the auditability and analytical usefulness of municipal financial statements — remains incomplete across most Indian states. Many smaller municipalities continue to maintain cash-based accounts that are difficult to reconcile and offer limited insight into their true financial position, including liabilities on pension obligations, infrastructure maintenance backlogs, and long-term loans. This accounting weakness directly impairs the effectiveness of the audit mandate under Article 243Z.

CAG performance audits of Smart Cities Mission projects and other centrally funded urban development programmes have revealed significant weaknesses in municipal accounts management, including failure to maintain project-specific accounts, delays in uploading expenditure to central monitoring systems, and inability to reconcile physical progress with financial expenditure. These findings, reported to state legislatures under Article 243Z's framework, provide important public accountability data but have generated limited remedial action in most cases, reflecting the gap between constitutional accountability requirements and their practical enforcement in the municipal domain.
