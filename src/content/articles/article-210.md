## Why it matters

A legislature functions properly only when its members can participate fully in it. The language in which proceedings are conducted is not a neutral or incidental administrative choice — it determines who can debate, who can scrutinise legislation, who can question ministers, and who can effectively represent their constituents. Article 210 navigates one of the most politically charged aspects of India's linguistic diversity: the language of State democratic institutions.

The framers faced a structural dilemma. The Constitution was committed to Hindi as the Union official language and to the eventual transition away from English in official life. Yet the States had enormously diverse linguistic profiles, and many legislators — especially in the first generation after independence — had received their education in English and were not fluent in Hindi or the newly designated State official languages. A rigid rule requiring the official State language immediately would have excluded many experienced legislators from effective participation. Article 210's tiered transitional framework was the constitutional compromise: allow English as a legislature language initially, phase it out after fifteen years, but allow States to retain it by law if their circumstances require it.

The mother-tongue proviso addresses a deeper dimension of the problem. Even the official State language does not capture all of India's linguistic diversity. In States such as Nagaland, Manipur, and Arunachal Pradesh, there are dozens of distinct spoken languages, and the official State language may itself be a second or third language for many legislators. The proviso allows presiding officers to accommodate this reality by permitting a member to speak in their mother tongue when they genuinely cannot adequately express themselves in the prescribed languages.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the language options**: State Legislature business may be transacted in the official language(s) of the State, in Hindi, or in English. States with multiple official languages can conduct proceedings in any of them. The provision applies "notwithstanding anything in Part XVII" (the Official Language provisions) but subject to Article 348, which specifies that proceedings of High Courts and authoritative texts of Acts shall be in English unless Parliament otherwise provides. Article 210 therefore operates within the broader language framework of the Constitution but has its own specific mandate for legislative proceedings, creating a distinct constitutional regime for State Legislatures.

**The mother-tongue proviso**: The presiding officer "may permit" — the word "may" confers discretion, not an obligation — a member who "cannot adequately express himself" in the official languages to speak in their mother tongue. Two conditions must be met: the member must be genuinely unable to adequately express themselves in the prescribed languages, and the presiding officer must exercise the discretion in their favour. In practice, the proviso is rarely invoked formally; more commonly, members informally switch languages and the presiding officer accommodates this without a formal ruling. The absence of interpretation infrastructure in many State Legislatures is a practical barrier to the proviso's effective use, since other members may not understand the mother tongue being spoken.

**Clause (2) — the transitional phase-out of English**: After fifteen years from 1950 (i.e., from 1965), the words "or in English" were to be omitted from clause (1) unless the State Legislature enacted a law to retain English. The transition periods were extended to twenty-five years for Himachal Pradesh, Manipur, Meghalaya, and Tripura, and to forty years for Arunachal Pradesh, Goa, and Mizoram — reflecting these States' later formation and the particular role of English as a linguistic bridge in their diverse linguistic environments. All these periods have now expired. The constitutional default — that English is no longer available as a legislature language — is technically in effect wherever State Legislatures have not enacted laws to retain English. The continued practical use of English in most State Legislatures without formal legislative extension creates a constitutional gap between text and practice.

**The relationship with Article 348**: Article 348 requires that authoritative texts of Acts of Parliament and State Legislatures shall be in English until Parliament provides otherwise. This creates an interesting interaction with Article 210: the proceedings of a State Legislature may be conducted in the official State language, but the authoritative text of Bills passed in those proceedings must still be in English (or in a form that Parliament has authorised). This means that the shift away from English as a legislature proceeding language does not fully resolve the role of English in the legislative process, since the binding legal text of State legislation continues to be prepared in English in many States.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The language provisions of the Constitution were among the most controversial matters in the Constituent Assembly, and Article 210's precursor generated substantial debate. Supporters of Hindi as the sole official language argued that the continued use of English in State Legislatures would perpetuate the colonial linguistic hierarchy and exclude non-English-educated citizens from participating in legislative culture. Supporters of English retention — particularly members from South India and the Northeast — argued that premature transition would exclude skilled legislators who had built their political and professional careers in English and would make technical legislation inaccessible.

The eventual compromise — a tiered transitional framework with State legislative power to extend English, and a mother-tongue proviso for members who could not express themselves in the prescribed languages — reflected the Constituent Assembly's awareness that language policy in a diverse democracy cannot be resolved by constitutional fiat alone. The framers also anticipated that the States Reorganisation Act (enacted in 1956) would settle some of the underlying tensions by creating linguistically organised States where most legislators would share the official language of their State.

Dr. Ambedkar and Jawaharlal Nehru both argued that the transition provisions would give India time to develop educational and administrative infrastructure for the regional languages before relying on them exclusively for legislative business. The fifteen-year transitional period was understood as a minimum, with States free to extend it. The experience of most States in maintaining English well beyond the transitional period suggests that the framers' caution was well-founded.

## Regional language practice across States

The practical operation of Article 210 varies enormously across India's State Legislatures. In large States with a well-established literary and administrative tradition in the official language — Tamil Nadu (Tamil), West Bengal (Bengali), Maharashtra (Marathi), Andhra Pradesh and Telangana (Telugu) — the official language dominates proceedings, with English used primarily for technical legislation or by members with professional backgrounds. In these States, the issue of language accessibility in the legislature has become a matter of regional pride and political commitment to linguistic democracy.

In northern States where Hindi is the official language, Hindi dominates proceedings, and the question of English retention is less salient. In smaller or newer States where the official State language does not correspond to any single mother tongue of the majority of legislators, English often continues to serve as a practical common language. Nagaland, Mizoram, Meghalaya, and Arunachal Pradesh are notable examples where English is the primary or a major legislative language, reflecting the reality that no single tribal language commands the allegiance of all legislators in these linguistically fragmented States.

## Key cases

**Gullapalli Nageswara Rao v APSRTC AIR 1959 SC 308** — Although primarily a case about natural justice, the Supreme Court's early recognition that effective participation in public processes requires that participants be able to understand and respond in a language accessible to them informed the constitutional significance of the mother-tongue proviso. The right to meaningfully participate in a legislature proceeding is undermined if a member cannot express themselves in any language the House recognises.

**National Commission for Linguistic Minorities v Union of India (various)** — The National Commission has examined the practical operation of Article 210's mother-tongue proviso across State Legislatures, finding that the availability of real-time interpretation services varies enormously. In States such as Nagaland and Arunachal Pradesh, English functions as the legislative lingua franca rather than any single tribal language, and the Commission has noted the tension between the constitutional framework and the practical reality of how these Assemblies function.

**Tamil Nadu v Union of India (various, on Official Language policy)** — Tamil Nadu's sustained and consistent resistance to Hindi imposition — including its refusal to adopt the three-language formula in schools and its practice of conducting Assembly proceedings exclusively in Tamil — illustrates how Article 210 operates in practice at the State level. Tamil Nadu has never formally enacted a law under clause (2) to retain English as an option for legislators, but English continues to appear in legislative documents and technical communications without formal constitutional challenge.

**Constituent Assembly Debates on Draft Article 159 (November 1949)** — The Constituent Assembly's extended debate on language in State Legislatures, which resulted in the tiered transition framework, is the primary source for understanding Article 210's purpose. Members from South India, the Northeast, and linguistically diverse States argued forcefully for the mother-tongue proviso and for flexibility in the transition arrangements, shaping the final text significantly. The debates confirm that the framers did not intend the English phase-out to be an absolute requirement overriding State legislative needs.

## Current significance

The gap between Article 210's formal requirements and the continued practical use of English in State Legislatures is the provision's most significant contemporary dimension. Technically, English is no longer a constitutionally available language in most State Legislatures under the default rule of clause (2) — the fifteen-year transition period expired in 1965. Yet English continues to be used, particularly for technical legislation, for communications between Houses in bicameral States, and by members who are more fluent in English than in the official State language or Hindi. States that have not enacted laws under clause (2) to formally extend English are in a constitutionally ambiguous position that has never been directly litigated.

The mother-tongue proviso has taken on renewed significance in the context of northeastern States where linguistic diversity is extreme and where English plays a unique role as a neutral lingua franca between communities with mutually unintelligible first languages. For legislators from small tribal communities in Arunachal Pradesh or Nagaland, neither the official State language nor Hindi may be accessible, and the mother-tongue proviso may be the only constitutional mechanism for full participation. The absence of interpretation infrastructure — the technical capacity to translate a member's mother tongue into a language understood by other members in real time — limits the practical value of the proviso and has been identified by the National Commission for Linguistic Minorities as a gap requiring institutional attention.

Regional language advocates in several States have argued for more exclusive use of the official State language in legislative proceedings, seeing the continued use of English as a form of colonial hangover that perpetuates elite access to legislative culture. These arguments raise important questions about access and inclusivity that run in opposite directions: English may be a barrier for citizens who want to understand their legislature's proceedings in the dominant regional language, but it is also a resource for legislators who were educated in English and whose linguistic competence is in that medium. Article 210's framework cannot fully resolve this tension; it provides a constitutional structure within which States must make their own linguistic policy choices, and the provision's flexibility — in allowing State legislatures to retain English by law — is the framers' constitutional acknowledgment that these choices are too contextually varied to be resolved by a single national rule.
