## Why it matters

Article 51 gives the Indian Constitution an outward-facing dimension that most domestic constitutional instruments lack. It expresses a commitment to international peace and security, to treaty obligations, to international law, and to arbitration as a method of dispute resolution — aspirations that reflect both the Constituent Assembly's idealism and India's founding foreign-policy identity. The drafters were conscious that independent India was entering a world still recovering from a devastating war, in which two nuclear-armed blocs were forming, and in which the newly established United Nations represented humanity's best available attempt to institutionalise peaceful coexistence. Nehru's vision of non-alignment and India's role as a voice of the newly decolonised world are the political context within which Article 51 was framed.

But Article 51 is more than foreign-policy aspiration. Clause (c) — to foster respect for international law and treaty obligations — has been used by courts as a constitutional bridge between India's domestic legal order and international law. The principle of treaty-consistent interpretation that courts apply draws on Article 51(c): when a domestic statute is capable of two interpretations, courts prefer the one consistent with India's treaty obligations because the Constitution itself commands respect for those obligations. This is not equivalent to the monist systems in which international treaties are automatically part of domestic law, but it is a constitutionally grounded directive toward interpretive harmonisation.

The article also reflects the Constituent Assembly's commitment to what they called "civilised" conduct in international affairs — the rule of law between nations rather than the law of the jungle. Article 51(d)'s encouragement of international arbitration anticipated the major role that treaty-based investor-state and commercial arbitration would eventually play, and provides a constitutional basis for India's participation in international arbitration regimes even when they limit what domestic courts can do in relation to foreign awards.

## Reading the provision

The most important judicial use of Article 51 is as a basis for importing international human rights standards into the interpretation of Fundamental Rights. The technique works as follows: Article 51(c) directs the State to foster respect for international law and treaty obligations; India has ratified various human rights treaties (ICCPR, CEDAW, CAT, the Convention on the Rights of the Child); the content of those treaties therefore informs what the Fundamental Rights in Part III mean, because interpreting rights consistently with treaty obligations is itself a constitutional requirement.

This approach has been used most prominently to expand the content of the right to life under Article 21 using international human rights standards, to develop gender-equality norms using CEDAW, and to prohibit practices such as torture and custodial violence using the Convention Against Torture. Courts have noted that while India's treaty ratifications do not automatically make treaty provisions part of domestic law, Article 51(c) creates a constitutional obligation to interpret domestic law — including the Constitution itself — in a manner consistent with those obligations.

Article 51 is also invoked in cases about the enforcement of foreign arbitral awards, where the question arises whether India's commitment to treaty-based arbitration (through the New York Convention) should be given generous judicial support. Courts have treated Article 51(d) as a constitutional signal that India's domestic courts should be supportive rather than obstructive of international arbitration, reinforcing the legislative policy of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act.

## Key cases

**Jolly George Varghese v. Bank of Cochin (1980)** — The Supreme Court examined whether arresting a person for civil debt — a practice permitted under the Code of Civil Procedure — was consistent with India's obligations under Article 11 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, which prohibits imprisonment merely for inability to fulfil a contractual obligation. The Court used Article 51(c) to note the constitutional directive toward consistency with treaty obligations, and held that the CPC provision, properly interpreted, did not permit arrest of a debtor who was genuinely unable (rather than willingly refusing) to pay. The judgment is a significant early instance of the Court using Article 51(c) to read a domestic procedural provision narrowly to avoid a conflict with an international human rights standard.

**Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997)** — The Supreme Court filled a legislative gap on sexual harassment at the workplace by deriving enforceable guidelines from the Constitution read alongside India's obligations under CEDAW (Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women). The Court explicitly relied on Article 51(c) as the constitutional basis for treating CEDAW's provisions as informing the content of the right to equality and the right to life with dignity under Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21. The judgment is the clearest instance in Indian constitutional law of Article 51(c) being used to give treaty obligations direct domestic legal effect, and the guidelines it issued remained the operative law on sexual harassment until the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act, 2013 was enacted.

**People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India (1997)** — The Supreme Court held that telephone tapping constitutes a serious invasion of the right to privacy under Article 21, and used Article 51(c) in conjunction with India's obligations under the ICCPR to hold that any restriction on privacy must satisfy the requirement of legality, necessity, and proportionality — standards drawn from international human rights law. The Court treated Article 51(c)'s directive to foster respect for international law as a constitutional mandate to read Article 21 expansively in light of the international right to privacy under ICCPR Article 17, thereby importing a proportionality framework that has since become standard in Indian fundamental rights adjudication.

**Gramophone Company v. Birendra Bahadur Pandey (1984)** — The Supreme Court considered whether a treaty to which India was a party was automatically part of Indian law or required parliamentary legislation to have domestic effect. The Court held that customary international law is part of domestic law to the extent not inconsistent with Indian statute or constitutional provisions, and that Article 51(c)'s directive to foster respect for international law informs the interpretive approach courts should take. The judgment established the dualist framework for treaty incorporation while acknowledging that Article 51(c) creates a constitutional directive toward harmonisation — meaning courts should avoid interpretations that unnecessarily conflict with India's international obligations.

## Current significance

Article 51 has become more rather than less important as India's integration into international legal regimes has deepened. International investment arbitration — particularly under bilateral investment treaties — raises questions about the limits that India's sovereignty places on treaty commitments, and Article 51(d)'s encouragement of arbitration is part of the constitutional landscape within which courts assess those limits. India's withdrawal from or renegotiation of bilateral investment treaties since 2016 and the resulting arbitration claims will generate constitutional questions touching on Article 51 for years to come.

Article 51(c) is also increasingly relevant in the context of human rights treaty obligations. India has not ratified the First Optional Protocol to the ICCPR (which would allow individual complaints to the Human Rights Committee), and it has resisted some international scrutiny mechanisms, but its treaty ratifications create obligations that are constitutionally acknowledged by Article 51(c). Courts and litigants continue to invoke these obligations in cases about custodial torture, the rights of migrant workers, the treatment of asylum seekers, and the constitutional standards applicable to anti-terrorism detention. The interaction between domestic security law and international human rights standards, mediated through Article 51(c), is one of the most active frontier questions in Indian constitutional law.
