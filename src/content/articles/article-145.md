  (3) [4***The minimum number] of Judges who are to sit for the purpose of deciding any case involving a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution or for the purpose of hearing any reference under article 143 shall be five:

  Provided that, where the Court hearing an appeal under any of the provisions of this Chapter other than article 132 consists of less than five Judges and in the course of the hearing of the appeal the Court is satisfied that the appeal involves a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution the determination of which is necessary for the disposal of the appeal, such Court shall refer the question for opinion to a Court constituted as required by this clause for the purpose of deciding any case involving such a question and shall on receipt of the opinion dispose of the appeal in conformity with such opinion.

## Why it matters

Article 145 is the constitutional source of the Supreme Court's power to govern itself. It authorises the Court to make rules covering every aspect of its practice and procedure, subject only to Parliamentary law and the President's approval. Three specific provisions of Article 145 are especially significant: the five-judge bench requirement for substantial constitutional questions, the requirement that judgments be delivered in open court, and the majority-with-dissent rule. These constraints — combined with the Court's rule-making autonomy — shape the institutional culture of the Court as much as any substantive constitutional provision.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) enumerates the subjects on which rules may be made: appearance of advocates, hearing of appeals and fundamental-rights matters, review petitions, costs, bail, stays, summary dismissal of frivolous appeals, and procedures for inquiries under Article 317. The enumeration is illustrative, not exhaustive. The Supreme Court Rules, 2013 — the current operative instrument — run to scores of Orders and are the practical governing text for every matter that comes before the Court.

Clause (3) is the most litigated provision. It mandates that at least five judges sit for any case involving a "substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution" or a Presidential Reference under Article 143. What counts as a "substantial question" is itself a question of law, and parties regularly contend that a smaller bench has wrongly determined a constitutional issue. The proviso to clause (3) provides a mechanism for referral upward if a smaller bench, mid-hearing, recognises that a constitutional question has arisen that requires a five-judge court.

Clauses (4) and (5) address the form of judicial decision-making. No judgment may be pronounced except in open court — a requirement aimed at transparency and public accountability. No judgment or advisory opinion is valid without the concurrence of a majority of the judges present at the hearing. The dissent right in clause (5) is constitutionally preserved, making the tradition of separate concurring and dissenting opinions part of the constitutional design rather than mere practice.

The review jurisdiction — the power to reconsider the Court's own judgments — is regulated under clause (1)(e), and the Supreme Court Rules confine it tightly. A further development is the curative petition jurisdiction recognised in Rupa Ashok Hurra v Ashok Hurra (2002), which allows a petitioner to seek relief from a final judgment on extremely limited grounds, primarily denial of natural justice or judicial bias. The curative petition is not mentioned in the Constitution; it sits at the junction of Article 145 rule-making power and the Court's inherent jurisdiction.

## Key cases

**Rojer Mathew v South Indian Bank Ltd (2019)** — A five-judge bench constituted under Article 145(3) struck down the Tribunal, Appellate Tribunal and other Authorities (Qualifications, Experience and Other Conditions of Service of Members) Rules, 2017, on the ground that they impermissibly diminished the independence of tribunals. The case confirmed that whenever a challenge raises a substantial constitutional question — including about the separation of powers — the five-judge quorum is mandatory.

**Rupa Ashok Hurra v Ashok Hurra (2002)** — A Constitution Bench, considering whether any remedy existed against a final Supreme Court order after a review petition had been dismissed, recognised the curative petition as an inherent jurisdiction of the Court, grounded partly in Article 145's rule-making power and partly in the constitutional principle that no litigant should be denied natural justice. This case effectively created a new layer of the Court's jurisdiction without any express constitutional amendment.

**Central Public Information Officer, Supreme Court of India v Subhash Chandra Agarwal (2019)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench held that the office of the Chief Justice of India is a public authority under the Right to Information Act and that information held by the Court in its judicial capacity is subject to the Act's transparency requirements, with appropriate protections for deliberative secrecy. The case required five judges because it turned on the interpretation of constitutional provisions governing the Court's autonomy and the fundamental right to information.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (Second Judges Case) (1993)** — The nine-judge bench affirmed that the Court's rule-making authority under Article 145 is an attribute of judicial independence and cannot be abridged by executive action. Any rule made under Article 145 that requires Presidential approval cannot be modified by the executive unilaterally; the Court's procedural autonomy is constitutionally entrenched.

## Current significance

The five-judge bench requirement under clause (3) is a persistent flashpoint. Litigants and commentators frequently argue that important constitutional decisions have been handed down by smaller benches without constituting the required court, leading to belated referrals to larger benches and consequent uncertainty in the law. The assignment of cases to particular benches — formally governed by the Chief Justice's roster power under the Supreme Court Rules — has itself become a transparency issue, with concerns about selective allocation of sensitive cases. The Supreme Court Rules 2013 were last comprehensively revised in 2013, but discussions about updating them to reflect the Court's expanded size and workload continue. Any revision requires Presidential approval, making the process of internal reform subject to a layer of executive oversight that sits in tension with the judicial independence rationale that underlies Article 145.

The requirement that judgments be delivered in open court — clause (4) — has taken on new meaning in the era of live-streaming of Supreme Court proceedings. The Court has authorised live-streaming of Constitution Bench hearings, treating this as consistent with and an extension of the open-court requirement. The open-court and majority-judgment requirements in clauses (4) and (5) together form the constitutional basis for the Court's culture of published, reasoned, public decision-making — a culture that distinguishes the Supreme Court from the many advisory and executive bodies that also exercise significant constitutional authority but do so without the same transparency obligations.
