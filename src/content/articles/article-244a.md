  [244A. Formation of an autonomous State comprising certain tribal areas in Assam and creation of local Legislature or Council of Ministers or both therefor.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, Parliament may, by law, form within the State of Assam an autonomous State comprising (whether wholly or in part) all or any of the tribal areas specified in [Part I] of the table appended to paragraph 20 of the Sixth Schedule and create therefor—

  (a) a body, whether elected or partly nominated and partly elected, to function as a Legislature for the autonomous State, or

  (b) a Council of Ministers, or both with such constitution, powers and functions, in each case, as may be specified in the law.

  (2) Any such law as is referred to in clause (1) may, in particular,—

  (a) specify the matters enumerated in the State List or the Concurrent List with respect to which the Legislature of the autonomous State shall have power to make laws for the whole or any part thereof, whether to the exclusion of the Legislature of the State of Assam or otherwise;

  (b) define the matters with respect to which the executive power of the autonomous State shall extend;

  (c) provide that any tax levied by the State of Assam shall be assigned to the autonomous State in so far as the proceeds thereof are attributable to the autonomous State;

  (d) provide that any reference to a State in any article of this Constitution shall be construed as including a reference to the autonomous State; and

  (e) make such supplemental, incidental and consequential provisions as may be deemed necessary.

  (3) An amendment of any such law as aforesaid in so far as such amendment relates to any of the matters specified in sub-clause (a) or sub-clause (b) of clause (2) shall have no effect unless the amendment is passed in each House of Parliament by not less than two-thirds of the members present and voting.

  (4) Any such law as is referred to in this article shall not be deemed to be an amendment of this Constitution for the purposes of article 368 notwithstanding that it contains any provision which amends or has the effect of amending this Constitution.]

## What this Article does

Article 244A was inserted by the Constitution (Twenty-Second Amendment) Act, 1969, in direct response to sustained demands by tribal communities in the hill districts of Assam — particularly the Khasi, Jaintia, Garo, Mizo, and Naga groups — for a separate homeland or meaningful autonomy. The political context was turbulent: the Mizo National Front insurgency began in 1966 and the Naga movement had been active for over a decade. Simply separating hill areas from Assam under Article 3 was resisted both by the Assam government and by Central governments that wished to preserve Assam's territorial integrity.

Article 244A was the constitutional answer: it empowers Parliament to create an "autonomous State" within Assam comprising the tribal areas listed in Part I of the table to paragraph 20 of the Sixth Schedule. This sub-State entity could be given its own Legislature (elected, or partly nominated and partly elected) and its own Council of Ministers — constitutional structures associated with full statehood, not merely with the Autonomous District Councils that already existed under the Sixth Schedule. The autonomous State could receive legislative powers over specified subjects from both the State List and the Concurrent List, and its taxes from the parent State of Assam could be assigned to it.

The provision is a sophisticated instrument of asymmetric federalism: it creates a constitutionally entrenched sub-State unit without requiring formal bifurcation of Assam, with its own democratic institutions but falling short of a full State's sovereign equivalence in the federal structure.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) gives Parliament the power to act and specifies the geographic scope (tribal areas in Part I of the Sixth Schedule table). The permissive "may" is significant — this is a faculty for Parliament, not a mandatory prescription.

Clause (2) sets out what the law creating the autonomous State may contain: legislative powers over specified State List or Concurrent List subjects, executive power definitions, tax revenue assignments, a provision that references to "State" in the Constitution shall include the autonomous State, and supplemental provisions. The ability to assign State List legislative powers to the autonomous State while "excluding the Legislature of the State of Assam or otherwise" means Parliament can create exclusive or concurrent legislative jurisdictions for the sub-State unit.

Clause (3) provides entrenchment: any amendment to the core legislative or executive powers of the autonomous State requires a two-thirds majority of members present and voting in each House of Parliament. This is a higher threshold than ordinary legislation but lower than the full Article 368 procedure. It reflects the quasi-permanent character of the arrangement.

Clause (4) explicitly provides that a law made under this article is not a constitutional amendment for the purposes of Article 368, even if it amends or has the effect of amending the Constitution. This is a parliamentary drafting device to ensure that Parliament can create (and modify) the autonomous State by ordinary legislative process — with the special majority for core powers — rather than going through the full constitutional amendment procedure.

## Key cases

**No judicial interpretation on the merits** — Article 244A has never been used to create an autonomous State, and no case has directly interpreted its substantive content. The article has been cited in scholarly and parliamentary debates but has not been the subject of dispositive litigation. This blank slate means several questions remain constitutionally open: What is the relationship between the autonomous State's legislature and the Assam Legislative Assembly for concurrent subjects? Can the autonomous State enforce its laws against Union citizens transiting through the area? Can it be dissolved by Assam?

**Assam Reorganisation Cases — background jurisprudence** — The reorganisation of north-eastern India through the North-Eastern Areas (Reorganisation) Act, 1971, relied on Parliament's power under Article 3 rather than Article 244A, creating the States of Meghalaya, Manipur, and Tripura. Courts in these matters confirmed that Article 3's power to form new States could carve territory from an existing State without that State's consent. Article 244A was available as an alternative but was not used — a legislative preference, not a constitutional compulsion.

**State of Assam v. Barga Gohain (1984)** — The Supreme Court addressed questions about the scope of Sixth Schedule powers in relation to Assam's jurisdiction over tribal areas, confirming that the Sixth Schedule creates a distinct tier of governance for tribal areas that coexists with State authority. Article 244A was available as a further evolution of this framework but was not invoked.

## The Bodoland question

The most discussed potential application of Article 244A concerns the Bodo community of Assam. Bodo groups sought a separate State of Bodoland through decades of political negotiation and, at times, armed insurgency. The Bodoland Territorial Areas District was created in 2003 under the Sixth Schedule through the Bodoland Territorial Council Accord between the National Democratic Front of Bodoland and the Central Government. This settlement, while substantial, falls well short of what Article 244A could have provided: a separate Legislature and Council of Ministers with independent lawmaking power.

The choice of the Sixth Schedule route over Article 244A reflected the political preferences of both the Bodo leadership (who wanted more than a District Council but were willing to accept an enhanced Sixth Schedule solution) and the Central Government (which was unwilling to create a quasi-State institution within Assam). Subsequent Bodo accords in 2020 further modified the territorial arrangements without invoking Article 244A. The continued dormancy of Article 244A in the Bodoland context illustrates the gap between a constitutional provision's theoretical capacity and its political feasibility.

## Current significance

Article 244A remains a latent constitutional instrument and a template for asymmetric federalism in India's north-east.
It is available for any future parliamentary settlement of tribal autonomy demands within Assam that goes beyond what the Sixth Schedule can provide but does not require full statehood under Article 3.
Its continued dormancy reflects the consistent preference of Central governments for peace accords combined with enhanced Sixth Schedule councils, which are administratively simpler to create, easier to dissolve, and politically less permanent than an Article 244A autonomous State.

Several unresolved questions would arise if Article 244A were ever invoked:

- Can the Assam Legislative Assembly legislate on a subject assigned exclusively to the autonomous State's legislature, and who has authority to resolve the conflict?
- Does the autonomous State have standing as a unit in the Council of States (Rajya Sabha), which is constituted on a State basis?
- How does the reference to "State" in Article 244A(2)(d) — requiring that any reference to a State in the Constitution be read as including the autonomous State — interact with provisions such as Article 356 (President's Rule) and Article 263 (inter-State councils)?
- Can the Governor of Assam exercise the functions of constitutional head over the autonomous State, or would a separate Lieutenant Governor-type appointment be required?

Comparativists note that Article 244A is unusual in constitutionalising a sub-State unit with its own legislature and cabinet — a structure more typical of asymmetric federal settlements in countries like Spain (autonomous communities) or the United Kingdom (devolution to Scotland and Wales).
The article's continued dormancy means its exact constitutional contours have never been tested judicially, leaving India with an untested but potentially significant instrument for managing sub-State territorial autonomy if future political conditions make its use preferable to full statehood or Sixth Schedule councils.
