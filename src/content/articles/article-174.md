## Why it matters

Article 174 is the constitutional switch that turns State Legislatures on and off. Without the Governor summoning the House, no legislation can pass, no questions can be asked, no confidence can be tested, and no budget can be voted. The provision is therefore not merely a housekeeping rule about the legislative calendar — it is the threshold condition for parliamentary democracy at the State level. When a Governor refuses to summon a session advised by the Cabinet, or unilaterally advances a session date to benefit one faction over another, or dissolves the Assembly to prevent an inconvenient majority from forming a government, the power in Article 174 becomes a constitutional weapon rather than a constitutional facility.

The six-month gap rule in clause (1) is the constitutional floor that prevents governments from governing without legislative sanction. It requires that a new session begin within six months of the last sitting. This is a hard rule — not a guideline, not a target — and its breach has potential constitutional consequences for the lawfulness of executive action taken without parliamentary cover. In practice, most State Legislatures hold at least three sessions annually (budget, monsoon, and winter), but the constitutional minimum is two sittings with no more than six months between them.

The political significance of Article 174 has grown sharply as India has experienced a series of high-stakes political crises at the State level. From Arunachal Pradesh in 2016 to Maharashtra in 2022 to Tamil Nadu and Punjab in 2023, the question of who controls the legislative calendar has become a proxy for the question of who controls the State government. The courts' progressive intervention to impose constitutional discipline on gubernatorial conduct under Article 174 reflects the depth of the problem: the formal text grants broad powers to the Governor, but the constitutional scheme requires those powers to be exercised to enable democratic governance, not to obstruct it.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the summoning obligation and the six-month rule**: The Governor "shall from time to time summon" the House. The mandatory language — "shall" — combined with the six-month interval requirement makes summoning an obligation, not merely a power. The Governor has discretion as to timing within that constraint, but cannot simply refuse to summon. The phrase "at such time and place as he thinks fit" grants procedural flexibility but is not a charter for indefinite delay. Courts have held that unreasonable delay in acting on a Cabinet summons request is itself a constitutional violation.

The six-month rule runs from the last sitting in the previous session to the date appointed for the first sitting of the next session. It is not measured between the last sitting of one session and the first sitting of the next — it is measured to the date appointed, meaning the notification of the session date must be issued in time. A failure to observe the six-month gap, if challenged, could affect the constitutional validity of executive acts that required but did not receive legislative authorisation during the gap period.

**Clause (2)(a) — prorogation**: Prorogation ends a session without dissolving the Assembly. Pending bills and resolutions lapse on prorogation unless rules provide otherwise. Prorogation is a formal executive act — it requires the Governor's action, but under the Article 163 principle, it must be on Cabinet advice. Prorogation without or against Cabinet advice is constitutionally impermissible unless the Governor can point to a specific constitutional provision authorising discretion in the circumstances.

**Clause (2)(b) — dissolution**: Dissolution ends the life of the Assembly and triggers a fresh election. It is the most consequential power in the article. Under the constitutional scheme, dissolution on the Cabinet's advice — typically when the Chief Minister advises dissolution after losing a confidence vote, or to seek a fresh mandate — is routine. But dissolution by the Governor without or against Cabinet advice, or in anticipation of a confidence vote that might have saved the existing government, is constitutionally contested. Bommai established that the floor of the House, not the Governor's assessment, is the correct test for a government's majority; dissolution before that test is taken is therefore not a neutral act.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's discussion of what became Article 174 reflected an unresolved tension between two models of gubernatorial power. Some framers — particularly those drawing on the colonial experience of provincial governors — argued that the Governor should have genuine control over the legislative calendar to act as a check on State Assemblies that might pass hasty or unconstitutional legislation. Others, committed to the Westminster parliamentary model, argued that the Governor was a constitutional head who should summon, prorogue, and dissolve only on Cabinet advice.

The final text reflects the parliamentary model: the formal powers are given to the Governor, but the constitutional architecture of Article 163 requires that they be exercised on ministerial advice. Dr. Ambedkar assured the Constituent Assembly that Governors would not become parallel political executives but would function as constitutional heads on the Westminster pattern, intervening independently only in situations of genuine constitutional failure. The subsequent history has substantially complicated that assurance, as the appointment of politically aligned Governors by Central governments has converted Article 174's formal powers into recurring instruments of inter-governmental confrontation.

The six-month rule was modelled on the Government of India Act 1935 and reflected the framers' determination that no government could simply govern indefinitely without returning to the legislature. The British parliamentary model — which relied on constitutional convention rather than a hard six-month rule — was adapted into a more explicit constitutional requirement, recognising that constitutional conventions could not be relied upon to the same extent in India's newly democratic polity.

## Governor-CM standoffs: the pattern of disputes

The recurring pattern in Article 174 disputes involves a State government of one political party facing a Governor appointed by a Central government of another party. The Governor uses the formal powers of Article 174 — delaying or refusing to summon sessions, advancing or postponing session dates, declining to communicate Cabinet decisions to the House — as instruments of political pressure. The elected government responds by seeking judicial intervention.

Several specific forms of Article 174 dispute have emerged as recurring constitutional flashpoints. The first is the refusal to summon a session: a Governor who declines to act on Cabinet advice to call a session within the constitutional six-month window, or who imposes conditions or seeks reasons before agreeing to summon, is in direct confrontation with the constitutional scheme. The second is the unilateral advancement or postponement of session dates: a Governor who, without Cabinet advice, announces a different session date than the one the government has recommended, effectively takes control of the legislative calendar away from the elected executive. The third is prorogation without or against Cabinet advice: ending a session early, before the government's legislative programme is complete, or proroguing to prevent an inconvenient confidence vote.

Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Punjab have been the most active sites of this third generation of Article 174 disputes in the 2020s. In Tamil Nadu, the Governor's repeated refusals to act on Cabinet advice — across multiple Bills, administrative appointments, and session-related decisions — led to the Supreme Court's landmark 2025 ruling that the Governor is bound by Cabinet advice on legislative matters and cannot substitute independent political judgments for those of the elected government. In Kerala, similar confrontations over session dates and Bills persisted across the same period. In Punjab, the Governor's attempt to unilaterally fix the date of the budget session and his refusal to summon a special session on Cabinet advice led to Supreme Court intervention in 2023.

## Key cases

**S R Bommai v Union of India AIR 1994 SC 1918** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench's landmark ruling on Article 356 addressed dissolution of State Assemblies as a fundamental component of federal protection. The Court held that a State government's majority must be tested on the floor of the House and that the Governor cannot form an independent assessment about whether a ministry commands confidence. Dissolving the Assembly before allowing a floor test — when a floor test was feasible — renders the subsequent President's Rule proclamation constitutionally suspect. The judgment established the floor test as the only constitutionally legitimate mechanism for determining majority, directly bearing on when dissolution under Article 174(2)(b) is permissible.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016) 8 SCC 1** — A five-judge Constitution Bench held that the Governor's power under Article 174(1) to summon the Assembly cannot be exercised unilaterally when a Council of Ministers enjoying majority confidence is in office. The Arunachal Pradesh Governor had advanced the date of the Assembly session — from the date the Cabinet had scheduled it — in order to enable the opposition's no-confidence motion against the Speaker to proceed. The Court struck down the advance summoning as unconstitutional, set aside the subsequent imposition of President's Rule, and reinstated the duly elected government. Nabam Rebia is the definitive authority on the relationship between Article 174 and the Article 163 ministerial advice principle, and it settled that Article 163(2)'s finality clause does not immunise patently unconstitutional gubernatorial conduct.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court struck down the imposition of President's Rule in Bihar following the 2005 elections, holding that the Governor's recommendation was itself based on unconstitutional conduct — specifically on intelligence about possible defections and horse-trading rather than on any floor test or constitutional event. The Governor's conduct in the period preceding the proclamation — including actions relating to the calling and timing of Assembly sessions — was scrutinised and found wanting. The judgment reinforced that Article 174's powers cannot be manipulated to prevent a newly elected Assembly from ever sitting.

**State of Punjab v Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab (2023)** — The Supreme Court held that the Governor of Punjab could not indefinitely withhold summoning of a special session that the Cabinet had advised him to call. The Court held that unreasonable delay by a Governor in responding to a Cabinet request to summon the Assembly is itself a violation of the constitutional scheme — not merely an irregularity. The Court issued directions to the Governor to act on the Cabinet's advice on the session question, representing one of the clearest instances of judicial mandamus directed at a Governor in relation to Article 174 powers.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023) 2 SCC 73** — While primarily addressed to the floor test question, the five-judge bench comprehensively restated the principle that the Governor's constitutional role in relation to the Assembly is triggered by specific constitutional events, not by independent political assessments. The Governor of Maharashtra's direction of an unsought floor test — based on defectors' letters rather than objective constitutional material — engaged the same constitutional framework as Article 174's summoning power: the Governor cannot deploy control over legislative procedure to tilt the political balance in favour of one party.

**Tamil Nadu Governor v State of Tamil Nadu (2025)** — The Supreme Court's most recent and comprehensive ruling on the Governor's constitutional role at the State level confirmed that the Governor of Tamil Nadu had acted unconstitutionally in withholding assent to Bills passed by the Assembly and in refusing to act on Cabinet advice on multiple legislative and administrative matters. The Court held that the Governor is a constitutional functionary bound by Cabinet advice on all but a narrow range of genuinely discretionary decisions, and that this principle applies with full force to the exercise of the summons, prorogation, and dissolution powers under Article 174.

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held that the President and Governor are constitutional heads who must act on the aid and advice of their respective Councils of Ministers in all but a handful of constitutionally specified discretionary situations. The Court's foundational reading of the Westminster parliamentary convention — that the constitutional head acts on elected advice — underpins the entire subsequent jurisprudence on Article 174 and establishes that the formal powers of the Governor are channelled through the Cabinet except where a specific constitutional provision confers discretion.

## Current significance

Article 174 has become one of the most litigated constitutional provisions governing Centre-State relations in the contemporary period. The pattern of disputes reflects the structural incentive for Governors appointed by one political combination at the Centre to use procedural control over the legislative calendar against State governments of a different political persuasion. The Supreme Court's jurisprudence from Nabam Rebia through Subhash Desai, Punjab v Principal Secretary, and the Tamil Nadu Governor case has progressively tightened the constitutional limits on this practice, but the litigation continues because each new confrontation raises new factual variations on the same constitutional question.

The six-month gap rule, once regarded as a formality, has gained fresh constitutional significance. If a Governor refuses to summon a session within the six-month window — whether by ignoring Cabinet advice or by placing conditions on the summoning — the constitutional floor of legislative accountability is breached. Courts have indicated they will not treat this as a purely political dispute unsuitable for judicial resolution: the six-month rule is a hard constitutional requirement, and its breach may affect the validity of executive action taken during the gap.

The interaction between Article 174 and the anti-defection provisions of the Tenth Schedule has produced a specific category of dispute in which Governors have been accused of timing sessions to advantage defecting MLAs over the parent party's direction — either by summoning sessions to enable defectors to demonstrate their numbers before disqualification proceedings are complete, or by delaying sessions to prevent confidence votes that might have resolved factional disputes. The constitutional resolution of these disputes continues to develop at the intersection of Article 174, Article 163, and the Tenth Schedule, with the Supreme Court increasingly willing to issue specific directions to Governors who exceed constitutional limits.
