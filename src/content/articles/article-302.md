## Why it matters

Article 302 is Parliament's express legislative power to derogate from the common-market guarantee in Article 301. Without it, Article 301's freedom would be absolute at the national level and Parliament would be powerless to regulate cross-border trade for public-interest reasons — food security, strategic resource management, crisis response, or national economic planning. Article 302 supplies that power, but links it to a "public interest" standard that courts have treated as a substantive requirement rather than a mere formality. The framers deliberately chose to vest this restriction power in Parliament rather than in State legislatures, reflecting the judgment that restrictions on the unified national market should be imposed by the nationally accountable legislature, not by individual States pursuing parochial interests.

Critically, Article 302 must be read together with Article 303(1), which prohibits Parliament from using its trade-and-commerce legislative powers to give preference to one state over another or to discriminate between states. The freedom Article 302 confers on Parliament is therefore subject to an internal equality-of-states constraint: restrictions must apply uniformly or be justified under the Article 303(2) scarcity-of-goods exception. This tension between parliamentary power and inter-state equality defines the working relationship between Articles 302 and 303 and prevents Article 302 from becoming a device by which Parliament could informally privilege economically powerful States.

The conceptual backdrop of Article 302 is the Constituent Assembly's aspiration — drawing partly on the Australian constitutional experience and partly on the framers' diagnosis of why pre-independence India's internal economy was fragmented — to make India a single economic unit from the moment of independence. Article 301 provided the constitutional guarantee; Article 302 provided the safety valve. Together, they were designed to place the management of any necessary departures from free internal trade in the hands of the democratically accountable national Parliament, subject to the equality discipline that prevented Parliament from favouring one region over another.

The relationship between Article 302 and the Essential Commodities Act has generated significant litigation. The Act empowers the Central Government to control production, supply, distribution, and price of essential commodities. Courts have analysed whether each exercise of that power — a procurement order, a movement restriction, a price control — is "required in the public interest" within Article 302's standard. The judicial record shows that courts have been deferential to Parliament's economic judgment in crisis conditions (drought, supply disruption, price spikes) but have intervened where restrictions served protectionist rather than public-interest purposes.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 302 is deliberately open-ended. "Trade, commerce or intercourse" tracks the language of Article 301 — both provisions share the same operative phrase — and courts have interpreted the phrase expansively to include not just the physical movement of goods but the entire commercial transaction including its financial, contractual, and regulatory dimensions. A restriction on the payment terms for inter-state purchases is as much a restriction on "trade and commerce" as a physical checkpoint on a highway. This expansive reading ensures that regulatory creativity cannot defeat the constitutional guarantee by choosing formal structures (licensing conditions, registration requirements, payment restrictions) rather than direct prohibitions.

The "public interest" standard in Article 302 is the key constitutional test. Courts distinguish between restrictions that genuinely serve a national public interest — managing scarcity, protecting public health, controlling strategic resources — and restrictions that are protectionist in effect, serving the economic interest of a particular region or industry group at the expense of the integrated market. Parliament's judgment about what is in the public interest is accorded deference, but it is not immune from judicial scrutiny. A court will look at whether there is a discernible rational nexus between the restriction and the claimed public purpose, whether the means chosen are proportionate, and whether the restriction has been uniformly applied or discriminates between regions.

Article 302 operates in a legislative hierarchy. Parliament uses it when legislating under entries in the Union List or the Concurrent List that relate to trade and commerce: for instance, inter-state trade in commodities (Entry 42, Union List), development of industries (Entry 52), or specific commodities like petroleum or atomic energy. State legislatures cannot invoke Article 302; their equivalent provision is Article 304(b), which requires the prior sanction of the President and permits only restrictions that are "reasonable." The difference in treatment — Parliament can restrict with the single test of "public interest," States face an additional procedural hurdle and a stricter "reasonable restriction" standard — reflects the constitutional choice to make inter-state trade management primarily a central responsibility.

## Key cases

**Atiabari Tea Company Ltd. v. State of Assam (1961) AIR 1961 SC 232** — The Supreme Court's first major ruling under Part XIII, establishing the foundational principle that only laws which directly and immediately restrict the free flow of trade and commerce attract Article 301 scrutiny (and therefore require justification under Article 302 or 304). A transit levy on tea moving through Assam was held to directly impede commerce. The "direct and immediate" test shaped the entire subsequent jurisprudence on when a law needs Part XIII justification.

**Automobile Transport (Rajasthan) Ltd. v. State of Rajasthan (1962) AIR 1962 SC 1406** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench introduced the compensatory-tax doctrine: a levy that is regulatory and compensatory in character — charging for services rendered to the trade — does not restrict trade and commerce within Article 301 and therefore does not require justification under Article 302. This doctrine was the dominant framework for seventy years before being reconsidered.

**State of Mysore v. H. Sanjeeviah (1967)** — The Court examined a State law restricting the movement of sandalwood out of Mysore, holding that Article 302's public interest standard requires more than a bald legislative assertion of public purpose. The restriction must serve a genuine, demonstrable national or broader public interest, not merely a State-level commercial interest dressed in public-interest language.

**Gujarat Electricity Board v. Shantilal R. Desai (1969)** — The Court upheld a Parliamentary enactment conferring exclusive rights on State electricity boards to transmit and supply electricity, holding this was a valid exercise of the Article 302 power in the public interest — national economic planning and equitable distribution of electricity were sufficient public-interest justifications even though the restriction operated as a de facto monopoly within each State.

**BSNL v. Telecom Regulatory Authority of India (2001)** — In the context of telecommunications regulation, the Court considered whether Parliament's licensing regime under the Telegraph Act constituted a restriction on "trade and commerce" in communications services. The ruling clarified that Article 302 applies to services as much as to the movement of goods, and that Parliament's power to restrict communication services is available as long as the public interest test is satisfied.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd. v. State of Haryana (2017) 12 SCC 1** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench overhauled the compensatory-tax doctrine from Automobile Transport, holding that the characterisation of a levy as "compensatory" could no longer by itself save it from Part XIII scrutiny. Any levy that directly impedes trade must satisfy the proportionality and public-interest tests applicable under Article 302 or 304. This restructuring of the doctrine has significant implications for the many State levies that had previously sheltered under the compensatory-tax shield.

## Current significance

Article 302's practical importance today is most visible in Parliament's powers over essential commodities and in the statutory framework governing inter-state movement of agricultural produce. The Essential Commodities (Amendment) Act 2020 — which removed several agricultural commodities from the list of controlled commodities — was partly justified by reference to the Article 301 freedom and the need to reduce Article 302-type restrictions on agricultural trade. Its subsequent partial retreat following the repeal of the three farm laws demonstrates how contested the balance between Article 302 Parliamentary power and Article 301 freedom remains in the agricultural economy.

With the GST Council now coordinating uniform indirect taxation for most goods and services, disputes under Article 302 increasingly arise in sectors excluded from GST — petroleum, alcohol, and real estate — where Parliament and States retain fragmented regulatory regimes with significant movement-control implications. Entry taxes on petroleum products, excise structures for alcohol, and stamp duty on property transactions all sit at the intersection of trade regulation and fiscal federalism in ways that Article 302 jurisprudence must eventually address.

The Jindal Stainless overruling has created doctrinal uncertainty that courts are still working through. State levies that were previously insulated from Article 301 challenge as "compensatory" now face a proportionality scrutiny that requires genuine public-interest justification. This makes Article 302 a live, consequential provision rather than an archival curiosity — Parliament's power to restrict trade, and the limits of that power set by Articles 301 and 303, will continue to be tested as India's internal market continues to integrate and as regulatory conflicts between the Centre and States multiply.
