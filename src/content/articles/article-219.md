## Why it matters

Article 219 is the moment at which a judicial appointment becomes a judicial
office. A person who has been recommended by the collegium and appointed by the
President's warrant is not yet a judge for the purposes of exercising
constitutional jurisdiction until they have made and subscribed the oath or
affirmation before the Governor. The article marks the precise threshold between
appointment and assumption of office.

The oath's content — allegiance to the Constitution, commitment to India's
sovereignty and integrity, and performance of duties without fear, favour,
affection, or ill-will — is not merely ceremonial. Courts have treated the oath
as the foundational statement of the institutional obligations that attach to
judicial office. "Without fear, favour, affection, or ill-will" is the formula
that distinguishes a judge from an appointee who might otherwise be expected to
remain loyal to those who appointed them.

The Governor's role as administering authority reflects the High Court's mixed
constitutional character. High Courts are constitutionally State institutions
— they sit within state territory, exercise jurisdiction over state subjects,
and interact daily with state governments — yet their judges are centrally
appointed on the recommendation of a national collegium. The oath before the
Governor acknowledges the State dimension of the institution while the
appointment process under Article 217 reflects its national character.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "make and subscribe" requires two steps: oral administration of the
oath (or affirmation, for those who object to swearing) and written subscription
— the judge signing the prescribed form. Both steps are required. The signed
form is preserved as a permanent record, so if the validity of the oath
ceremony is ever disputed, the written record serves as conclusive evidence.

The Third Schedule form for High Court judges commits them to: bearing true
faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India as by law established;
upholding the sovereignty and integrity of India; and duly and faithfully, to
the best of their ability, knowledge, and judgment performing the duties of
their office without fear, favour, affection, or ill-will. The form is
prescribed in the Constitution itself, so Parliament cannot alter it without a
constitutional amendment.

Judges transferred from one High Court to another under Article 222 do not
take a fresh oath at the transferred court. Courts have confirmed that the oath
is taken to the judicial office generally and to the Constitution, not to any
particular court or state. The original oath continues to govern all judicial
functions regardless of which High Court the judge serves in.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court held that the
Governor's role in administering oaths under Article 219 does not involve
independent constitutional discretion. The Governor acts on the aid and advice
of the Council of Ministers in performing constitutional functions of this
character. The obligation created by the oath, however, runs to the Constitution
rather than to any political authority — a distinction the Court treated as
fundamental to the nature of judicial office.

**K Veeraswami v Union of India (1991)** — While primarily about whether a
judge requires presidential sanction for criminal prosecution, the Court noted
that the oath taken under Article 219 creates a solemn constitutional obligation
to act without fear or favour. Breach of that obligation through corrupt conduct
was treated as a violation of the judge's constitutional undertaking rather than
merely a criminal act, reinforcing the constitutional character of the oath.

**In Re: Special Reference No. 1 of 1998 (Third Judges Case)** — The Court's
extensive analysis of the constitutional framework for judicial appointments
treated the oath requirement as one element of a system designed to make judges
institutionally independent of the appointing authorities. The constitutional
character of the oath was contrasted with the position of executive officers
whose oath of office does not carry the same institutional independence
guarantee.

## Current significance

Article 219's pledge to act without fear, favour, affection, or ill-will has
no dedicated enforcement mechanism short of the impeachment process under
Article 218. This gap between constitutional commitment and institutional
enforcement is a recurring theme in judicial accountability discourse. The
In-House Procedure and the collegium's informal supervisory role over the
conduct of High Court judges do not derive from Article 219 and have no
statutory grounding.

Debates about judicial recusal and conflict-of-interest disclosure have drawn
increasing attention to Article 219. Some constitutional scholars argue that
"without fear, favour, affection, or ill-will" creates an affirmative duty to
disclose personal or financial relationships that might give rise to actual or
apparent bias — a duty currently enforced only through informal convention and
the general law of natural justice.

The publicly broadcast oath ceremony — now common across many High Courts — has
become a transparency measure that reinforces the constitutional significance
of the moment of assumption of office. The practice of live-streaming the
ceremony reflects a broader shift towards judicial transparency, treating the
oath not merely as an internal administrative formality but as a public
constitutional act.

The parallel provision for Supreme Court judges is Article 124(6). The
structural difference — Supreme Court judges swear before the President, High
Court judges before the Governor — is a precise constitutional expression of
the two-tier judicial structure: a national apex court whose judges swear before
the national head of state, and State High Courts whose judges swear before the
constitutional head of each State.
