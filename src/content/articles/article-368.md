## Why it matters

Article 368 defines how constitutional change happens — and it became the battleground for the most fundamental question in Indian constitutional law: can a parliamentary majority use the amending power to eliminate the constitutional limits on parliamentary power itself? Can Parliament use Article 368 to abolish judicial review, eliminate fundamental rights, or destroy the federal structure? The Constitution's answer, as developed through a series of landmark cases, is no — because the amending power is a constituted power, not a constituent power, and cannot exceed the constitutional design that created it.

This question was not merely theoretical. Between 1951 and 1976, Parliament and the executive clashed repeatedly with the Supreme Court over property rights and land reform. Each time the Court struck down a law as violating fundamental rights, Parliament responded with a constitutional amendment. By 1976, Parliament had convinced itself that its amending power was unlimited — and the Forty-second Amendment, passed during the Emergency, declared that no amendment of the Constitution could be called in question in any court on any ground, and that Parliament's constituent power had no limitations whatsoever. The Court's response to this assertion — delivered in Kesavananda Bharati (1973), before the Forty-second Amendment but anticipating the direction of travel, and confirmed in Minerva Mills (1980) after it — is the basic structure doctrine: the proposition that certain features of the Constitution are so fundamental that they cannot be abrogated even by a constitutional amendment.

Article 368's significance is therefore not only procedural. The procedures it specifies — the special majority requirement, the State ratification requirement for certain provisions — are the formal requirements for valid amendment. But above those formal requirements sits the substantive limit imposed by the basic structure doctrine, which operates as a judicially enforced substantive constraint on what any amendment may do. India is one of the very few countries where a court has successfully claimed and exercised the power to strike down formal constitutional amendments on substantive grounds.

The tension between democratic legitimacy (Parliament elected by the people should be able to change any rule) and constitutional entrenchment (some things should not be changeable by bare majority, or at all) is the deepest structural problem in constitutional design. India has resolved it through the basic structure doctrine — a judicial answer that has been politically controversial but constitutionally stable for over fifty years.

## Reading the provision

**The amendment procedure**: Article 368(2) requires that a constitutional amendment Bill be passed in each House by (a) a majority of total membership of the House (an absolute majority — not just a majority of those present and voting) and (b) a majority of not less than two-thirds of members present and voting. Both requirements must be satisfied. This is a more demanding threshold than ordinary legislation, which requires only a simple majority of members present and voting, but less demanding than the US Congress's two-thirds requirement in each house for proposing amendments.

**State ratification**: The proviso to clause (2) identifies five categories of amendments that additionally require ratification by the legislatures of at least half the States. These categories are designed to protect federal arrangements: the electoral college for the President (Articles 54, 55), the distribution of executive power (Articles 73, 162), the Supreme Court and High Courts (Chapter IV Part V, Chapter V Part VI), the distribution of legislative powers (Chapter I Part XI), the Seventh Schedule's three lists, the representation of States in Parliament, and Article 368 itself. Any amendment touching these provisions must not only pass Parliament with the special majority but must also be ratified by the State legislatures. This is India's substitute for the equal-representation-in-Senate safeguard that protects small States in the American amendment procedure.

**Article 13 and Article 368(3)**: Article 13 declares that any law inconsistent with fundamental rights is void to the extent of the inconsistency. If constitutional amendments were "laws" for the purposes of Article 13, they could be struck down for violating fundamental rights. The twenty-fourth Amendment explicitly added clause (3) to Article 368, declaring that Article 13 does not apply to amendments under Article 368. The constitutional purpose was to restore Parliament's ability to amend fundamental rights — overruling the Supreme Court's 1967 Golaknath holding that Parliament had no power to take away or abridge fundamental rights. But the basic structure doctrine, established in Kesavananda Bharati six years later, imposed a different limit: amendments that destroy the basic structure are invalid not because they violate Article 13 but because they exceed Parliament's constituent power as derived from Article 368 itself.

**Clauses (4) and (5) — the Forty-second Amendment's failed ouster**: The forty-second Amendment added clauses (4) and (5) during the Emergency. Clause (4) declared that no amendment could be called in question in any court on any ground. Clause (5) declared that Parliament's amending power had no limitations whatsoever. These clauses were a direct legislative response to the basic structure doctrine in Kesavananda Bharati — Parliament attempting to reverse the Supreme Court's position by constitutional amendment. In Minerva Mills (1980), the Supreme Court struck down both clauses as unconstitutional, holding that a limited power cannot, by using itself, convert itself into an unlimited power. The amending power exists within the Constitution, not above it; it cannot expand itself to abolish the limits that the Constitution places on it. The text of clauses (4) and (5) therefore remains in the Constitution but has been held to be of no legal effect.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly had extensive debates about the amending procedure. One camp, represented by K. T. Shah, argued for a very rigid constitution — certain fundamental provisions should not be amendable at all. Another camp, represented by Dr. Ambedkar, argued for constitutional flexibility: a rigid, difficult-to-amend constitution would become unworkable in a rapidly changing society and would force change through extra-constitutional means rather than through orderly democratic process.

Dr. Ambedkar explicitly rejected the idea of making the Constitution unamendable: "If I may say so, if you make anything rigid and permanent, you stop a nation's growth, you stop a nation's development... People who are living cannot bind people who are yet to be born. People who are living must be free to change the constitution to suit their needs." The framers' choice of a flexible but procedurally demanding amendment process — requiring special parliamentary majorities and, for federal matters, State ratification — was a conscious compromise between rigidity and flexibility.

What neither Ambedkar nor any other framer anticipated was that the courts would develop a doctrine of unamendable constitutional identity. The basic structure doctrine, which emerged from the conflict between Parliament's amending power and the Supreme Court's judicial review function, was not foreseen in the Constituent Assembly debates. It is a judicial construction — both the most consequential and the most controversial act of judicial creativity in Indian constitutional history.

## The evolution from Shankari Prasad to Kesavananda

**Shankari Prasad v Union of India (1951)**: Parliament passed the First Amendment, placing certain land reform laws in the Ninth Schedule and protecting them from fundamental rights challenge under Article 13. The Supreme Court upheld the amendment, holding that Parliament's amending power under Article 368 was not subject to the fundamental rights chapter, and that "law" in Article 13 meant ordinary legislation, not constitutional amendments.

**Golaknath v State of Punjab (1967)**: The Supreme Court reversed course by a six-to-five majority. The Court held that constitutional amendments were "laws" within Article 13 and that Parliament had no power to take away or abridge fundamental rights under Part III. The immediate consequence was that the 24th, 25th, and 26th Amendments could all be challenged as unconstitutional. Parliament responded by enacting all three of those amendments specifically to overrule Golaknath.

**Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973)**: A thirteen-judge bench — the largest ever constituted in India — decided by the narrowest of margins (seven to six) that Parliament's amending power under Article 368 is limited by the "basic structure" of the Constitution. The Court did not accept the Golaknath position that fundamental rights could not be amended; it accepted that Parliament could amend fundamental rights. But it held that Parliament could not use the amending power to destroy the Constitution's basic structure — its essential identity. What constitutes basic structure was not exhaustively defined; the identification of further basic structure elements was left to be worked out case by case. The ratio that emerged from seven separate opinions in a case that runs to over 700 pages was: Parliament's amending power extends to every provision including fundamental rights, but an amendment that destroys or abrogates the basic structure is beyond the amending power and is void.

**Minerva Mills v Union of India (1980)**: The Forty-second Amendment's direct attack on the basic structure doctrine was struck down. Clauses (4) and (5) of Article 368, added by that amendment, were held unconstitutional because a limited amending power cannot enlarge itself to unlimited. The balance between fundamental rights and directive principles — the former justiciable, the latter non-justiciable — was held to be itself a basic structure element; an amendment that gave absolute primacy to directive principles over fundamental rights disturbed that balance and violated the basic structure.

## Key cases

**Shankari Prasad Singh Deo v Union of India (1951)** — Parliament's first substantive amendment (the First Amendment) was challenged on the ground that it could not abridge fundamental rights guaranteed in Part III. The Supreme Court upheld it, holding that "law" in Article 13 means ordinary statutory law, not constitutional amendments. This set the permissive framework for the first decade and a half of constitutional amendment, during which Parliament freely amended property rights protections without significant judicial check.

**Golaknath v State of Punjab (1967)** — A five-to-six-judge majority (Chief Justice Subba Rao writing for the majority) held that Parliament has no power to take away or abridge fundamental rights and that constitutional amendments are "laws" subject to Article 13. The ruling applied prospectively — the existing amendments stood, but no future amendment could diminish fundamental rights. The decision produced a constitutional crisis: it meant that Parliament could not pass the land reform and nationalisation legislation that its agrarian economic agenda required. The 24th, 25th, and 26th Amendments were passed specifically to reverse Golaknath. The case illustrates the limits of using fundamental rights doctrine as an absolute limit on parliamentary power — a democracy that cannot regulate property rights in the name of land reform faces a legitimacy crisis of its own.

**Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973)** — The foundational case of Indian constitutional law. The thirteen-judge bench sat for sixty-eight days between October 1972 and March 1973. The judgment ran to over 700 pages and produced six majority opinions and five dissenting opinions, making it the most complex case in Indian judicial history. The seven-justice majority established the basic structure doctrine: Parliament's amending power is a constituent power, derived from and therefore limited by the Constitution itself; it can change the form of any provision, but it cannot destroy the Constitution's essential identity. The doctrine has been applied in hundreds of subsequent cases and remains the outer boundary on constitutional amendment power. Chief Justice S M Sikri's summary, signed by six justices, listed the following as basic structure elements: supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, secular character, separation of powers, and federal character. Subsequent cases have added others.

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain (1975)** — The Thirty-ninth Amendment, passed during the Emergency, placed the Prime Minister's election in a special category protected from judicial review. Justice Mathew held, applying the basic structure doctrine, that an amendment that places the election of the Prime Minister beyond judicial scrutiny violates the basic structure because free and fair elections and the rule of law are essential features of the constitutional design. The amendment was struck down within months of the Emergency ending, demonstrating that the basic structure doctrine was judicially workable.

**Minerva Mills Ltd v Union of India (1980)** — After the Emergency ended, the Supreme Court applied the basic structure doctrine to strike down the two most significant Emergency-era constitutional changes. Clauses (4) and (5) of Article 368 — which had declared the amending power unlimited and insulated all amendments from judicial review — were struck down as unconstitutional. The Court held that judicial review, including the power to review constitutional amendments, is itself a basic structure element: Parliament cannot remove the Court's power to review its own amendments, because doing so would give Parliament unlimited power to reshape the Constitution at will, destroying the constitutional framework that limits its authority.

**S R Bommai v Union of India (1994)** — While primarily an Article 356 case, the nine-judge bench's holding that federalism is part of the basic structure has significant implications for Article 368. It means that constitutional amendments that significantly diminish the federal structure — for instance, by effectively abolishing State governments or removing the State List from the Seventh Schedule — would violate the basic structure and be invalid, even if passed with the required special majority.

**I R Coelho v State of Tamil Nadu (2007)** — The nine-judge bench unanimously held that laws placed in the Ninth Schedule after 24 April 1973 (the date of the Kesavananda judgment) can be reviewed if they violate the rights in Part III that form part of the basic structure. The Court declined to allow the Ninth Schedule — created specifically to shield land reform and other legislation from fundamental rights challenge — to function as a permanent immunity zone for laws that would otherwise be unconstitutional. This holding closed the legislative route to avoiding the basic structure doctrine by the simple expedient of placing laws in the Ninth Schedule.

**Nagaraj v Union of India (2006) and Jarnail Singh v Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018)** — A series of cases on reservations in promotions under Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) engaged the question of whether the special conditions imposed on reservations in promotions — the requirement of quantifiable data on backwardness and the creamy layer principle — are themselves basic structure requirements. The Court held that the equality code is part of the basic structure, and that an amendment that removes the protection of the equality code would be invalid.

## Current significance

The basic structure doctrine continues to operate as the supreme constraint on constitutional change. Every major proposed constitutional amendment — including periodic proposals to give Parliament more power to override Supreme Court decisions on judicial appointments, to restrict the scope of fundamental rights in the name of economic development, or to restructure the federal arrangement — is evaluated against the basic structure question: does this change something so fundamental that it cannot be changed by amendment?

The most active current area of basic structure debate involves judicial appointments. After the Supreme Court struck down the NJAC (National Judicial Appointments Commission) in 2015 as violating the independence of the judiciary — itself a basic structure element — the Government proposed various alternative appointment mechanisms. The constitutional question is whether any appointment mechanism that gives the executive a dominant or decisive role in selecting Supreme Court judges can survive basic structure scrutiny, since judicial independence is unambiguously a basic structure element.

The scope of the basic structure doctrine itself remains contested. Its critics argue that it allows unelected judges to entrench their own institutional position and prevent constitutional change that democratic majorities want. Its defenders argue that it is the only mechanism available to prevent a parliamentary majority from dismantling the constitutional framework — including the rights and structures that protect minorities against majority power. This debate is not merely academic: it is the central constitutional-political argument about the appropriate distribution of power between Parliament and the Supreme Court.

The relationship between Article 368's formal amendment procedure and the basic structure doctrine creates a distinction between constitutionally valid amendments (those that comply with formal requirements and do not destroy basic structure) and formally valid but substantively unconstitutional amendments (those that comply with formal requirements but violate the basic structure). India is one of very few countries where this distinction is judicially enforced — where courts will strike down not just violations of the formal amendment procedure but substantive violations of the constitutional identity, even by formally perfect amendments.
