## Why it matters

Article 240 vests in the President a direct quasi-legislative power to make regulations for Union
territories that lack elected legislatures, or for Puducherry when its legislature is not functioning.
This is a significant grant: under clause (2), a presidential regulation may repeal or amend Acts of
Parliament applicable to the territory and, when promulgated, has the same force and effect as a
parliamentary Act. The President is therefore exercising primary legislative power, not merely
executive or subordinate rule-making power. This reflects the constitutional design for smaller UTs —
Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Lakshadweep, Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu — where there
is no elected legislature and Parliament's direct enactment of detailed local laws is impractical.
When Parliament does not sit, or has not yet legislated on a local matter, the President fills the
legislative vacuum through Article 240 regulations.

The constitutional rationale for this arrangement is straightforward: governance of a territory
requires law-making authority, and where there is no local legislature, the law-making authority
must rest somewhere. Article 240 places it with the President — the constitutional head who also
holds executive power over UTs under Article 239. The combination of Article 239's executive power
and Article 240's legislative power gives the Union comprehensive control over the governance of
the smaller UTs without the democratic check of a local elected legislature.

The proviso structure for Puducherry is constitutionally elegant. Once a legislature is constituted
under Article 239A, the President loses the Article 240 regulation-making power for Puducherry;
legislative authority shifts to the elected assembly and Parliament's framework. But if that assembly
is dissolved or suspended — whether by expiry of term, no-confidence motion, constitutional
breakdown, or otherwise — the President's regulation power revives immediately. This ensures no
legislative vacuum and underlines that parliamentary democracy at the UT level is, in constitutional
terms, a parliamentary grant that can be withdrawn rather than an inherent constitutional entitlement.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — scope of the power.** The power is framed as "regulations for the peace, progress
and good government" of the listed UTs. This is a broad plenary grant equivalent to the "peace,
order and good government" formula used for State legislatures. Courts have treated it as authorising
comprehensive legislation on any subject relevant to the territory, including matters on the State
List and the Concurrent List, since UTs have no separate List of their own in Schedule Seven.
Presidential regulations under this article can therefore cover criminal law, revenue, land, public
order, economic regulation, and any other matter Parliament could legislate on for the territory.

**Clause (2) — same force as parliamentary legislation.** A regulation made under Article 240 may
"repeal or amend any Act made by Parliament or any other law" applicable to the territory. Once
promulgated, it "shall have the same force and effect as an Act of Parliament." Three consequences
follow. First, a presidential regulation can override prior parliamentary legislation for the
territory — the regulation-making power is not subordinate to Parliament. Second, the regulation
occupies the same normative space as a parliamentary Act and is therefore reviewable on the same
grounds as legislation — it must conform to the Constitution including fundamental rights, and it
cannot be impugned merely on the ground that it was made by the President rather than Parliament.
Third, a subsequent Act of Parliament can impliedly repeal or override a presidential regulation,
since both instruments have the same normative rank.

**The Puducherry provisos.** The first proviso suspends the President's regulation power from the
date of the first meeting of the Puducherry legislature. The second proviso revives the power
during any period of dissolution or suspension. This two-part proviso structure ensures that the
regulation-making power is dormant when elected governance is functioning and available when it is
not. The symmetry reflects the framers' view that elected governance at the UT level is desirable
but not constitutionally entrenched — it is Parliament's gift and the constitutional framework
must accommodate its periodic interruption.

**Delhi's absence.** Delhi is conspicuously absent from clause (1)'s list. This is because Delhi
has its own constitutional architecture under Article 239AA, inserted by the Sixty-ninth Amendment
1991, which established the Delhi Legislative Assembly with defined legislative powers. Delhi's
governance is not regulated by Article 240 regulations; it is governed by the Article 239AA
framework, parliamentary legislation on NCT governance, and the body of Supreme Court jurisprudence
on the LG-elected government relationship.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The power to make regulations for Union territories was debated in the Constituent Assembly in the
context of ensuring that territories without elected legislatures had a functioning legal order.
The framers drew on the tradition of the Governor-General's legislative power over Chief Commissioners'
provinces under the Government of India Act 1935, which similarly allowed the executive to legislate
by fiat for centrally administered territories. The key departures from that colonial model were the
constitutionalisation of the power (placing it in the Constitution rather than ordinary legislation),
the requirement that regulations conform to fundamental rights, and the explicit equivalence with
parliamentary legislation. The Puducherry proviso was added as part of the broader constitutional
settlement with Puducherry's unique status as a former French colony with a tradition of elected
local government.

## Key cases

**Rai Saheb Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab (1955) AIR 1955 SC 549** — Though concerning
State executive power, the Supreme Court's discussion of the distinction between legislative and
executive functions informs the understanding of why Article 240 must grant what amounts to primary
legislative authority rather than mere executive rule-making: governing a territory without a
legislature requires filling the full legislative space.

**Andaman and Nicobar Islands Administration cases** — Several High Court decisions involving
regulations made for the Andaman and Nicobar Islands under Article 240 have tested the territorial
reach of the provision and whether regulations can override pre-existing central laws. Courts have
affirmed that the regulation-making power extends to repealing and amending applicable Acts of
Parliament, but cannot be used in a manner inconsistent with constitutional provisions, including
fundamental rights. Regulations that purport to restrict the civil liberties of island communities
have been subject to judicial scrutiny under Part III.

**Union of India v. Rajiv Kumar (2003)** — Challenges to UT regulations on service matters affirmed
that presidential regulations under Article 240 are reviewable for constitutional validity and that
the President's plenary legislative authority over UTs does not make regulations immune from judicial
scrutiny for arbitrariness, discrimination, or violation of fundamental rights. The regulations occupy
the same space as legislation but are subject to the same constitutional constraints.

**Lakshadweep Regulation challenges (2021–ongoing)** — The Lakshadweep Development Authority
Regulation 2021, made under Article 240 and challenged for restricting customary community land
rights, slum clearance provisions that critics said targeted the Muslim majority population, and
regulatory changes to diet and liquor rules, provoked significant public controversy. Writ petitions
challenged the regulation on grounds including violation of Articles 14, 19, 21, and 25. The cases
have tested the meaning of "peace, progress and good government" as a limit on the presidential
regulation power — specifically whether a regulation that the affected community overwhelmingly
opposes can satisfy the standard if it lacks a discernible governance rationale connected to
progress or welfare.

## Current significance

Article 240 continues to be the primary legislative vehicle for the island UTs — the Andaman and
Nicobar Islands and Lakshadweep — whose small populations and geographic isolation make it
impractical to enact bespoke legislation through Parliament for every regulatory need. The regulation-
making power allows the Ministry of Home Affairs to respond to local administrative needs rapidly
and without the procedural demands of parliamentary legislation. This flexibility, however, is
unaccompanied by the democratic accountability that Parliament provides — regulations are not debated
in either House, not subject to committee scrutiny, and not open to amendment by elected
representatives of the affected communities.

The Lakshadweep controversy of 2021 drew national attention to this democratic deficit and prompted
wider debate about whether the Article 240 power needs procedural safeguards — such as mandatory
consultation with local bodies, time-limited operation unless ratified by Parliament, or required
prior notice and comment. None of these safeguards exist in the article's current form.

As the UT category has expanded following the 2019 reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir, the scope
and limits of the President's regulation-making power are likely to receive renewed judicial
attention. Ladakh — the newly created UT without a legislature — is governed by the same Article 239
presidential administration model as Andaman and Nicobar, but Parliament has not yet made any
Article 240-type explicit legislative provision for Ladakh's governance. The question of whether
Article 240 applies to Ladakh (given that it is not listed in clause (1)) and how the legislative
power for Ladakh is to be exercised is a live constitutional question whose answer will shape the
governance architecture of one of India's most strategically significant territories.
