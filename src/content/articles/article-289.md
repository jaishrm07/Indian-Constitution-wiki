  289. Exemption of property and income of a State from Union taxation.—(1) The property and income of a State shall be exempt from Union taxation.

  (2) Nothing in clause (1) shall prevent the Union from imposing, or authorising the imposition of, any tax to such extent, if any, as Parliament may by law provide in respect of a trade or business of any kind carried on by, or on behalf of, the Government of a State, or any operations connected therewith, or any property used or occupied for the purposes of such trade or business, or any income accruing or arising in connection therewith.

## Why it matters

Article 289 is the State-side counterpart to Article 285. Together, these two provisions create a bilateral fiscal immunity: Union property cannot be taxed by States (Article 285); State property and income cannot be taxed by the Union (Article 289). This reciprocity is fundamental to federalism — neither level of government should be able to use its taxing power as an instrument of financial coercion against the other.

The article's commercial-activity exception in clause (2) reflects a different concern: States should not be able to run businesses in competition with private enterprise and then claim constitutional immunity from the same Union taxes that their competitors pay. The exception prevents the immunity from operating as a competitive advantage for State enterprises in commercial markets.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) is the general rule: the property and income of a State are exempt from Union taxation. This covers State government land and buildings, State treasury receipts, royalties on State natural resources, and all income earned in the exercise of governmental functions.

Clause (2) is the commercial activity exception. Parliament may enact legislation taxing "a trade or business of any kind carried on by, or on behalf of, the Government of a State" and the property used for it or the income arising from it. This is a permissive power: the Union can tax State commercial activity if Parliament enacts appropriate legislation, but without such legislation the immunity remains intact.

Clause (3) creates a counter-exception within clause (2). Parliament can declare a particular class of State business to be "incidental to the ordinary functions of Government" — removing it from the clause (2) taxable category and restoring the immunity. This three-layer structure gives Parliament the last word: it can decide what is governmental (immune), what is commercial (taxable), and draw the line between them.

## The governmental versus commercial distinction

The central interpretive challenge is identifying when a State's activity crosses from governmental to commercial. Early cases looked at whether only the government could perform the activity (raising armies, administering courts, levying taxes) versus whether private parties could do the same for profit. The modern test is more functional: does the State operate as a sovereign exercising public power or as a market participant competing on commercial terms?

State electricity boards, road transport corporations, and industrial ventures owned by States sit in the contested middle. They perform activities that private companies also perform, but they do so under statutory mandates, often at regulated prices, with public service obligations. Courts have generally held that where the activity is commercially structured and competes in the market, the clause (2) exception is potentially applicable, though the Union has not comprehensively legislated to exercise this power over State commercial entities.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India AIR 1963 SC 1241** — The Supreme Court's landmark judgment on fiscal federalism in Part XII remains the foundational analysis of the relationship between Union legislative supremacy and State fiscal autonomy. The Court's treatment of the structural limits on Union power over State property provides the context within which Article 289's immunity is understood.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India AIR 1978 SC 68** — The Court reaffirmed that fiscal immunities under Part XII are structural constitutional guarantees, not statutory privileges, and cannot be overridden by Union executive action short of legislation under the applicable exception clause. The judgment underlined that Article 289's immunity survives even when the Union and State are in political conflict.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India AIR 1977 SC 1361** — In the context of challenges to Union executive pressure on State governments, the Court confirmed that the immunity in Article 289 protects the minimum fiscal independence that a constitutional federal structure requires, and that the exception in clause (2) can only be activated by Parliament through law, not by administrative or executive action.

**Andhra Pradesh State Road Transport Corporation v. Income Tax Officer AIR 1964 SC 1486** — The Court examined whether income earned by a State road transport corporation was exempt from Union income tax under Article 289. It held that a statutory corporation, though fully State-owned and carrying out a public function, was a distinct legal person and its income was not "income of the State" within the article's immunity — a key precedent for the treatment of State PSUs under Union tax law.

## Interaction with income tax on State-owned companies

Where a State enterprise is incorporated as a company under the Companies Act — even with 100 per cent State equity — courts have consistently held that the company is a distinct legal person and its income is not income of the "State" for Article 289(1) purposes. The Income Tax Act 1961 therefore applies to State-owned companies in the same way it applies to private companies. The State government receives dividends from such companies, which in turn flow into the State's accounts and may attract the immunity at that stage. This distinction — between the income of the State entity and the income of a company owned by the State — is a recurring source of litigation between State enterprises and Union tax authorities.

## Current significance

As States increasingly monetise public assets — leasing government land for commercial development, forming infrastructure investment trusts, entering joint ventures for mineral exploitation — the question of which income streams retain the Article 289 immunity and which fall within the clause (2) commercial activity exception is actively contested. Power sector revenues, toll incomes from State-built roads, and returns from State finance corporations all raise the boundary question. The article also retains its federal dignity dimension: the immunity is a structural guarantee that the Union cannot deploy income tax policy as a fiscal weapon against State governments. This protection matters most when Union-State relations are strained, making Article 289 as much a political safeguard as a fiscal one.
