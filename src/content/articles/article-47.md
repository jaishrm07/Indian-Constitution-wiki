## Why it matters

Article 47 is the constitutional expression of the social-welfare strand that runs through the entire scheme of Part IV. It frames nutrition and public health not as incidental government concerns but as "primary duties" of the State — a phrase that elevates them above ordinary policy preferences and places them in the same register as the core functions of governance. The Constituent Assembly was acutely aware of the mass malnutrition, hunger, and epidemic disease that marked post-Partition India, and Article 47 was intended to constitutionalise the State's commitment to addressing those conditions rather than leaving them to executive discretion that could be abandoned in lean fiscal years.

The article contains two distinct mandates. The first — to raise levels of nutrition and the standard of living and to improve public health — is general and expansive. It has been the primary basis for the judicial development of a right to health and a right to food as dimensions of the Article 21 right to life with dignity. Courts have reasoned that life stripped of adequate nutrition and basic healthcare is not life in the constitutional sense. The second mandate — to endeavour to bring about prohibition of intoxicating drinks and drugs injurious to health — is more targeted and has generated a distinct line of cases about the scope of State liquor prohibition legislation and the permissible extent of restrictions on the right to trade in alcohol under Article 19(1)(g).

The interaction between Article 47 and Article 21 has been one of the most judicially productive relationships in the Constitution. Through this interaction the Supreme Court has carved out justiciable rights to emergency medical care, safe drinking water, sanitation, and a minimum nutritional floor. Each of these rights is non-textual in the sense that Article 21 mentions only "life" and "personal liberty," but the Court has consistently used Article 47's directive as evidence that the Constitution treats health and nutrition as constitutive elements of life that the State is obliged to protect.

The prohibition limb of Article 47 has had a long and contested career in Indian constitutional law. Gujarat has maintained prohibition since the State's formation in 1960. Bihar reintroduced it in 2016 under the Bihar Prohibition and Excise Act. Nagaland operates under the Nagaland Liquor Total Prohibition Act. Maharashtra maintained partial prohibition for decades and still has active prohibition zones. In each case the State relies substantially on Article 47 to defend these restrictions against challenges under Article 19(1)(g). Courts have upheld such laws as a class but have also imposed limits: where prohibition generates a black market causing more social harm than regulated consumption would cause, courts have scrutinised the policy more closely. The prohibition directive is not a blank check; it requires the State to demonstrate that its chosen approach actually serves the constitutional objective of improving public health.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 47 contains four key elements: the "primary duties" formulation, the nutrition and standard-of-living mandate, the public health mandate, and the prohibition directive. Each has developed a distinct line of judicial interpretation.

**"Primary duties" — significance of the language.** The Constitution does not describe any other objective in Part IV as a "primary duty." The phrase distinguishes nutrition and public health from goals described elsewhere as things the State "shall endeavour" or "shall strive" to achieve. It signals that the Constituent Assembly regarded malnutrition and disease — the immediate material threats to Indian lives at independence — as the most pressing obligations the State needed to address. Courts have used this language to argue that when resources are allocated, the State may not simply defer nutrition and health programmes; they occupy a constitutionally preferred position among the directives.

**The nutrition and standard-of-living limb.** This limb is now read together with Article 21 to generate enforceable rights. The Supreme Court's approach in Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity (1996) established that the obligation in Article 47 to improve public health translates, through Article 21, into an enforceable duty to provide emergency medical care. The ICDS (Integrated Child Development Services) programme — providing nutrition, pre-school education, and health check-ups to children under six and to pregnant and lactating mothers — is supported by Article 47. The mid-day meal scheme for primary school children, now mandated by the Supreme Court, similarly draws constitutional authority from Article 47. Courts have used this limb to direct the State to ensure:

- ICDS centres are adequately staffed and supplied with nutrition supplements;
- Midday meals in government schools meet minimum calorie and protein standards;
- The public distribution system reaches the last mile in tribal and remote areas;
- Government food stocks are distributed rather than allowed to decay in godowns.

**The prohibition limb.** The State is directed to "endeavour to bring about prohibition" — a phrase identifying a constitutional aspiration rather than a strictly enforceable obligation to achieve total prohibition. Courts applying this limb examine whether the State's measure genuinely advances the objective of reducing harm from intoxicating substances. A law that prohibits consumption of alcohol generally qualifies; so does a law imposing strict licensing conditions, banning advertising, or restricting hours and locations of sale. Courts have confirmed that the prohibition directive extends beyond alcohol to any drug injurious to health, and has been invoked in the regulation of tobacco, gutka, and pan masala.

**"Except for medicinal purposes."** This constitutionally acknowledged exception has been used to uphold the supply of controlled substances through licensed pharmacies while maintaining broader restrictions. Courts have noted that the exception confirms the provision's public-health framing: the State is not morally condemning all use of such substances but is directing the elimination of recreational or habitual consumption that causes health harm.

**Relationship with Article 19(1)(g).** The right to carry on a trade or occupation under Article 19(1)(g) is available to persons engaged in the liquor trade. But courts have consistently held that trade in alcohol is not a fundamental right in the same sense as trade in ordinary commodities — the State's power to regulate or prohibit it is wide. Article 47 provides the constitutional public-purpose justification for restrictions, and the burden of showing that a restriction is unreasonable falls more heavily on the challenger when the restriction pursues an explicit constitutional objective. The Maharashtra Prohibition Act, the Gujarat Prohibition Act, and similar legislation across States have all been sustained against Article 19 challenges substantially on this basis.

## The right-to-health dimension

The judicial recognition of a right to health as a component of Article 21, grounded in Article 47, has produced a substantial body of doctrine. The right to health has been understood to include:

- The right to emergency medical care from State-run hospitals, regardless of the patient's ability to pay;
- The right to access essential medicines at affordable cost;
- The right to a safe workplace — protection from occupational health hazards including toxic chemicals, asbestos, and unsafe machinery;
- The right to safe drinking water and sanitation;
- The right to protection from the harmful effects of environmental pollution;
- The right to information about health risks from government-licensed products;
- The right to maternal and child health services including ante-natal care and institutional delivery;
- The right to protection from adulterated food and spurious drugs.

Each of these dimensions rests on the proposition that Article 47's mandate to improve public health is not discharged by merely enacting health legislation — the State must actively ensure that the conditions for a healthy life are available to people.

## State-level prohibition laws and their constitutional fate

The prohibition directive has been the constitutional anchor for a series of State prohibition regimes, each generating its own litigation history:

- **Gujarat**: Prohibition in force since 1960. The Gujarat Prohibition Act has been upheld repeatedly. Courts have distinguished between "dry" states where the constitutional objective is genuinely pursued and states where prohibition exists on paper but enforcement is absent. Gujarat's longstanding prohibition has been sustained because enforcement, though imperfect, is genuine.
- **Bihar**: The Bihar Prohibition and Excise Act, 2016 introduced total prohibition. It was challenged both on grounds that it violated Article 19(1)(g) and that it was too sweeping to be a reasonable restriction. Courts ultimately upheld the Act while directing the State to ensure that enforcement did not lead to overcrowding of jails through prosecution of petty offenders.
- **Nagaland**: The Nagaland Liquor Total Prohibition Act, 1989 was upheld by the courts as a valid exercise of State power, particularly noting that the State's legislative assembly had expressed a clear constitutional preference consistent with Article 47.
- **Maharashtra**: The Maharashtra Prohibition Act has operated with exceptions for tourists and certain classes of establishments, and its partial character has attracted the argument that a partial prohibition law cannot rely on Article 47 as fully as a total prohibition law. Courts have held that gradual progress toward the constitutional goal is constitutionally permissible.

The broader pattern from this litigation is that courts will sustain prohibition laws as a constitutional category under Article 47, but will examine whether the chosen method of regulation or prohibition is rationally connected to the constitutional objective of improving public health.

## Key cases

**State of Bombay v. F.N. Balsara (1951)** — One of the earliest cases to uphold prohibition legislation, the Supreme Court held that the Bombay Prohibition Act, 1949, was a valid exercise of State power supported by the constitutional directive in Article 47. The Court rejected the argument that the right to trade under Article 19(1)(g) was unreasonably restricted, holding that a State enacting liquor prohibition legislation pursues an explicit constitutional goal and is entitled to a wide margin of appreciation. The judgment established the pattern by which State dry-law legislation has been sheltered from constitutional challenge — the directive itself provides the public-purpose justification for restrictions on Fundamental Rights.

**Vincent v. Union of India (1987)** — The Supreme Court held that the State has a constitutional obligation under Article 47 to ensure that drugs and pharmaceutical products marketed in India are safe and efficacious, and that the licensing of injurious drugs undermines the directive's mandate. The Court treated Article 47 as imposing an active duty of vigilance on the State — not merely a passive obligation to avoid causing harm — and used it to direct stricter enforcement of drug regulation standards. The case established that the right to health has a regulatory dimension requiring the State to police private actors that threaten public health.

**Consumer Education and Research Centre v. Union of India (1995)** — In a landmark occupational health decision the Court read Article 47 alongside Article 21 to hold that the right to health and medical care is a fundamental right, and that the State's failure to enforce health and safety standards in asbestos-manufacturing industries violated this right. The Court directed that workers be provided medical examinations, safety equipment, and compensation for occupational diseases, grounding these directions in the constitutional mandate to improve public health. The judgment established that Article 47 extends to protection of workers from industrial health hazards and not only to the general population.

**Murli S. Deora v. Union of India (2001)** — The Supreme Court extended Article 47's public-health mandate to the regulation of smoking in public places. The Court held that compelling non-smokers to inhale tobacco smoke violates their right to life under Article 21, and that the State's constitutional duty under Article 47 to prohibit consumption of drugs injurious to health requires active measures to restrict public smoking. The judgment led directly to the enactment of the Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act, 2003, and established that Article 47 is a live directive that can be judicially enforced through directions to the executive to introduce protective legislation.

**Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity v. State of West Bengal (1996)** — A farm labourer who suffered serious head injuries in a train accident was turned away from multiple government hospitals that lacked facilities or beds. The Supreme Court held that the failure to provide emergency medical care was a violation of the right to life under Article 21 read with Article 47, since the constitutional mandate to improve public health creates an obligation to maintain an accessible emergency medical system. The Court directed all States to ensure government hospitals maintain sufficient facilities to provide emergency care, holding that financial constraints cannot justify failure to meet this minimum constitutional standard.

**People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India (2001 onwards)** — The right-to-food case is the most sustained exercise in translating Article 47's nutrition mandate into enforceable constitutional directions. The Supreme Court, invoking both Article 21 and Article 47, issued a series of interim orders directing the Union and State governments to operationalise the public distribution system, activate dormant ICDS centres, run midday meal schemes in government primary schools, and prevent starvation deaths. The Court held that food stored in government godowns must not be allowed to rot while people go hungry — a striking judicial application of the Article 47 "primary duty" to concrete executive action. These orders directly shaped the legislative framework that culminated in the National Food Security Act, 2013.

## Current significance

Article 47 is the constitutional foundation for ongoing public-interest litigation on hunger, malnutrition, and the adequacy of government nutrition programmes. The right to food recognised by the Supreme Court in People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India — which led to the National Food Security Act, 2013 — draws directly on the Article 47 mandate to raise the level of nutrition as a primary duty. India's malnutrition statistics — among the worst in any middle-income country by the measures of the Global Hunger Index — continue to attract judicial attention grounded in Article 47.

The article's reach across contemporary policy domains is broad:

- **Food security**: The National Food Security Act, 2013, entitles 75% of the rural population and 50% of the urban population to subsidised foodgrains through the public distribution system. The constitutional basis for this entitlement is Article 47, as interpreted through the right-to-food litigation.
- **Nutrition programmes**: ICDS (Integrated Child Development Services) and PM POSHAN (formerly Midday Meal Scheme) are judicially mandated programmes whose constitutional foundation is Article 47 read with Article 21. Courts have directed the State to ensure these programmes are not reduced or defunded.
- **Tobacco regulation**: The Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act, 2003, and the ban on public smoking are partly grounded in Article 47's directive on drugs injurious to health.
- **Food safety**: The Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006, and its enforcement through the Food Safety and Standards Authority of India, implement Article 47's mandate to protect public health from unsafe food.
- **Air quality**: Litigation about particulate pollution levels and emission standards increasingly invokes Article 47 alongside Article 48A as the constitutional basis for requiring the State to maintain air quality standards compatible with human health.

The prohibition limb continues to generate active litigation as States maintain or introduce dry laws. The Bihar liquor ban, introduced in 2016, was challenged extensively in the Patna High Court and before the Supreme Court. Courts examining these measures must weigh Article 47's directive against arguments about the practical failure of prohibition enforcement, the growth of illicit liquor trade causing hooch deaths, and the differential impact on poor households and women.

The COVID-19 pandemic sharpened awareness of Article 47's obligations and produced significant litigation invoking it to challenge government failures in healthcare delivery, oxygen supply, and vaccine distribution. Courts issued directions on hospital capacity, essential medicine supply, and oxygen availability, treating the pandemic as a test of whether the constitutional directive to improve public health had been translated into a sufficiently resilient public health system.

A persistent challenge in Article 47 litigation is enforceability. The article uses "primary duties" language that suggests stronger obligations than most DPSPs, but courts have not treated it as directly enforceable in the way that Fundamental Rights are. The practical result is a hybrid regime: courts use Article 47 to interpret the scope of Article 21 rights expansively, to provide constitutional justification for welfare legislation, and to issue directions against demonstrable State inaction, but they do not typically strike down State budget decisions for failing to allocate sufficient resources to nutrition or health.

The normative question — whether the "primary duty" language of Article 47 should generate stronger judicial enforcement than ordinary DPSPs — remains open and is likely to become more contested as courts are asked to evaluate the adequacy of India's public health infrastructure in the wake of the pandemic.

Article 47's connection with Article 21 also raises the question of horizontal application: can a citizen invoke the constitutional duty to improve public health against a private employer or a private hospital? The dominant position is that Part IV imposes duties only on the State and State-like entities, but courts have increasingly used Article 47 to interpret the obligations of private actors who perform public functions — private hospitals receiving government land grants, pharmaceutical companies with monopoly licences, and food manufacturers whose practices affect the nutritional choices available to consumers. The boundaries of this horizontal dimension of Article 47 are being developed case by case.
