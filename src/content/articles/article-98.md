  Article 98 gives each House of Parliament its own independent secretarial staff, while allowing for posts common to both. Parliament may regulate the recruitment and service conditions of secretarial staff by law; until it does so, the President may make rules after consulting the relevant presiding officer. officialText: |-
  98. Secretariat of Parliament.—(1) Each House of Parliament shall have a separate secretarial staff:

  Provided that nothing in this clause shall be construed as preventing the creation of posts common to both Houses of Parliament.

  (2) Parliament may by law regulate the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to the secretarial staff of either House of Parliament.

  (3) Until provision is made by Parliament under clause (2), the President may, after consultation with the Speaker of the House of the People or the Chairman of the Council of States, as the case may be, make rules regulating the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to the secretarial staff of the House of the People or the Council of States, and any rules so made shall have effect subject to the provisions of any law made under the said clause. ## Why it matters

Parliamentary independence is not only about the autonomy of members to speak freely or the privilege
of the House to manage its proceedings — it also requires that the institution's own administrative
resources be insulated from executive control. Article 98 provides that insulation by establishing
the Secretariats of the two Houses as constitutionally grounded institutions rather than departments
of the executive government. A Parliament whose research staff, records, and administrative support
were controlled by the Ministry in charge of parliamentary affairs would be institutionally dependent
on the executive in a way that compromises its scrutiny function. Article 98 prevents that
structural dependency.

The separation between Parliament's administrative machinery and the executive government is a
component of the broader separation of powers that the Constitution embeds across its structure.
The Ministry of Parliamentary Affairs exists to facilitate coordination between the legislature and
the executive — it manages government business in Parliament, provides information to Parliament on
behalf of the executive, and handles legislative scheduling from the government's side. Article 98
ensures that this ministry, however necessary for coordination, cannot direct the Secretariats or
control Parliament's own institutional functions. The Secretary-General of the Lok Sabha and the
Secretary-General of the Rajya Sabha are answerable to their respective presiding officers, not to
any minister or department.

The constitutional grounding of the Secretariats also has budgetary significance. The salaries and
allowances of the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha, the Speaker and Deputy Speaker
of the Lok Sabha, and the officers of Parliament are charged on the Consolidated Fund of India under
Article 97 — meaning they are not subject to annual vote by Parliament. The Secretariats' budgets
are separately presented in the demands for grants but reflect the same logic of institutional
independence: Parliament's administrative costs are not appropriated in a way that allows the
executive to withhold funding as a means of influencing parliamentary activity.

The two-Secretariat structure created by Article 98 operationalises a deeper constitutional
principle: that the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha are distinct constitutional institutions with
their own independent administrative identities, not merely procedural subdivisions of a single
legislature. Each House has its own Speaker or Chairman, its own rules, its own committee structure,
and — under Article 98 — its own secretarial support. The administrative separation mirrors and
reinforces the constitutional separation of the two Houses as separately constituted organs with
different compositions, tenures, and legislative roles.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes two separate secretariats as a constitutional matter. Each House has its
own staff — the Lok Sabha Secretariat and the Rajya Sabha Secretariat — reporting to their
respective presiding officers. The proviso allowing common posts prevents redundant parallel
structures for genuinely shared functions (for instance, joint parliamentary committee support),
without undermining the fundamental institutional separation. The phrase "separate secretarial
staff" is categorical: it is a constitutional floor below which administrative rationalisation
cannot go. Proposals to merge the two Secretariats in the interest of efficiency would require a
constitutional amendment, not merely administrative reorganisation.

The proviso is important in its own right. It recognises that certain administrative functions are
genuinely shared between the two Houses — maintaining joint committee records, operating common
information technology infrastructure, or staffing joint sessions convened under Article 108. By
allowing common posts for these functions without treating the proviso as undermining the
fundamental separation, Article 98(1) adopts a pragmatic flexibility while protecting institutional
independence. The line between permissible common posts and impermissible organisational merger is
drawn by the requirement that each House must have its own "separate secretarial staff" — common
posts supplement the two Secretariats, they do not replace them.

Clause (2) is the primary legislative power — Parliament may by law regulate the recruitment and
service conditions of both Secretariats. This means Parliament, as the institution, determines the
terms on which its own administrative staff are employed. Parliament has exercised this power through
several instruments, including rules governing service conditions, recruitment procedures, and pay
scales for Secretariat staff. The significance of this legislative power is that it is Parliament
regulating its own administrative apparatus — not the executive regulating Parliament's
administration. A law made under clause (2) cannot be amended by executive order or by the
President's rule-making power under clause (3).

Clause (3) is the transitional provision. Until Parliament legislates under clause (2), the
President may make rules after consulting the relevant presiding officer. The consultation
requirement is significant — the President does not act unilaterally on Secretariat matters but must
engage the Speaker or Chairman. The phrase "after consultation" implies a genuine process of seeking
and considering views, not merely a formal notification. Any presidential rules made under clause
(3) have effect only subject to subsequently enacted parliamentary legislation, confirming that the
President's rule-making under this clause is subordinate and residual — a gap-filling mechanism,
not an independent source of authority over parliamentary administration.

The transitional character of clause (3) is also relevant to its continued operation. In practice,
the Secretariats' recruitment and service conditions are now substantially governed by rules,
regulations, and parliamentary instruments that have accumulated over seven decades. The President's
rule-making power under clause (3) is now largely historical — though it remains available for gaps
not covered by existing instruments. The constitutional hierarchy is clear: parliamentary law takes
precedence over presidential rules, and both must be consistent with the constitutional structure
of independent Secretariats under clause (1).

## Key cases

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Supreme Court, examining the scope of
parliamentary privileges and judicial jurisdiction over parliamentary decisions, commented on the
administrative independence of the Parliamentary Secretariat and its constitutional basis in Article
98. The Court noted that the Secretariat's constitutional grounding means its functioning is not a
matter of executive grace — it is a constitutionally established institution. The judgment's broader
significance is its confirmation that the courts can examine the actions of parliamentary
institutions when constitutional rights are at issue, without that examination constituting
interference with parliamentary proceedings protected by Article 122.

**Tej Kiran Jain v. N. Sanjiva Reddy (1970)** — The Supreme Court addressed the relationship between
parliamentary officers and the constitutional immunities attaching to parliamentary proceedings,
engaging the institutional structure Article 98 establishes and confirming that Secretariat functions
are covered within the framework of parliamentary privilege. The case arose from a speech made in
Parliament and the question of whether the Speaker and Secretariat officers could be held liable for
enabling publication of the speech. The Court's holding that actions taken in the course of
parliamentary proceedings carry the full protection of parliamentary privilege applied with equal
force to Secretariat actions taken in the discharge of their constitutional functions.

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)** — While the case principally concerned election law
and the constitutional amendment power, the Supreme Court's discussion of the separation of powers
and the independence of constitutional institutions from executive direction is relevant to Article
98's structural logic. The Court's treatment of Parliament as a constitutional institution with its
own administrative sphere of independence, distinct from the executive, provides the background
principle against which Article 98's administrative separation must be understood.

## Current significance

Article 98 underpins the institutional independence of the Lok Sabha Secretariat and Rajya Sabha
Secretariat. Each Secretariat has its own budget, which is reflected in the demands for grants
presented to Parliament but which is separated from the Ministry of Parliamentary Affairs' own
budget, reflecting the constitutional separation between legislative and executive administration.
The Secretariats prepare Bills, maintain records, conduct research for members, and manage the
legislative drafting process — functions that would be compromised if subject to executive direction.

The Secretariats have expanded significantly in institutional capacity over the decades. The Lok
Sabha Secretariat now includes the Parliament Library, the Parliament Museum, and parliamentary
computing services — all of which provide informational and analytical support to members that
enhances the legislature's capacity for independent scrutiny. The Research and Information division
of the Lok Sabha Secretariat produces reference notes, background documents, and analysis for members
on Bills and policy questions, providing institutional support for parliamentary accountability that
would be structurally compromised if the Secretariat were administered by an executive department.
Parliamentary committees, which have become the primary instrument of legislative scrutiny over
executive departments, depend on Secretariat staff for research, record-keeping, and report drafting
— another dimension of the institutional autonomy that Article 98 protects.

The article's proviso permitting common posts has practical importance for joint parliamentary
committees and shared information infrastructure. The line between permissible common posts and
impermissible merger of the two Secretariats has occasionally been discussed in the context of
administrative efficiency proposals, with Article 98(1)'s "separate secretarial staff" requirement
functioning as the constitutional floor below which rationalisation cannot go. The digital
infrastructure supporting both Secretariats — including the e-Sansad portal and the Parliament's
digital library — has been developed jointly, reflecting the proviso's practical operation in the
contemporary context. The constitutional separation remains intact while administrative modernisation
has proceeded through jointly-maintained common infrastructure that the proviso expressly permits.
