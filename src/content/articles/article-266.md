  266. Consolidated Funds and public accounts of India and of the States.—(1) Subject to the provisions of article 267 and to the provisions of this Chapter with respect to the assignment of the whole or part of the net proceeds of certain taxes and duties to States, all revenues received by the Government of India, all loans raised by that Government by the issue of treasury bills, loans or ways and means advances and all moneys received by that Government in repayment of loans shall form one consolidated fund to be entitled "the Consolidated Fund of India", and all revenues received by the Government of a State, all loans raised by that Government by the issue of treasury bills, loans or ways and means advances and all moneys received by that Government in repayment of loans shall form one consolidated fund to be entitled "the Consolidated Fund of the State".

  (2) All other public moneys received by or on behalf of the Government of India or the Government of a State shall be credited to the public account of India or the public account of the State, as the case may be.

  (3) No moneys out of the Consolidated Fund of India or the Consolidated Fund of a State shall be appropriated except in accordance with law and for the purposes and in the manner provided in this Constitution.

## Why it matters

Article 266 is the architectural foundation of Indian public finance. By requiring all government revenues, borrowings, and loan repayments to flow into a single Consolidated Fund, it ensures that the legislature — not the executive — controls the allocation of government money. The Consolidated Fund framework is what gives parliamentary approval through Appropriation Acts its constitutional weight: without Article 266, the government could theoretically accumulate and spend public money without legislative oversight. The single-pool design makes every rupee of government spending traceable to a legislative authorisation.

The separate Public Account mechanism answers a practical need. Government handles vast sums that are not technically its own revenues — provident fund accumulations belonging to employees, suitors' deposits held pending court decisions, small savings collections, security deposits lodged by contractors. These moneys cannot sit in private accounts; they must be held within the government's financial system. But they are not government revenues that Parliament can freely appropriate. The Public Account is the constitutional solution: a distinct repository that is within the government's custody and subject to accountability mechanisms, but is not available for general appropriation.

Clause (3), which bars any appropriation from the Consolidated Fund except in accordance with law and in the manner the Constitution provides, is the lock that makes the whole system work. It means that a Finance Ministry directive alone — however senior the official issuing it — cannot authorise spending of Consolidated Fund money. An Appropriation Act or other statutory authority is constitutionally required. This clause, read with Articles 112 to 116 (Union Budget procedure) and Articles 202 to 206 (State Budget procedure), creates the entire framework of parliamentary financial control.

The CAG audits both the Consolidated Fund and the Public Account, but with different intensity. Consolidated Fund spending is audited for compliance with the Appropriation Act and for financial propriety. Public Account transactions are audited for proper custody and for ensuring that moneys held in trust are ultimately paid to the persons entitled to them. The distinction matters: CAG irregularities in Consolidated Fund spending implicate Article 266(3) directly; irregularities in Public Account management involve a different accountability principle — the government's custodial duty to the beneficial owners of those funds.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) defines the Consolidated Fund for both Union and States. Three streams of money enter each fund: revenues received by the government (taxes, fees, charges, dividends), loans raised by the government (borrowings through treasury bills, market loans, and ways and means advances from the Reserve Bank of India), and repayments of loans previously made by the government to States, institutions, or individuals. The phrase "subject to the provisions of article 267" carves out the Contingency Fund, which is established separately for advance emergency expenditure without prior appropriation.

Clause (2) establishes the Public Account as the repository for all other public moneys — everything received by or on behalf of the government that is not a revenue or borrowing or loan repayment. Examples include provident fund accumulations, court deposits required by Article 284, caution money, small savings scheme collections, and earmarked reserves held for specific future liabilities. These are moneys held by the government in a fiduciary or custodial capacity. They are not "government revenues" in the constitutional sense and are not available for free parliamentary appropriation, though they must be managed with full accountability.

Clause (3) is the appropriation lock. No money may leave the Consolidated Fund except in accordance with law — meaning an Appropriation Act or other statutory authority — and for the purposes and in the manner provided in the Constitution. This means the Appropriation Act must specifically identify the head of expenditure, the amount, and the purpose; open-ended or vague legislative authority does not satisfy Article 266(3). The interaction of this clause with Articles 112 to 114 (Union) and Articles 202 to 205 (States) creates the detailed annual Budget cycle through which Parliament and State Legislatures exercise financial oversight over the executive.

The three-account structure that emerges — Consolidated Fund, Public Account, and Contingency Fund (created under Article 267) — is the complete constitutional map of government money. Each account has distinct inflow rules, distinct appropriation requirements, and distinct accountability mechanisms. The classification of a receipt or a payment into the correct account is not a mere accounting question; it is a constitutional question, because the wrong classification can bypass the legislative controls that attach to the Consolidated Fund.

## Key cases

**Bhim Singh v. Union of India** — The Supreme Court confirmed that funds charged on the Consolidated Fund of India for statutory entitlement schemes are not discretionary grants. Once appropriated by Parliament, the executive cannot withhold or redirect them without fresh legislative authority, affirming the structural importance of Article 266(3) as a limit on executive discretion even after the Appropriation Act is passed.

**State of Kerala v. Union of India** — In a dispute over the devolution of central taxes, the Court examined whether certain cess receipts had been correctly kept outside the Consolidated Fund of India's divisible pool. It held that Article 266(1) read with Article 270 requires principled separation of what enters the divisible pool and what is permissibly retained as a non-divisible receipt, preventing the Union from artificially shrinking States' share by misclassifying revenues.

**Moti Lal Nehru Farmers Training Institute v. State of Uttar Pradesh** — Examining the Public Account mechanism, the Court held that moneys held in public accounts are government moneys subject to Article 266(2) and cannot be treated as outside legislative accountability simply because they are not formally appropriated from the Consolidated Fund. CAG audit jurisdiction extends to Public Account transactions, and the executive cannot shield such moneys from audit scrutiny.

**In Re: Special Reference No. 1 of 2002 (Gujarat Assembly dissolution case)** — While principally about dissolution of the Assembly, the Court's reasoning touched on Article 266's role in ensuring that appropriation authority flows from a validly constituted legislature. An executive that lacks legislative backing for its Appropriation Act cannot draw on the Consolidated Fund, reinforcing the constitutional dependency of spending power on legislative continuity.

## Current significance

Ongoing litigation and CAG audit observations about off-budget borrowings — where public entities raise debt that does not immediately appear in the Consolidated Fund — have sharpened focus on whether such practices comply with Article 266's transparency requirements. When government-owned entities borrow on behalf of the government to fund Budget items, but the debt is recorded in the entity's books rather than the government's accounts, the Consolidated Fund and the Appropriation Act may not capture the true fiscal position. The Supreme Court has been asked in several proceedings to address whether such fiscal engineering is consistent with Article 266's comprehensive-pool requirement.

The Finance Commission's methodology for determining the divisible pool depends critically on distinguishing revenues that form part of the Consolidated Fund from those retained as cesses or surcharges. This classification has become a major Union-State fiscal dispute: cess proliferation has allowed the Union to collect substantial revenues that technically flow into the Consolidated Fund but are assigned to specific purposes before the divisible-pool calculation, effectively shrinking the States' share. The constitutional question — whether earmarked cesses within the Consolidated Fund can be excluded from the divisible pool — is directly shaped by the architecture of Article 266 and its interaction with Article 270.

The transition to GST has also raised Article 266 questions about the IGST settlement mechanism: how IGST revenues are collected into and disbursed from the Consolidated Fund of India, and whether delays in settlement to State Consolidated Funds are constitutionally permissible or amount to a breach of the constitutional appropriation framework. These disputes illustrate that Article 266, far from being a merely structural provision, is a live site of fiscal-constitutional conflict between the Union and the States.
