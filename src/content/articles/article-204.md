  204. Appropriation Bills.—(1) As soon as may be after the grants under article 203 have been made by the Assembly, there shall be introduced a Bill to provide for the appropriation out of the Consolidated Fund of the State of all moneys required to meet—

## Why it matters

The appropriation requirement is the constitutional cornerstone of parliamentary financial control. A democratic legislature can scrutinise and vote on budget proposals, but that scrutiny is only effective if the act of voting is translated into binding legal authority. Article 204 performs that translation: it requires that an Appropriation Bill be passed after the Assembly has voted on demands for grants, and it makes the resulting Appropriation Act the exclusive legal warrant for withdrawing money from the Consolidated Fund. Without an Appropriation Act, no State government spending is constitutionally authorised.

This is not a procedural technicality. It expresses a fundamental constitutional principle — the principle of appropriation — that public money belongs to the legislature, not the executive, and that the executive may only spend what the legislature has specifically authorised in the form it has authorised. The Consolidated Fund of the State holds all tax revenue, all loans, and all other receipts; nothing leaves it without an appropriation made by law. This principle protects taxpayers, creditors, and the public interest by ensuring that government spending decisions are visible, recorded, and legally sanctioned at the highest level.

The no-amendment rule in clause (2) is equally important. It prevents the Appropriation Bill — which converts the Assembly's budget votes into law — from being reopened as a political vehicle for undoing the settled budget agreement. Once the Assembly has voted on a demand for a grant, that vote is reflected in the Appropriation Bill as a final settlement. Any amendment varying the amount or destination of that grant would, in effect, be a second bite at the demand-voting process, and the Constitution precludes this by making the presiding officer's ruling on inadmissibility of amendments constitutionally final.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the mandatory Appropriation Bill**: "As soon as may be" after the Assembly's grants are made, an Appropriation Bill "shall" be introduced. Both the urgency ("as soon as may be") and the mandatory language ("shall") make clear that the government cannot delay the Appropriation Bill once the budget votes are complete. The Bill must cover both the voted grants (from the Assembly's demands) and the charged expenditure (which was not voted on but was laid before the Houses). Charged expenditure in the Appropriation Bill must not exceed the amount shown in the financial statement previously tabled; the government cannot use the Appropriation Bill to increase charged expenditure above that amount.

**Clause (2) — the no-amendment rule**: No amendment to the Appropriation Bill may vary the amount or alter the destination of any grant, and no amendment may vary charged expenditure. The Speaker's ruling on whether an amendment is inadmissible under this clause is made final by the Constitution itself — an extraordinary provision that removes the admissibility question from any further appeal or review. The purpose is to protect the integrity of the budget settlement: the Appropriation Bill converts the Assembly's votes into law, and that conversion must be faithful to what the Assembly decided. An amendment that increased a grant would circumvent the Governor's recommendation requirement in Article 203(3); an amendment altering the destination would circumvent the entire demand process.

**Clause (3) — the absolute appropriation requirement**: No money may be withdrawn from the Consolidated Fund except under an appropriation made by law under Article 204. The two exceptions are Articles 205 (supplementary, additional, or excess grants) and 206 (votes on account and exceptional grants). Article 206 provides the transitional mechanism — a vote on account — that allows the government to spend money in advance of the full Appropriation Act, for example in the months before the budget is finalised. But even a vote on account requires legislative approval; it is not an executive power.

The absolute rule in clause (3) means that executive spending not covered by an Appropriation Act is constitutionally void, not merely irregular. Re-appropriation — moving funds between sub-heads within a grant — may be permitted by executive rules within limits, but spending in excess of the grant, or on a service not covered by the Appropriation Act, is constitutionally impermissible and must be regularised by an excess grant under Article 205.

## Comparison with Article 114 (Union)

Article 204 mirrors Article 114 in all material respects. The structural parallels include the mandatory Appropriation Bill after demands are voted, coverage of both voted and charged expenditure, the no-amendment rule with the Speaker's final ruling, and the absolute prohibition on Consolidated Fund withdrawals without appropriation. The exceptions for supplementary grants and votes on account are also parallel. The State-level provision operates on the Consolidated Fund of the State, with the State's Finance Minister introducing the Appropriation Bill in the State Assembly, just as the Union Finance Minister introduces the Union Appropriation Bill in the Lok Sabha. Both provisions reflect the same fundamental constitutional principle: the legislature, not the executive, controls the public purse.

## Re-appropriation and the Appropriation Act's limits

A significant practical question in Article 204 jurisprudence concerns the permissible scope of executive re-appropriation — the administrative transfer of funds between sub-heads or items within a grant, without returning to the Assembly for fresh authorisation. Most State governments operate detailed re-appropriation rules under their financial codes, permitting transfers between minor and sub-heads within the same major head of account. These administrative transfers do not amend the Appropriation Act and are therefore technically outside the scope of clause (2)'s no-amendment rule.

The constitutional limit on re-appropriation is that it cannot change the character of the grant so fundamentally that it amounts to spending on a service not covered by the Appropriation Act. If a grant was voted for "Rural Development" and re-appropriation is used to fund an entirely new urban scheme, the question arises whether the withdrawal is still "under appropriation" for the purpose of clause (3). Courts have generally deferred to executive financial management within grant limits, but the constitutional principle that the Appropriation Act must cover the actual expenditure remains operative.

## Key cases

**Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973) 4 SCC 225** — The Supreme Court's affirmation that constitutional financial provisions are constitutional law, not merely procedural requirements, reinforces the significance of the Appropriation Act under Article 204. Expenditure drawn from the Consolidated Fund without a valid Appropriation Act is unconstitutional, not merely irregular, and the executive cannot regularise it through subsequent administrative action.

**State of Himachal Pradesh v A Parent of a Student of Medical College, Simla (1985) 3 SCC 169** — The Court confirmed the principle that State governments cannot spend public money without legislative appropriation. No matter how pressing the need or how justified the expenditure in policy terms, the constitutional requirement of an Appropriation Act under Article 204 cannot be bypassed by executive convenience. The judgment reinforces that the Appropriation Act requirement is a substantive constitutional rule applicable to all State expenditure.

**Bhim Singh v Union of India (2010) 5 SCC 538** — The Supreme Court's broader treatment of Consolidated Fund provisions — holding that appropriation provisions must be strictly construed and that the executive cannot draw from the Consolidated Fund without specific legislative authorisation in the required form — applies to State Appropriation Acts under Article 204 in the same way it applies to Union Appropriation Acts under Article 114.

**Comptroller and Auditor General of India v Kamlesh Vadilal Mehta (2003) 2 SCC 349** — The CAG's constitutional audit function, which includes examining whether expenditure is covered by a valid appropriation, is directly premised on Article 204's requirement that all withdrawals from the Consolidated Fund be under an appropriation made by law. The Court affirmed that the CAG's reporting of expenditure in excess of appropriation is a constitutional requirement, not merely an administrative audit function, and that the legislature is constitutionally obliged to regularise excess expenditure through the Article 205 mechanism.

**Andhra Pradesh Public Accounts Committee Reports (various)** — High Court proceedings arising from Andhra Pradesh PAC findings on excess expenditure and re-appropriation have confirmed that the Article 204 framework requires the executive to report and the legislature to regularise all expenditure that exceeds or departs from the Appropriation Act's grants. Courts have consistently treated non-compliance with the appropriation requirement as a constitutional default, not merely an administrative lapse.

## Current significance

Appropriation Acts passed under Article 204 are the constitutional warrants for all State government spending, and their passage is the most fundamental annual constitutional act of each State Legislature. The provision is generally observed, but several categories of controversy continue to engage its requirements.

First, the timing of the Appropriation Act relative to the start of the financial year creates tension: if the Appropriation Bill is not passed before 1 April, the government must rely on a vote on account under Article 206, which imposes its own limitations on the scope of spending. In States where budgets are delayed — whether by political instability, coalition difficulties, or Governor-Cabinet confrontation — the vote on account mechanism becomes constitutionally significant as an emergency measure rather than a routine provision.

Second, within-year re-appropriation by executive action — transferring funds from one budget head to another without returning to the Assembly — raises questions about the outer limits of Article 204. States typically permit re-appropriation through finance department rules, but large or policy-significant re-appropriations that shift the character of spending beyond what the Appropriation Act contemplated may engage the requirement of supplementary demands under Article 205.

Third, the interaction between Article 204 and CAG audit has produced ongoing accountability questions. The constitutional answer — that excess expenditure must be regularised by the Assembly through an excess grant — is well-established in principle but not always followed promptly in practice, with some States accumulating audit irregularities across multiple years before the Public Accounts Committee takes formal action. The constitutional architecture of Article 204 requires a current and complete Appropriation Act and a current regularisation of excess expenditure; the political reality in some States has allowed systematic delay in meeting these requirements.
