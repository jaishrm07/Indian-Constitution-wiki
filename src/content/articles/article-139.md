## What this Article does

Article 139 authorises Parliament to confer on the Supreme Court the power to issue writs — habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto, certiorari, and their equivalents — for purposes beyond the enforcement of fundamental rights already guaranteed under Article 32(2). Article 32 gives the Supreme Court writ jurisdiction as a fundamental right remedy; Article 139 creates a parallel, statutory channel for writ jurisdiction in non-fundamental-rights contexts, subject to Parliament legislating for it.

The article recognises that the five great prerogative writs are not exclusively fundamental rights tools. They are administrative law remedies of general application, and a need exists for the Supreme Court to issue them in matters of public law that may not directly involve Part III rights. By making this expansion depend on parliamentary legislation rather than constitutional amendment, Article 139 keeps the Court's writ jurisdiction dynamic and responsive to evolving governance needs.

## Article 32 and Article 139 compared

The relationship between Articles 32 and 139 is one of foundation and extension:

- **Article 32(2)** gives the Supreme Court writ jurisdiction directly as part of the Constitution, enforceable as a fundamental right. This jurisdiction cannot be suspended except during an emergency declared under Article 359, and cannot be curtailed by ordinary legislation.
- **Article 139** enables Parliament to confer additional writ jurisdiction by ordinary statute, for purposes outside the Article 32(2) scope. This statutory jurisdiction is not itself a fundamental right and could be modified or withdrawn by Parliament.

The crucial phrase in Article 139 is "for any purposes other than those mentioned in clause (2) of article 32." This excludes Parliament from duplicating the fundamental-rights writ jurisdiction (which the Constitution has already directly conferred) but permits conferral for any other purpose — statutory enforcement, administrative law supervision, public body accountability, and inter-state matters.

## Article 226 and the High Courts

Article 226 gives High Courts a broader writ jurisdiction than the Supreme Court's Article 32 jurisdiction — High Courts can issue writs "for the enforcement of any of the rights conferred by Part III and for any other purpose." The "any other purpose" scope in Article 226 means High Courts already have the wider writ jurisdiction that Article 139 is designed to enable for the Supreme Court. This structural asymmetry reflects the original constitutional design: High Courts were the primary administrative law courts, and the Supreme Court's writ power was initially limited to fundamental rights enforcement.

Article 139's significance is partly to bring the Supreme Court's potential writ jurisdiction closer to the High Court's Article 226 scope, but only if Parliament chooses to act — and Parliament has not yet done so comprehensively.

## Why Parliament has not acted

Parliament's non-use of Article 139 reflects several factors:

1. **Article 32's expansive interpretation:** The Supreme Court has interpreted Article 32 broadly, allowing public interest litigation that goes far beyond traditional fundamental rights enforcement. Many matters that would formally require Article 139 jurisdiction are in practice accommodated within Article 32's expanded scope.
2. **Article 142:** The Court's power to do "complete justice" under Article 142 has allowed it to issue directions and orders in areas not directly covered by Article 32 writs, reducing the perceived gap.
3. **Article 136:** The Court's wide special leave jurisdiction allows it to reach any judicial or quasi-judicial matter, providing an alternative access route that does not depend on writ jurisdiction.

The practical result is that the statutory writ jurisdiction under Article 139 has never been necessary in the way the framers may have contemplated, because the Court found other constitutional tools sufficient.

## Key cases

**Dwarkanath v. Income Tax Officer (1965):** The Court examined the relationship between Article 32 and the potential for statutory writ jurisdiction, clarifying that the Article 32 writ power is an irreducible constitutional right and any Article 139 conferral by Parliament operates in addition to, not in derogation of, that jurisdiction.

**Fertilizer Corporation Kamgar Union v. Union of India (1981):** The Court discussed the scope of writ jurisdiction and noted that Parliament's power to extend writs to non-fundamental-rights contexts under Article 139 reflects a broader constitutional design of using prerogative-style remedies as instruments of public law accountability beyond the fundamental rights chapter.

**S.P. Gupta v. President of India (1981):** This locus standi judgment, which opened the door to public interest litigation, implicitly addressed the scope of Article 32 and noted that the Court's writ jurisdiction was not confined to the traditional categories of high-prerogative writ. This expansive approach to Article 32 reduced pressure to activate Article 139's statutory route.

## Current significance

Parliament has not enacted stand-alone legislation under Article 139. The provision retains prospective significance: should Parliament wish to give the Supreme Court a statutory writ jurisdiction over constitutional bodies that operate beyond the reach of High Courts under Article 226, or over matters involving inter-state detention or enforcement against bodies with pan-India reach, Article 139 provides the constitutional hook. Academic discourse has also noted that Article 139 could support a dedicated Supreme Court habeas corpus jurisdiction covering persons held in multiple states simultaneously — a situation where the High Court's territorial jurisdiction is inadequate. Any such legislation would need to be consistent with the basic structure and with the Article 226 jurisdiction of High Courts, which the L. Chandra Kumar line of cases protects from exclusion.
