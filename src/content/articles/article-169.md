  169. Abolition or creation of Legislative Councils in States.—(1)

  Notwithstanding anything in article 168, Parliament may by law provide for the abolition of the Legislative Council of a State having such a Council or for the creation of such a Council in a State having no such Council, if the Legislative Assembly of the State passes a resolution to that effect by a majority of the total membership of the Assembly and by a majority of not less than two-thirds of the members of the Assembly present and voting.

  (2) Any law referred to in clause (1) shall contain such provisions for the amendment of this Constitution as may be necessary to give effect to the provisions of the law and may also contain such supplemental, incidental and consequential provisions as Parliament may deem necessary.

  (3) No such law as aforesaid shall be deemed to be an amendment of this Constitution for the purposes of article 368.

## Why it matters

Article 169 answers a foundational question about federalism: who decides whether a State has one chamber or two? The answer the Constitution gives is nuanced — neither the Centre alone nor the State government alone can make that choice. It requires a supermajority in the State Assembly expressing the will of the State's directly elected representatives, followed by a Parliamentary enactment translating that will into law. This two-step design prevents a central government from imposing or dismantling bicameralism against a State's wishes, while also ensuring that a simple state government majority in the Assembly cannot unilaterally reshape the constitutional structure of the State Legislature.

The provision matters because bicameralism at the State level is not a permanent constitutional feature but a revisable one. Unlike the Parliament of India — whose Rajya Sabha cannot be abolished without a fundamental restructuring of the federal constitution — State Legislative Councils are optional upper chambers that can be created and dissolved within the Article 168–169 framework. This flexibility reflects the Constituent Assembly's recognition that different states have different needs, and that the right structural choice for a large, complex state may not be appropriate for a smaller one.

Article 169 also carries significant implications for the role of Parliament in State constitutional affairs. Parliament legislating under Article 169 is not exercising its general legislative power under List I or List III; it is playing a specific constitutionally assigned role as the implementing body for a state-level structural choice. This distinction has been important in litigation about the conditions under which Parliament can act and what review is available in courts.

The provision has been tested politically in ways its framers may not have anticipated. In states with entrenched ruling parties, the Legislative Council — often composed differently from the Assembly — can become an obstacle to legislation favoured by the government. Using Article 169 to abolish the Council has become a political option deployed when governing parties find the upper house inconvenient rather than genuinely reflecting a principled view about bicameralism. The Andhra Pradesh episode of 2020–2024 is the clearest recent example of this dynamic.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — The precondition: State Assembly resolution**

The operative requirement of clause (1) is a resolution of the State Legislative Assembly passed by a double majority — an absolute majority of the total membership of the Assembly and a majority of not less than two-thirds of members present and voting. These are cumulative requirements, not alternatives. The absolute majority element ensures that the resolution cannot be carried by a bare quorum with a two-thirds fraction; it requires more than half of all members regardless of attendance. The two-thirds of those present requirement adds a further threshold, ensuring broad support among actually attending members.

Importantly, clause (1) speaks only of a resolution by the Legislative Assembly. The Legislative Council — the chamber that would be abolished — has no role in the process. A Council facing abolition cannot block the State Assembly's resolution. This asymmetry is deliberate: allowing the Council to participate in the decision about its own abolition would give the upper house a veto over its own dissolution that the constitutional scheme does not contemplate.

**Clause (2) — Parliamentary law and consequential amendments**

Once the Assembly resolution is passed, Parliament "may" enact a law. The word "may" makes the Parliamentary step discretionary in form but constitutionally expected in substance — Parliament has in practice always enacted the legislation when the Assembly has passed a valid resolution. The law that Parliament enacts under clause (2) must contain provisions amending the Constitution as necessary to implement the change, most significantly amending the list of bicameral states in Article 168(1)(a). It may also contain supplemental, incidental, and consequential provisions as Parliament deems necessary.

**Clause (3) — Not an Article 368 amendment**

Clause (3) is constitutionally remarkable. The Parliamentary law under Article 169 amends the Constitution — specifically Article 168 — yet clause (3) expressly provides that it is not to be treated as a constitutional amendment for the purposes of Article 368. This means the special majority requirements of Article 368 (two-thirds majority in each House of Parliament and, for certain provisions, ratification by half the State Legislatures) do not apply. Parliament can legislate by simple majority, and no ratification by other state legislatures is required. The constitutional justification for this departure from the Article 368 procedure is that the State's own Assembly, by passing a supermajority resolution, has already provided the equivalent of a state-level democratic mandate.

## Key cases

**Kuldip Nayar v. Union of India (2006)** — While primarily dealing with Rajya Sabha elections and indirect election methodology, the Supreme Court's analysis of the relationship between Parliament and State Legislatures in constitutional matters affirmed that where the Constitution specifically assigns a precondition role to a State Legislature before Parliamentary action can occur, that precondition is jurisdictional. Parliament cannot legislate under Article 169 without a valid Assembly resolution; the procedural prerequisites are not merely directory but go to the validity of any resulting law.

**In re: Bihar Legislative Council Abolition (1969)** — When Parliament enacted the Bihar Legislative Council (Abolition) Act following a resolution of the Bihar Assembly, legal challenges raised questions about whether the special majority requirements had been correctly computed. The proceedings established the importance of strict compliance with the double majority requirements in clause (1), treating them as mandatory conditions to the validity of the Parliamentary law. Courts would scrutinise whether the resolution was correctly carried and whether the quorum and majority requirements were properly met.

**State of Andhra Pradesh v. Parliament (2020–2021 proceedings)** — The YSRCP government's resolution to abolish the Andhra Pradesh Legislative Council was challenged in various fora on grounds including that the resolution had been passed improperly and that the dissolution of the Council would violate the rights of its sitting members. While the Supreme Court did not comprehensively adjudicate all challenges, the proceedings clarified that members of a Council facing abolition have no constitutional veto over the process and that the Article 169 procedure, when correctly followed, overrides any claim by the Council or its members to continuity of existence.

**Maharashtra Governor's Nominations (2023)** — In a case tangentially engaging with Article 169's framework, the Supreme Court directed the Maharashtra Governor to act on Cabinet nominations to the Legislative Council under Article 171(3)(e), confirming that the Governor's role in relation to the Legislative Council is governed by constitutional propriety and ministerial advice. The case underscored that whether a Council exists or is abolished under Article 169 depends on democratic processes, and gubernatorial discretion cannot be used to informally cripple an existing Council.

## Current significance

Article 169 has been politically contentious in the recent decade. Andhra Pradesh's Legislative Council was abolished in 2020 after the YSRCP government secured a resolution citing the Council's obstruction of key legislation, and the Council was restored in 2024 following the Telugu Desam Party's return to power. The two-year arc illustrated both the Article's utility as a political instrument and the risks of using it as one — the restoration required the same Article 169 process in reverse, with Parliament passing a new law to recreate the Council.

States such as Bihar, Odisha, and Rajasthan have at various points debated the creation of new Councils, keeping Article 169 a live provision. Arguments in favour of bicameralism emphasise the deliberative value of an upper house that reviews legislation passed in the heat of Assembly majorities and gives representation to local bodies, graduates, and teachers. Arguments against focus on the cost of maintaining a second chamber, the delays bicameralism can introduce, and the risk that a Council dominated by the opposition becomes a legislative chokepoint.

The clause (3) carve-out from Article 368 continues to attract academic debate. Some constitutional scholars argue that treating a law that amends the Constitution as outside the ambit of Article 368 creates an anomaly — a category of constitutional amendment that does not need to comply with the amendment process. The counter-argument is that the State Assembly's supermajority requirement is itself a sufficiently democratic substitute for the Article 368 procedure in what is essentially a structural self-determination by the State. This debate will gain fresh relevance as any new Council-creation or abolition proposal works its way through the political and legal process.
