## Why it matters

A modern state's armed forces and security services operate under conditions that cannot easily accommodate the full range of fundamental rights guaranteed to ordinary citizens. Soldiers must obey orders without debate; intelligence officers must keep secrets; paramilitary personnel maintaining public order must act without industrial-action rights that could paralyse their function. The framers recognised this tension and vested in Parliament — not the executive — the power to define how far fundamental rights may be cut back for these categories of personnel.

The choice to make this a parliamentary power, rather than an executive one, was deliberate. The executive cannot unilaterally suspend the fundamental rights of service members; Parliament must enact a law. That law is then subject to constitutional scrutiny: courts will ask whether the restriction falls within Article 33's four categories, whether Parliament has actually legislated the restriction, and whether the restriction genuinely serves discipline or proper discharge of duty. Article 33 is therefore a grant of legislative latitude, not a constitutional blank cheque.

The provision was expanded by the Sixteenth Constitutional Amendment, 1963, which added the intelligence and telecommunications categories (clauses (c) and (d)), reflecting the growth of organised intelligence apparatus after Independence and the Sino-Indian War.

## Reading the provision

Article 33 names four classes of persons whose fundamental rights Parliament may restrict or abrogate:

- **Armed Forces members** — personnel in the Army, Navy, and Air Force governed by the Army Act 1950, the Navy Act 1957, and the Air Force Act 1950.
- **Forces charged with the maintenance of public order** — paramilitary forces such as the Central Reserve Police Force (CRPF), the Border Security Force (BSF), the Central Industrial Security Force (CISF), and state armed police.
- **Intelligence and counter-intelligence personnel** — employees of organisations such as the Intelligence Bureau and the Research and Analysis Wing.
- **Defence telecommunications personnel** — those operating communication systems serving any of the above forces or organisations.

The permissible purpose of restriction is dual and conjunctive: to ensure "the proper discharge of their duties" and "the maintenance of discipline among them." A parliamentary restriction that cannot be linked to either purpose would, in principle, be outside Article 33's authorisation.

The key statutes exercising this power include Section 21 of the Army Act 1950 (restricting the right to form associations or communicate with the press), parallel provisions in the Navy Act and Air Force Act, the CRPF Act 1949, and the BSF Act 1968. These laws also establish courts martial and service tribunals as the primary adjudicatory forums for disciplinary matters, displacing ordinary civil court jurisdiction in the first instance.

## Key cases

**Lieutenant Colonel Prithi Pal Singh Bedi v Union of India (1982)** — The Supreme Court upheld the Army Act's disciplinary provisions against challenge under Articles 14 and 21, holding that Article 33 gives Parliament broad latitude to create a distinct legal order for armed forces personnel. The ruling confirmed that courts martial operating under Parliament-enacted service law are not required to replicate every procedural guarantee of ordinary criminal courts, provided the basic requirements of fair hearing are met.

**Union of India v Major General Madan Lal Yadav (1996)** — The Court reiterated that Parliament's power under Article 33 allows a separate disciplinary regime for military personnel, and that challenges to court-martial proceedings must be assessed with the distinctive context of military service in mind rather than by direct transposition of civilian criminal-procedure standards.

**Bhupendra Nath Hazarika v State of Assam (2013)** — While primarily decided under service law, the Supreme Court's observations on the limits of executive action against paramilitary personnel reinforced the principle that Article 33's authorisation runs to Parliament's legislative choices and does not expand the executive's power to act outside those statutory frameworks. Personnel whose rights are restricted by law retain residual constitutional protection.

**Secretary, Ministry of Defence v Babita Puniya (2020)** — The Supreme Court held that women Short Service Commission officers in the Army were entitled to permanent commission on equal terms with men, rejecting the government's operational-considerations argument. The Court clarified that Article 33 does not insulate service law from all constitutional scrutiny — it only permits Parliament to restrict those rights that Parliament has chosen to restrict, and Article 14 continues to apply to the terms and conditions of military service not covered by any such specific restriction.

**Union of India v Ex-Flt. Lt. R.K. Arya (1997)** — The Court examined the interaction between service tribunals established under the Armed Forces Tribunal Act and the High Courts' supervisory jurisdiction, reinforcing that Article 33-based restrictions apply to substantive rights, not to the constitutional machinery for judicial oversight of those restrictions.

## Current significance

Article 33 sits at the intersection of civil liberties and national security in several live debates. The question of whether armed forces and paramilitary personnel may form service associations — analogous to trade unions — has been repeatedly raised and consistently resisted by the government on Article 33 grounds. The Supreme Court's directions on permanent commission for women officers have opened further examination of whether gender-based restrictions in service law are within Article 33's authorisation.

The expansion of paramilitary forces into counter-insurgency and border-management roles has sharpened questions about the extent to which Article 33 can justify restrictions on the rights of personnel that go beyond discipline into areas such as freedom from arbitrary detention or access to legal counsel. The Armed Forces Tribunal Act 2007 created a statutory appellate structure for service law disputes, and litigation before the AFT continuously tests the outer limits of parliamentary restrictions authorised under Article 33.
