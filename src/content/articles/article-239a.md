## Why it matters

Article 239A is the constitutional hinge that permits Parliament to introduce representative governance into a Union territory without triggering the full constitutional amendment procedure under Article 368. The default position under Article 239 is presidential administration through an administrator, with no elected local institutions. Article 239A creates a carve-out: for Puducherry (previously called Pondicherry), Parliament can by ordinary legislation establish an elected legislature, a council of ministers, or both, and can confer powers and functions on those bodies. This is an unusual constitutional mechanism because clause (2) explicitly provides that such legislation is not an amendment to the Constitution for the purposes of Article 368, even if it contains provisions that amend or have the effect of amending the Constitution. Parliament used this power to enact the Government of Union Territories Act, 1963, which established the Puducherry Legislative Assembly and Council of Ministers.

The provision reflects the practical reality of Puducherry's history: a former French colonial territory with a distinct political culture and electorate, merged into India in 1954 and formally ceded in 1962, for which some form of representative governance was considered necessary without conferring full statehood. The French enclaves had their own administrative tradition, and the population expected elected representation. Article 239A offered a constitutionally economical route: Parliament could act by ordinary legislation and could calibrate the powers of the new institutions to fit the UT framework, without having to go through the laborious process of a constitutional amendment that would have been needed to actually make Puducherry a State.

The significance of Article 239A extends well beyond Puducherry because it served as a conceptual model for what came later. When Parliament created legislative institutions for Delhi — initially through ordinary legislation and then through the 69th Constitutional Amendment in 1991 which inserted Article 239AA — it was building on the precedent that Union territories could have representative governance tailored by Parliament. The contrast between the Article 239A route (ordinary law) and the Article 239AA route (constitutional amendment establishing a "National Capital Territory" with its own special status) is itself instructive: Delhi required a full constitutional amendment because the stakes — the national capital's governance — were higher and the structure needed to be constitutionally entrenched rather than alterable by ordinary legislation.

The clause (2) deeming provision is constitutionally remarkable. It allows Parliament to use ordinary legislative procedure to make provisions that would otherwise require a constitutional amendment under Article 368. This is a deliberate grant of extraordinary legislative flexibility, acknowledging that fitting elected institutions into a UT framework may require adjustments to constitutional provisions that in any other context would demand the special amendment process. The provision has no direct parallel elsewhere in the Constitution and reflects the framers' pragmatic approach to the unique governance challenges of the Union territory category.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) sets out what Parliament may do: create for Puducherry either a body to function as a legislature (whether wholly elected or partly nominated and partly elected), or a Council of Ministers, or both. The law must specify the constitution, powers, and functions of the body it creates. This means the legislation itself — not the Constitution directly — determines how the Puducherry legislature operates, what subjects it can legislate on, the size of the house, reservation provisions, and the relationship between the council of ministers and the administrator. The Government of Union Territories Act, 1963 is the principal statute exercising this power for Puducherry.

Clause (2) addresses a structural difficulty: any law Parliament makes under clause (1) may need to modify existing constitutional provisions to make the new institutions work within the UT framework. For instance, provisions about the legislature's composition or the conditions under which the administrator can act might need to be adjusted. Without clause (2), such adjustments could be challenged as unconstitutional unless passed by the special majority and State ratification required by Article 368. Clause (2) forecloses that challenge by declaring that even if a clause (1) law amends or has the effect of amending the Constitution, it is not to be treated as a constitutional amendment for Article 368 purposes. This gives Parliament significant flexibility to design the legislative framework for Puducherry without procedural constraints beyond those of ordinary legislation.

It is important to note that Article 239A now applies only to Puducherry. The provision was originally capable of wider application — the original text referred to "such Union territories as Parliament may by law specify" — but successive amendments narrowed it to Puducherry specifically. Delhi was given its own tailored constitutional provision in the form of Article 239AA. The deliberate limitation of Article 239A to Puducherry after Delhi received its dedicated provision reflects a constitutional policy that governance arrangements for major or sensitive UTs should be individually specified at the constitutional level rather than left to Parliament's legislative discretion under a general enabling provision.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**Kiran Bedi v. Committee of Inquiry (1976)** — The Supreme Court considered the constitutional framework governing Puducherry and the relationship between the administrator and the elected government established under the Government of Union Territories Act, 1963. The ruling examined how the general UT administration structure under Article 239 interacts with the legislature and executive created under Article 239A, and clarified that the administrator's supervisory role does not extinguish the legislative and executive autonomy conferred on elected institutions by Parliament's law. This case established an early framework for understanding that a UT legislature created under Article 239A occupies a distinct constitutional space — not fully autonomous like a State government, but not a mere advisory body either.

**The 69th Constitutional Amendment and the NCT of Delhi Act, 1991** — While not a court case, the legislative history is crucial to understanding Article 239A's limits. When Parliament sought to give Delhi a stronger governance framework, it recognised that an ordinary Article 239A-type statute would not provide sufficient constitutional stability for the national capital. The result was the 69th Amendment (1991), which inserted Article 239AA into the Constitution itself, creating the National Capital Territory of Delhi with a constitutionally entrenched legislative assembly, a council of ministers collectively responsible to that assembly, and a specially defined relationship between the Lieutenant Governor and the elected government. The Government of NCT of Delhi Act, 1991 was enacted alongside as the implementing statute. The distinction between the Article 239A route taken for Puducherry and the Article 239AA route taken for Delhi marks the constitutional difference between a UT whose representative governance can be shaped by Parliament at any time and a UT whose governance architecture is constitutionally protected.

**NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018)** — This landmark five-judge Constitution Bench judgment addressed the distribution of powers between the Lieutenant Governor of Delhi and the elected Government of NCT of Delhi under Article 239AA. While it turns on Article 239AA rather than Article 239A, it is indispensable context because the structural question — how much real authority does the elected government have when the centrally appointed administrator retains supervisory powers — is identical to the question that arises in Puducherry under Article 239A. The Supreme Court held that the LG is bound by the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers on matters within the legislative competence of the Delhi assembly, and that the LG cannot act independently except on matters expressly reserved for the President or falling outside the assembly's competence. This principle, though decided under Article 239AA, shapes how courts and administrators interpret the relationship between elected governments and administrators in other UTs with legislatures, including Puducherry.

## Current significance

Puducherry remains the primary operative territory for Article 239A, and the tension between its elected government and the centrally appointed Lieutenant Governor continues to produce constitutional friction. The Puducherry government has on several occasions challenged the LG's exercise of powers in a manner that bypasses the council of ministers — including instances where the LG has withheld assent to Bills passed by the assembly or has issued independent executive orders on subjects within the assembly's legislative competence. These disputes closely parallel the Delhi governance controversy and are influenced by the principles the Supreme Court articulated in the NCT of Delhi case, even though Puducherry's framework rests on ordinary statute rather than constitutional provision.

The Puducherry assembly's legislative competence is defined by the Government of Union Territories Act, 1963, which broadly mirrors the State List and Concurrent List subjects but with significant exceptions: law and order, land, and certain other subjects are excluded from the assembly's purview and remain with the central government and the administrator. This narrower legislative domain compared to a full State means that the elected government operates under tighter constraints, and disagreements about whether a given matter falls within the assembly's competence or the administrator's reserve power are a recurrent source of conflict. The ongoing question of Puducherry's aspiration for statehood — which would remove the administrator's override and give the territory a full State government — keeps Article 239A in political focus.

Article 239A also serves as a structural reference point for the broader question of how Parliament can tailor governance arrangements for specific UTs without constitutional amendment. As India's UT map has evolved — most recently with the reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir into two UTs in 2019, one of which (J&K) was subsequently given a legislative assembly by Parliament — the precedent of Article 239A shows that the Constitution permits representative governance in a UT to be created and calibrated by Parliament through ordinary legislation, subject to the understanding that stronger or more stable arrangements require the constitutional amendment route. The Article 239A model of parliamentary flexibility, constrained by the Article 239AA model of constitutional entrenchment, together define the range of governance structures available for India's Union territories.
