## What this Article does

Article 316 is the foundational appointment and tenure provision for every Public Service Commission established under Part XIV of the Constitution. It determines who may appoint commission members, how long those members serve, and — crucially — that they cannot be reappointed once their term expires. Together these rules are designed to place the UPSC and every SPSC beyond routine political reach, so that recruitment to public services can be conducted on the basis of merit rather than ministerial preference.

The Union Public Service Commission is the central recruitment body for Group A and Group B central services, including the all-India services — Indian Administrative Service, Indian Police Service, and Indian Foreign Service. State Public Service Commissions perform the equivalent function for State cadres. Joint Commissions may be established by Parliament under Article 315(2) to serve two or more States, with the same appointment and tenure rules applying.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Appointment.** The President appoints the Chairman and members of the UPSC and any Joint Commission. The Governor of a State appoints the Chairman and members of an SPSC. This vertical separation — constitutional heads of the executive rather than ministers of the relevant department — marks the commission as a constitutional body rather than a departmental one. The appointment power sitting with the President or Governor signals that selections should be free from routine ministerial direction.

The composition proviso requires that as nearly as may be one-half of the members have held government office for at least ten years at the date of appointment. This blended-experience requirement ensures commissions include both career-administration expertise and outside perspectives. Service under the Crown in India before 1950 counts toward the ten-year threshold, a transitional concession in the original Constitution that was consequential for the early appointments.

**Clause (1A)**, added by the Constitution (Fifteenth Amendment) Act, 1963, provides for acting arrangements when the Chairman's office is vacant or the Chairman is incapacitated. The President (for UPSC or Joint Commissions) or the Governor (for an SPSC) designates one of the sitting members to discharge the Chairman's duties until the vacancy is filled or the Chairman resumes.

**Clause (2) — Term of office.** The term is six years from entry on office, or — for UPSC members — until age 65, and for SPSC or Joint Commission members until age 62, whichever comes first. The retirement age of 62 for State commission members aligns with High Court retirement ages, creating symmetry across constitutional bodies at the State level.

**Clause (3) — Bar on re-appointment.** A person whose term as a commission member expires is absolutely ineligible for re-appointment to that commission. This no-reappointment rule is the institutional centrepiece of the article: a member who knows that re-appointment is impossible has no structural incentive to please the appointing authority. The bar applies to re-appointment on the same commission; Article 319, however, imposes broader post-service restrictions on further government employment.

## The Madan Mohan Punchhi controversy

The tension between appointment discretion and independence surfaced prominently in the appointment of Justice Madan Mohan Punchhi as UPSC Chairman in 1998, shortly after his retirement as Chief Justice of India. Critics argued that appointing a retired Supreme Court judge who had served within the last few months as the country's top judicial officer — and who retained institutional ties to ongoing controversies — blurred the line between judicial independence and the executive's power to reward. The episode prompted debate in Parliament and among constitutional scholars about whether Article 316 should be read in light of broader norms preventing revolving-door movement between senior constitutional offices and commissions. The appointment was not struck down, but it intensified calls for a transparent selection process governed by statute rather than presidential discretion alone.

## Key cases

**State of Assam v Raghava Rajagopalachari (1966)** — The Supreme Court held that the UPSC's constitutional status derives from the recruitment function entrusted to it by Articles 315-323 and that any rules framed by the Union or a State under Article 309 must be consistent with those articles; a UPSC selection once completed could not be overridden by an ad hoc executive order.

**B S Yadav v State of Haryana (1981)** — While primarily a case about the All India Services, the Supreme Court stressed that commission recommendations following a proper selection process must ordinarily be followed by the Government, and that departures from a Commission's merit list required clear justification — the independence secured by Articles 316-317 would be hollow if the executive could routinely override selection decisions.

**A K Kraipak v Union of India (1969)** — The Supreme Court held that a selection board operating in exercise of statutory functions was subject to principles of natural justice, even before formal appointment. The reasoning, which treated governmental selection processes as quasi-judicial in nature, directly informed subsequent understanding of the standards applicable to UPSC-conducted selections.

**Secretary, UPSC v Papiah (1997)** — The Supreme Court reiterated that the UPSC's independence is a constitutional guarantee and that Art. 316's tenure protections are part of a coherent scheme: removal only by Supreme Court enquiry under Art. 317, fixed terms without reappointment, and salaries charged to the Consolidated Fund together constitute an insulation package.

## Article 319 — the post-service bar

Article 316 ends a member's term and bars re-appointment to the same commission, but Article 319 completes the independence architecture by restricting further government employment after the term. The Chairman of the UPSC is ineligible for any further employment under the Government of India or any State. Other UPSC members are eligible to be appointed as the Chairman of that Commission or of a State Commission but are ineligible for any other government employment. SPSC Chairmen and members are eligible to be Chairman or member of the UPSC or another SPSC, but are ineligible for other government service. These post-service restrictions reinforce the no-reappointment bar in Article 316(3) and prevent the "golden parachute" problem — a member angling for a post-retirement government appointment by making decisions favourable to the appointing ministry.

## Current significance

Article 316's independence architecture remains foundational to the Indian civil service system. The UPSC conducts the Civil Services Examination annually for thousands of posts, and its constitutional insulation from ministerial direction is the reason its results carry public legitimacy. Recurring debates about the UPSC include the extent to which lateral entry of private-sector professionals into senior civil service posts bypasses the commission — an arrangement that does not violate Article 316 as long as the lateral posts are not notified to the commission, but which raises questions about the merit-selection norms the article was designed to protect.

At the State level, the quality and independence of SPSCs has been uneven. Several commissions have been embroiled in recruitment scandals, prompting High Court interventions. The institutional design of Article 316 provides the tools — fixed terms, no-reappointment, presidential or gubernatorial appointment — but enforcement of those norms depends on the political culture surrounding each commission. Article 317's removal mechanism, requiring a Supreme Court inquiry, has been invoked sparingly; the more common form of accountability is High Court judicial review of selection decisions.
