## What this Article does — and why it is inoperative

Article 124B was inserted into the Constitution by the Constitution (Ninety-ninth Amendment) Act, 2014, as part of a package of three new articles (124A, 124B, 124C) establishing the National Judicial Appointments Commission. It set out the NJAC's three core functions: recommending appointments to the Supreme Court and High Courts, recommending transfers of High Court judges, and the integrity-and-ability gatekeeping function in clause (c).

The article is constitutionally inoperative. In Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015) — the NJAC case — a five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court struck down the 99th Amendment and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act, 2014, as unconstitutional. The Court held that the amendment violated the basic structure of the Constitution by impairing judicial independence. Articles 124A, 124B, and 124C were declared void.

The text of these articles remains printed in official editions of the Constitution — since the Supreme Court cannot formally remove constitutional text — but they have no legal effect. The pre-amendment collegium system was restored.

## The three functions defined in Article 124B

**Clause (a): Appointments.** The NJAC was to recommend persons for appointment as Chief Justice of India, other Supreme Court judges, Chief Justices of High Courts, and other High Court judges. This consolidated the appointment function that had been exercised by the collegium since the Second Judges case (1993) and the Third Judges case (1998).

**Clause (b): Transfers.** The NJAC was also to recommend the transfer of Chief Justices and other judges between High Courts. Transfers of judges had been a contentious area because they had historically been used by the executive as a tool to discipline judges who passed unfavourable orders. The collegium system had brought transfers under judicial primacy; the NJAC framework would have involved the executive-nominated members in transfer decisions.

**Clause (c): Ability and integrity.** This residual duty to "ensure" that recommended persons possess ability and integrity was the most conceptually significant clause. It was the basis for the veto provisions in the NJAC Act that allowed any two of the six NJAC members (which would include the Law Minister and two eminent persons) to block a recommendation. The Supreme Court found this veto — which could be exercised by non-judicial members — to be the central structural defect that violated judicial independence.

## The 99th Amendment package

Article 124B cannot be understood in isolation from Article 124A (which constituted the NJAC) and Article 124C (which empowered Parliament to legislate the procedure). Together they replaced the collegium system with a commission that included the Chief Justice of India, two senior Supreme Court judges, the Union Minister for Law and Justice, and two eminent persons nominated by the Prime Minister, Chief Justice, and Leader of the Opposition. The inclusion of the Law Minister as a voting member with a potential veto was the specific provision that the Court found to undermine judicial independence.

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015) — the NJAC case:** The Constitution Bench (4:1) struck down the 99th Amendment and the NJAC Act. The majority held that judicial independence is a basic feature of the Constitution, that the collegium system — though acknowledged to have deficiencies — embodied that independence, and that the NJAC's composition gave the executive and non-judicial members a structural ability to veto judicial appointments in a manner inconsistent with the basic structure. Justice J. Chelameswar dissented, finding the collegium system equally flawed and the NJAC to be a constitutionally valid improvement.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (1993) — the Second Judges case:** The original judgment that established judicial primacy in appointments through the collegium system. The NJAC amendment was Parliament's legislative response to decades of criticism of the collegium, making the Second Judges case the direct predecessor to the NJAC controversy.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1998 (Third Judges case):** The advisory opinion that expanded the collegium to include the CJI and four senior-most judges for Supreme Court appointments. The NJAC was designed to replace this regime; its invalidation reinstated it.

## The inoperative-text problem

After the NJAC judgment, India has an unusual constitutional situation: Articles 124A, 124B, and 124C exist in the printed text of the Constitution but have no legal effect. They are sometimes described as "dead letter" provisions. This raises questions about the relationship between constitutional text and constitutional law — the text records an amendment that was validly enacted but was subsequently voided by judicial review on basic-structure grounds. The provisions serve as a constitutional record of the political history and as a reminder that the debate about judicial appointment reform is unresolved.

## Current significance

The NJAC judgment restored the collegium but did not silence demands for reform. Successive Chief Justices and the Law Ministry have engaged in dialogue about making the collegium more transparent and accountable. The Memorandum of Procedure (MoP) governing collegium appointments remains unrevised. Article 124B, though inoperative, continues to represent the political consensus — reflected in the unanimous passage of the 99th Amendment in both Houses — that the collegium system as it existed was inadequate. Any future reform of judicial appointments will need to navigate the basic-structure constraints that the NJAC judgment identified, making Article 124B a significant reference point even in its inoperative state.
