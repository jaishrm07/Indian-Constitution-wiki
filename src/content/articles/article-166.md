  166. Conduct of Business of the Government of a State.—(1) All executive action of the Government of a State shall be expressed to be taken in the name of the Governor.

  (2) Orders and other instruments made and executed in the name of the Governor shall be authenticated in such manner as may be specified in rules to be made by the Governor, and the validity of an order or instrument which is so authenticated shall not be called in question on the ground that it is not an order or instrument made or executed by the Governor.

## Why it matters

Article 166 is the procedural constitutional law of the State executive.

It translates the abstract executive power vested in the Governor by Article 154 into a concrete legal regime for government acts. It does three things:

First, it requires that all State executive action be expressed through the Governor's name — creating a single, constitutionally identifiable source of legal authority for everything the State government does.

Second, it enables authenticated government orders to be legally valid without the Governor's personal signature — a practical necessity given the volume of orders a State government issues daily.

Third, it requires the Governor to make Rules of Business that allocate executive responsibilities among Ministers. These Rules of Business are the internal constitutional law of every State government: they specify which minister has authority over which subject, what level of decision requires Cabinet approval, and the form in which authenticated orders must be issued. Courts treat non-compliance with the Rules of Business as potentially vitiating an executive order altogether.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) requires all State executive action to be "expressed to be taken in the name of the Governor." This is a formal legal requirement, not a substantive one — it does not mean the Governor personally approves each decision. The purpose is to create a single, constitutionally identifiable source of State executive authority and to ensure that all State government action is traceable to a recognised constitutional head.

Clause (2) provides that properly authenticated orders are valid even if they are not personally executed by the Governor, preventing a cascade of invalidity challenges based purely on the Governor's non-involvement in the mechanics of order-making. The authentication method is set by rules made by the Governor, usually specifying which officer's countersignature suffices. Where authentication requirements are met, courts will not invalidate an order solely because the Governor did not personally execute it.

Clause (3) is the mandate for the Rules of Business — the Governor must make rules for "the more convenient transaction of the business of the Government of the State" and for portfolio allocation. These rules are the internal constitutional document of State governance, and their violation can be judicially reviewed. The phrase "in so far as it is not business with respect to which the Governor is by or under this Constitution required to act in his discretion" preserves the Governor's limited discretionary domain from being allocated by the rules to a minister — a necessary protection to prevent the Cabinet from using the Rules of Business to colonise even the narrow space of genuine gubernatorial discretion.

## Key cases

**Rai Sahib Ram Jawaya Kapur v State of Punjab (1955)** — One of the earliest Supreme Court rulings on State executive power, this case established that executive action exercised under the business rules made under Article 166(3) must conform to the allocation those rules prescribe. Action taken by a minister outside his allocated portfolio, or without the Cabinet approval that the Rules require for certain decisions, can be ultra vires. The Court established the foundational principle that the Rules of Business are not merely administrative documents but constitutional constraints on valid executive action.

**T. Venkata Reddy v State of Andhra Pradesh (1985)** — The Court distinguished between the authentication requirement in Article 166(2), which it held to be directory in the sense that mere authentication failure does not automatically void an otherwise competent order, and compliance with the Rules of Business allocation under Article 166(3), which it treated as substantively mandatory. The judgment clarified that a procedurally deficient order and a substantively unauthorised order are constitutionally different categories under Article 166.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v Jogendra Singh (1963)** — The Supreme Court reiterated that all executive acts of the State must satisfy the formal requirements of Article 166(1) and that government action that cannot be traced to a properly authenticated order expressed in the Governor's name is not a valid exercise of State executive power, a principle that courts have applied to challenge administrative orders made through informal channels.

## The Rules of Business in practice

Every State government operates under a set of Rules of Business and Allocation of Business Rules framed under Article 166(3). These rules typically specify: (a) which subjects fall within each Ministry's portfolio; (b) which categories of decision require individual Ministerial approval, which require the Chief Minister's approval, and which require full Cabinet decision; (c) the procedure for inter-ministerial consultation; and (d) the form of orders and the officers authorised to authenticate them on behalf of the Governor. Courts have used these rules as a yardstick for reviewing executive action: if an order on a Cabinet-reserved subject was issued by a joint secretary without Cabinet consideration, it fails the Article 166(3) test. High Courts across India have regularly applied this principle to strike down State executive orders in land acquisition, transfer and posting, and regulatory licensing on the ground of non-compliance with the applicable Rules of Business.

## Current significance

The Rules of Business framed under Article 166(3) have become a litigation battleground in States where governance structures are contested. High Courts and the Supreme Court have struck down executive orders passed by individual ministers where the applicable Rules required Cabinet decision or Chief Minister approval. The article has also been invoked in disputes between Governors and elected governments where Governors have declined to authenticate, or have delayed authentication of, Cabinet-approved orders — raising the question of whether the Governor's authentication role under clause (1) carries any independent discretion. The consistent judicial answer has been no: once the Cabinet has authorised an action, the Governor's name is a constitutional formality, and refusal to lend it is itself a violation of the constitutional framework established by Articles 154, 163, and 166 read together. The Supreme Court in its 2023 rulings on gubernatorial delays in assenting to State Bills implicitly treated the Article 166(1) authentication function as one that must flow from the Cabinet's authorisation, reinforcing the non-discretionary character of the Governor's role in the formal execution of State government acts.
