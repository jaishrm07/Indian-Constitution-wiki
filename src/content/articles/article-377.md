## What this Article did

Article 377 is the transitional provision for the Comptroller and Auditor-General (CAG) of India. The Constitution's Article 148 created the office of Comptroller and Auditor-General and specified its terms, conditions, and constitutional status. But when the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950, there was already an Auditor-General of India holding office under the earlier legal framework.

Article 377 resolved the transition by providing that the person holding the office of Auditor-General of India immediately before the Constitution's commencement would, unless he elected otherwise, automatically become the Comptroller and Auditor-General of India on that date. He became entitled to the salary and service rights specified in Article 148(3). Importantly, he could continue in office until the expiration of his term as originally determined under the pre-constitutional provisions applicable to him, rather than needing to be reappointed under the new constitutional process.

The redesignation from "Auditor-General" to "Comptroller and Auditor-General" was significant: the Constitution enhanced the office by giving it a broader remit over government accounts and by providing for a stronger form of security of tenure (removal only by an address of both Houses of Parliament).

## Historical context

The office of Auditor-General of India had existed under the Government of India Act, 1935 and its predecessors as the principal auditing authority for government accounts. The Auditor-General certified public accounts and reported to Parliament (then the Central Legislature). Under the new Constitution, the Comptroller and Auditor-General was envisioned as a constitutional guardian of financial accountability — independent, secure in tenure, and reporting directly to Parliament.

V. Narahari Rao was the Auditor-General of India at the time the Constitution came into force. Article 377 ensured his continuation in the new constitutional role without interruption, preserving institutional memory and operational continuity in the audit function at a moment when the government's accounts were being restructured from the dominion to the republic framework.

The provision reflects the same philosophy as Articles 375, 376, and 378 in Part XXI: constitutional offices newly created or reconstituted by the Constitution would be filled initially by their incumbents, avoiding the institutional disruption of a fresh appointment process at the moment of commencement.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional litigation arose specifically from Article 377. The transition of the Auditor-General to the Comptroller and Auditor-General was seamless, and no legal challenge to the authority or status of the continuing officeholder appears in the reported case law.

**Comptroller and Auditor-General of India v K S Jagannathan (1986) 2 SCC 679** — The Supreme Court considered the powers and functions of the CAG, affirming the independence and constitutional status of the office. This later judgment illustrates the importance of the strong constitutional framework for the CAG that the transitional provision in Article 377 helped inaugurate, by ensuring continuity of the institution through the constitutional transition.

## Current significance

Article 377 is spent. The person it applied to has long since retired. The provision's significance today is in demonstrating how the Constitution managed the transition of key constitutional offices. The Comptroller and Auditor-General remains one of India's most important constitutional watchdog institutions, and its uninterrupted continuity from the Auditor-General of the Dominion era is partly attributable to the smooth transition engineered by Article 377. The article also illustrates a broader pattern in Part XXI: the framers were attentive to the service rights of incumbents, protecting their salaries and tenure conditions even as those conditions were placed on a new constitutional footing.

## Why it matters

The transition of the Auditor-General to the Comptroller and Auditor-General was more than a name change. The new Constitution gave the CAG a constitutionally entrenched status that the pre-constitutional office had lacked. Under the Government of India Act, 1935, the Auditor-General held office at the pleasure of the Crown. Under the new Constitution, the CAG could only be removed by an address of both Houses of Parliament, giving the office genuine independence from executive pressure. Article 377 ensured that this constitutional enhancement applied immediately to the sitting officeholder, transforming the legal basis of the office without requiring a break in continuity.

The CAG's constitutional status under Article 148 — removed only by the same process as a Supreme Court judge, reporting directly to Parliament — makes the office one of India's most significant accountability mechanisms. The seamless transition created by Article 377 meant that this institution was fully operative and independent from 26 January 1950, with no administrative gap or period of diminished authority during the constitutional transition. The provision thus helped establish the strong institutional culture of independence that has characterised the CAG's office throughout the Republic's history.
