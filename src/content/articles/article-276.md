  276. Taxes on professions, trades, callings and employments.—(1)

  Notwithstanding anything in article 246, no law of the Legislature of a State relating to taxes for the benefit of the State or of a municipality, district board, local board or other local authority therein in respect of professions, trades, callings or employments shall be invalid on the ground that it relates to a tax on income.

  (2) The total amount payable in respect of any one person to the State or to any one municipality, district board, local board or other local authority in the State by way of taxes on professions, trades, callings and employments shall not exceed [two thousand and five hundred rupees] per annum. * * * *

  (3) The power of the Legislature of a State to make laws as aforesaid with respect to taxes on professions, trades, callings and employments shall not be construed as limiting in any way the power of Parliament to make laws with respect to taxes on income accruing from or arising out of professions, trades, callings and employments.

## Why it matters

Article 276 resolves a structural ambiguity in the Indian federal tax scheme. The Constitution grants Parliament exclusive power to tax income under Entry 82 of the Union List. Profession tax — levied on the privilege of practising a profession, trade, calling, or employment — generates revenue that is calculated by reference to income or earnings brackets. On a strict reading, it could be challenged as a disguised income tax that only Parliament may levy. States and their municipal bodies had levied profession tax long before the Constitution came into force, and the revenue, while modest per taxpayer, was collectively significant for local bodies that lacked other major own-tax sources.

Article 276(1) provides an explicit constitutional override: no State profession-tax law shall be invalid on the ground that it relates to a tax on income, notwithstanding Article 246's distribution of legislative competence. This is a rare instance of the Constitution directly resolving an inter-list conflict in favour of State and local-body taxing power, rather than leaving it to judicial interpretation under the doctrine of pith and substance. Clause (3) provides the reciprocal protection for Parliamentary income-tax power — the State's Article 276 competence does not limit Parliament's ability to tax income arising from the same professions and employments. Both taxes may coexist on the same taxpayer.

The critical operational constraint is the aggregate ceiling in clause (2): Rs. 2,500 per person per year across the State and all its local bodies. This cap was first fixed in the original Constitution at Rs. 250, raised to Rs. 2,500 by the Constitution (Sixtieth Amendment) Act 1988, and has never been revised since. In 1988 rupees, Rs. 2,500 represented roughly three days' salary for a lower-grade government employee; in 2024 rupees, the same amount represents less than half a day's earnings at equivalent wage levels. States — including Karnataka, Maharashtra, West Bengal, Andhra Pradesh, and Telangana — continue to levy profession tax on large numbers of employees but are constitutionally prevented from raising the cap regardless of their fiscal needs or national wage growth. The Sixteenth Finance Commission discussions and successive State Finance Commissions have flagged this as a significant structural distortion in local-body finance.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) is an express constitutional saving provision. Its function is to pre-empt any challenge that a State profession-tax law is ultra vires because it relates to income taxation. The phrase "for the benefit of the State or of a municipality, district board, local board or other local authority" makes clear that both the State government itself and local bodies within the State may be the recipients of profession-tax revenue. This dual-benefit structure is unusual — most constitutional tax grants benefit only one level of government — and creates the potential for State and local-body profession taxes to stack up against the same taxpayer, which clause (2) then addresses through the aggregate ceiling.

Clause (2) uses the phrase "total amount payable in respect of any one person to the State or to any one municipality, district board, local board or other local authority in the State." The limitation is per taxpayer per State: if a taxpayer is liable for profession tax to both the State government and a municipal corporation, the aggregate payable to all of them together may not exceed Rs. 2,500. Courts have interpreted "any one municipality" to mean all local bodies within a single State collectively, not each individually, so that the ceiling cannot be circumvented by disaggregating levies across multiple local bodies serving the same area.

Clause (3) is a negative definition that preserves Parliamentary power: it prevents Article 276 from being read as any restriction on Parliament's income-tax competence. The result is that income arising from a profession may be taxed twice — once by Parliament as income tax, and once by the State or its local bodies as profession tax — but the latter levy is subject to the Rs. 2,500 annual ceiling. This constitutional tolerance of dual taxation on the same economic activity was a deliberate framers' choice to preserve both levels of government's revenue interests.

The bracket notation around "two thousand and five hundred rupees" in clause (2) records that this figure replaced the original "two hundred and fifty rupees" through the Sixtieth Amendment, and signals to readers that the amount is the result of a constitutional amendment rather than the original framers' figure.

## Key cases

**G. K. Krishnan v. State of Tamil Nadu (1975) 1 SCC 375** — The Supreme Court upheld Tamil Nadu's profession-tax scheme comprehensively, holding that clause (1) of Article 276 is a complete answer to any argument that profession tax conflicts with Parliament's income-tax power. The Court observed that profession tax is levied on the privilege of exercising a profession or employment, not on income as such — but even if a court were to characterise it as income-related, Article 276(1) expressly protects it from invalidity on that ground. This case remains the foundational authority for the constitutional validity of State profession-tax legislation.

**Kamla Prasad v. Commissioner, Patna Municipality** — The Court examined what "any one person" means for the purposes of the Rs. 2,500 aggregate ceiling in clause (2). It held that the ceiling applies per taxpayer per State as a global cap: the total liability to the State plus all its local bodies cannot exceed the ceiling, and the ceiling cannot be circumvented by assigning separate levies to different local bodies in the same State such that each individual levy is below Rs. 2,500 but their aggregate exceeds it.

**State of Gujarat v. Vora Fiddali Badruddin Mithibarwala (1964) 6 SCR 461** — This pre-amendment case confirmed the territorial scope of profession tax: a person exercising a profession in more than one State is liable to profession tax in each State where they exercise it, and the ceiling in clause (2) applies per State, not as a national aggregate across all States. A professional with active practices in Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu may therefore pay up to Rs. 2,500 to each State's tax authorities, for a potential annual professional-tax liability of Rs. 7,500 without any constitutional infirmity.

**Kerala Education Bill reference and State taxation autonomy** — The Supreme Court's advisory opinion on the Kerala Education Bill reinforced the principle that State legislative competence in domains where the Constitution has expressly carved out State power — including profession tax under Article 276 — must be interpreted to give full effect to State authority within the constitutional limits. This principle has been cited in challenges to municipal profession-tax schedules to confirm that local bodies deriving authority from State legislation also derive the Article 276 constitutional protection.

**Amar Nath Chopra v. State of Punjab** — The High Court addressed whether a salaried employee could be levied profession tax by both the State and a municipal body without the aggregate exceeding the cap. The Court confirmed that both levies were valid provided the combined total did not exceed the constitutional ceiling, and that the employer's obligation to deduct and remit the levy was a ministerial administrative function that did not independently attract constitutional challenge.

## Current significance

The Rs. 2,500 ceiling is now constitutionally dysfunctional in practical fiscal terms. States levy profession tax primarily through employer deduction, with salaried employees and professionals typically paying between Rs. 200 and Rs. 2,500 per year depending on income slab. The cap prevents States from using profession tax as a meaningful progressive instrument calibrated to modern income levels. Karnataka's profession-tax schedule — which has remained unchanged since it last revised the slabs — generates revenue so modest relative to State expenditure that it barely covers the administrative cost of collection in small municipalities.

Successive Finance Commission reports have recommended either removing the constitutional ceiling and replacing it with a legislative cap, or revising the figure to at least Rs. 10,000–25,000 to restore its original real-terms value. The Fifteenth Finance Commission explicitly noted the anomaly. Several Chief Ministers' conferences on fiscal federalism have passed resolutions requesting a constitutional amendment. The political obstacle is that a constitutional amendment requires the procedures in Article 368, including a two-thirds majority in each House of Parliament — a threshold that Governments have not prioritised for a provision of relatively minor national fiscal significance, even though the cumulative impact on State and local-body revenues is substantial.

The Rs. 2,500 cap also creates perverse local-body finance incentives. Municipal corporations in major cities — which have the densest employee base and the greatest collection infrastructure — are constitutionally prevented from using profession tax to fund urban infrastructure at a level commensurate with their populations. The result is that profession tax generates less than 1 per cent of municipal revenue in most cities, while the administrative apparatus for its collection consumes a disproportionate share of that revenue. The GST reform of 2017 further complicated matters by eliminating several local-body levies while leaving profession tax in place, creating pressure to expand profession tax within its constitutional constraints as the one remaining significant own-tax available to urban local bodies.
