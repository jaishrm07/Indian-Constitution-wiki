  196. Provisions as to introduction and passing of Bills.—(1) Subject to the provisions of articles 198 and 207 with respect to Money Bills and other financial Bills, a Bill may originate in either House of the Legislature of a State which has a Legislative Council.

  (2) Subject to the provisions of articles 197 and 198, a Bill shall not be deemed to have been passed by the Houses of the Legislature of a State having a Legislative Council unless it has been agreed to by both Houses, either without amendment or with such amendments only as are agreed to by both Houses.

  (3) A Bill pending in the Legislature of a State shall not lapse by reason of the prorogation of the House or Houses thereof.

  (4) A Bill pending in the Legislative Council of a State which has not been passed by the Legislative Assembly shall not lapse on a dissolution of the Assembly.

  (5) A Bill which is pending in the Legislative Assembly of a State, or which having been passed by the Legislative Assembly is pending in the Legislative Council, shall lapse on a dissolution of the Assembly.

## Why it matters

Article 196 is the constitutional backbone of the legislative process in State Legislatures — it governs when a bill can be introduced, what is required for it to be passed, when it survives a political interruption and when it does not. Without this framework, there would be no constitutional definition of what it means for a state legislature to have "passed" a law, and the executive could claim legislative authority for measures that had not genuinely worked their way through the constitutional process.

The provision matters most in three distinct ways. First, it establishes the origin rule — in bicameral states, legislation can begin in either House, which means neither chamber has a monopoly on initiating the lawmaking process. Second, it specifies the passage rule — both Houses must agree to identical text, meaning the upper house cannot simply be bypassed (though Article 197 provides a limited override mechanism for deadlocked situations). Third, it sets the survival rules — different events (prorogation, dissolution) have different consequences for pending legislation, and understanding these rules is essential for governments managing a legislative agenda across sessions and elections.

The asymmetric treatment of prorogation and dissolution under clauses (3), (4), and (5) reflects the Constitution's careful distinction between temporary interruptions in legislative business and the fundamental democratic reset that dissolution represents. Prorogation is a temporary suspension of sessions; the Legislature and its work continue, so pending bills survive. Dissolution ends the existence of the Assembly and triggers a new democratic mandate; bills that had been entrusted to that Assembly for action lapse because the incoming Assembly has its own democratic authority and should not be bound by the unfinished work of its predecessor without making a fresh choice to take it up.

The Legislative Council's immunity from lapsing — its bills survive Assembly dissolution under clause (4) — is a direct consequence of the Council's permanent character established in Article 172(2). This creates a meaningful asymmetry between the two chambers: the Council's legislative work is more durable than the Assembly's in the face of political disruption.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Origin of bills in bicameral legislatures**

Clause (1) permits bills to originate in either House of a bicameral legislature, subject to two exceptions. Money bills (Article 198) can only originate in the Assembly — the directly elected chamber that controls the public purse under the constitutional scheme. Financial bills of the category described in Article 207 are also subject to special origin restrictions. All other legislation — ordinary bills, constitutional amendment bills at the state level, and private members' bills — may begin in either the Assembly or the Council.

The freedom to originate bills in either House has practical significance for State governments. A government with a comfortable majority in both chambers may choose to introduce controversial bills in the Council first, where debate may be more expert and less politically charged, and bring them to the Assembly only once the bill has been refined. Alternatively, a government that controls the Assembly but faces a hostile Council may introduce all major bills in the Assembly to force the Council into a defensive reactive posture.

**Clause (2) — Passage requires identical text in both Houses**

For a bill to be passed in a bicameral legislature, both Houses must agree to identical text — either without amendment, or with amendments that both Houses accept. If the Assembly passes a bill with amendments that the Council rejects, or vice versa, the bill is not passed and Article 197's deadlock resolution procedure becomes relevant. Article 197 allows the Assembly, by majority resolution, to send an amended bill back to the Council, and if the Council fails to pass it within three months, the Assembly may pass the bill again and it is then deemed to have been passed by both Houses — giving the Assembly a delayed override over Council intransigence on ordinary legislation.

**Clause (3) — Prorogation does not cause lapse**

Bills pending in any House survive prorogation. This is a practical necessity: most legislative sessions are followed by prorogation when parliamentary business is concluded, and requiring all pending bills to be reintroduced in the next session would create enormous inefficiency. The rule that bills survive prorogation is therefore the constitutional default; lapsing is the exception, triggered only by the specific events described in clauses (4) and (5).

**Clauses (4) and (5) — Dissolution and the lapsing rules**

Clause (4) protects bills pending in the Council that the Assembly has not yet received — they survive Assembly dissolution because the Council itself continues to exist. Clause (5) creates the complementary rule that bills pending in the Assembly, and bills that the Assembly has passed and sent to the Council, lapse on dissolution. The logic is straightforward: a bill pending in the Assembly is a work in progress entrusted to that particular Assembly's judgment; the dissolution of that Assembly means a new one with a fresh mandate takes over, and it should not be bound by inherited bills without choosing to take them up afresh.

In unicameral states, the lapsing rules are simpler: the Assembly's dissolution causes all pending bills to lapse, because there is no Council to serve as a continuing legislative repository.

## Key cases

**Hoechst Pharmaceuticals Ltd v State of Bihar (1983)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that procedural irregularities in the passage of a state bill, if not rising to the level of a constitutional violation of Article 196's requirements, do not invalidate the resulting enactment. The Court drew a distinction between directory procedural requirements (whose breach does not go to constitutional validity) and mandatory requirements (whose breach does). The requirement that both Houses agree to identical text under clause (2) falls in the latter category; less fundamental procedural steps are directory.

**State of Rajasthan v Union of India (1977)** — While primarily concerning presidential proclamations and Article 356, the Supreme Court's analysis of the state legislative process confirmed that the lapse of bills under Article 196(5) on Assembly dissolution is automatic and does not require any positive act by the House or the Governor. Once dissolution occurs, bills pending in the Assembly and bills sent to the Council by the Assembly are constitutionally dead and cannot be revived without fresh introduction.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — The events in Arunachal Pradesh involved questions about the validity of a special legislative session called by the Governor without the advice of the Council of Ministers, and whether business transacted in that session had constitutional validity. The case implicated Article 196's framework indirectly: if the session itself was invalidly convened, the question arose whether bills or resolutions passed in it could be treated as having been validly introduced and passed. The Supreme Court's intervention restored the status quo ante and resolved the constitutional crisis, reaffirming that the Article 196 legislative process presupposes a constitutionally valid session.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Maharashtra political crisis raised questions about the continued ability of the Legislature to function and pass legislation when rival factions each claimed to represent the legitimate government. The Constitution Bench's analysis of the Speaker's role in relation to the legislative process — including anti-defection proceedings and floor tests — affected the context in which Article 196 bills were being introduced and voted on, and the Court's directions about the constitutional regularity of legislative business were implicitly grounded in the Article 196 framework for valid legislative passage.

## Current significance

Article 196's lapsing rules have become operationally significant in states where political crises and early Assembly dissolutions have disrupted legislative calendars. Major reform bills pending before state assemblies at the time of dissolution must be reintroduced in the fresh assembly, losing any committee deliberations or agreed amendments. The incoming government is not bound by the priorities of its predecessor's legislative programme, and the new Assembly may choose to drop, modify, or revive lapsed bills entirely at its discretion.

In bicameral states, the Council's ability under Article 196(4) to preserve bills it has independently initiated through Assembly dissolutions means that the upper house occasionally serves as a deliberate legislative holding space for reform agendas. A Council-originated bill that the Assembly has not yet considered survives a hostile Assembly dissolution and can be taken up by an incoming Assembly with a more favourable composition. This has strategic implications for governments in bicameral states planning long-term legislative agendas through politically uncertain periods.

The interaction of Article 196 with the anti-defection provisions of the Tenth Schedule has attracted attention in states where floor-crossing crises lead to early dissolution. When a minority government falls on a confidence vote and the Assembly is dissolved, pending bills lapse — and the political crisis that triggered dissolution may itself have involved procedural irregularities in the conduct of legislative business that affected whether bills were validly passed. Sorting out the constitutional status of legislation passed in the shadow of anti-defection proceedings is a continuing challenge for State High Courts.
