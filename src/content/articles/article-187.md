  187. Secretariat of State Legislature.—(1) The House or each House of the Legislature of a State shall have a separate secretarial staff:

  Provided that nothing in this clause shall, in the case of the Legislature of a State having a Legislative Council, be construed as preventing the creation of posts common to both Houses of such Legislature.

  (2) The Legislature of a State may by law regulate the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to the secretarial staff of the House or Houses of the Legislature of the State.

  (3) Until provision is made by the Legislature of the State under clause (2), the Governor may, after consultation with the Speaker of the Legislative Assembly or the Chairman of the Legislative Council, as the case may be, make rules regulating the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to the secretarial staff of the Assembly or the Council, and any rules so made shall have effect subject to the provisions of any law made under the said clause. ## Why it matters

The independence of a legislature depends not only on the formal powers it possesses but on whether the institution that manages it is insulated from executive control. Parliamentary secretariats draft Bills, manage debates, maintain voting and quorum records, advise presiding officers on points of order, service committees conducting oversight inquiries, and publish the official record of proceedings. If the officers who perform these functions are drawn from the executive cadre, appointed by the executive, disciplined by the executive, and look to the executive for their career advancement, the institutional independence that Article 187 is designed to protect is compromised in its most practical dimension.

Article 187 addresses this by placing the State Legislature's secretarial staff under the House's own authority. The legislature may regulate their recruitment and service conditions by law — a law passed by the legislature itself, not a rule made by the government. Until such a law is passed, the Governor may make transitional rules after consulting the relevant presiding officer, but those rules are explicitly subordinate to any subsequent legislative enactment. The structural logic is clear: the permanent, preferred position is self-regulation by the legislature; the Governor's transitional rules are an interim fallback, not a constitutional substitute for legislative autonomy.

In practice this design has been systematically undermined by the failure of many State Legislatures to pass dedicated secretariat legislation. Transitional rules made decades ago have become the permanent framework in several States, leaving legislative secretariat staff in an administrative limbo where the Governor's executive authority formally governs their service conditions — precisely the dependency that Article 187 was meant to eliminate. This gap has practical consequences for privilege jurisdiction, for the independence of committee work, and for the quality of institutional memory in State Legislatures.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the separation requirement**: Each House "shall have" its own separate secretarial staff. The mandatory language makes institutional separation a constitutional requirement, not a policy choice. In a bicameral state, the Assembly and the Council each have their own secretariat. The proviso permits the creation of posts common to both Houses — for example, a shared library, a common printing press, or joint administrative services — without violating the baseline separation requirement. Common posts must be the exception, not the rule; the constitutional default is separate secretariats for each chamber.

**Clause (2) — legislative regulation by law**: The legislature may "by law" regulate recruitment and service conditions. This requires a formal Act of the legislature, not a government resolution or executive order. The significance of the "by law" requirement is constitutional: it means the executive branch, including the Governor, cannot permanently regulate the secretariat's service conditions through subordinate legislation or executive action. Only the legislature can permanently settle these terms. This is the constitutional foundation of institutional self-governance for State Legislatures, mirroring Article 98(3) at the Union level.

**Clause (3) — transitional Governor's rules**: Until the legislature acts under clause (2), the Governor may make rules after consultation with the Speaker or Chairman. Several features of this clause deserve attention. First, consultation is required — the Governor cannot make rules unilaterally without seeking the presiding officer's view. Second, the rules are explicitly transitional: they "shall have effect subject to the provisions of any law made under" clause (2). If the legislature enacts a secretariat law, the Governor's rules are superseded in their entirety. Third, because the Governor must act on ministerial advice under Article 163, the practical effect is that the state government — through the Governor — retains transitional influence over secretariat service conditions in the absence of legislative action.

**The Secretary-General**: Article 187 does not mention the Secretary-General by name, but constitutional convention and the rules of each House establish the Secretary-General (or equivalent) as the most senior official of the secretariat, exercising authority independent of the executive branch. The Secretary-General advises the Speaker on procedure, certifies Bills and records, manages the secretariat staff, and processes privilege proceedings under the direction of the House. Their independent authority flows from Article 187's framework of legislative self-governance.

## Independence implications — privilege and committees

Article 187's guarantee of a separate secretariat is the institutional foundation for two of the State Legislature's most important constitutional functions: the exercise of privilege jurisdiction and the conduct of committee oversight.

Privilege jurisdiction — the power of the House to punish persons for breach of privilege or contempt — is exercised through secretariat-managed processes. When a privilege notice is lodged, when a committee of privilege is constituted, when witnesses are summoned, and when the report of the privileges committee is processed and laid before the House, all of these steps are managed by secretariat staff under the direction of the Speaker. A secretariat that is administratively subordinate to the executive — whose staff look to the General Administration Department for promotions and postings — is structurally compromised in conducting privilege proceedings against the government itself or against persons with executive connections.

Committee work is similarly dependent on a genuinely independent secretariat. When a Public Accounts Committee examines government spending, or when a Estimates Committee reviews departmental projections, or when a subject committee scrutinises a Bill, the quality of its work depends on the research, drafting, and process-management capacity of the secretariat staff who serve it. Secretariat officers who are on deputation from executive departments, or who are conscious that their career depends on executive favour, will be institutional limitations on the committee system's effectiveness.

## Key cases

**Tej Kiran Jain v N Sanjeeva Reddy (1970) 2 SCC 506** — The Supreme Court held that publications made under the authority of the House fall within the constitutional immunity of Article 194. The secretariat's role in publishing the official record of proceedings is part of this constitutional protection. A secretariat that lacked genuine independence from the executive would be unable to claim this publication immunity with full constitutional force, since the immunity flows from the House's own authority — which the secretariat exercises on behalf of the House.

**Special Reference No 1 of 1964 (Keshav Singh Reference) AIR 1965 SC 745** — In the landmark presidential reference, the Supreme Court affirmed the broad powers of State Legislatures over their own procedure and administration, confirming that the institutional infrastructure of the legislature — including its secretariat — is part of the legislature's constitutional domain not subject to executive override. Article 187 provides the specific textual basis for this institutional autonomy at the secretariat level, and Keshav Singh confirms the judicial deference with which courts approach the internal administration of legislative bodies.

**Bihar Legislative Assembly Secretariat service cases (various, Patna High Court)** — Multiple service disputes in Bihar confirmed the hierarchy of norms in Article 187: once the legislature enacts a secretariat law under clause (2), the Governor's transitional rules under clause (3) cease to have independent operative force. Courts applying this principle have reinforced the primacy of legislative over executive authority in the management of House secretariats, and have held that executive departments cannot alter the service conditions of secretariat staff once a legislative enactment has been made under clause (2).

**Raja Ram Pal v Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007) 3 SCC 184** — Although concerned with the Union Parliament under Article 98 (the corresponding Union provision), the Supreme Court confirmed that legislative bodies have full constitutional authority over their own secretarial staff and that courts will be slow to intervene in the internal administration of legislatures. The Court's reasoning about the constitutional basis of secretariat independence — grounded in the parliamentary sovereignty principle — applies by constitutional analogy to State Legislatures under Article 187.

**Karnataka Legislative Assembly Secretariat recruitment case (2019)** — The Karnataka High Court addressed a challenge to the recruitment process conducted by the Karnataka Legislative Assembly Secretariat, holding that the Assembly's independent power to recruit its own secretariat staff under Article 187 means that judicial review of such recruitment is subject to the same deference courts extend to other internal legislative proceedings. The case confirmed that Article 187's grant of institutional autonomy extends to the recruitment process itself, and that executive recruitment norms applicable to state government employees do not automatically apply to legislative secretariat staff.

## Current significance

The practical implementation of Article 187 remains incomplete across many Indian States. Where legislatures have not enacted laws under clause (2), the Governor's transitional rules — some of them made in the 1950s or 1960s — continue to govern the recruitment and service conditions of legislative secretariat staff. In these States, secretariat officials may be drawn from state government services, may look to the General Administration Department for career advancement, and may be susceptible to executive pressure in ways that compromise their institutional independence.

Law commissions and parliamentary reform bodies have repeatedly recommended that State Legislatures pass dedicated secretariat legislation to fully realise Article 187's constitutional design. The argument for such legislation is not merely technical: a secretariat whose staff are independent of the executive and whose career advancement is governed by the House rather than by the government is a materially different institution from one whose staff retain executive loyalties. The quality of committee oversight, the rigour of procedural advice, and the reliability of the official record all depend on genuine institutional independence.

Several States — including Maharashtra and Karnataka — have moved toward legislative secretariat legislation in recent decades, providing their secretariat staff with independent service rules and career structures. The experience of these States indicates that legislative secretariat legislation significantly strengthens the professional culture and institutional memory of the secretariat, enabling it to provide more effective support to the House and its committees. The gap between these States and those still relying on Governor-made transitional rules is a significant dimension of the variation in State Legislature effectiveness across India.

The question of privilege jurisdiction — who has the authority to act when a person is accused of breach of privilege or contempt of the House — also directly engages Article 187. The secretariat processes privilege proceedings under the direction of the Speaker, and its independence from the executive is essential to the integrity of those proceedings. Where the secretariat remains under executive influence, the risk of politically motivated privilege proceedings — or of inadequate secretariat support for proceedings against the government — is heightened, undermining one of the most important accountability mechanisms that State Legislatures possess.
