## Why it matters

Article 243M reflects a considered political and constitutional judgment that the standard Panchayati Raj framework of Part IX cannot be automatically applied to areas with different historical, cultural, and legal traditions of governance. The exclusions fall into two conceptually distinct categories.

The first category — Scheduled Areas and tribal areas under Article 244 — represents territories where tribal customary law and traditional governance structures are constitutionally protected through the Fifth and Sixth Schedules. Applying the standard Gram Panchayat model directly to these areas would risk displacing or overriding those customary structures without adequate protection. The drafters of the 73rd Amendment were aware of this tension and therefore excluded these areas from automatic Part IX application while creating the mechanism in Article 243M(4)(b) for Parliament to extend Part IX with modifications. Parliament used this power in 1996 to enact PESA, which extends the Panchayati Raj framework to Scheduled Areas with substantial modifications that privilege Gram Sabha consent and tribal customary law.

The second category — Nagaland, Meghalaya, Mizoram, and the hill areas of Manipur — represents the Sixth Schedule areas of the North-East, where tribal governance through Autonomous District Councils and Village Councils has a strong historical basis under Article 244(2). These states have their own governance structures that predate and in some respects supersede the standard Panchayati Raj design. The exclusion of these states respects the constitutional guarantees already given to these regions.

## How courts use it

Courts use Article 243M primarily in two ways. First, they apply it as a jurisdictional filter: before any Part IX obligation or protection is invoked in a territory, the court must confirm that Article 243M does not exclude that territory. In practice, this is most litigated in states like Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, and Andhra Pradesh where Scheduled Area boundaries overlap with areas that have been subjected to both PESA-based Panchayat elections and standard Part IX elections.

Second, courts examine whether PESA — enacted under Article 243M(4)(b) — adequately carries out the constitutional mandate of the provision. PESA's legal status is clarified by Article 243M(4)(b) itself, which states that a parliamentary law extending Part IX to Scheduled Areas "shall not be deemed to be an amendment of this Constitution for the purposes of article 368." This means PESA is an ordinary parliamentary statute but is constitutionally authorised, so state laws that conflict with PESA's mandatory provisions are unconstitutional as violations of the Article 243M framework.

## Key cases

**Samatha v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1997)** — The Supreme Court's ruling on tribal land rights in Scheduled Areas is the foundational case on the governance of Scheduled Areas under the constitutional framework. The Court's strong emphasis on the protection of tribal rights and the primacy of the Gram Sabha in Scheduled Area governance reflects the constitutional intent underlying Article 243M: that the exclusion of Scheduled Areas from standard Panchayati Raj was not to leave them ungoverned but to require a more protective governance framework.

**Bharati v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2014)** — The Madhya Pradesh High Court addressed the relationship between PESA and standard state Panchayat laws in Scheduled Areas, holding that PESA's modifications prevail over state laws to the extent of inconsistency. The case applied the Article 243M(4)(b) framework to resolve a conflict between PESA's Gram Sabha consent requirements and a state land acquisition law that did not provide for such consent.

**Orissa Mining Corporation v. Ministry of Environment and Forests (2013)** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling on tribal consent for mining in Scheduled Areas applied the PESA framework (enacted under Article 243M(4)(b)) as the controlling constitutional standard. The Court's direction that the Gram Sabha's consent must be sought before forest clearance was granted reflected the purpose of Article 243M: that Scheduled Area governance must incorporate the consent and participation of tribal communities through constitutionally mandated local institutions.

## Current significance

The Article 243M framework continues to generate significant legal and policy controversy. PESA has been enacted for over twenty-five years but several states with large Scheduled Area populations have not brought their state Panchayat laws into conformity with PESA's requirements, creating a persistent gap between constitutional obligation and implementation. The Gram Sabha consent provisions of PESA — required under the Article 243M framework — are regularly bypassed in land acquisition, forest clearance, and mining cases, generating ongoing litigation.

In the North-East, the question of whether states like Nagaland or Meghalaya should use the Article 243M(4)(a) mechanism to voluntarily extend Part IX has been politically contested. Nagaland held its first urban local body elections only in 2023 after prolonged political resistance, and equivalent rural governance structures remain governed by traditional customary law rather than the constitutional Panchayati Raj framework. The Article 243M exclusion provides the constitutional basis for maintaining these alternative governance systems.
