  58. Qualifications for election as President.—(1) No person shall be eligible for election as President unless he—

  (a) is a citizen of India,

  (b) has completed the age of thirty-five years, and

  (c) is qualified for election as a member of the House of the People.

  (2) A person shall not be eligible for election as President if he holds any office of profit under the Government of India or the Government of any State or under any local or other authority subject to the control of any of the said Governments.

  Explanation.—For the purposes of this article, a person shall not be deemed to hold any office of profit by reason only that he is the President or Vice-President of the Union or the Governor of any State or is a Minister either for the Union or for any State.

## Why it matters

Article 58 reflects the Constituent Assembly's deliberate choice to keep the entry threshold for the Presidency broad and open rather than encumbered with specialist requirements. The framers could have imposed conditions like a prior track record in public service, a minimum period of residence in India, or a specific educational qualification. They rejected all such additions on the principle that the electoral college — Parliament and State Assemblies — was the appropriate screening mechanism. Elected representatives are better placed than a constitutional text to assess whether a candidate has the qualities to serve as President. The constitutional role of Article 58 is therefore not to define the ideal President but to set a constitutional floor below which no candidate should pass.

The citizenship requirement reflects the foundational principle that the head of state must owe undivided allegiance to the Indian Republic. The age requirement of thirty-five years balances youth eligibility with a reasonable expectation of constitutional maturity and life experience. The linkage to Lok Sabha qualification — rather than a separately enumerated set of presidential qualifications — creates a dynamic rather than static eligibility standard: any new disqualification Parliament adds to the Representation of the People Act, 1951, or any constitutional amendment to Article 102, automatically flows through to presidential candidacy without requiring a separate amendment to Article 58.

The office-of-profit bar in clause (2) is the provision's most operationally significant negative condition. It prevents serving public officials — civil servants, regulators, public sector executives, and judges — from standing for the Presidency while continuing to draw remuneration from the state they would be called upon to lead. The concern is dual: that a serving official would use state resources and institutional authority to advantage their campaign, and that voters in the electoral college might feel a degree of deference or institutional pressure toward a sitting superior. The bar removes both concerns by requiring disqualifying office-holders to relinquish their positions before entering the race.

The Explanation to clause (2) creates a constitutionally carved-out set of high office-holders for whom the office-of-profit bar would otherwise create a perverse result. Without the Explanation, a sitting Vice-President — constitutionally designated as the next in line to assume presidential functions — would need to resign the Vice-Presidency to stand in the presidential election. A sitting Governor would need to relinquish office. A Union Minister in a caretaker government would face disqualification. The Explanation resolves these anomalies by treating these positions as not constituting an "office of profit" for Article 58(2) purposes — a recognition that some public responsibilities are sufficiently elevated and distinct in character from ordinary profit-motivated public employment.

## Reading the provision

The three-part positive qualification in clause (1) operates cumulatively — all three must be satisfied simultaneously. The citizenship condition links to Part II of the Constitution and the Citizenship Act. The age condition is verified at the date of filing the nomination, not at the date of the election or the date of assuming office. The Lok Sabha qualification condition imports by reference the disqualifications in Article 102 (unsoundness of mind, undischarged insolvency, foreign citizenship, criminal conviction under certain laws) and those in the Representation of the People Act, 1951.

Clause (2)'s office-of-profit bar is explicitly subject to the Explanation, which narrows its scope. The Explanation lists specific offices — President, Vice-President, Governor, Union Minister, State Minister — and declares that holding any of these is not "by reason only" of that holding to be treated as an office of profit. The phrase "by reason only" is important: if a person holds one of these offices and simultaneously holds a separate income-generating position from a state-controlled entity, the combination might still fall within the bar even if the named office itself does not.

The nomination process in presidential elections gives the Article 58 qualifications immediate operational force. Each nomination paper must be subscribed by at least fifty members of the electoral college as proposers and fifty as seconders, and the Returning Officer scrutinises the nomination for eligibility. Any objection to a candidate's eligibility under Article 58 is adjudicated at this stage and, if not resolved, can eventually be the subject of a Supreme Court election petition under Article 71.

## Key cases

**Shibu Soren v Dayanand Sahay (2001)** — The Supreme Court examined the meaning of "office of profit" in a parliamentary disqualification context under Article 102; the principles inform Article 58(2) directly, since the phrase is shared. The Court emphasised that the test is whether the office carries remuneration from public funds and involves duties that could compromise independent functioning, not merely whether the holder receives any payment at all.

**Election Commission of India v Dr Subramaniam Swamy** — The Election Commission's scrutiny of nomination papers in presidential elections includes verification of the Article 58 qualifications. Courts have confirmed that the Supreme Court under Article 71 is the ultimate arbiter, but the Commission performs the initial gatekeeping function. The Commission's preliminary determination of eligibility is not a judicial act and does not bind the Supreme Court.

**Jyoti Basu's potential candidacy (1997) — political episode** — In 1997, the Left Front and several opposition parties considered fielding West Bengal Chief Minister Jyoti Basu as a consensus presidential candidate. A detailed examination of whether a Chief Minister's salary and allowances constituted an "office of profit" under Article 58(2) — and whether the Explanation's exemption for State Ministers extended to a Chief Minister — was conducted. The consensus view held that the Explanation covered Chief Ministers, who are Ministers of a State government, so Basu would have been eligible without resigning. Basu ultimately did not stand, citing political constraints, but the episode illustrated the practical operation of Article 58(2) and its Explanation at the level of real constitutional politics.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Court's analysis of presidential action touched on the qualifications of the incumbent and confirmed that constitutional qualifications for high office are not mere formalities — the electoral college process and the nomination scrutiny mechanism together ensure that Article 58's conditions are meaningfully applied before a person assumes the office.

**Neelam Devi v Union of India** — Challenges to presidential eligibility at the nomination stage confirmed that the Election Commission and ultimately the Supreme Court under Article 71 are the proper forums for such disputes. A candidate who has been declared ineligible at the nomination stage has recourse through Article 71 election petition proceedings rather than through a writ to the High Court, which lacks jurisdiction in presidential election matters.

**B.R. Kapur v State of Tamil Nadu (2001)** — The Supreme Court held that a person against whom charges have been framed by a court for offences carrying disqualifying penalties cannot be validly appointed to a high constitutional office requiring legislative eligibility. While the case arose in the context of Chief Ministerial appointment, the Court's reasoning is directly applicable to presidential candidacy: since Article 58(1)(c) ties presidential eligibility to Lok Sabha qualification, any disqualification under the Representation of the People Act that attaches upon conviction for a specified offence — or arguably upon charge framing under sufficiently serious provisions — would affect presidential eligibility.

**Pratibha Patil's candidacy (2007)** — The successful nomination and election of India's first woman President, Pratibha Patil, confirmed that the masculine pronoun "he" in Article 58's text is gender-inclusive under the General Clauses Act, 1897, and that no gender-specific bar exists at the constitutional level. The episode contributed to the practical understanding that Article 58's minimalist approach to qualifications is genuinely inclusive: neither gender, caste, nor community creates any additional constitutional bar beyond the three positive qualifications and the office-of-profit negative bar.

## Current significance

The Article 58 qualifications are tested at the nomination stage in every presidential election. The linkage to Lok Sabha eligibility means that changes in electoral disqualification law automatically affect presidential candidacy, creating an evolving eligibility standard without requiring constitutional amendment. The most active area of practical uncertainty involves the office-of-profit bar: as India's constitutional and statutory architecture has created numerous commissions, tribunals, regulators, and advisory bodies, the question of whether service on these bodies constitutes a disqualifying "office of profit" under Article 58(2) requires case-by-case analysis.

The Explanation's exemption for sitting Governors has taken on additional significance given the frequency with which Governors in recent years have become presidential candidates or been considered as potential presidential nominees. A sitting Governor in one State can stand in the presidential election without first resigning the governorship, ensuring continuity of the Governor's role until the presidential election outcome is known. This flexibility is operationally important given the political dynamics of coalition presidential elections.

Academic debate about whether the Article 58 qualifications should be expanded — to require prior public service, a minimum period of Indian residence, or some demonstrated experience of constitutional governance — has been consistently rejected on the ground that the democratic wisdom of the electoral college is a more reliable screen than any constitutional qualification checklist. This debate will likely resurface if any significant disqualification question arises in a future presidential election before the Supreme Court under Article 71.
