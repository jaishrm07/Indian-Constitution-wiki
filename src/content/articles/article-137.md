## What this Article does

Article 137 gives the Supreme Court the power to review any judgment or order it has itself pronounced or made. The power is exercised subject to any law made by Parliament and to the rules made under Article 145, which prescribe narrow grounds — error apparent on the face of the record, discovery of new and important matter, or some other sufficient reason. Review is not a second appeal and the Court's scope of interference is deliberately restricted to prevent settled judgments from being relitigated on the merits.

The constitutional provision is brief: "the Supreme Court shall have power to review any judgment pronounced or order made by it." The significant work of limiting and defining the power is done by Order XLVII Rule 1 of the Supreme Court Rules, 2013, made under Article 145.

## Grounds for review

The Supreme Court Rules limit review to three grounds:

1. **Error apparent on the face of the record:** A mistake so obvious that it does not require argument or examination to be apparent — a case of two plus two being stated as five, not a case where the court reached a conclusion that can be argued to be wrong.
2. **Discovery of new and important matter or evidence:** Evidence that was not available to the party and that, had it been before the court, might have produced a different result.
3. **Any other sufficient reason:** This residual ground is construed narrowly, consistent with the first two grounds, and does not open the door to re-argument on the merits.

The grounds are cumulative and any one is sufficient, but all three are understood as requiring something more than mere disagreement with the outcome.

## Review versus appeal versus curative petition

Review under Article 137 occupies a specific position in the hierarchy of post-judgment remedies at the Supreme Court:

- **Appeal (Article 136 or as of right):** A substantive re-examination of the merits, available before the Supreme Court delivers final judgment.
- **Review (Article 137):** A narrow post-judgment remedy available to correct specific categories of error, not to re-argue the case.
- **Curative petition (inherent jurisdiction):** Available only after review is exhausted, for the most egregious cases of miscarriage of justice involving violation of natural justice principles or the judgment-debtor's fundamental rights. Established in Rupa Ashok Hurra v. Ashok Hurra (2002).

Review is thus the second of three post-judgment tiers, each progressively narrower.

## Review in open court versus in chamber

Order XLVII Rule 6 of the Supreme Court Rules, 2013, provides that review petitions are ordinarily to be disposed of without oral arguments — that is, on circulation among judges in chamber, based on written submissions alone. This practice has been challenged in cases involving death sentences, where the Supreme Court has recognised that the gravity of the matter may warrant oral hearing. In Mohd. Arif v. Supreme Court of India (2014), a Constitution Bench held that death sentence review petitions must be heard in open court with oral arguments by at least a three-judge bench, carving out a specific exception to the in-chamber rule.

## Key cases

**Rupa Ashok Hurra v. Ashok Hurra (2002):** The Constitution Bench held that after exhausting review under Article 137, a litigant may, in rare circumstances, move a "curative petition" invoking the Court's inherent powers. The curative petition doctrine was established precisely because Article 137 review is so narrow: once review is dismissed, the ordinary judicial process is exhausted, and only a curative petition can correct a grave miscarriage of justice. This case defines Article 137's position in the hierarchy of post-judgment remedies.

**Northern India Caterers (India) Ltd. v. Lt. Governor of Delhi (1980):** The Court affirmed that review lies only for a manifest error on the face of the record and not for re-hearing or fresh consideration of the matter. A mere difference of opinion with the judgment, or a view that the decision was wrong, is not sufficient ground for invoking the Article 137 power.

**State of West Bengal v. Kamal Sengupta (2008):** The Court restated the principles governing Article 137 review, clarifying that the Court should be slow to interfere in review petitions and that the power must not be used to convert the review into an appeal in disguise.

**Mohd. Arif v. Supreme Court of India (2014):** A Constitution Bench held that death sentence review petitions under Article 137 must be heard orally in open court by a bench of at least three judges, overriding the ordinary in-chamber procedure. This is the leading case on the relationship between the review power and the right to life under Article 21.

## Current significance

Article 137 review petitions are the penultimate remedy before a curative petition. In high-profile criminal matters — particularly death penalty cases — review petitions have become a critical final safeguard, drawing sustained public and legal attention. The Mohd. Arif exception has created a two-track review procedure: the ordinary in-chamber route for most matters and the open-court oral-hearing route for death penalty cases. This differentiation raises broader questions about whether the in-chamber procedure for non-capital cases adequately protects the right of hearing, and whether other categories of grave matter — such as cases involving life imprisonment or permanent deprivation of liberty — should also require oral hearing in review.
