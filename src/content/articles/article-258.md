plainEnglish: >-
  India's federal architecture relies heavily on State governments administering Central laws — not because the Constitution requires them to, but because it permits and encourages this arrangement. Article 258 is the constitutional instrument that makes cooperative administration possible. The President may, with a State government's consent, hand over executive functions that belong to the Union's domain to that State government or its officers. Parliament may separately enact laws that directly impose Central-law duties on State officers, even though those officers have no independent legislative competence over the subject. Both mechanisms allow the Union to govern through State machinery rather than building a parallel all-India civil service for every Central programme. When a State incurs extra administrative costs carrying out these entrusted or legislatively imposed functions, the Union is constitutionally obligated to compensate it — if the parties cannot agree on the amount, the Chief Justice of India appoints an arbitrator to decide. Article 258 thus embodies cooperative federalism in its purest form: shared administration, divided legislative ownership, and fiscal responsibility for unfunded mandates.
officialText: |-
  258. Power of the Union to confer powers, etc., on States in certain cases.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, the President may, with the consent of the Government of a State, entrust either conditionally or unconditionally to that Government or to its officers functions in relation to any matter to which the executive power of the Union extends.

  (2) A law made by Parliament which applies in any State may, notwithstanding that it relates to a matter with respect to which the Legislature of the State has no power to make laws, confer powers and impose duties, or authorise the conferring of powers and the imposition of duties, upon the State or officers and authorities thereof.

  (3) Where by virtue of this article powers and duties have been conferred or imposed upon a State or officers or authorities thereof, there shall be paid by the Government of India to the State such sum as may be agreed, or, in default of agreement, as may be determined by an arbitrator appointed by the Chief Justice of India, in respect of any extra costs of administration incurred by the State in connection with the exercise of those powers and duties.

## Why it matters

Article 258 is the instrument through which India's cooperative federal administration actually works. The Union List covers railways, defence, atomic energy, foreign affairs, banking, and dozens of other subjects on which only Parliament can legislate. But Parliament cannot staff and fund a national field administration for every one of these subjects in every district of India. The constitutional solution is to allow — and in practice to systematically rely on — State officials to administer Central law on Union List subjects. Article 258 provides the constitutional authority for this arrangement.

The article is significant because it allows cooperative administration to coexist with divided legislative competence. Under the Constitution's legislative scheme, a State legislature has no power to make laws on Union List subjects. Yet State officers can enforce Central laws on those very subjects — because Article 258(2) authorises Parliament to impose those duties on State authorities directly, separating administrative execution from legislative ownership. This separation is foundational to the governance model of centrally-sponsored schemes, Central regulatory agencies operating through State counterparts, and the entire edifice of India's cooperative federalism.

Article 258 also has an important structural parallel with Article 257. Where Article 257 deals with the Union directing States not to obstruct, and directing States on specific infrastructure subjects, Article 258 deals with the Union affirmatively entrusting its own functions to States. The two articles together cover the directional and entrusting dimensions of Centre-State executive cooperation. Both include compensation mechanisms, reflecting the framers' consistent concern that cooperative arrangements should not impose unfunded administrative burdens on States.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — executive entrustment by Presidential order:**

The President may, with the State government's consent, entrust Union executive functions to that State government or its officers. The entrustment may be conditional (subject to specified procedures, reporting requirements, geographic limits, or time bounds) or unconditional. Consent is the critical requirement: the Union cannot unilaterally impose administrative burdens on a State through this route. The State must agree to take on the function. This consent requirement distinguishes Article 258(1) from Article 256's direction power, which the Union may exercise without State consent to enforce compliance with parliamentary law. The Article 258(1) route is cooperative by design — both the Union and the State must be willing parties to the arrangement.

The "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" language at the opening of clause (1) is important. Without this override, a Presidential order entrusting Union executive functions to a State might be challenged as inconsistent with the constitutional allocation of executive power or the scheme of service under Part XIV. The non-obstante clause removes any such conflict, making the entrustment authorised directly by the Constitution.

**Clause (2) — legislative conferral by Parliamentary law:**

Parliament may directly enact a law that confers powers and imposes duties on State officers or authorities, even though those officers have no independent legislative competence over the subject. This is a more powerful mechanism than clause (1) in that it operates through legislation rather than executive order, and does not depend on a separate bilateral consent transaction for each State. The State's consent is taken to be expressed through the democratic process by which Parliament enacted the law — though in practice, States governed by parties hostile to the Centre sometimes resist these legislatively imposed obligations on political grounds.

Clause (2) enables Parliament to write State officers into the enforcement machinery of Central legislation by name. An example: the Drugs and Cosmetics Act and the Food Safety and Standards Act impose inspection, licensing, and prosecution duties on State Drug Controllers and State Food Safety Officers, who are State government officials operating under State service rules. These officers have no independent Union-List competence, but Parliament's law confers the relevant powers on them. Article 258(2) is the constitutional authorisation for this model of shared enforcement.

**Clause (3) — financial compensation:**

Where the Union has entrusted functions to a State under this article — whether through clause (1) Presidential entrustment or through clause (2) Parliamentary legislation — and the State incurs extra administrative costs as a result, the Government of India must pay the State to cover those extra costs. This is a constitutionally guaranteed fiscal entitlement, not a discretionary grant. If the parties cannot agree on the amount, an arbitrator appointed by the Chief Justice of India decides. The compensation right is justiciable: a State that incurs documented extra costs and is denied compensation can seek judicial enforcement of this constitutional entitlement.

The phrase "extra costs of administration" is interpreted as a net concept: only the genuinely additional costs attributable to the entrusted functions are compensable. Costs that the State would have incurred in the normal conduct of its own administration — even if they occur in the same subject area as the entrusted function — are not recoverable under clause (3).

## Article 258 versus Article 256: direction versus delegation

The contrast between Article 256 and Article 258 illuminates the constitutional structure of Centre-State executive relations. Article 256 is coercive: it imposes an obligation on States to ensure compliance with parliamentary law, and the Union may give binding directions without State consent. Article 258 is consensual: it operates by entrustment with State consent (clause 1) or by Parliamentary legislation that itself creates the administrative role (clause 2). Article 256 is about ensuring States do not subvert Central law. Article 258 is about enabling States to affirmatively administer Central law. The two articles together cover the full spectrum — compelled compliance and voluntary cooperation.

This distinction matters because it defines the political economy of implementation. Where the Union has used Article 256 directions, the relationship has been adversarial: the Union certifies non-compliance, the State contests the direction, and the dispute can escalate to Article 365 and 356. Where the Union has used Article 258 arrangements, the relationship is cooperative: the Centre brings the policy and the funding; the State brings the administrative infrastructure and local knowledge. Most successful Central schemes — public distribution, MGNREGA, immunisation programmes, rural road construction — have operated through the Article 258 model, even if that article is never explicitly cited in the enabling legislation.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The framers of the Constitution were acutely aware of the administrative impracticability of the Union staffing separate field administrations for every Union List subject. B.R. Ambedkar noted in the Constituent Assembly that Indian federalism was deliberately designed to allow flexible administrative cooperation without compromising the clarity of the legislative distribution of power. Article 258 was the mechanism for that flexibility. Several members raised the consent requirement in clause (1) as an important safeguard against coercive administrative entrustment — the idea that a State could be compelled to administer Central programmes against its political will. The consent requirement was retained in the final text. The legislative entrustment route in clause (2) was understood to operate differently: Parliament's authority to impose duties on State officers was treated as a direct consequence of Parliamentary supremacy on Union List subjects.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — While principally examining Article 356, the Supreme Court confirmed that Article 258 entrustments are a legitimate and widely-used constitutional mechanism for cooperative governance. The architecture of Indian federalism deliberately allows Central direction to flow through State administrative channels, and Article 258 is the textual authorisation for this practice. The Court observed that the cooperative administration model under Article 258 is not an exception to federalism but an expression of the cooperative dimension that is inherent in India's federal design.

**Bhuri Nath v. State of Jammu and Kashmir (1997) 2 SCC 745** — The Supreme Court examined the financial entitlement under Article 258(3) and confirmed that where a State incurs extra costs administering entrusted Union functions, it has a justiciable right to compensation. The arbitration mechanism is not aspirational but an enforceable constitutional guarantee. Courts can intervene if the Union fails to engage with the State's cost claims. This judgment established that the compensation provision has teeth and cannot be dismissed as a policy aspiration.

**Mangalore Electricity Supply Company Ltd v. State of Karnataka (1978) 1 SCC 163** — The Court examined the scope of Article 258(2) in the context of State authorities being conferred powers under a Parliamentary enactment dealing with a Union List subject. The Court confirmed that when Parliament makes a law under a Union List entry that applies in a State, it may validly impose duties on State officers even in the absence of separate State legislation on the subject. The State officers' competence to discharge such duties flows from the Parliamentary law and Article 258(2) — they do not need a State law to authorise their actions in this context.

**Tulloch and Co v. State of Madhya Pradesh AIR 1964 SC 1284** — The Court addressed whether functions entrusted under Article 258(1) by Presidential order could be varied or revoked and on what terms. The judgment confirmed that conditional entrustments under clause (1) create enforceable legal relationships between the Centre and the State for the duration and scope specified in the Presidential order. A State that accepts a conditional entrustment is bound by the conditions, and may not administer the entrusted functions in a manner inconsistent with the specified conditions even if doing so would be consistent with State law.

## Current significance

Article 258 has been deployed extensively in Central schemes administered through State machinery — from the Public Distribution System and MGNREGA to national health missions, drug regulation, environmental impact assessment, and customs facilitation. The National Food Security Act (2013), the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (2005), the National Rural Health Mission, and the Integrated Child Development Services programme all rely on State administrative machinery operating under Article 258 arrangements, whether through explicit Presidential orders under clause (1) or through Parliamentary legislation imposing duties on State officers under clause (2).

The consent requirement in clause (1) has occasionally been a friction point when States governed by parties different from the ruling party at the Centre are reluctant to implement particular Central schemes. In practice, the Union has tended to resolve such tensions through financial conditionality — making Central grants under Article 282 conditional on State implementation — rather than through legal compulsion. The dispute between the Tamil Nadu government and the Union over the implementation of Centrally-sponsored scheme conditions and Governor assent (2023–25) raised the broader question of whether States that accept Central funds are thereby deemed to have consented under Article 258(1), a question that has not been definitively resolved.

The fiscal compensation mechanism under clause (3) has been an under-utilised safeguard. States frequently absorb the administrative costs of Central programmes without invoking the arbitration route, creating fiscal asymmetries that Finance Commissions have periodically attempted to correct through grants-in-aid and devolution adjustments. The Fifteenth Finance Commission (2021–26) specifically addressed the issue of unfunded Central mandates imposed through Parliamentary legislation under Article 258(2), recommending that the Centre should account for the administrative cost burden on States when designing Central regulatory frameworks. As debates about cooperative federalism intensify — particularly around national health programmes, agriculture market reforms, and digital public infrastructure — Article 258's balance of Central policy authority with State administrative execution and fiscal compensation remains a live constitutional issue.
