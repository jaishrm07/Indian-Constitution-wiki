  293. Borrowing by States.—(1) Subject to the provisions of this article, the executive power of a State extends to borrowing within the territory of India upon the security of the Consolidated Fund of the State within such limits, if any, as may from time to time be fixed by the Legislature of such State by law and to the giving of guarantees within such limits, if any, as may be so fixed.

  (2) The Government of India may, subject to such conditions as may be laid down by or under any law made by Parliament, make loans to any State or, so long as any limits fixed under article 292 are not exceeded, give guarantees in respect of loans raised by any State, and any sums required for the purpose of making such loans shall be charged on the Consolidated Fund of India.

## Why it matters

Article 293 defines the financial autonomy of States in the most practical sense — how much they can borrow and on whose approval. Borrowing is the primary mechanism through which State governments finance capital expenditure, bridge revenue shortfalls, and respond to natural disasters. The article creates a two-tier structure: nominal State autonomy in clause (1), comprehensively qualified by the Union's veto in clause (3). Because virtually every State has an outstanding loan from the Union — accumulated over decades of central assistance, calamity relief, and infrastructure lending — the consent requirement in clause (3) operates in practice as a permanent condition rather than an occasional check. This structural feature has made Article 293 one of the most contested provisions in India's fiscal federalism debates, with States arguing that the consent condition is used as a political lever to enforce compliance with Union policy preferences that are outside the Centre's legislative competence.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) gives each State government the executive power to borrow against the security of its Consolidated Fund, within limits its own legislature may set by law. This mirrors Article 292 for the Union but is immediately subject to the rest of the article.

Clause (2) gives the Union the power to lend to States and to guarantee State loans, with the amounts charged on the Consolidated Fund of India — making Union lending to States a constitutional obligation rather than a discretionary grant once Parliament or the Finance Commission has approved it.

Clause (3) is the operative control: no State may borrow if any part of a Union loan to it is outstanding, without the Union's prior consent. The breadth of the trigger is notable — even a single rupee of outstanding Union loan subjects the State's entire borrowing programme to Union approval. Clause (4) compounds this by expressly permitting the Union to attach conditions to its consent, opening the door to conditionality-based fiscal management.

## Key cases

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India AIR 1978 SC 68** — A Constitution Bench examined the constitutional limits of Union control over State financial affairs. The Court's analysis of fiscal federalism articulated the principle that Union powers — including the consent power under Article 293(3) — must be exercised in a manner consistent with genuine federal autonomy, and that the Centre cannot use financial controls as a pretext to interfere with areas of State legislative competence.

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India AIR 1963 SC 1241** — The Court's landmark examination of the Union's power over State property and the breadth of parliamentary supremacy in fiscal matters described the asymmetric federal structure that Article 293 exemplifies — States have borrowing authority in principle but are constitutionally subject to Union consent whenever any prior Union loan is outstanding.

**Kerala v. Union of India (2022, Borrowing Limits Challenge)** — Kerala and several other States challenged the Union's reduction of their net borrowing limits for 2022-23 as a consequence of off-budget borrowings by State electricity boards and road development corporations. The States argued that these were independent entities, not State borrowings, and that the Centre was unlawfully using the Article 293 consent framework to penalise them for the financial decisions of separate statutory bodies. The dispute crystallised the tension between Union fiscal oversight and State commercial autonomy.

## The fiscal federalism dimension

The consent requirement in Article 293(3) interacts with the Finance Commission's role in a significant way. Successive Finance Commissions have recommended annual gross borrowing ceilings for each State as a percentage of GDP, and the Union has generally implemented these as conditions of borrowing consent under clause (3)-(4). This means the Finance Commission's fiscal consolidation framework is given constitutional teeth through the Article 293 consent mechanism — a combination that has generally been seen as legitimate, since the Finance Commission is a constitutionally independent body.

The controversy arises when the Union uses its Article 293(4) conditional consent power to attach requirements that go beyond fiscal parameters — for instance, making borrowing approval conditional on implementing power sector reforms, reducing subsidies, or complying with particular policy benchmarks. Opposition-governed States have argued that such conditions exceed the legitimate scope of fiscal conditionality and amount to coercive federalism. The debates over States' off-budget borrowing through special purpose vehicles and State-owned enterprises also engage Article 293, as the Union has moved to count such borrowings toward States' effective debt ceilings even when they are not formal State loans.

## Current significance

Article 293(3) and (4) are central to live arguments about fiscal federalism in India. The 16th Finance Commission's recommendations on State borrowing ceilings — expected in 2025-26 — will set the framework within which Article 293 consent conditions operate for the next five years. The structural asymmetry between Union borrowing (self-limited by Parliament, which the Union controls) and State borrowing (externally limited by Union consent) is increasingly questioned by economists and political scientists who argue that States' ability to respond to their own spending needs is constitutionally constrained in ways that undermine democratic accountability at the State level. The article's interaction with GST devolution shortfalls, back-ended Finance Commission grants, and the FRBM framework makes it a central node in India's complex web of intergovernmental fiscal relations.
