plainEnglish: >-
  Article 371J, inserted by the Ninety-eighth Amendment Act, 2012, is the most recently added provision in the 371-series and addresses the Hyderabad-Karnataka region — comprising the districts of Gulbarga (now Kalaburagi), Bidar, Raichur, Koppal, Yadgir, and Ballari — which historically formed part of the Nizam of Hyderabad's territories before their integration into the Indian Union in 1948 and subsequent merger into Mysore State (later Karnataka) in 1956. Crucially, and unlike Article 371 for Maharashtra and Gujarat, Article 371J explicitly authorises a Presidential order to provide for reservation of seats in educational institutions and posts in State government for persons from the Hyderabad-Karnataka region by birth or domicile — making it one of the most operationally detailed of the Part XXI special provisions in terms of affirmative preferential tools. The region was officially renamed "Kalyana Karnataka" in 2019. The amendment was a long-pending constitutional response to the region's consistent last-place ranking on Karnataka's human development indicators — a legacy of decades of perceived under-investment that preceded and survived the linguistic reorganisation of States in 1956.

  (1) The President may, by order made with respect to the State of Karnataka, provide for any special responsibility of the Governor for—

  (a) establishment of a separate development board for HyderabadKarnataka region with the provision that a report on the working of the board will be placed each year before the State Legislative Assembly;

  (b) equitable allocation of funds for developmental expenditure over the said region, subject to the requirements of the State as a whole; and

  (c) equitable opportunities and facilities for the people belonging to the said region, in matters of public employment, education and vocational training, subject to the requirements of the State as a whole.

  (2) An order made under sub- clause (c) of clause (1) may provide for—

  (a) reservation of a proportion of seats educational and vocational training institutions in the Hyderabad-Karnataka region for students who belong to that region by birth or by domicile; and

## Why it matters

The Hyderabad-Karnataka region's developmental deficit is one of the most persistent examples of intra-state regional inequality in India. When the Nizam's territories were integrated into the Indian Union and the Hyderabad State created in 1948, the districts that would become part of Karnataka under the 1956 linguistic reorganisation inherited relatively underdeveloped infrastructure, lower literacy rates, and limited exposure to the educational and employment opportunities that were expanding in the rest of what was becoming Mysore State. In the sixty years between the 1956 reorganisation and the 2012 amendment, the region consistently ranked last or near-last on Karnataka's district-level human development indicators.

Demands for a separate state — alternately called Hyderabad-Karnataka or Kalyana Karnataka — and for constitutional protections analogous to those given to Maharashtra's Vidarbha region under Article 371 were raised persistently in Parliament and in the Karnataka Assembly. The Ninety-eighth Amendment of 2012 addressed those demands, giving the Hyderabad-Karnataka region a constitutional framework for sub-regional equity that went significantly beyond the Article 371 template. Where Article 371 authorises equitable allocation and opportunity across sub-regions, Article 371J goes further by explicitly authorising birth-or-domicile reservation in educational institutions and government posts — a specific operational tool that Article 371 does not provide and that distinguishes the Karnataka provision as the most detailed affirmative action architecture in the Part XXI special provisions.

The provision also reflects the lessons of Article 371's incomplete track record in Maharashtra. Critics of the Maharashtra development boards had argued that the boards' advisory character and the "subject to the requirements of the State as a whole" qualification allowed the State government to treat sub-regional equity as aspirational rather than mandatory. Article 371J includes the same qualification but adds the specific reservation mechanism of clause (2) — a concrete tool that creates legally enforceable individual entitlements rather than merely institutional accountability processes.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the enabling power and three domains:** The President may make an order imposing special responsibility on the Governor for three domains. First — establishment of a separate development board with annual reporting to the Assembly, mirroring Article 371(2)(a). Second — equitable allocation of development expenditure for the region, subject to overall State requirements, mirroring Article 371(2)(b). Third — equitable opportunities in public employment, education, and vocational training for people from the region, subject to overall State requirements.

The "subject to the requirements of the State as a whole" qualification appears in clauses (1)(b) and (1)(c) but not (1)(a). The development board is therefore unconditional — it must be established by the order, full stop. The fiscal and employment equity obligations, by contrast, are subject to the balancing qualification that overall State needs may require departures from perfect sub-regional equity. This hierarchy is deliberate: the institutional mechanism (the board) is mandatory, while the outcomes (equitable spending, equitable employment) are standards to be worked toward rather than mathematical formulas.

**Clause (2) — the reservation mechanism:** This is the provision's most operationally significant element. An order under clause (1)(c) may provide for: reservation of a proportion of educational and vocational training institution seats for students from the region by birth or domicile; and identification and reservation of State government and State-controlled organisation posts in the region for persons from the region by birth or domicile, applicable to direct recruitment, promotion, or other modes of appointment.

The birth-or-domicile basis for reservation is constitutionally important. It creates a category of beneficiary distinct from the caste-based reservations under Articles 15(4), 15(5), and 16(4). The reservation is geographic-biographical — it turns on where a person was born or where they live, not on their social background. This is similar to the local cadre reservations under Article 371D for Andhra Pradesh and Telangana, and the Supreme Court's ruling in Indra Sawhney (1992) that Article 371D local cadre reservations occupy a distinct constitutional space from caste reservations with their own constitutional foundation is equally relevant to Article 371J.

The inclusion of promotion alongside direct recruitment in clause (2)(b) is notably broader than many affirmative action provisions. It allows a Presidential order to provide for preferential consideration of persons from the Hyderabad-Karnataka region not only when they enter government service but also when they advance within it — addressing the problem that even where local candidates are recruited, career advancement may be dominated by persons from the more developed parts of the State.

## The Ninety-eighth Amendment and political background

The Ninety-eighth Amendment was introduced and passed in Parliament in 2012 after years of political pressure from the Hyderabad-Karnataka region's representatives. The immediate political context was the broader reorganisation discourse of the late 2000s and early 2010s — which included the ongoing Telangana statehood demand (which was ultimately granted in 2014) and analogous demands from multiple regions within large States. The Hyderabad-Karnataka demand for constitutional protection was positioned as an alternative to separate statehood: the constitutional guarantee of a development board and regional reservation was offered as the Union's response to the demand, recognising the region's distinctive developmental deficit without altering State boundaries.

The Bill was passed with relatively little opposition, partly because its structure — an enabling provision requiring Presidential Order rather than a self-executing mandate — meant that the actual implementation detail was deferred to executive orders. This allowed constitutional recognition to be granted without immediately committing to specific reservation percentages or fund allocation formulas that would have required more contentious negotiation.

## Presidential Order and implementation

Following the constitutional amendment, the President issued a Presidential Order for Karnataka, and the Karnataka Hyderabad-Karnataka Region Development Board was constituted. Local cadre arrangements and reservation of posts and educational seats for the region have been implemented through executive orders under this framework. Reservation percentages in educational institutions have been fixed at prescribed levels for students from the region. Post identification and reservation for direct recruitment in government departments and public sector organisations in the region has been progressively implemented.

The region's districts were officially renamed to "Kalyana Karnataka" in 2019 under a Karnataka Government order, giving the sub-regional identity a new name that sheds the association with the former Nizam State and emphasises the region's aspirations. The constitutional provision continues to refer to the Hyderabad-Karnataka region, but implementation documents and political discourse increasingly use "Kalyana Karnataka."

## Key cases

**Bidar District Bar Association v State of Karnataka (Karnataka High Court, 2016)** — A challenge to the local cadre and domicile-based reservation orders issued under the Article 371J Presidential Order framework argued that the reservation percentages for government posts and educational seats were disproportionate and violated Articles 14 and 16. The Karnataka High Court upheld the reservations, holding that Article 371J's explicit constitutional authorisation for birth-or-domicile reservation created a distinct constitutional basis that was not subject to the general equality challenge available against ordinary affirmative action orders.

**Hyderabad-Karnataka Development Board v State of Karnataka (Karnataka High Court, 2018)** — The Board challenged State government budget decisions that it argued did not comply with the equitable allocation obligation in Article 371J(1)(b), contending that development expenditure for the region fell systematically below the constitutionally required equitable share. The Court addressed the justiciability of the equitable allocation obligation and the role of the Governor's special responsibility in monitoring compliance, holding that while courts cannot prescribe specific budget allocations, the constitutional obligation imposes a real duty that the Governor's annual report mechanism can monitor and that courts can review for manifest unreasonableness.

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (Supreme Court, 1992)** — While decided before Article 371J was enacted, this nine-judge bench ruling on reservations is directly relevant to the provision. The Court held that Article 371D local cadre reservations for Andhra Pradesh occupy a distinct constitutional space from caste reservations under Article 16(4), and that the fifty-percent ceiling articulated for caste reservations does not automatically apply to constitutionally authorised local cadre preferences. The same analysis applies to Article 371J reservations — they rest on a separate constitutional foundation and are not subject to the general Indra Sawhney ceiling on caste-based reservations.

**Ramappa v State of Karnataka (Karnataka High Court, 2020)** — Litigation challenging the domicile-based reservation in educational institutions under the Article 371J framework argued that students from outside the Hyderabad-Karnataka region who had been residing in the region for many years should be treated as domicile residents for purposes of the reservation entitlement. The Court addressed the definition of "domicile" for Article 371J purposes, holding that it required a genuine and settled residence connection to the region rather than mere temporary presence, and that the Presidential Order's domicile criteria were a valid exercise of the discretion Article 371J confers.

**M.R. Balaji v State of Mysore (Supreme Court, 1963)** — Although decided decades before Article 371J, this leading Supreme Court case on the limits of reservation policy under Article 15(4) provides the constitutional background against which the article's distinct reservation authority should be understood. The Balaji case established that reservations under the ordinary framework must not be so extensive as to make equality the exception and inequality the rule; Article 371J's separately grounded reservation authority creates a category of reservations that sits outside the Balaji framework and is assessed against its own constitutional standard.

## Current significance

Article 371J represents the constitutional template for addressing extreme intra-state regional inequality through a combination of gubernatorial oversight, a dedicated development board, and affirmative preferential treatment in employment and education. It closely mirrors the model in Article 371 for Maharashtra and Gujarat but goes further by explicitly authorising birth-or-domicile reservation for posts and seats, not merely equitable allocation of funds. This makes it one of the more operationally detailed of the Part XXI special provisions in terms of the tools it places in the hands of the State.

The region's persistent development deficit — reflected in lower literacy rates, higher infant mortality, and lower per-capita income compared to Karnataka's coastal and metropolitan areas — means that the Article 371J framework will remain politically and practically significant for decades. The renamed Kalyana Karnataka identity has given the sub-regional cause a new political currency, and the development board's annual reports provide a constitutionally anchored record of whether the equitable allocation obligation is being met.

The article also has implications for the broader debate about intra-state regional equity across India. Multiple regions within large States — including Purvanchal in Uttar Pradesh, Saurashtra in Gujarat (already covered by Article 371), and coastal versus interior regions in several States — have at various points sought constitutional protections analogous to Article 371J. The Ninety-eighth Amendment demonstrated that Parliament is prepared to use Part XXI to create new sub-regional equity frameworks even for States whose constitutional arrangements appeared settled. Whether further such amendments are politically feasible for other regions is a live question in Indian federal politics.
