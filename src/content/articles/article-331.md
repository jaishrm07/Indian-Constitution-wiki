## What this Article did

Article 331 gave the President of India the power to nominate up to two members of the Anglo-Indian community to the House of the People if, in the President's opinion, the community was not adequately represented there through direct elections. The nomination was discretionary and contingent on a presidential assessment of representational adequacy; it was not an automatic entitlement.

The article operated from the commencement of the Constitution on 26 January 1950 until it was deleted by the Constitution (One Hundred and Fourth Amendment) Act, 2020, which came into force on 25 January 2020. The amendment also deleted Article 333, which provided a parallel nomination power for Anglo-Indians in State Legislative Assemblies.

## Reading the provision

The article operated as an exception to Article 81, which governs the composition of the Lok Sabha through direct elections from territorial constituencies. By opening with "notwithstanding anything in article 81," it carved out a space for executive appointment that sat outside the elected-membership framework. The ceiling of two nominated seats meant the Anglo-Indian representation could never exceed about 0.4 percent of House membership — a token presence designed to give a voice to a small, historically distinctive community rather than to alter the democratic balance of the House.

The President's opinion about "adequate representation" was entirely discretionary. No Anglo-Indian candidate had a justiciable right to nomination, and the assessment of adequacy was made by the President acting on ministerial advice. In practice, the power was exercised after every general election when no Anglo-Indian candidate won a constituency seat, and sometimes concurrently with electoral victory by one Anglo-Indian member.

## The Anglo-Indian community and the Constituent Assembly debates

"Anglo-Indian" was defined in Article 366(2) as a person whose father or any male progenitor in the male line is or was of European descent, and who is domiciled within the territory of India and born within its territory. The community's origins lie in the colonial period, when mixed descent from British or other European fathers and Indian mothers created a distinct social group — over generations becoming a recognized Indian community with its own cultural, educational, and professional identity, concentrated in railway workshops, postal and telegraph services, and Anglo-Indian schools.

Frank Anthony, the most prominent Anglo-Indian leader and a member of the Constituent Assembly, argued forcefully for the nominated seats. He contended that the community's historical association with British administrative structures left it politically exposed in the new democracy and that a guaranteed voice in Parliament would secure its loyalty and ease its integration. The provision was conceived as transitional, with the expectation — shared by Dr B R Ambedkar — that over time the community would integrate into mainstream political participation and the nominated seats would become unnecessary.

## Article 334 and repeated extension

Article 334 originally limited special representation provisions — including the framework surrounding Anglo-Indian nominations — to ten years from 1950. Parliament renewed the broader reservation framework eight successive times: by the 8th, 23rd, 45th, 62nd, 79th, 95th, 100th, and 104th Amendments. The 104th Amendment (2020), rather than extending again, removed the Anglo-Indian nomination provisions from Articles 331 and 333 while simultaneously extending SC/ST reserved seats in Parliament and State Assemblies to 2030. The political judgment was that seventy years of transitional protection had been sufficient and that the community had integrated sufficiently into electoral competition.

## Nominated versus reserved seats

The mechanism of Article 331 differed structurally from the SC/ST reserved-seat system under Article 330. SC/ST seats are won by direct election from constituencies where only candidates from the specified community may stand. Anglo-Indian seats were filled by presidential nomination — the executive acted on an opinion rather than voters choosing among candidates. The nominated members had full voting rights and spoke from the floor on the same footing as elected members, but they owed their presence to executive discretion rather than democratic mandate. This structural difference shaped the debate about extension: critics argued that nominated seats were always a weaker form of representation than elected reserved seats and that the community's interests would not suffer from their removal.

## Key cases

**Frank Anthony v Union of India (1976)** — The Supreme Court considered various aspects of the Anglo-Indian community's constitutional protections, including the nature of Article 331. The Court confirmed that the nomination power was entirely executive in character and that no court could compel the President to exercise or not exercise it; the only check was the factual precondition of inadequate representation.

**P A Jacob v Union of India (1964)** — The Allahabad High Court addressed whether the President's satisfaction regarding adequate representation was reviewable, holding that the President's determination was a matter of executive discretion guided by political judgment, not a quasi-judicial finding subject to evidentiary review.

**Stokes v State of Uttar Pradesh (1984)** — Addressing the analogous Article 333 nomination power, the Allahabad High Court held that the nomination power imposed no time limit and could be exercised at any point during the life of the Assembly. The reasoning applied with equal force to Article 331.

## Frank Anthony and the exercise of the nomination power

In practice, the nomination power under Article 331 was exercised after every general election by the President on the advice of the Council of Ministers. Frank Anthony himself was nominated to the Lok Sabha after successive elections from 1952 until his death in 1993 — an almost unbroken run spanning more than four decades. He served on Parliamentary committees and used his platform primarily to advocate for Anglo-Indian schools, for the community's service representation, and for the protection of Article 336 (which reserved railway and postal service jobs for Anglo-Indians on a transitional basis). His sustained presence illustrated both the value of the nomination mechanism and the extent to which it became personalised around a single dominant community leader rather than generating a wider pool of Anglo-Indian parliamentary representatives.

## Current significance

Article 331 is deleted from the Constitution. The text reproduced here is the provision as it stood before the 104th Amendment. No Anglo-Indian members have been nominated to Lok Sabha since January 2020. The community continues to exist and is entitled to contest elections on the same basis as any other citizen, but there is no longer any constitutional mechanism specifically reserved for Anglo-Indian representation in Parliament.

The provision retains historical and comparative significance. It is a study in the design of minority representation through nomination rather than electoral reservation — a lighter-touch mechanism that avoids altering constituency boundaries but depends entirely on executive goodwill. The deletion, after seventy years and eight extensions, also illustrates how constitutional sunset clauses that are repeatedly extended can eventually be allowed to lapse without significant political contest once the demographic and political conditions that justified the original provision have sufficiently changed.

The Anglo-Indian nominated seat model is also a reference point in debates about whether expert or community nominations to the Rajya Sabha under Article 80(1)(a) could serve similar representational functions for other small or marginalised communities, and whether nomination by the executive is an adequate constitutional substitute for electoral reservation as a mechanism of minority representation in a democracy.
