  101. Vacation of seats.— (1) No person shall be a member of both Houses of Parliament and provision shall be made by Parliament by law for the vacation by a person who is chosen a member of both Houses of his seat in one House or the other.

  (2) No person shall be a member both of Parliament and of a House of the Legislature of a State , and if a person is chosen a member both of Parliament and of a House of the Legislature of [a State], then, at the expiration of such period as may be specified in rules made by the President, that person's seat in Parliament shall become vacant, unless he has previously resigned his seat in the Legislature of the State.

  (a) becomes subject to any of the disqualifications mentioned in [clause (1) or clause (2) of article 102], or [(b) resigns his seat by writing under his hand addressed to the Chairman or the Speaker, as the case may be, and his resignation is accepted by the Chairman or the Speaker, as the case may be,] his seat shall thereupon become vacant:

## Why it matters

Article 101 governs the full lifecycle of a parliamentary seat from election to vacancy. It prevents structural conflicts of interest by prohibiting simultaneous membership of both Houses of Parliament and of Parliament and a State Legislature — a member who finds themselves in both must choose. Beyond these structural rules, the article provides the legal mechanism by which disqualification under Article 102 translates into an actual vacant seat, how voluntary resignations operate, and how persistent unauthorised absence can be addressed.

The constitutional significance of the resignation clause — particularly the requirement that the presiding officer be satisfied the resignation is voluntary and genuine before accepting it — has grown enormously in the era of competitive coalition politics. Members engineering a change of government sometimes coordinate resignation waves to destabilise ruling majorities, and the Speaker's discretion to accept or reject those resignations has become a potent political lever.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — dual House membership:** A person cannot simultaneously be a member of both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha. Parliament has legislated on the vacation of the "other" seat through the Representation of the People Act 1951.

**Clause (2) — Parliament and State Legislature:** A person cannot simultaneously hold a parliamentary seat and a State Legislature seat. If elected to both, the State seat must be vacated within the period prescribed by the President's rules (typically fourteen days), failing which the parliamentary seat becomes vacant by operation of the clause.

**Clause (3) — disqualification and resignation:** Disqualification under Article 102(1) or (2) triggers automatic vacation. Resignation requires a written communication to the Chairman (Rajya Sabha) or Speaker (Lok Sabha) addressed personally, and the presiding officer must satisfy themselves of voluntariness before acceptance. A presiding officer who accepts a resignation without genuine inquiry, or who refuses to accept a clearly genuine resignation, acts unconstitutionally.

**Clause (4) — sixty-day absence:** A member who stays absent for sixty sitting days without House permission may have the seat declared vacant. The sixty days counts only sitting days, excluding prorogation and multi-day adjournments.

## Key cases

**Jagjit Singh v. State of Haryana (2006):** The Supreme Court examined the relationship between legislative membership and Tenth Schedule disqualification as it engages Article 101(3), clarifying that a seat becomes vacant automatically when the disqualification conditions are met, and that the presiding officer's duty to verify voluntariness of resignations under clause (b) is mandatory rather than discretionary.

**Balchandra L. Jarkiholi v. B.S. Yediyurappa (2011):** The Supreme Court addressed the Speaker's power to accept or reject resignations, holding that a Speaker who accepts a resignation without properly inquiring into voluntariness has acted unconstitutionally, and that courts may review whether the inquiry was genuinely conducted.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016):** A five-judge Constitution Bench held that a Speaker who is themselves facing a notice of removal cannot preside over disqualification proceedings that may affect the political arithmetic of their own removal — a principle that has been applied by analogy to resignation acceptance in contested circumstances.

**Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2019):** The Supreme Court directed the Speaker to decide resignation and disqualification petitions within a reasonable time, establishing that indefinite delay by the presiding officer is itself subject to judicial supervision.

## Current significance

Article 101 sits at the intersection of three live constitutional debates. First, the resignation-voluntariness inquiry has become highly contentious in the era of political defections: Speakers of different political persuasions have been accused of accepting or rejecting resignations to serve partisan ends. Second, the 2019 Karnataka and 2020 Madhya Pradesh crises showed that resignation waves followed by anti-defection disqualification create overlapping legal questions — should a member who resigned and was also disqualified be treated under clause (3)(b) or clause (3)(a)?

Third, the 60-day absence rule, though rarely invoked, has been discussed as a potential mechanism to address proxy attendance and ghost membership in Parliament. Its practical rarity reflects both the political difficulty of using it against members of one's own coalition and the availability of the whip system as an alternative discipline mechanism.
