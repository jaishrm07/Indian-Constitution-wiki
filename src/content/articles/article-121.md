## What this Article does

Article 121 protects the independence of the higher judiciary by restricting parliamentary discussion of judges' conduct in office to one specific context: a formal impeachment motion under Article 124(4). Outside such a motion, Parliament may not debate, criticise, or pass resolutions about how a Supreme Court or High Court judge has discharged their judicial duties. The restriction applies to "conduct in the discharge of duties" — meaning judicial acts such as decisions, orders, and courtroom conduct — not to a judge's general character or extra-judicial conduct in an entirely private capacity.

This restriction is the parliamentary counterpart to the sub judice rule and the broader tradition of judicial independence that runs through the Constitution's separation of powers design. It ensures that parliamentary majorities cannot use the floor of Parliament to intimidate judges through targeted criticism, which could impair the impartiality the constitutional scheme depends upon. The corollary is that the only legitimate parliamentary mechanism for addressing serious judicial misconduct is the impeachment procedure under Article 124(4), which requires a special majority in both Houses and an inquiry process — a deliberately high threshold.

## The boundary of the restriction

The restriction is directed at "conduct in the discharge of duties." This phrase draws a line between:

- Judicial conduct (protected from parliamentary discussion): decisions made from the bench, orders, rulings on evidence, case management choices, courtroom behaviour — all of these fall within "conduct in the discharge of duties" and cannot be discussed outside the impeachment process.
- Extra-judicial conduct (not automatically protected): a judge's personal financial dealings, public speeches made in a private capacity, alleged corruption unconnected to judicial decisions, and so on. Article 121 does not bar all parliamentary discussion of a judge; it bars discussion of how they have performed their judicial role.

This distinction is significant in practice. When allegations of financial corruption against a judge are raised — as they were in the Ramaswami impeachment motion — parliamentary discussion may encompass matters that are partly judicial and partly extra-judicial. Article 121 does not prevent parliament from initiating the formal motion; it prevents informal criticism outside that formal channel.

## The state counterpart

Article 211 is the state legislative equivalent of Article 121, applying the same restriction to state legislative assemblies in relation to the conduct of High Court judges. The two provisions together create a comprehensive shield against parliamentary and legislative criticism of the judiciary operating outside the impeachment framework.

## Key cases

**Sub-Committee on Judicial Accountability v. Union of India (1991):** The Supreme Court examined Article 121 in the context of the impeachment motion against Justice V. Ramaswami. The Court confirmed that Article 121 does not prevent Parliament from initiating the formal impeachment procedure under Article 124(4), but bars any general debate, censure motion, or discussion outside that process. The restriction reinforces judicial independence as a constitutional value that the impeachment process itself is designed to serve, not undermine.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (1993):** The Second Judges case affirmed that judicial independence is a basic feature of the Constitution. Article 121 was identified as one of the interlocking provisions — alongside security of tenure, security of salary, and immunity from executive direction — that together constitute the constitutional architecture of judicial independence.

**Union of India v. Sankalchand Himatlal Sheth (1977):** The Court's analysis of judicial independence in the context of judge transfers confirmed that Article 121 is one of several provisions — alongside Articles 124(2), 125, and the impeachment procedure — that work together to insulate the judiciary from political pressure. The provision is not a mere procedural technicality but a structural guarantee.

**In re: C.S. Karnan (2017):** The Supreme Court's exercise of contempt jurisdiction against a sitting High Court judge raised the Article 121 question indirectly. Since Parliament had not initiated any impeachment motion against Justice Karnan, no parliamentary discussion of his conduct in office could lawfully take place. The matter was handled entirely within the judicial branch through the contempt jurisdiction. This illustrated both the strength of Article 121 as a shield for judicial independence and its limits as a mechanism for accountability — the judiciary was left to police its own member without parliamentary check.

## S.C. Advocates-on-Record and the NJAC context

The Supreme Court's decision in Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015) — the NJAC case — restated the constitutional importance of judicial independence at length. The judgment cited Article 121 as one of the constitutional provisions that embed judicial independence structurally rather than merely declaring it as a value. The Court's reasoning implied that any legislative or constitutional arrangement that bypassed or weakened provisions like Article 121 would be subject to basic-structure scrutiny.

## Current significance

Article 121's restriction has come under examination as public criticism of individual judges has intensified through social media and political discourse outside Parliament. While the provision formally restrains only parliamentary discussion, its underlying rationale — that targeted political criticism of sitting judges undermines judicial independence — has been invoked in debates about whether ministers' public remarks, party press conferences, and post-hearing statements constitute a de facto violation of the Article 121 spirit, even when they technically occur outside the parliamentary chamber.

The Supreme Court's in-house procedure for complaints against judges has been criticised as inadequate. The failed Ramaswami impeachment, the Karnan contempt episode, and more recent controversies about judicial appointments have renewed calls for an independent judicial accountability commission that would provide an alternative to both the Article 121 silence and the inadequate in-house procedure. Any such body would need to be designed to complement rather than circumvent Article 121's core protection against parliamentary pressure on the judiciary.
