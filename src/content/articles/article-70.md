## Why it matters

Article 70 closes the most dangerous constitutional gap in India's executive succession architecture. Articles 65 to 69 provide two levels of succession: when the President cannot act, the Vice-President steps in. But what if both the President and Vice-President are simultaneously unavailable? The Constitution itself provides no third designated successor. If Article 70 did not exist — or if Parliament had not acted under it — a simultaneous double vacancy would produce a constitutional vacuum at the apex of the Union executive: no one would be constitutionally authorised to assent to Bills, issue ordinances under Article 123, proclaim emergencies under Articles 352–360, or exercise mercy under Article 72.

The provision is ingeniously minimal: rather than naming a specific third-in-line successor in the Constitution (which would fix the successor and create its own problems if that person also became unavailable), Article 70 delegates to Parliament the power to make whatever provision it thinks fit. This parliamentary-delegation model is both more flexible than a constitutional naming and more secure than leaving the gap completely open: Parliament must act in advance, and its legislation operates within constitutional constraints, but Parliament can periodically review and update the mechanism without constitutional amendment.

The enabling character of Article 70 — "Parliament may make such provision as it thinks fit" — means Parliament has full legislative latitude in designing the contingency mechanism. It could designate the Chief Justice of India, the Speaker of the Lok Sabha, the Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha, the senior-most Governor, or any other constitutional authority. It could create a rotating or collective mechanism. It could specify different authorities for different functions. The only limits are those imposed by the rest of the Constitution — whoever is designated must still take the Article 60 oath and exercise functions within the constitutional framework that applies to the President.

## Parliament's response — the Presidential (Discharge of Functions) Act, 1969

Parliament exercised its Article 70 power by enacting the Presidential (Discharge of Functions) Act, 1969. The Act designates the Chief Justice of India as the authority to discharge presidential functions if both the President and Vice-President are unable to act. The Chief Justice is required to take the presidential oath under Article 60 before exercising any presidential functions, maintaining the constitutional continuity of the oath requirement that applies to every person exercising presidential powers.

The 1969 Act was triggered by a specific historical crisis. Acting President V.V. Giri had resigned the Vice-Presidency in July 1969 to contest the presidential election. This created a brief period in which no elected incumbent was available to act as President — neither the President (who had died) nor the Vice-President (who had resigned to stand for election). The Chief Justice of India, M. Hidayatullah, discharged presidential functions for a period from 20 July to 24 August 1969 under the authority of the 1969 Act. Chief Justice Hidayatullah took the Article 60 oath before exercising any presidential functions. This is the only instance in which the Article 70 succession mechanism has been activated in Indian constitutional history.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "any contingency not provided for in this Chapter" defines Article 70's scope by reference to what is absent in Chapter I of Part V. The Chapter covers the President's election (Articles 54–55), term (Article 56), eligibility (Articles 57–58), conditions (Article 59), oath (Article 60), impeachment (Article 61), timing of elections (Article 62), and the Vice-President's succession role (Articles 65–69). Article 70 fills the residual space beyond all of these — any gap, any unforeseen scenario, any double or triple vacancy — with a parliamentary delegation.

The words "Parliament may make such provision as it thinks fit" contain both the grant of power and its limit. "May" makes the provision enabling rather than mandatory — Parliament can legislate but is not constitutionally compelled to do so. If Parliament fails to enact any provision under Article 70, and a double vacancy occurs, there would be no constitutional authority to discharge presidential functions — an outcome the Constitution tolerates only because it treats the risk as low enough not to require mandatory advance provision. "As it thinks fit" gives Parliament the widest possible discretion in designing the succession mechanism, subject only to the Constitution's other constraints.

The designation of functions to be "discharged" rather than "exercised as President" is subtle but constitutionally significant. Article 65 uses "acts as President" and "discharges the functions of the President" to distinguish true vacancies from temporary incapacities. Article 70 uses only "discharge of the functions" — language suggesting that Parliament's designee exercises functions in a ministerial or substitution capacity, not as a full constitutional successor. The Chief Justice designated under the 1969 Act is not "acting President" in the Article 65(1) sense; the Chief Justice discharges functions. This distinction may affect the scope of the designated authority's powers relative to the full presidential office.

## Key cases

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court's survey of Part V noted Article 70 as a deliberate acknowledgement by the framers that a written constitution cannot enumerate every contingency. Parliamentary flexibility is the appropriate constitutional response to residual gaps in succession planning. The Court confirmed that the Article 70 mechanism, when exercised through the 1969 Act, is constitutionally valid and that the Chief Justice acting under the Act is bound by the same Cabinet-advice obligation as any other holder of presidential functions.

**Chief Justice Hidayatullah's exercise of presidential functions (1969)** — The activation of the 1969 Act during the 1969 presidential election gave Article 70 its only real-world test. Chief Justice M. Hidayatullah took the Article 60 oath and discharged presidential functions for approximately thirty-five days. During this period he signed several presidential orders, illustrating that the Article 70 mechanism is operational in practice. The episode also highlighted the separation-of-powers tension inherent in the 1969 Act's design: the Chief Justice was simultaneously performing judicial and executive functions, though the brevity of the period and the limited character of the functions exercised made this practically manageable.

**Constituent Assembly Debates on Article 70** — The framers debated whether to name specific successors in the Constitution itself. Several members proposed designating the Chief Justice or the senior-most Governor directly. The framers ultimately rejected the direct-naming approach as inflexible — a constitutionally named successor might themselves become unavailable, the appropriate designee might change as India's constitutional institutions evolved, and a fixed constitutional list would require amendment to update. The parliamentary-delegation model prevailed, giving the Constitution flexibility while still providing a mechanism for filling the gap.

**Presidential (Discharge of Functions) Act, 1969 — constitutional validity** — While the 1969 Act has never been directly challenged in the Supreme Court, its constitutional basis under Article 70 is widely accepted. The Act's designation of the Chief Justice has attracted academic commentary questioning whether placing the head of the judiciary in a temporary executive role is consistent with the basic structure principle of separation of powers. No court has yet directly ruled on this question, but the argument that the Article 70 Act must be consistent with basic structure constraints — including separation of powers — remains a live academic proposition.

## Current significance

Article 70 and the Presidential (Discharge of Functions) Act, 1969 together constitute India's contingency presidential succession law. The provision's actual significance is inversely proportional to its constitutional visibility — it is most important precisely in the most extreme scenarios that are least likely to occur. Its background presence in the constitutional architecture is, however, a genuine safeguard: without it and the 1969 Act, a double presidential-Vice-Presidential vacancy would produce a constitutional crisis without any available legal resolution.

The designation of the Chief Justice as the Article 70 authority has acquired indirect relevance in discussions about judicial independence and separation of powers. Critics of judicial overreach have noted that the 1969 Act places the nation's most senior judge temporarily in an executive role — with all the Article 60 oath obligations and Cabinet-advice constraints that entails. The possibility, however remote, that a Chief Justice might face a conflict of interest between adjudicating a case involving the Union government and discharging presidential functions simultaneously remains an unresolved constitutional tension.

The provision also periodically resurfaces in comparative discussions of presidential succession legislation. The US Presidential Succession Act provides an extended statutory line of Cabinet successors; some Indian constitutional scholars have argued that a more comprehensive succession statute — one that goes beyond the single-designee approach of the 1969 Act to name an extended line of alternates — would better serve constitutional continuity. Whether Parliament should update the 1969 Act to address more complex failure scenarios remains an open question of constitutional policy rather than constitutional doctrine.
