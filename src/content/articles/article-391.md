## What this Article did

Article 391 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that gave the President power to amend the First and Fourth Schedules in certain contingencies. It has been omitted.

The First Schedule to the Constitution lists the States and Union Territories of India with their territories. The Fourth Schedule allocates seats in the Rajya Sabha (Council of States) to the various States. At commencement, both these Schedules needed to accommodate the ongoing process of integration of princely states and the completion of territorial consolidation. The full integration of all territories, definition of their boundaries, and finalisation of Rajya Sabha allocations had not been completely settled on 26 January 1950.

Article 391 gave the President power to amend these two Schedules to address such contingencies — allowing changes to the list of States/territories and to Rajya Sabha seat allocations as the territorial integration was completed, without requiring a full constitutional amendment under Article 368 for each such adjustment.

## Why it was omitted

Article 391 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. The major territorial adjustments that needed to be made — including the reorganisation of States on linguistic lines under the States Reorganisation Act, 1956 — were by that time being handled through direct constitutional amendment and the States Reorganisation Act itself. The presidential power under Article 391 was superseded by the comprehensive reorganisation scheme and was no longer needed.

## Historical context

After independence, the integration of princely states into India was a continuous process. The Indian Independence Act, 1947 had divided British India into two dominions, but the princely states had to accede individually. While the major accessions and integrations had occurred by 1950, some territorial questions remained unsettled. Article 391 provided the constitutional mechanism for the President to update the Schedules as these questions were resolved.

The article was part of the original Constitution's recognition that the political map of India was still being finalised and that constitutional flexibility was needed to manage these ongoing adjustments. The broader constitutional amendment procedure under Article 368 was reserved for fundamental changes, while Article 391 provided a lighter-touch mechanism for Schedule adjustments arising from the completion of territorial integration.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge to the exercise of presidential power under Article 391 is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 391 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in providing the constitutional mechanism for managing the territorial transition from the dominion period to the republic, particularly in relation to the ongoing integration of princely states and the completion of Indian territorial consolidation. The provision illustrates the Constitution's approach of providing flexible, executive-exercisable powers for transitional adjustments while reserving the full Article 368 process for more fundamental constitutional changes.

The territorial reorganisation that Article 391 helped manage was ultimately superseded by the States Reorganisation Act, 1956, which reorganised India's States on a comprehensive linguistic basis and also prompted the constitutional amendments (including the Seventh Amendment) that abolished the Part A/B/C State categorisation and omitted Article 391 itself. The provision's history thus illustrates how temporary, executive-exercisable constitutional flexibility was eventually replaced by comprehensive statutory and constitutional reorganisation once the scale of the required changes became apparent.

The power in Article 391 to amend the First and Fourth Schedules by presidential order reflected a broader strategy in the original Constitution of providing executive-exercisable powers for Schedule amendments that needed to happen frequently or rapidly. The First Schedule in particular changed several times in the early years as territories were integrated, boundaries were settled, and princely states were merged. Rather than requiring a full Article 368 amendment process for each such change, Article 391 provided a lighter mechanism — at the cost of giving the President unilateral Schedule-amending power, which the Constitution's framers judged acceptable given the strictly transitional and administrative nature of the changes involved.
