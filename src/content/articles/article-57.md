## Why it matters

Article 57 removes any constitutional bar on a serving or former President seeking re-election. Unlike the United States, where a two-term limit is constitutionally fixed by the Twenty-Second Amendment, or France, where the Constitution limits presidents to two consecutive terms, India's Constitution imposes no ceiling. A President may stand for as many elections as the electoral college is willing to support, subject only to continuing to meet the qualifications in Article 58 and the conditions in Article 59.

The framers' deliberate choice to permit unlimited re-election reflects their view that the largely ceremonial nature of the presidential office makes term limits less urgent than in systems with directly elected, politically powerful presidents. Where the President exercises real executive power, term limits prevent the concentration of authority in a single individual and guard against incumbency entrenchment. Where the President is a constitutional figurehead constrained by Article 74 to act on Cabinet advice, the political risks of repeated tenure are considerably lower. The framers essentially reasoned that the office's limited real power made the dangers of unlimited tenure manageable.

The phrase "subject to the other provisions of this Constitution" in Article 57 is a saving clause of some constitutional complexity. Its most obvious application is to the disqualification provisions of Article 58 — a re-election candidate must still be a citizen, be at least thirty-five, qualify for Lok Sabha membership, and hold no disqualifying office of profit. But the clause also engages the less obvious question of whether a President who has been impeached under Article 61 — removed for violation of the Constitution — would be eligible for re-election. Article 61 is silent on future disqualification. Article 57's "subject to" phrase means any constitutional provision that might bar re-election would prevail, but without an express disqualification for impeached former Presidents, the question of whether such a person could again stand is technically unresolved.

The historical record of re-elections provides important context. Rajendra Prasad is the only President to have served two full consecutive terms, from 1950 to 1962. His re-election in 1957 occurred over significant constitutional debate within early Congress circles about whether the absence of a term limit was wise. He was an immensely respected figure whose re-election reflected genuine national esteem rather than political machine pressure. Since Prasad, no President has sought or obtained a second consecutive term, though several — including Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan, Zakir Husain, and Pratibha Patil — served single terms by personal choice rather than constitutional compulsion. This pattern of voluntary restraint has been the practical check on unlimited re-eligibility.

## Reading the provision

The provision is deceptively simple — a single sentence — but its constitutional implications extend into several areas. The eligibility it preserves is personal: it attaches to anyone who "holds, or who has held, office." The present tense ("holds") covers a sitting President who wishes to stand while still in office; the past tense ("has held") covers former Presidents returning after a gap, even if the gap spans multiple terms and other presidents. There is no "within a consecutive period" restriction, unlike some comparative constitutional models.

The words "eligible for re-election to that office" expressly frame this as re-eligibility to the same office, not a general continuation of constitutional eligibility. They operate as a permissive provision removing what would otherwise be a presumptive bar — that having already held the office, one might be thought to have been "used up" in constitutional terms. Article 57 affirmatively restores the full slate of electoral rights that every other qualified citizen possesses under Article 58.

The interaction with Article 59(1) — which automatically vacates the legislative seat of any MP or MLA upon becoming President — means a President seeking re-election would not need to vacate any other seat (since she holds none, having vacated it on first election). But the interaction with Article 59(2) — which prohibits a President from holding any other office of profit — means a former President who has taken up any such position would need to vacate it before standing for re-election to comply with Article 58(2), since the Explanation to Article 58 only exempts sitting Presidents and a few other named offices, not former Presidents who have returned to other public service.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court's analysis of the presidential office's ceremonial character underlies the practical significance of Article 57. Because the President exercises power on ministerial advice rather than independently, unlimited re-eligibility does not create the concentration-of-executive-power concerns that motivate term limits in presidential systems. The absence of an independent executive mandate makes re-election a matter of symbolic honour and electoral validation rather than political entrenchment in the American sense.

**V.V. Giri's election (1969)** — The constitutional question of whether a sitting President could simultaneously continue in office while effectively canvassing for re-election was raised in the context of V.V. Giri's decision to contest the 1969 presidential election while serving as Acting President under Article 65. Giri ultimately resigned the Vice-Presidency (not the Acting Presidency) before standing, but the episode illustrated that Article 57's permissiveness on re-election created practical ambiguities about campaigning while in office that the Constitution left unresolved.

**Rajendra Prasad's re-election (1957) — constitutional controversy** — When Prasad was proposed for a second term, a formal legal opinion from the Attorney General was sought on whether Article 57 permitted it. The opinion confirmed that the Article's plain text imposed no bar. However, a dissenting view circulated within the Nehru Cabinet holding that constitutional convention in parliamentary democracies should limit the President to one term. The episode established that Article 57 is legally permissive and that any limitation would have to come from constitutional amendment rather than convention.

**Neelam Devi v Union of India** — Challenges to presidential eligibility at the nomination stage have confirmed that the Election Commission and ultimately the Supreme Court under Article 71 are the proper forums for resolving any dispute about whether a candidate meets the conditions of Articles 57 and 58. The combination of Article 57's permissive re-eligibility with Article 71's exclusive Supreme Court jurisdiction means that any challenge to a re-election bid on grounds of prior conduct would have to survive Article 71's finality of Supreme Court adjudication.

## Current significance

The unlimited re-eligibility under Article 57 has acquired renewed relevance in comparative constitutional debate. India's parliamentary model — in which the President is a ceremonial head acting on Cabinet advice — is regularly compared with directly elected presidencies where term limits are considered an essential safeguard against incumbency entrenchment. Academic commentary has argued that a two-term limit amendment would bring India into alignment with international parliamentary best practice without materially affecting the presidential office's day-to-day functioning. No such amendment has been seriously pursued in Parliament.

The question also arises in the context of the Vice-Presidency. Since Vice-Presidents are eligible to become President under Article 65 and can subsequently stand for election in their own right, a person could theoretically serve multiple terms in different capacities. Several Vice-Presidents have subsequently been elected President, including Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan and Zakir Husain. Article 57's unlimited re-eligibility means that such multi-role careers in the constitutional executive are fully permissible.

Perhaps the deeper constitutional significance of Article 57 is the implicit statement it makes about the nature of constitutional trust. By refusing to impose a term limit, the Constituent Assembly signalled its confidence that the electoral college — composed of elected representatives who are themselves accountable to voters — would exercise sufficient democratic discipline over presidential tenure. The Constitution trusts political accountability through the electoral process more than constitutional prescription as the mechanism for preventing presidential overstay.
