## Why it matters

The Seventy-Fourth Amendment recognised that functional devolution under Article 243W is meaningless without fiscal empowerment. A municipality that governs urban planning or public health on paper but cannot raise or retain revenue sufficient for those functions is a constitutional institution in form only. Article 243X establishes four fiscal channels: the municipality's own tax-raising power, assignment of State taxes, grants from the State's Consolidated Fund, and the constitution of a dedicated municipal fund. Article 243Y, which must be read alongside 243X, mandates a State Finance Commission every five years to review the financial position of municipalities and recommend the principles for distributing taxes between the State and municipalities. Together these two provisions create the constitutional architecture of municipal fiscal autonomy — an architecture that has, in practice, been severely underimplemented.

The centrality of fiscal autonomy to meaningful urban self-government cannot be overstated. Without own-source revenue that the elected council controls, municipalities are in permanent dependence on state and central government grants, which arrive with conditions, are often delayed, and are calibrated to the grantor's priorities rather than local needs. The Fourteenth Finance Commission highlighted that Indian urban local bodies collectively raise own-source revenues equal to approximately one per cent of GDP, compared to six to eight per cent in comparable middle-income democracies. This revenue poverty is not primarily a consequence of limited urban wealth — Indian cities are economically productive — but a consequence of the systematic failure to implement Article 243X's fiscal devolution framework. States retain the most lucrative urban revenue sources — stamp duty, real estate development charges, entertainment taxes — and what they authorise municipalities to levy is typically a narrow set of charges with constrained rates.

Property tax is the constitutional centrepiece of municipal own-source revenue under Article 243X(a), and its reform has been a recurring policy priority for decades. The choice of assessment basis — capital value, annual rental value, or area-based valuation — affects both revenue yield and progressivity. Most Indian municipalities use outdated annual rental value systems with rate schedules that have not been revised in decades, producing both under-collection and horizontal inequity. Area-based systems, which calculate tax liability on the basis of built-up area and location rather than notional rental value, are simpler to administer and harder to undervalue, and the Fifteenth Finance Commission conditioned a portion of urban grants on States notifying floor property tax rates. But political resistance to property tax increases — from property-owning urban voters who are also an important electoral constituency — has made reform slow and inconsistent.

The fourth channel — the constitution of dedicated municipal funds under Article 243X(d) — has a specific constitutional significance in the context of Smart Cities Mission Special Purpose Vehicles. SPVs receive and spend funds for urban development projects outside the municipal fund structure, which critics argue violates the constitutional requirement that moneys received by or on behalf of municipalities be credited to constitutionally constituted funds. If the SPV is legally an entity distinct from the municipality, the constitutional argument is more complex; but if the SPV is performing functions that are constitutionally the municipality's functions under Article 243W and the Twelfth Schedule, then the routing of those funds outside the municipal fund structure raises a legitimate constitutional question that the courts have not yet definitively resolved.

## Reading the provision

The opening phrase — "The Legislature of a State may, by law" — establishes the permissive character of Article 243X. Unlike the mandatory election requirements in Article 243U or the mandatory reservation requirements in Article 243T, fiscal empowerment of municipalities is entirely at the discretion of State legislatures. No municipality has a self-executing right to levy any tax under Article 243X. This design reflects the historical position of municipal taxation within the State List and the federal fiscal arrangement, but it also means that Article 243X depends entirely on State willingness to actually empower municipalities financially.

Clause (a) — "authorise a Municipality to levy, collect and appropriate such taxes, duties, tolls and fees in accordance with such procedure and subject to such limits" — is the own-source revenue clause. Three verbs are significant: levy, collect, and appropriate. A municipality that is authorised to levy a tax but required to remit the collections to the State government, which then determines how much to return, has not received full fiscal authority under clause (a). Genuine own-source revenue requires the right to appropriate — to actually keep and spend — the proceeds. States that authorise property tax but subject appropriation to State approval or offset against grants have not fully implemented clause (a).

Clause (b) — "assign to a Municipality such taxes, duties, tolls and fees levied and collected by the State Government" — is the tax-assignment channel. This covers taxes that remain under State levy and collection but whose proceeds are earmarked for municipalities. State-collected taxes that are commonly assigned under this mechanism include entertainment tax, professional tax, and a share of stamp duty on property transactions. The conditions and limits the State may impose under this clause have in practice allowed States to treat assignments as variable grants rather than entitlements, subject to annual adjustment at State discretion.

Clause (c) — "provide for making such grants-in-aid to the Municipalities from the Consolidated Fund of the State" — is the grant channel. Grants are the primary fiscal relationship between States and municipalities in most Indian States. However, grants that are conditional, unpredictable, or tied to specific schemes give the State government ongoing control over municipal expenditure priorities, which limits the financial autonomy that Article 243W is meant to create. The constitutional architecture envisions grants as a supplement to own-source revenue and assigned taxes, not a substitute for them.

Clause (d) — "provide for constitution of such Funds for crediting all moneys received, respectively, by or on behalf of the Municipalities and also for the withdrawal of such moneys therefrom" — mandates that a framework for municipal funds be established by law. The requirement that all moneys received by or on behalf of the municipality be credited to a single constituted fund, and that withdrawals be governed by legal rules, creates a constitutional framework for municipal financial accountability. This clause has been invoked in challenges to practices where municipal revenues are routed through State-level accounts rather than directly to municipal funds, reducing the elected body's control over its own finances.

## Key cases

**Bihar State Electricity Board v. Bhanu Prasad (2006) — State Finance Commission recommendations and municipal fiscal rights** — The Supreme Court addressed the constitutional status of State Finance Commission recommendations and held that while SFC recommendations are not automatically enforceable as legal entitlements, the State government's obligation to constitute the SFC and to act on its recommendations is a constitutional obligation under the 74th Amendment framework. Repeated non-implementation of SFC recommendations was treated as raising a serious constitutional concern about the viability of municipal self-government. The ruling reinforced that the fiscal architecture of Article 243X and 243Y operates as an integrated constitutional scheme, not a set of optional administrative mechanisms.

**Municipal Council Ratlam v. Vardhichand (1980) — Municipal fiscal capacity and constitutional obligations** — Decided before the Seventy-Fourth Amendment but routinely cited in post-amendment municipal fiscal cases, the Supreme Court held that a municipality cannot use lack of funds as a permanent excuse for failing to discharge its statutory and constitutional functions. The ruling created pressure on State governments to ensure adequate fiscal devolution to enable municipalities to meet their obligations — pressure reinforced and constitutionalised by Article 243X after 1992. The Ratlam principle remains one of the most frequently invoked precedents in High Court mandamus proceedings against municipalities.

**State of West Bengal v. Committee for Protection of Democratic Rights (2010)** — The Supreme Court's observations about the constitutional character of local government bodies, and the State's obligations to enable their genuine functioning, have been applied by High Courts to argue that Article 243X requires the State to ensure that municipal funds are constituted and operated in a manner that gives elected bodies real budgetary control rather than subjecting every expenditure to State approval. The ruling's framework for constitutional accountability of State obligations to local bodies has been important in property tax reform litigation.

**Comptroller and Auditor General Reports on Smart Cities Mission (2021-22)** — While not a court decision, the CAG's constitutional audit findings on Smart Cities Mission SPVs directly engage Article 243X(d). The CAG found that large amounts of urban development funds were flowing through SPV structures that were not subject to the municipal fund framework required by Article 243X, and that this structure reduced financial accountability and democratic oversight. These audit reports have been cited in PIL proceedings before several High Courts challenging the constitutional validity of the SPV model.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of the integrated local finance framework under Articles 243X, 243Y, 243I, and related provisions established that fiscal devolution is not a collateral or secondary aspect of the 74th Amendment but is constitutive of the constitutional design. The Court's holding that the 74th Amendment created a constitutional obligation to make urban local bodies genuinely functional — not merely formally constituted — has been applied to Article 243X fiscal devolution arguments in subsequent cases.

## Current significance

Urban local body fiscal autonomy remains one of the most significant unresolved problems of Indian constitutional governance. Indian municipalities collect revenue equal to roughly one per cent of GDP, compared to six to eight per cent in comparable middle-income democracies. This revenue poverty persists despite India's rapid urbanisation and despite the clear constitutional framework that Article 243X provides. State Finance Commissions are frequently constituted late, their reports are not tabled in State legislatures within the prescribed period, and their recommendations are partially or wholly disregarded. The Fifteenth Finance Commission made a direct grant of Rs. 4.36 lakh crore to urban local bodies conditional on publication of audited accounts and notification of floor property tax rates — an attempt to use Central fiscal transfers to force States to comply with their Article 243X and 243Y obligations. The leverage was partially effective: most large urban local bodies complied with the audited accounts condition, though property tax floor notification was more uneven.

The property tax reform agenda is the live frontier of Article 243X implementation. Property tax has the potential to be a large and buoyant own-source revenue stream for Indian municipalities. Studies consistently show that Indian cities collect two to six times less property tax than their assessed potential, due to outdated valuation rolls, poor coverage of new properties, weak enforcement, and political resistance to rate revision. Reform proposals include shifting from annual rental value to capital value or area-based assessment, using GIS mapping to extend coverage to unlisted properties, and automated valuation using market transaction data. The 15th Finance Commission's floor-rate condition was a step in this direction, but without accompanying administrative reform and political commitment, fiscal devolution under Article 243X remains far below the constitutional potential.

The tension between Smart Cities Mission Special Purpose Vehicles, which are typically incorporated as companies and bypass the municipal fund structure, and the constitutional requirement under Article 243X(d) that municipal moneys flow through constitutionally constituted funds, has been flagged by the Comptroller and Auditor General and remains an area of ongoing constitutional debate. As the Smart Cities Mission winds down and its successor programmes are designed, the question of whether Union-funded urban development can structurally bypass elected municipal bodies and their constitutionally mandated fiscal architecture is one of the defining constitutional questions of Indian urban governance. The answer will determine whether Article 243X remains a constitutional aspiration or becomes the foundation of a genuinely autonomous urban fiscal system.
