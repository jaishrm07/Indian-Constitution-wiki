## Why it matters

In an era when most constitutional rights protect citizens from the state, Article 17 is exceptional: it reaches into private social relations. A Dalit person denied entry to a temple, refused water from a common well, or subjected to social boycott by their village community is not suffering from state action in any ordinary sense. They are suffering from a social practice sustained by private actors. Yet Article 17 makes that practice a constitutional wrong and a criminal offence. This horizontal reach — the constitution's direct command operating against one private person's conduct toward another — is rare in constitutional design and reflects the Constituent Assembly's determination that the abolition of untouchability was too urgent to leave to ordinary law alone.

The framers knew what they were addressing. B.R. Ambedkar, who had spent his life fighting untouchability-based exclusion and humiliation, understood that caste-based disability was not incidental to India's social fabric but structural to it. The word "Untouchability" appears in quotation marks in the constitutional text — a deliberate choice signalling that the term carries a specific historical meaning rooted in caste hierarchy and the ritual pollution doctrines of certain strands of Hindu practice. By placing abolition in the fundamental rights chapter rather than in the Directive Principles, the Constitution made it immediately justiciable and enforceable, not a mere aspiration to be achieved at the state's leisure.

The provision is also important for what it says about constitutional equality. Article 15 prohibits the state from discriminating on grounds of caste. Article 17 prohibits everyone from enforcing caste-based disability. Together, they create a two-pronged constitutional assault on the social infrastructure of caste discrimination — from above through state action and from below through private conduct. The failure over seven decades to fully realise Article 17's potential reflects the gap between constitutional text and social reality that defines some of the most painful failures of post-independence India.

The legislative history of Article 17's implementation tracks India's evolving recognition of that gap. The Untouchability Offences Act 1955 (renamed the Protection of Civil Rights Act in 1976) was the first implementation, but its weak enforcement and limited penalties produced inadequate deterrence. The SC/ST Prevention of Atrocities Act 1989 created a more comprehensive framework, adding physical violence, economic boycott, sexual offences, and social humiliation to the list of punishable acts. Amendments in 2015 and 2018 further strengthened the framework, added new offences, and addressed procedural concerns that arose from judicial interpretation that appeared to dilute protections.

## Reading the provision

The short text of Article 17 contains several significant elements. "Untouchability" is in quotation marks and begins with a capital letter — both conventions that signal a defined term carrying legal weight beyond its ordinary meaning. Courts have interpreted this as covering not only the specific historical practices of caste-Hindu society but any disability imposed on a person on account of birth in a caste regarded as untouchable, whether or not the exact form of that disability matches the traditional practices.

The phrase "in any form" forecloses narrow textual arguments. The practice of untouchability evolves — modern forms may not involve ritual pollution concepts at all, but may involve social ostracism, economic coercion, or denial of civic participation — and the constitutional prohibition covers all these manifestations. Courts have held that the phrase "in any form" was deliberately chosen to prevent creative re-descriptions of untouchability-based discrimination from escaping constitutional scrutiny.

"Forbidden" is categorical. Unlike the rights under Article 19, which are subject to reasonable restrictions in specified interests, Article 17 contains no qualifying clause. The abolition is complete and admits no exception. There is no ground on which the practice of untouchability can be constitutionally permitted — not custom, not religion, not regional tradition, not private autonomy. The absoluteness of the prohibition distinguishes Article 17 from virtually every other provision in Part III and reflects the constitutional judgment that untouchability represents a wrong so fundamental that no competing interest can justify it.

"Punishable in accordance with law" means that Parliament and state legislatures are required to provide criminal sanction for practices of untouchability — the Constitution mandates legislation, not just declares a principle. The words "in accordance with law" also mean that specific legislation defines what is punishable, how it is prosecuted, and what penalties apply. Courts have used this to hold that the state has a positive obligation to enact and enforce adequate legislation — a failure to prosecute untouchability-related offences can itself constitute a constitutional breach.

## Key cases

### The absolute character of the prohibition

**State of Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale (1993)** — Members of an upper-caste community in Karnataka prevented a Scheduled Caste family from drawing water from a common well, asserting traditional caste entitlements. The Supreme Court held unequivocally that Article 17 is absolute in character: no custom, tradition, or religious belief can justify the enforcement of untouchability-based disability. The argument that the exclusion was part of a long-standing local practice was specifically rejected — the Constitution's abolition operates even against the most deeply ingrained social custom. The judgment also confirmed that Article 17's horizontal operation against private persons is constitutionally valid: the enforcement of untouchability by private individuals constitutes an offence regardless of state involvement.

**People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (1982)** — The Asiad Games case primarily addressed forced labour under Articles 23 and 24, but the Court's analysis recognised the intersection between economic exploitation and untouchability. The underpayment and coercive employment of Dalit labourers in construction was inseparable from the social hierarchy that Article 17 abolished. The judgment treated the economic exploitation of Dalit workers as a constitutional wrong that engaged Article 17 alongside the forced labour prohibition, establishing the principle that untouchability-based disability can operate through economic coercion rather than only through social or religious exclusion.

### Manual scavenging and Article 17

**Safai Karamchari Andolan v. Union of India (2014)** — The Supreme Court addressed the practice of manual scavenging — the manual removal of human waste by members of specific Scheduled Caste communities, an occupation they were allocated by caste status rather than by choice. The Court held that manual scavenging is a form of untouchability within Article 17 and a violation of Article 21's right to dignity. The Constitution assigns to specific castes the most degrading and dangerous form of labour not because they chose it but because of untouchability-based caste hierarchy. The judgment directed immediate identification and rehabilitation of all manual scavengers under the Prohibition of Employment as Manual Scavengers and Their Rehabilitation Act 2013, and held that continued non-enforcement of the prohibition was a constitutional wrong for which the State was directly accountable. It represents the Court's most explicit treatment of occupational untouchability as a constitutional violation.

**Devarajiah v. B. Padmanna (1958)** — An early Mysore High Court decision that interpreted the then-Untouchability Offences Act against the backdrop of Article 17. A tea stall owner refused to serve a customer on account of the customer's Scheduled Caste identity. The Court held that this refusal constituted the practice of untouchability within the constitutional prohibition and fell squarely within the Act's penal provisions. The decision established early on that Article 17's horizontal reach extends to purely private commercial transactions and that the right to equal access to public eating establishments is constitutionally protected.

**Jai Singh v. Union of India (Delhi High Court, 1980)** — The Court examined a case in which a Scheduled Caste candidate was denied appointment in a private school and awarded relief under the Protection of Civil Rights Act. The decision contributed to the emerging interpretation that private employers operating educational institutions or other facilities of public character cannot enforce caste-based disability without violating Article 17.

### The Atrocities Act and its interpretation

**Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra (2018)** — The Supreme Court issued directions that were widely perceived as diluting procedural protections under the SC/ST Prevention of Atrocities Act, including a requirement for a preliminary inquiry by a senior officer before arrest and a bar on anticipatory bail to the accused. The directions triggered immediate legislative response: the Constitution (123rd Amendment) Act and consequent legislation restored the original procedural framework of the Atrocities Act, reversed the court-imposed requirements for preliminary inquiry, and reinstated the prohibition on anticipatory bail in atrocities cases. A subsequent bench of the Supreme Court stayed the Mahajan directions and the matter was placed before a larger bench. The episode illustrates the political and constitutional sensitivity of Article 17's legislative implementation and the accountability framework it creates.

## Amendment and legislative history

The Protection of Civil Rights Act 1955 (originally the Untouchability Offences Act) created the first criminal enforcement framework for Article 17, defining specific offences — preventing entry to temples, public restaurants, hospitals, and educational institutions on grounds of untouchability; enforcing social disabilities; preaching untouchability — and providing for punishment including imprisonment and fines. The 1976 amendment strengthened penalties and renamed the Act.

The SC/ST Prevention of Atrocities Act 1989 recognised that untouchability-based harm had expanded beyond the specific forms addressed in the 1955 Act to encompass physical violence, sexual exploitation, social and economic boycott, and offences involving humiliation and degradation. The Act created special courts for trial of atrocity cases, required states to appoint nodal officers and monitor implementation, and established a framework for victim relief and rehabilitation. The 2015 amendment added new offences — including denial of access to water sources, imposition of social or economic boycott, and garland with footwear — and strengthened the relief entitlement of victims. The 2018 amendment, enacted in direct response to the Mahajan directions, clarified the arrest provisions and definitional scope.

## Current significance

Despite seven decades of constitutional abolition and multiple layers of legislation, untouchability-based discrimination persists across India in ways that are extensively documented by human rights organisations, government data, and journalistic investigation. Inter-caste violence, access restrictions to water and cremation grounds, discrimination in labour markets, and sexual violence against Dalit women as an instrument of caste assertion all continue to generate litigation under Article 17 and the Atrocities Act framework.

The constitutional status of Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims — communities that converted from Hinduism or Buddhism but continue to face caste-based discrimination identical to that faced by Hindu Dalits — is a live question with direct Article 17 implications. The Presidential Order on Scheduled Castes limits SC status to Hindus, Sikhs, and Buddhists, excluding Christians and Muslims even if they belong to castes historically regarded as untouchable. Several petitions before the Supreme Court challenge this exclusion, arguing that caste-based disability persists regardless of religious conversion and that the exclusion of Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims from constitutional protections violates Article 17's comprehensive abolition. The resolution of this question will determine whether Article 17's reach is coextensive with the social practice it was designed to abolish, or whether it is artificially bounded by religious identity.

The intersection of Article 17 with gender — the particular forms of untouchability-related violence and exclusion directed at Dalit women — is increasingly recognised as a distinct constitutional problem requiring focused attention. Dalit women face overlapping discrimination on grounds of both caste and sex, and the constitutional framework has not always provided adequate mechanisms to address this intersection. Proposals to strengthen the Atrocities Act's provisions on sexual violence and to improve investigation and prosecution of gender-based caste atrocities engage both Article 17 and Articles 14, 15, and 21.
