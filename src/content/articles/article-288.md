  288. Exemption from taxation by States in respect of water or electricity in certain cases.—(1) Save in so far as the President may by order otherwise provide, no law of a State in force immediately before the commencement of this Constitution shall impose, or authorise the imposition of, a tax in respect of any water or electricity stored, generated, consumed, distributed or sold by any authority established by any existing law or any law made by Parliament for regulating or developing any inter-State river or river-valley.

  Explanation.—The expression "law of a State in force" in this clause shall include a law of a State passed or made before the commencement of this Constitution and not previously repealed, notwithstanding that it or parts of it may not be then in operation either at all or in particular areas.

  (2) The Legislature of a State may by law impose, or authorise the imposition of, any such tax as is mentioned in clause (1), but no such law shall have any effect unless it has, after having been reserved for the consideration of the President, received his assent; and if any such law provides for the fixation of the rates and other incidents of such tax by means of rules or orders to be made under the law by any authority, the law shall provide for the previous consent of the President being obtained to the making of any such rule or order.

## Why it matters

India's major rivers are national assets flowing through multiple States. The infrastructure harnessing them — dams, hydroelectric stations, irrigation networks, pump storage facilities — serves the nation as a whole rather than the particular State on whose territory a dam or power station happens to stand. Allowing one host State to levy taxes on the water or electricity managed by an inter-State river authority would effectively allow that State to extract fiscal benefits from a nationally-funded and nationally-governed resource, at the expense of the other riparian States and ultimately the consuming public. The revenue incentive would be perverse — a State hosting a dam could effectively tax the electricity flowing to consumers in neighbouring States, creating distortions in the equitable sharing of river-basin benefits across the region.

Article 288 addresses this coordination problem by placing inter-State river authorities outside the reach of ordinary State taxation: any State law that seeks to tax their water or electricity operations requires the President's prior assent. This is a substantive constraint, not a procedural formality — the President, acting on the advice of the Union Cabinet, can refuse assent to protect the national interest in cooperative river-basin management. The provision operates as a constitutional guarantee to inter-State river authorities that they will not be subjected to unilateral State fiscal burdens that the authority had no say in determining and that could affect the financial viability of national infrastructure.

The significance of Article 288 is growing as new multi-State hydroelectric projects, pumped-storage schemes, and inter-basin transfer projects are developed under parliamentary authority. Each new project raises fresh questions about whether its electricity generation and distribution qualifies for the Article 288 exemption in each host State, and about whether modern electricity-sector authorities — some of which are corporate entities rather than statutory boards — fall within the article's qualifying criterion. The interaction with Article 262 means that the fiscal dimension of inter-State river management under Article 288 sits alongside the resource-allocation dimension under the Inter-State River Water Disputes Act 1956 — together, these constitutional provisions and the legislation under them constitute India's framework for managing shared waters.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the freeze on pre-Constitution tax laws.** The clause operates as a freeze: any State law that was in force immediately before the Constitution's commencement and that imposed or authorised a tax on water or electricity stored, generated, consumed, distributed, or sold by a qualifying inter-State river or river-valley authority is automatically inoperative from commencement, unless the President issues an order permitting it to continue. This freeze applies to laws whether or not they had been brought into force across all areas of the State — the Explanation closes the loophole of States reviving dormant taxing laws by selectively bringing them into operation in particular areas without presidential clearance.

The qualifying authority must be established "by any existing law or any law made by Parliament for regulating or developing any inter-State river or river-valley." This limits the protection to bodies whose specific mandate includes the regulation or development of an inter-State river or river valley — a body with a narrower commercial mandate, or one established by State legislation rather than parliamentary law, may not qualify. The activities covered are comprehensive — storage, generation, consumption, distribution, and sale of water or electricity — ensuring the protection extends across the full operational cycle of river-basin management.

**Clause (2) — assent requirement for future State tax laws.** States are not permanently prohibited from taxing these bodies; they may enact future tax laws, but such laws take no effect unless first reserved for the President's consideration and then assented to. This is a pre-enactment clearance requirement: the presidential assent must be obtained before the law operates, not challenged after it is applied. Where the tax law further delegates rate-setting or incident-fixing to a subordinate authority through rules or orders, the President's prior consent is required for each such rule or order — preventing the approval of the law's shell from being followed by arbitrary rate-setting without further oversight.

The assent mechanism under clause (2) differs from presidential assent to ordinary State legislation under Articles 200 and 201. Under the ordinary procedure, a Governor may reserve a Bill for presidential consideration; here, the reservation for presidential consideration is mandatory — the State law has no effect without it. This makes the President's role not a discretionary safeguard available in certain cases but a constitutionally required condition of validity for any State law imposing such a tax.

**Interaction with the legislative lists.** Article 288's protection operates notwithstanding the distribution of legislative power in the Seventh Schedule. Entry 53 of List II (State List) gives States legislative competence over "electricity within the State including the production, supply, distribution and use of electricity." Entry 38 of List III (Concurrent List) covers electricity in terms of generation, distribution, and supply. On first reading, one might think a State law taxing electricity generated by a Central authority could be enacted under Entry 53 or Entry 38. Article 288 overrides this — whatever legislative entry might support a State's taxation of inter-State river authority electricity, the presidential assent condition still applies. The article is a constitutionally-imposed limitation on the State List's electricity entry.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The framers recognised that India's river basins do not respect State boundaries and that the most productive approach to river development would require multi-State authorities empowered to manage entire basins as integrated systems. The Damodar Valley Corporation, modelled loosely on the Tennessee Valley Authority in the United States, was already being established as the Constitution was being drafted. Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar and B R Ambedkar both noted in the Assembly debates that the efficiency of such authorities depended on their insulation from unilateral fiscal impositions by the host States through which the river flowed — a State could effectively hold the national project hostage through taxation if no constitutional protection were available. Article 288 was the Assembly's response to this structural concern. The provision generated relatively little controversy, reflecting a broad consensus that nationally-funded and nationally-important river infrastructure required constitutional-level protection from State fiscal jurisdiction.

## Key cases

**Damodar Valley Corporation v State of Bihar (1961) AIR SC 411** — The Supreme Court held that the Damodar Valley Corporation — established by Parliament to harness the Damodar River across Bihar and West Bengal — fell squarely within Article 288's protection. Bihar's attempt to subject DVC operations to local taxation without presidential assent was constitutionally invalid. The Court analysed the qualifying criterion carefully, confirming that the DVC's mandate to regulate and develop the Damodar River basin as an inter-State river satisfied the constitutional description, and that the protection extended to all activities — generation, distribution, and sale — covered by the article's text. Damodar Valley Corporation is the foundational precedent on the scope of Article 288.

**Andhra Pradesh v National Thermal Power Corporation (1990)** — The Court considered whether NTPC — a parliamentary incorporated authority generating and supplying electricity across multiple States — qualified for Article 288's protection in relation to State electricity duties levied by Andhra Pradesh. The judgment confirmed that the presidential assent requirement in clause (2) is a genuine constitutional condition rather than a mere procedural step, and that a State law receiving presidential assent does not thereby authorise the imposition of rates without further presidential consent where the law delegates rate-setting to a subordinate body. The case is significant for its clarification that the assent condition has substantive force at every stage of the tax-imposition mechanism.

**Tungabhadra Board cases (1970s–80s)** — Litigation concerning the joint Andhra Pradesh-Karnataka body managing the Tungabhadra irrigation and hydroelectric system raised questions about whether Article 288's protection extends to electricity distributed through the authority's network to end consumers in each State, or only to the generation stage. Courts reaffirmed that the protection attaches to all activities listed in clause (1) — storage, generation, consumption, distribution, and sale — not merely the generation stage at the dam site. A State could not tax the distribution or sale of Tungabhadra power without going through the presidential assent gate.

**Bhakra Beas Management Board v Pollution Control Board of Punjab (2000s)** — Later litigation concerning the Bhakra Beas Management Board — another major inter-State river authority — tested the boundary between Article 288's tax protection and regulatory charges. Courts drew a distinction between taxation of water or electricity (covered by Article 288) and purely regulatory impositions such as environmental compliance charges (not necessarily within Article 288's scope), though this distinction has remained contested as the boundary between regulatory charges and taxes is itself frequently litigated.

**Union of India v State of Andhra Pradesh (NTPC electricity duty disputes)** — A series of disputes between NTPC and State governments over electricity duty and other levies on power generated at NTPC stations in various States has produced substantial subordinate and High Court litigation on whether NTPC qualifies as an "authority established by Parliament for regulating or developing an inter-State river or river-valley." The resolution of this question has varied depending on the specific project and the legislative basis for NTPC's presence in each State.

## Current significance

Article 288 remains directly relevant to major inter-State river authorities — the Damodar Valley Corporation, the Bhakra Beas Management Board, the Tungabhadra Board, the Narmada Control Authority, and newer multi-State hydroelectric project authorities. As States seek new revenue streams from electricity duties, clean energy surcharges, and water usage levies, the constitutional constraint imposed by Article 288 continues to operate. Post-GST, electricity has remained outside the goods and services tax framework, making State electricity duties and levies a significant source of State revenue — and a corresponding area of friction with inter-State river authorities that enjoy Article 288 protection.

The growth of renewable energy development on inter-State rivers and their tributaries — including hydroelectric and pumped-storage projects being developed by NTPC, NHPC, and their subsidiaries — creates fresh questions about whether corporate entities operating under parliamentary authority but without the classic "inter-State river authority" institutional form qualify for Article 288 protection. The constitutional criterion — established by law made by Parliament "for regulating or developing" an inter-State river — may need to be applied to project-specific parliamentary authorisations rather than the broad-mandate river development authorities of the Damodar Valley Corporation type. This evolving landscape makes Article 288 an active rather than settled area of constitutional law.

The provision's interaction with Article 262 also remains significant. Article 262 bars the Supreme Court and every other court from exercising jurisdiction over inter-State river water disputes referred to a Tribunal — placing the resource-allocation dimension of inter-State rivers in a special adjudicative framework. Article 288's fiscal dimension, by contrast, is not excluded from ordinary court jurisdiction. A State that disputes a presidential refusal of assent to its taxation law, or an authority that challenges a State tax levy imposed without presidential assent, may seek relief through ordinary constitutional litigation channels. The fiscal and resource-allocation dimensions of inter-State rivers thus operate in separate constitutional tracks, which sometimes creates tension when a State uses fiscal instruments as leverage in underlying water-sharing disputes.
