  243V. Disqualifications for membership.—(1) A person shall be disqualified for being chosen as, and for being, a member of a Municipality—

  (a) if he is so disqualified by or under any law for the time being in force for the purposes of elections to the Legislature of the State concerned:

  Provided that no person shall be disqualified on the ground that he is less than twenty-five years of age, if he has attained the age of twenty-one years;

  (b) if he is so disqualified by or under any law made by the Legislature of the State.

  (2) If any question arises as to whether a member of a Municipality has become subject to any of the disqualifications mentioned in clause (1), the question shall be referred for the decision of such authority and in such manner as the Legislature of a State may, by law, provide.

## Why it matters

Article 243V reflects the same constitutional design as Article 243F for Panchayats: it establishes a framework for disqualification from municipal membership that balances the democratic principle of open participation with the legislature's power to impose conditions on public office. The provision is structured to keep municipal governance accessible — by lowering the age floor to 21 — while giving state legislatures significant flexibility to add disqualification grounds that reflect local governance policy priorities.

The article is directly relevant to some of the most contested areas of municipal democracy in India. State laws have imposed disqualifications based on outstanding dues to the municipality, on having more than two children, on minimum educational qualifications, on ownership of a water connection or sanitation facility, and on conviction for certain offences not included in the standard Representation of the People Act grounds. Each of these disqualifications has been challenged as either an unreasonable restriction on the fundamental right to stand for election under Article 19 or as a violation of equality under Article 14.

The disqualification framework also interacts with the anti-defection provisions that some states have applied to municipal councillors through state legislation. Where a councillor votes against the party line on a municipal resolution and is disqualified on that ground under state law, Article 243V's framework determines whether that disqualification is constitutionally valid — an intersection with the Tenth Schedule that has been litigated in several High Courts.

## How courts use it

Courts applying Article 243V follow the jurisprudence developed under Article 243F, which is the parallel Panchayat provision. The leading principles are: first, that the power to add disqualifications under Article 243V(1)(b) is broad but not unlimited, and must satisfy the test of reasonableness under Articles 14 and 19; second, that disqualifications tied to the conduct or status of the individual (arrears, criminal conviction, two-child norm) are more readily sustainable than those tied to immutable characteristics; and third, that the adjudicatory mechanism under Article 243V(2) must provide natural justice — the affected councillor must be heard before a disqualification is declared.

Courts have also used Article 243V when testing whether nominated seats on municipal councils — which some states provide for — are consistent with the qualification and disqualification framework. Where nominees do not satisfy the basic eligibility requirements that would apply to directly elected members, courts have held that this inconsistency cannot be justified simply by virtue of the nomination power.

## Key cases

**Javed v. State of Haryana (2003)** — Although technically an Article 243F case (addressing Panchayat disqualification), the Supreme Court's reasoning applies equally to Article 243V. The Court's upholding of the two-child norm disqualification as a permissible exercise of the legislature's power to add disqualification grounds has been directly cited in municipal election disqualification cases to support the validity of equivalent provisions in municipal legislation.

**Rajbala v. State of Haryana (2015)** — The Supreme Court upheld minimum educational qualifications for Panchayat election candidates under Article 243F(1)(b), and the same reasoning has been applied in challenges to educational qualification requirements for municipal candidates under Article 243V(1)(b). The case's reasoning — that education is a legitimate proxy for the capacity to perform local governance functions — has been adopted by courts in the municipal context, though with the same criticisms about exclusionary effect.

**Municipal Corporation of Greater Mumbai v. M/S Advance Builders India Pvt. Ltd. (2021)** — The Bombay High Court addressed the interaction between contractual disqualifications (persons with contracts with the municipality) and Article 243V, holding that the state law's disqualification of persons having specific types of contracts with the municipal corporation was a permissible exercise of the Article 243V(1)(b) power aimed at preventing conflicts of interest in municipal governance.

## Current significance

Article 243V disqualification law is most actively litigated in the context of the two-child norm and educational qualifications. Several states — including Rajasthan, Himachal Pradesh, and Andhra Pradesh — have enacted two-child disqualifications for both Panchayat and municipal elections. These provisions continue to generate litigation about their application to situations where the third child was born before the disqualification law came into force, or where one parent had the third child after a divorce or remarriage. The courts have had to develop a nuanced case-by-case jurisprudence applying the broad principles of Javed to these specific factual variations.

In the context of municipal elections specifically, the question of disqualification based on outstanding property tax or water dues — enacted under Article 243V(1)(b) by several states — has been increasingly challenged as penalising poverty in urban areas where many residents cannot afford to pay arrears. The Article 243V framework continues to be the constitutional battleground for these disputes.
