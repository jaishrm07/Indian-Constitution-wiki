officialText: |-
  243C. Composition of Panchayats.—(1) Subject to the provisions of this Part, the Legislature of a State may, by law, make provisions with respect to the composition of Panchayats:

  Provided that the ratio between the population of the territorial area of a Panchayat at any level and the number of seats in such Panchayat to be filled by election shall, so far as practicable, be the same throughout the State.

  (2) All the seats in a Panchayat shall be filled by persons chosen by direct election from territorial constituencies in the Panchayat area and, for this purpose, each Panchayat area shall be divided into territorial constituencies in such manner that the ratio between the population of each constituency and the number of seats allotted to it shall, so far as practicable, be the same throughout the Panchayat area.

  (3) The Legislature of a State may, by law, provide for the representation—

  (a) of the Chairpersons of the Panchayats at the village level, in the Panchayats at the intermediate level or, in the case of a State not having Panchayats at the intermediate level, in the Panchayats at the district level;

  (b) of the Chairpersons of the Panchayats at the intermediate level, in the Panchayats at the district level;

  (c) of the members of the House of the People and the members of the Legislative Assembly of the State representing constituencies which comprise wholly or partly a Panchayat area at a level other than the village level, in such Panchayat;

  (d) of the members of the Council of States and the members of the Legislative Council of the State, where they are registered as electors within—

  (i) a Panchayat area at the intermediate level, in Panchayat at the intermediate level; (ii) a Panchayat area at the district level, in Panchayat at the district level.

  (4) The Chairperson of a Panchayat and other members of a Panchayat whether or not chosen by direct election from territorial constituencies in the Panchayat area shall have the right to vote in the meetings of the Panchayats.

  (5) The Chairperson of—

  (a) a Panchayat at the village level shall be elected in such manner as the Legislature of a State may, by law, provide; and

  (b) a Panchayat at the intermediate level or district level shall be elected by, and from amongst, the elected members thereof.

## Why it matters

Article 243C lays down the democratic architecture of Panchayats and is the constitutional provision that gives Part IX its character as a genuine reform of rural governance rather than merely a reorganisation of State administrative structures. Before the 73rd Amendment, Panchayat composition varied enormously across States — some bodies had large nominated components, others had weighted representation for landlords or dominant castes, and many had Chairpersons appointed by State officials rather than elected by the people. Article 243C sweeps this away by requiring that all seats be filled by direct election from territorial constituencies. This is not a default rule that States may vary; it is a constitutional floor that State legislation must respect. No State law can reserve a majority of Panchayat seats for nomination, appointment, or indirect election — these can only supplement the directly elected core, and even supplementary members receive voting rights on the same footing as directly elected ones.

The proportionality requirement — that the ratio of population to seats should be, as far as practicable, the same throughout the State — imports the one-person-one-vote equality norm that was established for Parliamentary constituencies by Articles 81 and 170 into the third tier of government. This prevents a situation where one ward of a Panchayat area has a thousand voters per seat while a neighbouring ward has ten thousand, giving some citizens disproportionate influence over the body's decisions. Delimitation of Panchayat constituencies directly determines whether Article 243C's proportionality requirement is met, making the timing and methodology of ward delimitation a constitutional question, not merely an administrative one. The qualifier "so far as practicable" acknowledges that strict proportionality may be impossible — particularly when natural boundaries, scheduled area regulations, or administrative units constrain how constituencies can be drawn — but requires that proportionality be achieved as closely as circumstances allow.

The structural integration of higher-tier Panchayats with the parliamentary and legislative systems — through the permissive clause allowing representation of MPs, MLAs, and Rajya Sabha and State upper house members in intermediate and district Panchayats — reflects a deliberate design choice to link the three democratic tiers rather than insulating them from each other. This linkage can improve information flow and facilitate scheme implementation, but it also creates risks: if MPs and MLAs dominate Panchayat meetings, the democratic voice of the locally elected members can be muted. The constitutional text manages this risk by making such representation permissive and by guaranteeing voting rights to all members under clause (4), ensuring that State law cannot give ex-officio members superior voting status compared to directly elected ones.

The rules on Chairpersonship (clause 5) reflect a compromise between local democratic traditions and the 73rd Amendment's democratising purpose. At the village level, States retain flexibility to decide how the Gram Panchayat Chairperson (Sarpanch) is elected — direct election by voters, indirect election by the Panchayat, or a combination. Many States use direct election by the entire village electorate, maximising the democratic mandate of the Sarpanch. At the intermediate and district levels, the Constitution requires indirect election by and from among the elected members: the Chairperson must be a member of the body and must be chosen by its elected members, ensuring that the executive head of the body is accountable to its elected membership rather than to the general public directly.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes the general framework: States may legislate on Panchayat composition subject to Part IX's constraints. The proviso to clause (1) and clause (2) together set the proportionality and direct-election requirements. Reading these clauses together, the constitutional framework is: (a) all principal seats must be filled by direct election; (b) constituencies must be drawn proportionately to population; (c) State law may additionally provide for indirect or ex-officio membership to supplement the directly elected core. The supplementary members permitted under clause (3) are additive, not substitutive — they join a body already constituted by direct election and cannot form a majority of the body.

Clause (4) is a crucial equality provision within the Panchayat body: every member, whether directly elected or ex-officio, has the right to vote. This prevents the creation of a two-class membership system where directly elected members have full voting rights and ex-officio or nominated members are merely observers. At the same time, it does not prevent State law from creating categories of membership (such as Chairperson representation from lower tiers under clause 3(a)) that have limited or specialised roles within the larger body. The constitutional floor is simply that all who are members must have voting rights — which is the minimum condition for democratic accountability within the institution.

The delimitation of Panchayat areas and their division into territorial constituencies is left to State legislatures under clause (1), subject to the proportionality constraint. This means that periodic delimitation — to keep constituency population ratios current as the population shifts — is not merely a statutory requirement but a constitutional one. A State that fixes constituency boundaries decades in the past and never revises them is in structural tension with Article 243C's proportionality requirement, even if elections are held regularly. The population base for delimitation calculations is defined in Article 243 as the last published census figures, which creates a separate issue where census delays allow stale data to anchor delimitation exercises.

## Key cases

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004)** — The Supreme Court examined the constitutional framework for Panchayat elections under Part IX, including the requirement for direct elections to fill seats. The case clarified that eligibility conditions imposed by State legislatures on candidates for Panchayat elections must be consistent with the underlying democratic character of Article 243C. Restrictions that effectively exclude large sections of the population from standing for election — such as minimum educational qualification requirements — were scrutinised for consistency with Part IX's design of broad-based direct democratic participation.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** — While primarily concerned with OBC reservation under Article 243D, the Supreme Court's five-judge bench analysis necessarily engaged with the composition framework of Article 243C. The Court emphasised that any reservation or allocation of seats must be consistent with the proportionality principle underlying the article and that the direct-election requirement remains the primary constitutional mode of filling Panchayat seats. Reservation schemes, including OBC reservation, work within the framework of Article 243C's direct-election mandate — they determine who may stand for election from particular constituencies, not whether the seat is directly elected.

**Gram Panchayat of Village Jamalpur v. Malwinder Singh (1985)** — This pre-73rd Amendment decision addressed the representative character of village Panchayat bodies and established that the legitimacy of Panchayat decisions depends on the representative mandate of the members. It foreshadowed the constitutional reform by recognising that Panchayats exercising governmental functions required a democratic foundation — a principle that Article 243C subsequently entrenched through the direct-election mandate.

**Rajiv Gandhi Panchayat Sansthan v. State of Rajasthan (2013)** — The Rajasthan High Court addressed challenges to State laws that sought to add substantial nominated membership to Panchayat bodies at the intermediate tier, potentially giving nominees significant influence over the body's decisions. The Court held that Article 243C(2)'s direct-election requirement is a constitutional minimum that cannot be displaced by State legislative choices about composition, and that nominated or ex-officio members added under clause (3) must remain a secondary and supplementary element of a body whose primary character is defined by direct election.

**Sarpanch, Gram Panchayat Kallar v. State of Himachal Pradesh (2016)** — The High Court addressed a challenge to delimitation that resulted in disproportionate constituency sizes within a Panchayat area, breaching the proportionality requirement of Article 243C(2). The Court held that the qualifier "so far as practicable" does not permit deliberate malapportionment or indefinite delay in delimitation revision; it only accommodates genuine physical or administrative constraints that make strict proportionality impossible.

## Current significance

Delimitation of Panchayat constituencies — which directly determines the composition of each Panchayat under Article 243C — has become contested in States that have allowed ward boundaries to remain unrevised for extended periods, sometimes decades. Stale delimitation distorts the population-to-seat ratios that Article 243C requires to be kept, as nearly as practicable, uniform. This has been compounded by the postponement of the 2021 census, which means that even recent delimitation exercises are based on 2011 population figures that are increasingly remote from demographic reality.

Several States also face litigation over the eligibility conditions they have imposed on candidates — particularly minimum educational qualifications and disqualifications related to arrears on cooperative loans, or requirements that candidates have functional toilets at home. Critics argue these conditions effectively narrow the pool of directly elected representatives in ways that cut against Article 243C's democratic purpose by excluding the poorest and least formally educated citizens from standing for the offices designed specifically to represent them. The Supreme Court has upheld some such qualifications as rationally linked to the need for effective local governance, but the constitutional tension with Article 243C's direct-election mandate continues to generate litigation.

The composition and constituency framework of Article 243C intersects with the reservation obligations of Article 243D and the OBC reservation controversies tracked by the [local-body-election-delays-and-sec-independence](../current-affairs/local-body-election-delays-and-sec-independence) desk. Where States delay delimitation in order to defer OBC reservation decisions, the Article 243C requirement for current and proportionate constituency boundaries provides a basis for judicial intervention requiring fresh delimitation before elections proceed. The interaction between the two articles — delimitation driving reservation calculations which in turn drive election schedules — has become one of the most litigated structural dynamics in local government constitutional law.
