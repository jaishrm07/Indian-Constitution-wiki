## Why it matters

Article 11 is the constitutional pivot between the Part II transitional provisions and Parliament's ongoing authority over citizenship law. Without it, there would be a plausible argument that the detailed rules in Articles 5 to 10 — specifying who was a citizen at commencement and on what terms — were exhaustive, leaving no room for Parliament to modify those rules by ordinary legislation. Article 11 forecloses that argument completely: Parliament's power over citizenship is full and unlimited as against Part II. The Part II articles were a necessary stopgap for the founding moment, not a ceiling on legislative power.

The practical stakes are enormous. Citizenship law determines who may vote, who may hold constitutional offices, who is entitled to the suite of rights in Part III that are reserved for citizens (Articles 15, 16, 19, 29, 30), who may acquire agricultural land under State land-reform legislation that restricts ownership to citizens, and — in the context of the National Register of Citizens — who is subject to potential statelessness. Parliament's Article 11 power over these questions is therefore one of the most consequential legislative grants in the Constitution.

Article 11 also situates India within a global pattern of constitutions that reserve nationality law for ordinary legislation rather than entrenching it as a fundamental right. The Constitution does not guarantee citizenship — it confers it at commencement (Part II) and then hands the ongoing regulation of that status to Parliament. This structural choice has important consequences: it means that citizenship can be extended or restricted by simple parliamentary majorities, subject only to the external constraint of Part III. The debate about whether that constraint is sufficient to check majoritarian impulses in citizenship legislation — most acutely in the context of CAA 2019 — is one of the central constitutional controversies of contemporary India.

## Reading the provision

**"Nothing in the foregoing provisions of this Part"** — "Foregoing provisions" means Articles 5 to 10. Each of those articles contains detailed rules: domicile-based citizenship at commencement (Article 5), citizenship by migration from Pakistan (Article 6), removal of citizenship for Pakistan migrants who have returned (Article 7), overseas diaspora citizenship by registration (Article 8), automatic continuation of citizenship (Article 10), and disqualification on voluntary acquisition of foreign nationality (Article 9). Article 11 declares that none of these detailed rules restricts Parliament's own power.

**"shall derogate from the power of Parliament"** — "Derogate from" means limit or diminish. The structure is permissive rather than conferring: Article 11 does not say "Parliament shall have power" but rather "Parliament's power is not reduced by Articles 5 to 10." The source of Parliament's power over citizenship, in the first instance, is Entry 17 of List I of the Seventh Schedule (citizenship), read with Article 246. Article 11 reinforces and clarifies that power by immunising it from implied restriction by Part II's transitional framework.

**"to make any provision with respect to the acquisition and termination of citizenship"** — "Acquisition" covers all ways of gaining citizenship — birth, descent, registration, naturalisation, incorporation of territory. "Termination" covers renunciation by the citizen and deprivation by the government. The pair of words is comprehensive: every mode of citizenship arising and citizenship ending is within Parliament's reach.

**"and all other matters relating to citizenship"** — The residual phrase is deliberately expansive. The Supreme Court in Mirza Ali Akbar Kashani held it covers the full spectrum of nationality law — overseas citizenship, documentary requirements, dual nationality (including its prohibition), the consequences of citizenship loss, and the administrative and procedural architecture of citizenship. There is no judicially recognised category of citizenship-related matter that falls outside this phrase.

## The Citizenship Act, 1955 — Parliament's primary exercise

Parliament exercised its Article 11 power almost immediately after commencement by enacting the Citizenship Act, 1955. The Act provides the complete post-commencement code for Indian nationality. It specifies five modes of acquiring citizenship:

- **Citizenship by birth** — originally an unconditional jus soli rule; substantially modified by the 2003 amendments to require that at least one parent be a citizen and neither parent be an illegal migrant, replacing unconditional birthright citizenship with a modified jus sanguinis framework.
- **Citizenship by descent** — for persons born outside India to a citizen parent; registration with an Indian consulate is required in many cases.
- **Citizenship by registration** — for persons of Indian origin who are ordinarily resident in India, or who are married to Indian citizens, or who are minors with citizen parents, among other categories.
- **Citizenship by naturalisation** — for foreigners who have resided in India for eleven years (with a reduced qualifying period for persons of Indian origin); requires renunciation of prior nationality.
- **Citizenship by incorporation of territory** — automatic citizenship upon a territory becoming part of India, as happened with Goa in 1961 and as specified by Parliament's power under Article 2.

The Act also provides for termination — by voluntary renunciation — and deprivation, which is cancellation by government order on grounds including acquisition of citizenship by fraud or misrepresentation, disloyalty, trading with an enemy, or conviction of certain offences.

Key amendments include the 1986 amendments (first restriction on jus soli), the 1992 amendments (citizenship by descent through the mother as well as the father), the 2003 amendments (removal of unconditional birthright citizenship), the 2005 amendments (creation of the Overseas Citizen of India scheme), the 2015 amendments (merger of PIO cards into OCI), and the 2019 amendments (the CAA 2019).

## Limits on Parliament's Article 11 power — Part III

Article 11 does not place citizenship legislation beyond constitutional review. Laws made under Article 11 are laws made by the State within the meaning of Article 12, and by Article 13 they must not be inconsistent with Part III rights. Two Part III provisions are particularly important.

**Article 14** — equality before law and equal protection of laws — applies to citizenship legislation. Any classification of persons must have a rational basis and a nexus to a legitimate state object. A law that distinguishes between persons based on religion, ethnicity, or other suspect criteria requires careful justification. The Supreme Court has confirmed that citizenship legislation is not immune from Article 14 scrutiny (Prabodh Verma).

**Article 21** — no person shall be deprived of life or liberty except by procedure established by law — has been invoked in the context of deportation, statelessness, and detention of persons whose citizenship status is disputed. The Foreigners Tribunals process in Assam, under which persons who fail to establish citizenship may be declared foreigners and detained, has been subject to Article 21 challenges on grounds of procedural unfairness. The Supreme Court has read procedural safeguards into the process.

Additionally, Article 15 (non-discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth) may be relevant to legislation that classifies persons for citizenship purposes on the basis of religious affiliation — the precise ground on which the CAA 2019 is challenged.

## The CAA 2019 — Parliament's most contested exercise

The Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 (CAA 2019) amended Section 2(1)(b) of the Citizenship Act, 1955 by inserting a proviso that exempts from the definition of "illegal migrant" persons belonging to the Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, or Christian communities who entered India from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, or Pakistan before 31 December 2014. Such persons are also made eligible for citizenship by naturalisation on a reduced qualifying period of five years (instead of the standard eleven).

The effect is that Muslim migrants from those three countries remain "illegal migrants" under the Act and are ineligible for the fast-track naturalisation pathway. The Government's stated rationale is that these six religious communities are religious minorities in their countries of origin who have faced persecution, and India has a historical and civilisational obligation to provide them refuge. The exclusion of Muslims is justified on the ground that Muslims are not a minority in any of the three specified countries and therefore do not face state-sponsored religious persecution of the kind that attracted them to India.

Critics — including petitioners in the 200-plus writ petitions that are pending before the Supreme Court's Constitution Bench under WP(C) 1474/2019 — argue that the religious classification violates Article 14. The main strands of the constitutional challenge are: (a) the exclusion of Muslims has no rational nexus to the legitimate state object of protecting persecuted minorities, because Muslim subgroups (Shias, Ahmadis, Hazaras) do face persecution in the specified countries; (b) the selection of only three countries is arbitrary, excluding persecution in Sri Lanka (Tamils), Myanmar (Rohingya), and China (Tibetans); (c) the law uses religion as a classificatory criterion in a manner that is expressly prohibited by Article 15(1); and (d) the exclusion of Indian Muslims — who are not illegal migrants — from the benefit of the law is nonetheless constitutionally corrosive because it signals that India's citizenship law distinguishes between faiths.

The Union government's response is that Parliament's Article 11 power, exercised to address a specific persecuted-minority crisis in a defined geographical context, meets the Article 14 "intelligible differentia with rational nexus" standard, and that the CAA does not affect the citizenship rights of any Indian citizen.

As of 2025, the Constitution Bench has heard arguments but has not delivered its judgment. The case is one of the most significant pending constitutional matters in India.

## Key cases

**Mirza Ali Akbar Kashani v. United Arab Republic (1966) AIR 1966 SC 230** — The Supreme Court held that Parliament's power under Article 11 covers the full spectrum of citizenship law — not just acquisition and termination but "all other matters" including registration, naturalisation, deprivation, renunciation, and overseas citizenship. The Court confirmed that the phrase "all other matters relating to citizenship" is to be read in its widest sense.

**Prabodh Verma v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1985) 1 SCC 137** — The Court confirmed that laws made under Article 11 must be consistent with the fundamental rights in Part III. Parliament's plenary power over citizenship does not exempt citizenship legislation from equality review under Article 14 or liberty review under Article 21. This holding directly frames the constitutional challenge to CAA 2019.

**State of Assam v. Moslem Mondal (2013) Gauhati High Court** — The High Court held that Parliament's Citizenship Act entirely supersedes the transitional provisions of Part II for citizenship questions arising after 26 January 1950. Any claim to citizenship arising after commencement must be tested against the statutory code, not against the original constitutional articles.

**Sarbananda Sonowal v. Union of India (2005) 5 SCC 665** — The Supreme Court struck down the Illegal Migrants (Determination by Tribunals) Act, 1983 as applicable to Assam, holding that unchecked migration from Bangladesh constituted an external aggression under Article 355 and that a legislation giving migrant-friendly procedural advantages was unconstitutional. The case demonstrates that Parliament's Article 11 power to define and treat migrants is not unlimited and must account for other constitutional concerns.

**Assam Sanmilita Mahasangha v. Union of India (2015) Constitution Bench** — The Constitution Bench referred a cluster of questions about the National Register of Citizens process in Assam and the application of the Citizenship Act to long-resident migrants. The case raised fundamental questions about whether the Article 11 power permits a cut-off date approach — treating migrants who arrived before a specified date differently from those who arrived after — and how this interacts with Article 14's equality guarantee.

**WP(C) 1474/2019 (CAA Challenge, pending)** — The Constitution Bench reference involving 200-plus petitions challenging the CAA 2019. The bench will decide whether Parliament's exercise of Article 11 power through the CAA 2019 is consistent with Article 14's equality guarantee, Article 15's non-discrimination norm, and Article 25's right to freedom of religion. The outcome will settle the constitutional limits of Parliament's Article 11 power with respect to religious classifications in citizenship law.

## Current significance

Article 11 is the constitutional engine running in the background of every significant citizenship dispute in contemporary India. The National Register of Citizens process in Assam — which potentially affects millions of residents whose citizenship documentation is inadequate — is predicated on Parliament's Article 11 power to define and enforce citizenship through the Citizenship Act. The Foreigners Tribunals operating under the Foreigners Act, 1946, and now under the Illegal Migrants framework, are downstream institutions of that Article 11 authority.

The CAA 2019 and its constitutional challenge are the most visible expression of the tension at Article 11's heart — between Parliament's plenary authority over citizenship and the constitutional constraints that prevent that authority from being exercised in ways that violate equality or dignity. How the Supreme Court resolves the CAA challenge will define the boundary of Article 11 power for decades. If the Court upholds the Act, it will confirm that Parliament may use religious affiliation as a criterion for differentiating citizenship eligibility, provided a rational nexus to a legitimate object can be articulated. If the Court strikes down the Act, it will establish that religion is a constitutionally impermissible classification criterion in citizenship legislation, regardless of the parliamentary majority behind it.

Either way, Article 11 will remain at the centre of Indian constitutional discourse — not as an abstract textual provision but as the living authority under which Parliament reshapes the legal meaning of Indian belonging.
