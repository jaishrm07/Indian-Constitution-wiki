  339. Control of the Union over the administration of Scheduled Areas and the welfare of Scheduled Tribes.—(1) The President may at any time and shall, at the expiration of ten years from the commencement of this Constitution by order appoint a Commission to report on the administration of the Scheduled Areas and the welfare of the Scheduled Tribes in the States . ## Why it matters

Article 339 encodes the Union's special responsibility for India's tribal communities, going beyond the ordinary federal arrangement by allowing the Centre to direct States on what it considers essential for tribal welfare. This is constitutionally significant because tribal welfare — land rights, forest access, displacement, and cultural preservation — is not a Central List subject. Under the normal federal model, the Union government cannot direct a State how to manage subjects within the State List. Article 339(2) creates a targeted exception to that model, acknowledging that tribal communities are peculiarly vulnerable to State-level neglect and that the national commitment to their welfare requires a Union-level backstop.

The article's two-clause structure reflects different timescales of oversight. Clause (1) creates a mechanism for periodic deep-review: the President's commission takes evidence from across all States and Scheduled Areas, assesses the condition of Scheduled Tribes comprehensively, and makes systemic recommendations. Clause (2) provides a continuous operational power: the Union can, at any time, direct a State to draw up and execute specific welfare schemes. Together they give the Centre both the information (from clause (1) commissions) and the operational authority (from clause (2) directions) needed to discharge its special responsibility for tribal welfare.

The article is also constitutionally significant as one of the few provisions that explicitly extends Union executive power into the State domain on a subject-specific basis — a design choice that reflects the framers' understanding that post-Independence India's federal structure needed safety valves for the protection of the most marginalised communities against the majoritarian tendencies of State-level politics in regions where non-tribal communities dominated the political economy.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)** creates two distinct bases for presidential commission appointments: the permissive power ("may at any time") allows the President to appoint a commission whenever needed; the mandatory obligation ("shall, at the expiration of ten years") required the President to appoint a commission by 26 January 1960. The mandatory commission was the First Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes Commission, headed by U.N. Dhebar, appointed in 1960 and presenting its report in 1961. The clause gives the President wide latitude to define composition, powers, and procedure, meaning commissions can be tailored to the specific issues of the time — a more flexible mechanism than the standing NCST but less permanent.

**Clause (2)** extends Union executive power to directing States on schemes the Union considers essential for tribal welfare. The phrase "essential for the welfare of the Scheduled Tribes in the State" gives the Union considerable discretion in what it can direct — the standard is the Union's assessment of essentiality, not a judicially defined threshold. This power complements the Centre's power to make grants to States under Article 282 and to set conditions on Central grants under Article 275, both of which are regularly used to fund tribal sub-plan and other tribal welfare schemes with attached conditions.

## Fifth Schedule and Sixth Schedule connection

The Scheduled Areas referenced in clause (1) are those notified under the Fifth Schedule (schedule-v) of the Constitution, which establishes a special governance regime for tribal regions predominantly in central and eastern India including Jharkhand, Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Gujarat, Himachal Pradesh, and Rajasthan. The Governor of each relevant State has constitutional responsibility for annually reporting to the President on the administration of the Scheduled Areas in the State under paragraph 3 of the Fifth Schedule. These annual reports feed into the oversight mechanism Article 339 contemplates.

A separate regime under the Sixth Schedule (schedule-vi) applies to tribal areas in the northeastern States of Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram, governed through autonomous district councils with legislative, executive, and judicial powers. Article 339's reference to Scheduled Tribes encompasses communities in both Fifth and Sixth Schedule areas, though the administrative structures differ significantly.

## PESA and legislative implementation

The Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996 (PESA) extended the panchayati raj framework to Fifth Schedule areas while preserving customary law and traditional governance of tribal communities. PESA requires gram sabha consent for land acquisition, minor mineral leases, and management of minor water bodies in Scheduled Areas. It is commonly understood as a legislative implementation of the goals Article 339 sets out, and the Union's power to direct States to implement PESA-conforming legislation flows partly from the direction power in Article 339(2). Several States have been slow or partial in enacting PESA-conforming State legislation — creating the compliance gap that the NCST's reports have repeatedly flagged and that Article 339(2) directions could in principle address.

## Dhebar Commission and subsequent reviews

The First Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes Commission (Dhebar Commission, 1960–61) fulfilled the constitutional mandate of clause (1). Its report found widespread problems with tribal land alienation, inadequate development expenditure in Scheduled Areas, and the failure of the administrative machinery to implement the Fifth Schedule's protective provisions. The Commission's recommendations included strengthening land alienation prohibitions, improving educational and health infrastructure, and ensuring that development funds were actually reaching tribal populations rather than being diverted to non-tribal intermediaries.

Subsequent reviews have been conducted through the Planning Commission's tribal sub-plan framework, through Ministry of Tribal Affairs assessments, and through parliamentary standing committee inquiries — all less formal than a clause (1) presidential commission but providing ongoing assessment of the conditions the article was designed to address. There is a significant body of opinion, reflected in NCST annual reports, that India is overdue for a fresh comprehensive Article 339(1) commission to assess the condition of Scheduled Tribes after seventy-five years of constitutional operation.

## Key cases

**Samatha v State of Andhra Pradesh (1997)** — The Supreme Court held that land transfers in Scheduled Areas to non-tribals and private corporations are unconstitutional under the Fifth Schedule framework. The Court drew on the constitutional design of tribal protection — including the Union's oversight role under Article 339 — to hold that the protective purpose of the Fifth Schedule cannot be circumvented by State governments granting mining leases to non-tribal corporations, even if the State government argues that this serves development purposes.

**Orissa Mining Corporation v Ministry of Environment and Forests (2013)** — The Court directed that the consent of gram sabhas in the affected Scheduled Areas must be obtained before any forest clearance for the Niyamgiri bauxite mining project. The judgment synthesised the Fifth Schedule, PESA, the Forest Rights Act, and the constitutional protection of tribal rights — all within the framework that Article 339 and the Fifth Schedule collectively establish for Union and State responsibilities toward Scheduled Tribes.

**State of Andhra Pradesh v Samatha (2000)** — The government's review petition against the Samatha judgment was dismissed, confirming the precedent that Fifth Schedule Governors' powers and the Union's oversight responsibilities under Article 339 cannot be bypassed by State governments in the name of development policy.

**High Court proceedings on PESA non-compliance** — Several High Courts, particularly in Jharkhand, Odisha, and Chhattisgarh, have entertained petitions challenging State failures to enact PESA-conforming legislation and to implement gram sabha consent requirements. These proceedings have drawn on the Union's duty under Article 339(2) to argue that the Centre can and should issue directions to recalcitrant States on PESA implementation.

## Current significance

Article 339 remains constitutionally important but institutionally underused. The direction power in clause (2) has rarely been formally invoked as a constitutional direction; the Union prefers to use financial conditionality through Central-sponsored schemes and tribal sub-plan allocations to influence State tribal welfare expenditure. A formal direction under clause (2) would have stronger legal consequences — a State refusing to comply would be in constitutional violation of an executive direction — but the political costs of such confrontation with State governments have generally deterred its use.

The intersection of Article 339(2) with forest rights and development project clearances is a live constitutional issue. The Union's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change routinely grants forest clearances for projects in Scheduled Areas — sometimes without adequate gram sabha consultation as required by PESA and the Forest Rights Act. The constitutional argument that the Union's direction power under Article 339(2) requires it to condition such clearances on compliance with tribal welfare standards has been raised in litigation and accepted in part in cases like the Niyamgiri judgment.

The creation of the National Commission for Scheduled Tribes under Article 338A provides a permanent institutional complement to the periodic review mechanism under Article 339(1): the NCST provides continuous monitoring and complaint-handling, while an Article 339 commission can conduct a deep, comprehensive review whenever the President determines one is needed. The two mechanisms are constitutionally complementary, and advocates for tribal rights have argued that a fresh Article 339(1) commission — drawing on the NCST's accumulated reports and the experience of the Forest Rights Act's implementation — would provide the comprehensive assessment needed to reform tribal policy for the next generation.
