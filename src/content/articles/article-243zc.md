## Why it matters

Article 243ZC reflects a deliberate decision by the Constitution's framers — reiterated during the Seventy-Fourth Amendment — that the standard municipal governance model is not automatically appropriate for India's Scheduled and tribal areas. These areas have been placed under special protective frameworks: the Fifth Schedule for Scheduled Areas in mainland India (where tribal self-governance and land protection are provided through Tribes Advisory Councils and Governor's regulations) and the Sixth Schedule for certain tribal areas in the north-east (where Autonomous District Councils with extensive legislative, executive, and judicial powers operate). Imposing a standard urban municipal structure on these areas without adaptation could displace or undermine the customary governance institutions and land rights protections that the Fifth and Sixth Schedules are designed to preserve.

Clause (2) gives a specific protection to the Darjeeling Gorkha Hill Council (DGHC), the autonomous body established under the Darjeeling Gorkha Hill Council Act, 1988 to govern the hill districts of Darjeeling. The DGHC (and since 2011, the Gorkhaland Territorial Administration) exercises functions that overlap with what would ordinarily be municipal and panchayat jurisdictions. Article 243ZC ensures that Part IXA's municipal provisions cannot be read to displace or curtail the DGHC's powers, preserving the distinctive hill-area governance arrangement as a matter of constitutional priority.

## The PESA extension and its significance

Parliament exercised the clause (3) power in 1996 by enacting the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996 — known as PESA — which extended Part IX's panchayat provisions to Scheduled Areas with substantial modifications that required Gram Sabhas to play a central governance role, gave tribal communities authority over minor forest produce and natural resources, and insisted on gram sabha consent for land acquisition. No equivalent legislation has been enacted to extend Part IXA's municipal provisions to Scheduled Areas with similarly adapted protections for urban tribal communities. This gap is significant because India's tribal-majority districts include urban agglomerations — district headquarters, mining towns, and market centres — where residents have the same constitutional entitlement to meaningful local self-government as residents elsewhere. The absence of a PESA-equivalent for urban tribal areas means that these towns are often governed by State municipal bodies whose frameworks do not reflect tribal governance traditions or land rights protections.

## Clause (3) and the Article 368 bypass

The provision that a parliamentary extension law "shall not be deemed to be an amendment of this Constitution for the purposes of article 368" has doctrinal significance beyond the specific context of tribal areas. It is a constitutional instruction to treat a law that alters the territorial scope of a constitutional Part as ordinary legislation rather than a constitutional amendment, thereby avoiding the special majority and ratification requirements of Article 368. Similar non-amendment clauses appear elsewhere in the Constitution (including in Article 243M for Part IX). The practical effect is to lower the legislative threshold for Parliament to extend protective constitutional frameworks to new areas, while leaving the special protections of the Fifth and Sixth Schedules intact unless Parliament explicitly displaces them. Courts have interpreted these non-amendment clauses as carving out a category of parliamentary legislation that is constitutional in effect but ordinary in procedure.

## Comparison with Article 243M

Article 243ZC is the municipal counterpart of Article 243M, which performs the same exclusion function for Part IX panchayat provisions. Article 243M is more detailed, listing specific States (Nagaland, Meghalaya, Mizoram) and providing for a special legislative procedure for those States to opt into Part IX. Article 243ZC does not replicate these State-specific provisions, reflecting the reality that fully tribal States in the north-east had fewer urban agglomerations to which Part IXA would apply, and the political accommodation for those States had already been built into the panchayat framework.

## Current significance

The question of urban tribal governance in Scheduled Areas remains an unresolved constitutional issue. Many of the mineral-rich and forest-fringe towns of Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, and Madhya Pradesh are simultaneously within Scheduled Areas and have significant urban populations. The absence of an adapted Part IXA framework for these areas means that State municipal laws apply without the customary land-rights and community-governance protections that PESA has provided for rural Scheduled Areas. Tribal rights organisations and academic commentators have pressed for Parliament to enact an Urban Tribal Areas Municipal Governance Act analogous to PESA, but as of 2025 no such legislation has been enacted.
