plainEnglish: >-
  Article 85 places three critical parliamentary life-cycle powers in the President's hands — the power to summon each House, to prorogue the Houses, and to dissolve the Lok Sabha — while imposing the only hard constitutional constraint on how often Parliament must meet (no more than six months between sessions). Because the President acts on the binding advice of the Council of Ministers under Article 74, these powers are in practice Cabinet powers, making the dissolution of the Lok Sabha an executive act dressed in presidential form. The Supreme Court's Bommai judgment established that dissolution is justiciable and can be set aside if mala fide, ending the earlier assumption that it was an unreviewable prerogative. The six-month interval rule has come under scrutiny as parliamentary sitting days have declined and as the COVID-19 pandemic raised the question of whether any constitutional exception could justify a longer gap.
officialText: |-
  [85. Sessions of Parliament, prorogation and dissolution.—(1) The President shall from time to time summon each House of Parliament to meet at such time and place as he thinks fit, but six months shall not intervene between its last sitting in one session and the date appointed for its first sitting in the next session.

  (2) The President may from time to time—

  (a) prorogue the Houses or either House;

  (b) dissolve the House of the People.]

## Why it matters

Article 85 controls the rhythm of democratic governance. Parliament cannot legislate, scrutinise
the executive, or vote on confidence unless it is in session — and it can only be in session if the
President summons it. By placing the summoning, prorogation, and dissolution powers formally with
the President but operationally with the Cabinet under Article 74, the Constitution ensures that
the executive controls parliamentary timing without appearing to do so directly. The article's
structural significance lies in what it reveals about parliamentary democracy's design: the
government of the day determines when Parliament meets, how long it meets, and — in the most
consequential exercise — when the Lok Sabha's life is ended and a fresh popular mandate is sought.

The six-month rule in clause (1) is the Constitution's minimum guarantee of parliamentary activity.
It does not guarantee that Parliament will pass legislation, scrutinise the budget meaningfully, or
hold the executive to account — but it does guarantee that Parliament must at least be convened.
This floor-level guarantee has proved increasingly inadequate as the number of actual sitting days
per year has declined from an average of over 120 days in the 1950s and 1960s to often fewer than
60-70 days in recent Parliaments. The gap between the constitutional floor and the democratic ideal
of continuous parliamentary oversight has been a persistent feature of commentary on Indian
parliamentary governance.

The prorogation power under clause (2)(a) is structurally less dramatic than dissolution but is
operationally important. It ends each session, determines which pending business will lapse, and
shapes the legislative calendar. The distinction between prorogation and adjournment — the latter
being a within-session suspension that the House controls through its own rules, the former being
a between-session break that the President (on Cabinet advice) controls — is a crucial separation
between parliamentary autonomy over its own timetable during a session and executive control over
the session structure itself.

The dissolution power under clause (2)(b) is the most politically charged power in Article 85.
Dissolution ends the Lok Sabha and the tenure of every sitting member, triggering a general
election. The political circumstances under which a Prime Minister may advise the President to
dissolve — whether the government has lost a confidence vote, whether the PM seeks an early
election at a favourable moment, or whether dissolution is used to forestall a viable alternative
government — have been extensively litigated and constitute some of the most important
constitutional law on executive-legislative relations.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) imposes the six-month rule. Sessions must be arranged so that no more than six months
elapse between the last sitting of one session and the first sitting of the next. This ensures
Parliament meets at minimum twice a year. The rule is mandatory — it is expressed as an obligation,
not a discretion. India's parliamentary calendar typically has three sessions (Budget, Monsoon,
Winter), though the Winter Session has occasionally been curtailed or cancelled. In 2021, the
Winter Session was not held — marking the first time since the 1975 Emergency that a Winter Session
had been entirely skipped. The Budget and Monsoon Sessions were sufficient to maintain compliance
with the six-month rule, but the cancellation signalled a normalisation of executive control over
parliamentary time that drew significant criticism.

The three-session convention — Budget (February-May), Monsoon (July-August), Winter
(November-December) — has no constitutional basis; it is a parliamentary practice that developed
over time. The Constitution requires only that the six-month gap not be exceeded. A government
could, in principle, call Parliament for two very short sessions per year at six-month intervals
and be technically compliant while ensuring minimal parliamentary scrutiny. The constitutional
provision provides no protection against this outcome, which has led constitutional commentators
to argue that minimum sitting-days requirements should be written into the Constitution or into a
statute.

Clause (2) distinguishes prorogation from dissolution. Prorogation ends a session; pending
business lapses unless a Bill has already been passed by both Houses and awaits Presidential
assent. Dissolution ends the Lok Sabha itself — all members lose their seats, all pending Bills
that have not been passed by both Houses lapse entirely, and a general election must follow. The
Rajya Sabha, being a permanent body under Article 83(1), cannot be dissolved; it continues to
exist between Lok Sabha elections. Jointly introduced Bills, Bills pending in one House, and
addresses that were not completed all lapse on dissolution — requiring the new Parliament to
re-introduce legislation from scratch.

Prorogation also has legal consequences for parliamentary privileges. During prorogation,
parliamentary privilege does not protect members in the same way it does during session. Summons
that were in mid-process at prorogation do not continue automatically. The legal consequences of
prorogation were dramatically illustrated in the United Kingdom in 2019, when the Supreme Court
of the United Kingdom held in Miller II that the advice to prorogue Parliament was unlawful because
it had the effect of preventing Parliament from exercising its constitutional functions during a
critical period. While India's constitutional structure differs — the President is bound by Article
74 — the Miller II reasoning about the purpose-limitation on the prorogation power has been
discussed in Indian constitutional scholarship as potentially applicable to a mala fide Article 85
exercise.

## Key cases

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench held that dissolution
of a Legislative Assembly (the constitutional analogue of Lok Sabha dissolution under Article
85(2)(b)) is subject to judicial review and may be set aside if mala fide or without a rational
basis. The judgment confirmed that dissolution is not an unreviewable prerogative; it must have a
legitimate constitutional purpose. The same logic applies to Union-level dissolution under Article
85(2)(b). Bommai is the foundational authority for the proposition that the President's power under
Article 85 is not absolute and cannot be exercised to further improper political purposes —
including to prevent a party or coalition that commands a majority from forming a government.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Supreme Court set aside the
Governor's advancement of the Assembly session and the ensuing Speaker's rulings, holding that the
power to summon and prorogue the House (under Article 174, the State equivalent of Article 85)
must be exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. The President's Article 85
powers are governed by the same principle under Article 74. The case confirmed that an executive
that summons, prorogues, or advances a session without proper ministerial advice acts
unconstitutionally — a holding directly applicable to the Union Parliament.

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006)** — The Court struck down the dissolution of the
Bihar Legislative Assembly, confirming that dissolution is justiciable and drawing on the structural
logic of Article 85 and its State counterpart to reason that dissolution must be based on a
legitimate constitutional purpose and cannot be used to prevent an incoming government from proving
its majority. The Court's reasoning emphasised that dissolution is a drastic step — it extinguishes
an entire elected House — and must therefore be more difficult to justify than a mere prorogation.

**Miller II (R (Miller) v. Prime Minister, UK Supreme Court, 2019)** — While not an Indian case,
the UK Supreme Court's unanimous judgment that the advice to prorogue Parliament for five weeks
was unlawful because it frustrated Parliament's ability to carry out its constitutional functions
has been extensively discussed in India. The Court held that the prorogation power is limited by
its constitutional purpose and cannot be used to prevent Parliament from performing its scrutiny
role. Indian constitutional scholars have considered whether a similar purpose-limitation applies
to Article 85(2)(a), though the different constitutional context means that the mala fide standard
from Bommai is more likely to be the operative Indian framework.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The basic structure doctrine established in
Kesavananda constrains the amendment power under Article 368. Article 85 is not merely a
procedural provision — the summoning of Parliament is connected to the basic structure of
parliamentary democracy. An amendment that allowed the executive to indefinitely defer calling
Parliament or to keep the Lok Sabha permanently dissolved would be an attack on the basic
structure, regardless of whether the amendment was formally valid under Article 368.

## Current significance

The summoning power's practical consequences have become politically significant as the number of
Parliament's sitting days has declined sharply over the decades. Critics argue that the six-month
rule is insufficient to ensure meaningful legislative scrutiny and accountability, and that a
constitutional amendment raising the minimum sitting-day requirement would better serve
parliamentary democracy. The COVID-19 pandemic illustrated the problem: Parliament was not convened
for extended periods in 2020, approaching the six-month constitutional limit and raising unanswered
questions about whether a pandemic constitutes a circumstance that could justify exceeding it
without a formal Emergency Proclamation under Article 352.

The 2021 decision not to hold the Winter Session reinforced concerns about executive control over
parliamentary time. The Winter Session had been a fixture of the parliamentary calendar since the
Constitution came into force, with the 1975 Emergency being the only prior occasion it had been
skipped. The absence of any constitutional requirement to hold a specific number of sessions or to
meet for a minimum number of days means that the executive's control over the parliamentary
calendar is effectively unchecked as long as the six-month rule is technically observed.
Parliamentary reform proposals have repeatedly included minimum sitting-day requirements of 100-120
days per year, but these proposals have never advanced to legislation.

The dissolution power also intersects with ongoing debates about One Nation One Election. If Lok
Sabha and State elections are to be synchronised, the President's power under Article 85(2)(b) to
dissolve early would need to be constitutionally constrained — an elected government that loses a
confidence vote could not simply dissolve the House and trigger an election; fresh elections would
need to be suspended until the next synchronised cycle, requiring the House to somehow continue
without a stable majority. The Ram Nath Kovind committee report on simultaneous elections (2024)
addressed some of these structural tensions, but the constitutional amendments required to implement
the proposal remain highly contested and would need to address Article 85's dissolution power
directly.
