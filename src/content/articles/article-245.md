  245. Extent of laws made by Parliament and by the Legislatures of States.—(1) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, Parliament may make laws for the whole or any part of the territory of India, and the Legislature of a State may make laws for the whole or any part of the State.

## Why it matters

Article 245 is the foundational grant of legislative competence in the Indian constitutional scheme. It sets the territorial ceiling on State legislative power while simultaneously freeing Parliament from strict territorial limits when national or international interests are engaged. Every question about whether a law is within the competence of Parliament or a State Legislature starts here before moving to the subject-matter allocation in Article 246 and the Seventh Schedule.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes a two-tier territorial framework: Parliament's reach is the entire territory of India or any part; a State Legislature's reach stops at its own borders. The phrase "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" means that territorial reach alone does not determine validity — the legislature must also satisfy the subject-matter competence rules in Articles 246–248 and the Seventh Schedule lists.

Clause (2) addresses extra-territorial operation. A parliamentary law may regulate conduct, persons, or transactions that occur outside India, and the mere fact of such extra-territorial reach is not a ground of invalidity. The courts have, however, insisted that clause (2) does not give Parliament an unlimited licence — there must still be a sufficient nexus between the law and India or Indian interests.

## The extra-territorial nexus doctrine

The Privy Council and then the Supreme Court developed the nexus requirement to prevent clause (2) from becoming a blank cheque. A law purportedly directed at foreign conduct with no real connection to India would fail not because it is extra-territorial but because it lacks legislative competence on its subject matter. The nexus test asks whether the regulated person, act, or property has a real and proximate connection to Indian territory or to interests that India legitimately controls.

This doctrine became important in taxation and securities regulation, where Parliament has sought to tax overseas transactions by persons or entities connected to India, and in consumer protection and competition law applied to platforms operating outside India but affecting Indian markets.

## Key cases

**GVK Industries Ltd v. Income Tax Officer (2011) 4 SCC 36** — The Supreme Court held that Article 245(2) does not confer unlimited extra-territorial legislative power. Parliament may regulate foreign conduct only if there is a real and proximate nexus between the law's subject matter and India. The Court clarified that the bar on challenging a law for extra-territorial operation does not override the requirement that the law must fall within a competence head under the Constitution; it only bars "extra-territorial operation" as an independent ground of attack.

**Mobarik Ali Ahmed v. State of Bombay AIR 1957 SC 857** — An early Supreme Court case establishing that Parliament may legislate against fraud committed abroad when the effects are felt in India. The Court recognised the extra-territorial nexus principle in a criminal law context and held that the Indian Penal Code could validly apply to a non-resident whose fraudulent letters arrived in Bombay and caused loss there.

**State of Bihar v. Union of India** — The Court affirmed that Article 245 read with the three Lists in the Seventh Schedule creates an exhaustive scheme. Residuary power stays with Parliament, State legislative power is territorially confined, and overlaps are resolved through supremacy and repugnancy rules rather than by questioning territorial reach.

## How courts use it

Article 245 is typically invoked at the threshold of constitutional challenges to determine which legislative body had competence to enact a law, before examining whether the law violates fundamental rights or other constitutional provisions. State laws that purport to regulate conduct outside the State's borders are struck down under Article 245(1) if they lack a sufficient State-nexus, even though no equivalent extra-territorial bar is placed on parliamentary laws. This asymmetry reflects the federal design: uniformity on cross-border matters is a Union responsibility.

## Current significance

The rise of platform-economy regulation and cross-border data flows has renewed debate over how far Parliament may extend Indian law to foreign entities with a digital presence in India, testing the nexus principle from GVK Industries. GST's integrated design, which treats supply across State lines as a Union matter under Article 246A, has also re-energised questions about where territorial legislative limits operate. Courts are increasingly asked to define what constitutes a sufficient India-nexus for consumer protection, competition, and tax legislation targeting non-resident actors.
