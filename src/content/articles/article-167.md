  167. Duties of Chief Minister as respects the furnishing of information to Governor, etc.—It shall be the duty of the Chief Minister of each State—

  (a) to communicate to the Governor of the State all decisions of the Council of Ministers relating to the administration of the affairs of the State and proposals for legislation;

  (b) to furnish such information relating to the administration of the affairs of the State and proposals for legislation as the Governor may call for; and

## Why it matters

Article 167 is the State-level counterpart of Article 78, which imposes the same three duties on the Prime Minister in relation to the President. The article gives constitutional content to the consultative relationship between the elected government and the constitutional head. Its purpose is to ensure that the Governor — who retains limited but real discretionary functions under Article 163 — has sufficient information to perform those functions responsibly, without converting the information conduit into a supervisory channel through which the Governor could second-guess or delay Cabinet action. The article's careful design — duties of communication and referral, not duties of approval or consent — reflects the Constitution's fundamental commitment to Cabinet primacy in State executive governance.

The article's placement in the Constitution is deliberate: it follows Articles 163 (requirement of ministerial advice) and 166 (conduct of government business), and precedes Article 168 (the constitution of State Legislatures). Article 167 is thus the bridge between the executive machinery in Articles 163-166 and the legislative structure that follows — it connects the Cabinet's decision-making to the Governor's constitutional position by ensuring both sides of the relationship have an information flow that enables responsible governance.

## Reading the provision

Clause (a) is a standing, proactive duty: the Chief Minister must communicate all decisions of the Council of Ministers without waiting to be asked.

This is not a periodic report or a selective briefing; it is a continuous obligation to keep the Governor informed in real time of what the Cabinet decides — including decisions the Governor may disagree with or that relate to subjects on which the Governor has pending concerns.

Clause (b) is a reactive duty: the Chief Minister must furnish whatever additional information the Governor calls for on administration and proposed legislation.

Governors have sometimes used this clause to demand broad categories of policy documents — Cabinet notes, departmental files, correspondence with the Centre — which Chief Ministers have resisted as exceeding any informational need connected to the Governor's constitutional functions. The correct scope of clause (b) is information relevant to the Governor's constitutional duties, not information sought as a tool of political supervision.

Clause (c) is the Cabinet-referral power: the Governor can require that a decision taken by an individual Minister without collective Cabinet consideration be brought before the full Cabinet. This clause is a check against ministers operating as autonomous decision-makers outside the Cabinet system, not a tool for reversing decisions the Governor disagrees with.

The Governor's referral under clause (c) obliges Cabinet consideration; it does not entitle the Governor to determine the outcome of that consideration, to veto the Cabinet's reaffirmation of the original decision, or to repeat the referral indefinitely.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Constitution Bench held that the Governor must generally act on Cabinet advice and that the information duties in Article 167 serve to enable the Governor to perform his limited discretionary functions responsibly. The Court firmly rejected the proposition that the information-access provisions of Article 167 translate into any supervisory or directive authority over Cabinet decisions.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Supreme Court reaffirmed in the context of the Arunachal Pradesh constitutional crisis that the Governor's right to receive information under Article 167 does not authorise unilateral intervention in the business of the Cabinet or the Legislature. Gubernatorial action unsupported by ministerial advice and not within a recognised category of genuine discretion is constitutionally impermissible, regardless of how the Governor characterises his information requirements.

**Governor of Andhra Pradesh v Panyam Chettis (1960)** — An early Andhra Pradesh High Court ruling that explored the relationship between the Governor's informational rights and the Cabinet's collective decision-making authority, holding that the Governor's position as constitutional head does not give him a right to veto or delay Cabinet decisions even when those decisions have not been communicated to him under Article 167(a).

## The limits of the Governor's information rights

Article 167 does not transform the Governor into a supervisory authority over the Cabinet's substantive decisions. The three duties it imposes — communication, information-supply, and Cabinet-referral — are all procedural in character. The Governor cannot use Article 167(b) to demand information for the purpose of delay, second-guessing, or public criticism of Cabinet decisions. Nor can the Governor use Article 167(c) to engineer a different Cabinet outcome by repeatedly referring the same matter back until the Cabinet changes its position. The Shamsher Singh principle — that the Governor must in general act on and accept Cabinet advice — constrains what the Governor can do with the information he receives under Article 167. If a Governor receives Cabinet decisions under clause (a) and chooses not to act on them — withholding assent, refusing to authenticate orders, or declining to forward legislation — the constitutional failure lies in the Governor's refusal to perform his executive functions, not in any permission the information rights in Article 167 give him to act unilaterally.

## Current significance

Article 167 has become a flashpoint in States where Governors and Chief Ministers are in open political conflict. Governors in Kerala, Punjab, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, and West Bengal have publicly complained of not being kept informed of Cabinet decisions, citing Article 167(a), while Chief Ministers have countered that the Governor was using information requests as pretexts for interference. The provision's structural weakness is its enforceability gap: it imposes a constitutional duty on the Chief Minister but provides no independent judicial mechanism through which a Governor can compel compliance in real time. Courts can eventually review alleged violations, but a Governor who is persistently denied information under Article 167 has no swift constitutional remedy short of communicating his concerns to the President under Article 163's reservation clause or triggering a constitutional crisis through a public dispute. The article thus operates as much through political convention and public accountability as through legal enforcement, making it one of the more porous constitutional duties in Part VI. The Supreme Court's 2023 rulings on the Governor-versus-elected-government conflicts in Tamil Nadu and Kerala reinforced that Article 167 creates duties, not supervisory powers, and that the Governor cannot use the information clause as a ground for obstructing the elected government's constitutional functioning.
