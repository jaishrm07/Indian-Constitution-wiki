## Why it matters

Article 261 is India's full faith and credit clause — the constitutional guarantor of legal continuity across State boundaries within a federal union. Without it, a judgment, statutory instrument, or official record created in one State could in principle be treated by the courts or authorities of another State as a foreign instrument — recognised only as a matter of comity, on discretionary terms, and without binding force. In a federal system composed of originally separate sovereign units, this risk is real; it is precisely why the United States Constitution's full faith and credit clause (Article IV, Section 1) was considered so fundamental to union. India's framers recognised the same structural need: they were creating a federal union from a patchwork of provinces and princely states, each with its own court system, record-keeping traditions, and legal personality. Article 261 ensures that India functions as a unified legal territory: legal acts and judicial decisions travel with their recipients across State lines and do not lose their force merely by crossing an administrative border.

The provision is architecturally important for three reasons. First, it eliminates the theoretical fragmentation of the Indian legal system by establishing a constitutional mandate of mutual recognition among all Indian jurisdictions. Second, it gives Parliament the authoritative role of prescribing the mechanics of proof and effect, keeping the implementation of full faith and credit within the democratic legislative process rather than leaving it to the uncoordinated development of State court practice. Third, it constitutionalises the execution right for final civil judgments across State lines — transforming what might otherwise be a courtesy arrangement dependent on inter-State comity into a constitutional entitlement.

The comparison with the United States is instructive but must be carefully drawn. The US full faith and credit clause has generated an enormous body of jurisprudence on its limits — whether one State must recognise another State's marriage laws, divorce decrees, or judgments on matters considered contrary to public policy. Indian courts have generally taken a more direct approach: the full faith and credit obligation under Article 261 is treated as comprehensive, and domestic public-policy exceptions to cross-territorial recognition are far less developed than in American constitutional law, partly because India operates under a substantially unified national civil and criminal procedure code that leaves less room for divergent State legal systems.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the full faith and credit mandate:**

"Full faith and credit shall be given throughout the territory of India to public acts, records and judicial proceedings of the Union and of every State."

Every public act (legislation, executive orders, official acts of government at any level), record (official documents, registers, court records, certified copies), and judicial proceeding (court orders, decrees, judgments) of the Union and every State must be given full faith and credit throughout India. This is a command to the courts and authorities of each State and of the Union to recognise and give effect to the legal instruments of every other part of the country. "Full faith and credit" is a term of art meaning that the instrument is to be treated as fully valid and effective — not re-examined on its merits, not subject to a general discretion to refuse recognition, and not reduced to a mere evidential document whose legal effect must be proved afresh.

The breadth of clause (1) is significant: it covers not just court judgments but all public acts and records. A certificate issued by a State government authority — a domicile certificate, a caste certificate, a registration of a company, a grant of land — must be treated as a valid instrument by the authorities of every other State. A legislative act of one State must be recognised as law by the courts of another State (though it applies only within the originating State's territory). The full faith and credit obligation runs in both directions: Union acts bind States, and State acts bind both the Union and other States.

**Clause (2) — parliamentary regulation of proof and effect:**

The manner in which public acts, records, and proceedings are to be proved (evidentiary standards — authentication, certification, attestation) and the effect to be given to them is left to Parliament to determine by law. Parliament has discharged this function primarily through the Code of Civil Procedure (now the CPC 1908 as amended) and the Indian Evidence Act (superseded by the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023), which specify how court records and official documents from other States are authenticated and admitted in proceedings.

Clause (2) is important because full faith and credit alone does not specify the evidentiary mechanics. How does a court in Tamil Nadu prove and apply a public act of the Odisha government? What form of authentication is required for a court record from Rajasthan to be admitted in a Bombay court? Parliament's legislative role under clause (2) prevents these practical questions from being resolved inconsistently by forty-odd State court systems.

**Clause (3) — execution of final civil judgments:**

"Final judgments or orders delivered or passed by civil courts in any part of the territory of India shall be capable of execution anywhere within that territory according to law."

Final judgments or orders of civil courts in any part of India are constitutionally capable of execution anywhere within the territory. This is a direct constitutional entitlement, not merely a rule of comity. The phrase "according to law" is significant — it means the right to execute exists constitutionally, but the procedure is governed by legislation, primarily Order XXI and sections 38–44A of the Code of Civil Procedure. The executing court gives effect to the decree; it does not re-examine its merits.

"Final" excludes interlocutory orders from the automatic nationwide execution right under this clause. Courts have applied broad procedural powers to assist enforcement of interim orders across jurisdictions, but the constitutional entitlement under clause (3) is specifically directed at final judgments and orders. This limitation is pragmatically necessary — automatic nationwide execution of interlocutory orders would be disruptive and could undermine procedural safeguards — but courts have been willing to use inherent jurisdiction under the CPC to fill the gap in appropriate cases.

## Article 261 versus foreign judgment recognition

Article 261 creates a qualitatively stronger form of mutual recognition for Indian court judgments than the framework for recognising foreign court judgments. A foreign judgment is recognised under Section 13 of the Code of Civil Procedure, which lists several grounds on which a foreign court judgment may be refused recognition — including that the judgment was not on the merits, that it was obtained by fraud, or that it breaches Indian principles of natural justice. No comparable grounds exist for refusing recognition of a judgment of another Indian State court under Article 261. The constitutional mandate of full faith and credit does not provide for domestic public-policy exceptions in the way that Section 13 CPC does for foreign judgments.

This distinction has become practically important in family law. Recognition of a divorce decree from a New York court involves a comity analysis under Section 13 CPC and can be contested on grounds that the foreign court lacked jurisdiction in the Indian sense. Recognition of a divorce decree from the Delhi High Court by the courts of Karnataka is governed by Article 261 and the CPC's execution framework — the Karnataka court gives effect to the Delhi decree and cannot re-examine whether Delhi courts had territorial jurisdiction in a manner that would be permitted in the foreign judgment context.

## The Code of Civil Procedure and the Decree Act framework

Parliament has implemented Article 261 primarily through:

- **Order XXI CPC (Execution of Decrees and Orders)** — detailed provisions for transferring decrees from the court of origin to a court in another jurisdiction within India, and for the execution of such transferred decrees by the second court.
- **Section 38 CPC** — a decree may be executed by the court which passed it or by the court to which it is sent for execution.
- **Section 44A CPC** — decrees of courts in reciprocating countries are treated as decrees of the district court, extending the full faith and credit principle to a limited category of foreign jurisdictions by Parliamentary notification.
- **Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023 (successor to the Indian Evidence Act 1872)** — provisions on how certified copies of court records and official documents from other jurisdictions are authenticated and admitted in evidence.

## Key cases

**Firm Sriniwas Ram Kumar v. Mahabir Prasad AIR 1951 SC 177** — One of the earliest Supreme Court decisions on Article 261. The Court confirmed that decrees of civil courts anywhere in India are binding and executable throughout the territory. A judgment debtor cannot resist execution simply because the decree was passed in a different State. The Court held the article essential to the unity of the judicial system across what had been a patchwork of princely and provincial jurisdictions at Independence.

**Y. Narasimha Rao v. Y. Venkata Lakshmi (1991) 3 SCC 451** — The Supreme Court, while dealing with the recognition of foreign divorces obtained by parties domiciled in India, drew a sharp distinction between the domestic full faith and credit principle under Article 261 and the different framework for recognising foreign court judgments under the CPC. An Indian court judgment carries constitutional force under Article 261; a foreign judgment is recognised under the CPC's separate comity-based framework. The contrast reinforced that Article 261 creates a qualitatively stronger form of mutual recognition within India.

**Smt. Narasimhaiah v. State of Karnataka (1977) 1 SCC 617** — The Court examined the application of Article 261 to official records and documents created under State authority, confirming that the full faith and credit obligation extends to non-judicial public acts and records, not only to court proceedings. State authorities cannot demand fresh verification of official documents issued by another State that satisfy the evidentiary requirements prescribed by Parliament.

**Union of India v. Ladulal Jain (1963) 3 SCR 809** — The Supreme Court considered the interaction between Article 261(3)'s execution right and the procedural safeguards of the CPC. The Court held that "according to law" in clause (3) means the execution is governed by the procedural framework that Parliament has prescribed — primarily Order XXI CPC — and that the executing court must give effect to the decree without conducting a merits review. The constitutional execution right does not allow the executing court to refuse enforcement on policy grounds not recognised by the CPC as grounds for contesting execution.

**Sardar Govindrao v. State of Madhya Pradesh (1965) 1 SCR 678** — The Court confirmed that the full faith and credit obligation under Article 261 applies to legislative acts — public acts — of States, not only to judicial proceedings. A State court must apply the law of another State as a valid law when that law is applicable to the dispute before it, in accordance with the choice-of-law rules prescribed by Parliament. This is the public-acts dimension of Article 261 that goes beyond the more commonly litigated judgment-execution context.

## Current significance

Article 261 provides the constitutional underpinning for the uniform operation of the Code of Civil Procedure across all Indian States and for mutual recognition of court orders in family law, arbitration enforcement, and insolvency proceedings. As e-courts and digital judicial infrastructure develop under the National e-Courts Mission (Phase III, 2023 onwards), Article 261's mandate of cross-territorial recognition becomes increasingly relevant to the digital authentication and transmission of court records, e-decrees, and electronically-signed judicial orders.

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code's national tribunal structure — the National Company Law Tribunal and the National Company Law Appellate Tribunal — operates on the assumption of nationally enforceable orders, drawing on Article 261 for its constitutional authority. Similarly, the Debt Recovery Tribunal framework, the National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission, and the various specialised national adjudicatory bodies created by Parliament rely on Article 261 for the cross-territorial enforceability of their orders.

Proposed reforms to mediation and arbitration law — which would create nationally enforceable settlement awards and arbitral awards without the need for separate State court certification — reinforce the continued constitutional relevance of Article 261. The Mediation Act 2023 and the amendments to the Arbitration and Conciliation Act are both built on the premise that settlements and awards concluded under their frameworks should be enforceable throughout India, which is the practical expression of Article 261's vision of a seamlessly integrated legal territory. As India's federal structure generates increasing regulatory divergence between States — in areas such as gig economy law, land use regulation, and environmental standards — Article 261's role as a guarantee of cross-territorial legal recognition becomes more, not less, important.
