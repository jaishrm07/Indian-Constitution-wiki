## What this Article does

Article 140 is a broadly worded enabling clause that authorises Parliament to confer supplemental — that is, ancillary and incidental — powers on the Supreme Court to help it more effectively exercise the jurisdiction already vested in it by or under the Constitution. Two constraints bound this authority: the powers must not be inconsistent with any constitutional provision, and they must be directed toward enabling more effective exercise of existing jurisdiction rather than creating entirely new jurisdictional heads. The Article thus occupies the space between express constitutional grants and the Court's inherent jurisdiction, allowing Parliament to fill operational and procedural gaps by legislation.

The provision reflects the framers' awareness that a written constitution could not anticipate every procedural or administrative power the apex court might need. Rather than risking operational lacunae, the Constitution delegates to Parliament the authority to fill such gaps by law, subject to the constitutional consistency constraint. The phrase "supplemental powers" is deliberately open-ended: it reaches powers of procedure, practice, administration, and enforcement that make existing jurisdictional grants workable in practice.

## Reading the provision

The language "not inconsistent with any of the provisions of this Constitution" is a substantive limit. Parliament cannot use Article 140 to override the Court's constitutional independence, to alter the composition of the Court fixed by Article 124, or to strip away a fundamental right of a litigant. What it can do is fill procedural gaps: conferring on the Court the power to punish contempt beyond the narrow original jurisdiction, creating offices like the Court's Registrar-General, enabling the Court to frame rules that have the force of law, or granting criminal appellate jurisdiction additional to that specified in Articles 134 and 136.

The phrase "may appear to be necessary or desirable" grants Parliament a wide margin of appreciation. Courts reviewing Parliament's judgment under Article 140 therefore apply a light-touch standard: if the legislation plausibly advances the effectiveness of the Court's jurisdiction, it satisfies the test. The test is purposive, not strictly necessary.

## How Parliament has used this power

Several landmark statutes draw their constitutional pedigree from Article 140:

**Supreme Court (Number of Judges) Act, 1956** — Originally the Constitution fixed the strength of the Court; Parliament used Article 140 read with Article 124(1) to expand the sanctioned strength from eight to its current ceiling, enabling the Court to dispose of its expanding docket.

**Contempt of Courts Act, 1971** — Parliament codified and extended the Court's contempt jurisdiction, including civil and criminal contempt by authorities outside the original parties, making the Court's enforcement power comprehensive. The legislation rests on both Article 129 (court of record) and the ancillary power under Article 140.

**Supreme Court (Enlargement of Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction) Act, 1970** — This Act enlarged the class of High Court criminal judgments from which an appeal lies as of right to the Supreme Court, supplementing the jurisdiction in Article 134 without inconsistency with the Constitution's scheme.

**Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (as amended)** — Order XLVII and other CPC provisions applicable to the Supreme Court regulate review jurisdiction. Their validity has been sustained on the basis that they supplement the exercise of the Court's review power under Article 137.

**Supreme Court Rules, 2013** — Framed under Article 145, these Rules are themselves enabled in part by Article 140, conferring on the Court detailed procedural and administrative powers over its registry, cause lists, and enforcement machinery.

## Key cases

**Delhi Judicial Service Association v. State of Gujarat (1991) 4 SCC 406** — The Supreme Court's assertion of extraordinary jurisdiction to direct the release of a magistrate held in police custody relied on the combined effect of Articles 129, 136, 141, and 142. The Court noted that Parliament's power under Article 140 to legislate supplemental jurisdiction reinforces — but does not exhaust — the Court's own inherent power as a court of record to protect its process and dignity.

**Supreme Court Bar Association v. Union of India (1998) 4 SCC 409** — The Court drew on Article 140 when examining the limits of contempt jurisdiction exercised by the Supreme Court under the Contempt of Courts Act, holding that Parliament's codification of contempt via a law authorised by Article 140 is constitutionally valid provided it does not curtail the Court's constitutional contempt power under Article 129.

**All India Judges' Association v. Union of India (2002) 4 SCC 247** — In the course of restructuring judicial service conditions, the Court acknowledged that administrative infrastructure supporting the Supreme Court — including cadres, staffing norms, and officer appointments — can be legislated or regulated through measures deriving authority from Article 140, because such infrastructure is necessary for the Court to exercise its jurisdiction effectively.

**Salem Advocate Bar Association v. Union of India (2005) 6 SCC 344** — The Court upheld amendments to the CPC that introduced case management measures applicable in Supreme Court proceedings, reasoning that Parliament's authority to streamline the Court's procedural framework falls squarely within the supplemental powers authorised by Article 140.

## Current significance

Article 140 remains an active constitutional peg for ongoing legislative and administrative reform. Proposals for a dedicated Supreme Court of India Act — consolidating all statute-based supplemental powers into one comprehensive instrument — would draw directly on Article 140. The provision matters in live debates about electronic proceedings, e-filing infrastructure, and virtual hearing frameworks: while the Court has implemented these by administrative order and rules, placing them on a firmer legislative footing via Article 140 would enhance their enforceability against reluctant litigants or State authorities.

The Article also has a role in discussions about establishing a permanent Supreme Court secretariat, regulating amicus curiae appointments, and codifying the Court's original jurisdiction over inter-State disputes. Each of these involves conferring powers "not inconsistent with" the Constitution that make existing jurisdiction more effective — precisely the space Article 140 occupies. As the Court's docket continues to grow and its administrative complexity increases, this enabling clause will remain an important constitutional resource for Parliament and the Court alike.
