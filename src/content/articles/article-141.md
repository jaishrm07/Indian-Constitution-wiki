## Why it matters

Article 141 is the constitutional command that makes the Supreme Court's rulings uniformly binding across India's multi-tiered judiciary — from the lowest district court to every High Court. Without it, the same statutory provision could mean different things in different parts of the country. The provision is the formal basis of stare decisis in the Indian system, but it also raises difficult questions about what exactly "the law declared" means, which parts of a judgment bind, and how the Court can depart from its own earlier decisions.

India's judicial system has approximately 25 High Courts and over 18,000 district and subordinate courts. Without a constitutionally enforced supremacy of Supreme Court precedent, the uniformity of law would be impossible to maintain across this vast structure. Article 141 is the linchpin of that uniformity. When a High Court refuses to follow or distinguishes a Supreme Court ruling without adequate justification, the aggrieved party's remedy is an appeal to the Supreme Court, which can then reassert the binding precedent — an enforcement loop that makes Article 141 self-sustaining in practice.

## Reading the provision

Article 141 contains only one sentence, but its implications range across the entire judicial system and every field of law in which the Supreme Court has ruled.

The phrase "law declared" is narrower than it looks. It covers the ratio decidendi — the legal principle that is essential to the decision — but not every observation or dictum a judge makes in reasoning toward the outcome. Courts distinguish between the ratio, which is binding, and obiter dicta, which are persuasive but not mandatory. Even within the Supreme Court's own judgments, the task of identifying the ratio is often contested, particularly in cases where several judges wrote separately and there is no single majority opinion.

Article 141 binds "all courts within the territory of India." The Supreme Court itself is not named and is not strictly bound by its own earlier decisions in the way an intermediate appellate court is bound by a higher court. However, in practice the Court treats its earlier Constitution Bench decisions as binding on smaller benches, and departures require a bench of equal or greater size. The Court cannot use Article 142 as a substitute for overruling: it cannot simply issue an order "in complete justice" that contradicts the law it has itself declared under Article 141.

The binding force under Article 141 extends beyond the courts in the narrow sense. Tribunals exercising judicial functions are also bound. Administrative authorities making quasi-judicial decisions are expected to follow Supreme Court rulings on the relevant legal questions. Constitutional authorities — the Election Commission, for instance — are bound by the Court's rulings on the scope of their powers. The phrase "all courts" has been read in context to mean all adjudicatory bodies exercising judicial functions, not merely civil courts.

The per incuriam doctrine — recognising that a decision rendered without awareness of a binding precedent is not itself binding — is the principal safety valve. But its invocation is tightly controlled: courts cannot readily dismiss a Supreme Court ruling as per incuriam, and the doctrine operates only in narrow circumstances where the earlier ruling was rendered in ignorance of a directly applicable binding authority. Distinguishing a Supreme Court decision on its facts is permissible; refusing to follow it on the ground that it was wrongly decided is not.

## Key cases

**Municipal Corporation of Delhi v Gurnam Kaur (1989)** — The Court held that it is only the ratio decidendi that constitutes "law declared" within Article 141. Obiter observations, however authoritative in their context, do not bind subordinate courts. This case remains the foundational statement on the ratio-obiter distinction in Indian precedent law. The practical difficulty is that identifying the ratio of a multi-judge Constitution Bench decision — where each judge may write separately — is often a complex interpretive task in itself, one that generates its own downstream disputes.

**Union of India v Raghubir Singh (1989)** — The Supreme Court emphasised that the Article 141 obligation is mandatory, not merely persuasive. The judgment also addressed the per incuriam doctrine, holding that only a decision rendered without considering a directly applicable binding authority qualifies — a threshold high enough to make per incuriam arguments rarely succeed. The Court noted that the remedy for a wrongly decided Supreme Court judgment is an appeal to a larger bench, not a subordinate court's refusal to follow it.

**Central Board of Dawoodi Bohra Community v State of Maharashtra (2004)** — A Constitution Bench definitively settled the internal hierarchy of the Court: a smaller bench cannot overrule or even express doubt about a larger bench's decision. The rule directly implements Article 141 within the Court itself, preventing judge-shopping through the mechanism of bench composition. The judgment also clarified that a three-judge bench cannot even "refer" a Constitution Bench decision for reconsideration by expressing disagreement — it can only refer a question to a larger bench on the ground that a substantial question arises.

**Director of Settlements, AP v M R Apparao (2002)** — The Court held that even a summary Supreme Court order that decides a point of law carries binding force under Article 141, even if full reasons are not recorded. This ruling extends the reach of Article 141 well beyond fully reasoned Constitution Bench decisions and has significant implications for the weight of the Court's day-to-day order-making. Given that the Supreme Court disposes of hundreds of cases summarily each week, this holding means that binding law is created at a volume and speed that far exceeds what any law reporting system can adequately capture.

## Stare decisis within the Supreme Court itself

Article 141 creates an external binding obligation — all courts below the Supreme Court must follow the law it declares. But it also creates an internal norm within the Court: the established practice, shaped by the Dawoodi Bohra case, is that a bench of fewer judges cannot depart from a larger bench's decision. If a three-judge bench believes a Constitution Bench ruling is wrong, it must refer the matter to a larger bench rather than simply declining to follow the earlier decision. This internal stare decisis norm is not textually commanded by Article 141 — the provision speaks of "all courts" and the Supreme Court is not technically a "court" below itself — but it has been treated as constitutionally necessary to ensure consistency and to give the Article its intended effect.

The process of overruling is also governed by emerging practice. The Court has recognised that overruling should be done expressly, not implicitly, and that where a Constitution Bench decision is to be departed from, the later bench must explain why the earlier decision was wrong in law rather than simply reaching a contrary conclusion. Implicit overruling — where a later decision is inconsistent with an earlier one without saying so — creates the most doctrinal confusion, and the Court has repeatedly tried to resolve such conflicts by referring questions to larger benches.

Article 141 also has implications for the functioning of the National Company Law Appellate Tribunal, the National Green Tribunal, and other statutory appellate bodies exercising judicial functions. These bodies are bound by Supreme Court decisions under Article 141 even though they are not courts in the constitutional sense. Whether they are also bound by High Court decisions on the same questions — and whether their decisions are subject to the same Article 141 binding regime if challenged before a High Court — has generated procedural litigation that reflects the complexity of applying a court-oriented precedent doctrine to a system that now has many specialist adjudicatory bodies.

## Article 141 and Article 142 in tension

A persistent doctrinal question is whether the Court can use Article 142's complete-justice power to depart from the law it has itself declared under Article 141. The Court has consistently answered no — Article 142 fills legislative gaps but cannot be deployed to override or bypass binding precedent. The two provisions serve complementary functions: Article 141 ensures horizontal uniformity across the judicial hierarchy, and Article 142 equips the Court to give full practical effect to its decisions. If Article 142 could override Article 141, the entire edifice of precedent-based adjudication would be undermined. This boundary is repeatedly tested in cases where the Court is asked to pass a "one-time" order departing from its own settled rules.

## Retroactive effect of overruling

When the Supreme Court overrules an earlier decision, it declares that the earlier decision was never good law. This retroactive theory of overruling is doctrinally orthodox in India, but it creates serious practical difficulties when parties have acted in reliance on the overruled decision — particularly in tax matters, service law, and criminal procedure where large numbers of concluded transactions may be affected. The Court has developed a practice of "prospective overruling" — announcing that the new rule will apply only to future cases — to soften this impact, but it has treated prospective overruling as an exceptional device. The tension between doctrinal orthodoxy (all prior cases were decided wrongly) and practical fairness (people acted on the old rule in good faith) continues to generate complex litigation at the intersection of Article 141 and the doctrine of legitimate expectations.

The prospective overruling doctrine itself raises Article 141 questions. If the law declared by the Supreme Court is binding on all courts from the date of the decision, a prospective-only ruling appears to contradict the premise of Article 141 — it announces that the law will change on a future date rather than declaring what the law has always been. The Court has not resolved this doctrinal tension fully, and prospective overruling is applied on an ad hoc basis without a principled framework for determining when it is appropriate.

## Conflicting benches and the reference mechanism

One of the practical consequences of having a large court is that different benches sometimes reach conflicting conclusions on the same legal point. When a bench discovers a conflict with an earlier same-size bench, it should — under the Dawoodi Bohra rule — refer the matter to a larger bench rather than simply following one set of cases. In practice, references to Constitution Benches have become more common and the list of pending Constitution Bench matters has grown substantially. This pending docket of referred questions is itself a measure of how actively Article 141 and the internal hierarchy it implies are litigated within the Court.

## Current significance

The volume of Supreme Court decisions has created a persistent problem of conflicting precedents, and the Court now regularly constitutes larger benches to resolve inter-bench conflicts. The distinction between ratio and obiter has become practically significant in rights jurisprudence: when a Constitution Bench decides a case partly on grounds A and partly on grounds B, later courts must determine which is the binding ratio. The relationship between Article 141 and the overruling power of the Supreme Court — and the retroactive effects of overruling — continues to generate litigation, particularly in tax and service-law matters where settled positions are disturbed by later Constitution Bench decisions. The growing use of curative petitions and review petitions as a means of revisiting Article 141-binding decisions also keeps this provision in active debate. At a systemic level, the growth in the Court's output — thousands of reportable judgments each year — and the difficulty of tracking binding precedent across all benches presents an institutional challenge to the uniformity that Article 141 is designed to guarantee.

The increasing availability of electronic databases has improved access to Supreme Court judgments but has also made the problem of conflicting precedent more visible to litigants and advocates who can now search comprehensively. This has led to a greater frequency of arguments that an earlier bench reached a conclusion inconsistent with a later one, forcing the Court to regularly re-examine and clarify its own precedent. Article 141's mandate of uniformity thus continues to drive the Court's docket as much as any substantive constitutional development.

Article 141 has also taken on renewed relevance in the context of international law. Indian courts have increasingly been asked whether decisions of international bodies — the International Court of Justice, international arbitral tribunals, and foreign apex courts — carry any weight in domestic proceedings. The position is that such decisions are not binding under Article 141, which is confined to the Supreme Court of India, but they may be persuasive as comparative material. This distinction between binding domestic precedent under Article 141 and persuasive foreign authority has become important as Indian constitutional law is increasingly in dialogue with international human rights standards.

Article 141 thus operates simultaneously as a rule of judicial hierarchy, a doctrine of precedent, and a structural guarantee of equal treatment under law — the same law must mean the same thing in every court in India. Its effectiveness depends not only on the Court's own decisions being consistent and clearly reasoned, but also on the lower courts faithfully implementing those decisions. The gap between these normative expectations and the reality of a judicial system under enormous pressure of volume is the enduring tension at the heart of Article 141's operation. Strengthening the practical force of Article 141 — through better law reporting, clearer headnotes, judicial education about precedent, and a more disciplined approach to consistency within the Court itself — is one of the most tractable institutional reform challenges in the Indian legal system.

Article 141 is thus a provision of deceptive simplicity whose full operation depends on a sophisticated common-law doctrine of precedent, an internal hierarchy within the Court, and the practical commitment of thousands of judges across India to actually follow what the Supreme Court has decided.
