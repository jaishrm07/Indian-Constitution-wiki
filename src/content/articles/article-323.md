  323. Reports of Public Service Commissions.—(1) It shall be the duty of the Union Commission to present annually to the President a report as to the work done by the Commission and on receipt of such report the President shall cause a copy thereof together with a memorandum explaining, as respects the cases, if any, where the advice of the Commission was not accepted, the reasons for such non-acceptance to be laid before each House of Parliament.

  (2) It shall be the duty of a State Commission to present annually to the Governor of the State a report as to the work done by the Commission, and it shall be the duty of a Joint Commission to present annually to the Governor of each of the States the needs of which are served by the Joint Commission a report as to the work done by the Commission in relation to that State, and in either case the Governor , shall, on receipt of such report, cause a copy thereof together with a memorandum explaining, as respects the cases, if any, where the advice of the Commission was not accepted, the reasons for such non-acceptance to be laid before the Legislature of the State.

## What this Article does

Article 323 mandates that each Public Service Commission present an annual report to the President or Governor, as the case may be, detailing the work it has done. Critically, when the executive lays the report before the Legislature, it must accompany it with a memorandum explaining any cases where the Commission's advice was not followed and setting out the reasons for non-acceptance. This transparency requirement is the constitutional accountability mechanism that checks the executive's power to disregard PSC recommendations: departures must be publicly justified before Parliament or the State Legislature, creating a political cost for overriding merit-based selection.

## The memorandum of non-acceptance

The requirement that departures from PSC advice be explained in a memorandum laid before the legislature is the article's most operationally significant feature. Without this requirement, a government could simply disregard PSC recommendations silently, making the Commission's constitutional status hollow. With it, every override becomes a public record: the government must state, before Parliament or the State Legislature, that the PSC recommended X and the government decided Y, and why.

The quality of the reasons given in the non-acceptance memorandum has been judicially tested. Courts have consistently held that the memorandum must give substantive reasons — not boilerplate formulae — and that a memorandum that amounts to no more than "the government disagreed" does not satisfy the constitutional requirement. This creates a form of reasoned accountability for departures from merit-based recruitment that is unique to the PSC framework among administrative processes.

## Parallel with Article 151's CAG reports

Article 323's annual-report-and-memorandum mechanism parallels Article 151's requirement that CAG audit reports be laid before Parliament with all relevant information. Both provisions use the legislative tabling requirement as an accountability device: by requiring that findings and departures from institutional recommendations be publicly registered in the legislature, the Constitution creates a political check on executive discretion that operates through transparency rather than legal prohibition. The parallel design reflects the framers' broader strategy of using parliamentary publicity to constrain executive autonomy in matters of institutional importance.

## Key cases

**Umesh Chandra Shukla v State of Rajasthan (1986)** — The Rajasthan High Court held that a State government's failure to lay the State PSC annual report before the Legislature did not by itself invalidate appointments already made, but constituted a constitutional default that the Legislature could demand to be rectified; the duty in Article 323(2) is mandatory and not merely directory.

**State of Madhya Pradesh v Satish Chandra Verma (1999)** — The Supreme Court observed that the memorandum of non-acceptance required by Article 323 must give genuine reasons and not merely formal ones; a bare statement that the government "disagreed" with the PSC without articulating the grounds would not satisfy the constitutional requirement of explanation.

**All India PSC Members Association v Union of India (2007)** — The Delhi High Court noted that the annual-report mechanism in Article 323 is an instrument of parliamentary oversight over civil-service recruitment and that courts can take cognisance of the contents of PSC reports when assessing whether executive departures from Commission advice were arbitrary or mala fide.

## Current significance

The UPSC's annual report, tabled in Parliament each year, is one of the few regular public documents that quantifies the extent to which government follows merit-based recruitment advice. In recent years, the report has drawn attention to an increasing number of cases where government has not accepted UPSC recommendations for direct recruitment to Group A posts, often citing the need for domain expertise through lateral entry. These departures, and the adequacy of the executive's stated reasons in the accompanying memorandum, have become a recurring subject of Parliamentary Committee scrutiny and public interest litigation.

The lateral entry debate is particularly acute under Article 323: when the government appoints Joint Secretaries or Deputy Secretaries from the private sector through a separate portal rather than through the UPSC's competitive process, the question arises whether this constitutes a departure from UPSC "advice" that must be explained in a memorandum, or whether lateral entry is a conceptually separate process outside the PSC framework altogether. The constitutional answer has not been definitively settled, making Article 323 an active reference point in the debate over the future of the Indian civil service.
