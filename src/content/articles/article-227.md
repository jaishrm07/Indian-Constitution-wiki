  227. Power of superintendence over all courts by the High Court.— [(1) Every High Court shall have superintendence over all courts and tribunals throughout the territories in relation to which it exercises jurisdiction.]

  (2) Without prejudice to the generality of the foregoing provision, the High Court may—

  (a) call for returns from such courts;

  (b) make and issue general rules and prescribe forms for regulating the practice and proceedings of such courts; and

  (c) prescribe forms in which books, entries and accounts shall be kept by the officers of any such courts.

  (3) The High Court may also settle tables of fees to be allowed to the sheriff and all clerks and officers of such courts and to attorneys, advocates and pleaders practising therein:

  Provided that any rules made, forms prescribed or tables settled under clause (2) or clause (3) shall not be inconsistent with the provision of any law for the time being in force, and shall require the previous approval of the Governor.

  (4) Nothing in this article shall be deemed to confer on a High Court powers of superintendence over any court or tribunal constituted by or under any law relating to the Armed Forces.

## Why it matters

Article 227 gives every High Court a constitutional anchor for its supervisory
role over the entire subordinate judicial system within its territory. Without
this provision, the High Court's authority over subordinate courts would rest
only on appellate and revisional jurisdiction — jurisdiction that can be
created, defined, and limited by legislation. Article 227's superintendence
power is constitutionally granted and therefore cannot be removed by an ordinary
statute or a tribunal ouster clause.

The distinction between superintendence and appellate jurisdiction matters
enormously in practice. A legislature can, by statute, make the orders of a
particular tribunal or court final and non-appealable. What it cannot do —
after L. Chandra Kumar (1997) — is exclude the High Court's Article 227
superintendence. Litigants therefore always retain a constitutional avenue to
the High Court to challenge jurisdictional excesses or failures of justice, even
when ordinary appeals are closed.

## Reading the provision

Article 227(1) confers superintendence over "all courts and tribunals throughout
the territories." The word "tribunals" was added by the Constitution
(Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976, reflecting the proliferation of statutory
adjudicatory bodies outside the court system. The specific powers in clauses
(2) and (3) — calling for returns, prescribing rules and forms, settling fee
tables — are administrative in character. They represent the High Court's
authority to regulate the procedural machinery of all subordinate courts, not
just to hear appeals from their decisions. Rules and tables framed under these
clauses require the Governor's prior approval and must not contradict existing
law.

Clause (4)'s exclusion of Armed Forces courts and tribunals from Article 227
superintendence reflects the special constitutional status of military justice,
which is governed by a separate statutory framework and is subject to the
Supreme Court's jurisdiction directly.

The scope of Article 227 superintendence is narrower than Article 226 writ
jurisdiction in one respect: it does not extend to a body that is not a court
or tribunal in the adjudicatory sense. Pure administrative authorities do not
fall within Article 227 superintendence, though they may be subject to Article
226. On the other hand, Article 227 is available for correcting errors of
subordinate courts that fall short of the threshold for certiorari or
prohibition under Article 226.

## Key cases

**Waryam Singh v Amarnath (1954)** — The Supreme Court drew the foundational
distinction between revisional jurisdiction under the Code of Civil Procedure
and the supervisory jurisdiction under Article 227. The latter is broader: it
enables the High Court to correct jurisdictional errors and gross procedural
irregularities that cause injustice even when no formal statutory revision lies.
This case established Article 227 as an independent constitutional ground of
intervention.

**Surya Dev Rai v Ram Chander Rai (2003)** — The Court provided a comprehensive
analysis of the relationship between Article 226 and Article 227. Article 227
is primarily a power to keep inferior courts within their jurisdiction —
corrective and administrative in character. Article 226 is directed at the
enforcement of legal rights. The Court explained that Article 227 is appropriate
where the High Court supervises a court making a jurisdictional or procedural
error; Article 226 is appropriate where the challenge is to a legal right or
statutory power.

**L. Chandra Kumar v Union of India (1997)** — A seven-judge bench confirmed
that the Article 227 superintendence power is part of the High Court's
constitutionally protected core jurisdiction and cannot be excluded by
legislation creating tribunals. Ouster clauses that purport to make tribunal
orders final and unreviewable are invalid to the extent they prevent the High
Court from exercising Article 227 superintendence. Both Articles 226 and 227
are protected by the basic structure doctrine.

**Radhey Shyam v Chhabi Nath (2015)** — A three-judge bench held that civil
court proceedings cannot be challenged under Article 226 — because a civil court
is not an authority amenable to writ jurisdiction — but may be challenged under
Article 227 superintendence. This clarification sharpened the line between the
two provisions and corrected the practice, sanctioned in Surya Dev Rai, of
treating them as interchangeable for civil court supervision.

## Current significance

Article 227 is one of the most frequently invoked provisions in everyday High
Court practice. In commercial, tenancy, matrimonial, and service matters,
litigants routinely use Article 227 petitions to challenge interlocutory orders
of subordinate courts or tribunals that are not revisable under the relevant
statute. The practical effect is that virtually every order of a subordinate
court or tribunal can, in principle, be brought before the High Court on the
ground that it reflects a jurisdictional excess or failure.

The proliferation of statutory tribunals — for taxation, environmental
regulation, service matters, and consumer disputes — has raised the question
of how far Article 227 superintendence extends to bodies that exercise
quasi-judicial functions but are not traditional courts. After L. Chandra Kumar,
the answer is clear that tribunals cannot exclude Article 227 by statute; what
remains contested is the precise standard of review that Article 227 applies
to specialised tribunal decisions where the High Court ought to show deference
to technical expertise.

The Governor's prior approval requirement for rules made under Article 227(2)
has occasionally become a source of friction when a High Court wishes to
modernise subordinate court procedures but the state government, through the
Governor, withholds or delays approval. This administrative interface between
judicial rulemaking authority and executive oversight is a recurring minor
flashpoint in state-judiciary relations.
