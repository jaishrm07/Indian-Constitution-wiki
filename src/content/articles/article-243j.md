## Why it matters

Article 243J provides the constitutional basis for financial accountability in Panchayati Raj institutions. Panchayats in India are significant recipients of public funds: beyond their own-source revenues, they receive tied grants under centrally sponsored schemes such as the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Scheme (MGNREGS), the Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana, and the Jal Jeevan Mission, as well as the untied grants recommended by the Finance Commission. The cumulative flow of funds through Panchayat accounts runs to tens of thousands of crores annually. Without a robust accounts and audit framework, this expenditure cannot be effectively scrutinised.

The article's simplicity — a single sentence — belies the complexity of the accountability framework it underpins. "Maintenance of accounts" encompasses the design of account heads, the double-entry bookkeeping requirements, the maintenance of cash books and vouchers, and the format of annual accounts. "Auditing" covers both the internal audit functions that states may establish and the external audit role — whether of the state's own audit institutions or the Comptroller and Auditor-General of India. The CAG's role in Panchayat audit has been established through the provisions of the Comptroller and Auditor-General's (Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service) Act, 1971, which gives the CAG the right to audit accounts of bodies that receive substantial government funding.

## How courts use it

Courts have used Article 243J primarily in two contexts. The first is to establish that the accounts of Panchayats are public documents subject to the Right to Information Act and to judicial scrutiny in appropriate cases. High Courts have held that Panchayat financial records are not private accounts but constitutional records of a public institution, giving citizens and audit authorities access to them in public interest proceedings.

The second context involves social audit. Several state laws have enacted social audit provisions that treat the Gram Sabha (acting under Article 243A) as an additional accountability mechanism — a community-based audit that complements the formal audit under Article 243J. Courts have generally upheld social audit frameworks as consistent with the constitutional scheme, treating them as complementary to rather than substitutes for the formal audit that Article 243J requires.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Association for Democratic Reforms (2002)** — While primarily addressing candidate asset disclosure in parliamentary elections, the Supreme Court's broader reasoning about transparency in public institutions and public-funded bodies has been applied to Panchayat accounts, supporting the principle that financial information about institutions performing public functions must be publicly accessible.

**State of Bihar v. Project Uchcha Vidya, Siksha Samiti (2006)** — The Patna High Court addressed accountability for funds flowing through Panchayat bodies under educational schemes, holding that the audit provisions applicable to Panchayats under state law (enacted pursuant to Article 243J) applied to all schemes run through Panchayat accounts, not merely to the Panchayat's own revenues. The case reinforced the broad scope of the audit framework under Article 243J.

**Swaraj Abhiyan v. Union of India (2016)** — The Supreme Court's examination of accountability mechanisms for rural funds engaged with the adequacy of audit frameworks at the Panchayat level. The Court noted that audit findings by the CAG about misappropriation of funds in MGNREGS were not being adequately acted upon, and issued directions for strengthening the accountability chain from the Panchayat up through the state and central governments.

## Current significance

The CAG has consistently reported significant irregularities in Panchayat accounts across multiple states in its annual reports on local body finances. Common findings include unspent grants held in private bank accounts rather than designated Panchayat Fund accounts, vouchers for expenditure that cannot be traced to physical works, and large unreconciled balances in accounts. These findings directly reflect the gap between the constitutional requirement of Article 243J and the capacity and integrity of actual Panchayat accounting systems.

The digital transformation of Panchayat accounting — through platforms such as the Government of India's PFMS (Public Financial Management System) — is gradually improving the auditability of Panchayat expenditure. However, the transition to digital accounts has itself created new audit vulnerabilities, as mismatches between physical records and digital entries, and access control weaknesses, have been flagged by audit authorities. The constitutional framework of Article 243J remains the legal foundation for all these accountability mechanisms, and its permissive language continues to result in uneven implementation across states.
