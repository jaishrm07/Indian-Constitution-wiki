## What this Article does

Article 5 established who was a citizen of India on 26 January 1950, the date the Constitution came into force. It uses a domicile-plus-birth (or long residence) test: a person domiciled in the territory of India who was born there, whose parent was born there, or who had been ordinarily resident in India for five or more years before commencement was automatically a citizen. No application was required — citizenship vested by operation of the constitutional text itself. Articles 6, 7, and 8 overlay this baseline for special categories arising from Partition, and Article 9 removes citizenship from those who had voluntarily acquired foreign nationality.

## The two-limb test — domicile and connection to India

Article 5's test has two cumulative requirements. First, domicile in the territory of India: a person must have made India the country of their intended permanent home, not merely be passing through or temporarily resident. The Supreme Court held in Izhar Ahmad Khan that "domicile" means domicile in India as a single unit — there is no State-level domicile for citizenship purposes, distinguishing Indian citizenship from citizenship in federal systems that recognise sub-national citizenship.

Second, one of the three conditions in clauses (a), (b), and (c) must be satisfied: birth in India; a parent born in India; or five years of ordinary residence in India preceding 26 January 1950. The five-year residence route in clause (c) was especially important for persons who had recently immigrated from neighbouring countries or from other parts of the former British Empire. "Ordinarily resident" means a settled, continuous way of life in India — temporary absences do not break the chain, but a person living principally elsewhere could not qualify.

## The Partition context

Part II was drafted in the shadow of the Partition of British India in August 1947 and the mass displacement it caused. Millions of people moved between what became India and what became Pakistan (both West and East). The framers had to decide who, among this population, would be Indian citizens on commencement day. Article 5 set the baseline for those already domiciled in India's territory with the requisite birth or residence connection. Articles 6 and 7 then created special rules for persons who had migrated to or from Pakistan. The interaction of these articles produced a complete, if complex, code for allocating citizenship across the subcontinent.

Critically, the "territory of India" in Article 5 means post-Partition India, not undivided British India. A person born in what is now Pakistan or Bangladesh does not satisfy clause (a) unless they had also acquired domicile in India and met one of the other conditions.

## The 1935 Act distinction

Articles 6 and 8 use the phrase "born in India as defined in the Government of India Act, 1935 (as originally enacted)," which includes undivided pre-Partition India — current Pakistan territory and what is now Bangladesh. Article 5, by contrast, uses only "territory of India" — meaning post-Partition India. This geographic scope difference is crucial: Article 5 is restrictive, while Articles 6 and 8 are more generous in their ancestral connection test by reaching back to the pre-Partition subcontinent.

## Key cases

**Izhar Ahmad Khan v. Union of India (1962) AIR 1962 SC 1052** — The Supreme Court held that "domicile" in Article 5 means domicile in India as a single unit, confirming that Part II operates on a pan-Indian basis and does not produce State-level citizenship.

**State of Bihar v. Kumar Amar Singh (1955) AIR 1955 SC 282** — An early ruling that examined what constitutes "ordinarily resident" under clause (c), holding that temporary absences do not break the continuity of residence and that the five-year period must be assessed by looking at the person's settled way of life in India.

**Shanno Devi v. Mangal Sain Wason (1961) AIR 1961 SC 58** — The Court confirmed that Articles 5 to 8 form a complete code for citizenship at commencement, that each successive article qualifies the one before, and that the evidentiary onus to prove ordinary residence lies on the claimant.

## Relationship to the Citizenship Act, 1955

Article 5 is a once-and-for-all provision: it determined citizenship at commencement and has no prospective operation. From 26 January 1950 onwards, acquisition and loss of citizenship is governed by the Citizenship Act, 1955, enacted under Article 11. Courts interpreting the Act regularly refer back to Article 5's domicile-plus-birth framework when construing statutory citizenship provisions in historical cases.

## Current significance

Article 5 retains relevance in litigation involving persons whose documents or family histories trace back to Partition-era migration, including National Register of Citizens proceedings in Assam where authorities examine whether an individual or their ancestor was a citizen at commencement or at any specified reference date. The article is also the constitutional baseline against which the Supreme Court evaluates retrospective citizenship claims and the proper scope of citizenship-stripping procedures under the Citizenship Act.
