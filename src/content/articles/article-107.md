officialText: |-
  107. Provisions as to introduction and passing of Bills.—(1) Subject to the provisions of articles 109 and 117 with respect to Money Bills and other financial Bills, a Bill may originate in either House of Parliament.

  (2) Subject to the provisions of articles 108 and 109, a Bill shall not be deemed to have been passed by the Houses of Parliament unless it has been agreed to by both Houses, either without amendment or with such amendments only as are agreed to by both Houses.

  (3) A Bill pending in Parliament shall not lapse by reason of the prorogation of the Houses.

  (4) A Bill pending in the Council of States which has not been passed by the House of the People shall not lapse on a dissolution of the House of the People.

  (5) A Bill which is pending in the House of the People, or which having been passed by the House of the People is pending in the Council of States, shall, subject to the provisions of article 108, lapse on a dissolution of the House of the People.

## Why it matters

Article 107 is the constitutional spine of the bill-passage process. It answers the most basic questions about legislation: where can a Bill begin, what does "passing" a Bill actually require, and what happens to incomplete Bills when Parliament ends a session or is dissolved altogether? The answers shape every government's legislative strategy and Rajya Sabha's ability to influence law even when the government lacks a majority in the upper chamber.

The lapse rules interact directly with Indian democracy's five-year electoral cycle. Every dissolution of Lok Sabha resets the legislative agenda, giving incoming governments a clean slate but also requiring them to re-introduce Bills that their predecessors were unable to pass. This makes Article 107 not merely a procedural rule but a structural feature of democratic accountability: voters at each general election are, in effect, voting on which Bills will be introduced and pursued in the new Parliament.

Article 107 also sets up the relationship between Articles 108, 109, and 110. It establishes the general rule (Bills may originate anywhere, both Houses must agree), while those later Articles establish the exceptions (joint sitting for deadlock, special procedure for Money Bills). Reading Article 107 alone gives an incomplete picture of the bill-passage process; it must be read together with the entire Chapter framework.

## Reading the provision

**The three-readings process:** The Constitution itself does not prescribe "three readings" but the Rules of Procedure of each House do, under the authority the Constitution confers to regulate procedure. A Bill goes through a first reading (introduction and circulation), a second reading (detailed committee and clause-by-clause scrutiny), and a third reading (final vote on the Bill as amended). Article 107(2) then imposes the bicameral requirement: the Bill is not "passed" until both Houses have agreed to the same text, either identically or through a process of amendment and agreement.

**Clause (1) — origin:** Money Bills under Article 110 and Category A financial Bills under Article 117(1) must originate in Lok Sabha. All other Bills — including Constitution amendment Bills — may originate in either House. This gives Rajya Sabha an equal right of first mover on ordinary legislation, a significant power that governments manage through legislative strategy.

**Clause (2) — bicameral agreement:** Both Houses must agree before a Bill is "passed." Disagreement triggers either the Article 108 joint-sitting mechanism (for ordinary Bills) or the Bill lapsing. This is what gives Rajya Sabha its effective veto on ordinary legislation: if the upper house refuses to agree, the only recourse is a joint sitting — a drastic step the government has used only once.

**Clauses (3)–(5) — lapse rules summarised:**

- Prorogation between sessions: Bills do not lapse. Business carries over.
- Dissolution of Lok Sabha: Bills pending in Lok Sabha lapse.
- Dissolution of Lok Sabha: Bills passed by Lok Sabha but pending in Rajya Sabha lapse.
- Dissolution of Lok Sabha: Bills pending only in Rajya Sabha (never passed by Lok Sabha) do NOT lapse.

**Constitution amendment Bills:** The Constitution does not expressly exempt them from the lapse rule, and they are treated as ordinary Bills for the purposes of Article 107. A constitutional amendment Bill pending in Lok Sabha at dissolution thus lapses and must be reintroduced.

**Select Committees and Joint Committees:** Article 107 does not address the committee stage directly — that is governed by the Rules of Procedure under Article 118. But it provides the constitutional backdrop: a Bill referred to a Select or Joint Committee is still a "pending" Bill within the meaning of clause (3), protected from lapsing by prorogation but still vulnerable to lapse on dissolution if it was originally introduced in Lok Sabha.

## Key cases

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1964 (In re: Kerala Education Bill, 1957):** The Supreme Court confirmed in an advisory opinion that bicameral agreement under Article 107(2) is a mandatory constitutional condition, not a formality. A Bill is not an Act until both Houses have agreed on the same text, and no presidential assent can cure a failure of bicameral passage.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd. v. State of Haryana (2016):** The Constitution Bench's analysis of legislative procedure across Articles 107–110 affirmed that origin and passage requirements form an integrated constitutional framework. Courts will examine whether the correct procedure was followed when the constitutional validity of a law is challenged on procedural grounds.

**S. R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994):** The nine-judge bench's examination of dissolution and legislative continuity touched on Article 107(5), confirming that a dissolved Lok Sabha's incomplete legislative agenda cannot be inherited by a new House without fresh introduction, and that dissolution is a constitutional reset of the popular chamber's mandate.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973):** The thirteen-judge bench's foundational discussion of Article 368's special majority requirement necessarily engaged Article 107's general passage framework, clarifying that the special majority requirements for constitutional amendments operate within — and not as exceptions to — the basic bicameral agreement structure that Article 107(2) establishes. Any constitutional amendment Bill that bypasses either House, even with the requisite majority in one House, is not a valid exercise of the Article 368 power.

## Current significance

The lapse rule under clause (5) drives the most intense end-of-term legislative racing. Governments approaching general elections — particularly in the final Rajya Sabha session before dissolution — attempt to clear as many Bills as possible through both Houses before dissolution wipes the Lok Sabha slate. When a government lacks a Rajya Sabha majority, the knowledge that a lapsed Bill must survive both chambers all over again in the next Parliament gives Rajya Sabha enormous informal leverage even without formally blocking legislation.

The prorogation-does-not-lapse rule under clause (3) has become relevant following controversies over executive use of prorogation, particularly at the State level, where governors have been accused of proroguing sessions to prevent votes of confidence. Courts have confirmed that while prorogation does not kill Bills, it does suspend sittings — making it a tool to delay but not to permanently foreclose legislative action.

The Article 107 framework has also generated constitutional debate about what it means for both Houses to have "agreed" on a Bill. When Bills pass Lok Sabha in a session in which the opposition has walked out or is not participating, questions arise about whether minimal participation by one of the Houses is constitutionally sufficient, or whether the agreement contemplated by clause (2) requires genuine bicameral scrutiny rather than formal passage. Courts have generally treated the procedural question of whether both Houses voted affirmatively as sufficient, with Article 122 protecting the proceedings from deeper inquiry, but the normative question about the quality of bicameral deliberation remains live in academic and reform debates.

The three-reading process, conducted under the Rules of Procedure that each House makes under Article 118, is the primary mechanism through which Parliament's Standing Committees examine Bills before they proceed to the third reading. The decision whether to refer a Bill to a Parliamentary Standing Committee or a Select Committee is not constitutionally mandated by Article 107 but is made under the Houses' procedural rules. Increasingly, governments have bypassed committee referral — presenting Bills directly for a vote without committee scrutiny — raising concerns about whether Article 107's bicameral agreement requirement is being met substantively rather than merely formally.

The "deemed passed" mechanism in Article 109 for Money Bills effectively creates an exception to Article 107(2)'s bicameral agreement requirement. For Money Bills, Rajya Sabha's silence or rejection is constitutionally irrelevant — the Bill is deemed passed anyway. This is the most significant structural tension in the Indian bill-passage framework, and one that the pending Rojer Mathew seven-judge bench reference is expected to address by clarifying the boundaries of the Money Bill exception to the bicameral principle Article 107 otherwise establishes as the default rule.
