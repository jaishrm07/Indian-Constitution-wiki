## Why it matters

Article 239 establishes the foundational constitutional model for administering Union territories:
direct presidential control channelled through an appointed administrator. Unlike a State, where
executive authority vests in an elected government under Article 154, a UT has no inherent
constitutional executive of its own. The President's power is plenary subject only to Parliament's
overriding legislative authority. This design reflects the original constitutional choice to treat
certain territories — small, strategically sensitive, recently integrated, or specially circumstanced
— as units requiring a degree of central supervision that the full federal model of statehood would
not allow. The administrator's designation (Lieutenant Governor, Administrator, Chief Commissioner)
is flexible and specified by the President; the title carries no independent constitutional weight.
The source of authority is always the presidential appointment under this article, and the
administrator acts as the President's delegate.

The saving clause in clause (1) — "save as otherwise provided by Parliament by law" — is
constitutionally significant. It makes clear that Parliament can create local legislatures, councils
of ministers, and other institutional structures for particular UTs without amending the Constitution.
Parliament has exercised this power through the Government of Union Territories Act 1963, through
the statutory scheme for Puducherry, and most significantly through Article 239AA (inserted by the
Sixty-ninth Amendment 1991) for Delhi. Article 239 therefore sits at the base of a layered scheme:
it is the default rule, and Parliament holds the power to modify the governance architecture for
individual territories, creating a spectrum from purely centrally administered territories to
partial-federal arrangements. The Delhi cases of 2018 and 2023 are about delineating how much of
the Article 239 presidential model survives once Parliament has created an elected government
through Article 239AA.

Clause (2) addresses the appointment of a State Governor as administrator of an adjoining UT.
This dual-role arrangement has been used for practical administrative reasons — Puducherry has
historically been administered by the Governor of Tamil Nadu acting in a dual capacity. The
constitutional requirement that such a Governor acts "independently of his Council of Ministers"
prevents the State government from using its council's advice-giving role to extend State executive
influence into the UT. When the Governor wears the UT administrator's hat, the State Cabinet cannot
instruct or bind them. This independence requirement reflects the different constitutional basis of
UT administration (presidential, not parliamentary in the State sense) and prevents the informal
absorption of UTs into neighbouring States' executive domains.

## Reading the provision

**The "to such extent as he thinks fit" discretion.** This phrase in clause (1) gives the President
a significant discretion over the extent of delegation to the administrator. The President could
in principle exercise UT executive power personally through the Cabinet, or could delegate virtually
all executive functions to the administrator. In practice, the administrator acts with considerable
day-to-day autonomy, subject to instructions from the Ministry of Home Affairs on behalf of the
President. Courts have held that "acting through an administrator" does not mean the administrator
has independent constitutional authority — any act of the administrator is legally an act of the
President, and the administrator can be overridden, recalled, or given specific instructions at
any time. The administrator is a delegate, not an independent constitutional authority.

**"With such designation as he may specify."** This gives the President complete freedom over the
administrator's title. The current designations — Lieutenant Governor for Delhi, Jammu and Kashmir,
Puducherry, and Andaman and Nicobar Islands; Administrator for Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman
and Diu, Lakshadweep; Lieutenant Governor for Ladakh — are all executive choices reflecting
historical and political considerations, not constitutional requirements. The designation confers no
additional power; the power flows from the presidential appointment.

**Interaction with Articles 239A and 239AA.** Where Parliament has enacted specific governance
legislation for a particular UT, that legislation prevails over the general Article 239 default.
The lex specialis principle means Article 239AA governs Delhi's governance architecture primarily,
but Article 239 continues to operate as the constitutional foundation. The Delhi cases are
precisely about delineating how much of Article 239's presidential power survives once Article 239AA
has established an elected government — and the answer has evolved significantly through the 2018
Constitution Bench ruling and the 2023 Services judgment, and has then been partially reversed by
the GNCTD (Amendment) Act 2023.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debates on Part VIII were relatively brief compared to the extensive
treatment of States' rights. The framers accepted without great controversy that certain territories
— the former French and Portuguese enclaves, the Andaman Islands, the Coorg-type residual areas —
needed a form of administration that could not be assimilated to the full State model. The
administrator model was seen as a transitional arrangement for most territories, with the expectation
that territories demonstrating sufficient administrative capacity would eventually be elevated to
statehood. The saving clause — "save as otherwise provided by Parliament" — was deliberately included
to preserve flexibility for Parliament to grant greater self-governance to individual territories
as and when appropriate, without requiring constitutional amendment each time.

## Key cases

**Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab (1955) AIR 1955 SC 549** — Though decided in a State context,
this foundational ruling on the relationship between executive power and legislative authority
informs how courts treat the President's plenary executive power over UTs under Article 239.
Executive power fills the constitutional space Parliament has not occupied, and the administrator
acts within that space as the President's delegate. The principle that executive power must not
travel beyond the legislative domain has special resonance for UTs where the same authority —
the Union — holds both legislative and executive power.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — The seven-judge Constitution Bench
confirmed that constitutional provisions directing a Governor or President to act through an
administrator must be read against the overall scheme of parliamentary government. Even in UT
administration, the structural preference for accountable governance shapes how Article 239 is
interpreted. The Court cautioned against reading the presidential administration model as creating
an unchecked executive power.

**NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018) 8 SCC 501** — The five-judge Constitution Bench, reading
Article 239 alongside Article 239AA, held that Article 239 provides the general framework but is
substantially qualified for Delhi by the special provisions of Article 239AA. The Lieutenant
Governor cannot act as an independent constitutional authority in matters covered by the Delhi
Legislative Assembly's jurisdiction; the Court used the relationship between Articles 239 and 239AA
to work out the respective domains of the LG and the elected government, holding that the LG must
normally act on the aid and advice of the Delhi Council of Ministers except on matters reserved to
the Union. The judgment affirmed that democratic accountability is a constitutional imperative even
within the UT governance framework.

**Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2023) — Services judgment** — A five-judge
Constitution Bench held that the Delhi government (Council of Ministers) had control over the
transfer and posting of bureaucrats serving in the NCT, subject to the Union's reserved powers
over public order, police, and land. The Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi
(Amendment) Act 2023, passed by Parliament in response, returned service-matter control to the
Lieutenant Governor. The episode is the most recent chapter in the contest between Article 239's
presidential administration model and Article 239AA's elected-government model and illustrates
how Parliament's power to "provide otherwise by law" can be used to tilt the balance back toward
central control.

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — The Court articulated principles about
the limits of executive discretion in constitutional governance that apply analogically to UT
administration: even where the Constitution vests plenary-seeming powers in the President, those
powers must be exercised to advance constitutional purposes and cannot be used arbitrarily or for
political advantage.

**Ladakh UT Governance cases** — Following J&K's reorganisation in 2019, petitions and cases
about the governance structure of Ladakh — which unlike J&K has no legislature — affirmed that
Ladakh is administered under the pure Article 239 model. Courts confirmed that the absence of a
legislature in Ladakh is a constitutional choice Parliament has made, and that democratic deficits
arising from that choice must be addressed by Parliament exercising the saving-clause power to
create appropriate institutions, not by judicial intervention.

## Current significance

Article 239 remains directly operative for the UTs of Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Lakshadweep,
Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu, and Ladakh — all administered without local legislatures
under the pure presidential model. It is also the residual constitutional anchor for Delhi and
Puducherry, since the special provisions for those territories are grafted onto Article 239's
basic framework rather than replacing it. The conversion of Jammu and Kashmir from a full State
into a UT in 2019 brought a large and politically significant territory — with a population greater
than many States — within the UT administration model, making Article 239 newly prominent as a
governance baseline for a region that had previously had the most elaborate autonomous constitutional
status in the country.

The Delhi governance controversy has generated the most sustained constitutional litigation around
Article 239 in the last decade. The contest between the elected Delhi government's claim to
executive autonomy and the Union's claim to presidential oversight through the LG has been partially
resolved by legislative intervention — the GNCTD Amendment Act 2023 shifted the balance back toward
the Article 239 presidential administration end of the spectrum. But the underlying constitutional
tension between Article 239's presidential model and the Article 239AA elected-government model is
unresolved at a doctrinal level and is likely to generate further litigation.

The adequacy of the UT model for large, densely populated territories is a continuing constitutional
debate. Delhi is among the world's largest cities administered without full statehood. Jammu and
Kashmir — returned to UT status after decades as a special-status State — has a population and
administrative complexity far exceeding any other UT. Advocates for restoration of statehood to
J&K and for full statehood for Delhi both invoke democratic accountability as a constitutional value
that Article 239's model, however practically convenient, progressively undermines as a territory
grows. The Supreme Court's 2023 observation in the Delhi services case — that democratic
accountability is a constitutional imperative that limits the extent to which the Article 239 model
can be used to displace elected governance — suggests that the constitutional limits of that model
will continue to be tested.
