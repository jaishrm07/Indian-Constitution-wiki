## Why it matters

Article 239AB is Delhi's equivalent of Article 356, the emergency provision that enables central intervention when constitutional governance at the State level breaks down. It allows the President — acting on a report from the Lieutenant Governor or on any other information — to suspend the operation of any part of the Article 239AA framework and to administer Delhi directly under the basic UT model in Article 239. Critically, it does not trigger a formal Emergency under Part XVIII; it is a targeted UT-specific instrument, but its effect is similar: elected institutions are set aside and direct central administration substituted.

The provision reflects the strategic logic of Delhi's special constitutional status. Because Delhi is the national capital hosting Parliament, the Supreme Court, and the central executive, the Constitution provides the Centre with a mechanism to step in if the elected government or the institutional framework established under Article 239AA ceases to function. The article gives the President broad discretion: the trigger in clause (b) — that suspension is "necessary or expedient" for proper administration — is a wide formulation that goes beyond pure institutional failure and extends to a general governance adequacy test.

## How courts use it

Article 239AB has not been frequently invoked in practice, but courts have used it interpretively to understand the limits of presidential power over Delhi and the conditions under which the elected government's mandate can be overridden. The provision is read in the light of the general principle from Article 356 jurisprudence — particularly S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) — that emergency powers must be exercised in good faith on the basis of relevant and objective material, and that courts will examine whether the conditions precedent for invocation have been satisfied, though they defer on the merits of the executive's assessment.

The article also informs the interpretation of Article 239AA itself: its existence as an emergency override confirms that the ordinary Article 239AA framework is intended to operate with the elected government's primacy, because if the Centre always had the power to override, the emergency provision would be unnecessary.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)** — The seven-judge bench's foundational ruling on the concept of constitutional machinery failure, though decided under State provisions, provides the doctrinal background for reading Article 239AB. The Court held that the constitutional machinery provisions must be read as exceptional measures, not as routine grants of executive authority, and that the President's satisfaction must rest on objective, relevant material rather than on political preference. This framework carries over to Article 239AB interpretation.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — While concerned with Article 356, the nine-judge Constitution Bench ruling set the judicial review parameters for presidential proclamations based on constitutional machinery failure, holding that such proclamations are justiciable, must rest on relevant material, and cannot be imposed on a government that commands a legislative majority. Courts have applied this reasoning when examining the preconditions for invoking Article 239AB.

**NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018)** — The Constitution Bench noted that Article 239AB is the extraordinary mechanism that accompanies the ordinary Article 239AA framework, and that the existence of Article 239AB as an override option reinforces the interpretation that the normal constitutional design gives primacy to the elected government, since the emergency provision would otherwise be redundant.

## Current significance

Article 239AB has remained in the background of the sustained Delhi governance controversy but has not been formally invoked. The ongoing dispute between the elected government and the Centre has instead been fought through legislative and judicial means — the Government of NCT of Delhi (Amendment) Act, 2023 on services, challenges before the Supreme Court, and administrative standoffs over files and appointments — rather than through a formal suspension of the Article 239AA framework.

The broad language of clause (b) — that the President may suspend if it is "necessary or expedient" for proper administration — creates a potential constitutional pressure point. Critics of the central government's approach to Delhi governance have argued that the progressive legislative curtailment of the elected government's powers is an indirect use of the substantive effect of Article 239AB without the procedural constraints and accountability that a formal invocation would carry. Any future formal invocation of Article 239AB would face immediate judicial challenge, and the constitutional validity of the President's satisfaction would be tested against the Bommai standards of justiciability and objective material.
