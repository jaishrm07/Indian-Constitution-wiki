  (a) to make any grant in advance in respect of the estimated expenditure for a part of any financial year pending the completion of the procedure prescribed in article 113 for the voting of such grant and the passing of the law in accordance with the provisions of article 114 in relation to that expenditure;

## Why it matters

Article 116 recognises that the annual budget cycle cannot anticipate every circumstance under which the government needs to spend public money. Elections delay the full budget; unexpected crises create demands that cannot be estimated in advance; one-off expenditures fall outside the regular service year. The article provides constitutionally prescribed instruments for each of these situations while maintaining the core principle that no money leaves the Consolidated Fund without parliamentary authorisation.

The vote on account — used in every election year — is the most practically significant of the three. It keeps the government operational for the period (typically two to four months) between the expiry of the previous year's Appropriation Act and the passing of the full budget by the new government. Without it, the machinery of government would halt on 1 April every election year. The constitutional design is careful to route even this emergency authorisation through Parliament rather than leaving it to executive order.

## Reading the provision

Article 116 provides three exceptional financial instruments that allow Parliament to authorise government spending outside the normal annual budget cycle.

**Vote on account (clause (1)(a))** — the most commonly used — allows Lok Sabha to grant the government funds to operate for the first few months of a financial year while the full budget process is being completed. Critical in election years when the Budget may be presented by a caretaker government or delayed, this is the constitutional mechanism (not the informal "Interim Budget" label) that actually authorises temporary spending.

**Vote of credit (clause (1)(b))** — covers unexpected demands of such scale or indefinite character that they cannot be detailed in the normal estimates format. This instrument is rare and designed for genuine emergencies where the normal specificity of budget estimates is impractical — for example, funding an unforeseen natural disaster response or a rapid military build-up where operational security prevents itemised disclosure.

**Exceptional grant (clause (1)(c))** — covers one-time expenditure entirely outside the current year's regular service — for example, a special commemorative project or a one-off structural reform that does not fit within any regular departmental demand.

All three mechanisms preserve parliamentary sovereignty: clause (2) applies the Article 113–114 procedure, meaning a law must authorise any withdrawal from the Consolidated Fund even for these exceptional purposes.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Bhana Mal Gulzari Mal (1960)** — An early Supreme Court case that confirmed the constitutionality of votes on account as a legitimate interim mechanism, holding that the Article 116(1)(a) procedure does not bypass the constitutional requirement of parliamentary authorisation but rather provides a constitutionally prescribed expedited form of that authorisation pending the full budget cycle.

**State of Jammu & Kashmir v. Triloki Nath Khosa (1974)** — While focused on service law, the judgment's discussion of government financial powers confirmed that executive spending authority derives entirely from constitutional appropriation instruments including votes on account, and that no administrative practice or executive instruction can substitute for the parliamentary authorisation Article 116 requires.

**Madan Mohan Pathak v. Union of India (1978)** — A Constitution Bench case addressing whether government financial commitments could be retrospectively withdrawn, which engaged the principle that authorised expenditure under Appropriation Acts and votes on account creates enforceable obligations, reinforcing the constitutional significance of the Article 116 mechanism.

## Current significance

Votes on account have become a routine feature of the political calendar, presented in election years as part of an "Interim Budget" that the outgoing government tables in February before general elections. The convention is that caretaker governments present only a vote on account without major policy announcements, though this practice has occasionally been stretched — the 2019 Interim Budget under the Modi government included income tax slab changes and a farmers' income support scheme, prompting debate about whether policy-heavy interim budgets stretch or violate the spirit of Article 116. The constitutional text does not itself restrict the content of a vote on account beyond the temporal and purpose limits in clause (1)(a).
