## Why it matters

Article 48 is the only directive principle that directly addresses the protection of a specific category of animal. It places the prohibition of cattle slaughter within the constitutional framework as a directive with both economic and cultural dimensions — the economic case resting on the contribution of cattle to agricultural production and rural livelihoods, the cultural case reflecting the significance of the cow in the religious traditions of a large portion of India's population. The constitutional choice to address this in the Directive Principles rather than the Fundamental Rights or the Fundamental Duties was deliberate: the framers recognised that a right to cow protection in Part III would create an absolute legal bar against slaughter, which was politically and constitutionally unacceptable to communities for whom cattle slaughter is a livelihood or a religious practice.

The Constituent Assembly debates on Article 48 are revealing. Seth Govind Das and others pressed for an outright constitutional prohibition on cow slaughter, arguing that the cow holds a sacred status that the Constitution ought to honour. B.R. Ambedkar and Jawaharlal Nehru resisted, arguing that a Constitution must be secular and cannot enshrine the religious practices of one community as enforceable law. The compromise — placing the protection in the Directive Principles, framing it in economic rather than religious terms — was a deliberate choice to respect both the cultural significance of the cow to many Hindus and the constitutional commitment to secularism and the rights of minority communities. That compromise continues to define the constitutional politics of the provision.

Article 48 addresses India's agrarian foundation and the economic centrality of livestock in rural life. The directive's first limb — to organise agriculture and animal husbandry on modern and scientific lines — reflects the Constituent Assembly's commitment to raising agricultural productivity through better crop varieties, mechanisation, irrigation, and scientific breeding of livestock. Independent India inherited an agricultural economy devastated by repeated famines, partitioned from major grain-surplus regions, and ill-equipped for modern farming. The directive was meant to give constitutional backing to rural development, land improvement, and veterinary science programmes as core functions of the developmental state.

The second limb — prohibiting the slaughter of cows and calves and other milch and draught cattle — is among the most contested provisions in Part IV. Its inclusion was a compromise negotiated in the Constituent Assembly, where it was placed in the Directive Principles rather than the Fundamental Rights or the Fundamental Duties precisely because it was understood to be a matter on which the Constitution could not impose a uniform rule enforceable as a right, but on which it could express a constitutional preference. The provision acknowledges both the economic value of cattle in an agricultural economy and the cultural and religious significance of the cow to large sections of the population, while stopping short of making it an absolute Fundamental Right.

The tension between Article 48's prohibition directive and the rights of butchers, meat traders, and members of communities for whom cattle slaughter is a livelihood or a religious practice under Articles 19(1)(g) and 25 has produced a substantial body of constitutional law. Courts have had to balance a non-justiciable directive against justiciable Fundamental Rights, ultimately treating Article 48 as providing a "compelling state interest" that can justify reasonable restrictions — but not unlimited restrictions — on those rights. The interpretive evolution across seven decades of litigation shows how the relationship between a directive principle and a fundamental right is not fixed: as the Court's composition and judicial philosophy have changed, the weight given to Article 48 relative to Articles 19 and 25 has shifted considerably.

## Reading the provision

The provision contains two distinct constitutional directions. The first — to organise agriculture and animal husbandry "on modern and scientific lines" — is a developmental directive: it imposes an obligation to improve agricultural productivity and animal breeds through modern techniques. The second — to "take steps for preserving and improving the breeds, and prohibiting the slaughter, of cows and calves and other milch and draught cattle" — is a protective directive that directly addresses the rights of specific animals.

The phrase "milch and draught cattle" is the key limiting phrase in the second directive. Milch cattle are those that produce milk; draught cattle are those used for agricultural work. The constitutional language suggests a functional test: the protection is justified by the animal's economic contribution to agriculture and rural livelihoods. This language was the basis for the Supreme Court's initial holding in Hanif Quareshi that restrictions on economically useless old bulls and bullocks could not be justified under Article 48 because such animals no longer fell within the milch-and-draught category.

The provision says the State shall "take steps" for prohibiting slaughter — it does not create an absolute prohibition in itself. This is an important constitutional distinction: Article 48 is a directive to legislate, not a self-executing prohibition. A State without a cattle-slaughter law is not in immediate violation of Article 48; rather, it is obliged to take legislative steps toward the goal the article sets. Once a State enacts such legislation, however, the legislation draws its constitutional warrant from Article 48 and must be assessed against both the directive's purpose and the fundamental rights it restricts.

Article 48 interacts with Article 25 — the right freely to profess, practise, and propagate religion — in a distinctive way. Communities for which cattle slaughter forms part of religious observance (notably Muslims during the festival of Eid ul-Adha) argue that a prohibition on such slaughter violates their religious practice protected under Article 25. Courts have consistently held that secular economic reasons — not religious sentiment — are the primary constitutional basis for cattle protection laws under Article 48, which means Article 25 is not automatically overridden by the Article 48 directive. However, the practical effect of total cattle-slaughter bans on the religious practices of minority communities has never been satisfactorily addressed in the case law.

## Key cases

**Hanif Quareshi v. State of Bihar (1958)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench examined the constitutional validity of total bans on cow and bull slaughter under various State laws. It upheld bans on the slaughter of cows and calves absolutely, and on bulls and bullocks useful for agriculture, relying on Article 48. But it struck down bans on the slaughter of useless old bulls and bullocks, holding that a complete prohibition on economically unproductive cattle would amount to an unreasonable restriction on the right to trade under Article 19(1)(g). The Court's functional cattle-utility test set the framework for cow-slaughter litigation for nearly five decades and reflected the prevailing view that Article 48 was primarily an economic rather than religious directive — permitting restriction on the basis of agricultural utility, not sentiment or tradition.

**State of Gujarat v. Mirzapur Moti Kureshi Kassab Jamat (2005)** — A seven-judge bench revisited and substantially overruled the utility limitation established in Hanif Quareshi. The Court upheld Gujarat's total ban on cattle slaughter, including useless bullocks and buffaloes, on the ground that the economic evidence had changed — cattle have utility not only for draught and milk but also for bio-gas, organic manure, and leather — and that the State's assessment of that utility was entitled to deference under Article 48. The judgment reflected a broader shift toward greater judicial respect for legislative choices on socio-economic matters covered by Directive Principles and significantly expanded the scope of permissible restriction under Article 48. The seven-judge majority effectively read Article 48 as justifying a near-total cattle-slaughter ban, a reading that now governs the constitutional validity of the most sweeping State cow-protection laws. Justice Kapadia's concurring opinion grounded the decision additionally in the concept of the State's power to protect its national resources, framing cattle as a community resource whose protection is within the State's constitutional mandate.

**Abdul Hakim Quraishi v. State of Bihar (1961)** — The Supreme Court examined the impact of cattle-slaughter restrictions on the livelihoods of Muslim butchers and the religious practice of Bakr-Id sacrifice. The Court acknowledged the tension between Article 48 and Article 25 but held that the constitutional directive to protect cattle represented a sufficiently compelling economic state interest to justify restrictions that incidentally affected religious practice, provided the restrictions were reasonable and not directed at the religious practice as such. The case established the principle that the economic justification under Article 48 and the religious practice under Article 25 must be evaluated separately — the State cannot use religious symbolism to justify the restriction, but may do so on economic grounds. This distinction between incidental effect on religious practice (permissible) and targeted interference with religious practice (impermissible) remains the key analytical tool in the slaughter-ban-Article 25 interface.

## Current significance

Article 48 remains highly charged in contemporary constitutional debate. Across multiple States, beef-ban laws and anti-cattle-slaughter ordinances have extended restrictions well beyond the traditional milch-and-draught-cattle category, and litigation about these laws raises questions about the economic rights of farmers who cannot sell aged or injured cattle, the rights of communities that consume beef, the livelihoods of the leather and meat processing industries, and the constitutionality of vigilante enforcement that has accompanied formal legislation.

The Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act and the regulation of cattle markets and transport have become major sites of Article 48 jurisprudence. The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change rules of 2017 regulating cattle markets drew on Article 48 to restrict the sale of cattle for slaughter at animal markets, generating immediate challenge before multiple High Courts and the Supreme Court. The Kerala High Court in Narayan Bhatt v. Union of India stayed the rules, and the Supreme Court subsequently permitted regulated cattle sale. This episode illustrated how Article 48 continues to be deployed as both legislative authorisation and constitutional limit in the regulation of cattle trade.

The interaction between Article 48, Article 25 (religious freedom), Article 21 (right to livelihood), and Article 19(1)(g) (right to practise any trade) continues to generate controversy. Following Mirzapur Moti Kureshi, the constitutional space for challenging total cattle-slaughter bans on fundamental rights grounds has significantly narrowed, but courts are still called upon to address whether restrictions on inter-state transport of cattle, night-time slaughter prohibitions, and the inclusion of non-bovine animals within slaughter-ban laws find sufficient constitutional warrant in Article 48.

The agricultural-modernisation limb of Article 48 is increasingly relevant to debates about climate-resilient farming, sustainable land use, genetically modified crops, and the protection of indigenous cattle breeds through government breeding programmes. It also intersects with Article 48A's environmental mandate in discussions about the ecological footprint of industrial livestock farming and the need to balance productive agriculture with environmental sustainability. These dimensions of Article 48 are generating new litigation and policy argument as the consequences of agricultural intensification and climate change become more acute.

The evolution from Hanif Quareshi to Mirzapur Moti Kureshi illustrates a broader pattern in the Supreme Court's approach to directive principles: the space of permissible State action under a directive has expanded over time as courts have increasingly deferred to the legislature's assessment of social and economic fact. This deference has been criticised as abandoning the Court's role as guarantor of fundamental rights, particularly for the communities — Muslim butchers, Dalit leather workers, tribal communities — whose livelihoods depend on the cattle trade. The constitutional question of whether Article 48 can continue to justify increasingly comprehensive restrictions on fundamental rights, in the face of changing social and political rather than genuinely economic justifications, remains open.

The relationship between Article 48 and Article 51A(g) — the fundamental duty to have compassion for living creatures — has also generated constitutional argument. Some scholars and litigants have contended that the duty of compassion in Article 51A(g) reinforces and extends Article 48's cattle-protection mandate to cover all animals, not merely milch and draught cattle — a reading that has not gained judicial traction but that continues to feature in animal welfare advocacy before courts and in legislative debate about the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals Act.

The economic dimension of Article 48's first limb — organising agriculture on modern and scientific lines — also intersects with the farmers' rights debate. Agricultural reform legislation, including the three contentious farm laws enacted in 2020 and subsequently repealed following farmer protests, was tested constitutionally against Article 48's mandate for scientific organisation of agriculture alongside constitutional provisions about trade and agriculture under the Seventh Schedule. Article 48 was invoked on both sides: proponents of liberalisation argued that market-oriented reform was consistent with the modernisation mandate; opponents argued that the constitutional directive required State support and protection of farming livelihoods, not their exposure to unregulated market forces.

The protection of cattle breeds — the second part of Article 48's first sentence — is a component that has received relatively little attention compared to the slaughter-ban controversy. The National Bureau of Animal Genetic Resources and State-level cattle breed protection programmes draw their constitutional mandate from this part of Article 48. As indigenous cattle breeds face pressure from commercial dairy farming that favours high-yield foreign breeds, the constitutional obligation to preserve and improve native breeds has acquired new urgency in biodiversity and food security debates.

Article 48's agricultural-modernisation directive also connects with the intellectual property rights framework for plant varieties and farmers' rights. The Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights Act 2001, which balances breeders' intellectual property rights with farmers' traditional rights to save and share seeds, draws its constitutional justification partly from Article 48's mandate to organise agriculture on scientific lines and partly from Article 39's redistribution directives. Courts reviewing challenges to the Act's provisions on farmers' varieties have cited the constitutional commitment to agricultural modernisation and equitable distribution as guides to the interpretation of the statute's farmer-protective provisions.

The emerging area of precision agriculture, drone-based crop monitoring, soil health management, and satellite-guided irrigation systems raises fresh questions about what "modern and scientific lines" means for twenty-first-century Indian agriculture. Article 48's mandate is not frozen in the agricultural technology of 1950; it is a continuing obligation to advance the scientific organisation of agriculture. Courts, regulatory bodies, and agricultural departments invoke this mandate when assessing whether government programmes for agricultural technology adoption, rural infrastructure, and credit access are consistent with the State's constitutional obligations.

The constitutional politics of Article 48 are unique within Part IV. No other directive principle has generated the combination of legal controversy, communal tension, and popular mobilisation that the cattle-slaughter provisions have produced. From the Constituent Assembly debates, through the Supreme Court's evolution from Hanif Quareshi to Mirzapur Moti Kureshi, to the wave of beef-ban legislation in the 2010s and the associated violence, Article 48's second limb has been a constitutional fault line. The framers' decision to place it in the directive principles — rather than either the fundamental rights or the fundamental duties — was an attempt to navigate that fault line. Whether it has succeeded in providing a stable constitutional framework for one of India's most contentious social and political controversies remains an open and genuinely difficult constitutional question.

The case law on Article 48 also illustrates more generally how the relationship between a directive principle and a fundamental right can evolve over time. The Hanif Quareshi framework — under which Article 48 justified some but not all cattle-slaughter restrictions — reflected a Court committed to balancing the economic directive against the trade rights of the butcher community. The Mirzapur Moti Kureshi framework — under which Article 48 justifies near-comprehensive restrictions — reflects a Court more deferential to legislative assessments of public interest. This evolution tracks the broader history of Indian judicial philosophy about the relationship between Part III and Part IV: from the early tension of Champakam Dorairajan (1951) to the attempted synthesis of Kesavananda (1973) to the post-liberalisation jurisprudence that has generally accorded greater weight to economic regulation under directive principles. Article 48's case law is a microcosm of that larger constitutional story.

Article 48 is, in the final analysis, a constitutional provision that cannot be understood outside the social, religious, and economic context in which it was drafted and has been applied. Its two limbs address fundamentally different concerns — agricultural development and cattle protection — that happen to share a constitutional home, generating a rich and contested jurisprudence that continues to evolve. The provision illustrates the framers' pragmatic constitutionalism: their willingness to address concrete social and economic realities in constitutional text even where ideal constitutional solutions were unavailable.

Reading Article 48 alongside Article 25's religious freedom guarantees, Article 19(1)(g)'s trade rights, Article 14's equality guarantee, and Article 51A(g)'s duty of compassion for living creatures reveals the Constitution's recognition that some questions — including this one — resist simple resolution, and that constitutional text must instead provide a framework for their ongoing negotiation in courts, legislatures, and civil society.

The interaction between Article 48 and the criminal law of cattle theft, cattle transport, and anti-conversion laws illustrates the breadth of the provision's contemporary reach. State anti-cattle slaughter Acts have increasingly included provisions about transport, possession of beef, and buying of cattle across state borders — provisions that go well beyond what Article 48's economic rationale for protecting milch and draught cattle could support. The constitutional validity of this extended enforcement architecture — including anti-lynching protections raised in response to vigilante violence — represents an ongoing contest between the Article 48 directive and the fundamental rights to life, liberty, and equality under Articles 14, 19, and 21. Courts have not yet delivered a comprehensive framework for evaluating this extended architecture, leaving the outer limits of Article 48's constitutional warrant unresolved.
