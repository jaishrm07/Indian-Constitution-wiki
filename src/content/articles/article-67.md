  67. Term of office of Vice-President.—The Vice-President shall hold office for a term of five years from the date on which he enters upon his office:

  Provided that—

  (a) a Vice-President may, by writing under his hand addressed to the President, resign his office;

  (b) a Vice-President may be removed from his office by a resolution of the Council of States passed by a majority of all the then members of the Council and agreed to by the House of the People; but no resolution for the purpose of this clause shall be moved unless at least fourteen days' notice has been given of the intention to move the resolution;

  (c) a Vice-President shall, notwithstanding the expiration of his term, continue to hold office until his successor enters upon his office.

## Why it matters

Article 67 reflects a constitutional calibration of institutional security. The Vice-President enjoys a fixed five-year term with genuine protection against arbitrary removal — but that protection is considerably weaker than the President's. The President can only be removed by impeachment for constitutional violation under Article 61, requiring a two-thirds majority of each House's total membership and following an elaborate investigation procedure. The Vice-President can be removed by a majority of all Rajya Sabha members with Lok Sabha agreement — no charge of violation is required, no investigation is mandatory, and the threshold is considerably lower.

This difference in removal security is not an oversight — it reflects the different constitutional roles of the two offices. The President is the constitutional head of the Republic, whose authority encompasses the entirety of executive power under Articles 52–53, whose appointment and removal must be insulated from ordinary political fluctuations, and whose institutional independence is a structural feature of the constitutional order. The Vice-President, while the designated presidential successor and a constitutionally important officer, is primarily a Rajya Sabha presiding officer. Since Rajya Sabha proceedings can be disrupted by a presiding officer who loses the confidence of the House's membership, the Constitution provides a removal mechanism that is genuinely available if the upper House finds the Chairman's conduct intolerable — without requiring the extreme constitutional-violation standard needed to remove the President.

The routing of the Vice-President's resignation through the President — rather than through the Speaker, the Prime Minister, or the Rajya Sabha itself — reflects constitutional hierarchy. The President is the senior constitutional officer, and a resignation from the second constitutional office appropriately goes upward to the first. Unlike Article 56(2)'s requirement that the Vice-President communicate a presidential resignation to the Speaker of the Lok Sabha, Article 67 contains no equivalent communication obligation — the President simply receives and acknowledges the Vice-President's resignation.

The fourteen-day notice requirement before a removal resolution can be moved serves a deliberate constitutional purpose. It prevents surprise removal motions that could be moved and passed before the Vice-President has any opportunity to address the charge, prepare a response, or mobilise political support. The notice period creates a constitutionally mandated deliberation window — a minimum cooling-off period that distinguishes removal from a snap vote of no-confidence.

## Reading the provision

The five-year term runs from "the date on which he enters upon his office" — the date of the oath under Article 69, not the date of the election or the election result notification. Under Article 68(1), the fresh election must be completed before the current term expires, anchored to the Article 69 oath date.

Proviso (a) requires resignation "by writing under his hand addressed to the President." This is mandatory form: an oral or informal resignation is constitutionally ineffective. The resignation takes effect when received and acknowledged — the Constitution provides no mechanism for the President to decline a Vice-Presidential resignation.

Proviso (b)'s removal standard — "a majority of all the then members of the Council" — means an absolute majority of the Rajya Sabha's current membership, not merely a majority of those present and voting. The phrase "then members" refers to the actual members at the time the resolution is voted on, excluding vacant seats. The Lok Sabha's role is one of agreement, not initiation: the Rajya Sabha acts first, and the Lok Sabha's concurrence is needed for the removal to take effect. Notably, no specific ground for removal is stated — the Rajya Sabha need not allege misconduct, constitutional violation, or any defined cause.

The structural tension in proviso (b) is that the Rajya Sabha is asked to remove its own presiding officer through a resolution that the presiding officer would normally entertain and put to a vote. Constitutional practice holds that the Deputy Chairman presides over the House when a removal resolution is moved against the Chairman — analogous to the convention that a Speaker does not preside over a motion for their own removal.

## Key cases

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court's survey of Part V observed that the contrasting removal thresholds for the President (Article 61: two-thirds of total membership in both Houses on a constitutional-violation charge) and the Vice-President (Article 67: majority of all Rajya Sabha members plus Lok Sabha agreement, no ground required) reflects the distinct constitutional weight of each office. The Vice-President's greater removability is consistent with a role that is primarily legislative rather than executive.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Constitution Bench's analysis of the relationship between a presiding officer and removal proceedings has direct relevance to Article 67(b). The Court held that a Speaker cannot continue to exercise procedural powers to the prejudice of Members while a resolution for the Speaker's removal is pending. The same logic applies to the Rajya Sabha Chairman: once a removal resolution is given the requisite notice under Article 67(b), the Chairman's continued exercise of partisan procedural powers would be constitutionally suspect.

**Vice-Presidential removal attempt (2023)** — Opposition parties served notice of a removal resolution under Article 67(b) against Vice-President Jagdeep Dhankhar in December 2023 — the first such notice in Indian constitutional history. The motion did not muster the required majority. The episode generated extensive constitutional debate about open procedural questions: whether the Deputy Chairman must preside over the vote, what happens if the Deputy Chairman is also challenged simultaneously, and how "majority of all the then members" is computed. Article 67(b) became a live constitutional provision for the first time.

**Election Commission of India v Returning Officer, Vice-Presidential Election** — Litigation confirmed that Article 68 read with Article 67 requires the Election Commission to complete a fresh election before the five-year term expires, maintaining the same timeline discipline as Article 62(1) imposes on presidential elections.

**Constituent Assembly Debates on Article 67** — Dr B.R. Ambedkar defended the lower removal threshold by noting that the Vice-President's primary role as Rajya Sabha Chairman meant that Parliament should retain a realistic accountability mechanism for the presiding officer. An unreachable removal threshold — like Article 61's — would entrench a dysfunctional presiding officer indefinitely, which would be more harmful to parliamentary functioning than for the executive branch.

## Current significance

The lower removal threshold of Article 67 compared to Article 61 has been tested — at least politically — for the first time in 2023. While the motion failed for want of numbers, it drew attention to several gaps in the removal procedure that no parliamentary rule or precedent had previously addressed. The question of who presides when the removal resolution is actually voted on has been partially answered by constitutional convention (the Deputy Chairman would preside), but there is no express Rule of Procedure of the Rajya Sabha that mandates this. This procedural gap will need to be filled by either a parliamentary rule change or a Supreme Court ruling before any future removal attempt is made in earnest.

The holdover clause of proviso (c) has practical significance in every Vice-Presidential election cycle. The Election Commission schedules Vice-Presidential elections to conclude before the five-year term expires under Article 68(1), but complications could delay the new Vice-President's oath even after the election. In 2022, the incoming Vice-President was elected on 6 August and sworn in on 11 August, while the outgoing term had expired on 10 August. The holdover clause ensured continuity without disruption.

The prohibition on removing the Vice-President without fourteen days' prior notice creates a minimum constitutional protection against surprise removal. If the office were removable without notice, the ruling majority could theoretically create a temporary Rajya Sabha chairmanship vacuum whenever it suited their legislative strategy — for instance, immediately before a critical vote on a Bill they wanted passed without effective presiding-officer intervention. The fourteen-day notice requirement prevents this tactical deployment of the removal provision.
