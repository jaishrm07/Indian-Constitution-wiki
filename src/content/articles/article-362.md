## Why it matters

The privy purse system that Article 362 protected was, at the time of the Constitution's
commencement in 1950, an unusual form of constitutional entrenchment of what were
essentially political concessions made under duress of national integration. The Indian
National Congress had opposed the princely-state system throughout the independence
movement; the guarantees embedded in the Constitution were the price of rapid peaceful
integration. This political tension — between the constitutional commitment to equality
and democratic governance, on one hand, and the constitutional protection of hereditary
privilege, on the other — was always likely to produce confrontation when the political
winds shifted.

Article 362 is a spent provision — it was omitted from the Constitution over fifty
years ago — but its constitutional history illuminates several enduring principles
about the limits of executive power, the scope of the amending power under Article 368,
and the relationship between political bargains and justiciable constitutional rights.
The episode it encapsulates is one of the most dramatic confrontations in Indian
constitutional history: an elected government's attempt to extinguish a constitutional
right by executive order, a Supreme Court ruling vindicating constitutional supremacy,
and Parliament's subsequent use of its amending power to achieve by amendment what it
could not achieve by executive fiat.

The integration of the Indian princely states between 1947 and 1950 was one of the
largest political consolidations in modern history. Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel's
negotiations with the rulers of approximately 565 princely states produced accession
instruments and merger agreements through which the states joined the Indian Union.
In exchange for surrendering sovereignty, the rulers received guarantees of personal
privileges, ceremonial recognition, and — most significantly financially — privy purses.
The privy purse was a tax-free, hereditary payment calculated as a percentage of the
former State's revenues, payable from the public treasury in perpetuity.

Article 362 gave these negotiated commitments constitutional protection, placing them
beyond the reach of ordinary parliamentary legislation. By the late 1960s, the privy
purse system — symbolically associated with the privileges of the old aristocracy and
practically costing the public exchequer tens of millions of rupees annually — had
become politically controversial. Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's government sought to
abolish it, partly as a matter of socialist principle and partly for political advantage
in the 1971 elections. The government's chosen method — a Presidential Order
de-recognising the rulers — was constitutionally inadequate because Article 362's
protections were embedded in the Constitution itself.

The Madhavrao Scindia case is the pivotal legal moment in the Article 362 story. The
Government of India sought to de-recognise the Rulers by Presidential Order, relying
on the constitutional grant of power to the President to recognise Indian States and
their Rulers. The argument was that the same power that recognised could un-recognise —
and that un-recognition would extinguish the constitutional guarantees that attached
to recognised Ruler status. The Supreme Court rejected this reasoning. The recognition
power was a power to honour existing guarantees at the time of recognition, not a power
to revoke recognition retrospectively and thereby extinguish already-accrued
constitutional rights. To extinguish those rights required the formal amendment
procedure of Article 368.

The episode matters constitutionally not because Article 362 itself is of continuing
significance but because of what it illustrates. Constitutional rights — even those that
are historically contingent, politically contentious, and financially costly — cannot
be extinguished by executive action. They require the formal amendment procedure
prescribed by Article 368. When the Madhavrao Scindia Court held that a Presidential
Order was insufficient to override Article 362's protections, it was reaffirming that
constitutional supremacy operates even against politically motivated executive action.
And when Parliament then successfully used the Article 368 process, the episode also
confirmed that constitutional entrenchment does not mean constitutional permanence —
Parliament retains the power to amend, including to abolish, constitutionally protected
rights, provided it follows the proper procedure.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 362 as originally enacted read: "In the exercise of his powers in
the recognition of Indian States and the granting of rights to Rulers of Indian States,
the President shall have due regard to the guarantee or assurance given to the Ruler of
any Indian State by or under any treaty, agreement, covenant, engagement, sanad or other
similar instrument with respect to the rights, privileges, and dignities of the Ruler,
and shall at the time of such recognition prescribe and guarantee such rights, privileges,
and dignities." This formulation — centred on the guarantee given at the time of
recognition — was interpreted by the Courts as conferring justiciable constitutional
rights on the rulers, not merely a policy direction to the President.

The provision's structure reflected the transitional constitutional architecture of
post-Independence integration. The Constitution needed to accommodate both the political
reality that hundreds of princely states had been absorbed under negotiated terms, and
the constitutional principle that a unified republic could not indefinitely maintain
special classes of hereditary privilege. Article 362 attempted this accommodation by
constitutionalising the commitments made at the time of accession, while leaving
Parliament with the ultimate power to amend those guarantees through Article 368.

The interplay between Article 362 and Article 363 in this context is important. Article
363 bars courts from exercising jurisdiction over disputes arising out of pre-constitutional
accession instruments. Article 362, by contrast, created a distinct constitutional right
that was enforceable in the courts — not as a dispute about the instrument itself, but
as a challenge to executive action inconsistent with constitutionally protected rights.
The Madhavrao Scindia case turned precisely on this distinction: the plaintiffs were
not asking courts to interpret their accession instruments (barred by Article 363) but
to prevent the executive from overriding the constitutional protection that Article 362
had provided for rights arising from those instruments.

## Key cases

The litigation around Article 362 produced some of the clearest statements in Indian
constitutional law about the limits of executive power in the face of constitutional
entrenchment, and about the difference between administrative discretion and
constitutional amendment.

**Madhav Rao Scindia v Union of India (1971) 1 SCC 85** — The Supreme Court struck
down the 1969 Presidential Orders de-recognising the Rulers of Indian States as
unconstitutional. The majority held that Article 362 conferred justiciable constitutional
rights on the Rulers and that those rights could not be extinguished without following
the Article 368 amendment procedure. The Court found that the attempts to use executive
orders to abolish privy purses and de-recognise the rulers were unconstitutional. The
decision directly forced Parliament to proceed by constitutional amendment, leading to
the Twenty-sixth Amendment of 1971.

**State of Gujarat v Vora Fiddali Badruddin Mithibarwala (1964) 6 SCR 461** — An early
case that established the scope of rights protected by Article 362 and the relationship
between those rights and the general constitutional framework for property and revenue.
The Court held that Article 362 rights were justiciable and that the State could not,
through ordinary administrative measures, reduce or modify the guaranteed rights without
following the constitutional amendment route.

**P Madhavan Thampi v Union of India (1972)** — Post-Twenty-sixth Amendment litigation
challenging whether the constitutional omission of Article 362 could itself be challenged
as unconstitutional. The challenge failed. The Court held that the omission was a valid
exercise of Parliament's amending power under Article 368, and that the Kesavananda
basic structure doctrine did not protect privy purse rights as part of the basic
structure. The case confirmed that the Twenty-sixth Amendment was constitutionally valid
and that the rights formerly protected by Article 362 were definitively extinguished.

**Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973) 4 SCC 225** — While not directly concerned
with Article 362, the thirteen-judge bench in Kesavananda considered the broader question
of whether Parliament's amending power under Article 368 is unlimited. The Madhavrao
Scindia episode — in which the Court had forced Parliament to use Article 368 rather
than executive power to extinguish constitutional rights — was part of the context in
which Kesavananda was decided. The judgment established the basic structure doctrine,
which limits Parliament's amending power.

**Yeshwant Rao Mukut Rao Holkar v Union of India (1987)** — A post-Twenty-sixth
Amendment case in which a former ruler sought to maintain certain ceremonial and personal
privileges that had been guaranteed in accession instruments, arguing that their
extinguishment under the Twenty-sixth Amendment violated the principles of natural
justice and the right to property. The Court held that the Twenty-sixth Amendment was
a valid exercise of constituent power under Article 368 and that the former rulers had
no residual constitutional claim to privy purse or other personal privileges following
the amendment.

## Historical context: the integration of the princely states

The political background to Article 362 is essential to understanding what the provision
was protecting and why its removal was constitutionally significant. At Independence,
approximately 565 princely states occupied roughly two-fifths of the subcontinent's
territory. These states ranged enormously in size, wealth, and administrative development
— from Hyderabad (larger than France) and Mysore to tiny estates barely distinguishable
from zamindari holdings.

Sardar Patel and V P Menon of the States Ministry conducted the integration through a
combination of political persuasion, economic inducement, and the implicit understanding
that non-accession in a subcontinent of two newly independent and militarily limited
states would be existentially dangerous. The Instrument of Accession, signed by each
ruler, ceded sovereignty over defence, foreign affairs, and communications to the
Dominion of India while retaining a substantial degree of internal autonomy. Merger
agreements, signed by most rulers by 1949–50, completed the integration by merging the
princely territories into the administrative structure of the Indian Union.

The privy purse commitments made to the rulers as part of this process were themselves
negotiated individually and varied widely. But their constitutional entrenchment under
Article 362 reflected the political promise underlying the entire integration project:
that the rulers and their successors would be treated with dignity and their negotiated
entitlements would be respected. The Twenty-sixth Amendment's abolition of these
entitlements — which came after a bruising political and legal battle — formally
concluded the constitutional chapter of princely integration.

## Current significance

Article 362 has no operative significance today — it was omitted more than fifty years
ago and the rights it protected have been definitively extinguished. Its historical
significance, however, endures as an illustration of several constitutional principles
that remain live. The episode established that constitutional entrenchment is real —
executive action cannot override constitutional rights, even when the rights in question
are politically controversial and the executive is the most powerful elected government
in the country's history to that point.

The episode contributed to the broader constitutional debate that culminated in
Kesavananda's basic structure doctrine — a doctrine that limits Parliament's amending
power in the interest of preserving constitutional identity. The specific rights formerly
in Article 362 are not candidates for basic structure protection, but the more general
principle — that constitutional amendment cannot destroy the essential features of the
Constitution — emerged in part from the political confrontation over the manner in
which rights under Articles 362 and 363 were sought to be extinguished.

For students of comparative constitutional law, the Article 362 episode is a case study
in the constitutional management of post-colonial state formation. The integration of
hundreds of semi-sovereign polities into a democratic republic required political
bargains that were legally embodied in the Constitution. When those bargains became
politically inconvenient, the question was whether democratic majorities could simply
override them by executive action or whether the rule of law required formal amendment.
India's constitutional experience answered firmly: the rule of law required formal
amendment, and the executive had to yield to that requirement.
