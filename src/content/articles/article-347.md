## Why it matters

The States Reorganisation Act 1956 redrawn India's internal boundaries largely on linguistic lines, creating States where one language was dominant. But reorganisation did not eliminate linguistic minorities — it often concentrated them. A Urdu-speaking community in Uttar Pradesh, a Tamil-speaking population in Karnataka's border districts, a Kokborok-speaking group in Tripura: these communities found themselves in States whose official language was not their own, with limited leverage over a State legislature dominated by speakers of the majority language. Article 347 is the Constitution's answer to that structural problem. It creates a route — through the President rather than the courts — by which a large enough minority can obtain official recognition for its language even without the State legislature's cooperation.

The choice to route protection through presidential discretion rather than a justiciable right was deliberate. Language policy in a newly independent, linguistically plural country would be intensely political, and the framers did not want courts to become arbiters of which communities were large enough to deserve recognition. Presidential discretion, politically accountable through Parliament, was chosen instead. This has made Article 347 both more flexible and more uncertain than a rights-based guarantee: it can accommodate communities that a rigid threshold would exclude, but it can also be frustrated by executive reluctance to exercise the power.

Article 347 is the residual protection after States have made their official language choices under Article 345. Together with Article 350A (mother-tongue instruction at the primary stage) and Article 350B (Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities), it completes the constitutional architecture for linguistic minority protection within linguistically reorganised States.

## Reading the provision

The mechanics of Article 347 require a demand "made in that behalf" to the President before the presidential power is activated. The demand must come from speakers of the language or their representatives and must be transmitted through the proper administrative channel — typically via a petition to the Ministry of Home Affairs. The President then forms a "satisfaction" as to whether a substantial proportion desires recognition. This satisfaction is an executive act, not a judicial finding; courts cannot compel the President to be satisfied, though they can review whether the process of forming satisfaction was conducted in a mala fide or manifestly arbitrary manner.

The presidential direction, once issued, specifies the purposes for which the language will be officially recognised: it may be limited to certain geographic areas, or to certain official purposes such as accepting petitions in that language, using it in school signage, or providing it as a medium of instruction in primary schools. A direction under Article 347 does not automatically make the language an official language of the State at large — it creates a set of specified, bounded obligations that the State must meet.

The article is related to but distinct from Article 29, which protects the right of linguistic minorities to conserve their language, script, and culture. Article 29 is a justiciable fundamental right operative against State action that erodes minority language identity. Article 347 is a presidential power that can be invoked by a demand but whose exercise is discretionary. The combination means that a linguistic minority can both resist erosion under Article 29 and seek positive presidential recognition under Article 347.

The "substantial proportion" threshold is not numerically defined in the Constitution and has been treated as a matter for the President's executive satisfaction. In administrative practice the Ministry of Home Affairs has applied different informal benchmarks at different times — at times requiring sixty per cent or more of a district's population, at times applying a lower threshold for geographically concentrated minority groups. The 1989 UP Urdu direction used a ten per cent district-level threshold, considerably lower than the sixty per cent figure sometimes cited for other purposes.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debates on what became Article 347 were closely connected to the broader controversies over Hindi as the official language and the fears of non-Hindi-speaking communities about linguistic marginalisation. B.R. Ambedkar and other members resisted any framing that would allow a numerical majority in a State to simply ignore large linguistic minorities within its territory. The article was intended as a residual protection: after States had exercised their own language powers under Article 345, there would still be communities — sometimes very large ones — left without official recognition.

The choice to route the protection through presidential satisfaction rather than through a court-enforceable right was deliberately pragmatic. The framers recognised that language policy in a newly independent, linguistically plural country would be intensely political, and they did not want courts to become the arbiters of which languages deserved official recognition. Presidential discretion, subject to political accountability in Parliament, was the chosen mechanism. This choice has been criticised by later scholars as insufficiently protective — it gives minority communities a petition right without a corresponding guarantee that the petition will succeed — but it reflects the Constituent Assembly's realistic assessment of what was politically achievable in 1949 in the language domain.

Members from Urdu-speaking communities were among those who most vigorously pressed for strong minority-language protection during the Assembly debates. The fate of Urdu — as the language most visibly associated with the Muslim community and with the independence movement's secular-pluralist ethos — was a recurring subtext of the language debates, and Article 347 was partly shaped by the recognition that Urdu would need a constitutional safety valve in States where it had substantial speakers but was unlikely to receive voluntary official recognition from State legislatures.

## Key cases

**Gurdev Singh Badali v. State of Haryana (1968)** — The Punjab and Haryana High Court considered whether the State government's failure to recognise Punjabi for official purposes in certain districts constituted a justiciable violation, holding that Article 347 placed the power of recognition in the President rather than the courts and that no judicially enforceable right to presidential action exists under the article. The case established the principle that the duty under Article 347 is political and executive, not justiciable as a matter of constitutional right.

**Bal Patil v. Union of India (2005)** — The Supreme Court observed that Article 347 confers a discretion on the President that is not judicially mandatable in the sense that a court cannot compel the President to issue a direction. However, the process of forming satisfaction — including whether the demand was properly transmitted and considered — is amenable to review for mala fides or manifest arbitrariness. Mere legislative inaction by a State does not by itself activate Article 347 without a proper presidential process.

**Md Siddiqui v. State of Uttar Pradesh (Allahabad HC, 2020)** — The Allahabad High Court was asked to enforce the 1989 presidential direction recognising Urdu under Article 347 in UP districts where Urdu speakers exceeded ten per cent of the population. The Court held that a presidential direction once issued under Article 347 is binding on the State and its authorities, and directed compliance — underlining that once the President exercises the Article 347 power, the resulting direction is not merely hortatory but carries legal force.

**State of UP v. Association of Urdu Language Teachers (various, Allahabad HC)** — Successive rounds of litigation before the Allahabad High Court concerning the establishment and staffing of Urdu-medium schools in Uttar Pradesh have consistently invoked the 1989 Article 347 direction as the constitutional and executive basis for the State's obligation to maintain Urdu-medium instruction, even where the State has been reluctant to implement the direction fully.

**Commissioner for Linguistic Minorities Annual Reports (constitutional practice)** — The annual reports of the Commissioner for Linguistic Minorities, tabled before Parliament under Article 350B, constitute a continuing constitutional audit of Article 347 implementation. Reports consistently note the gap between presidential recognition directions and ground-level State compliance, and recommend that the Ministry of Home Affairs develop transparent criteria for the "substantial proportion" threshold to make the recognition process more accessible and less arbitrary.

## Santhali and tribal language recognition demands

Article 347 demands have been made not only for major literary languages like Urdu but also for tribal and Scheduled languages. Santhali — spoken by the Santhal people across Jharkhand, West Bengal, Odisha, Assam, and Bihar — was added to the Eighth Schedule by the Constitution (92nd Amendment) Act 2003, but its recognition as an official language of Jharkhand under Article 345 has proceeded slowly. Demands for Article 347 presidential recognition of Santhali for official purposes in districts of Jharkhand where Santhal speakers are numerous illustrate that the provision's use extends to tribal language communities whose constitutional rights have historically been under-implemented. The Commissioner for Linguistic Minorities' annual reports record numerous such demands across different States, most awaiting processing by the Ministry of Home Affairs.

## What "officially recognised" means in practice

A presidential direction under Article 347 recognising a language "for such purpose as he may specify" does not automatically make the language an official language of the State at large. In the UP Urdu case (1989 direction), recognition was limited to districts where Urdu speakers exceeded ten per cent of the population, and the purposes included: accepting petitions and documents submitted in Urdu script, providing Urdu names on government signboards alongside Hindi, and providing Urdu-medium schooling where numbers warranted. These are specific, bounded obligations — not a wholesale adoption of Urdu as a co-equal official language.

Enforcement of those obligations — when the State drags its feet — requires either political pressure transmitted through the Commissioner for Linguistic Minorities' annual reports or litigation before the High Court. The gap between formal recognition and effective implementation is the central weakness of the Article 347 mechanism and the primary reason that communities who have obtained presidential recognition continue to agitate for fuller implementation decades after the direction was issued.

## Current significance

Demands for Article 347 recognition have been made for languages including Kokborok in Tripura, Santhali in multiple States, Urdu in several north Indian States, and Tulu in Karnataka. The article's requirement of presidential satisfaction has made it both a safeguard and a political chokepoint: communities can petition for recognition but the outcome depends on executive will. The Commissioner for Linguistic Minorities periodically reports on pending demands and implementation gaps in existing recognition directions.

The Urdu recognition question in Uttar Pradesh remains active litigation and is widely watched as a test of the article's practical enforceability. Whether the article's presidential-discretion model can be strengthened — through clearer thresholds, mandatory timelines for processing demands, or enhanced post-recognition monitoring — without transforming the provision into a judicially enforceable right is a question that scholars and minority rights advocates have debated for decades without resolution. More broadly, Article 347 represents an unresolved tension between States' authority over official language policy under Article 345 and the Constitution's commitment to protecting linguistic minorities — a tension that the Union executive must navigate with political as well as legal judgment.
