## Why it matters

Article 349 was the constitutional guarantee that during the most sensitive transition period — the first fifteen years of the Republic — the language of India's courts and its laws could not be changed by a bare parliamentary majority driven by the numerical dominance of Hindi-speaking constituencies. The provision inserted the Language Commission machinery of Article 344 as a mandatory precondition for any parliamentary Bill touching the Article 348 court-language domain. This meant that even if Hindi nationalists commanded a majority in Parliament, they could not introduce a Bill to replace English with Hindi in the Supreme Court or High Courts without the President's prior sanction — and the President could not sanction without considering the Commission's assessment of the consequences for non-Hindi communities in public services.

The article was a concession extracted by non-Hindi members in the Constituent Assembly as the price of their acceptance of Hindi as the official language of the Union. It gave those members a structural guarantee that the most consequential language changes — those affecting courts and legislation — would proceed only through an expert and parliamentary filtering process that was constitutionally required to give weight to non-Hindi interests. This made the fifteen-year transitional period under Article 343 not a countdown to unilateral Hindi imposition but a period of institutionally managed consideration.

The article's expiry on 26 January 1965 coincided almost exactly with the outbreak of the Tamil Nadu anti-Hindi agitations. The coincidence was not lost on participants: the procedural safeguard expired, the political crisis erupted, and the lesson drawn was that removing the institutional gatekeeper without putting a permanent political settlement in place had been a mistake. The 1967 amendment to the Official Languages Act, guaranteeing English's continuation indefinitely, was the political replacement for the lapsed Article 349 protection.

## Reading the provision

Article 349 operated on a precise and narrow scope. It applied only to Bills or amendments "making provision for the language to be used for any of the purposes mentioned in clause (1) of article 348" — that is, Bills changing the language of Supreme Court and High Court proceedings or the authoritative text of legislation. It did not apply to Bills about the official language for general Union government work (those were governed by Article 343 and the Official Languages Act), only to the specific domain of courts and legislative texts covered by Article 348.

The presidential sanction requirement had two conditions precedent that had to be met sequentially. First, the President had to take into consideration the recommendations of the Commission constituted under Article 344(1). Second, the President had to take into consideration the report of the Parliamentary Committee constituted under Article 344(4). Only after both preconditions were met could the President sanction the Bill's introduction, and only after presidential sanction could the Bill be introduced in either House.

This sequential procedural requirement was deliberately demanding. It was not enough for the President to have received the Commission's and Committee's reports — the President had to actually take them into consideration, meaning that a presidential sanction given without genuine engagement with the advisory body outputs would be challengeable. Non-Hindi members who supported the provision understood it as a guarantee that their communities' interests, represented in the Article 344(3) safeguard for non-Hindi public service aspirants, would be on the record before any court-language legislation could proceed.

The fifteen-year period ran from 26 January 1950 to 26 January 1965. During this period, the First Official Language Commission (1956, B.G. Kher) and the Second Commission (1960, M.C. Chagla) both reported, and the Parliamentary Committee under Govind Ballabh Pant reviewed the First Commission's recommendations. The Official Languages Act 1963 was enacted within the Article 349 window, after both the Commission and Committee had reported, and it was the primary legislative product of the consultation process Article 349 mandated.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Article 349 was among the provisions that non-Hindi members from southern States and Bengal pressed for most insistently as a structural protection against Hindi majoritarian steamrolling. T.T. Krishnamachari, K.M. Munshi, and other members who had negotiated the language compromise understood that giving Hindi its constitutional status under Article 343 while protecting non-Hindi communities under Article 348's English-language court provisions required a procedural safeguard that would survive the political pressures of parliamentary majorities.

The key demand was that changes to the court-language regime — which most directly affected lawyers and litigants across all States — should not be possible through ordinary parliamentary action during the transitional period. The mandatory prior consultation with the Language Commission and Parliamentary Committee, and the requirement of presidential sanction, were the mechanisms selected. Hindi nationalist members accepted these constraints because they expected the Commission to recommend Hindi expansion and the President to sanction legislation accordingly; non-Hindi members accepted the language compromise partly because Article 349 gave them a structural guarantee that the transition would be managed rather than imposed.

The fifteen-year duration — tying Article 349 to the same period as the Article 343(2) English-language transitional period — was itself a political choice. It reflected the framers' assessment that the first generation of independent India's administrative and legal class would need time to adapt to any language transition, and that the institutional machinery for assessing the right pace and scope of that transition needed to operate through at least two full Commission cycles (the five-year and ten-year Commissions under Article 344) before Parliament should be free to act without the procedural safeguard.

## The 1956 Language Commission, the 1963 Official Languages Act, and the Shastri formula

The First Official Language Commission, constituted under Article 344 and chaired by B.G. Kher, submitted its report in 1956. The Parliamentary Committee under Govind Ballabh Pant reviewed the recommendations and produced a more cautious report that fed into the drafting of the Official Languages Act 1963. That Act — enacted within the Article 349 window, after the Commission and Committee had both reported — adopted a parallel-languages approach: it preserved English indefinitely alongside Hindi for official Union purposes.

The 1965 date, when Article 349's constraint expired and the Hindi transition was expected to take full effect, instead triggered severe anti-Hindi agitations in Tamil Nadu, Pondicherry, and other southern States. Prime Minister Lal Bahadur Shastri's assurance — delivered in the heat of the crisis — that English would continue as an associate official language alongside Hindi became the "Shastri formula" and the political foundation for continued peaceful coexistence of the two languages in official use. The 1967 amendment to the Official Languages Act translated that assurance into statutory form, filling the gap left by Article 349's lapse.

The lesson drawn by subsequent governments was that Article 349's procedural safeguard had served its purpose during the critical transition period: it had slowed the Hindi lobby sufficiently to allow the political consensus for the Official Languages Act's parallel-language approach to emerge, and it had prevented the enactment of Hindi-in-courts legislation during the period when non-Hindi communities were most vulnerable to majoritarian pressure in Parliament.

## Key cases

**State of Madras v. V.G. Row (1952)** — The Supreme Court's early affirmation that procedural safeguards in the Constitution are not mere technicalities but integral to constitutional legitimacy informs how courts read special-procedure provisions like Article 349 when reviewing language legislation. A law enacted without complying with the mandatory prior-sanction process under Article 349 would be invalid, not merely irregular.

**Official Languages Act, 1963 — Parliamentary debates** — The Bill was enacted within the Article 349 window after the Language Commission and Parliamentary Committee had reported. Parliamentary debates illustrate how Article 349's mandatory consultation requirement shaped the content of the legislation, with non-Hindi members invoking the Commission's own protections for non-Hindi speakers to resist aggressive Hindi-expansion proposals. The Act's parallel-language approach was the direct product of the consultation process Article 349 compelled.

**Constituent Assembly Debates (November–December 1949)** — The debates record the reasoning behind Article 349's consultation requirement: non-Hindi members, particularly from the south, insisted that any parliamentary legislation touching court language must be preceded by the Article 344 advisory process so that their interests could not be bypassed by a simple legislative majority during the transitional years.

**Rajasthan High Court Language Controversy (2022)** — Contemporary proposals to change the language of High Court proceedings or judgments — whether through gubernatorial consent under Article 348(2) or through prospective parliamentary legislation under Article 348(1) — are invariably assessed against the political standards established during the Article 349 era: that language changes in the judicial domain require broad political consensus, extensive consultation, and sensitivity to non-Hindi professional and litigant interests. The article's procedural logic lives on as a constitutional convention even though its mandatory force expired in 1965.

## Current significance

Although Article 349's procedural constraint expired in 1965, its legacy endures as a constitutional convention: major changes to the language of the higher judiciary require broad political consensus rather than bare parliamentary majorities. Current debates about allowing regional languages in the Supreme Court and High Courts proceed with the implicit understanding that the constitutional framework — Article 348 read with the now-lapsed Article 349 discipline — contemplates change only through deliberate and consultative parliamentary action.

Any future Bill seeking to amend Article 348 to permit proceedings in Hindi or other Eighth Schedule languages would need to navigate the political sensitivities that Article 349 was originally designed to manage through institutional process, and would almost certainly revive the federalism and minority-rights concerns that drove the anti-Hindi agitations of 1965. The provision's expiry without adequate political replacement was a constitutional warning about what happens when institutional safeguards lapse before permanent political settlements are reached — a warning that has shaped every subsequent language-change debate in India's political life.

The article also has relevance as a model for constitutional design: it demonstrates how a time-limited procedural safeguard can serve an important function during a critical transition period while leaving Parliament free to act once the institutional consultation processes have run their course. Whether a similar mechanism might be useful for other politically contested transitions — such as changes to reservation policy or electoral law — is a question that constitutional designers have considered in light of Article 349's history.
