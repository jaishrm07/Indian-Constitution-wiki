## Why it matters

Elections are the heartbeat of a constitutional democracy, and their proper conduct depends on a degree of insulation from mid-stream judicial disruption. Article 329 embodies the constitutional judgment that the ordinary courts — including the High Courts under Article 226 and the Supreme Court under Article 32 — should not be available as forums to challenge the electoral process while it is in motion. The framers understood that litigation, if allowed to flow freely during elections, could be weaponised to delay, derail, or delegitimise electoral outcomes before they are even declared. The article therefore draws a sharp line — challenges to delimitation are absolutely barred in any court, and challenges to actual elections must travel through the election petition route after polling is complete.

The bar is not merely a procedural technicality. It reflects a deeper constitutional philosophy about institutional competence. The Representation of the People Act 1951 creates a specialised adjudicatory framework — the election tribunal, now exercised by the High Courts under Part VI of the Act — with rules of evidence, pleadings, and timelines calibrated specifically for electoral disputes. Article 329(b) directs litigants to that specialised forum rather than to the general writ jurisdiction. This channels complex factual disputes about bribery, booth-capturing, impersonation, or corrupt practices to courts equipped to handle them with the appropriate procedural tools rather than to the general constitutional writ stream, which is designed for individual rights enforcement and not for the forensic examination of mass-scale electoral fraud.

The scope of clause (a) is even wider. No court — including the Supreme Court — can question the validity of any law relating to the delimitation of constituencies or the allotment of seats, whether made under Article 327 or Article 328. This absolute bar was deliberately crafted to prevent the kind of litigation that could unravel an entire electoral map. A constituency whose boundaries are under court challenge cannot hold elections in any normal sense; Article 329(a) eliminates that possibility by removing the court's jurisdiction entirely. Challenges to the political or demographic logic of delimitation are left to the political process and to Parliament, not the courts.

## Reading the provision

**Opening words — "Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution":** The non-obstante clause overrides even the fundamental rights chapter and the writ jurisdiction provisions of the Constitution. A petition seeking to challenge a delimitation law by invoking Article 14 (equality) or Article 19 (freedom of speech) or a writ petition under Article 226 or Article 32 is still barred under Article 329(a). The explicit override of the entire Constitution removes any argument that the fundamental rights provisions or the writ jurisdiction articles provide an escape route from the bar. This is one of the strongest non-obstante clauses in the Constitution.

**Clause (a) — delimitation bar:** The bar covers "any law relating to the delimitation of constituencies or the allotment of seats." This includes the Delimitation Act and orders made under it, the Representation of the People Act insofar as it deals with seat allotment, and State legislation enacted under Article 328. The phrase "made or purporting to be made" extends the bar even to laws of questionable validity — a litigant cannot argue that a law only "purports" to be a delimitation law and is therefore outside the bar. Even if a law was enacted without proper authority or through a defective procedure, the court cannot examine whether it was validly made under Article 327 or 328 if its subject matter is delimitation or seat allotment.

**Clause (b) — election petition as exclusive remedy:** The phrase "shall not be called in question except by an election petition" is the crux. "Calling in question" has been interpreted broadly to cover any legal challenge whose practical effect is to contest the validity of an election. The election petition must be presented "to such authority and in such manner as may be provided for by or under any law made by the appropriate Legislature" — currently the Representation of the People Act 1951, which vests jurisdiction in the relevant High Court.

**The Article 329(b) exception — what the bar does not cover:** Article 329(b) bars challenges to elections, not the regulation of future electoral conduct or the enforcement of voters' rights. Courts have consistently held that issuing directions to the Election Commission about disclosure norms, model code enforcement, or procedural standards does not "call in question" any election and therefore does not fall within the bar. The distinction is between a court that disrupts or invalidates an election (barred) and a court that directs the regulatory authority overseeing elections to follow constitutional standards (permissible). This carve-out is the constitutional basis for the Association for Democratic Reforms line of cases.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The framers were alert to the danger of elections being immobilised by litigation. The experience under colonial constitutional arrangements had included instances of nomination rejections being challenged by writ, polling being disrupted by injunctions, and election results being thrown into prolonged uncertainty by legal challenges. The Constituent Assembly's decision to channel electoral disputes through the election petition framework — a specialised statutory route rather than general writ jurisdiction — reflected the judgment that electoral disputes require a focused, time-bound inquiry rather than the open-ended process of constitutional litigation. The non-obstante clause overriding the entire Constitution was included to foreclose the argument that fundamental rights petitions could bypass the bar.

B.R. Ambedkar emphasised in the Assembly debates that democratic elections require finality — not the finality of ignoring fraud or malpractice, but the finality of having a designated forum and timeline for challenging them. The election petition system was designed to provide that finality, and Article 329's bar was designed to make that system exclusive.

## Key cases

**N.P. Ponnuswami v Returning Officer, Namakkal Constituency AIR 1952 SC 64** — This foundational ruling established that Article 329(b) creates an exclusive and comprehensive bar. A candidate whose nomination had been wrongly rejected argued that a writ under Article 226 was immediately available. The Supreme Court rejected this, holding that the election petition is the sole remedy once the election process has begun. The Court articulated the principle that an election is a single, continuous process from the issue of notification to the declaration of result — interference at any intermediate step disrupts the entire process, which is why the article routes all challenges to the post-election petition.

**Hari Vishnu Kamath v Ahmad Ishaque AIR 1955 SC 233** — The Supreme Court clarified the meaning of "calling in question" an election under Article 329(b). The Court held that an election is "called in question" when the legal challenge seeks to set aside the result, annul the declaration, or otherwise affect the validity of the election as a whole. A challenge to a specific administrative act during the election — such as a nomination rejection — amounts to calling the election in question if the practical effect of relief would be to affect the returned result. The case drew the doctrinal line between challenges that go to the validity of the election (barred during election) and those that do not.

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain (1975) 2 SCC 159** — The Constitution Bench considered whether an election petition challenging the return of Indira Gandhi to the Lok Sabha from Rae Bareli was properly before the Allahabad High Court under Article 329(b). The Court confirmed that the election petition route applies even to the highest constitutional office holders — the Prime Minister is not above the election petition jurisdiction. The case is also significant for the Court's rejection of Parliament's attempt to use the Thirty-ninth Amendment to retrospectively validate the election against which the petition had been filed, holding that this was not a legitimate exercise of parliamentary power under Article 327.

**Inderjit Barua v Election Commission of India AIR 1984 SC 1151** — The Court confirmed that the bar under Article 329(a) is absolute and admits of no exception. Even an argument that a delimitation law violates Article 14 or the basic structure doctrine cannot be entertained by any court. The non-obstante clause is comprehensive, and the provision was deliberately designed to insulate the delimitation process from judicial disruption. No matter how compelling the constitutional argument against a delimitation law may appear, it cannot be heard.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India (2002) 5 SCC 294** — The Supreme Court held that Article 329(b) does not prevent courts from issuing directions to the Election Commission about the conduct of elections before the election petition stage. Directions requiring candidates to disclose criminal antecedents and financial assets before polling do not "call in question" any election — they regulate the information environment within which voters make choices. This ruling drew the functional distinction between regulating the electoral process prospectively (permissible) and challenging the validity of an election retrospectively (barred unless through election petition).

**Mohinder Singh Gill v Chief Election Commissioner (1978) 1 SCC 405** — The Court held that Article 329(b) does not bar the Court from reviewing decisions of the Election Commission under Article 324 that do not amount to calling an election in question. The Commission's power under Article 324 to countermand or postpone an election is reviewable after the process concludes, but not through a writ that disrupts the election midstream. Fraud by election officials during polling must be challenged by election petition after the result, not by writ during counting. The case confirmed that the bar is targeted at mid-stream disruption, not at post-election accountability.

## Current significance

The scope of judicial intervention before and during elections remains a contested boundary. High Courts continue to receive writ petitions relating to electoral rolls, candidate nominations, the Election Commission's conduct during elections, and alleged violations of the Model Code of Conduct. Recurring arguments arise about where Article 329's bar begins — whether a petition challenging a nomination rejection "calls in question" an election, or whether it is an independent administrative law challenge that can be disposed of before polling. The Supreme Court has generally held that challenges to nomination rejections must travel through the election petition route, but individual High Court orders have sometimes intervened, generating appeals that can themselves delay the electoral process.

The rise of digital campaigning and social media disinformation has sharpened the tension between the need for timely judicial redress and the constitutional preference for deferring electoral disputes to the post-election petition route. Litigants frequently seek emergency injunctions during election campaigns on grounds of inflammatory speech or fake news. Courts must navigate whether such requests touch the validity of the election itself (barred) or regulate electoral conduct in a manner that does not challenge any election result (potentially permissible). The Election Commission's Model Code of Conduct enforcement — and the question of whether Commission inaction on code violations can be the subject of court-directed mandamus — sits at the edge of the Article 329 bar.

The practical enforcement gap is a serious structural problem. Election petitions are notoriously slow — a petition filed after a 2019 election may not be decided before the 2024 election. This dilutes the deterrent effect of Article 329(b)'s exclusive remedy because a successful petition five years later offers cold comfort to a wrongfully defeated candidate. Multiple Law Commission reports have recommended dedicated High Court benches for election petitions, expedited timelines, and enhanced discovery powers, but Parliament has not yet enacted these reforms. The combination of a robust bar against mid-stream challenges and an underperforming post-election petition remedy means the practical architecture of Article 329 does not fully deliver on its constitutional promise.
