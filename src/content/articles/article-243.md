officialText: |-
  243. Definitions.—In this Part, unless the context otherwise requires,—

  (a) "district" means a district in a State;

  (b) "Gram Sabha" means a body consisting of persons registered in the electoral rolls relating to a village comprised within the area of Panchayat at the village level;

  (c) "intermediate level" means a level between the village and district levels specified by the Governor of a State by public notification to be the intermediate level for the purposes of this Part;

  (d) "Panchayat" means an institution (by whatever name called) of self-government constituted under article 243B, for the rural areas;

  (e) "Panchayat area" means the territorial area of a Panchayat;

  (f) "Population" means the population as ascertained at the last preceding census of which the relevant figures have been published;

  (g) "village" means a village specified by the Governor by public notification to be a village for the purposes of this Part and includes a group of villages so specified.

## Why it matters

Article 243 is the definitional gateway to the entire Panchayati Raj constitutional framework inserted by the Constitution (Seventy-third Amendment) Act, 1992. Every operative obligation and right in Part IX — the mandatory three-tier structure, the reservation of seats, the powers of the Finance Commission, the bar on judicial interference in electoral matters — depends on how the defined terms are read. The definitions are not merely technical; they make deliberate structural choices. By defining "Panchayat" as any institution "by whatever name called," the article ensures that pre-existing local bodies known by regional names (Mandal Panchayat, Zilla Parishad, Gram Panchayat, etc.) are captured within the constitutional framework. The definition of "Gram Sabha" as the body of all persons registered in the electoral rolls of the village — rather than a selectorate or notified body — embeds universal adult franchise at the very base of rural governance.

Two definitions carry particular structural weight. "Village" is not defined by any fixed criterion but is notified by the Governor, preserving administrative flexibility while placing the power to draw village boundaries outside purely local determination. "Intermediate level" is similarly Governor-notified, recognising that the administrative geography of a large state like Uttar Pradesh or Madhya Pradesh is entirely different from a smaller or hill state. This flexibility was deliberate; the Joint Parliamentary Committee on the 73rd Amendment noted that a uniform national definition would not fit the diversity of India's revenue and administrative systems.

## How courts use it

Courts use Article 243's definitions when testing whether a particular body falls within the constitutional protections and obligations of Part IX, or whether a state law that uses different terminology nonetheless creates or affects a Panchayat for constitutional purposes. The phrase "by whatever name called" in the Panchayat definition has been used to prevent states from escaping Part IX obligations by relabelling bodies. High Courts have also applied the Gram Sabha definition when testing PESA compliance in Scheduled Area cases: since PESA vests specific consent powers in the Gram Sabha, the constitutional definition determines who constitutes the Gram Sabha for those purposes and whether a different body substituted by state law satisfies the constitutional requirement.

The population definition — pegged to "the last preceding census of which the relevant figures have been published" — is litigated when delimitation of Panchayat constituencies or reservation calculations are based on stale census data, as happened extensively after the 2011 census figures were delayed for specific sub-district tabulations.

## Key cases

**Samatha v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1997)** — The Supreme Court extensively discussed the Gram Sabha and the structural significance of village-level democratic institutions in the context of Scheduled Areas. While the core holding addressed land transfer restrictions, the Court's reasoning about the role of the Gram Sabha drew directly on the Article 243 definition, confirming that the Gram Sabha is not an advisory or consultative body added by state law but a constitutionally defined democratic institution whose composition is fixed by the electoral roll.

**State of Rajasthan v. Basant Nahata (2005)** — The Court's discussion of what constitutes a "rural area" for the purposes of Panchayat jurisdiction engaged with the Article 243 definitional framework, particularly the interaction between "village" as Governor-notified and the administrative designations used in state revenue and local-government law. The case illustrated how the definitions in Article 243 can create jurisdictional overlap questions when urban expansion encroaches on areas notified as villages.

**Kuldip Nayar v. Union of India (2006)** — In the broader context of electoral rolls and voter registration, the Supreme Court's discussion of the constitutional significance of electoral rolls as the basis of democratic participation is relevant to the Gram Sabha definition, which anchors the composition of the foundational rural democratic body in the voter rolls rather than in any administrative list or property-based register.

## Current significance

The definitions in Article 243 have become increasingly important as Panchayat boundaries overlap with expanding urban agglomerations. The Governor-notified definition of "village" determines which areas remain subject to Part IX rather than Part IXA (Municipalities), a distinction with significant consequences for land use, development authority, and the applicability of PESA in Scheduled Areas.

The population definition's dependency on published census figures has created a structural problem as census cycles lengthen. The 2021 census has been repeatedly postponed, leaving Part IX calculations — reservation quotas, delimitation ratios, Finance Commission assessments — frozen on 2011 figures well into the 2020s. This creates a growing mismatch between constitutional requirements and demographic reality that no State legislature can unilaterally resolve without Parliament publishing the census results.
