  62. Time of holding election to fill vacancy in the office of President and the term of office of person elected to fill casual vacancy.—(1) An election to fill a vacancy caused by the expiration of the term of office of President shall be completed before the expiration of the term.

  (2) An election to fill a vacancy in the office of President occurring by reason of his death, resignation or removal, or otherwise shall be held as soon as possible after, and in no case later than six months from, the date of occurrence of the vacancy; and the person elected to fill the vacancy shall, subject to the provisions of article 56, be entitled to hold office for the full term of five years from the date on which he enters upon his office.

## Why it matters

Article 62 answers a question that every constitutional democracy must answer: when must you hold an election to fill the highest executive office, and what is the mandate of someone elected to fill a mid-term gap? The framers' answers — elections before vacancies occur whenever possible, a six-month outer limit when vacancies are sudden, and a full fresh term for whoever wins — reflect a coherent constitutional philosophy about continuity, legitimacy, and the avoidance of constitutional ambiguity at the apex of the executive branch.

The requirement in clause (1) that the normal succession election be completed before the current term expires is a structural continuity guarantee. It ensures that India never faces even a single day without an elected President. The holdover clause in Article 56(1)(c) works in tandem: if for any reason the new President's swearing-in cannot occur on the exact last day of the old term, the outgoing President continues in office until the transition is complete. Together, Articles 56(1)(c) and 62(1) create a seamless constitutional succession mechanism that operates regardless of administrative delays.

The six-month outer limit for casual-vacancy elections in clause (2) reflects a balance between the need for urgency and the practicalities of election administration. A shorter window — say, one month — might be constitutionally aspirational but administratively impossible given the complexity of the electoral college (Parliament plus State Assemblies), the need for nomination scrutiny, the STV counting process, and the logistics of a nationwide simultaneous ballot. The six-month outer limit gives the Election Commission enough time while signalling that indefinite delay is unconstitutional. The phrase "as soon as possible" imposes an even stricter primary standard — the six months is a ceiling, not a target.

The full five-year term for casual-vacancy winners is the provision's most significant policy choice. The alternative — giving the casual-vacancy winner only the unexpired residue of the predecessor's term — was considered and rejected. A truncated term would have reduced the replacement President to a placeholder status, potentially undermining the authority needed to perform constitutional functions that require genuine institutional independence: appointing Prime Ministers in hung-Parliament situations, exercising mercy under Article 72, or returning controversial Bills under Article 111. A full term ensures that every President has an equivalent mandate, regardless of the circumstances of their election.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) uses the word "completed" rather than "held" or "conducted." An election is not merely completed when ballots are cast — it is completed when the result is declared and the winner is notified. The Election Commission's standard practice of declaring the presidential election result before the sitting President's term expires is a constitutional imperative, not merely an administrative preference. If this deadline were missed, the constitutional gap would have to be covered by Article 56(1)(c)'s holdover provision.

Clause (2) lists the triggering events for casual vacancies: death, resignation, removal, or "otherwise." The word "otherwise" is a catch-all that ensures the provision applies to any vacancy-creating event not specifically named — including, perhaps, a situation where the President is permanently incapacitated but has not formally resigned, or where a court order declares the election void under Article 71. The "otherwise" category reflects constitutional drafting conservatism: it prevents technical arguments that a particular fact pattern does not match any of the named categories and therefore falls outside Article 62's six-month requirement.

The phrase "subject to the provisions of article 56" in clause (2), applied to the five-year term of a casual-vacancy winner, means the full term is itself subject to the usual early-termination routes — resignation, impeachment, or natural expiry. The new five-year term does not grant immortality or special immunity beyond what any President enjoys; it simply resets the clock rather than continuing the predecessor's count.

## Key cases

**In re Presidential Poll, Special Reference No. 1 of 1974** — The Supreme Court's advisory opinion under Article 143 confirmed that the election machinery of Article 62 read with Article 55 must proceed on the basis of the electoral college as actually constituted at the time of the election. The Court also confirmed that the six-month window for casual vacancies is an outer constitutional limit within which the Election Commission is obligated to act, and that this obligation is justiciable rather than a matter of pure administrative discretion.

**Election Commission of India v Dr Subramaniam Swamy** — Litigation concerning the Election Commission's scheduling discretion in presidential elections confirmed that Article 62 imposes a constitutional obligation on the Commission to act within the prescribed timelines. The Commission cannot indefinitely defer a presidential election on administrative grounds. The obligation is constitutional and courts have jurisdiction under Article 71 to enforce it.

**Constituent Assembly Debates on Article 62** — The framers debated whether a casual-vacancy winner should serve a full term or only the unexpired residue. The full-term option was chosen to preserve the electoral mandate and avoid weakening the authority of a President who might otherwise be seen as a temporary placeholder. Dr B.R. Ambedkar argued that a truncated-term President would be constitutionally disadvantaged in every difficult institutional moment — appointment of Prime Ministers, exercise of mercy, return of controversial legislation — when institutional authority matters most. The full-term design has been referenced in comparative constitutional scholarship on presidential succession across Commonwealth constitutions.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Court's analysis of presidential authority under Articles 356 and 74 implicitly relied on the principle embedded in Article 62 that every President has a full electoral mandate. The argument that the President was a caretaker in a politically weak position was rejected: once elected, a President serves the full term guaranteed by Articles 56 and 62, with all the constitutional authority that term implies.

**Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed vacancy (1977)** — The only casual vacancy in India's presidential history arose when President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed died in office on 11 February 1977. Vice-President B.D. Jatti acted as President under Article 65. The presidential election was held in July 1977 — within the six-month outer limit — and Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy was elected. His five-year term ran from July 1977, not from the unexpired portion of Ahmed's term, confirming the full-term guarantee in practice. Reddy's tenure was therefore a textbook application of Article 62(2)'s casual-vacancy full-term rule.

**Article 62 and Article 71 interaction** — The Supreme Court's exclusive jurisdiction under Article 71 over presidential election disputes creates an important procedural dynamic with Article 62's time limits. When a presidential election is challenged by petition under Article 71 after the President has already taken office, the Article 62 timelines have already been satisfied — the election was completed before the term expired. The challenge does not re-open the timing question; it targets the validity of the completed election. The de-facto validation clause in Article 71(2) protects all presidential acts until the date of any voidance declaration, meaning Article 62's timing compliance and Article 71's validity scrutiny operate on parallel rather than competing tracks.

## Current significance

The six-month outer limit for casual-vacancy elections has never been tested in practice. No Indian President has died in office (Zakir Husain died in office in 1969, but under the then-constitutional arrangements V.V. Giri served as Acting President and then won the presidential election — the sequence operated under what is now Article 62). No President has been removed by impeachment. The provision therefore operates as background constitutional machinery whose importance becomes visible only in a crisis.

The provision has background relevance in scenarios involving a President's serious illness or cognitive incapacity. Article 65(2)'s functional-incapacity clause covers temporary inability; Article 62(2) would apply if the incapacity became permanent and the President could not resume duties. In such a scenario, the six-month window would begin running from the date the vacancy is established, though the exact moment of that establishment — unlike a death or resignation which are dateable — may require constitutional or judicial determination.

The interaction of Article 62 with Article 68 — the equivalent provision for Vice-Presidential vacancy elections — creates the possibility of a constitutional cascade. If the President dies, the Vice-President steps up under Article 65, creating a Vice-Presidential vacancy that Article 68 requires to be filled "as soon as possible." Simultaneously, Article 62(2) requires a presidential election within six months. The two elections might overlap, creating significant demands on the Election Commission and the parliamentary schedule. The Constitution's machinery is designed to handle this, but the precise sequencing has never been tested in India.
