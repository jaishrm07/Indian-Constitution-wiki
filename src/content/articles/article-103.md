## Why it matters

Article 103 provides the procedural architecture for resolving disputed disqualification questions under Article 102(1). By routing these questions through the Election Commission — a constitutionally independent body — rather than through Parliament's own presiding officers, the article gives these determinations a degree of independence and institutional legitimacy that the anti-defection route under the Tenth Schedule, administered by the Speaker, conspicuously lacks.

The constitutional scheme deliberately separates the two disqualification tracks. The Article 103 route — President plus binding ECI opinion — applies to the Article 102(1) grounds involving factual questions about a member's status (Does this person hold an office of profit? Are they a foreign citizen?). The Tenth Schedule route, by contrast, involves a political judgment about party loyalty administered by a partisan officeholder, which explains the ongoing controversy that route has generated.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — presidential reference:** When a question arises about whether a member has become subject to an Article 102(1) disqualification, the question is referred to the President. The President's decision is declared final, which prevents courts from reviewing the merits of the decision on ordinary grounds — though courts retain jurisdiction if the constitutionally mandated procedure was not followed.

**Clause (2) — binding ECI opinion:** The President must obtain the ECI's opinion and must act in accordance with it. This makes the ECI the substantive decision-maker: the President has no discretion to depart from the ECI's recommendation. The finality in clause (1) therefore attaches to a decision that is in substance the ECI's determination.

**Scope — Article 102(1) only:** Article 103 does not apply to Tenth Schedule disqualifications under Article 102(2). Those are decided by the Speaker of the relevant House. Nor does it apply to automatic statutory disqualification under the Representation of the People Act — when Section 8 of the RPA triggers disqualification by the automatic operation of a conviction, no presidential reference is required.

## Key cases

**Kuldip Nayar v. Union of India (2006):** A five-judge Constitution Bench confirmed that Article 103(2) makes the Election Commission's opinion binding on the President. The President has no discretion to depart from the ECI's recommendation; the finality in clause (1) attaches to the President's decision once the ECI opinion is followed, not to any independent presidential assessment.

**Lily Thomas v. Union of India (2013):** The Supreme Court struck down Section 8(4) of the Representation of the People Act 1951, which had previously allowed convicted members to continue in office while their appeal was pending. After this judgment, conviction and a sentence of two or more years' imprisonment produces immediate disqualification under the RPA read with Article 102(1)(e), without any presidential reference under Article 103.

**Rahul Gandhi's disqualification and restoration (2023):** Gandhi was disqualified from the Lok Sabha in March 2023 on the day of his conviction in a criminal defamation case under Section 499 IPC. The disqualification was automatic under Section 8(3) of the RPA — no Article 103 reference arose. The Supreme Court stayed the conviction in August 2023 and the Lok Sabha Secretariat restored his membership, underlining that the RPA automatic route and the Article 103 opinion-based route are structurally separate mechanisms.

## Current significance

The interaction between Article 103's opinion-based procedure and the RPA's automatic disqualification regime continues to generate litigation. Because Lily Thomas removed the appeal-pending buffer that Section 8(4) once provided, any conviction with a two-year-plus sentence now triggers immediate disqualification — a rule that critics argue may be weaponised through targeted prosecutions, since the disqualification operates before an appeal can be heard.

Article 103's own procedure, requiring ECI involvement, is comparatively rare and applies to cases such as office of profit or disputed citizenship, where a factual investigation is genuinely needed rather than a simple reading of a court order. The distinction between the Speaker's Tenth Schedule jurisdiction and the President-ECI route under Article 103 also matters practically — Tenth Schedule decisions are made by a partisan officeholder and have been chronically delayed, whereas Article 103 decisions, routed through the ECI, carry stronger institutional legitimacy. Reform proposals have occasionally suggested extending an ECI-type advisory model to Tenth Schedule cases for precisely this reason.
