## Why it matters

Article 157 sets the only two formal constitutional qualifications for appointment as Governor: the
person must be a citizen of India and must have completed the age of thirty-five years. These are
the minimum conditions — and they are genuinely minimal. The article deliberately sets a low
eligibility floor, leaving the real selection criteria to political convention and executive
discretion rather than constitutional mandate. In contrast to Article 58 (which sets qualifications
for the President and requires the person to be qualified for election to the Lok Sabha), Article 157
does not impose any legislative-eligibility criterion, reflecting the distinct nature of the
gubernatorial office as a presidential appointee rather than an elected official.

The minimal threshold has practical consequences that have been widely criticised. Active politicians,
recently defeated electoral candidates, party functionaries who need a post-retirement sinecure, and
political fixers who may be useful in a particular State have all been appointed as Governors without
violating any constitutional qualification. Every normative expectation about what kind of person
should be appointed as Governor — impartiality, eminence, distance from active politics — exists
outside the constitutional text, in commission recommendations and parliamentary debates, and is
entirely unenforceable by courts.

The significance of Article 157 extends beyond individual appointments. Because the qualification
bar is so low, the entire moral weight of the gubernatorial system rests on the executive propriety
of the Union Cabinet making appointments and on the personal integrity of the Governor once in
office. Constitutional framers who chose this design were relying on responsible exercise of
executive discretion in a way that subsequent experience has not consistently validated.

## Reading the provision

The article uses a negative formulation — "no person shall be eligible for appointment unless" —
which makes the two conditions necessary (without them, ineligibility) but not sufficient (meeting
them does not guarantee appointment). The two conditions:

**Indian citizenship.** The Governor must be a citizen of India under Part II of the Constitution
and the Citizenship Act. A person who has dual nationality or whose citizenship is uncertain would
not satisfy this requirement. The citizenship requirement ties the Governor to the constitutional
community and ensures no alien can hold the constitutional headship of a State.

**Age of thirty-five years.** The same age requirement applies to the President under Article 58,
reflecting a judgment that a person holding high constitutional office should have a degree of
maturity associated with this age. Unlike the Lok Sabha membership qualification, there is no
requirement that the person not be a member of a State Legislature or Parliament as a condition of
eligibility — membership of a legislature is dealt with as a post-appointment consequence under
Article 158(1), not as a pre-appointment disqualification.

**What is absent.** The article does not require: (a) prior absence from active party politics;
(b) non-membership of the ruling party at the Centre; (c) non-residence in or non-connection with
the State concerned; (d) any professional qualification or distinction; (e) the person not to have
held an office of profit (this is covered by Article 158(2) as a post-appointment condition, not
a pre-appointment bar). All of these — though widely accepted as normative expectations — have
no constitutional basis in Article 157.

## Comparison with other high constitutional offices

Article 58 for the President requires not just citizenship and age of thirty-five but also
qualification for election as a member of the House of the People — which imports the disqualification
provisions of Articles 84 and 102, including disqualification for holding an office of profit,
insolvency, unsoundness of mind, and allegiance to a foreign state. Article 157 imports none of
these for the Governor.

The Vice-President's qualifications under Article 66 require qualification for election as a member
of the Council of States (again importing disqualification provisions) plus the condition of not
being a member of the House of the People or of a State Legislature. Article 157 imposes no such
dual-office bar, though Article 158(1) effectively creates it as a consequence of appointment.

The contrast is stark: the Governor — who is the constitutional head of a State with powers to
summon and prorogue the State Legislature, to give or withhold assent to State Bills, to recommend
or refuse to recommend Presidential Rule, and to exercise residual discretionary power under Article
163 — holds office under the most minimal constitutional qualification regime of any senior
constitutional functionary.

## The Sarkaria and Punchhi Commission recommendations

The Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1988) recommended that a Governor should be:
a person not connected with the political party in power at the Centre or in the State; a detached
figure not connected with local politics; an eminent person in some walk of life; and not connected
with the politics of the State to which they are appointed. The Punchhi Commission on Centre-State
Relations (2010) reiterated these recommendations and added that due consideration should be given
to the representation of minority communities, and that the Chief Minister of the concerned State
should be consulted before appointment.

Both commissions framed these as recommendations to be observed in practice by the Union government,
not as proposed constitutional amendments. Neither recommendation has been incorporated into Article
157 or into any statute. The legal consequence is that the Sarkaria and Punchhi criteria are
entirely aspirational — a Governor who is a recently active politician of the ruling party at the
Centre, appointed without consulting the Chief Minister, and assigned to a State where they have
deep political connections, has violated every one of those criteria without violating Article 157
or any other constitutional provision.

## Article 158 and the post-appointment scheme

While Article 157 sets the eligibility conditions, Article 158 governs the conditions of the office
itself once appointment occurs. Article 158(1) provides that a member of Parliament or of a State
Legislature who is appointed Governor is deemed to have vacated their legislative seat on entering
upon the office. This removes the formal connection between the Governor and a legislative
constituency — the Governor cannot simultaneously hold the constitutional headship of a State and
a legislative seat. The combination of Articles 157 and 158 creates a scheme in which the initial
eligibility bar is very low, but the conditions attached to the office immediately sever the new
Governor from the partisan legislative base through which political parties normally exercise
organisational power.

Article 158(2) prohibits the Governor from holding any other office of profit. Article 158(3)
entitles the Governor to official residences, emoluments, allowances, and privileges as determined
by Parliament, and ensures that these are charged to the Consolidated Fund of the State, making
them non-votable. Article 158(4) provides that where the same person is appointed Governor of two
or more States, the emoluments are shared as the President may determine by order. These provisions
supplement Article 157's minimal eligibility scheme with a more detailed regime governing the
financial and institutional conditions of the office.

## Key cases

**B.P. Singhal v. Union of India (2010) 6 SCC 331** — While the case principally concerned removal,
the Constitution Bench noted that Article 157's minimal qualification requirements are by design,
and the broader constitutional expectation of propriety and non-partisanship rests on convention
rather than legal mandate. The Court declined to add implied qualifications to the Governor's office
beyond those expressly stated in Article 157 — there is no judicial power to supplement the
constitutional text with a requirement of political neutrality. The judgment confirmed that once
the two Article 157 criteria are satisfied, the appointment is beyond challenge on the grounds of
political affiliation, prior political activity, or perceived lack of eminence.

**Harshvardhan v. Union of India** — Petitions challenging appointments of individuals alleged to
be politically affiliated have been dismissed because courts have held that Article 157 exhausts the
justiciable eligibility criteria. Qualitative fitness — impartiality, non-partisanship, eminence
— is a matter for executive judgment and democratic accountability rather than judicial review. Courts
will not review the wisdom of the Union executive's choice provided the Article 157 criteria are met.
The Sarkaria Convention is non-justiciable precisely because it has no statutory or constitutional
foundation.

**Governor's tenure cases generally** — Courts have consistently held that once the Article 157
qualifications are met, neither the manner of appointment under Article 155 nor the conditions of
service under Article 158 can be supplemented by judicially imposed criteria, preserving the
textual simplicity of the article at the cost of judicial accountability for selection quality.

## Current significance

The minimal qualification threshold continues to be a focal point of constitutional reform proposals.
Several parliamentary standing committees, law commission reports, and constitutional law scholars
have recommended amending Article 157 to include criteria such as a minimum period of
non-participation in party politics, or at least that statutory guidelines be enacted to give legal
force to the Sarkaria conventions. The National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution
(2002) under Justice M.N. Venkatachalaiah recommended that a person who has held a political office
should not be appointed Governor within at least two to three years of vacating it. This
recommendation has not been enacted.

In the absence of such reform, Article 157 imposes little effective constraint on the Union
government's choice beyond citizenship and age, allowing the appointment power under Article 155
to operate almost entirely on political logic. The resulting appointments have produced a series of
Governor-State government confrontations — over Bills withheld from assent, over the summoning of
Assemblies, over the timing of trust votes — that might have been avoided had the appointment
mechanism produced Governors with genuine constitutional independence. Article 157's silence on
the qualities of a Governor is one of the points at which the Constitution's reliance on
constitutional morality over enforceable constraint has been most visibly tested.
