## What this Article did

Article 268A was inserted by the Constitution (Eighty-eighth Amendment) Act, 2003, effective 15 January 2004. It created a new constitutional category for service tax — a levy on the provision of taxable services. Under Article 268A, service tax was to be levied by the Union but collected and appropriated by the Union and the States according to the principles formulated by Parliament by law. The conceptual design was similar to the shared taxes framework of Article 270: Parliament would levy the tax centrally, but the proceeds would be shared with States that had the administrative infrastructure to collect it within their territories.

The provision was needed because the Constitution as originally adopted had not separately categorised service tax in the Union, State, or Concurrent Lists. Services grew substantially as a share of the Indian economy through the 1990s and 2000s, and the service tax — first introduced by Finance Minister Manmohan Singh in the 1994 budget at a rate of 5% on a narrow base — expanded rapidly. The constitutional basis for service tax had always been uncertain; the 88th Amendment was intended to resolve this uncertainty by creating an explicit entry in the Seventh Schedule (Entry 92C of the Union List) alongside the Article 268A revenue-sharing mechanism.

In practice, however, Parliament never enacted the full revenue-sharing framework that Article 268A contemplated. The Finance Act provisions that levied and regulated service tax treated it as a Union tax, with amounts distributed to States only as grants-in-aid. The Article remained partly inoperative in its co-operative fiscal aspect until its omission.

## Why it was omitted: the GST transition

The Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act, 2016 was the most significant structural reform of India's fiscal federalism since 1950. It inserted Articles 246A (concurrent power to legislate on GST), 269A (inter-State IGST), 279A (GST Council), and comprehensively amended the Seventh Schedule. At the same time, it omitted several existing finance articles that had become redundant or were inconsistent with the new GST framework. Article 268A was omitted by section 7 of the 101st Amendment, effective 16 September 2016.

Service tax, which had grown to cover about 116 service categories at the time of GST implementation with a rate of 14% plus 0.5% Swachh Bharat cess and 0.5% Krishi Kalyan cess, was subsumed into GST. Under GST, the supply of services is taxed as part of the comprehensive CGST (Central), SGST (State), and IGST (inter-State) framework rather than as a standalone levy. The omission of Article 268A was therefore the formal constitutional housekeeping that followed the substantive shift to GST.

## Key cases

**Association of Leasing and Financial Services Companies v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of service tax on finance leasing, holding that financial and banking services fell within the legislative competence of Parliament under the residuary Entry 97 of the Union List. The decision helped consolidate service tax's constitutional footing before the 88th Amendment.

**Federation of Hotel and Restaurant Associations of India v. Union of India (1989)** — An earlier challenge to service tax elements (then called luxury tax) litigated the question of whether the transaction was a sale of goods or provision of services, foreshadowing decades of classification disputes that culminated in the unified GST base.

## Constitutional significance

Article 268A's brief operational life illustrates how constitutional amendments must sometimes follow rather than lead economic and fiscal evolution. The provision was inserted to resolve a constitutional uncertainty that had arisen from the organic growth of service sector taxation over a decade. Its omission followed an even more radical transformation — the replacement of the entire fragmented indirect tax system with a unified goods-and-services levy.

The transition from a world requiring Article 268A (federal service tax sharing) to one governed by Article 246A (concurrent GST power) represents the most substantial revision of India's fiscal federalism architecture since the Constitution came into force. Article 268A's tombstone entry in the constitutional text marks one node in that transformation.
