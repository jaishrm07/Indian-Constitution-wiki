## Why it matters

Article 242 is a spent provision — a constitutional placeholder that records what is no longer there. In the original 1950 Constitution, the article made special provision for the administration of Coorg, a small hill territory in what is now south Karnataka. Coorg had been a Chief Commissioner's Province under British India — directly administered by the Crown, without a legislative council or elected government — and was retained as a separate Part C State under the First Schedule to the Constitution when it came into force on 26 January 1950.

Coorg's distinct character made a special provision seem appropriate to the framers. It was a small territory with a population of just over 100,000 at independence, dominated by the Kodava community — a martial, landowning, Kannada-adjacent but culturally distinct people with a proud tradition of military service, a distinct language (Kodava takk), and a long history of coexistence with but separateness from the surrounding regions.

The territory's coffee and cardamom economy, its forested hills, and its compact administrative structure made it a self-contained unit that was administratively manageable as a standalone Chief Commissioner's Province. India's post-independence administrators inherited Coorg as an intact, functioning territorial unit, and the framers of the Constitution saw no immediate reason to disturb that arrangement in 1950. Article 242 was thus born as a holding provision — a constitutional acknowledgment of Coorg's continued separate administration while the larger question of India's territorial organisation was worked out through the political and legislative processes that culminated in the States Reorganisation Act.

The original text of Article 242 (before omission) provided that the executive power of the Union in relation to Coorg should extend to the giving of directions to the State of Coorg on any matter relating to which Parliament had the power to make laws. This effectively placed Coorg's administration under Central government direction — consistent with its status as a directly administered territory rather than a self-governing State with its own legislature and ministry.

## How courts use it

Because Article 242 was omitted with effect from 1 November 1956 and no replacement provision was enacted, courts do not interpret or apply the article in contemporary litigation. It is referenced only in constitutional history and territorial jurisdiction surveys where the question is whether a provision of pre-1956 law applicable to Coorg continues to have legal force in the successor jurisdiction of Karnataka, or whether any claim to special status for the Kodava community and the Coorg region (now Kodagu district) has a constitutional basis.

Courts have consistently held that the omission was complete. The States Reorganisation Act, 1956, and the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, together effected a full legal merger of Coorg into the State of Mysore. No residual constitutional status for Coorg survived. Laws made specifically for the Coorg Chief Commissioner's Province before 1956 continued in force as laws of Karnataka under the general saving provisions of the reorganisation legislation, but this continuation derived from the reorganisation statutes, not from any surviving force of Article 242 itself.

The States Reorganisation Act challenges decided by the Supreme Court in the 1950s and early 1960s — which addressed the reorganisation of various State territories — did not produce any significant ruling specifically about Coorg's merger or Article 242's omission. The legal transition was uncontested in court.

## Key cases

There are no significant Supreme Court cases directly interpreting Article 242 in its operative period (1950–1956). The article's pre-omission function was administrative rather than rights-bearing: it provided a constitutional basis for the Central government to administer Coorg directly through a Chief Commissioner, but this did not give rise to justiciable rights that could be enforced against the administration. The courts did not have occasion to develop doctrine around the provision before it was removed.

Challenges to the States Reorganisation Act, 1956, were brought in various High Courts and in the Supreme Court on grounds that the reorganisation violated constitutional provisions protecting States' rights. However, those challenges targeted the States Reorganisation Act and the Seventh Amendment as a whole. No challenge was mounted specifically against the merger of Coorg or the omission of Article 242. The Supreme Court upheld the Parliament's power to reorganise States under Article 3 as a broad legislative authority that did not require the consent of the affected States.

**Babulal Parate v. State of Bombay (1960)** — Though this case concerned the reorganisation of Maharashtra and Gujarat rather than Coorg, the Supreme Court's ruling upholding Parliament's wide powers under Article 3 to alter the area, name, and boundaries of States without the States' consent established the constitutional doctrine that governs the legality of all State mergers and reorganisations under the 1956 Act, including Coorg's absorption into Mysore.

## The States Reorganisation Commission and Coorg's fate

The States Reorganisation Commission (SRC), constituted under Justice Fazl Ali's chairmanship in 1953, examined the cases of all territories seeking reorganisation or separate status. Coorg was one of the territories that appeared before the Commission. The Kodava community's representatives made a case for Coorg's continued separate existence, citing the region's distinct culture, its compact administrative efficiency, and the community's record of service to the nation.

The SRC's 1955 report, however, recommended Coorg's merger into the enlarged Mysore State. The Commission's reasoning rested on several grounds:

- **Viability**: Coorg's population of roughly 100,000 and its limited revenue base could not sustain a full State government apparatus, including a legislature, a high court, and the range of administrative departments that statehood requires.
- **Language**: The Kodava takk language is closely related to the Kannada language group, and Kannada-speaking populations bordered and substantially overlapped Coorg's territory. The linguistic rationale for separate statehood was weaker than for communities with clearly distinct and geographically concentrated languages.
- **Geography**: Coorg is a compact, land-locked territory within the Mysore region. Its natural administrative connections ran toward the Mysore administration rather than toward any other State.

The Commission was not sympathetic to arguments for preserving small administrative units on grounds of cultural distinctiveness alone, and its recommendation on Coorg was consistent with its broader approach of prioritising larger, more viable linguistic States over smaller cultural enclaves. The Central government accepted the recommendation, and the States Reorganisation Act, 1956, implemented the merger effective 1 November 1956.

## Coorg's constitutional legacy and the Kodava identity claim

The omission of Article 242 did not end the political salience of Coorg's distinct identity. The Kodava community, though not large in number, has maintained a sustained presence in national political debates about constitutional recognition.

The central demands have been:

- **Scheduled Tribe status**: Recognition of Kodavas as a Scheduled Tribe under the Constitution — a demand that has been examined and deferred by multiple National Commissions for Scheduled Tribes and committees of the Ministry of Tribal Affairs without resolution. The community's social and economic characteristics have been contested, with some assessments concluding that Kodavas do not fit the profile of historically marginalised tribal communities that the Scheduled Tribe framework is designed to protect.
- **Cultural and land rights**: Special protections for the Kodava community's customary practices regarding ancestral land, joint family property, and inheritance patterns, which differ from standard Hindu law and are threatened by general legislation.
- **Statehood or Union Territory status**: A periodically revived demand, most audible when Karnataka's political relationship with the Kodagu region is strained, for the district to be carved out as a separate Union Territory or State.

None of these demands has a basis in Article 242 itself — the article's omission removed whatever special constitutional status Coorg had held, and the post-1956 legal framework treats Kodagu as one district of Karnataka with no special constitutional status. The Scheduled Tribe demand, if granted, would proceed through the Presidential Orders amending the First Schedule lists under Article 342, not through any territorial provision. The statehood demand, if pursued, would require a bill under Article 3 reorganising State boundaries.

## Current significance

Article 242 carries historical significance as a record of India's complex post-independence territorial consolidation. At independence, the country's map was a patchwork of provinces, princely states, and Chief Commissioner's Provinces. The reorganisation process that Coorg's article documents was the first major wave of rationalisation — the replacement of an administratively fractured inheritance with a coherent federal structure organised largely along linguistic lines.

The article also illustrates the broader phenomenon of constitutional omissions and deletions. The Indian Constitution carries dozens of numbered blank spaces where provisions have been omitted by amendment. These blanks are not constitutional vacuums — the omitting amendment makes express provision for the legal consequences of the omission — but they record the constitutional history of arrangements that the framers considered necessary to address and that subsequent history rendered obsolete. Article 242's omission was among the earliest of these episodes, occurring just six years after the Constitution came into force and reflecting how rapidly the political geography of the new republic was being reshaped.

Part VIII of the Constitution, under which Article 242 sits, has itself undergone extensive change since 1950. Territories that were once separately administered Chief Commissioner's Provinces or Part C States — Himachal Pradesh, Manipur, Tripura, Goa — subsequently became full States. Others, like the Andaman and Nicobar Islands and Lakshadweep, remain Union Territories. Coorg's story — a territory that existed as a separate constitutional unit and was then merged — is the exception: the usual direction of Part VIII's evolution has been from Union Territory status toward Statehood, not from separate existence toward merger and constitutional silence.

The gap in the constitutional text where Article 242 once stood is a reminder that the Constitution is a living document whose numbered provisions do not all survive unchanged. The text records both what is and what was, and the blank where Article 242 stood is as instructive about India's territorial history as many provisions that remain in force.

For constitutional scholars, spent articles like Article 242 are evidence of the framers' specific concerns at a particular historical moment — and of the processes by which those concerns were resolved and then memorialised only as a footnote in the constitutional text. They also serve as a reminder that constitutional provisions addressing administrative arrangements are always provisional: the political geography that makes a particular arrangement necessary today may change within a decade, as it did for Coorg between 1950 and 1956. The Constitution's amendment procedures, particularly the relatively accessible procedures for reorganising States under Article 3, mean that provisions like the original Article 242 can be rendered obsolete without any derogation of constitutional principles — merely as a result of Parliament's sovereign legislative choice about how best to organise India's federal structure.
