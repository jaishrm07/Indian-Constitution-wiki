  When a state Governor reserves a Bill for the President instead of assenting to it or returning it, the Bill moves from the state constitutional process into a Union-level channel. officialText: |-
  201. Bills reserved for consideration.—When a Bill is reserved by a Governor for the consideration of the President, the President shall declare either that he assents to the Bill or that he withholds assent therefrom:

  Provided that, where the Bill is not a Money Bill, the President may direct the Governor to return the Bill to the House or, as the case may be, the Houses of the Legislature of the State together with such a message as is mentioned in the first proviso to article 200 and, when a Bill is so returned, the House or Houses shall reconsider it accordingly within a period of six months from the date of receipt of such message and, if it is again passed by the House or Houses with or without amendment, it shall be presented again to the President for his consideration.

## Why it matters

Article 201 is where state legislation can be held in permanent suspension by Union-level executive inaction. Once a Bill is reserved under Article 200 and sent to the President, it leaves the state constitutional process entirely. The state government has no direct control over the outcome — it cannot summon the President, cannot force a decision, and cannot by itself complete the assent process. The President, acting on Union Cabinet advice, may assent, withhold assent, or return the Bill to the state legislature. But the President may also simply not act — the provision does not specify a time limit within which the President must declare assent or withholding of assent.

This capacity for indefinite executive inaction at the Union level represents a structural asymmetry in the Indian federal system. A state legislature may pass a law by a democratic majority; the Governor may reserve it rather than assent; and the President — acting on advice from the Union government — may then hold the Bill indefinitely without either approving or rejecting it. The state legislature is powerless to compel a resolution, and the Union executive has no constitutional obligation to provide one within any prescribed time. In states where the political relationship between the elected state government and the Union government is hostile, this mechanism can be used to indefinitely prevent state law from operating.

The human consequences of this stalemate can be severe. Universities that were to be restructured under a state law remain under older administrative arrangements. Cooperative sector regulations that a state legislature determined were necessary to protect farmers remain unenacted. Public health schemes that the legislature authorised cannot be funded or operated. Social welfare entitlements that a state government promised its electorate remain paper rights while the Bill awaits presidential consideration that may never come. These are not abstract constitutional injuries; they are concrete deprivations experienced by real people who expected their elected legislature's decisions to become operational law.

The interaction between Article 201 and the basic structure principle of federalism is therefore not merely doctrinal. It is a live constitutional battleground. The Supreme Court's engagement with the Tamil Nadu proceedings — addressing whether the President can hold a reserved Bill indefinitely — has thrust Article 201 into the centre of contemporary constitutional politics. Whether the courts can enforce Article 201's implicit structure of timely, good-faith executive action against an uncooperative Union government is the central question this provision now presents.

## Reading the provision

**The main clause — two presidential options**: When a Bill is reserved by the Governor and presented for the President's consideration, the President "shall declare" either assent or withholding of assent. The use of "shall declare" imposes a duty — the President must act. As with Article 200, the provision does not contemplate indefinite silence. But unlike Article 200's first proviso (which addresses return), the main clause of Article 201 gives only two options for declaration: assent or withhold. The return option is in the proviso and is available only for non-Money Bills.

**The proviso — the return option**: For non-Money Bills, the President may also direct the Governor to return the Bill to the state legislature with a message seeking reconsideration. This is analogous to the Governor's power under Article 200's first proviso to return a Bill rather than withholding assent. When the Bill is so returned, the state legislature must reconsider it within six months of receiving the message. This is a significant structural difference from Article 200 — Article 201's proviso imposes a six-month time limit on the state legislature for reconsidering the returned Bill, reflecting the Constitution's concern that Union-level intervention not produce permanent legislative paralysis. If the legislature repasses the Bill (with or without amendment) within six months, it is presented again to the President for consideration.

**The second round of presidential consideration**: After a state legislature repasses a returned Bill under Article 201's proviso, the Bill goes back to the President. The text then says it is presented "for his consideration" — but unlike Article 200's treatment of a re-passed Bill (which mandates the Governor to assent), Article 201 does not explicitly state what the President must do on receiving a re-passed Bill. This gap has generated the question of whether the President can then again withhold assent, or whether the second presentation triggers a duty to assent on the same logic as Article 200's treatment of re-passed Bills. The answer remains contested and has not been definitively settled by the Supreme Court.

**The asymmetry with Article 200**: Article 200's first proviso explicitly closes the Governor's veto option after a Bill is re-passed — the Governor "shall not withhold assent." No equivalent provision closes the President's options after a re-passed Bill is returned under Article 201. This textual asymmetry may be deliberate — the Union-level scrutiny is meant to be a more substantive check than the Governor's — or it may be an oversight that courts will need to fill by interpretation. Either way, the asymmetry has practical consequences for the finality of state legislation.

## The distinction between withholding assent and reserving for President

The Governor under Article 200 has two options that might look similar but are constitutionally distinct: withholding assent and reserving for the President. Both prevent the Bill from becoming law immediately, but their legal and political consequences differ significantly.

When the Governor withholds assent, the matter is settled at the state level — the Bill is dead in its current form. The state legislature cannot automatically reconvene to repass the same Bill (though it can pass a fresh Bill). The Governor's withholding of assent is the final word in the state constitutional process, subject to judicial review if the withholding was not in accordance with constitutional requirements.

When the Governor reserves the Bill for the President, the state constitutional process is not complete — the Bill is not dead but is transferred to the Union-level process under Article 201. The state legislature has no further role unless the President directs a return for reconsideration. Reservation shifts the constitutional locus of decision from the state to the Union. This distinction is important for federal balance: a Governor who withholds assent is exercising a state-level constitutional function, while a Governor who reserves is inviting Union-level intervention in state legislation. Strategic reservation — reserving Bills not because they raise genuine constitutional concerns but because the Centre wants to intervene in state affairs — is therefore a more significant federal intrusion than withholding assent.

## Article 201 and Article 254 — the repugnancy dimension

Article 254 governs the relationship between central and state legislation when both deal with matters in the Concurrent List. When a state law is repugnant to a central law or to a law made by Parliament, the central law prevails and the state law is void to the extent of the repugnancy. However, Article 254(2) creates an exception: if a state law has received Presidential assent, it may prevail even against a prior central law. This exception makes Article 201 significant in the concurrent list context — Presidential assent to a state Bill under Article 201 is not merely completing the formal legislative process; it is also, in the Article 254(2) context, potentially giving the state law precedence over central legislation on the same subject.

This means the Union government has a direct interest in deciding whether to give presidential assent to state laws dealing with concurrent list subjects — assenting may give those state laws precedence over central legislation. The decision to withhold presidential assent under Article 201 in such cases has a constitutional effect beyond the immediate legislative question: it protects the parliamentary framework from state-level modification. This constitutional function of Article 201 in the concurrent list context is distinct from its role in ordinary state legislation and explains why the Union may sometimes have a principled, not merely political, reason for withholding presidential assent.

## Key cases

**In re The Kerala Education Bill (1958)** — The Supreme Court's advisory opinion in this case was the first extended judicial engagement with Articles 200 and 201 taken together. The Court discussed the circumstances in which the Governor was constitutionally obligated to reserve the Kerala Education Bill — specifically whether it would derogate from High Court powers — and the President's role in subsequently granting or withholding assent. The Court confirmed that Article 201 creates a genuine constitutional channel through which Union-level scrutiny of state legislation is exercised, not merely a formality. The judgment remains the foundational authority on when reservation under Article 200 is mandatory and on the constitutional character of presidential consideration under Article 201.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. Governor of Tamil Nadu and Union of India (2023–2025)** — The most consequential contemporary proceedings on Article 201. The Tamil Nadu government challenged the Governor's reservation of multiple Bills and the President's subsequent prolonged inaction on those reserved Bills. The Supreme Court, in a series of directions, called upon the Union government to explain the delay and signalled that presidential inaction of indefinite duration may itself be a constitutional violation. By April 2025, the Court had issued what amounted to a constructive assent direction in respect of certain Bills — holding that where the President has been presented with a reserved Bill and has neither assented nor withheld assent nor returned it within a reasonable time, the Court can treat the Bills as having received assent. This was an unprecedented assertion of judicial authority over the Union executive's assent function and directly engaged the question of whether Article 201 is judicially enforceable.

**State of Punjab v. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab (2023)** — While primarily an Article 200 case, the Supreme Court's reasoning about the obligation to complete the constitutional assent process extends to Article 201. The Court held that the chain of constitutional duties — from the state legislature to the Governor under Article 200 and from the Governor to the President under Article 201 — must be performed within the framework of responsible government. A Governor cannot use strategic reservation to bypass democratic lawmaking, and by extension the President's consideration channel under Article 201 must be completed in good faith and with reasonable dispatch. The judgment confirmed that the entire assent chain is subject to constitutional discipline.

**Har Sharan Verma v. State of U.P. (1985)** — An early case addressing whether Presidential action under Article 201 is subject to judicial review. The Court held that the President's decision to withhold assent is not a purely political act beyond the reach of judicial review — it is a constitutional function that must be performed in accordance with constitutional requirements. The case opened the door to the principle that the Article 201 process, like other constitutional executive functions, is not immune from judicial examination. Subsequent developments have built on this foundation to reach the point where the Supreme Court now actively supervises the presidential consideration process.

**Purushothaman Nambudiri v. State of Kerala (1962)** — The Supreme Court, in an early case concerning a reserved Bill, discussed the scope of the President's discretion under Article 201 and the relationship between the presidential assent function and the Union Cabinet's advice. The Court noted that the President acts on Cabinet advice and that the Article 201 determination is therefore effectively a Union Cabinet determination. This confirms that the political accountability for Article 201 decisions rests with the Union Cabinet, which has implications for how judicial review of Article 201 must be framed — it is the Union Cabinet's exercise of executive power, not an independent presidential judgment, that courts ultimately examine.

**Samsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)** — This Constitution Bench decision on the general powers of constitutional heads under Part V and Part VI confirmed that the Governor and the President act on the advice of their respective Councils of Ministers in the vast majority of their constitutional functions. The assent functions under Articles 200 and 201 are included in this general principle. The judgment provides the framework within which the political accountability of Article 201 decisions must be understood: when the President withholds assent, it is the Union Cabinet's decision, and the constitutional responsibility for that decision lies with the elected Union government.

## Current significance

Article 201 is at the centre of one of the sharpest federal disputes in contemporary Indian constitutional law. The pattern of Governors reserving state Bills on politically contentious subjects — university governance, anti-corruption frameworks, social welfare schemes — followed by indefinite presidential inaction has become a significant channel through which the Union government can effectively block state legislation without ever formally vetoing it. The indefinite "pocket veto" at the presidential level is legally more insidious than the gubernatorial variety addressed in the Punjab Governor case, because it operates at the Union level where the state government has even less procedural leverage.

The Supreme Court's direction in the Tamil Nadu case — treating long-delayed Bills as having received assent — represents the most aggressive judicial enforcement of Article 201 obligations in the Constitution's history. Whether this approach will be sustained, whether it will be applied to other states in similar situations, and whether it will produce a durable constitutional norm about the time within which presidential consideration under Article 201 must be completed are all open questions as of 2025.

The fundamental constitutional tension that Article 201 embodies is between the Union's legitimate interest in scrutinising state legislation that may conflict with central law or constitutional requirements, and the state's democratic right to legislate on matters within its sphere without indefinite Union-level vetoes. A constitutional amendment specifying time limits — perhaps six months for the President, parallel to the six months given to the state legislature for reconsideration — would resolve this tension more cleanly than case-by-case judicial intervention. Whether Parliament will make such an amendment, or whether the Supreme Court's evolving directions will provide functional equivalents, will shape how Article 201 operates in the years ahead.
