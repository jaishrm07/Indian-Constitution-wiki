## What this Article does

Article 264 performs the structural function of a definitions clause for all of Part XII. It specifies that wherever "Finance Commission" appears in Part XII — in Articles 270, 273, 275, 280, and 281 — the phrase refers exclusively to a Finance Commission constituted under Article 280 by presidential order. This excludes from the definition any State Finance Commission constituted under Article 243-I (for Panchayats) or Article 243-Y (for Municipalities), which are separate constitutional bodies with a different mandate.

The placement of this definition at Article 264 — the very first article of Part XII — has constitutional significance. Part XII covers Finance, Property, Contracts and Suits. By opening the Part with a definitional anchor, the framers ensured that the institutional architecture of the Finance Commission could not be interpreted expansively to include informal advisory bodies or executive committees. The Finance Commission must be constitutionally constituted; its mandate and composition are prescribed by Article 280.

Article 264 also indirectly performs a limiting function. Because "Finance Commission" under Article 264 refers only to the body constituted under Article 280, no executive body — including the erstwhile Planning Commission or the present NITI Aayog — can claim the constitutional authority or the quasi-binding status that Finance Commission recommendations carry by virtue of Articles 280 and 281.

## How courts use it

Courts have rarely needed to construe Article 264 in isolation, because it functions as a definition that derives meaning from Article 280. However, in cases involving fiscal federalism disputes, courts have relied on the Article 264 definition to distinguish Finance Commission recommendations (which carry constitutional weight under Articles 280-281) from planning body awards, NITI Aayog transfer recommendations, or executive grant-in-aid orders under Article 282.

The distinction matters when States challenge the Union's departure from Finance Commission awards: if the Union argues that a NITI Aayog recommendation or a Cabinet decision supersedes a Finance Commission grant, courts will invoke Article 264 to confirm that only the Article 280 body enjoys the constitutional status that Part XII confers.

## Key cases

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1977)** — The Supreme Court's examination of fiscal federalism in the context of Centre-State relations touched on the exclusive constitutional character of the Finance Commission, emphasising that the body defined in Article 264 occupies a different constitutional tier from executive advisory bodies and that its recommendations cannot be displaced by executive convenience.

**In re: Presidential Reference on Finance Commission Terms of Reference** — Constitutional scholars and dissenting State Attorneys-General have invoked Article 264 in submissions arguing that the Finance Commission's terms of reference (issued by the President under Article 280) cannot be so narrowly drawn as to exclude issues that are textually within Part XII's scope — because the body being constituted is, by definition, the only "Finance Commission" known to the Constitution.

## Current significance

The definition in Article 264 has acquired sharper relevance in the GST era. After the introduction of the Goods and Services Tax Council under Article 279A by the One Hundred and First Amendment (2016), a new constitutionally mandated body entered the fiscal architecture of Part XII. The GST Council is not a Finance Commission, and the Article 264 definition ensures there is no conflation: revenue-sharing decisions under Article 270 that are to be made on Finance Commission recommendations cannot be substituted by GST Council recommendations, and vice versa. As the Sixteenth Finance Commission (2024-29) formulates recommendations for the post-GST fiscal architecture, Article 264's definitional clarity underpins the constitutional separation between these two bodies.
