  Article 232 was the original interpretation clause for the chapter on High Courts in States, providing definitions of terms used in the chapter. It was substituted — along with the then-existing Articles 230 and 231 — by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, effective 1 November 1956, as part of the comprehensive reorganisation of States along linguistic lines. The Seventh Amendment restructured Articles 230 and 231 to address how High Court jurisdiction should be organised following the creation of new States from the existing twenty-seven states and Part C states. The definitions that Article 232 originally provided were rendered unnecessary because the revised Articles 230 and 231 directly addressed the substantive questions those definitions had been designed to clarify. The entry on this page records the fact of that substitution — a marker of constitutional evolution rather than a substantive provision.

## Why it matters

Article 232 in its surviving form is a textual marker documenting the Seventh Amendment's restructuring of the High Courts chapter. Its constitutional significance lies less in any continuing operative content and more in what it tells us about the Seventh Amendment as an event in constitutional history. The 1956 reorganisation was one of the most consequential constitutional moments outside the framing period — it redrew the political map of India, creating States on linguistic lines, dissolving or merging former Part B and Part C States, and requiring a wholesale revision of the constitutional provisions that assumed the earlier State structure.

The High Courts chapter was particularly affected because High Courts had been established under pre-constitutional instruments for the former British Indian provinces and princely state territories. The new States created by the States Reorganisation Act, 1956 did not correspond to those earlier units. Articles 230, 231, and 232 — which had been drafted on the assumption of a relatively stable set of States — needed to be replaced by provisions capable of addressing common courts, jurisdictional extension to Union territories, and the attribution of constitutional administrative authority across multi-State court jurisdictions.

## Reading the provision

The effective date of 1 November 1956 is constitutionally significant. The States Reorganisation Act, 1956 also came into force on that date, meaning the constitutional amendment and the statutory reorganisation were coordinated to take effect simultaneously. The new State map and the new High Court jurisdiction framework became operative on the same day — preventing any transitional gap in which the constitutional provisions governing High Courts would be inconsistent with the actual State boundaries.

The substitution technique used here — replacing one set of provisions with new ones through a numbered amendment section, and retaining the article numbers with a bracketed annotation recording the substitution — is standard constitutional drafting practice in India. The article numbers are preserved because renumbering would disrupt cross-references in all subsequent judgments, legal texts, and constitutional commentaries that cite those article numbers. The tombstone-style annotation tells the reader that the current text of Articles 230 and 231 replaced what previously appeared as Articles 230, 231, and 232.

## Key cases

**Gauhati High Court v. Union of India (1981)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of the northeastern common High Court arrangement required engagement with the Seventh Amendment's restructuring of the jurisdictional provisions, since the Gauhati High Court's constitutional basis was shaped by the 1956 reorganisation. The judgment acknowledges the significance of the Seventh Amendment in creating the framework within which common High Courts operate.

**S. P. Gupta v. Union of India (1981) (First Judges Case)** — The judgment's detailed treatment of the constitutional history of High Court provisions — including the 1956 amendments — provides the interpretive background for the current text of Articles 230 and 231 and the substituted Article 232. The Court's recognition that the Seventh Amendment was a comprehensive reorganisation of the High Court chapter informs how the current provisions are read in light of their purpose.

**Re: The Special Courts Bill 1978 (1979)** — The Supreme Court's broader analysis of the constitutional framework for establishing courts and distributing judicial jurisdiction is relevant to understanding the 1956 restructuring. The Court's recognition that Parliament has wide latitude to organise judicial institutions within constitutional limits informs how the Seventh Amendment's exercise of that latitude is read.

## Current significance

Article 232 in its present form is of primarily historical and textual significance. Its chief importance for the practitioner is as a reminder that the current scheme of Articles 230 and 231 replaced an earlier set of provisions and that the Seventh Amendment was a constitutionally significant reorganisation of the High Court framework. For practitioners dealing with the northeastern States — where the common High Court model has its longest continuous application — the history of the 1956 reorganisation and its constitutional instruments is directly relevant to understanding the genesis of the current bench structure and jurisdictional arrangements of the Gauhati High Court.

Article 232 also stands as a precedent for the technique of substituting one constitutional provision with another through amendment. The 1956 substitution was constitutionally uncontroversial because it was part of a broader reorganisation that commanded broad consensus. That same technique applied in more contested circumstances — as with the Emergency-era Forty-second Amendment's insertion of Articles 226A and 228A — generated significant constitutional litigation. The contrast illustrates that the constitutional validity of an amendment using any particular drafting technique depends not on the technique itself but on whether the substantive change the technique effects is consistent with the Constitution's basic structure.
