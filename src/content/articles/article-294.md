## Why it matters

Constitutional transitions create a fundamental question of legal succession: when a new state is born, does it inherit the contracts, debts, and property of its predecessor, or does it start with a clean slate? The 1947 Indian Independence Act divided British India between India and Pakistan as Dominions, and the 1950 Constitution transformed India from a Dominion into a Republic. Article 294 answers the succession question definitively for the Republic's commencement: it vested all Crown property automatically in the new constitutional governments and transferred all rights, liabilities, and obligations without requiring any fresh conveyance, assignment, or novation.

Without Article 294, the constitutional transition would have created an enormous legal vacuum. Every railway, every defence installation, every government building, every pending contract, every outstanding public debt would have needed fresh legal instruments to establish the new government's title. The article's "automatic vesting" approach collapsed all of this complexity into a single constitutional moment — 26 January 1950 — making the Union and the States the seamless legal successors of the Dominion government and the Governor's Provinces respectively.

## Reading the provision

Clause (a) covers property. All property held by His Majesty for the purposes of the Dominion of India vested in the Union; all property held for the purposes of each Governor's Province vested in the corresponding State. The vesting was automatic — no instrument of conveyance was needed and the Constitution itself operated as the title document.

"For the purposes of" is the key phrase: property held for Dominion purposes (railways, postal services, central armed forces, central secretariat buildings) went to the Union; property held for provincial purposes (State roads, provincial secretariats, land revenue records) went to the corresponding State. Where property was used for overlapping purposes — joint facilities, shared buildings — the vesting question required interpretation and occasionally litigation.

Clause (b) covers rights, liabilities, and obligations. All contractual and non-contractual obligations of the Dominion passed to the Union; all obligations of each Governor's Province passed to the corresponding State. This included outstanding public debt, civil servants' service and pension rights, and pending contracts for infrastructure, supplies, and services. The proviso in clause (b) acknowledges that partition-related adjustments — divisions of assets and liabilities between India and Pakistan — were still incomplete in 1950 and remained subject to further settlement.

## Key cases

**State of Bihar v. Union of India AIR 1970 SC 1436** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the vesting effected by Article 294 operated by force of the Constitution itself and required no additional executive or legislative action to perfect the transferee government's title. The judgment is the foundational authority for the automatic, self-executing nature of the Article 294 succession.

**Union of India v. Sukumar Sen AIR 1956 SC 444** — Early post-Constitution litigation established that the succession of obligations under Article 294(b) was comprehensive and included employment-related liabilities: civil servants' accumulated pension rights, service entitlements, and continuity of service obligations were obligations of the Dominion government that passed to the Union.

**Promode Chandra Deb v. State of Orissa AIR 1962 SC 1288** — A case involving the succession of provincial government assets in which the Court applied Article 294's vesting provision to determine which government held title to disputed property that had been used for purposes straddling provincial and central functions before 1950. The judgment provides guidance on how to characterise the "purposes" for which property was held when that purpose was shared or ambiguous.

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India AIR 1963 SC 1241** — The Court's broader analysis of fiscal federalism in Part XII touched on the succession established by Articles 294 and 295, confirming that the automatic vesting created a complete legal continuity between the Crown-era governments and the constitutional successor governments, and that this continuity included the full body of property rights and liabilities, not merely the physical assets.

## The partition adjustment proviso

The proviso to clause (b) acknowledges the incomplete state of the India-Pakistan asset division in 1950. The 1947 Partition involved complex negotiations over how to divide the pre-partition government's assets — the Reserve Bank of India's gold reserves, railway rolling stock and infrastructure, military equipment, central government securities, and the Dominion's outstanding debt. Many of these negotiations were unresolved at the Constitution's commencement. The proviso preserved the possibility of further adjustment without treating the Article 294 vesting as a final settlement — a pragmatic acknowledgment that constitutional succession and diplomatic negotiation were proceeding in parallel.

## Relationship with Articles 295 and 300

Article 294 addresses the directly British-administered territories — the Dominion and Governor's Provinces. Article 295 provides the parallel succession framework for Indian States (princely states) that had signed instruments of accession and merged with the Indian Union. Together, Articles 294 and 295 provided a complete property succession framework for the entire territory of independent India. Article 300 then governs the right to sue and be sued by the successor governments in respect of their inherited rights and liabilities, completing the legal continuity chain.

## Current significance

Article 294 operates entirely in the past tense — its vesting function was completed on 26 January 1950. However, it is regularly invoked in property title disputes where the historical chain of ownership passes through Crown or Dominion title. Railway land encroachments, cantonment boundary disputes, port trust land conflicts, and government housing colony title questions all require tracing title through the Article 294 vesting. Constitutional historians also invoke the article as the textual foundation of India's legal continuity: by accepting all obligations of the Dominion without disclaimer, the Republic of India assumed responsibility for the full legal personality of its predecessor — including international treaty obligations, government bonds held by foreign creditors, and the pre-1950 contractual commitments of the Government of India.
