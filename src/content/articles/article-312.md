  Parliament can create services — like the IAS and IPS — that are common to both the Union and the States, but only after the Rajya Sabha passes a resolution supported by not less than two-thirds of the members present and voting declaring that such a service is necessary in the national interest. Officers in these all-India services are recruited centrally through a competitive examination and can be deployed in any State, giving the Union a cadre of officials present throughout India while those officers serve predominantly under State governments. The IAS and IPS are explicitly deemed to have been created under this article even though they predate the Constitution, preserving the continuity of India's senior civil and police services. A third tier — the all-India judicial service to staff district judgeships from a common national pool — has been constitutionally authorised since 1977 but has never been brought into existence, remaining one of the most debated unimplemented constitutional provisions. The Sarkaria Commission (1988) and successive law commissions have examined the all-India services framework, alternately recommending expansion and reorganisation depending on the Centre-State tensions of the period. The Rajya Sabha super-majority requirement is a federal safeguard — no new all-India service can be imposed on States without the consent of the States' house of Parliament.

  312. All-India services.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in [Chapter VI of Part VI or Part XI], if the Council of States has declared by resolution supported by not less than two-thirds of the members present and voting that it is necessary or expedient in the national interest so to do, Parliament may by law provide for the creation of one or more all India services [(including an all-India judicial service)] common to the Union and the States, and, subject to the other provisions of this Chapter, regulate the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to any such service.

  (2) The services known at the commencement of this Constitution as the Indian Administrative Service and the Indian Police Service shall be deemed to be services created by Parliament under this article. [(3) The all-India judicial service referred to in clause (1) shall not include any post inferior to that of a district judge as defined in article 236.

  (4) The law providing for the creation of the all-India judicial service aforesaid may contain such provisions for the amendment of Chapter VI of Part VI as may be necessary for giving effect to the provisions of that law and no such law shall be deemed to be an amendment of this Constitution for the purposes of article 368.]

## Why it matters

Federal systems face a recurring tension: how do you ensure national standards of administration in States without turning the States into mere agents of the Centre? The all-India services are India's distinctive constitutional answer. Officers recruited through a common national examination are allotted to State cadres, serve predominantly in State governments, but remain governed by Central service rules and retain the Union Government's backing. This dual accountability means that an IAS District Collector enforces State law and answers to the Chief Minister day to day, yet cannot easily be coerced into irregularities because the Centre has concurrent disciplinary authority.

Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel, who championed the services against critics who wanted a fully State-controlled bureaucracy, described the IAS as the "steel frame" holding India together — a phrase that has survived every proposal to dismantle it. The constitutional entrenchment in Article 312 ensures that no State government can unilaterally abolish the cadre of centrally recruited officers posted within its territory. The all-India services thus represent a structural compromise between federal autonomy and national cohesion: States get officers who know local conditions and serve local governance needs, while the Centre retains a stake in the quality and integrity of those officers.

The Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1988) examined the all-India services at length and recommended their continuation and, in some cases, expansion. The Commission found that the services had been instrumental in maintaining administrative cohesion across a diverse country, particularly during periods of political instability in States, and that the constitutional safeguards in Article 312 — especially the Rajya Sabha super-majority requirement — were an adequate federal check on the Centre's power to create new services without State consent.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — The Rajya Sabha super-majority requirement**: The requirement that a Rajya Sabha resolution supported by not less than two-thirds of the members present and voting must precede Parliament's creation of a new all-India service is the article's most important federal safeguard. The Rajya Sabha is the Council of States — the chamber in which States have representation — and requiring its super-majority consent before a new pan-India service is imposed on State governments is a structural protection of federal autonomy. Ordinary legislation by simple majority is not enough; the States' house must affirmatively endorse the national interest case. This requirement is the reason no new all-India service has been created since the Indian Forest Service in 1966 — every proposal since has either failed to garner sufficient Rajya Sabha support or has not been brought to a vote.

**Clause (2) — Grandfathering of IAS and IPS**: The IAS and IPS predated the Constitution; they were successor services to the Indian Civil Service and the Indian Police of British India, formally constituted in 1946 and 1948 respectively. Clause (2) resolves the potential constitutional problem of services that existed before the Article 312 process was available by deeming them to have been created by Parliament under the article. This is a legal fiction with significant practical effect — it means the IAS and IPS derive their constitutional standing directly from Article 312 and not merely from pre-constitutional continuity under Article 313.

**Clauses (3) and (4) — The all-India judicial service**: Added by the Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act 1976, these clauses authorise the creation of an all-India judicial service to fill district judge posts from a common national pool. Clause (3) sets the minimum rank — no post inferior to a district judge (as defined in Article 236) may be included. Clause (4) provides that the enabling legislation may amend Chapter VI of Part VI (which deals with the subordinate judiciary) and that such amendments will not themselves be treated as constitutional amendments requiring the Article 368 procedure. This is a facilitative clause: it removes procedural obstacles that might otherwise delay implementation. Despite this constitutional machinery being in place for nearly five decades, the all-India judicial service has not been created, principally because High Courts and State governments have consistently opposed the transfer of control over district judiciary appointments to a centralised mechanism.

The dual-control structure that flows from Article 312 means that an all-India service officer has two principals: the State Government for day-to-day work and postings within the State, and the Central Government for cadre management, empanelment to Central deputation posts, and major disciplinary proceedings. Rules made under the All India Services Act 1951 — the principal legislation giving effect to Article 312 — give concrete expression to this division of authority between Centre and State.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debates on what became Article 312 were among the most charged in the entire Constitution-making process. The ICS and IP were colonial institutions, and many members of the Assembly were former freedom fighters who had been prosecuted or harassed by these very services. Jawaharlal Nehru himself was ambivalent, doubting whether a unified service was appropriate for an independent democracy.

It was Sardar Patel — himself a former member of the ICS who had resigned — who made the decisive argument for retaining a central all-India service. In his speech of 10 October 1949, Patel argued that without "an efficient, disciplined and contented service … which has the independence to speak out its mind" there would be "administrative break-down." He accepted that the services had served colonial interests but argued that the same qualities of reliability, probity, and institutional continuity that had made them instruments of empire could, with appropriate accountability, make them instruments of democratic governance. The Assembly ultimately accepted this argument, and Article 312 as enacted reflects Patel's vision of a constitutionally embedded all-India service as a federal cohesion device rather than a colonial relic.

## Key cases

**State of Haryana v. P C Wadhwa (1987)** — The Court confirmed that the dual-control structure of all-India services flows directly from the constitutional design and that a State Government cannot unilaterally impose conditions of service that contradict Central service rules applicable to IAS officers. Even where the Central rules are silent on a particular point, a State cannot fill the gap in a way inconsistent with the overall scheme of Central service regulation under Article 312.

**Ashok Kumar Gupta v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1997) 5 SCC 201** — The Court held that reservation policies applicable to State services extend to IAS cadre posts when they are filled by promotion from State services, affirming that Article 312's Central oversight of all-India services does not displace a State's constitutional obligation to implement its own reservation rules for State-level vacancies.

**G Venkataraman v. Union of India (1990)** — The Court held that IAS (Cadre) Rules framed under Article 312 prevail over inconsistent State executive instructions on cadre management, underscoring the supremacy of Central legislation in regulating the all-India services and the limits of State discretion in deploying centrally recruited officers.

**S R Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court's landmark federalism decision acknowledged that the all-India services created under Article 312 represent a deliberate constitutional choice to interweave Union and State administration, and that the dual-control structure cannot be used as a device by either Centre or State to undermine the constitutional balance.

**Union of India v. Rajendra Singh (2022)** — The Supreme Court examined the 2021 amendment to the IAS (Cadre) Rules that proposed enlarging the Central Government's power to compulsorily depute State-cadre officers to Union postings against State government preference. The Court's observations stressed that any amendment to cadre rules must preserve the essential federal bargain embedded in Article 312 and that compulsory deputation without State consent raises significant constitutional questions about the Centre-State balance in managing all-India services.

## Current significance

Article 312 sits at the centre of an ongoing constitutional and political debate about Centre-State relations in public administration. The recurring controversy over compulsory Central deputation of IAS officers — particularly the proposed 2021 amendment that several State governments resisted as an encroachment on State autonomy — tests the limits of the Centre's authority under Article 312 to override State preferences in managing officers allotted to State cadres.

Lateral-entry appointments to Joint Secretary and Director-level posts in the Union Government also raise questions about whether the IAS's de facto monopoly on senior administration is a constitutional requirement or a structural choice that Parliament could revisit. The Constitution creates the framework for all-India services but does not mandate that any particular service must be the exclusive route to all senior posts. Whether the expanding use of lateral entry and contract appointments alongside the all-India services is consistent with the constitutional design of Article 312 — and whether it weakens the "steel frame" or modernises it — is a live policy and constitutional question.

The all-India judicial service, authorised but never created, remains perhaps the most significant unrealised constitutional potential in Part XIV. Successive Law Commission reports have recommended its establishment as a means of attracting talent to the district judiciary, ensuring national minimum standards, and reducing political influence in judicial appointments at the State level. The objections — principally from High Courts concerned about losing supervisory control, and from State governments concerned about federal autonomy — illustrate exactly the tensions that the Rajya Sabha super-majority requirement in clause (1) was designed to manage.
