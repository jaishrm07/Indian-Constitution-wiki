  309. Recruitment and conditions of service of persons serving the Union or a State.—Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, Acts of the appropriate Legislature may regulate the recruitment, and conditions of service of persons appointed, to public services and posts in connection with the affairs of the Union or of any State:

  Provided that it shall be competent for the President or such person as he may direct in the case of services and posts in connection with the affairs of the Union, and for the Governor of a State or such person as he may direct in the case of services and posts in connection with the affairs of the State, to make rules regulating the recruitment, and the conditions of service of persons appointed, to such services and posts until provision in that behalf is made by or under an Act of the appropriate Legislature under this article, and any rules so made shall have effect subject to the provisions of any such Act.

## Why it matters

The civil services of the Union and the States are the operational backbone of constitutional
governance. Millions of public servants — from IAS officers to Class IV employees — owe their
recruitment, tenure, pay, promotion, disciplinary proceedings, and retirement benefits to a legal
framework that must itself be constitutionally grounded. Article 309 is that constitutional anchor.
It settles two foundational questions at once: which institution has the primary power to set the
rules of public employment, and what happens in the gap before that institution acts.

Without a clear constitutional anchor for service rules, governments could bypass legislative
scrutiny and alter recruitment standards or employment conditions through ad hoc executive orders
without accountability. Article 309 resolves this by placing the primary power to regulate
recruitment and service conditions in the legislature, while giving the executive a carefully
bounded gap-filling authority. By linking service regulation directly to the Constitution, the
article also ensures that any rules made — whether legislative or executive — must conform to
fundamental rights, especially Articles 14 (equality before law) and 16 (equality of opportunity in
public employment). This creates a framework in which the civil service is governed by rule of law
rather than executive whim.

The significance of distinguishing between a legislative statute and an Article 309 executive rule
is not merely technical. A service statute — like the All India Services Act 1951 — can only be
amended by a legislative majority in the appropriate legislature; it cannot be altered by executive
circular. Article 309 executive rules, by contrast, can be amended by the President or Governor (or
a delegate), which makes them faster to update but also more susceptible to unilateral change. The
constitutional hierarchy is clear: a legislative statute displaces inconsistent executive rules. But
for the vast bulk of India's civil services, the day-to-day framework of service conditions is in
fact governed by executive rules made under the Article 309 proviso — not by statute — making the
proviso far more practically important than the main clause.

The article's opening phrase — "Subject to the provisions of this Constitution" — has particular
force in the service context. Courts have consistently held that no Article 309 statute or rule,
however clearly legislated or promulgated, can override fundamental rights. A service rule that
discriminates on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth violates Article 16(2) and
is void. A rule that arbitrarily disadvantages a class of civil servants violates Article 14. A rule
that silences a public servant's speech beyond what is reasonably necessary violates Article 19. The
civil service does not exist in a constitutional vacuum; it is staffed by citizens who retain their
fundamental rights, albeit subject to the reasonable restrictions that service obligations demand.

## Reading the provision

Article 309 operates in two tiers, created by the main clause and the proviso respectively. The main
clause vests primary legislative power in Parliament (for Union services) and State Legislatures
(for State services). These legislatures may enact statutes setting out recruitment procedures, pay
scales, promotion criteria, disciplinary processes, retirement benefits, and conditions of service
of any kind. This is an ordinary legislative power exercisable through normal legislative procedure,
including the possibility of delegating detailed rule-making to a subordinate executive authority
under the parent statute.

The proviso creates a separate and independent executive rule-making power in the President (for
Union services) and the Governor (for State services). This power is exercisable by the President or
Governor directly, or by delegation to any person they direct. It is specifically limited by the
words "until provision in that behalf is made by or under an Act of the appropriate Legislature": it
is a gap-filling power that operates in the absence of legislative coverage. Once Parliament or a
State Legislature enacts a statute "in that behalf" — covering the same subject matter of
recruitment and service conditions — the executive rules remain in force but become subordinate to
the statute. Any inconsistency is resolved in favour of the Act.

The phrase "by or under an Act" in the proviso is important. Rules made under delegated authority by
an Act of Parliament or a State Legislature are treated as legislative rules for the purpose of
displacing executive rules under Article 309. This means that if Parliament enacts a broad service
statute and authorises the executive to make detailed rules under it, those statutory rules
(technically executive in origin but legislative in authority) will displace earlier standalone
Article 309 executive rules in any area of inconsistency.

The difference between a "service statute" and "Article 309 rules" has concrete significance for
public servants. Service statutes, being Acts of Parliament or State Legislatures, provide the
protections of the legislative process — parliamentary debate, committee scrutiny, public record.
Article 309 executive rules have no such procedural protections: they can be amended, suspended, or
revoked by executive order without parliamentary approval. A public servant whose service conditions
rest only on Article 309 executive rules has less procedural protection against unilateral change
than one whose conditions are fixed by statute. This reality drives the ongoing demand in civil
service reform debates for statutory codification of service conditions.

## Key cases

**D.S. Nakara v. Union of India (1983) 1 SCC 305** — A Constitution Bench struck down a pension rule under the Central Civil Services (Pension) Rules that divided retirees into two groups based on date of retirement, granting a more favourable formula only to those retiring after a cut-off date. The Court held that pensioners form a homogeneous class and that an Article 309 rule creating an arbitrary sub-class within that homogeneous class violates Article 14. This remains the principal authority for subjecting service rules — whether legislative or executive — to equality scrutiny, and for the proposition that classification within a service is permissible only if it rests on an intelligible differentia with a rational nexus to the objective sought.

**B.S. Vadera v. Union of India (1969) SCR (2) 642** — The Court confirmed that rules made by the President under the proviso to Article 309 carry full legal force independent of any parliamentary enactment. The proviso is not a residual or temporary concession but a free-standing rule-making power; executive rules made under it are as binding as statutory rules until displaced by legislation. This settled a longstanding uncertainty about whether Article 309 executive rules had the same binding character as statutory service rules, and confirmed that public servants appointed under such rules hold their appointments on legally enforceable terms.

**State of Jammu and Kashmir v. Triloki Nath Khosa (1974) 1 SCC 19** — A Constitution Bench upheld different promotional criteria for graduate and diploma-holding engineers in State service, affirming that the legislature and executive retain wide latitude under Article 309 to classify public servants by educational qualification, specialisation, and experience. The classification must have a rational nexus to the nature of the higher post to which promotion is sought, and must not arbitrarily foreclose promotion opportunities to an entire class of employees. This case established the analytical framework for testing promotional rules under Article 14.

**A.V.S. Narasimha Rao v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1970) SCR (3) 647** — The Court held that rules framed under Article 309 can operate retrospectively if the legislature or executive clearly so intends, but retrospective operation that takes away or abridges a vested right already crystallised in a public servant's favour must be tested against Article 14 fairness standards. A rule that retroactively changes the basis of promotion or seniority after a public servant has been confirmed and acted on a reasonable expectation of settled service conditions may be unconstitutional even if it is within the formal scope of Article 309 power.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v. Chandra Mohan Nigam (1977) 3 SCC 83** — The Court examined whether a State Governor could validly make rules under the Article 309 proviso even after Parliament had enacted a partial statutory framework for the relevant service. The decision confirmed that the proviso power is displaced only to the extent that a legislative enactment actually covers the field: where the statute is silent, the executive retains power to make rules to fill the gap. This ruling refined the understanding of field-occupation in the Article 309 context and confirmed that partial legislative coverage does not entirely extinguish the executive rule-making power.

**Mahe District Cooperative Bank v. State of Kerala (2010) 3 SCC 20** — The Court reiterated that the distinction between a service under Article 309 and a cooperative or statutory body service is constitutionally significant: Article 309 applies specifically to persons appointed to "public services and posts in connection with the affairs of the Union or of any State." Employees of statutory corporations, cooperative bodies, or autonomous institutions are not automatically covered unless they can be shown to hold posts in connection with the affairs of the Union or a State in the constitutional sense.

## Current significance

Article 309 is the constitutional foundation of every major Central service regulation, from the IAS
(Cadre) Rules to the Central Civil Services (Pension) Rules, the Central Civil Services
(Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules, and the service rules of every Central ministry and
department. Its interaction with Article 16 drives recurring litigation over reservation policy in
promotions, the validity of lateral-entry appointments to senior posts, and the use of short-term
contractual appointments to fill positions that would ordinarily require a Public Service Commission
examination.

The question of lateral entry into senior government positions — the appointment of mid-career
professionals from the private sector, academia, and civil society to Joint Secretary and Secretary-
level posts — has been a recurring Article 309 controversy. Critics argue that lateral entry
appointments, made through executive orders rather than statute, lack the procedural safeguards and
merit-test transparency that statutory recruitment processes provide. Supporters argue that the
Article 309 proviso expressly authorises the executive to make rules on recruitment, and that
lateral entry is a permissible form of direct appointment. The constitutional question of whether
lateral entry to positions ordinarily filled by scheduled services bypasses the reservation
protections of Article 16(4) and (4A) remains unresolved.

The pension reform debate — from defined-benefit pensions to the National Pension System (NPS) and
subsequent proposals to restore guaranteed pensions through the Unified Pension Scheme — is an
Article 309 controversy at its core. Each transition has involved modifications to executive rules
made under the Article 309 proviso, raising questions about whether changes to pension entitlements
constitute the taking away of vested rights and whether such changes, affecting millions of serving
and retired public servants, should require statutory rather than merely executive action. The
tension between the executive's flexibility to reform service conditions and the public servant's
reasonable expectation of settled service terms will continue to generate litigation and policy
debate under Article 309.
