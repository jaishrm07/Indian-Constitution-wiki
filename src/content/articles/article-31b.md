## Why it matters

Article 31B created the Ninth Schedule as a constitutionally immune safe harbour for legislation that Parliament wished to protect from judicial invalidation on fundamental rights grounds. Originally designed to protect zamindari abolition and land reform laws from challenges under Articles 14, 19, and 31, the Schedule grew over subsequent decades into a repository of over 280 laws covering subjects from banking regulation to linguistic minorities and caste-based reservations. Article 31B is the constitutional mechanism through which Parliament attempted to insulate policy choices from judicial oversight — a power that has been progressively constrained by the basic structure doctrine.

## Reading the provision

The Article operates by deeming any Act or Regulation listed in the Ninth Schedule to be not void, and never to have been void, on the ground of inconsistency with any provision of Part III. The phrase "ever to have become void" gives it retrospective curative effect: even a court judgment that had struck down a law is overridden when the law is inserted into the Schedule. The phrase "notwithstanding any judgment, decree or order of any court or Tribunal" makes the override explicit. The protection is from fundamental rights challenge only — it does not shield Ninth Schedule laws from challenge on other constitutional grounds such as legislative competence.

## Origins in land reform conflict

The Ninth Schedule was inserted by the Constitution (First Amendment) Act 1951 in direct response to judicial decisions that threatened to block the zamindari abolition programme. State zamindari abolition laws had been struck down or challenged under Articles 14 and 19(1)(f) and the then-Article 31. Rather than redraft each law to pass constitutional muster, Parliament chose to list them in the Schedule and declare them immune from fundamental rights review. This blunt legislative tool allowed land reform to proceed but planted the seeds of a major constitutional controversy about the limits of parliamentary power.

## The expanding Schedule

Over the following decades, successive governments inserted laws on subjects far beyond land reform: bank nationalisation acts, minority educational institution regulation, reservation laws including the Tamil Nadu 69% reservation statute, laws affecting linguistic minorities, and urban land ceiling legislation. By the time the I.R. Coelho case was decided in 2007, the Schedule had grown to over 280 entries. Critics argued that the Schedule had become an instrument for insulating politically convenient but constitutionally dubious legislation from scrutiny.

## Key cases

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The thirteen-judge bench ruling on the basic structure doctrine implicitly constrained Article 31B: if Parliament cannot amend the Constitution in a manner that destroys its basic structure, then a constitutional provision itself cannot be used to shield laws that violate basic structure rights. The judgment set the conceptual foundation for the eventual direct limitation of Article 31B protection.

**Waman Rao v. Union of India (1981)** — The Supreme Court held that all Acts inserted into the Ninth Schedule before 24 April 1973 (the date of the Kesavananda Bharati judgment) retain full immunity under Article 31B. Acts inserted after that date are open to challenge if they violate the basic structure of the Constitution or abridge fundamental rights that form part of the basic structure. This temporal dividing line has governed the scope of Ninth Schedule protection ever since.

**I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007)** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench unanimously confirmed and elaborated the Waman Rao distinction. The Court held that the Ninth Schedule cannot operate as a device to bypass fundamental rights review for post-1973 legislation. Any law inserted after April 1973 that abridges or abrogates rights under Part III which form part of the basic structure is subject to full judicial scrutiny under Article 32. The Court identified Articles 14, 15, 19, 20, 21, and 25 as rights whose essential elements form part of the basic structure, meaning post-1973 Schedule entries that infringe these rights can be struck down.

## Implications of I.R. Coelho

After I.R. Coelho, the Ninth Schedule's immunity has two tiers. Pre-April 1973 entries retain blanket protection under Article 31B. Post-April 1973 entries must pass the basic structure test — effectively meaning that courts examine whether the fundamental rights infringed by the law are themselves basic structure elements. Laws that only infringe peripheral or non-basic-structure rights may still enjoy Schedule immunity; laws that violate basic structure rights are fully reviewable despite their Schedule listing. This has substantially limited the practical utility of inserting new laws into the Schedule.

## Current significance

The Ninth Schedule currently contains over 280 laws. The I.R. Coelho framework means that challenges to post-1973 entries remain viable before the Supreme Court, particularly for laws inserted to protect reservation schemes. The Tamil Nadu 69% reservation law (the Ninety-Third Amendment, 2006) and certain state-level reservation laws placed in the Schedule have been subjected to ongoing constitutional scrutiny. The relationship between Article 31B's immunity and the fundamental rights of competing communities — particularly in reservation cases — is an active area of constitutional litigation.
