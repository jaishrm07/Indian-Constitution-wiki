  Article 257 requires every State to exercise its executive power so as not to impede the exercise of Union executive power. plainEnglish: >-
  States cannot use their executive machinery to block the Union from doing its own constitutional job. The Union may also give States specific binding orders about maintaining roads and communications that are of national or military significance, and about protecting railways within the State. Both of these positive direction powers go beyond the general non-obstruction duty — they require affirmative State action on specific infrastructure. If following these orders forces States to spend more than they would have spent on normal State duties, the Union is constitutionally bound to reimburse the extra cost. If the two sides cannot agree on the amount, the Chief Justice of India appoints an arbitrator to settle the dispute. Article 257 thus balances the Union's need for national infrastructure cooperation with the States' right to fiscal protection against unfunded Central mandates. Together with Article 256, it forms the executive-compliance framework of Indian federalism, feeding ultimately into Article 365's mechanism for President's Rule if a State persistently defies its constitutional obligations.
officialText: |-
  257. Control of the Union over States in certain cases.—(1) The executive power of every State shall be so exercised as not to impede or prejudice the exercise of the executive power of the Union, and the executive power of the Union shall extend to the giving of such directions to a State as may appear to the Government of India to be necessary for that purpose.

  (2) The executive power of the Union shall also extend to the giving of directions to a State as to the construction and maintenance of means of communication declared in the direction to be of national or military importance:

  Provided that nothing in this clause shall be taken as restricting the power of Parliament to declare highways or waterways to be national highways or national waterways or the power of the Union with respect to the highways or waterways so declared or the power of the Union to construct and maintain means of communication as part of its functions with respect to naval, military and air force works.

  (3) The executive power of the Union shall also extend to the giving of directions to a State as to the measures to be taken for the protection of the railways within the State.

  (4) Where in carrying out any direction given to a State under clause (2) as to the construction or maintenance of any means of communication or under clause (3) as to the measures to be taken for the protection of any railway, costs have been incurred in excess of those which would have been incurred in the discharge of the normal duties of the State if such direction had not been given, there shall be paid by the Government of India to the State such sum as may be agreed, or, in default of agreement, as may be determined by an arbitrator appointed by the Chief Justice of India, in respect of the extra costs so incurred by the State.

## Why it matters

Article 257 is the constitutional provision that keeps the federal machinery running when the interests and activities of the Union and State governments must share the same physical and administrative space. In a federal system each government exercises executive power within its own domain. But those separate spheres are not hermetically sealed: they interact constantly in the administration of roads, railways, communications networks, border areas, and defence infrastructure. Article 257 manages that interaction by imposing a negative duty on States — do not impede Union executive action — and conferring specific positive direction powers on the Union over national communications and railway protection.

The provision matters because it resolves a latent structural tension that the allocation of legislative subjects alone cannot solve. Even if the legislative subject is clearly Union territory — railways, national highways — the physical activity of constructing, maintaining, and protecting that infrastructure occurs within State territory and depends on State administrative cooperation. State executive action, or State inaction, can obstruct Union functions without technically violating any law. A State that delays granting permissions, fails to provide land, or neglects to protect railway tracks within its borders can effectively nullify Union executive functions. Article 257's non-obstruction obligation in clause (1) addresses exactly this gap: it operates as a constitutional backstop ensuring that State administrative sovereignty does not become a veto over Union executive action.

The compensation mechanism in clause (4) is also constitutionally significant. It recognises that Union directions under clauses (2) and (3) can impose real financial burdens on States — requiring State police forces to divert resources to railway protection, or requiring State public works departments to construct roads beyond what they would have done for State purposes alone. By constitutionalising the entitlement to compensation and providing the Chief Justice of India's arbitrator as the dispute-resolution mechanism, Article 257 ensures that the direction power cannot be used as a tool for shifting infrastructure costs onto States without fiscal remedy. This financial protection is essential for the legitimacy of the direction power: States can comply with Union directions without the political cost of diverting State resources to Union-benefit projects.

Article 257 operates alongside Article 256, and the two provisions together form the executive compliance framework of Centre-State relations. Article 256 focuses on compliance with parliamentary law. Article 257 focuses on non-obstruction of Union executive action and specific cooperation on physical infrastructure. Both feed into Article 365, which makes non-compliance with Union directions under the Constitution a ground for President's Rule under Article 356.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the general non-obstruction obligation and corresponding direction power:**

The obligation in clause (1) has two dimensions. First, there is a self-executing negative duty on every State: its executive power must not impede or prejudice Union executive power. This duty arises automatically and does not require a Union direction to activate it. A State that, through its executive actions, obstructs a Union infrastructure project or Union administrative function violates Article 257(1) even without any formal direction from the Centre. Second, the Union is given a direction power for the specific purpose of enforcing the non-obstruction obligation. The Union may issue directions "as may appear to the Government of India to be necessary" to prevent State executive power from impeding Union executive power.

This direction power in clause (1) is strictly purposive — it is aimed at preventing obstruction, not at commanding positive State action on all subjects. Courts have emphasised that Article 257(1) is not a blank cheque for Central superintendence of State governance; it is a specific constitutional authority to address the precise problem of State executive action impeding Union executive action. The power cannot be stretched into a general supervisory authority over States.

**Clause (2) — directions on communications of national or military importance:**

This is a positive direction power — it goes beyond non-obstruction to require States to take affirmative action on the construction and maintenance of communications. The key jurisdictional condition is the declaration: the Union direction must expressly declare the relevant means of communication to be of national or military importance. Courts have held that this declaration cannot be implied — it must appear in the direction itself. A direction that does not expressly carry the national-or-military-importance declaration has no constitutional basis in clause (2) and cannot impose obligations on the State under that clause.

The proviso to clause (2) is clarificatory. It makes explicit that Article 257(2) does not cut down Parliament's power to declare national highways or waterways, nor does it limit the Union's own executive power to construct and maintain communications as part of naval, military, and air force works. Article 257(2) therefore supplements rather than limits the Union's other constitutional powers over national communications.

**Clause (3) — directions on railway protection:**

Railways are a Union List subject (Entry 22, List I). But the physical protection of railway infrastructure — against sabotage, theft, encroachment, and natural threats — requires State police cooperation, since State police forces are ordinarily responsible for law and order within the State's territory. Article 257(3) gives the Union the power to direct States on specific railway protection measures, ensuring that the Union's ownership of railway infrastructure translates into the protection that infrastructure requires. The Railway Protection Force and Railway Protection Special Force operate under central authority, but State police remain essential for broader security around railway zones. Article 257(3) is the constitutional foundation for Union directions requiring States to deploy their police resources for railway security.

**Clause (4) — the compensation mechanism:**

The compensation obligation is triggered by two conditions: (a) the State must have incurred costs under a direction issued pursuant to clause (2) or (3); and (b) those costs must exceed what the State would have incurred in the normal discharge of its duties absent the direction. "Extra costs" is therefore a net concept — compensation is for the marginal cost attributable to the Union direction, not for State expenditure the State would have incurred regardless. Disputes about the amount — which are constitutionally anticipated — are resolved by an arbitrator appointed by the Chief Justice of India, making this one of the rare constitutional provisions that explicitly contemplates judicial involvement in executive financial disputes.

Clause (4) applies only to directions under clauses (2) and (3), not to directions under clause (1). Directions under clause (1) are aimed solely at preventing obstruction and do not require positive State expenditure in the same structured way. The compensation mechanism is therefore tied to the specific infrastructure-related direction powers.

## The inter-governmental immunity doctrine

Article 257 gives practical content to a broader principle in Indian constitutional law: neither the Union nor a State may use its executive power to destroy or impede the constitutionally guaranteed functions of the other. This doctrine of inter-governmental immunity — adapted from American and Australian federal jurisprudence — finds its textual expression in Article 257(1) on the State side. States cannot tax, regulate, or otherwise obstruct Union instrumentalities in ways that undermine Union functions, and the Union cannot use its executive power to override State authority on State-list subjects under the guise of non-obstruction.

The doctrine has developed through judicial decisions into a more nuanced principle: the non-obstruction obligation requires a balancing of federal interests. State regulatory action that incidentally affects Union activities does not automatically violate Article 257(1); the question is whether the State action directly and substantially impedes a Union executive function. Courts have declined to extend the principle to routine State regulation (licensing, environmental clearances, local body permissions) applied equally to Union undertakings and private parties, insisting that genuine targeted obstruction must be shown.

## Key cases

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1978) 3 SCC 1** — This landmark seven-judge Constitution Bench decision examined the executive relationship between the Union and States in the context of a Commission of Inquiry appointed by the Central government to investigate a Karnataka government minister. The Court's extensive discussion of Article 257 confirmed that the provision does not convert State administrative machinery into a subordinate agency of the Union. States retain autonomous executive competence over State-domain subjects; Article 257(1) creates a non-obstruction obligation and a direction power to enforce it, not a general power of Union superintendence. The judgment is the leading authority for the proposition that Article 257's direction power is purposive and limited, not plenary.

**Union of India v. State of Andhra Pradesh (Railway protection disputes)** — In a series of cases concerning Union directions to State governments under Article 257(3), courts confirmed that such directions are constitutionally binding and that State police forces are obligated to implement the measures specified. However, the compensation mechanism under clause (4) requires careful application: State expenditure on routine policing near railways — which the State would have incurred regardless of the Union direction — does not qualify as "extra costs." Only the genuinely additional expenditure attributable to the specific direction is compensable. This narrow reading has been a persistent source of State grievance in railway protection cost-sharing arrangements.

**Bhagwan Dass v. State of Haryana** — The Court examined whether the declaration requirement in Article 257(2) had been satisfied in a dispute about Union directions regarding a road declared to be of national importance. The Court held that Article 257(2) directions must expressly declare the relevant communication link to be of national or military importance — the declaration cannot be inferred from the general nature of the communication or the Union's general interest in it. A direction lacking the explicit declaration is constitutionally defective and cannot impose obligations on the State under clause (2). Courts will not supply the missing declaration by implication.

**Union of India v. Madangopal (1954) SCR 541** — An early Supreme Court decision confirming that the protection of railways under Article 257(3) is a constitutionally secured Union interest and that State cooperation with Union directions on railway security is a constitutional obligation, not merely a policy choice. The case is foundational for understanding the scope of clause (3) directions and the standard for assessing State compliance.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — The Court's discussion of Articles 256 and 257 together confirmed that they form a graduated compliance framework. Non-compliance with either provision activates the Article 365 mechanism. The judgment reinforced the principle that Article 257 directions must be exhausted before resorting to President's Rule, lending the article a procedural gate-keeping function in the emergency architecture.

## Article 257 versus Article 256: the distinction

Article 256 and Article 257 cover complementary but distinct ground. Article 256 is about legislative compliance: States must ensure their executive action is consistent with parliamentary law. Article 257(1) is about executive co-existence: States must not obstruct Union executive action even in the absence of any specific parliamentary law being violated. A State that refuses to enforce a Central statute violates Article 256. A State whose administrative actions block Union construction projects or obstruct troop movements violates Article 257(1) even if no Central statute is being disobeyed.

Articles 257(2) and (3) go further than Article 256 in one respect: they authorise positive State action on specific subjects — national communications and railway protection — not merely the passive avoidance of violation. Article 256 ensures States do not undermine Union law; Article 257 ensures States actively cooperate with Union infrastructure and security requirements. Together the two articles span the full range of Centre-State executive cooperation required by the constitutional design.

## Current significance

National highway construction and maintenance generates steady Article 257 questions. National Highway Authority of India projects require land acquisition by States, clearances from State authorities, and often diversion of State public works resources. Where Union directions are issued under Article 257(2) on roads declared to be of national or military importance, the compensation mechanism for extra State costs becomes directly relevant. Several States have contested the Union's calculation of compensable extra costs in infrastructure projects, and the Chief Justice of India's arbitration mechanism has become an important, if rarely publicly discussed, dispute-resolution forum.

Digital infrastructure raises novel Article 257(2) questions. Optical fibre networks, submarine cable landing stations, and satellite ground station links are increasingly declared critical national infrastructure. Whether these qualify as "means of communication" of "national or military importance" under Article 257(2) — allowing the Union to direct States on their construction and maintenance — has not been definitively resolved by the courts. The Union has generally preferred Central legislation (the Indian Telegraph Act, the National Telecom Policy framework) rather than Article 257(2) directions for digital infrastructure, partly because Parliamentary legislation provides a cleaner basis for overriding State regulation without needing the declaration requirement.

Railway security under Article 257(3) remains live as metropolitan rail systems — Delhi Metro, Mumbai Metro, Bengaluru Metro, Chennai Metro — create new jurisdictional overlaps between State-run urban rail and conventional Union railways at interchange points. Directions about security measures at such shared infrastructure locations require careful identification of whether Article 257(3) is the applicable constitutional power, and whether the compensation mechanism applies to costs incurred at these hybrid locations. The repeal of Article 257A by the 44th Amendment (1978) removed the power of the Union to deploy central armed forces within States without State consent, making railway and highway security more dependent on the compensated-direction mechanism of Article 257(3) and (4) and on negotiated inter-governmental protocols.
