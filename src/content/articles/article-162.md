## What this Article does

Article 162 is the mirror provision to Article 73, which fixes the extent of Union executive power. Just as the Union's executive authority extends to matters over which Parliament can legislate, the State's executive authority extends to matters over which the State Legislature can legislate. The provision ties executive competence directly to legislative competence: a State cannot take executive action in a field outside its legislative domain.

This linkage is constitutionally significant because it prevents a situation in which executive power is claimed in fields where the State has no legislative backing. At the same time, the article does not require that every executive action be preceded by a specific legislative enactment — the leading authority of Rai Sahib Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab (1955) established that the executive can act in any field of competence without specific legislative authorisation, provided no law prohibits or conditions the action.

## The main clause and Article 246 read together

The main clause of Article 162 must be read alongside Article 246, which distributes legislative competence between the Union and the States through the Seventh Schedule. The State Legislature has exclusive power over State List subjects (List II), concurrent power over Concurrent List subjects (List III), and no power over Union List subjects (List I). Article 162 translates this legislative map into an executive map: the State's executive power extends to List II subjects exclusively, and to List III subjects subject to the proviso.

## The proviso and the hierarchy of executive power

The proviso is the critical qualifier in concurrent-list territory. Where both Parliament and the State Legislature have power to make laws on the same subject, the State's executive power becomes subordinate to whatever executive authority Parliament or the Constitution has expressly conferred on the Union or its instrumentalities. Two conditions must be met before the State's executive power is displaced: (1) the subject must be one on which both Parliament and the State Legislature have legislative power; and (2) the Union's executive authority in that subject must have been "expressly conferred" either by the Constitution or by a law of Parliament.

The word "expressly" is important. The Centre cannot claim executive supremacy in a concurrent-list field merely because Parliament has legislated on the subject; there must be an express conferral of executive authority — either by a constitutional provision or by a specific legislative grant — before the State's executive power is limited. This creates a higher threshold for Union executive dominance in concurrent fields than the legislative supremacy rule under Article 254, which kicks in automatically when parliamentary and state legislation on the same subject conflict.

## Executive power without prior legislation

A foundational principle derived from Article 162 jurisprudence is that a State government need not enact legislation before taking executive action in a field of its legislative competence. The executive can enter into contracts, issue directions, create agencies, and take policy decisions in the State List without waiting for enabling legislation, subject only to the requirement that no existing law restricts the action. This doctrine of inherent executive competence is the constitutional foundation for many State government schemes, welfare programmes, and administrative arrangements that operate without explicit statutory backing.

## Key cases

**Rai Sahib Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab (1955)** — The Supreme Court's foundational ruling on executive power held that executive action need not always rest on prior legislative authority; the executive can act in any field in which it has competence to legislate, provided no law specifically restricts or regulates the action. This principle, enunciated in the context of Article 162, continues to define the outer boundary of State executive autonomy.

**A.V. Nachane v. Union of India (1982)** — The Court applied the Article 162 framework to clarify that where Parliament has legislated on a Concurrent List subject and expressly conferred executive authority on the Union, a State's executive power in that subject is correspondingly limited by the proviso to Article 162. The ruling is the leading precedent on the hierarchy of executive competence in concurrent-list domains.

**State of Rajasthan v Union of India (1977)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of Article 256 directions — which require States to exercise their executive power in conformity with Union laws — was grounded in the Article 162 framework. The Court confirmed that the Union can direct a State only in areas where the proviso to Article 162 gives the Centre executive supremacy, and cannot override the State's exclusive executive authority in List II subjects by Article 256 directions alone.

**GNCT of Delhi v Union of India (2023)** — The Constitution Bench's analysis of the executive relationship between the Lieutenant Governor and the elected Government of Delhi drew on the Article 162 principle that executive power tracks legislative power. The Court's reasoning confirmed that where an elected government has legislative competence it also has executive competence, subject only to express constitutional limitations.

## Current significance

Article 162 is at the heart of contemporary disputes over the executive reach of States in areas such as agricultural markets, electricity regulation, police administration, and land acquisition — fields where both the Union and the States claim executive competence. The growing use of centrally sponsored schemes and Union-directed executive orders in State-list subjects has intensified reliance on the proviso to Article 162 as an instrument by which the Centre asserts supremacy. Several State governments have challenged central directions on the ground that the executive power in question has not been "expressly conferred" on the Union, making the precise scope of the proviso an active constitutional question. The article's interaction with Articles 257 and 365 — which allow the Centre to give directions and to treat non-compliance as grounds for Article 356 action — also keeps Article 162 at the centre of federalism debates.
