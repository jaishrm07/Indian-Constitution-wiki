## Why it matters

Article 38 sits at the apex of Part IV and announces the broadest purpose of the entire chapter: the State must secure a social order in which social, economic, and political justice pervades all national institutions. The Preamble's promise of justice is not merely declaratory; Article 38 makes it a continuing constitutional obligation on every organ of the State. The phrase "shall strive" acknowledges that the welfare state cannot be built overnight, but it insists that sincere and progressive effort is constitutionally mandatory, not optional.

Clause (2), inserted by the 44th Constitutional Amendment in 1978 in the wake of the Emergency, sharpens the general welfare mandate into a targeted redistributive duty. The State must minimise inequalities in income and must endeavour to eliminate inequalities in status, facilities, and opportunities — not only among individuals but also among groups residing in different areas or engaged in different vocations. This group-level dimension is constitutionally significant: it provides a textual basis for regional development programmes, occupational welfare schemes, and reservation policies that address structural, not merely individual, disadvantage.

Together, the two clauses make Article 38 the constitutional foundation for the entire architecture of redistributive law in India — minimum wages, progressive taxation, land reform, social security, affirmative action, and rural development programmes all draw legitimacy from it. Courts have consistently treated Article 38 not as aspirational rhetoric but as a substantive guide to interpreting legislation and evaluating the good faith of State action.

In the judicial reasoning that built India's transformative constitution, Article 38 plays the role of an overarching norm that gives meaning and direction to more specific provisions. It tells the reader of Part IV — and of the Constitution as a whole — what all the specific directives are in service of: a social order characterised by justice. When the Preamble's promise of justice is measured against the constitutional text, Article 38 is the provision that makes the measurement possible.

The relationship between Article 38 and the Preamble is particularly important. The Preamble resolves to secure to all citizens "JUSTICE, social, economic and political." Article 38 gives this resolution its most direct operative expression within the constitutional text, making the Preamble's ideals actionable as governing standards for legislative and executive conduct. Courts have used this connection to hold that laws passed in deliberate contradiction of the social justice mandate of Article 38 — for example, laws that demonstrably worsen the conditions of the most disadvantaged groups for the benefit of more powerful interests — cannot claim the presumption of constitutionality that ordinarily attaches to parliamentary legislation.

## Reading the provision

Article 38 has two interlocking clauses. Clause (1), in its original form, commits the State to "securing and protecting" a social order informed by justice — the three varieties of justice (social, economic, political) tracking the Preamble directly. The verb "inform" is deliberate: justice must permeate the institutions of national life, not merely exist alongside them as a formal aspiration.

The phrase "as effectively as it may" in clause (1) introduces a practical qualification: the State must make the most earnest effort its resources permit. This is a standard of genuine endeavour rather than an absolute standard of outcomes. But courts have consistently held that this qualifier does not permit a government to abandon the welfare orientation of policy or to structure reforms in ways that predictably worsen the position of the most disadvantaged. The "as effectively as it may" standard is aspirational but not meaningless.

Clause (2) was not part of the original Constitution. It was one of the substantive additions made by the 44th Amendment, a Parliament dominated by Janata Party legislators who included this provision partly as a corrective to the authoritarian excesses of the Emergency period and partly to introduce a redistributive commitment that the original text had left implicit. The clause distinguishes between inequalities in income (which the State must minimise) and inequalities in status, facilities, and opportunities (which the State must endeavour to eliminate). This gradation is significant: income redistribution is a target to approach progressively, but status and opportunity inequality carry a stronger elimination mandate.

The phrase "not only amongst individuals but also amongst groups of people residing in different areas or engaged in different vocations" anchors the constitutional concern in group and regional disadvantage, providing textual support for area-based development programmes, scheduled-caste and scheduled-tribe welfare measures, and occupational welfare schemes targeted at particularly disadvantaged categories of workers. It also gives Article 38 a spatial dimension: the constitutional obligation extends to the unequal distribution of public goods across regions, not merely between social classes.

Courts read Article 38 alongside the Preamble's justice clause, Article 14's equality guarantee, and Article 21's right to life. When these provisions are read together, they produce a constitutional framework in which distributive justice is not an optional policy preference but a governing constitutional standard against which legislative and executive action must be measured.

## Key cases

The following cases illustrate the range of judicial contexts in which Article 38 has been applied — from individual liberty to occupational health to labour law — demonstrating its function as a constitutional standard that pervades the entire body of welfare and social justice jurisprudence.

**Francis Coralie Mullin v. Union Territory of Delhi (1981)** — The Supreme Court held that the right to life under Article 21 includes the right to live with basic human dignity and the minimum necessities of life. Justice Bhagwati, writing for the Court, explicitly drew on Article 38 and other directive principles to give substantive content to the right to life, reasoning that a constitutional order committed to social, economic, and political justice cannot treat Article 21 as protecting only physical survival. This judgment established the technique of reading Part IV directives into the Article 21 guarantee, a technique that has shaped Indian socio-economic rights jurisprudence for over four decades. Without Article 38, the implied right-to-livelihood cases would have lacked their most direct constitutional warrant.

**Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985)** — A Constitution Bench held that the right to livelihood is an inseparable component of the right to life, and that pavement dwellers could not be evicted without procedural fairness and reasonable notice. The Court cited Article 38's mandate for a just social order to emphasise that the constitutional scheme treats the livelihood of the poor as a value deserving protection, not an inconvenience to be cleared away. The decision remains the foundation for subsequent judgments on the rights of urban informal workers and homeless persons. The Court's explicit invocation of Article 38 in a case about the most marginal urban inhabitants remains one of the most powerful deployments of the welfare-state directive in Indian constitutional history.

**SAIL v. National Union Water Front Workers (2001)** — The Supreme Court, in a matter concerning contract labour and the applicability of welfare legislation, cited Articles 38 and 39 to confirm that the interpretive direction for social welfare legislation must be guided by the constitutional mandate to secure a just social order. The Court emphasised that when statutory provisions are capable of multiple readings, the reading that better advances the constitutional purpose of reducing inequality and protecting the dignity of labour must be preferred. This case illustrates how Article 38 functions as an ever-present background norm in labour law interpretation.

**Consumer Education and Research Centre v. Union of India (1995)** — A three-judge bench read Article 38 with Article 21 to hold that the right to health is a fundamental right of workers. The Court required industries to provide occupational health infrastructure, treating the constitutional goal of a welfare state as an enforceable standard rather than a distant aspiration. It held that employers operating under statutory licence carry a corresponding obligation to ensure that working conditions meet the social-order standard Article 38 demands, linking corporate compliance directly to constitutional welfare obligations.

## Current significance

Article 38 is routinely cited in public interest litigation concerning inequality, denial of welfare entitlements, and the adequacy of social security provision. Courts invoke it when evaluating whether flagship schemes — the National Food Security Act, MGNREGA, health insurance programmes — are being implemented in good faith or are being structured in ways that exclude their intended beneficiaries. The article's demand for group-level equality has also been used to support litigation about regional underdevelopment and the unequal distribution of public infrastructure across states and districts.

The 44th Amendment's addition of clause (2) continues to carry weight in debates about wealth concentration, tax policy, and whether constitutional norms impose any obligation on the State to curb economic inequality through fiscal means. As India's income and wealth gaps have widened in recent decades, constitutional scholars and litigants have returned to Article 38(2) as a text that makes redistribution not merely permissible but constitutionally required. The article provides a principled constitutional anchor for arguments that the State cannot design economic policy in ways that are indifferent to, or actively worsen, structural inequality between groups and regions.

Article 38 is also increasingly relevant to debates about the concentration of market power in digital and platform economies. Litigants argue that Article 38(2)'s mandate to eliminate inequalities in status, facilities, and opportunities is violated when regulatory frameworks permit a small number of private platforms to control access to essential economic infrastructure — employment, credit, commerce, information — without accountability to the social-order norms the Constitution demands. This argument connects the constitutional welfare-state tradition of Article 38 to contemporary concerns about the political economy of digital capitalism.

Finally, Article 38 continues to provide the constitutional vocabulary for public interest litigation in areas where specific directive principles do not directly apply. Its broad mandate — secure a social order informed by justice — operates as a catch-all constitutional standard that courts invoke when confronted with State action or inaction that produces outcomes deeply inconsistent with the constitutional design, even where no more specific provision directly applies. This general welfare clause function of Article 38 makes it the most versatile tool in the directive principles toolkit for litigants pursuing distributive justice in courts.

The Kesavananda Bharati judgment (1973), which established the basic structure doctrine, identified the welfare state character of the Constitution as a core element of that structure. Article 38's mandate for a social order characterised by justice is the textual expression of that welfare-state character, making it not merely a directive to be progressively implemented but a constitutional identity norm whose destruction would itself amount to a violation of the basic structure. This reading, developed by Justices Mathew and Krishna Iyer and reinforced in subsequent cases, gives Article 38 a constitutional status that goes beyond the merely directive.

The courts have also connected Article 38 to the environmental constitutionalism that has developed since the 1970s. A social order characterised by social, economic, and political justice cannot be achieved in an environment degraded beyond its capacity to sustain human life. Courts have used Article 38's social order mandate alongside Article 48A's environmental directive and Article 51A(g)'s environmental duty to develop the constitutional basis for environmental protection as a dimension of social justice — not merely a regulatory obligation but a requirement of the just social order that Article 38 demands. This linkage between environmental sustainability and social justice is one of the most important recent developments in Article 38's constitutional career.
