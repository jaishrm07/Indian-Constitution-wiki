## Why it matters

Article 154 is the State-level counterpart of Article 53, which vests the Union's executive power in the President. Together, these two articles define the formal architecture of executive authority across India's federal structure. The vesting formula — "shall be vested in the Governor" — is identical in language to the presidential vesting clause, deliberately creating a symmetry between the Union and State executives. Yet like Article 53, Article 154 does not make the Governor an independent political executive. It is the gateway through which all State executive power must flow; where that power goes once it enters the gateway is determined by Article 163, which requires the Governor to act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers.

The article is foundational for understanding who bears legal responsibility for State executive action. When a State government is sued for its executive acts, the legal fiction is that the Governor has acted; when courts grant relief against the State's executive action, that relief formally binds the Governor's office. Article 154 also defines the outer boundary of what can be achieved through the Governor's discretionary authority: the "in accordance with this Constitution" qualifier means that no exercise of executive power — however characterised — can escape constitutional scrutiny.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) vests executive power in the Governor and provides that it may be exercised "either directly or through officers subordinate to him in accordance with this Constitution."

The phrase "in accordance with this Constitution" is the operative constraint. It means that the manner of exercise must be constitutionally valid, and constitutional validity in turn requires that the Governor act on Cabinet advice except in the narrow category of truly discretionary matters recognised by Article 163.

Clause (2)(a) preserves the functions of statutory authorities: vesting executive power in the Governor does not transfer to him functions that Parliament or the State Legislature has already conferred on other bodies. The Companies Act, the Income Tax Act, and a multitude of State regulatory statutes confer executive functions on specific statutory authorities; Article 154(2)(a) ensures that this pre-existing statutory delegation survives the constitutional vesting clause.

Clause (2)(b) ensures the legislature can continue to create new subordinate executive authorities — boards, commissions, regulators, development authorities — without those bodies being absorbed into the Governor's personal executive sphere. The Governor is not the only organ through which the State may exercise executive power; he is the constitutional apex, but the legislature may channel executive authority through whatever subordinate structure it chooses.

The net effect is that Article 154 is descriptive of where executive power formally resides, not prescriptive of how it is exercised. It must be read with Articles 163 and 164 to understand the actual constitutional mechanics of State governance.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Constitution Bench read Article 154 together with Article 163 and held that the executive power nominally vested in the Governor must, with very narrow exceptions, be exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. The Court rejected any reading of Article 154 that would permit the Governor to act as an autonomous political executive, drawing an analogy with the President's position under Articles 53 and 74.

**State of Rajasthan v Union of India (1977)** — While primarily an Article 356 case, the Court noted that Article 154's vesting of State executive power in the Governor does not make the Governor an agent of the Union. He is a constitutional office-holder through whom State executive power flows in accordance with the Constitution, not a representative of the Centre displacing the elected State government.

**Ram Jawaya Kapur v State of Punjab (1955)** — The Court held that executive power under Articles 154 and 162 can extend to all matters with respect to which the State Legislature can make laws, even in the absence of specific legislation, unless the Constitution or a statute explicitly limits it. This delineated the broad scope of State executive power that the Governor formally holds.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — A five-judge bench reaffirmed that the executive power vested in the Governor by Article 154 cannot be used to destabilise or displace a government that continues to command a legislative majority. The Court linked Article 154 to the principle of responsible government: executive power must be wielded to support, not subvert, the elected government's constitutional position.

## Relationship to other Articles

Article 154 cannot be read in isolation. Article 153 establishes that there shall be a Governor for each State; Article 155 provides the appointment mechanism (by the President); Article 163 requires the Governor to act on Cabinet advice; and Article 164 operationalises the appointment and removal of Ministers. Together, Articles 153–168 form an integrated scheme of State executive governance. Article 154 is the apex vesting clause in this scheme — the provision from which all other executive powers derive their constitutional authority. The exception in Article 163(1) — that the Governor may act in his discretion in matters where the Constitution expressly requires him to do so — must always be traced back to Article 154 as the source of the power being exercised; if it cannot be so traced, the action is ultra vires.

## Current significance

Article 154 is the textual anchor for litigation over whether a Governor has lawfully exercised State executive power. Disputes have arisen over Governors withholding or delaying Cabinet decisions, promulgating ordinances contrary to ministerial advice, refusing to convene Assembly sessions, or withholding assent from bills passed by State Legislatures. The article's interaction with Article 163 — specifically the question of whether any given gubernatorial action falls within the narrow category of genuine discretion — continues to produce constitutional adjudication. States including Kerala, Telangana, Punjab, and Tamil Nadu have challenged gubernatorial conduct in the Supreme Court on the ground that it exceeds the executive authority the Constitution assigns to the Governor's office under a responsible-government framework. The Supreme Court in 2023 took up the question of Governor-directed delays in granting or withholding assent to State Bills, with Article 154 forming a key textual reference for the Court's analysis of what executive authority the Governor may independently exercise.
