  392. Power of the President to remove difficulties.—(1) The President may, for the purpose of removing any difficulties, particularly in relation to the transition from the provisions of the Government of India Act, 1935, to the provisions of this Constitution, by order direct that this Constitution shall, during such period as may be specified in the order, have effect subject to such adaptations, whether by way of modification, addition or omission, as he may deem to be necessary or expedient:

  Provided that no such order shall be made after the first meeting of Parliament duly constituted under Chapter II of Part V.

  (2) Every order made under clause (1) shall be laid before Parliament.

  (3) The powers conferred on the President by this article, by article 324, by clause (3) of article 367 and by article 391 shall, before the commencement of this Constitution, be exercisable by the Governor-General of the Dominion of India.

## What this Article did

Article 392 is the "removal of difficulties" provision for the transitional period. Unlike most of the articles in the 379-391 cluster, it has not been omitted and remains in the Constitution as enacted, though it is entirely spent.

The article empowered the President to make orders directing that the Constitution would have effect subject to such adaptations (by way of modification, addition, or omission) as the President deemed necessary or expedient to remove difficulties, particularly in relation to the transition from the Government of India Act, 1935 to the new Constitution. This was a broad, flexible power to smooth out transitional problems that the framers may not have foreseen.

However, the power was strictly time-limited: no such order could be made after the first meeting of Parliament duly constituted under Chapter II of Part V (i.e., the First Lok Sabha after the 1951-52 elections). Every order made had to be laid before Parliament.

Clause (3) added a significant historical dimension: the powers under Article 392, along with the powers under Articles 324, 367(3), and 391, could be exercised by the Governor-General of the Dominion of India before the Constitution's commencement. This meant the Governor-General could make adaptation orders in preparation for the constitutional transition before 26 January 1950.

## Historical context

The transition from the Government of India Act, 1935 to the Constitution of India involved replacing an entire legal and constitutional framework. No matter how carefully the Constituent Assembly had planned, practical difficulties in the transition were inevitable. Some provisions of the 1935 Act would need to be adapted rather than simply repealed; some references would need updating; some procedural rules would be incompatible with the new framework.

Article 392 gave the President (and, before commencement, the Governor-General) the power to handle these difficulties administratively, without requiring either a formal constitutional amendment or specific legislative action in each case. The Adaptation of Laws Order, 1950, was a major exercise of this kind of power — it adapted hundreds of central and State laws to conform to the new constitutional framework by replacing references to the Governor-General with references to the President, replacing references to Provinces with references to States, and making similar textual adjustments throughout the statute book.

The proviso limiting the power to before the first meeting of the constitutionally constituted Parliament was important: it meant the adaptation power was strictly transitional and could not be used to make permanent constitutional modifications. Once Parliament was properly constituted under the new Constitution, the normal legislative and constitutional amendment processes would take over.

## Key cases

**Rao Shiv Bahadur Singh v State of VP AIR 1954 SC 322** — The Supreme Court considered the Adaptation of Laws Order, 1950 made under this power, affirming that it was a valid exercise of the transitional adaptation authority. The case illustrated how the Article 392 power was used to align the existing statutory framework with the new constitutional order.

No case appears to have successfully challenged a presidential order made under Article 392. The broad "removal of difficulties" formulation gave the President wide latitude, and courts were reluctant to second-guess transitional adaptation decisions.

## Current significance

Article 392 is spent. The power it conferred could only be exercised before the first meeting of the constitutionally constituted Parliament, which was April 1952. No valid Article 392 order can now be made. The provision remains in the text of the Constitution as an illustration of how the framers built in a flexible executive authority for managing the inevitable complexities of constitutional transition. It is a model of the kind of "final resort" adaptive power that constitutional framers sometimes include to handle unforeseen transitional difficulties.
