## Why it matters

The Seventy-Fourth Amendment created a comprehensive municipal governance framework for States, but India's constitutional map includes Union territories with varying degrees of self-governance. At the time of the Amendment, there were seven UTs with populations ranging from a few thousand (Lakshadweep) to millions (Delhi). Article 243ZB is the bridging provision that extends the entire Part IXA framework — including elected municipalities, the five-year term guarantee, mandatory elections under the SEC's superintendence, ward delimitation, reservation of seats, and Finance Commission provisions — to all Union territories, adapted for their different constitutional positions.

The bridging device Article 243ZB uses is substitution: wherever Part IXA refers to the Governor, the Administrator stands in; wherever it refers to the State Legislature or Legislative Assembly, the UT's own Legislature (where it exists) takes that role. This textual economy allows the entire municipal governance framework to apply to UTs without requiring a separate parallel set of provisions. It also means that constitutional developments in the interpretation of Part IXA provisions — in cases involving States — automatically apply to the equivalent UT context, because the same statutory text (with substituted actors) is in force.

The presidential modification power in the proviso gives the President discretionary authority to tailor Part IXA's application to any UT as needed. This is important because UTs range from Delhi — a metropolis of twenty million people with its own Legislature, High Court, and complex multi-actor governance structure — to island territories or newly created UTs with modest urban populations and minimal pre-existing institutional infrastructure. The proviso allows the President to recognise these differences without treating any UT as exempt from Part IXA's democratic guarantees, which remain the constitutional baseline. Modifications may adjust composition requirements, election procedures, or specific institutional forms to fit UT circumstances, but they cannot remove the fundamental democratic requirements: elected municipalities, constitutional terms, independent election management, and mandatory reservation.

The article also matters because several major constitutional controversies in Indian urban governance have arisen in UT contexts. Delhi — governed under the special arrangement created by Articles 239AA and 239AB after the 69th Amendment — has been the site of sustained litigation over the distribution of authority between the elected government, the Lieutenant Governor, and the Central government. Article 243ZB is the constitutional anchor confirming that Part IXA's protections apply to Delhi's municipalities, meaning that the elected government's control over the Municipal Corporation of Delhi, ward delimitation processes, and SEC-supervised elections is constitutionally grounded, not merely a matter of Central government concession.

## Reading the provision

The main body of Article 243ZB contains two substitution rules. The Governor-to-Administrator substitution is straightforward: wherever Part IXA vests authority in the Governor — for appointments, notifications, or modifications — that authority vests in the Administrator of the UT. The Administrator is appointed by the President under Article 239 and acts as the President's representative; unlike the Governor of a State, the Administrator does not occupy a constitutionally independent position but is effectively an instrument of the Central government's executive authority. This means that the constitutional protections that apply to Governor-exercised powers under Part IXA (such as the requirement to act on the advice of the State government for certain functions) must be recalibrated in the UT context: for UTs without a Legislature, the Administrator acts on the advice of the Central government, and for UTs with a Legislature, the position depends on the specific constitutional arrangement applicable to that UT.

The Legislature/Legislative Assembly substitution applies to UTs that have their own legislative bodies: Delhi, Puducherry, and (since 2019) the reorganised Jammu and Kashmir. For UTs without a Legislature — Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Chandigarh, Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu, Lakshadweep, and Ladakh — Parliament exercises the legislative authority that Part IXA assigns to the State Legislature. This means that municipal laws for these territories are enacted by Parliament, and the Central government exercises the executive functions that State governments exercise in the ordinary Part IXA framework.

The proviso is drafted as a Presidential power to apply Part IXA with "exceptions and modifications" — not as an exemption power. This means the baseline position is that Part IXA applies to all UTs, and the President may only adjust its application to particular UTs, not remove it entirely. Presidential notifications under the proviso have been issued for various UTs to address specific institutional requirements, and the pattern of those notifications provides interpretive guidance on the range of modifications that are constitutionally permissible.

## Key cases

**Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018)** — The Supreme Court's five-judge bench ruling on the constitutional relationship between the elected Government of Delhi, the Lieutenant Governor, and the Central government is directly relevant to Article 243ZB's application to Delhi. The Court held that the elected Government of Delhi has substantive authority over matters within the UT's legislative competence — which includes subjects relevant to municipal governance — and that the LG must act on the aid and advice of the elected government for those matters, not on independent judgment. This ruling provides the framework within which Article 243ZB's municipal governance provisions operate in Delhi.

**State of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2023)** — The Supreme Court's ruling in the services dispute — concerning control over the Delhi government's bureaucracy — has indirect significance for Article 243ZB's application because the relationship between the elected government and the Administrative Officers who manage municipal departments affects whether the municipal governance framework is genuinely under democratic control. The ruling's emphasis on democratic accountability as a constitutional principle reinforces the basis for Article 243ZB's guarantee that urban residents of UTs enjoy the same elected local governance as State residents.

**Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of the City of Ahmedabad (2006)** — While this case concerned a State municipality rather than a UT, the Supreme Court's analysis of the SEC's exclusive constitutional jurisdiction over the conduct of municipal elections under Article 243ZA — and the limits of State legislative interference with that jurisdiction — applies equally to municipalities in UTs by virtue of Article 243ZB's incorporation of the Part IXA framework. The ruling is routinely cited in UT municipal election disputes.

**Municipal Corporation of Delhi v. Birla Cotton, Spinning and Weaving Mills (1968)** — This pre-74th Amendment case established foundational principles about the legal personality and scope of delegated authority of municipal corporations in Delhi. While predating the constitutional framework of Part IXA, the case's recognition that Delhi municipal institutions have genuine governmental functions — not merely delegated administrative tasks — contextualises why Article 243ZB's application of constitutional municipal governance standards to Delhi matters: these are institutions with real governmental power, and the constitutional framework that Article 243ZB applies to them carries genuine democratic significance.

**Mohd. Aslam v. Union of India (2003)** — The Supreme Court addressed the mandatory character of the 74th Amendment's institutional obligations, holding that the constitutional obligation to constitute the institutions of urban self-government is non-derogable. This ruling's application to UTs through Article 243ZB means that the Central government and UT Administrators cannot treat the constitution of municipalities, the conduct of elections, and the implementation of reservation requirements as discretionary matters.

## Current significance

The reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir into two Union territories in 2019 — J&K (with a Legislature) and Ladakh (without) — created new questions about the application of Part IXA municipal provisions to territories with substantial urban agglomerations but without the institutional history of established State-level municipal law. The J&K Reorganisation Act, 2019 and subsequent Presidential notifications addressed many of these transitions. Article 243ZB remains the constitutional basis for ensuring that urban residents in J&K and Ladakh are entitled to locally elected municipal governance.

Delhi's municipal governance has been among the most politically contested in India, and much of that contestation operates against the backdrop of Article 243ZB. The reunification of the three Delhi municipal corporations into the single Municipal Corporation of Delhi in 2022, the extended period without elections that preceded the reunification, and the subsequent MCD elections of December 2022 all played out within the constitutional framework that Article 243ZB anchors. The SEC conducted the MCD elections under the same constitutional superintendence obligation that applies to State municipal elections, and the constitutional protections of Part IXA — including mandatory elected governance, fixed five-year terms, and reservation requirements — applied in full.

The scope of permissible presidential "modifications" under the Article 243ZB proviso has not been definitively litigated, but the general principle from analogous provisions is that a modification may adjust timing, procedural form, or the composition of bodies to fit UT conditions — it cannot modify away the constitutional core of elected governance and independent election management. A presidential notification purporting to indefinitely exempt a UT from conducting municipal elections would be constitutionally vulnerable regardless of the word "modifications" in the proviso, because it would negate the right to elected local self-government that Part IXA creates.

Chandigarh, the capital territory shared by Punjab and Haryana, presents a distinctive Article 243ZB context: it is a UT without a Legislature but with municipal governance obligations extending to a major city that serves as the capital of two States. Presidential notifications under Article 243ZB have calibrated Part IXA's application there to address the specific intergovernmental complexities that this arrangement creates.
