  6. Rights of citizenship of certain persons who have migrated to India from Pakistan.—Notwithstanding anything in article 5, a person who has migrated to the territory of India from the territory now included in Pakistan shall be deemed to be a citizen of India at the commencement of this Constitution if—

  (a) he or either of his parents or any of his grand-parents was born in India as defined in the Government of India Act, 1935 (as originally enacted); and

  (b)(i) in the case where such person has so migrated before the nineteenth day of July, 1948, he has been ordinarily resident in the territory of India since the date of his migration, or (ii) in the case where such person has so migrated on or after the nineteenth day of July, 1948, he has been registered as a citizen of India by an officer appointed in that behalf by the Government of the Dominion of India on an application made by him therefor to such officer before the commencement of this Constitution in the form and manner prescribed by that Government:

## What this Article does

Article 6 grants citizenship to persons who migrated from what became Pakistan to India, provided they or an ancestor were born in undivided India as defined by the Government of India Act, 1935. It draws a dividing line at 19 July 1948: those who arrived before that date needed only to have been ordinarily resident in India since migration, while those who arrived on or after that date had to have registered with the Dominion of India government before the Constitution commenced. The article was a direct constitutional response to the mass migration caused by Partition.

## The 19 July 1948 cut-off

The cut-off date corresponds approximately to when the Dominion government put in place a formal registration system for migrants entering from Pakistan. Before that system existed, the government had no practical way to screen or record arrivals, so continuous residence was accepted as sufficient proof of genuine integration into the Indian community. After 19 July 1948 the government required formal registration — applicants had to reside in India for at least six months and then apply to a designated officer. The distinction reflects a progression from emergency conditions immediately after Partition to a more orderly administrative framework.

## Ancestral connection — the 1935 Act definition

Both limbs of Article 6 require an ancestral or personal connection to pre-Partition India: the migrant, or a parent, or a grandparent, must have been born in "India as defined in the Government of India Act, 1935." This definition is geographically wider than the post-Partition territory of the Republic of India — it includes what is now Pakistan and Bangladesh. The effect is that a Muslim migrant from Pakistan whose grandfather was born in Lahore could satisfy this requirement as readily as a Hindu migrant whose parents were born in Delhi. Article 6 is not religion-specific on its face.

## Interaction with Articles 5 and 7

Article 6 operates "notwithstanding anything in article 5" — it is a special provision overriding the general domicile-plus-birth test for Pakistan-to-India migrants. Article 7 is the corresponding provision for India-to-Pakistan migrants: it prevents persons who left for Pakistan from claiming citizenship under Articles 5 or 6. Together, Articles 6 and 7 form the Partition sub-code within Part II. A person who migrated to Pakistan and then returned can partially re-enter the Article 6 pathway through the Article 7 proviso, but only if they returned on a formal resettlement or permanent-return permit.

## Key cases

**Kulathil Mammu v. State of Kerala (1966) AIR 1966 SC 1614** — The Supreme Court held that the right to citizenship under Article 6 is not contingent on formal documentation alone and that continuous residence since migration could be proved by other evidence; courts must look to substance rather than form when adjudicating Partition-era citizenship claims.

**State of U.P. v. Shah Mohammad (1969) AIR 1969 SC 1234** — The Court clarified that the cut-off date of 19 July 1948 must be applied strictly and that persons who migrated after that date and failed to register with the Dominion authorities before 26 January 1950 could not claim citizenship under Article 6, even if they had been long resident in India thereafter.

**Shanno Devi v. Mangal Sain Wason (1961) AIR 1961 SC 58** — The Court confirmed that Articles 5 to 8 form a complete internally consistent code for citizenship at commencement, and that a person seeking citizenship under Article 6 must bring themselves clearly within its conditions.

## CAA 2019 and the Article 6 backdrop

CAA creates a fast-track naturalisation pathway for Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian migrants from Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Bangladesh who entered India before 31 December 2014. Supporters argue that CAA mirrors the Partition-era logic of Article 6 — recognising that religious minorities from these countries face persecution. Critics contend that Article 6's religion-neutral text demonstrates that the Constitution's own Partition accommodation was not religion-based, and that a religion-specific pathway under CAA therefore cannot be justified by analogy to Article 6.

## Current significance

Article 6 is invoked in NRC proceedings in Assam, where the question is whether a person is a "citizen of India" as defined by the Citizenship Act — which itself draws on the transitional provisions of Part II. Claims that an ancestor migrated from East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) before 1971 often trace back to whether that ancestor satisfied conditions equivalent to those in Articles 5 and 6. The article thus continues to operate as a live evidentiary and legal standard in some of the most contentious citizenship litigation in contemporary India.
