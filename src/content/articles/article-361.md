officialText: |-
  361. Protection of President and Governors and Rajpramukhs.—(1) The President, or the Governor or Rajpramukh of a State, shall not be answerable to any court for the exercise and performance of the powers and duties of his office or for any act done or purporting to be done by him in the exercise and performance of those powers and duties:

  Provided that the conduct of the President may be brought under review by any court, tribunal or body appointed or designated by either House of Parliament for the investigation of a charge under article 61:

  Provided further that nothing in this clause shall be construed as restricting the right of any person to bring appropriate proceedings against the Government of India or the Government of a State.

  (2) No criminal proceedings whatsoever shall be instituted or continued against the President, or the Governor of a State, in any court during his term of office.

  (3) No process for the arrest or imprisonment of the President, or the Governor of a State, shall issue from any court during his term of office.

  (4) No civil proceedings in which relief is claimed against the President, or the Governor of a State, shall be instituted during his term of office in any court in respect of any act done or purporting to be done by him in his personal capacity, whether before or after he entered upon his office as President, or as Governor of such State, until the expiration of two months next after notice in writing has been delivered to the President or the Governor ***, as the case may be, or left at his office stating the nature of the proceedings, the cause of action therefor, the name, description and place of residence of the party by whom such proceedings are to be instituted and the relief which he claims.

## Why it matters

The President and State Governors occupy the apex of the constitutional executive at the Union and State levels respectively. They act on the advice of Councils of Ministers, discharge formal constitutional duties, and exercise discretionary powers in a range of constitutionally prescribed situations. Article 361 confers personal immunity on these office-holders to allow them to discharge their constitutional functions without the risk of personal legal harassment or politically motivated prosecution.

The immunity has four distinct dimensions. Clause (1) bars courts from holding the President or Governor personally answerable for official acts or purported official acts — though the Government itself remains liable for those actions. Clause (2) is absolute within its scope: no criminal proceedings of any kind can be initiated or continued against a sitting President or Governor. Clause (3) bars arrest or imprisonment orders. Clause (4) imposes a two-month notice requirement before civil proceedings for personal acts can be brought — a delay rather than an absolute bar.

The theoretical justification for this immunity is the need to ensure that the highest constitutional offices function without the distraction and intimidation of personal litigation, and that official acts can be taken decisively without the actor fearing post-hoc civil or criminal liability. The historical model is the common law principle of Crown immunity and the US constitutional doctrine of executive immunity.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — immunity for official acts.** No court can question the exercise of constitutional powers and duties by the President or a Governor, or any act done in the exercise of those powers. This is an absolute bar on personal liability for official action. The crucial qualifications are: (a) the first proviso allows the President's conduct to be examined by a body constituted by Parliament for the purpose of impeachment under Article 61; and (b) the second proviso preserves the right of any person to bring proceedings against the Government of India or a State Government. This means the immunity protects the person, not the State — citizens can still sue the Government for wrongs done by gubernatorial action.

**Clause (2) — absolute criminal immunity.** The prohibition on criminal proceedings during the term of office is unqualified. It covers any criminal proceeding in any court, regardless of whether the alleged offence was committed in an official or personal capacity and regardless of whether it pre-dates or post-dates the assumption of office. A Governor who committed an offence as a private person before appointment cannot be prosecuted while holding office. The prosecution must wait until the term ends.

**Clause (3) — no arrest or imprisonment.** No court can issue process for the arrest or imprisonment of a sitting President or Governor. This reinforces the criminal immunity by ensuring that even proceedings that might have been initiated before assuming office cannot result in coercive process against the office-holder during their term.

**Clause (4) — notice for civil proceedings.** Civil proceedings for personal acts — that is, acts not done in the exercise of official constitutional powers — are not barred, but must be preceded by two months' written notice. The notice must specify the nature of the proceedings, the cause of action, the claimant's details, and the relief sought. This requirement is a procedural protection, not an absolute immunity: it ensures the President or Governor is aware of impending litigation and has time to respond before proceedings are filed.

**The limits of the immunity.** Article 361 protects the person, not the office. Courts retain full jurisdiction to examine whether a constitutional duty has been performed, whether the constitutional conditions for an executive act have been met, and whether the Government (as opposed to the individual) is liable for wrongful action. The Supreme Court has been clear that Article 361 is an immunity from personal suit, not an immunity from the constitutional consequences of official action.

## Key cases

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court was asked whether a Governor's recommendation for President's Rule under Article 356 could be challenged on grounds of mala fides. The Court held that Article 361(1) bars examination of the Governor's subjective satisfaction in the exercise of official powers, but that this does not protect the President's proclamation from judicial review — courts can examine the validity of the proclamation at the presidential level even if they cannot question the Governor's intent. The judgment drew the critical line between personal immunity from suit and the justiciability of constitutional consequences flowing from executive action.

**B P Singhal v Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court considered the removal of Governors by the President acting on the advice of the Council of Ministers, and held that while the President has the power to remove a Governor at pleasure with no obligation to give reasons, such removal cannot be based on political or collateral considerations such as the Governor being associated with a different political persuasion from the Centre. The judgment confirmed that Article 361 immunity during the term of office does not insulate the act of removal from judicial review for arbitrariness or mala fides — it only shields the office-holder from personal suit during the term.

**State of Tamil Nadu v Governor of Tamil Nadu (2025 INSC 481)** — The Supreme Court addressed the Governor of Tamil Nadu's prolonged failure to give assent to bills passed by the State Legislature. The Court held that the Governor's inaction could amount to a breach of constitutional duty under Articles 200 and 201 and that courts can issue appropriate directions requiring constitutional duties to be discharged — even when they cannot summon the Governor personally as a defendant under Article 361(1). The judgment reinforced that Article 361 is a shield against personal liability, not a licence to avoid constitutional obligations.

## Current significance

Governor immunity under Article 361 has become acutely contested in the context of the recurring constitutional friction between Raj Bhavans and State Governments led by parties opposed to the Centre. The pattern of conduct that has attracted legal challenge includes: prolonged withholding of assent to State legislation; refusal to convene sessions of State Legislatures; transmission of adverse Article 356 reports without adequate factual basis; and allegations of politically motivated use of the Governor's formal powers.

The absolute criminal immunity in clause (2) means a sitting Governor cannot be prosecuted for any offence committed at any time, including before assuming office. Where a Governor's appointment is alleged to have been made precisely to provide immunity from prosecution, the provision creates a constitutionally uncomfortable situation that Article 361 itself provides no mechanism to address.

Scholarly debate centres on whether the immunity strikes the right balance. Those defending the current position argue that the indirect accountability mechanisms — impeachment at the Union level, judicial review of constitutional consequences, liability of the Government — are adequate. Critics argue that the combination of Article 361 immunity, security of tenure for part of the term, and the Governor's role as a political appointee creates a structural accountability deficit that is increasingly being exploited. The Supreme Court's 2025 Tamil Nadu judgment signals that courts are willing to push the boundaries of what is reviewable around the edges of Article 361, but the core personal immunity for criminal prosecution during office remains untouched.
