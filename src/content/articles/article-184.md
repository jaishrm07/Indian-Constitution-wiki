  184. Power of the Deputy Chairman or other person to perform the duties of the office of, or to act as, Chairman.—(1) While the office of Chairman is vacant, the duties of the office shall be performed by the Deputy Chairman or, if the office of Deputy Chairman is also vacant, by such member of the Council as the Governor may appoint for the purpose.

  (2) During the absence of the Chairman from any sitting of the Council the Deputy Chairman or, if he is also absent, such person as may be determined by the rules of procedure of the Council, or, if no such person is present, such other person as may be determined by the Council, shall act as Chairman.

## What this Article does

Article 184 mirrors Article 180 for the Legislative Council, establishing the same cascading succession of authority when the Chairman's office is vacant or the Chairman is absent from a sitting. The Deputy Chairman takes precedence; if both offices are vacant, the Governor appoints a member to discharge the duties; and for ordinary sitting-level absences, the rules of procedure or the Council itself may identify a pro-tem chair. The distinction between filling a vacancy (a continuing function) and presiding during an absence (a sitting-specific function) is preserved.

Because the Legislative Council is a permanent body that never faces dissolution, the vacancy scenario under clause (1) is more likely to arise from resignation, removal, or death rather than the dissolution-related continuity concerns that make the equivalent Assembly provision (Article 180) more frequently invoked. The Governor's power to appoint a member to discharge duties when both offices are vacant is one of the limited instances where the executive has a formal role in the internal organisation of the Council.

## The two-tier succession scheme

Article 184 creates a carefully layered sequence. For the vacancy scenario under clause (1), the succession moves first to the Deputy Chairman and then, if that office is also empty, to a member appointed by the Governor. This Governor-appointment backstop has no parallel in the Assembly context — Assembly presiding officers in vacancy situations are managed differently — and it reflects the somewhat more complex circumstances that can arise when both senior officers of the Council are absent simultaneously.

For the sitting-level absence under clause (2), the article establishes an even broader cascade: Deputy Chairman, then a person identified by the rules of procedure, then any person determined by the Council for that sitting. This flexibility ensures that the Council can always find someone to preside, regardless of the number of officers available. The person so presiding during an absence exercises the full procedural powers of the Chair for the duration of the sitting, but their authority ends when the sitting concludes.

## Key cases

**State legislative practice in Maharashtra and Bihar** — Council Chairmen in bicameral states have from time to time been unable to preside due to illness, political controversy, or simultaneous membership crises, activating Article 184(2). Rulings from these Houses confirm that a Deputy Chairman acting under this provision exercises the full powers of the Chairman for the duration of the sitting but cannot take final decisions on pending disqualification petitions filed under the Tenth Schedule without specific authorisation.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — Although the Nabam Rebia case arose in the Assembly context, the Supreme Court's analysis of the powers of a deputy or acting presiding officer when the main office is contested or compromised has been understood to apply by analogy to the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Council under Articles 184 and 185.

**Kihoto Hollohan v Zachillhu (1992)** — The Constitution Bench's holding that the presiding officer's quasi-judicial anti-defection functions cannot be delegated without constitutional sanction limits what a person presiding under Article 184(2) can do in respect of pending Tenth Schedule petitions, an important boundary on the Article 184 succession scheme.

## Current significance

The Governor's residual appointment power under Article 184(1) has attracted attention in states where bicameral politics has become contentious and both Council presiding officers are simultaneously in dispute. Courts have cautioned that the Governor's power to appoint under this provision is strictly limited to procedural necessity — ensuring the Council can function — and does not confer on the appointed member any powers beyond those needed to convene and preside over sessions, reflecting the general principle that executive intervention in legislative proceedings is to be read narrowly.

The provision's reliance on rules of procedure for the clause (2) chain of succession places a premium on each Council maintaining up-to-date procedural rules that clearly identify who presides in various absence scenarios. States where rules have not kept pace with the Article 184 framework have experienced procedural disputes about the validity of decisions taken when the designated succession was unclear.

The comparison with Article 180 — the Assembly counterpart — is instructive. Article 180 does not include a Governor-appointment backstop for the vacancy of both Assembly presiding officers simultaneously, reflecting the different constitutional position of the Assembly as an institution that is directly elected and ultimately subject to dissolution. The Legislative Council's permanent character, and the corresponding need to ensure continuous presiding authority without recourse to dissolution, explains the additional layer of executive backup that Article 184(1) provides.
