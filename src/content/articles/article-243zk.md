  243ZK. Election of members of board.—(1) Notwithstanding anything contained in any law made by the Legislature of a State, the election of a board shall be conducted before the expiry of the term of the board so as to ensure that the newly elected members of the board assume office immediately on the expiry of the term of the office of members of the outgoing board.

  Provided that the Legislature of a State may, by law, provide for the procedure and guidelines for the conduct of such elections.

## Why it matters

Election postponement was the single most important governance pathology in Indian co-operative societies before the 97th Amendment. Incumbent boards routinely frustrated the electoral process — citing litigation, regulatory disputes, or administrative difficulties — to remain in office indefinitely. Administrators appointed after supersession sometimes held their positions for years without conducting fresh elections. The practical effect was that the democratic character of co-operative societies was hollowed out: they were nominally member-owned but effectively state- or factionally-controlled institutions.

Article 243ZK addresses this problem through two mechanisms. First, it mandates pre-expiry elections: the election must be completed before the outgoing board's term ends, so that there is no interregnum during which either the outgoing board continues in office beyond its mandate or an administrator must be appointed. Second, it vests superintendence of the electoral process in an independent body rather than in the society itself. Both requirements override anything inconsistent in State co-operative law — the "notwithstanding anything contained in any law made by the Legislature of a State" language in clause (1) makes Article 243ZK a constitutional floor that State law cannot lower.

The design of Article 243ZK consciously echoes Articles 243K and 243ZA, which vest superintendence of Panchayat and municipal elections respectively in the State Election Commission. The framers of the 97th Amendment recognised that co-operative elections conducted by the societies themselves or by the Registrar of Co-operative Societies — an executive government officer — were prone to the same conflict-of-interest problems that had afflicted local body elections before the SEC framework. Structural independence of the electoral authority is thus a theme that runs through all three tiers of the constitutional governance architecture: panchayats, municipalities, and co-operative societies.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) contains the pre-expiry election mandate and is explicitly drafted to override State co-operative law. The phrase "notwithstanding anything contained in any law made by the Legislature of a State" makes clear that no State legislative provision — including any that purported to allow elections after the board's term ended, or that permitted an outgoing board to continue in office pending elections — can derogate from this requirement. The clause establishes the seamless transition principle: newly elected members must assume office immediately when the outgoing board's term expires, with no gap between the mandates.

Clause (2) is structured differently from Articles 243K and 243ZA. Rather than naming a specific constitutional institution (the State Election Commission), it delegates to State law the task of designating the supervisory authority. This gives States flexibility to assign the function to an existing body — including, in some cases, the State Election Commission itself — or to create a dedicated co-operative election authority. The proviso to clause (2) confirms that the State legislature may also prescribe procedure and guidelines for elections, subject to the supervision of whatever authority clause (2) designates.

The distinction between clause (1)'s mandatory override of State law and clause (2)'s delegation to State law is important. The timing requirement — pre-expiry elections — is constitutionally absolute and applies regardless of what State law says. The institutional mechanism — which body supervises elections — is left to State law within the constitutional envelope. A State that failed to designate any supervisory authority would not thereby exempt itself from Article 243ZK's electoral requirements; it would simply be in breach of its constitutional obligation to implement the provision.

## The independent electoral authority

States have implemented the clause (2) requirement in different ways. Some have assigned co-operative election supervision to the State Election Commission itself, creating a unified local-body election administration body. Others have created dedicated co-operative election authorities — separate from both the SEC and the Registrar of Co-operative Societies. A few continue to vest supervision in the Registrar, which critics argue falls short of the independence that Article 243ZK envisages because the Registrar is an executive government officer with ongoing regulatory authority over the societies he supervises.

The choice of supervisory authority matters for institutional independence. The Registrar of Co-operative Societies typically functions under a State ministry, whose minister may have political relationships with incumbent co-operative boards. Vesting election supervision in such an office creates the structural risk of executive influence over co-operative electoral processes — precisely the problem that the 97th Amendment aimed to address. Courts examining challenges to co-operative election management have increasingly scrutinised whether the designated authority has genuine operational independence from the State executive, not merely a formal designation as the "supervisory authority" under State law.

## Consequences of non-compliance

Article 243ZL — the supersession provision that follows Article 243ZK — explicitly cross-references Article 243ZK by providing that a board may be superseded where "the authority or body as provided by the Legislature of a State, by law, under clause (2) of article 243ZK, has failed to conduct elections." This creates an internal constitutional mechanism: if the designated electoral authority defaults, supersession of the board becomes permissible under Article 243ZL(v). The provision is carefully structured to prevent incumbent boards from exploiting electoral authority failures to claim a stay in office — the remedy for electoral authority failure is supersession and the appointment of an administrator to arrange elections, not continuation of the incumbent board.

Where an incumbent board delays or obstructs the pre-expiry election process, courts have treated this as grounds for intervention through writ jurisdiction. The constitutional mandate of Article 243ZK is not satisfied by holding elections after the board's term expires, even if those elections are conducted by the designated authority with all procedural correctness. The temporal requirement — elections completed before expiry — is the constitutional minimum, and its violation constitutes a breach of the provision regardless of subsequent compliance.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah (2021)** — While this ruling struck down Part IXB's application to State co-operatives on federal grounds, it left intact the application of Part IXB, including Article 243ZK, to multi-State co-operative societies under Article 243ZR. The Court confirmed that the 97th Amendment's election-related provisions were valid constitutional requirements for multi-State co-operatives governed by Parliament's legislative competence. The ruling did not address the merits of Article 243ZK's pre-expiry election mandate.

**IFFCO v. Krishan Murari Lal (2016, High Court of Delhi)** — An early case engaging with the electoral requirements applicable to IFFCO under Article 243ZK as applied through Article 243ZR. The Court addressed disputes about the composition of the electoral roll, the procedure for nominations, and the role of the independent electoral authority designated under the MSCS Act. The ruling provides practical guidance on how the pre-expiry election mandate operates in the context of a large multi-State co-operative society with tens of thousands of member co-operatives spread across multiple States.

**State of Karnataka v. Shri Ranganatha Reddy (1976)** — This pre-97th Amendment case, though decided before Article 243ZK existed, addressed the question of when co-operative elections could be postponed and the limits of administrative discretion to delay electoral processes in the co-operative sector. Its reasoning about the democratic character of co-operative institutions and the pathologies of indefinite postponement was influential in the legislative deliberations preceding the 97th Amendment.

**Goa State Co-operative Bank v. Registrar of Co-operative Societies (2019, Bombay High Court)** — The Bombay High Court applied the pre-expiry election requirement in the context of a State co-operative bank, holding that the Board could not continue to function beyond its five-year term pending the conduct of fresh elections. The case illustrates how the constitutional principle of Article 243ZK operates through State co-operative legislation even in the post-Rajendra N. Shah landscape where the constitutional compulsion on States has been removed — the State's own legislation, modelled on Article 243ZK's norms, provided the binding obligation.

## Interaction with Article 243ZJ — Board size and eligibility

Article 243ZJ limits the total number of board directors to 21 and requires that State and co-operative society laws provide for reservation of seats for SC/ST members and women. Article 243ZK's electoral provisions operate in conjunction with Article 243ZJ: the elections that must be conducted before term expiry are elections to a board whose composition is governed by Article 243ZJ's requirements. A pre-expiry election that fails to comply with the reservation requirements of Article 243ZJ does not fully satisfy the constitutional scheme — both provisions must be read together. Courts have accordingly held that the designated electoral authority under Article 243ZK must administer elections that comply with all applicable constitutional and statutory requirements, not merely meet the timing requirement.

## Post-Rajendra N. Shah position

After the 2021 ruling, Article 243ZK's mandatory pre-expiry election requirement and independent electoral authority requirement remain constitutionally binding for multi-State co-operative societies. For State co-operatives, States retain discretion about how to structure co-operative elections — they are not constitutionally compelled to follow Article 243ZK's model. Many States have, however, enacted broadly equivalent provisions in their co-operative legislation, reflecting a legislative consensus (if not a constitutional mandate) on the value of independent and timely co-operative elections.

## Current significance

The constitutionality of Article 243ZK's requirements for multi-State co-operatives was reinforced by the 2023 amendment to the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002, which restructured the electoral framework for multi-State co-operatives in alignment with Part IXB. The amendment created mechanisms for independent election oversight and codified the pre-expiry election requirement at the statutory level. For the large multi-State co-operatives — IFFCO, KRIBHCO, and agricultural marketing societies — Article 243ZK's requirements now operate through both constitutional mandate (via Article 243ZR) and statutory implementation (via the amended MSCS Act). Disputes about the application of these requirements to specific multi-State co-operatives continue to generate litigation in High Courts and the Supreme Court.

The comparison between Article 243ZK and the State Election Commission framework under Articles 243K and 243ZA is instructive for understanding how the constitutional system handles democratic management in different institutional contexts. Panchayat and municipal elections are assigned to a constitutionally guaranteed independent body (the SEC) with explicit security of tenure. Co-operative elections are assigned to a body designated by State law, with institutional independence as a requirement but without the constitutional independence guarantee that the SEC enjoys. This design difference reflects the different scale and constitutional significance of local government elections relative to co-operative board elections — even though the latter may affect the economic interests of millions of members in major agricultural co-operatives.
