## What this Article does

Article 173 sets out the minimum positive qualifications for membership of a State Legislature. Three requirements are specified: citizenship with an oath or affirmation of allegiance to the Constitution, a minimum age (twenty-five years for the Legislative Assembly, thirty for the Legislative Council), and any additional qualifications Parliament may prescribe by law. The provision is framed as a negative condition — a person shall not be qualified unless these requirements are met — making the listed criteria the floor below which no candidate may stand.

Article 173 is the State-level counterpart to Article 84, which prescribes qualifications for membership of Parliament. The two provisions are virtually identical in structure, and courts regularly read them together when developing the general constitutional framework for legislative membership eligibility. The State provisions must also be read alongside Article 191 (disqualifications for State legislators) and the Representation of the People Act, 1951, which Parliament has enacted under Article 173(c) to prescribe additional statutory qualifications and the detailed machinery for their verification.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a) — Citizenship and oath**

A candidate must be a citizen of India. The citizenship requirement is verified at the nomination stage: election officers scrutinise nomination papers for evidence of citizenship. Clause (a) further requires the candidate to make and subscribe before an officer authorised by the Election Commission an oath or affirmation in the form set out in the Third Schedule. This oath — to bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India, to uphold its sovereignty, and to discharge duties faithfully — is a constitutional commitment, not a mere formality. A candidate who refuses to take the oath or makes a false declaration of citizenship can be invalidated.

The Third Schedule form for State legislators is the same in substance as that for Members of Parliament, creating a uniform constitutional commitment across all Houses.

**Clause (b) — Age**

The minimum age of twenty-five for the Legislative Assembly and thirty for the Legislative Council reflects the framers' view that the upper house should draw on greater experience and maturity. The same age differential appears in Article 84 for the Lok Sabha (twenty-five) and Rajya Sabha (thirty). Age is verified by the election officer at nomination; age certificates from school records, birth certificates, or affidavits are the standard evidentiary forms.

The Constituent Assembly considered but rejected a lower minimum age of twenty-one for the Assembly, concluding that twenty-five struck the right balance between youth participation and legislative seriousness. Several constitutional reform commissions have since recommended reducing the Assembly age to twenty-one to align with the voting age, but this change has not been made.

**Clause (c) — Parliament's power to prescribe additional qualifications**

Parliament's power under clause (c) to prescribe qualifications by law is the most practically significant element of Article 173. The Representation of the People Act, 1951 exercises this power extensively. Section 5 of the Act adds the requirement that a candidate for the Assembly be an elector in any constituency within the State — a requirement of electoral registration that complements citizenship. For candidates from reserved constituencies (Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes), additional eligibility criteria apply under the Act and the Delimitation Orders.

The power under clause (c) is plenary subject to constitutional constraints. Parliament can add positive qualifications — educational requirements, professional experience, electoral registration — but cannot impose qualifications that discriminate on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth contrary to Part III, or that violate the basic structure of democracy. The question of how far Parliament can go in prescribing qualifications without infringing the constitutional right to contest elections has generated significant litigation.

## Comparison with Article 84

Article 84 (qualifications for Parliamentary membership) and Article 173 are structurally identical. Both require citizenship with an oath, prescribe age minimums differentiating between the two Houses, and delegate to Parliament the power to add qualifications. The same case law on the scope of Parliament's delegated authority, the nature of the oath obligation, and the cumulative character of qualifications applies across both provisions.

One difference is that Article 84 expressly uses the phrase "subscribes" before the Election Commission-authorised officer; Article 173(a) was substituted in 1974 by the Constitution (Thirty-fifth Amendment) Act to align the state provision with the parliamentary one and to extend the oath/affirmation form to include Sikkim's accession, though the substantive framework is unchanged.

## Key cases

**Election Commission of India v. Saka Venkata Subba Rao (1953) SCR 1144** — The Supreme Court held that qualifications prescribed under Article 173 and the Representation of the People Act are cumulative. A candidate who satisfies the constitutional requirements but fails a statutory qualification under clause (c) is equally disqualified. This confirmed that Parliament's delegation under clause (c) is a real and enforceable addition to the constitutional floor, not merely directory guidance.

**Lily Thomas v. Union of India (2013) 7 SCC 653** — While primarily arising under Articles 102 and 191, the Court's ruling that a sitting member convicted and sentenced to two or more years of imprisonment is immediately disqualified — with no saving for a pending appeal — confirmed that the qualifications and disqualifications framework as a whole operates without judicial suspension pending challenge. The judgment reinforced the importance of the nexus between positive qualifications under Article 173 and the disqualification regime under Article 191.

**Rajbala v. State of Haryana (2015) 16 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court upheld minimum educational qualifications for Panchayat elections under the Haryana Panchayati Raj Act, holding that education is a constitutionally permissible additional qualification for local-body office. However, the Court's reasoning drew extensively on the Article 173 framework for State legislative elections, distinguishing local-body qualifications from the constitutional scheme for legislative membership where the Constitution itself sets the qualification floor.

**Manoj Narula v. Union of India (2014) 9 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court refused to read into the Constitution a disqualification for persons charge-sheeted for serious offences, holding that only Parliament can legislate additional disqualifications under Articles 102 and 191, and qualifications under Articles 84 and 173. The judgment confirmed that courts cannot themselves add to the qualifications regime; only Parliament can do so through the delegated power in clause (c).

## Current significance

Article 173 has acquired renewed relevance as debates intensify about criminalisation of politics. Following the Supreme Court's directions in Public Interest Foundation v. Union of India (2018), candidates are now required to declare pending criminal cases prominently. However, the Court confirmed that it cannot itself impose a disqualification; only Parliament, through its power under Article 173(c), can convert a pending criminal charge into a qualification bar.

Proposals to lower the minimum age for Assembly membership from twenty-five to twenty-one have been recommended by the Law Commission of India and linked to the Constitutional (Sixty-first Amendment) Act, 1988, which reduced the voting age to eighteen. Bringing the candidacy age down to align with the voting age remains a pending reform debate.

The Election Commission's role in administering the oath under clause (a) — and the consequences of a false declaration of citizenship — has also generated litigation in cases involving persons who may have dual citizenship or uncertain status under the Citizenship Act, 1955. As migration patterns and overseas citizenship questions multiply, the citizenship-and-oath gateway of Article 173(a) will continue to attract scrutiny.
