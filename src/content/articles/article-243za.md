## Why it matters

Article 243ZA is the municipal counterpart of Article 243K and applies the same constitutional framework of independent election management — established for Panchayats in 1992 by the 73rd Amendment — to municipalities through the 74th Amendment of the same year. Before 1992, municipal elections in most States were conducted by State governments directly, through election departments under the control of the State executive. This created the same structural opportunity for manipulation that plagued Panchayat elections: the authority responsible for conducting elections was also controlled by the political actors most immediately affected by election outcomes. States routinely deferred municipal elections to extend the terms of appointed administrators or to avoid electoral accountability for poor urban governance.

The 74th Amendment's answer to this was to vest municipal election authority in the same independent State Election Commission that Article 243K created for Panchayat elections. This is a deliberate structural choice: rather than creating a separate urban election commission, the constitutional design assigns both rural (Part IX) and urban (Part IXA) election management to a single independent body. This unification creates economies of scale and prevents the possibility of a State government playing the two election systems off against each other by cooperating with one while resisting the other. The SEC's constitutional superintendence under Articles 243K and 243ZA together means that any State executive interference with the timing or conduct of local elections — whether rural or urban — is constitutionally invalid.

The provision matters especially because municipal elections touch political and economic interests of a different magnitude from village Panchayat elections. Municipalities control large budgets — particularly major city corporations — and their decisions about land use, construction permissions, contracts, and service delivery have enormous financial consequences for private actors. The incentive to delay or manipulate municipal elections is correspondingly greater. Article 243ZA's application of the SEC's constitutional independence to this domain removes from State governments the most obvious mechanism of municipal election manipulation: direct administrative control over the election process.

The SEC established under Article 243K and operating under Article 243ZA for municipalities has a dual function: it must enforce the constitutional election timeline (Article 243U requires elections to be completed before the term expires or within six months of dissolution, parallel to Article 243E for Panchayats) while managing the logistical complexity of urban elections, which can involve millions of voters in a single municipal corporation. The constitutional independence the provision guarantees is not merely an institutional nicety; it is the precondition for the functioning of the entire democratic accountability framework that Part IXA creates.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) tracks the exact language of Article 243K(1), substituting "Municipalities" for "Panchayats." The reference to the "State Election Commission referred to in article 243K" confirms that the same SEC — not a separate municipal election commission — handles both sets of elections. This means that all of the jurisprudence on SEC independence, security of tenure, and State government cooperation obligations developed under Article 243K applies equally to the SEC's exercise of its functions under Article 243ZA.

Clause (2) preserves the State legislature's authority to legislate on all matters relating to or connected with municipal elections — subject to the Constitution. This is a broad legislative domain: States may prescribe qualifications and disqualifications for candidates and voters, determine ward boundaries through delimitation exercises, fix the number of wards and seats, prescribe reservation requirements (subject to the constitutional floors and triple-test methodology for OBC reservation), establish electoral roll processes, and set rules for election disputes. The constitutional limit is that none of this legislative authority may be used to vest any part of the SEC's superintendence, direction, and control over the conduct of elections in any other body or person. The State legislature may shape the framework within which the SEC operates; it cannot take back the constitutional authority that clause (1) vests in the SEC.

The interaction between clause (2) and the SEC's authority has been tested particularly in the context of OBC reservation. State laws that declared that elections would not be scheduled until OBC reservation was complete effectively conditioned the SEC's ability to schedule elections on the resolution of a State legislative policy question — which courts held was a constitutionally impermissible encroachment on the SEC's superintendence function. The SEC's authority to schedule elections is not contingent on the resolution of any pending legislative or administrative question; it flows directly from clause (1) and is limited only by the constitutional requirements of Part IXA itself.

The reservation architecture that clause (2) enables is multi-layered. Article 243T mandates reservation of seats for SCs and STs in proportion to their population, reservation of not less than one-third of seats for women across all categories, and reservation for OBCs if the State legislature so provides. Delimitation of wards, rotation of reserved constituencies, and the procedure for notifying reservation are all matters on which State law may legislate under clause (2). The SEC's role is to implement the reservation framework as enacted — it does not independently determine reservation percentages, but it must schedule and conduct elections within whatever reservation framework the State has lawfully enacted, and it must proceed if no lawful reservation framework exists for a required category.

## Key cases

**K. Krishnamurthy v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court held, in the context of OBC reservation for local body elections, that the SEC's authority to conduct elections cannot be held hostage to unresolved reservation policy disputes. Once an election is due under State law and the constitutional timeline, the SEC must proceed to conduct it, and neither the State government nor courts can indefinitely stay the election process. This case established that SEC independence under Article 243ZA has a temporal dimension: the SEC must be permitted to act within the time frame the constitutional scheme requires.

**Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of the City of Ahmedabad (2006)** — The Supreme Court held that the State Election Commission's exclusive constitutional jurisdiction over the conduct of municipal elections under Article 243ZA means that any State law or executive action purporting to direct the SEC on election scheduling, reserve list finalisation, or other election management matters is invalid. The case is particularly important for its analysis of the SEC's authority over the reservation rotation roster: the Court held that the SEC, once the State government has completed the required pre-election statutory processes, has the constitutional authority and duty to proceed with elections without further reference to the State executive.

**State of Uttaranchal v. Shri Balwant Singh Chaufal (2010)** — The Supreme Court held that the SEC's constitutional mandate to conduct elections cannot be suspended by the simple device of not delimiting wards or not notifying election schedules. When a State government's delay threatens to create a governance vacuum in a municipality, courts may issue mandamus both to the State to complete pre-electoral processes and to the SEC to proceed with elections once those processes are done. The case reinforced that Article 243ZA creates a non-derogable constitutional timetable.

**Vikas Kishanrao Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021)** — While primarily addressing OBC reservation methodology under the triple test, the Supreme Court's orders in this case directly engaged with the SEC's authority under Article 243ZA to proceed with elections even when OBC reservation was not in place. The Court's direction that elections must proceed without OBC reservation if the constitutional timeline cannot otherwise be met confirmed that the SEC's duty under Article 243ZA is not conditioned on the resolution of reservation disputes.

**Anoop Baranwal v. Union of India (2023)** — Though primarily concerned with the Election Commission of India, the Supreme Court's analysis of institutional independence for election management bodies has been applied in High Court proceedings concerning State Election Commissioner appointments. The ruling's emphasis on the constitutional imperative of independent election management has reinforced the SEC's position under Article 243ZA against executive encroachment.

## Current significance

State Election Commissions administering municipal elections under Article 243ZA have faced sustained pressure from State governments seeking to delay urban local body elections — particularly where OBC reservation disputes, ward delimitation controversies, or political calculations create incentives for incumbents to extend their terms. The Supreme Court has repeatedly issued directions under Articles 243ZA and 243U compelling States to hold overdue municipal elections, and there are now dozens of reported cases in which High Courts and the Supreme Court have had to intervene to enforce what the constitutional text treats as a straightforward obligation.

The OBC reservation cycle — States establishing commissions after elections are struck down without reservation, commissions taking extended time to gather data, elections being held without reservation in the interim — has produced a structural instability in municipal governance across several States. In States like Maharashtra and Madhya Pradesh, large urban local bodies have operated for years under administrator rule rather than elected governance, because the resolution of OBC reservation disputes took longer than Article 243U's six-month post-dissolution election window. This governance vacuum — where elected municipal councils with constitutional democratic mandates are replaced by State-appointed administrators with no electoral accountability — is precisely what the 74th Amendment and Article 243ZA were designed to prevent.

The relationship between Article 243ZA and Article 243ZG (bar to court interference in municipal elections) requires attention. Article 243ZG bars courts from questioning delimitation laws and requires all post-election challenges to be made by election petition. This bar applies to completed elections; it does not prevent courts from issuing pre-election mandamus directions to State governments or the SEC to comply with their constitutional obligations. The interaction between judicial enforcement of the SEC's duty under Article 243ZA and judicial restraint under Article 243ZG means that courts retain supervisory jurisdiction over the election management process up to the point of polling, but step back once results are declared and the election petition forum becomes available.

SEC capacity — in terms of staffing, technical resources, and administrative infrastructure — remains uneven across States, raising questions about whether the constitutional design of independent municipal election management is being realised in practice.
