## Why it matters

Article 105 is the constitutional foundation of parliamentary privilege — a doctrine that gives the legislative branch a protected sphere of autonomy from judicial interference. The purpose is deeply functional: members of Parliament must be able to speak, debate, and vote without fear that an opponent, an aggrieved minister, or a powerful private party will haul them into court for something they said in the House. If parliamentary speech were actionable in ordinary courts, members would self-censor, the adversarial quality of legislative debate would be destroyed, and the executive could silence critical voices by threatening defamation suits. The immunity under Article 105(2) is therefore absolute in its terms — no proceedings in any court for anything said or any vote given.

But the same immunity that protects vigorous democratic debate can also, if read too broadly, protect corruption. When a member accepts money to vote in a particular way, two things happen: the corrupt agreement (a criminal act), and the vote itself (a parliamentary act). The question of whether the immunity covers not just the vote but the entire transaction including the bribe at its foundation was the central dispute in PV Narasimha Rao (1998) and was definitively resolved — in the opposite direction — by the seven-judge bench in Sita Soren (2024). The evolution from Narasimha Rao to Sita Soren is the most important doctrinal development in the history of Article 105.

Beyond individual member immunity, Article 105(3) preserves the collective privileges of Parliament as an institution — the power to regulate its own proceedings, punish members for contempt or misconduct, exclude strangers from its galleries, and control the publication of its debates. These collective privileges, which Parliament has never codified into legislation as clause (3) anticipates, are sourced from the practices of the British House of Commons as they existed before the Forty-fourth Amendment came into force in 1978. They represent a substantial reserve of institutional authority that courts will not lightly second-guess, though they will intervene in cases of clear illegality or fundamental procedural irregularity.

The corresponding provision for State legislatures is Article 194. The two articles are substantively parallel, ensuring that state legislative assemblies enjoy equivalent autonomy from judicial scrutiny of their internal proceedings.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Freedom of speech in Parliament**: The guarantee of free speech in Parliament is subject only to the provisions of the Constitution itself and to the rules and standing orders of each House. This means the Speaker or Chairman can enforce procedural rules that require relevance, courtesy, and order — but cannot use those rules to suppress substantive political speech. The guarantee applies to all members, regardless of whether they are supporting or opposing the government. Ministers addressing Parliament are also protected; a minister who misleads Parliament may face political consequences but not legal liability for the statement itself.

**Clause (2) — Immunity from court proceedings**: The scope of this immunity is carefully defined but very broad. It covers anything "said" in Parliament — not just formal speeches but all oral contributions to debate, questions, answers, and interjections. It covers any "vote given" — including votes on bills, resolutions, motions of confidence or no-confidence, and procedural votes. It extends to publications made by or under the authority of a House — the verbatim record (Hansard equivalent), official reports, committee documents, and formal notices. The immunity from "any proceedings in any court" is comprehensive: civil defamation suits, criminal prosecutions, and administrative proceedings are all excluded. A member cannot be compelled to testify in any court about what they said in Parliament.

**Clause (3) — Uncodified privileges**: Parliament has the power to define its privileges by legislation but has never done so. The result is that the privileges continue to be determined by reference to the practices of the House of Commons as they existed before 26 January 1978 — a moving historical baseline that makes the precise scope of privileges uncertain. This uncertainty has been criticised as incompatible with the rule of law, since citizens and journalists who may face privilege notices cannot know in advance what conduct may be treated as a breach of privilege. The Law Commission and parliamentary reform committees have repeatedly recommended codification, but Parliament has not acted.

**Clause (4) — Extension to non-members**: The immunities of clauses (1), (2), and (3) extend to persons who have a constitutional right to participate in parliamentary proceedings even though they are not members — the Attorney General (Article 88), ministers who are members of one House but address the other, and presidential nominees to the Rajya Sabha. This ensures that the full range of participants in parliamentary proceedings enjoy the same protection as elected members.

## Key cases

**PV Narasimha Rao v State (1998)** — In the most consequential parliamentary privilege case before Sita Soren, a five-judge Constitution Bench considered the JMM bribery scandal in which members of Parliament were allegedly paid to vote in a particular way during a no-confidence motion against the Narasimha Rao government in 1993. The majority of three held that members who actually voted were fully protected by Article 105(2), reasoning that the vote was an act within Parliament and that the immunity was absolute regardless of what had induced the vote. The minority of two (Justice Anand and Justice Bharucha) took the sounder view that the immunity should not protect corrupt inducements, since extending it to bribery would make Parliament a safe harbour for corruption. The majority result was widely condemned as producing the absurd outcome that a member who was bribed and voted as directed was immune, while a member who was bribed but voted differently could be prosecuted. This result stood for twenty-six years until Sita Soren.

**Sita Soren v Union of India (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench unanimously overruled the majority in PV Narasimha Rao. The Court held that the immunity under Article 105(2) does not extend to a member who accepts a bribe in connection with a vote or speech in Parliament, even if the act of voting or speaking itself remains immune. The Court drew a crucial distinction between the parliamentary act (protected) and the corrupt agreement that precedes it (not protected). A member's acceptance of a bribe is not itself a vote or speech; it is a criminal act that takes place outside Parliament, and Article 105(2) immunises acts in Parliament, not anterior corrupt transactions. The Court held that immunity for bribery was incompatible with constitutional morality, the rule of law, and the public's legitimate expectation that their representatives have not been bought. Chief Justice Chandrachud's unanimous judgment also emphasised that parliamentary privilege is a functional protection, not a personal benefit, and must be construed to serve, not defeat, the democratic purposes for which it exists.

**Raja Ram Pal v Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Court upheld the expulsion of ten members of Parliament who were caught on video apparently accepting money in exchange for agreeing to raise questions in the House (the "cash for questions" sting operation). Parliament expelled the members under its uncodified power to maintain its own dignity and discipline. The members challenged their expulsion in court, arguing that expulsion required explicit constitutional or statutory authorisation. A five-judge bench held that the power of expulsion was part of Parliament's inherent jurisdiction over its own composition and proceedings, preserved under Article 105(3). The Court further held that while Article 105 proceedings are in principle justiciable, courts should interfere only in cases of clear constitutional violation — not merely because they disagree with the House's judgment about the seriousness of misconduct. The judgment confirmed that courts cannot reinstate an expelled member by reviewing the merits of the expulsion decision.

**Kalpana Mehta v Union of India (2018)** — The Supreme Court addressed whether it could receive the report of a Parliamentary Standing Committee as evidence in a case challenging a pharmaceutical company's conduct. The Committee had inquired into the same matter. The Court held that the report could be received and that courts do not violate Article 105(2) by referring to parliamentary materials as evidence of fact, as long as they do not hold a member liable for what they said in Parliament. The judgment drew a nuanced line between using parliamentary proceedings as a factual record (permissible) and imposing legal liability for parliamentary speech (prohibited). This distinction has significant practical importance for public interest litigation that draws on committee reports and parliamentary debates.

**Subramanian Swamy v Union of India (2016)** — In the context of criminal defamation, the Court noted that Article 105(2) creates an absolute immunity for parliamentary speech that is broader than the partial protection of the right to reputation under Article 21. The contrast illuminated the peculiarly strong protection given to legislative speech compared to other categories of speech in India. A journalist who makes the same allegation outside Parliament can face criminal defamation proceedings; a member who makes it inside Parliament cannot.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1964 (Keshav Singh case)** — Following the imprisonment of a citizen for breach of privilege by the Uttar Pradesh Legislative Assembly, the President referred to the Supreme Court the question of whether a High Court could grant bail to a person committed for contempt by a legislature. The Court held that it could, and that the High Court had jurisdiction to inquire into the legality of a legislature's detention order even though legislative proceedings themselves were immune from judicial scrutiny. This case established that Article 105 immunity is wide but not absolute — it covers internal legislative proceedings but does not insulate detentions or other coercive acts against non-members from judicial review.

## Parliamentary privilege and the media

A recurring source of controversy under Article 105 is the Houses' practice of issuing privilege notices to journalists, editors, and commentators for reporting on parliamentary proceedings in ways that the House considers inaccurate or disrespectful. These notices invoke Parliament's uncodified contempt power — preserved under Article 105(3) — to summon private citizens before the House's privilege committee.

The constitutional tension is stark: Article 105(3) preserves Parliament's institutional authority to maintain its own dignity; Articles 19(1)(a) and 19(2) protect the press's right to comment on public affairs including parliamentary proceedings. Courts have been reluctant to prevent Parliament from issuing privilege notices on the ground that doing so would itself constitute interference with legislative proceedings. But they have also signalled, particularly in the Rajya Sabha's privilege proceedings against journalists in 2005-06, that the power to summon non-members for privilege should be exercised with restraint and should not be used to silence legitimate press criticism of Parliament.

The failure to codify parliamentary privileges, as envisaged by Article 105(3), means that journalists face an undefined and unpredictable threat whenever their reporting displeases the House. Parliamentary reform advocates have consistently argued that codification — clearly specifying which acts amount to breach of privilege and what penalties can be imposed — is the only constitutionally appropriate solution, since the present system leaves citizens unable to know in advance what conduct puts them at risk of privilege proceedings.

## Current significance

The Sita Soren (2024) decision has fundamentally reset the landscape of legislative privilege in India. For the first time, members of Parliament who accept bribes in connection with their votes or speeches face criminal prosecution that they cannot defeat by pointing to Article 105(2). Ongoing prosecutions against members implicated in alleged bribery are now proceeding on the basis that the pre-Sita Soren immunity no longer applies. The practical question — how prosecutors can prove the corrupt agreement that preceded the vote, given that parliamentary speech itself remains inadmissible as the basis for liability — will occupy courts for years.

The expulsion and disqualification power under Article 105(3), confirmed in Raja Ram Pal, continues to generate controversy. The Tenth Schedule's anti-defection provisions interact with Article 105(3) in complex ways: a member who votes against the party whip may simultaneously face anti-defection disqualification proceedings under Schedule X (before the Speaker) and potential expulsion proceedings under Article 105(3) (before the full House). The Supreme Court's restraint in reviewing both processes creates a significant accountability gap when both mechanisms are delayed or manipulated.

The question of whether Article 105 privileges are subject to the basic structure doctrine — whether Parliament could by law so define its privileges as to effectively immunise criminal conduct — was not squarely addressed in Sita Soren but lurks as a future constitutional question. The Court's reasoning that immunity for bribery is incompatible with constitutional morality suggests that some constitutional limit on the scope of privilege may be derivable from the basic structure.
