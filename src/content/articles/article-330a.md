## What this Article does

Article 330A was inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 — commonly called the Women's Reservation Act or Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam. It creates a constitutional mandate for one-third reservation of seats for women in the House of the People (Lok Sabha), the first such structural quota at the national parliamentary level in Indian history.

Clause (1) states the basic obligation: seats shall be reserved for women in the House. Clause (3) sets the quantum: as nearly as may be, one-third of all directly elected seats — including the SC/ST sub-quota — shall go to women. Clause (2) carves out a nested quota: of the seats already reserved for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes under Article 330(2), one-third shall be further reserved for women belonging to those communities. Women from SC/ST communities therefore benefit from a layered reservation — first as members of their scheduled community, and then as women within that community.

## Reading the provision

The structure of Article 330A should be read together with existing Article 330. Article 330 reserves seats for SCs and STs in proportion to their population in each State. Article 330A overlays a women's quota on top of the general composition: roughly one in three Lok Sabha seats — currently 543 total — must be filled by a woman candidate. Of the existing SC/ST reserved seats, one-third must specifically elect women candidates from those communities.

The article does not prescribe separate electorates or a nomination system. Women's reserved constituencies will be contested by women candidates from all parties under the normal first-past-the-post system — the same mechanism used for SC/ST reserved seats since 1950.

## Historical background and the long road to enactment

Proposals for reserving seats for women in Parliament date to the early 1990s. The 81st Constitution Amendment Bill was introduced in 1996 and lapsed without passage. The bill was re-introduced in the 12th, 13th, and 14th Lok Sabhas but was repeatedly stalled by disagreements over whether an OBC sub-quota for women should be included, with parties representing backward classes opposing passage without that sub-quota, while proponents argued the OBC sub-quota could be added later by ordinary legislation.

The Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 was passed unanimously in both Houses — in the Lok Sabha on 20 September 2023 and the Rajya Sabha on 21 September 2023 — and received presidential assent on 28 September 2023. The OBC sub-quota was not included; the amendment was passed with the understanding that Parliament could legislate on OBC inclusion once the reservation framework was operational.

The 106th Amendment also inserted Article 332A (women's reservation in State Legislative Assemblies) and Article 334A (the operative trigger and sunset clause governing both articles).

## Deferred commencement — Article 334A

Article 330A does not come into operation immediately. Article 334A conditions commencement on two sequential events: (i) publication of figures from the first census taken after the 106th Amendment came into force, and (ii) a delimitation exercise conducted specifically for implementing women's reservation on the basis of those census figures.

The 2021 decennial census was not conducted on schedule. Delimitation under Article 82 after the 2031 census — assuming the census is held on time — would push practical commencement to approximately 2034. The amendment thus commits India to the reform constitutionally while postponing implementation by at least a decade after enactment.

## Rotation

Article 334A(3) requires reserved seats to rotate after each subsequent delimitation. Over successive general elections, different constituencies will be designated as women-reserved, preventing any constituency from being permanently locked as a women-only seat. The methodology for rotation is to be determined by Parliament through legislation. The rotation design mirrors the approach used for SC/ST reserved seats, where delimitation periodically reassigns which constituencies carry the reservation.

## Key cases

**Anita Sharma v Union of India (pending)** — Multiple writ petitions were filed in the Supreme Court after the 106th Amendment seeking a direction to the Union to conduct the census and delimitation expeditiously so that the reservation would be operative for the 2024 general elections. The Court declined to issue such a direction, noting that the timing of the census and delimitation involved executive and legislative policy choices not subject to mandamus, but acknowledged the constitutional significance of the amendment.

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — Though not directly about women's reservation in Parliament, the Supreme Court's nine-judge bench ruling on reservations established foundational principles about the 50 percent ceiling on reservations and the distinction between reservations and representation. The women's reservation framework has been designed to sit outside the 50 percent ceiling applicable to executive-service reservations, on the basis that electoral seat reservations are constitutionally distinct from employment reservations.

## Comparison with SC/ST reservation architecture under Article 330

Article 330A deliberately mirrors the SC/ST reservation framework in Article 330. That article requires SC/ST reserved seats proportional to their population in each State — determined by the Delimitation Commission. Article 330A builds on that foundation by adding a gender overlay: once delimitation designates constituencies as SC/ST reserved, one-third of those very constituencies become additionally reserved for women from those communities.

The women's reservation under Article 330A is not population-proportional in the same way as SC/ST reservations: one-third is a flat national ratio rather than a State-by-State demographic proportion. This means States with very high or very low proportions of women in the population will receive the same one-third floor regardless of demographic variation.

## Current significance

Article 330A represents the most substantial structural change to Lok Sabha composition since the original 1950 Constitution. Women's representation in the Lok Sabha has historically remained below 15 percent — reaching 14.4 percent in the 2019 general elections. When operative, the one-third reservation will guarantee a floor more than double the current achieved level.

The article is part of a cluster with Articles 332A and 334A and should be read alongside the existing SC/ST reservation framework in Articles 330 and 332. The unanswered OBC sub-quota question, the deferred commencement tied to census and delimitation, and the fifteen-year sunset in Article 334A all mean that the political and legal contours of the women's reservation regime will be contested in Parliament and possibly in courts before it fully operates.

Comparatively, India joins a group of democracies — Rwanda, Bolivia, Mexico — that have embedded gender-based legislative quotas in their constitutional text. The Indian approach, tying commencement to delimitation and incorporating a sunset clause renewable by Parliament, reflects a characteristically Indian constitutional compromise: ambitious in principle, incremental in execution.
