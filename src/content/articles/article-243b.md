## Why it matters

Article 243B is the foundational structural provision of the 73rd Amendment (1992), which inserted Part IX into the Constitution and gave Panchayati Raj institutions constitutional status for the first time in India's independent history. Before 1992, Panchayats existed only under ordinary State legislation — the Balwant Rai Mehta Committee (1957) had recommended a three-tier democratic structure for rural governance, and several States had enacted Panchayat laws in response, but these bodies could be created, altered, superseded, or abolished at the will of the State government. The pre-constitutional reality was one of chronic instability: elections were deferred for years, bodies were kept superseded under official administration, and local democratic governance was treated as a concession to be given or withdrawn based on State political calculations.

The 73rd Amendment's central achievement was to convert this discretionary landscape into a constitutional mandate. Article 243B does this by requiring that Panchayats "shall be constituted" at all three tiers — village, intermediate, and district — in every State. The shift from "may" to "shall" is the constitutional transformation: no State government, however politically motivated, can now legally dissolve all Panchayats at a particular tier or choose not to constitute them at all. This mandatory three-tier structure reflects the framework recommended by the L.M. Singhvi Committee (1986), which found that genuine decentralisation required constitutionally entrenched institutions rather than bodies wholly dependent on State legislative goodwill.

The three tiers serve distinct governance functions. The village-level Panchayat (Gram Panchayat) is the primary interface between the State and individual citizens in rural areas — responsible for local public works, sanitation, water supply, and community assets. The intermediate tier (Panchayat Samiti or Block Panchayat) coordinates across villages and links central schemes to the village level. The district-level Panchayat (Zilla Parishad) provides planning and supervision functions across the district, often managing secondary schools, rural hospitals, and larger infrastructure. Together, they constitute India's third tier of government — subordinate to but constitutionally co-equal with the State and Union in the democratic legitimacy that flows from direct elections.

The exception for States with populations under twenty lakhs (clause 2) is a practical concession to the reality that a tiny State may not have enough population or administrative capacity to justify three full tiers. This provision was relevant to Goa and some Union territories in 1992 but has become effectively academic for most purposes. Demographic change has pushed most relevant units beyond this threshold, and States that once claimed it have typically chosen to constitute all three tiers regardless.

## Reading the provision

The operative word of Article 243B(1) is "shall be constituted" — this is a positive constitutional obligation on the State government, not merely a permission or a directive principle aspiration. The provision must be read alongside Article 40 (Directive Principle on Gram Panchayats), which preceded it and which the 73rd Amendment was designed to operationalise. Where Article 40 says the State "shall take steps" to organise village Panchayats, Article 243B converts that aspiration into a concrete structural requirement: three tiers, all States, no exception beyond the minor population carve-out.

The phrase "in accordance with the provisions of this Part" is equally significant. It means that the constitution of Panchayats under Article 243B is not a formality — the bodies constituted must comply with the democratic requirements of Articles 243C (direct elections), 243D (reservations), 243E (five-year terms), and 243K (State Election Commission superintendence). A State cannot satisfy Article 243B by constituting bodies that are Panchayats in name but lack the democratic character required by the rest of Part IX. The three-tier structure is thus not merely organisational but constitutional — each tier must be a functioning democratic institution with elected members, reserved seats, and a fixed term.

The exception in clause (2) is carefully drafted as "Panchayats at the intermediate level may not be constituted" — it covers only the intermediate tier, not the village or district levels. Even the smallest State must have Gram Panchayats and, if it crosses the twenty-lakh threshold, must constitute all three tiers. This design reflects the view that the village and district tiers are the structural anchors of Panchayati Raj, with the intermediate tier serving primarily as a coordination mechanism that can be dispensed with where the administrative geography is simple enough to make it unnecessary.

## Key cases

**State of Uttarakhand v. Balwant Singh Chaufal (2010)** — The Supreme Court addressed governance accountability at the local level and underscored that Panchayats constitute a distinct tier of government with obligations flowing from Part IX. The Court characterised the three-tier structure created by Article 243B as not merely organisational but constitutionally normative: Panchayats are constitutional institutions whose functioning — or failure to function — is judicially reviewable. Delays in constituting Panchayats or allowing them to remain defunct were characterised as violations of constitutional duty, not merely administrative lapses.

**Gram Panchayat of Village Jamalpur v. Malwinder Singh (1985)** — Decided before the 73rd Amendment, this Supreme Court case addressed the status of village Panchayats under pre-constitutional State law. The Court recognised that Panchayats had a distinct legal personality and that interference with their functioning required clear statutory authority. The case is part of the pre-Amendment jurisprudential landscape that the Constitution-makers sought to improve upon by inserting Part IX: it showed both that Panchayats could function as legal entities and that they remained wholly dependent on State legislative goodwill — the gap that Article 243B was designed to close.

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004)** — The Supreme Court considered obligations under Part IX regarding the completeness of the three-tier structure. The Court held that constitutional provisions relating to the constitution of Panchayats must be read purposively, ensuring that the mandatory obligation to have functioning bodies at all levels is not diluted by procedural or administrative justifications for delay or non-constitution. The ruling reinforced that Article 243B creates a justiciable obligation that courts can enforce through mandamus.

**Kishan Singh v. State of Rajasthan (2006)** — The High Court considered a challenge to the State government's attempt to delay reconstitution of Panchayats following dissolution. The Court held that the obligation under Article 243B to constitute Panchayats at all three tiers is continuous, not one-time: dissolution of a body triggers the fresh-election obligation under Article 243E, and neither dissolution nor the post-dissolution election window can be extended on the ground that reconstituting the tier is administratively inconvenient.

**Rajiv Gandhi Panchayat Sansthan v. State of Rajasthan (2013)** — A case addressing the reorganisation of Panchayat tiers through state legislation. The Court held that while States retain legislative discretion over the specific form, nomenclature, and powers of each tier, they cannot use that discretion to effectively nullify the three-tier mandate by creating bodies at only two levels and relabelling them as three. The structural integrity of the three-tier system required by Article 243B is not satisfied by formal compliance while substantively dismantling the intermediate or district tiers.

## Current significance

The three-tier structure mandated by Article 243B remains contested terrain in several States where intermediate-level Panchayats have been weakened, kept unfunded, or had their elections deferred. Tensions arise between State governments — which retain legislative control over the specific form and powers of each tier — and the constitutional floor established by Article 243B, which removes their discretion to abolish any tier entirely. This has become particularly visible in States that have attempted structural reorganisation of rural local bodies without adequately reconstituting the intermediate tier, sometimes holding only village-level and district-level elections for extended periods.

The fiscal dimension of Article 243B's guarantee has become more prominent as State Finance Commissions are required by Article 243I to recommend devolution of funds to Panchayat bodies at all three tiers. Where intermediate tiers are not properly constituted or are kept administratively dormant, they cannot be effective channels for financial devolution — undermining the three-F framework (funds, functions, functionaries) that the 73rd Amendment's architects envisaged. The Fourteenth and Fifteenth Finance Commissions each noted that devolution to the third tier depends on all three tiers being constituted and functional, making Article 243B's structural mandate a precondition for the financial architecture of Panchayati Raj.

The article also feeds into the broader set of controversies tracked by the [local-body-election-delays-and-sec-independence](../current-affairs/local-body-election-delays-and-sec-independence) current-affairs page. When States delay elections to reconstituted or newly delimited Panchayat areas, the constitutional obligation under Article 243B to have functioning bodies at all three levels provides the petitioners' primary textual hook for High Court and Supreme Court intervention. The five-year election cycle required by Article 243E, the SEC's constitutional superintendence under Article 243K, and the structural mandate of Article 243B together form the constitutional framework within which local democracy must operate.
