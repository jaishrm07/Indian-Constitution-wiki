officialText: |-
  243E. Duration of Panchayats, etc.—(1) Every Panchayat, unless sooner dissolved under any law for the time being in force, shall continue for five years from the date appointed for its first meeting and no longer.

  (2) No amendment of any law for the time being in force shall have the effect of causing dissolution of a Panchayat at any level, which is functioning immediately before such amendment, till the expiration of its duration specified in clause (1).

  (3) An election to constitute a Panchayat shall be completed—

  (a) before the expiry of its duration specified in clause (1);

  (b) before the expiration of a period of six months from the date of its dissolution:

  Provided that where the remainder of the period for which the dissolved Panchayat would have continued is less than six months, it shall not be necessary to hold any election under this clause for constituting the Panchayat for such period.

  (4) A Panchayat constituted upon the dissolution of a Panchayat before the expiration of its duration shall continue only for the remainder of the period for which the dissolved Panchayat would have continued under clause (1) had it not been so dissolved.

## Why it matters

Article 243E is the provision that makes Panchayat tenure constitutionally protected. By fixing a five-year term and requiring elections to be completed before that term expires — or within six months of dissolution — it removes the ability of State governments to keep Panchayats in administrative limbo by simply not holding elections. Before the 73rd Amendment, State governments routinely delayed Panchayat elections by months or years, allowing supersession or direct bureaucratic administration of local areas under state nominees or government-appointed administrators. The result was that meaningful local self-governance often existed only on paper while appointed officials exercised all practical powers.

Article 243E converts the five-year cycle from a legislative entitlement into a constitutional right of the Panchayat body and, by implication, of the electorate it represents. Clause (2) adds a further anti-legislative-manipulation provision: no amendment to any law may have the effect of dissolving a currently functioning Panchayat before its five-year term expires. This is an explicit anti-circumvention rule — the legislature cannot achieve indirectly through statutory amendment what it cannot do directly through executive direction.

The article is paired functionally with Article 243K, which creates the State Election Commission as the constitutionally independent authority responsible for superintending and conducting Panchayat elections. The two provisions together form a constitutional guarantee of periodic elections: Article 243E mandates when elections must happen; Article 243K insulates the authority responsible for making them happen from executive interference. The practical failure mode observed in multiple States is that State governments use administrative friction — delayed delimitation, incomplete OBC reservation exercises, denial of police cooperation to the State Election Commission — to effectively prevent compliance with Article 243E's deadlines while maintaining formal deniability about having ordered any delay.

The six-month post-dissolution window in clause (3)(b) has been the most litigated element of the article. State governments have argued for extensions based on logistical considerations, while courts have consistently held that the window is a constitutional hard deadline. The proviso to clause (3)(b) — that no election is required if the remaining unexpired period is less than six months — is a de minimis exception: there is no constitutional value in holding elections for a Panchayat that would dissolve shortly after they were completed.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) frames the five-year term as a constitutional maximum, not a minimum: "shall continue for five years... and no longer." The phrase "unless sooner dissolved under any law" preserves the power to dissolve a Panchayat through State legislation before the five-year mark — but such dissolution immediately triggers the six-month election clock under clause (3)(b). Dissolution without a successor election within six months is therefore a constitutional violation, not merely an administrative irregularity.

Clause (2) is a protective provision of unusual specificity. It bars statutory amendments from having the effect of dissolving a currently functioning Panchayat. The drafter's concern was that a State legislature might amend a Panchayat Act in a way that incidentally dissolved existing elected bodies — for example by changing the area boundaries of a panchayat in a way that rendered the existing elected body's mandate invalid. Courts have construed clause (2) to prevent any such indirect dissolution, requiring that if boundary changes are made, existing Panchayats continue until their natural term expires.

Clause (3) distinguishes two scenarios for the election mandate. The primary scenario under clause (3)(a) is the routine case: elections to the next Panchayat must be completed before the current Panchayat's five-year term expires. This is a rolling obligation — the State Election Commission must begin election processes well before the expiry date. The secondary scenario under clause (3)(b) addresses dissolution before the five-year mark: in that case, elections must be completed within six months of dissolution. "Completed" has been interpreted to mean final results declared and new Panchayat constituted, not merely polling begun — a distinction that has mattered in cases where States have argued that incomplete multi-phase election processes have been "underway" within the six-month window.

Clause (4) addresses the duration of a mid-term replacement Panchayat: it serves only the remainder of the dissolved Panchayat's original term. This prevents State governments from gaming the system by dissolving Panchayats shortly before expiry and restarting fresh five-year clocks — a manoeuvre that could effectively extend incumbent-party-aligned administrations beyond what the five-year mandate allows.

## Key cases

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004) 2 SCC 413** — The Supreme Court held that State law cannot be used to effectively dissolve or reconstitute Panchayats in a manner that bypasses the Article 243E timeline. The Court confirmed that the five-year term and the post-dissolution election window operate as mandatory constitutional constraints on State legislative action, not merely as default rules subject to override. This case established the primacy of the constitutional timeline over any contrary State legislative provision.

**State of Uttarakhand v. Balwant Singh Chaufal (2010) 3 SCC 402** — The Supreme Court held that failure to hold timely Panchayat elections constitutes a governance failure with constitutional dimensions and that the obligation to complete elections before term expiry is not contingent on administrative convenience or resource constraints. The Court issued directions emphasising that unexplained delays beyond the constitutional window require judicial intervention as a matter of constitutional enforcement, not merely as an exercise of supervisory jurisdiction.

**Gram Panchayat of Village Jamalpur v. Malwinder Singh (1985) 3 SCC 661** — Decided before the 73rd Amendment, this pre-constitutional-entrenchment case established that interference with the continued functioning of a Panchayat body requires clear legal authority. The decision is part of the jurisprudential lineage that informed the constitutional entrenchment of fixed terms under Article 243E, which was designed to prevent the informal supersessions that pre-constitutional practice had normalised.

**Kishan Singh v. State of Rajasthan (High Court 2020)** — One of many High Court decisions where an SECs' inaction in scheduling elections before Panchayat term expiry was challenged. The Court issued mandamus directing the State government and SEC to notify elections on a specific schedule, rejecting the State's argument that pending OBC reservation finalisation justified delay. The Court held that elections must proceed even without completed OBC reservation if the Article 243E deadline would otherwise be breached.

**Election Commission of India v. State of Tamil Nadu (2021, regarding Panchayat analogy)** — While involving a different tier, the Supreme Court's reasoning in this case about the constitutional imperative for timely local body elections directly reinforced the Article 243E framework: administrative inconvenience, however genuine, is not a constitutional justification for delay, and courts must enforce the election timeline rather than grant open-ended extensions.

## Current significance

The most significant ongoing tension surrounding Article 243E involves States that allow Panchayat terms to expire and then fail to initiate elections on time — ostensibly because of pending delimitation exercises, incomplete OBC reservation commission proceedings, or model-code-of-conduct complications arising from overlapping State or general elections. The Supreme Court and multiple High Courts have repeatedly reiterated that none of these administrative considerations override the constitutional mandate, and that elections must proceed even without completed OBC reservation exercises if the six-month window would otherwise be breached.

A distinctive pressure point that has emerged since the Vikas Kishanrao Gawali decision (2021) involves States that dissolve Panchayats approaching their term ends in order to restart the election process with an OBC commission inquiry — effectively using clause (3)(b)'s six-month dissolution window in preference to clause (3)(a)'s pre-expiry election requirement, in hopes of gaining more time to complete the triple-test process. Courts have scrutinised these dissolutions to determine whether they are constitutional acts of governance or administrative devices to circumvent the election deadline, with varying results across different High Courts.

The interaction between Article 243E's fixed timeline and the broader political economy of local body election delays — including State governments' incentives to keep favoured administrators in place rather than risk elections — remains one of the most active areas of constitutional litigation at the High Court level across India.
