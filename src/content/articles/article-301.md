## Why it matters

Article 301 is the economic constitution within the Constitution. It converts India's federal
structure — with its twenty-eight states and eight union territories, each capable of levying taxes
and erecting regulatory requirements — into a single internal common market. Without it, every state
border would be a potential economic barrier. The framers drew conscious inspiration from the
Commerce Clause of the United States Constitution and the freedom-of-movement guarantees in
Australian federalism, but deliberately placed the freedom in a self-contained Part (XIII) rather
than in the Fundamental Rights chapter, making it enforceable not through Article 32 writs but
through ordinary legislative challenge before the High Courts and the Supreme Court.

The text is deliberately spare: "trade, commerce and intercourse throughout the territory of India
shall be free." Courts have read "intercourse" broadly to cover movement of persons, goods, and
services across state lines. The phrase "subject to the other provisions of this Part" makes clear
that Articles 302, 303, 304, 305, and 307 are the permitted gateways through which legislative
restrictions must pass. Any law that impedes the free flow of trade must find its authority in one
of those gateway articles; it cannot stand on any other head of legislative power. This structural
exclusivity is Part XIII's most distinctive feature — freedom is the rule, restriction is the
exception, and exceptions must meet specific constitutional conditions.

The structural significance of Part XIII extends beyond individual trade transactions. Article 301
embodies the constitutional vision of India as an integrated economic unit — not a collection of
competing provincial economies, but a single territory within which goods, people, and capital move
freely. This vision was particularly important at the time of framing: the newly independent country
inherited a fragmented economy in which successor states to British-administered provinces and
princely states all had distinct regulatory regimes, tariff walls, and procurement preferences.
Article 301 swept those away at the constitutional level. The placement outside the Fundamental
Rights chapter means violations are challenged through ordinary jurisdiction under Article 226 or
Article 136, and the remedy is ordinarily a declaration that the offending law is void.

## Reading the provision

The operative phrase "trade, commerce and intercourse" uses three distinct words to signal a
comprehensive scope. "Trade" covers commercial exchange — the buying and selling of goods and
services. "Commerce" adds the wider infrastructure of commercial activity: transportation, finance,
warehousing, and the service industries that support trade. "Intercourse" is the broadest term,
extending to the movement of persons themselves and to non-commercial interactions that cross state
borders. Together the three terms create a single unified freedom covering virtually every form of
cross-border economic and personal movement within India.

The word "free" in Article 301 has been interpreted to mean unimpeded by legislative or executive
action — not merely free from explicit prohibition, but free from indirect burdens, fiscal
impediments, and regulatory conditions that substantially restrict the flow of trade. A restriction
need not be absolute to fall foul of Article 301; it is enough that it directly and immediately
burdens or impedes trade, even if trade remains technically possible at higher cost or after
compliance with onerous conditions. This "direct and immediate effect" test — first articulated in
Atiabari and refined across fifty years — distinguishes a restriction on trade from a mere
regulation of trade. A regulatory measure that facilitates trade, or charges a genuinely
compensatory levy for services provided, was traditionally treated as outside Article 301's
prohibition. Jindal Stainless (2017) collapsed that distinction and returned the analysis to the
single primary question: direct and immediate restriction, yes or no?

"Subject to the other provisions of this Part" is the critical textual limitation. Article 302
permits Parliament to impose restrictions on trade freedom in the public interest. Article 303
prohibits Parliament and State Legislatures from giving preference to one state over another.
Article 304 is the key State-level provision — a State Legislature may impose non-discriminatory
taxes on goods from other states (clause a) and may impose reasonable restrictions in the public
interest (clause b), but only with the prior sanction of the President. Article 305 saves existing
laws and laws giving effect to directive principles, and Article 307 allows Parliament to appoint an
authority to carry out the purposes of Articles 301–304. Any restriction on trade freedom must pass
through one of these gateways; it cannot be justified by any other constitutional provision, however
broad.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debates on Part XIII reflected acute awareness of the fragmented economy
the framers were inheriting. T.T. Krishnamachari, who piloted the early draft, emphasised that the
new republic must not replicate the internal customs barriers that had made the colonial economy
inefficient. The drafters debated whether to model the provision on the Australian commerce power
(which was then interpreted narrowly) or the American commerce clause (which had by the 1940s been
expanded to cover virtually all economic activity). The compromise was Part XIII's structured
freedom: a sweeping guarantee in Article 301 qualified by carefully delineated gateways that permit
both Parliament and State Legislatures to impose restrictions in defined circumstances. This
architecture — freedom as the rule, restriction as the exception, exceptions must meet constitutional
conditions — reflects a deliberate middle path between extreme centralisation and unbounded State
regulatory power.

B.R. Ambedkar cautioned in the debates that the article must be understood as creating a genuine
and enforceable right rather than a mere aspiration; the Atiabari bench later vindicated this by
holding Part XIII rights to be justiciable guarantees. The framers also debated whether inter-State
trade in agricultural commodities — then heavily regulated by State marketing boards — should be
expressly exempted, but rejected any such carve-out, leaving agricultural marketing restrictions to
be tested against Article 301's general standard.

## Key cases

**Atiabari Tea Co. Ltd. v. State of Assam (1961) SCR (1) 809** — The Supreme Court's seven-judge
Constitution Bench held that the Assam Taxation (on Goods Carried by Roads or Inland Waterways)
Act, levying a tax on goods in transit, directly and immediately impeded the free flow of trade and
was void under Article 301. The Court firmly rejected the contention that Part XIII freedoms are
declaratory or non-justiciable and established the foundational "direct and immediate effect" test
that governed Part XIII litigation for decades. Any tax that directly and immediately restricts the
freedom of trade falls within the prohibition of Article 301 unless validated under Article 304.

**Automobile Transport Ltd. v. State of Rajasthan (1963) SCR (1) 491** — A seven-judge bench refined
Atiabari by drawing the critical distinction between a "regulatory" measure and a "compensatory tax."
A motor vehicle tax levied to compensate for road wear caused by commercial transport was held to
be compensatory and outside Article 301's prohibition. This compensatory-tax doctrine became the
dominant analytical tool in Part XIII cases for half a century — allowing states to justify a wide
range of levies as the price of using state-provided infrastructure — until it was comprehensively
overruled in Jindal Stainless (2017).

**State of Bombay v. R.M.D. Chamarbaugwala (1957) SCR 874** — The Court held that Article 301 covers
only activities that are themselves legitimate trade; activities inherently harmful or immoral (such
as gambling) do not attract Part XIII protection. The article protects freedom of commerce, not the
freedom to conduct activities that fall outside the constitutional conception of legitimate trade.
This ruling established an important threshold: not every economic activity labelled as "trade"
automatically attracts Article 301 protection.

**G.K. Krishnan v. State of Tamil Nadu (1975) SCR (2) 715** — The Court applied the
regulatory/compensatory distinction to a tax on motor vehicles and confirmed that the distinction
turns on the functional relationship between the tax and specific services or facilities provided to
the taxpaying traders. A tax with no discernible nexus with any service rendered is not compensatory
and must be saved under Article 304(b) or it will fall as a violation of Article 301.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd. v. State of Haryana (2017) 12 SCC 1** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench
comprehensively overhauled the "compensatory tax" doctrine from Automobile Transport, holding that
it had become unworkable and unpredictable over five decades of application. Courts were ill-equipped
to determine whether a tax was genuinely compensatory, and the doctrine had produced inconsistent
results. The new framework returns to the primary Atiabari question: does the tax or levy directly
and immediately impede the free flow of trade? If yes, it must satisfy Article 304(b)'s requirements.
Jindal Stainless revitalised Article 301 as a live check on entry taxes, octroi, and levies on
petroleum and non-GST commodities.

**Saghir Ahmad v. State of U.P. (1955) SCR 707** — An early decision of the Constitution Bench
invalidating a State law that created a state monopoly in road transport, holding that an exclusive
State monopoly that directly forecloses private trade violates Article 301 and must be validated
under an express constitutional provision permitting such monopoly or under Article 304(b) with
presidential sanction.

## Current significance

The introduction of the Goods and Services Tax in 2017 has transformed the practical landscape of
Part XIII. The GST Council — a constitutional body created by the 101st Amendment and Article 279A —
has largely displaced inter-state trade barriers for GST-covered goods and services by replacing the
web of central excise, VAT, entry tax, and octroi levies with a unified regime. For most goods
crossing state borders, the discriminatory fiscal barriers that Article 301 was primarily designed to
prevent no longer arise, because the GST rate is uniform and the mechanism for inter-state supply
(IGST) is centrally administered. The introduction of GST represents the most significant structural
implementation of Article 301's vision since the Constitution came into force.

However, Part XIII has not been superseded. Article 301 continues to govern petroleum products
(crude oil, petrol, diesel, natural gas, aviation turbine fuel), alcohol for human consumption, real
estate transactions in certain configurations, and any non-GST levy that a state might impose. These
excluded categories are among India's highest-value commodities and their continued exclusion from
GST means that state-level taxation remains a live Article 301 issue. Entry taxes, toll levies, and
other fiscal impositions on petroleum movement across state borders continue to be challenged under
Articles 301 and 304, now tested by the post-Jindal Stainless direct-restriction inquiry rather than
the abandoned compensatory-tax doctrine.

Beyond taxation, Article 301 retains relevance in the regulatory sphere. State agricultural
marketing laws (APMC Acts) that restrict the movement of agricultural produce across state lines,
state-level licensing requirements that impose differential conditions on traders from other states,
and procurement mandates that favour local suppliers all raise Article 301 questions that the GST
transformation has not resolved. The three farm laws passed by Parliament in 2020 — which were
withdrawn in 2021 — rested in part on the Article 301 constitutional common-market principle,
illustrating how the provision continues to shape national economic policy debates. As India
continues to integrate its internal market through the National Infrastructure Pipeline, the PM Gati
Shakti plan, and the national logistics policy, the constitutional foundation of that integration
remains Article 301.
