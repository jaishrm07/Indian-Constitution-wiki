## Why it matters

Article 83 is the constitutional foundation of parliamentary tenure. It determines when the Lok Sabha must face the electorate and why the Rajya Sabha never does so as a whole. These two design choices — the five-year maximum for the popular house and permanent continuity for the federal house — are among the most consequential structural decisions in Part V. They shape the relationship between the government and the governed, the nature of electoral accountability, and the institutional character of the upper house.

The five-year limit in Article 83(2) is not merely a scheduling convenience. It encodes the principle that a democratically elected lower house must periodically renew its mandate from the people. Once the five-year clock runs, the dissolution is automatic: the Constitution says the expiry of the period "shall operate as a dissolution." No executive action is required. This self-executing quality prevents any government from simply sitting on its hands to avoid dissolution — the constitutional term expires whether or not Parliament has been formally dissolved by the President under Article 85(2).

The Emergency proviso to Article 83(2) is the article's most politically charged feature. The framers recognised that elections cannot always be held during armed conflict or grave internal disturbance, so they built in a mechanism to extend the Lok Sabha's life by one year at a time while an Emergency is in force. But they also capped that extension — it cannot run more than six months beyond the cessation of the Emergency. This ceiling was designed to ensure that emergency extensions remained genuinely temporary rather than becoming a mechanism for prolonging political power indefinitely. The 1975 Emergency and the 42nd Amendment tested that design in ways that exposed a dangerous gap between the letter and spirit of the provision.

The permanent character of the Rajya Sabha under Article 83(1) reflects the framers' conception of the upper house as a continuous forum for second-opinion deliberation and federal representation. Because individual members retire in staggered batches rather than the whole house dissolving at once, there is always an experienced and sitting Rajya Sabha, even when the Lok Sabha has been dissolved and the country is in a period of electoral transition. This continuity is not merely administrative — it means that the Rajya Sabha can meet and conduct business even when no Lok Sabha exists, a fact that has constitutional relevance for the passage of Ordinances, resolutions on the National Emergency, and motions of no-confidence or privilege.

## Reading the provision

Article 83(1) establishes the permanence of the Rajya Sabha by prohibiting its dissolution as a whole. Instead of periodic general elections, the Rajya Sabha renews itself through the retirement of roughly one-third of its members every two years. Each Rajya Sabha member sits for a six-year term, and their retirements are staggered so that the chamber is never entirely fresh — it always contains a mix of long-serving and recently elected members. The phrase "as nearly as possible one-third" acknowledges the practical difficulty of achieving exact thirds when the total membership is not perfectly divisible, and delegates to Parliament the power to make laws governing the precise mechanics of biennial retirement.

Article 83(2) is the clock that governs the Lok Sabha. The five-year period runs from "the date appointed for its first meeting" — not from the date of the election results, not from the date of swearing-in, but from the constitutionally appointed date of the first sitting. This starting point matters for calculating the outer limit of the Lok Sabha's life, particularly when premature dissolution means a new house is formed and a fresh five-year clock begins. The phrase "unless sooner dissolved" preserves the President's power under Article 85(2) to dissolve the Lok Sabha on the advice of the Prime Minister before the five-year term expires, which is how almost every Lok Sabha in modern Indian political history has actually ended.

The proviso to Article 83(2) introduces the Emergency extension. Three conditions must be jointly satisfied: a Proclamation of Emergency must be "in operation" at the time Parliament acts, the extension must be effected "by law" (meaning by ordinary legislative enactment, not by executive order), and the extension cannot exceed one year at a time nor run beyond six months after the Emergency ceases. The "by law" requirement is significant — it means the Lok Sabha itself must vote for its own extension, creating a situation where the body whose life is being prolonged is also the body authorising that prolongation. The framers accepted this structural oddity as an unavoidable consequence of the parliamentary model; the alternative safeguard was the one-year limit and the six-month sunset.

The original Constitution set the Lok Sabha's term at five years. The 42nd Amendment (1976) extended it to six years during the Emergency. The 44th Amendment (1978), passed after the Emergency ended, restored it to five years. This episode demonstrated that the Emergency proviso itself could be manipulated: rather than extending the existing Lok Sabha term one year at a time under the proviso, the Indira Gandhi government chose to amend Article 83(2) itself to alter the baseline term to six years. The 44th Amendment's restoration of five years was a deliberate act of constitutional repair.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**The 1975 Emergency and the 42nd Amendment** — The most consequential test of Article 83 was not a court case but a constitutional amendment. After declaring a National Emergency in June 1975, the Indira Gandhi government used its parliamentary majority to pass the 42nd Amendment in 1976, extending the Lok Sabha's term from five to six years. This manoeuvre bypassed the Emergency proviso's own structure — rather than invoking the one-year extension mechanism while an Emergency was in force, the government permanently altered the baseline term. It also applied the six-year term to State Assemblies under Article 172. The 44th Amendment, passed by the Janata government in 1978, reversed both changes and restored five-year terms. The episode is a canonical illustration of how Emergency provisions can be exploited to entrench political power, and it is a primary reason why subsequent constitutional commentators treat the Emergency proviso as a strict and narrow exception.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1974** — Before the formal Emergency declaration, the Supreme Court was asked advisory questions touching on electoral processes. The Court's reasoning confirmed that the five-year period in Article 83(2) is the constitutional maximum for the Lok Sabha's mandate and that any extension must remain within the strictly bounded proviso. The Court treated the five-year limit not as a default of administrative convenience but as an expression of the democratic principle that the popular house must periodically seek a fresh mandate.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — Although the nine-judge bench was principally concerned with Article 356, it addressed the structural relationship between the dissolution of legislatures and democratic accountability. The Court's reasoning reinforced that dissolution — whether of the Lok Sabha under Article 85(2) or a State Assembly — is a serious constitutional act with electoral consequences, and that the constitutional time limits on legislative bodies (Article 83 for Parliament, Article 172 for State Assemblies) are among the few areas where the Constitution makes democratic renewal mandatory rather than optional.

**A.C. Jose v. Sivan Pillai (1984)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the five-year period in Article 83(2) runs from the date of the first meeting of the House, and that a newly elected Lok Sabha following a dissolution begins an entirely fresh constitutional life. This clarification was important for election timing disputes, as it established that premature dissolutions followed by fresh elections reset the constitutional clock entirely rather than carrying over the remaining portion of the previous term.

## Current significance

The One Nation One Election proposal — which would synchronise Lok Sabha and State Assembly elections into a single national cycle — directly implicates Article 83. Implementing it would require either curtailing the remaining term of some Houses or extending the term of others to align their cycles. Both operations would require constitutional amendments, and any extension of a Lok Sabha term beyond five years would require navigating Article 83(2)'s outer limit. Critics argue that the proposal effectively attempts through amendment what the Emergency proviso was designed to prevent through crisis mechanisms — the prolongation of legislative mandates beyond five years for reasons of administrative convenience rather than national emergency.

The permanent character of the Rajya Sabha under Article 83(1) is increasingly relevant to questions of legislative deadlock. When a ruling coalition controls the Lok Sabha but not the Rajya Sabha — as was the case for extended periods in the 1990s, 2000s, and early 2010s — the government cannot simply dissolve the upper house to resolve the gridlock. The staggered retirement cycle means any majority shift in the Rajya Sabha is gradual rather than sudden, giving the federal house a resistance to sudden swings in government composition that the Lok Sabha, which dissolves all at once, does not have. This structural asymmetry between the two houses is embedded in Article 83.

The Emergency extension proviso remains relevant in broader comparative context as Indian constitutional debates intersect with global discussions about election postponement during public-health emergencies, security crises, or natural disasters. The Indian framers' decision to permit extensions only by law — requiring the legislature itself to extend its own life — rather than by executive decree, and to cap those extensions firmly, has influenced constitutional drafting in other democracies. The proviso is now studied as an example of a constitutional design that allows emergency flexibility without enabling permanent postponement of democratic accountability.
