  Article 234 governs appointments to the State judicial service below the level of district judge — civil judges, judicial magistrates brought within the framework by Article 237 notifications, and equivalent posts. Such appointments are made by the Governor in accordance with rules framed after consultation with both the State Public Service Commission and the High Court. The PSC ensures merit-based selection through competitive examination; the High Court ensures that the examination tests legal knowledge appropriate to the judicial function. This dual consultation requirement distinguishes judicial service recruitment from general civil service recruitment and insulates entry-level judicial independence from purely political appointment. The Supreme Court has, since the All India Judges Association cases of the 1990s, exercised continuing oversight over State compliance with this framework and has directed States to fill sanctioned vacancies within specified timelines — since persistent large-scale vacancies in the subordinate courts are themselves a constitutional failure to maintain an effective judicial system.
officialText: 234. Recruitment of persons other than district judges to the judicial service.—Appointments of persons other than district judges to the judicial service of a State shall be made by the Governor of the State in accordance with rules made by him in that behalf after consultation with the State Public Service Commission and with the High Court exercising jurisdiction in relation to such State.

## Why it matters

Article 234 is the constitutional anchor for the recruitment of India's subordinate civil judiciary — the civil judges and equivalent officers who handle the overwhelming majority of civil disputes in the country at first instance. The dual consultation requirement is not ceremonial. The PSC brings expertise in designing fair selection processes at scale; the High Court brings substantive expertise in evaluating legal competence and fitness for judicial office. Together they create a structural guarantee that entry into the judicial service is based on merit and judicial competence rather than political patronage.

The contrast with Article 233 — which governs district judges and requires only the Governor's consultation with the High Court, not the PSC — reflects a deliberate design choice. District judge appointments are typically drawn from serving judicial officers (promotions) or from the practising bar (direct appointments). Both pools are sufficiently small and individually assessable that the PSC's mass-examination machinery is unnecessary. The much larger subordinate cadre, however, requires structured competitive selection at scale — making the PSC's involvement constitutionally appropriate for that tier.

## Reading the provision

The consultation under Article 234 must be genuine — not a formality. The Governor (acting on the advice of the State government) must share relevant materials with both the PSC and the High Court, allow them to form views, and take those views into account in finalising recruitment rules and individual appointments. Departures from the positions of either consultor must be explicable; arbitrary deviations render the rules constitutionally vulnerable.

The Governor's rule-making power operationalises the consultation inputs into standing recruitment rules covering eligibility, examination structure, qualifying marks, reservation rosters, and selection procedures. Once rules are made, individual appointments under those rules must conform to the framework — an appointment that deviates from the prescribed rules is constitutionally irregular and may be challenged.

Article 234 sits within the broader framework of Chapter VI (Articles 233-237), which erects a constitutional ring-fence around the subordinate judiciary at every level — appointments, recruitment, ongoing administrative control, definitional scope, and extension to the magistracy. Article 234 governs the entry gate; Article 235 governs careers thereafter.

Reservation requirements under Articles 15(4), 16(4), and Article 335 apply to judicial service recruitment under Article 234. The High Court's consultative role in framing recruitment rules includes a role in determining how reservation criteria interact with the judicial competence requirements specific to the service, and several High Courts have published model recruitment rules and guidelines on these issues.

## Key cases

**Chandra Mohan v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1966)** — The Supreme Court held that deviations from the consultation requirements under Articles 233 and 234 are not mere procedural irregularities but constitutional infirmities that render the appointment void. The constitutional framework must be strictly observed.

**Triloki Nath Khosa v. State of Jammu and Kashmir (1974)** — The Supreme Court held that the Governor's rule-making power under Article 234 must genuinely reflect the consultative inputs received from the PSC and the High Court. Rules that deviate substantially from those positions without justification are constitutionally vulnerable.

**All India Judges' Association v. Union of India (1993)** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling requiring enhanced pay scales and improved service conditions for the subordinate judiciary was grounded in the constitutional status of judicial service under Articles 233 and 234. The Court held that adequate remuneration is a constitutional requirement for maintaining the independence and quality of the subordinate judiciary, and directed implementation of improved pay structures.

**State of Haryana v. Inder Parkash Anand (1976)** — The Punjab and Haryana High Court examined whether a particular category of judicial officer below district judge fell within the scope of Article 234 and required the tripartite consultation framework for appointment.

## Current significance

Article 234 is directly implicated in India's persistent subordinate court vacancy crisis. Sanctioned judicial strength across all States has been set at over 24,000 positions, but actual working strength has persistently run 20 to 25 percent below sanctioned strength. The constitutional framework of Article 234 governs the very process by which those vacancies are to be filled — and failures to initiate timely recruitment cycles, to constitute PSC examination boards promptly, or to process recommendations without unnecessary delay are all failures within this constitutional framework.

The Supreme Court in the All India Judges Association monitoring proceedings has periodically directed States to explain delays in recruitment and to complete pending selection processes within specified timelines. The constitutional status of the recruitment obligation under Article 234 makes it justiciable — a State government's failure to maintain adequate judicial strength by following the prescribed recruitment framework can be challenged before the High Court under Article 226 and before the Supreme Court in the monitoring proceedings.

The emergence of State Judicial Service examinations as large-scale competitive processes — drawing tens of thousands of candidates for a few hundred posts in each cycle — has generated its own body of case law on reservation rosters, carry-forward rules, qualifying marks structures, and the division of posts between the direct-recruitment and promotion channels. That case law rests on the constitutional foundation of Article 234, which establishes the judicial service as a distinctive cadre with special recruitment requirements that cannot be reduced to ordinary departmental appointment.

Recurring recommendations from the All India Judges Association on pay parity with civil services and on infrastructure for subordinate courts rest partly on the constitutional framework of Article 234, which establishes the judicial service as requiring a quality of candidates that competitive pay and adequate working conditions are necessary to attract and retain.
