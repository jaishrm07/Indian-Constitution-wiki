  Article 228A no longer exists — it was inserted by the Emergency-era Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976, and repealed by the Constitution (Forty-third Amendment) Act, 1977, effective 13 April 1978. During its brief life it required any High Court bench that encountered a question of constitutional validity of a State law to refer that question to a bench of at least five judges rather than allowing the ordinary division bench to decide it — a procedural requirement designed to slow down judicial review by creating a mandatory large-bench filter for all constitutional validity challenges to State legislation. ## Why it matters

Article 228A was one of several provisions in the Forty-second Amendment aimed at restructuring the relationship between the courts and the elected branches in ways that reduced judicial capacity to strike down legislation. Unlike Article 226A — which barred High Courts from considering the constitutional validity of Central laws at all — Article 228A took a more procedural approach: it did not remove the High Court's power to invalidate State legislation, but made it far more cumbersome to exercise.

By requiring a minimum bench of five judges for any constitutional validity question about a State law, the provision created significant practical obstacles. Constituting a five-judge bench requires reorganising an entire High Court's sitting schedule — only the largest High Courts routinely have five judges available for a special bench on short notice. Cases that could have been decided by an ordinary two- or three-judge division bench in weeks would instead wait months for a five-judge bench to be constituted, briefed, and heard. The delay itself functioned as a form of protection for State legislation from immediate constitutional challenge.

The Emergency-era pairing of Articles 226A and 228A thus attempted to shield both Central and State laws from effective High Court constitutional review — Central laws by removing jurisdiction entirely, State laws by making the jurisdictional exercise procedurally unworkable. That both provisions were removed within two years illustrates the political fragility of constitutional amendments that openly obstruct judicial independence.

## Reading the provision

The article is retained in the Constitution only as a square-bracket heading followed by the omission note — the same tombstone-entry technique used for Article 226A and other omitted provisions. The effective life of Article 228A was short: the Forty-second Amendment was notified in January 1977, the Emergency ended in March 1977, the general election was held in March 1977, and the Forty-third Amendment came into force in April 1978. Article 228A's operation therefore coincided almost exactly with the final period of the Emergency and the transitional months immediately after.

No significant body of doctrine developed under Article 228A given its brief life. Courts that encountered the provision during its operation had little opportunity to develop a settled understanding of its scope — for instance, whether the five-judge requirement applied only to constitutional invalidity as a primary ground, or also to cases where validity was raised incidentally.

## Key cases

No judgments applying Article 228A are reported, given its brief effective life. The relevant case law concerns the Forty-second and Forty-third Amendments as constitutional events.

**Minerva Mills Ltd v. Union of India (1980)** — The Supreme Court's invalidation of key provisions of the Forty-second Amendment — on the ground that they damaged the basic structure of the Constitution by undermining judicial review — is the constitutional backdrop against which both Article 228A and its repeal are understood. The Court's reasoning confirms that mandatory procedural obstacles to constitutional review of legislation are as constitutionally suspect as direct jurisdiction-removal provisions.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1977)** — This case, concerning the relationship between the Union and State governments, was decided in the period when the Emergency-era amendments were in force, and the Court's approach to constitutional review of Central and State action in that context illustrates the practical impact of provisions like Article 228A on the tempo of constitutional adjudication.

## Current significance

The story of Article 228A is cited alongside Article 226A when analysing the Emergency's judicial legacy. Both provisions illustrate that constitutional text can be weaponised against judicial independence and then reversed once political conditions change. The procedural device of mandatory large-bench references for constitutional validity questions — which Article 228A briefly imposed on High Courts — periodically resurfaces in policy discussions about reducing inconsistency in constitutional rulings across High Courts, but in that contemporary context it is motivated by concern about conflicting judgments rather than executive control. The constitutional character of such a proposal would be entirely different from the Emergency-era version.

Article 228A is also a reminder that constitutional amendments can be reversed. The amendment power under Article 368 — while broad — does not render constitutional changes permanent if they are incompatible with constitutional values strongly enough held by the electorate and the successor government. The Emergency's constitutional overreach was substantially reversed within the life of the next Parliament, making the 1976-1978 episode one of the clearest examples in India's constitutional history of a constitutional amendment that was politically and constitutionally unsustainable.
