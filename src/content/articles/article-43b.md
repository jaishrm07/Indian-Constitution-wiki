## Why it matters

Article 43B was inserted by the 97th Constitutional Amendment in 2011 and represents the Constitution's most recent addition to Part IV. It directs the State to promote the voluntary formation, autonomous functioning, democratic control, and professional management of co-operative societies. Each of those four attributes — voluntary, autonomous, democratic, professionally managed — reflects a considered response to the chronic pathologies that had afflicted India's co-operative sector for decades: State capture, forced membership or compulsory affiliation, supersession of elected boards by State-appointed administrators, and mismanagement by politically appointed officials.

Co-operatives occupy a constitutionally and economically distinctive space. They are neither purely private enterprises nor State instrumentalities: they are collective self-help institutions, owned and governed by their members, that allow individuals with limited individual resources to pool capital, access credit, market produce, and acquire inputs at scale. The dairy co-operatives of Gujarat, the sugar co-operatives of Maharashtra, the fishing co-operatives of Kerala, and the urban housing co-operatives of several states are all examples of how co-operative organisation can transform economic outcomes for lower-middle-income groups. Article 43B gives this mode of economic organisation explicit constitutional standing and protection.

The directive is also a response to the history of State interference in co-operative societies. State governments have used their power to legislate under Entry 32 of the State List to control co-operative societies in ways that compromise their autonomy — appointing administrator after administrator when elected boards were superseded, using co-operative banks as instruments of political patronage, and deferring mandatory elections for years. By specifying that the State's obligation is to promote autonomous functioning and democratic control, Article 43B creates a constitutional norm against State capture of co-operative institutions.

## Reading the provision

Article 43B has been used primarily in litigation about whether State legislation governing co-operative societies is consistent with the constitutional mandate of autonomy and democratic control. Where State co-operative societies acts contain provisions allowing prolonged supersession of elected boards without fresh elections, or giving the Registrar of Co-operative Societies sweeping powers to intervene in management decisions, courts have measured those provisions against the Article 43B standard.

The 97th Amendment itself produced significant constitutional litigation because of questions about the procedure through which it was enacted. In Rajendra N. Shah v. Union of India (2021), a Constitution Bench held that the provisions of the 97th Amendment relating to State co-operative societies were unconstitutional for not having been ratified by the required number of State legislatures under the proviso to Article 368(2) — since co-operative societies fall within the State List, amendments affecting them require State legislative consent. However, the provisions relating to multi-state co-operative societies, which fall under the Union List, were upheld. This ruling reinforced the federal dimension of Article 43B: the co-operative sector is a shared constitutional concern, with different rules applying to State-level and multi-state entities.

Courts have also drawn on Article 43B when interpreting the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002, and its subsequent amendments, particularly in relation to election procedures, member rights, and the scope of Central government regulatory authority over multi-state entities such as NAFED and IFFCO.

## Key cases

**Rajendra N. Shah v. Union of India (2021)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench examined the constitutional validity of the 97th Amendment, which had inserted Article 43B and Part IXB (containing detailed provisions about co-operative societies) into the Constitution. The Court held that the provisions of Part IXB relating to State co-operatives were void for non-compliance with the State ratification requirement under Article 368(2), since they encroached on the State List. However, Article 43B itself, as a directive principle, was upheld. The judgment is the principal authority on the constitutional architecture of the 97th Amendment and the federal limits on Central regulation of the co-operative sector.

**Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002 litigation** — Several High Courts have applied Article 43B when reviewing provisions of the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act that regulate elections, membership, and management of multi-state entities. Courts have consistently read the Act in light of the constitutional preference for democratic control and autonomous functioning, striking down or reading down administrative directions that substituted Central government judgment for the democratic decisions of member-societies.

**State amendments post-97th Amendment** — Following the amendment, multiple State governments revised their co-operative societies acts to align with the constitutional mandate of periodic elections, limited supersession periods, and professional management standards. Litigation arising from these amendments — including challenges to State governments' continued use of extended administrative supersession — has invoked Article 43B as the constitutional standard against which the adequacy of State legislative reform is measured.

## Current significance

The Ministry of Co-operation, established in 2021 as a separate central ministry, and the new Multi-State Co-operative Societies (Amendment) Act, 2023, represent the most sustained recent legislative and administrative engagement with Article 43B's mandate. The amendments include provisions for strengthening electoral processes, improving financial accountability, and enabling multi-state co-operatives to attract investment. Whether these reforms achieve the constitutional objectives of autonomy and democratic control — rather than simply expanding Central government regulatory reach over the sector — remains a contested question in co-operative law circles.

Article 43B is also relevant to debates about the use of Primary Agricultural Credit Societies (PACS) and co-operative banks as channels for rural credit and financial inclusion. The constitutional directive that co-operatives must be genuinely autonomous and democratically governed constrains the extent to which these institutions can be used as vehicles for government-directed lending without becoming extensions of the State apparatus rather than member-governed institutions. This tension between development goals and the autonomy mandate of Article 43B continues to define the regulatory boundary between government and the co-operative sector.
