## Why it matters

Article 23 is one of the few fundamental rights provisions with explicit horizontal application — it binds private individuals, not merely the state. This was a deliberate and radical choice by the Constituent Assembly, reflecting the understanding that the worst exploitation in Indian society came not from the colonial state but from entrenched private hierarchies — the zamindar over the tenant, the upper-caste village authority over the Dalit agricultural labourer, the moneylender whose debt trap converted a loan into a lifetime of bonded service. By making traffic in human beings, begar, and forced labour constitutional offences punishable by law, the framers placed the coercive power of criminal sanction at the service of the most vulnerable workers in an overwhelmingly agricultural and informal economy.

The provision encompasses three distinct but overlapping categories of exploitation. Traffic in human beings is the broadest — it covers slavery, the sale of human beings, and commercial sexual exploitation, including what later legislation addressed as immoral trafficking. Begar is historically specific — the system under which lower-caste workers and tenants were compelled to provide free labour for landlords, village headmen, or state functionaries, often under threat of eviction or physical violence, without any right to refuse or to seek compensation. "Other similar forms of forced labour" is the expanding clause, and it is here that the Supreme Court has done its most significant interpretive work, holding that the category extends to any coerced service where the worker's consent is nominal rather than real — including economic coercion.

The economic-compulsion holding is Article 23's most transformative doctrinal contribution. Before PUDR v Union of India (1982), it might have been argued that a person who receives wages — even sub-minimum wages — is not being "forced" in the relevant sense. The Supreme Court rejected this formalism decisively, recognising that a labourer in poverty who faces starvation unless she accepts exploitative terms has no real alternative and no genuine consent. This reading links Article 23 to the directive principles in Article 39 and to the minimum-wages legislation as constitutional minimum requirements, not mere statutory preferences. It has given the provision reach into the vast informal economy where physical coercion is rare but economic compulsion is endemic.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) contains the three prohibitions and the offence-creation mechanism. The structure is important: the article does not merely direct the state to prevent these practices; it declares them offences. The state has a corresponding obligation to enact and enforce criminal law giving content to this constitutional prohibition. This is why the Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976, the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act 1956, and the Minimum Wages Act 1948 all carry constitutional backing under Article 23 — failure to enforce them is itself a constitutional dereliction.

"Traffic in human beings" — The Supreme Court has interpreted this as encompassing the full range of commercial and coercive trade in persons: slavery, sale of persons for work, trade in women and children for sexual exploitation, and recruitment of persons into exploitative conditions through deception or debt. The phrase is distinct from but overlaps with the more modern concept of "trafficking in persons" as developed in subsequent legislation and international instruments (the SAARC Convention on Preventing and Combating Trafficking in Women and Children 2002; the Trafficking in Persons (Prevention, Care and Rehabilitation) Bill debates). The constitutional prohibition predates and grounds all subsequent statutory frameworks.

"Begar" — The term derives from the Persian/Urdu word for forced labour without remuneration. Historically it described the institution under which zamindars, jagirdars, and village authorities could demand free compulsory labour from tenants and lower-caste villagers. The Constituent Assembly debates show acute awareness of this system as one of the principal instruments of caste-correlated economic exploitation that constitutional democracy was required to eliminate. Its explicit mention — rather than subsumption under "forced labour" — reflects its historical salience and signals that no residual legislative or customary permission for begar can survive constitutional scrutiny.

"Other similar forms of forced labour" — This phrase has been the vehicle for the Court's expansion of Article 23's scope. The test is whether the worker exercises genuine free choice. Factors the Court considers include: whether the worker has received an advance that creates debt bondage; whether wages are so far below the statutory minimum that working them off is the worker's only alternative to destitution; whether physical confinement prevents departure; and whether social or caste pressure operates to remove any practical alternative to continued service. The Court has repeatedly emphasised that the absence of physical force does not mean the absence of compulsion — the coercion relevant to Article 23 is structural and economic as much as it is physical.

Clause (2) permits the state to impose compulsory service for public purposes — military service, jury duty (theoretically), mandatory civic contributions — but attaches a non-discrimination requirement. The state cannot select those required to serve on grounds of religion, race, caste, or class. This clause has been used to justify existing requirements of compulsory service in civil defence and disaster management contexts.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The debate on what became Article 23 in the Constituent Assembly focused principally on begar. Speakers from regions where zamindari was most entrenched — Bengal, Bihar, and parts of what is now Uttar Pradesh — described in detail the mechanisms of forced unpaid labour extraction and their connections to caste hierarchy. Dr Ambedkar, as the principal drafter, insisted on the horizontal application of the prohibition — it had to bind private persons, not merely the state, because the state was rarely the direct extractor of begar; the zamindar and the village panchayat were. The debate also addressed the compulsory-service exception, with members agreeing that military service and analogous public obligations were legitimate provided they were non-discriminatory in selection.

## Key cases

**People's Union for Democratic Rights v Union of India AIR 1983 SC 1473 (Asiad Workers case, 1982)** — The foundational Article 23 decision. The Supreme Court held that "forced labour" in Article 23 includes any situation where a worker is compelled to provide service for remuneration below the statutory minimum wage, because the compulsion of dire economic necessity is as much compulsion as physical force or legal obligation. The Court directed enforcement of the Equal Remuneration Act, the Contract Labour Act, the Inter-State Migrant Workmen Act, and the Minimum Wages Act at Asiad construction sites. The ruling confirmed that Article 23 imposes positive obligations on the state: where a situation of forced labour is brought to official attention, inaction is itself a constitutional violation.

**Bandhua Mukti Morcha v Union of India AIR 1984 SC 802** — The Court treated bonded labour as forced labour within Article 23 and connected it to the right to live with human dignity under Article 21. It held that the state has an affirmative obligation to identify, release, and rehabilitate bonded labourers — the obligation is proactive, not dependent on individual victims filing complaints. The judgment directed the establishment of vigilance committees at district level and comprehensive rehabilitation programmes. It is the foundational precedent for systemic court-supervised enforcement of bonded labour abolition.

**Sanjit Roy v State of Rajasthan AIR 1983 SC 328** — The Supreme Court struck down a provision of the Rajasthan Famine Relief Works Employees Act that exempted famine-relief employers from the obligation to pay minimum wages. Drought-affected labourers participating in famine-relief works had no realistic economic alternative — compelling them to work for below-minimum wages in those circumstances was forced labour under Article 23. The decision directly applied the PUDR economic-compulsion reading and established that legislative exemptions from minimum-wage requirements cannot override the constitutional prohibition.

**Deena v Union of India AIR 1983 SC 1155** — Prison labour was held to fall within Article 23's prohibition of forced labour where prisoners were compelled to work for nominal or zero wages. The Court held that while prison labour is not categorically unconstitutional, it must be remunerated adequately. Compelling prisoners to work without wages transforms incarceration into a regime of forced labour that is not authorised by Article 23's exception for compulsory service for public purposes. The ruling brought the conditions of prison workshops within constitutional scrutiny.

**MC Mehta v State of Tamil Nadu (1996) 6 SCC 756** — Although primarily an Article 24 case on child labour in the Sivakasi match and firecracker industry, the Court's directions addressed Article 23 concerns as well, finding that children working in hazardous industries without adequate wages and under conditions of family debt bondage were subjected to a combination of forced labour and child exploitation. The comprehensive rehabilitation directions — including a Child Labour Rehabilitation-cum-Welfare Fund financed by employer contributions — became a model for enforcement of both articles.

**Neeraja Chaudhary v State of MP (1984) 3 SCC 243** — The Court directed the release of bonded labourers in stone quarries in Madhya Pradesh and held that a person identified as a bonded labourer must be presumed to be in a state of forced labour until the contrary is established. The burden of demonstrating freedom from bondage lies on the employer, not the labourer. This evidential rule — reversing the normal burden of proof — reflects the structural power imbalance that Article 23 addresses.

**State of Gujarat v Hon'ble High Court of Gujarat (1998) 7 SCC 392** — The Supreme Court revisited the conditions of prison labour and held that prisoners performing skilled trades must be paid wages comparable to minimum wages applicable in the relevant sector — confining wage entitlement to token amounts under prison manuals amounted to forced labour under Article 23.

## Legislative implementation

The Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976 provides the primary statutory framework implementing Article 23 with respect to debt bondage and bonded labour. It renders all bonded debts void, requires district magistrates to identify and release bonded labourers, and mandates rehabilitation through the Central Sector Scheme on Rehabilitation of Bonded Labour. The Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act 1956, substantially amended in 1986, addresses trafficking for sexual exploitation. The Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act 1989 overlaps with Article 23 to the extent that caste-correlated forced labour constitutes an atrocity within the Act's scope. The Minimum Wages Act 1948 is constitutionally underpinned by Article 23 — sub-minimum-wage payment has direct constitutional significance, not merely statutory consequences. Parliament has debated consolidated anti-trafficking legislation in recent sessions, reflecting the ongoing gap between the constitutional mandate and comprehensive statutory implementation.

## Current significance

Article 23 remains the primary constitutional instrument for attacking bonded labour, human trafficking, and coercive working conditions across India's vast informal economy — agriculture, construction, brick kilns, domestic service, and stone quarrying. The economic-compulsion doctrine gives the provision reach wherever structural poverty strips workers of genuine choice, making it relevant to debates about contract farming, migrant labour during distress periods, and the recruitment of tribal workers into urban informal employment through exploitative advances. Courts continue to apply the Asiad Workers reasoning to unorganised sector litigation, and the interplay between Article 23 and Article 21's right to livelihood generates ongoing directions about the adequacy of state enforcement and rehabilitation programmes.

The emergence of international frameworks on human trafficking — the UN Palermo Protocol 2000 and its domestic legislative descendants — has given Article 23's prohibition of traffic in human beings renewed significance. Courts now read the constitutional prohibition in light of these instruments, extending its scope to newer forms of trafficking including cyber-enabled recruitment and exploitation. The Prevention of Trafficking in Persons Bills debated in recent Parliamentary sessions draw their constitutional mandate directly from Article 23, and the Supreme Court's willingness to supervise implementation through continuing mandamus ensures that the gap between constitutional text and ground reality remains a live judicial concern.

Caste-correlated forced labour — the persistence of begar-like conditions for Dalit agricultural workers in parts of rural India — continues to attract both PIL litigation and special rapporteur attention. Article 23 read with Articles 17 (abolition of untouchability) and 46 (protection of scheduled castes and scheduled tribes) provides the constitutional framework for holding state authorities accountable when they fail to enforce bonded labour abolition in areas where such conditions are known to persist.
