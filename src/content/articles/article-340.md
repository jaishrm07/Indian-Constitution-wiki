  340. Appointment of a Commission to investigate the conditions of backward classes.—(1) The President may by order appoint a Commission consisting of such persons as he thinks fit to investigate the conditions of socially and educationally backward classes within the territory of India and the difficulties under which they labour and to make recommendations as to the steps that should be taken by the Union or any State to remove such difficulties and to improve their condition and as to the grants that should be made for the purpose by the Union or any State and the conditions subject to which such grants should be made, and the order appointing such Commission shall define the procedure to be followed by the Commission.

  (2) A Commission so appointed shall investigate the matters referred to them and present to the President a report setting out the facts as found by them and making such recommendations as they think proper.

  (3) The President shall cause a copy of the report so presented together with a memorandum explaining the action taken thereon to be laid before each House of Parliament.

## Why it matters

Article 340 is the constitutional root of India's OBC reservation framework — the provision from which the Mandal Commission and all that followed ultimately derives. The article embodies the Constituent Assembly's recognition that, beyond the historically defined communities of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, there existed a vast and ill-defined population of socially and educationally backward classes whose conditions of deprivation were not captured by the SC and ST frameworks but were equally real. Because OBCs were not identified by Presidential notification in the way SCs and STs were, Article 340 created a commission-based mechanism for investigation and recommendation, leaving to Parliament the question of what measures to implement.

The fifty years between Article 340's enactment and the Mandal Commission's implementation saw India's most politically explosive constitutional controversy. The Kaka Kalelkar Commission (1953) identified OBC communities and recommended reservation, but the government rejected the recommendations on the grounds that using caste as the basis for identifying backward classes would entrench rather than dissolve caste consciousness. The Mandal Commission (1978–1980) reached essentially the same conclusion as Kalelkar — that caste was an unavoidable proxy for social and educational backwardness — but its recommendations, when implemented by the V.P. Singh government in 1990, produced nationwide protests, political upheaval, and a constitutional crisis that ended in the Supreme Court's nine-judge bench in Indra Sawhney (1992). That case remains the foundational text of Indian OBC reservation jurisprudence.

The article's significance is not merely historical. The demand for OBC status by communities such as Jats, Patels, Marathas, and Kapus — all powerful farming castes with political leverage at the State level — has kept Article 340's framework at the centre of Indian constitutional politics. The 102nd Amendment (2018) creating a constitutional NCBC and a Central OBC list under Article 342A has added new institutional architecture, but the Article 340 commission mechanism retains an independent role for comprehensive fresh investigations whenever the accumulated social data on OBC conditions warrants a thorough review.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)** grants the President a discretionary power — "may by order appoint" — to constitute a Commission to investigate conditions of socially and educationally backward classes across India. The Commission must be tasked with three things: investigating conditions and difficulties; recommending remedial steps for the Union and States; and recommending the grants that should be made and the conditions on those grants. The Commission's procedure is defined by the appointing order, giving the President flexibility to tailor the process. The word "socially and educationally" is significant: it excludes purely economically backward communities — poverty alone, without accompanying social or educational disadvantage rooted in group identity, does not bring a community within the Article 340 framework. This distinction was central to the Indra Sawhney judgment's analysis.

**Clause (2)** sets out the Commission's core function: investigating and presenting a report with factual findings and recommendations. The report is presented to the President, not to Parliament directly. The commission's quasi-judicial or expert-body character means it investigates rather than legislates, producing a factual record and recommendations that then feed into the political process.

**Clause (3)** requires the President to cause the report and a memorandum on action taken to be laid before Parliament. Unlike Article 339(1) which requires only a report, the Article 340(3) memorandum must explain the action taken — not merely action proposed. This is a transparency obligation that creates a publicly tabled record of the government's response to commission recommendations. However, "action taken" encompasses no action, and governments have relied on Article 340(3)'s lack of a mandatory implementation requirement to decline to act on commission recommendations without constitutional consequence.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debates on Article 340 turned on the fundamental question of whether backward class identification should be caste-based or should rest on social and economic criteria independent of caste. Jawaharlal Nehru expressed a preference for moving toward caste-neutral criteria, while members from SC and ST communities argued that the reality of Indian social disadvantage was so caste-determined that caste could not be set aside as an identification criterion. The Constituent Assembly ultimately left the question open: Article 340 authorised investigation without prescribing the methodology, leaving it to the Commission to determine how "socially and educationally backward" should be defined. This deliberate ambiguity became the core issue in both the Kalelkar and Mandal Commission exercises.

Dr Ambedkar's position was nuanced: he accepted that backward class identification for OBCs would need to go beyond the SC and ST Presidential-notification model, but he was cautious about the open-ended social disruption that a large OBC reservation might produce. The Constituent Assembly's decision to use a commission mechanism rather than direct constitutional identification — as was done for SCs and STs — reflects this caution: Article 340 defers the hard definitional and political choices to a later expert body rather than embedding them in the constitutional text.

## Key cases

**M.R. Balaji v State of Mysore (1963)** — The Supreme Court held that Article 340 did not confer an independent power to grant reservations; the power to reserve posts flowed from Article 16(4), and the identification of backward classes under Article 340 was an input to that exercise, not self-sufficient authority. The Court rejected a State order reserving 68 per cent of seats for backward classes, holding that such a quantum was unreasonable and that reservation could not extend to such a large proportion as to extinguish merit-based selection altogether.

**State of Kerala v N.M. Thomas (1976)** — While examining the relationship between Articles 16(1) and 16(4), the Court affirmed that the Article 340 commission mechanism was intended to generate an empirical basis for backward-class identification. The judgment's discussion of the multiple ways in which the State may address structural inequality provided the doctrinal underpinning for the broader OBC reservation arguments that came before the Mandal case.

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench is the central authority on OBC reservation law. The Court upheld the Mandal Commission's identification of OBCs and the V.P. Singh government's 27 per cent reservation for OBCs in central services under Article 16(4). The Court held that caste is a permissible — and in the Indian context often necessary — criterion for identifying social and educational backwardness for the purpose of Article 16(4). It established the creamy-layer doctrine (advanced OBC members must be excluded from reservation), confirmed the fifty per cent ceiling on total reservation (SC plus ST plus OBC), held that promotional reservation for OBCs is not permissible (only initial appointment), and that OBC identification must exclude the creamy layer on an ongoing basis. Indra Sawhney also examined the Article 340 framework directly, holding that the Mandal Commission's investigation was a constitutionally valid exercise of the Article 340 power and that the government was constitutionally entitled to implement its recommendations through Article 16(4).

**Ashok Kumar Thakur v Union of India (2008)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Constitutional (Ninety-third Amendment) Act, 2005 inserting Article 15(5), which allowed reservation in private unaided educational institutions, and the Central Educational Institutions (Reservation in Admission) Act, 2006 extending OBC reservation to centrally funded institutions, subject to the exclusion of the OBC creamy layer. The Court reaffirmed Indra Sawhney's core holdings including the creamy-layer requirement and the fifty per cent ceiling.

**Janhit Abhiyan v Union of India (2022)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the 103rd Amendment inserting Articles 15(6) and 16(6), which introduced 10 per cent Economically Weaker Section (EWS) reservation for communities not covered by SC, ST, or OBC reservation. The Court held by a majority that EWS reservation was constitutionally valid despite exceeding the fifty per cent ceiling (since it was an independent category), but the dissents questioned whether economic criteria alone — without the social and educational backwardness framework of Article 340 — were constitutionally sufficient. The case raises the question of the relationship between Article 340's "socially and educationally backward" identification framework and the purely economic EWS criterion.

**Maratha Reservation Case — Dr Jaishri Laxmanrao Patil v Chief Minister, Maharashtra (2021)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench struck down Maharashtra's 16 per cent reservation for the Maratha community as a Socially and Educationally Backward Class, holding that the community had not been shown to be backward within the meaning of Articles 15 and 16 and that the reservation pushed the total well above the fifty per cent ceiling without exceptional circumstances justifying it. The case affirmed Indra Sawhney's ceiling and backwardness-identification requirements for OBC reservation.

## Current significance

The OBC reservation framework built on Article 340's foundation is politically the most contested area of Indian reservation law. The demand by powerful farming communities — Jats, Patels, Marathas, Kapus, Gujjars — for OBC status reflects the enormous material stakes attached to reservation in a country where government employment and higher education seats are rationed goods. These demands have produced State-level reservation laws that have been struck down (Maharashtra's Maratha reservation), prolonged litigation (Jats in Rajasthan and Haryana), and political mobilisation that shapes State and Central elections.

The 102nd Amendment (2018) inserting Articles 338B (NCBC) and 342A (Central OBC list) has changed the institutional landscape significantly. The Central OBC list is now maintained by Presidential notification on the advice of the constitutional NCBC, with Parliament holding the exclusive power to modify it — exactly paralleling the SC and ST list mechanism under Articles 341 and 342. This brings OBC list management into a framework with greater constitutional stability and parliamentary accountability, reducing the scope for State-level executive manipulation of OBC lists.

The Article 340 commission mechanism remains available for comprehensive fresh investigations. The Mandal Commission was forty-five years ago; India's OBC population, its distribution across occupational and educational categories, and its relationship to the creamy-layer cutoffs have changed enormously since 1980. There is a significant body of academic and policy opinion that a fresh Article 340 commission — with updated census data, disaggregated educational and economic indicators, and a methodology for identifying communities that are genuinely backward rather than communities that have merely failed to achieve political inclusion in the OBC list — is constitutionally appropriate and practically necessary. Whether any government will bear the political costs of a fresh comprehensive investigation remains the central political question about Article 340's future role.
