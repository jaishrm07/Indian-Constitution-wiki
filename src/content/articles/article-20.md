  20. Protection in respect of conviction for offences.—(1) No person shall be convicted of any offence except for violation of a law in force at the time of the commission of the Act charged as an offence, nor be subjected to a penalty greater than that which might have been inflicted under the law in force at the time of the commission of the offence.

## Why it matters

Article 20 places firm constitutional limits on state power at three critical points in the criminal process: the definition of the offence, the prosecution for that offence, and the gathering of evidence against an accused. Each clause addresses a distinct form of abuse — retrospective punishment, serial prosecution, and forced self-incrimination — that legislatures or investigators might otherwise use to coerce or oppress individuals. Together they form a foundational guarantee of fairness in criminal proceedings applicable to citizens and non-citizens alike.

## Reading the provision

Article 20(1) contains two distinct prohibitions: no person may be convicted under a law that did not exist at the time of the act, and no person may receive a heavier penalty than the law applicable at the time of the offence allowed. This is the ex post facto or nulla poena sine lege guarantee. Article 20(2) is the double-jeopardy clause: prosecution and punishment for the same offence cannot be repeated. Article 20(3) is the privilege against self-incrimination: a person accused of an offence cannot be compelled to be a witness against herself or himself.

## How courts use it

All three clauses generate regular litigation. Article 20(1) is typically invoked when Parliament increases penalties and attempts to apply them retroactively, or when a new penal provision is argued to cover conduct that was not unlawful when it occurred. Article 20(2) most commonly arises when departmental or disciplinary proceedings run in parallel with, or succeed, criminal prosecution for the same underlying conduct. Article 20(3) is at the centre of debates about the legality of forensic interrogation techniques and the scope of the right to silence.

## Key cases

**Maqbool Hussain v. State of Bombay (1953)** — The Supreme Court held that proceedings before a customs authority are not a "prosecution" for the purposes of Article 20(2), because the customs authority is not a court or judicial tribunal exercising criminal jurisdiction. The ruling established the foundational distinction between departmental or quasi-judicial proceedings and criminal prosecutions, shaping how the double-jeopardy clause interacts with parallel administrative processes.

**M.P. Sharma v. Satish Chandra (1954)** — An eight-judge bench held that the protection against self-incrimination in Article 20(3) does not extend to documents seized under a search warrant, because the accused is not being "compelled" in the constitutional sense when documents are taken by state force rather than extracted from the accused's own testimony. This narrow reading of "compelled to be a witness" was influential but has been significantly qualified by later decisions.

**Nandini Satpathy v. P.L. Dani (1978)** — The Supreme Court substantially broadened the scope of Article 20(3) by holding that the protection applies not only at the trial stage but also during police interrogation. Any compulsion to answer questions that may expose a person to criminal liability falls within the guarantee, and the accused retains the right to silence throughout the pre-trial phase. The decision placed constitutional constraints on investigative questioning.

**State of Bombay v. S.L. Apte (1961)** — The Court examined the scope of Article 20(2) where a person had been convicted under one statute and was subsequently prosecuted under a different statute for the same facts. The judgment drew the distinction between being prosecuted for the "same offence" and being prosecuted for the same facts but under a different statutory provision. The same set of facts may give rise to multiple offences, each separately prosecutable.

**Selvi v. State of Karnataka (2010)** — A three-judge bench held that involuntary administration of narco-analysis, brain-mapping, and polygraph tests violates Article 20(3) as well as the right to life under Article 21. The Court reasoned that the testimonial act elicited through such techniques — even if unconscious or involuntary — constitutes compulsion to be a witness against oneself. This ruling brought forensic neuroscience squarely within constitutional limits.

## Interaction with the Emergency

Article 20 is one of only three fundamental rights (along with Articles 21 and 22) that cannot be suspended during a national emergency under Article 358 or Article 359. This means that even during an emergency declared under Article 352, the protections against ex post facto laws, double jeopardy, and self-incrimination remain fully operative. This was a deliberate choice by the framers to create an irreducible minimum of criminal-law protection for all persons regardless of the political situation.

## Current significance

Article 20(3) is at the centre of ongoing debates about narco-analysis, brain-mapping, polygraph tests, and digital forensic techniques as investigative tools. The Selvi ruling set boundaries, but law enforcement agencies continue to seek methods at the edge of the prohibition. The ex post facto bar in Article 20(1) is regularly invoked when legislatures impose retrospective criminal liability — including recent debates about retrospective application of money-laundering and benami property provisions. The double-jeopardy clause shapes the interaction between departmental inquiries and criminal prosecutions in corruption, financial fraud, and misconduct cases involving public servants.
