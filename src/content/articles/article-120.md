## Why it matters

Language is a constitutional flashpoint in India. Article 120 reflects the Constituent Assembly's attempt to balance two competing imperatives — promoting Hindi as the national language, as mandated by Part XVII, and preserving the functional participation of members from non-Hindi states who would be effectively silenced if required to conduct all parliamentary business in Hindi alone. The article is a procedural compromise within a larger language-policy settlement that has never fully resolved the tensions between Hindi promotion and linguistic pluralism.

The clause (2) sunset — under which English was to disappear from parliamentary use by 1965 unless Parliament legislated otherwise — was a ticking constitutional clock that Parliament chose to defuse through the Official Languages Act 1963. The political decision to retain English has been repeated in successive parliaments, reflecting a pragmatic recognition that India's linguistic diversity makes any single-language mandate for parliamentary business divisive and impractical.

## Reading the provision

Article 120 sets the constitutional default for language of parliamentary business: Hindi or English. It operates as a specific override of the general language provisions in Part XVII, while remaining subject to Article 348 (which governs the language of authoritative texts of parliamentary Bills and Acts). The proviso allows the presiding officer to permit a member who cannot adequately express themselves in either Hindi or English to speak in their mother tongue — a concession to India's linguistic diversity.

**The mother-tongue proviso** is framed as a permission the presiding officer "may" grant, not a right the member holds. In practice, the Lok Sabha Speaker and Rajya Sabha Chairman have generally permitted members to address the House in regional languages when they indicate they cannot adequately express themselves in Hindi or English, with the Lok Sabha Secretariat providing simultaneous interpretation in the major regional languages. The extent to which this discretion has been withheld is limited; the controversy has typically been about the quality or availability of interpretation rather than denial of the permission itself.

**Clause (2) and the English sunset:** Clause (2) stipulated that after fifteen years from the Constitution's commencement (i.e., after 26 January 1965), Parliament would conduct business in Hindi only unless Parliament legislated to retain English. Parliament passed the Official Languages Act 1963 before this sunset, retaining English as an associate official language and preserving it for parliamentary business. Successive renewals have maintained this position, and English continues in practical daily use in both Houses alongside Hindi.

**Relationship with Article 348:** Article 348 requires the authoritative texts of Bills introduced in Parliament, Acts of Parliament, and certain court proceedings to be in English until Parliament provides otherwise. Article 120 governs the language in which parliamentary business is transacted — debate, questions, answers — while Article 348 governs the authoritative legal text of the legislative output. A Bill may be debated in Hindi but its authoritative text remains in English until Parliament decides otherwise.

## Key cases

**Jayantilal Amrit Lal Shodhan v. F. N. Rana (1964)** — The Supreme Court considered the relationship between Article 120 and Article 348 on the question of the authoritative text of legislation, holding that while parliamentary proceedings may occur in Hindi or English, the authoritative text of enacted law is governed by Article 348, and translation questions do not affect the validity of the underlying legislative act.

**S. R. Chaudhuri v. State of Punjab (2001)** — Though primarily about legislative disqualification, the Supreme Court's discussion of parliamentary proceedings and their record-keeping touched on how Article 120's language rules interact with the requirement that parliamentary records be maintained in a form accessible to all members.

**T. M. A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The eleven-judge Constitution Bench's analysis of language rights and minority educational institutions examined the broader constitutional framework of language policy, within which Article 120 sits as the parliamentary procedural component — distinct from the language rights of citizens under Part XVII but part of the same constitutional architecture balancing Hindi promotion with linguistic pluralism.

## Current significance

The mother-tongue proviso has taken on renewed significance as regional parties with strong linguistic identities have grown in parliamentary representation. Speakers from states such as Tamil Nadu, West Bengal, and Andhra Pradesh have increasingly chosen to address Parliament in their regional languages, with the Lok Sabha Secretariat providing simultaneous interpretation services. The constitutional question of whether the presiding officer's discretion to "permit" mother-tongue use is truly discretionary or constitutionally obligated when a member genuinely cannot express themselves adequately in Hindi or English has not been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court. The larger political question — whether Article 120(2)'s potential English sunset could still be activated by parliamentary repeal of the Official Languages Act — periodically resurfaces in debates about India's language policy.
