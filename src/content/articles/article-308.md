## What this Article does

Article 308 is the definitions clause for Part XIV of the Constitution — the Part that deals with Services Under the Union and the States, covering recruitment to and conditions of service in all-India services, Central and State services, and the Public Service Commission system. It gives the word "State" a specific meaning for purposes of that Part.

The article's sole operative content is the exclusion — now in square brackets to indicate textual modification — of "the State of Jammu and Kashmir" from the definition of "State" for Part XIV purposes. This exclusion existed from the Constitution's commencement because Jammu and Kashmir had its own constitutional arrangement under Article 370, including its own Public Service Commission framework, and the general services structure in Part XIV did not apply to it in full.

## Why it matters

Interpretation clauses like Article 308 serve a structural function: they define the scope of the entire Part to which they belong, establishing which entities are subject to the constitutional framework. In Part XIV, the question of whether a given public body is a "State" for the purposes of Articles 309–323 determines whether its employees enjoy constitutional protections, whether its recruitment must go through a PSC, and whether its service rules must be made under legislative authority.

Courts have consistently held that the word "State" in Article 308 should be read in the context of the entire Part XIV scheme. A local authority, statutory corporation, or autonomous body may not be a "State" for Article 308 purposes even if it is "State" under Article 12 for fundamental rights purposes. This distinction is critical: an employee of a government-owned company may be protected under fundamental rights via Article 12, but may not have Part XIV service-rule protections unless the company is itself constituted as a service under Article 309. The Part XIV definition of "State" tracks constitutional accountability in the public employment sphere — it marks out the entities that owe civil servants the full suite of procedural and substantive protections the Part provides.

The narrower definition also has a limiting function. If "State" in Part XIV were read as broadly as under Article 12, the UPSC consultation requirements in Article 320, the removal protections in Article 311, and the service-rule framework in Article 309 would apply to an enormous and heterogeneous range of bodies — universities, development authorities, port trusts, cooperative banks — many of which have their own sector-specific personnel frameworks. The narrower reading keeps the Part XIV system coherent and manageable.

## The Jammu and Kashmir dimension

The bracketed exclusion has a layered constitutional history. From 1950 to 2019, Jammu and Kashmir was governed by its own Constitution (the Constitution of Jammu and Kashmir, 1956) alongside the Indian Constitution, and Article 370 restricted the application of various parts of the Indian Constitution to J&K. The services framework under Part XIV was among the provisions that applied only in a modified form to J&K.

The Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order 2019 and the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act 2019 fundamentally altered this. Following the reorganisation of J&K into two Union Territories — Jammu and Kashmir (with a Legislature) and Ladakh (without) — the constitutional framework of Part XIV was extended to both Union Territories. The original J&K exclusion in Article 308, while still present in the text in brackets, no longer has practical operative effect. Both Union Territories now fall within the Part XIV framework.

## Key cases

**State of Assam v. Kuseswar Saikia (1982)** — The Gauhati High Court confirmed that the interpretation clause in Article 308, read with the overall structure of Part XIV, defines a narrower class of "States" than Article 12's expansive "other authorities" test. Statutory corporations created by State law are not automatically subject to Part XIV simply because they are amenable to fundamental rights challenge under Article 12.

**Union of India v. Tulsiram Patel (1985) 3 SCC 398** — The Supreme Court's landmark decision on Article 311 (dismissal and removal protections) relied heavily on the Part XIV framework defined by Article 308 to determine which persons and services fell within constitutional protection. The Court held that Part XIV governs persons holding civil posts under the Union or a State, and Article 308 determines the meaning of "State" for that purpose.

## Current significance

After the 2019 reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir, Article 308 is primarily a technical interpretation provision with limited live controversy. Its significance lies mainly in the boundary it draws between Part XIV's specific services framework and the broader concept of "State" under Article 12. As new models of public employment emerge — contract workers on government programmes, employees of autonomous bodies, public digital infrastructure workers — the interpretation clause in Article 308 periodically becomes relevant in determining whether a given category of public employee can claim the constitutional protections that Part XIV provides. The answer to that question begins with Article 308's definition of "State."
