  269. Taxes levied and collected by the Union but assigned to the States.—1[(1) Taxes on the sale or purchase of goods and taxes on the consignment of goods [except as provided in article 269A] shall be levied and collected by the Government of India but shall be assigned and shall be deemed to have been assigned to the States on or after the 1st day of April, 1996 in the manner provided in clause (2).

  Explanation.—For the purposes of this clause,—

  (a) the expression "taxes on the sale or purchase of goods" shall mean taxes on sale or purchase of goods other than newspapers, where such sale or purchase takes place in the course of inter-State trade or commerce;

  (b) the expression "taxes on the consignment of goods" shall mean taxes on the consignment of goods (whether the consignment is to the person making it or to any other person), where such consignment takes place in the course of inter-State trade or commerce.

  (2) The net proceeds in any financial year of any such tax, except in so far as those proceeds represent proceeds attributable to Union territories, shall not form part of the Consolidated Fund of India, but shall be assigned to the States within which that tax is leviable in that year, and shall be distributed among those States in accordance with such principles of distribution as may be formulated by Parliament by law.] [(3) Parliament may by law formulate principles for determining when a [sale or purchase of, or consignment of goods] takes place in the course of inter-State trade or commerce.]

## Why it matters

Article 269 sits at a structural fault line in Indian fiscal federalism: the Union has exclusive legislative competence over inter-State commerce under Article 246 and the Union List, yet the economic benefit of taxing that commerce ought logically to flow to the States where the underlying transactions take place. Article 269 resolves this tension by separating the power to levy (retained by the Union, who alone can regulate inter-State trade) from the benefit of the revenue (assigned to the States where the taxable event occurs). This is not a voluntary grant — the assignment is mandatory and automatic, with the proceeds legally excluded from the Consolidated Fund of India the moment they are collected.

The rationale for Union levy but State assignment is both constitutional and practical. Constitutionally, inter-State sales cannot be taxed by the origin State alone without creating trade barriers, and the destination State cannot levy taxes on goods that have already crossed its border; so the Union must act as the taxing authority for commercial uniformity. Practically, however, allowing the Union to retain those revenues would amount to a subsidy from commercial activity to the central exchequer at the expense of the producing or consuming States, distorting the fiscal balance that Articles 268 to 275 collectively construct.

The 101st Constitutional Amendment (2016) significantly restructured Article 269 by inserting the phrase "except as provided in article 269A." Article 269A created a parallel track for IGST — the integrated goods and services tax on inter-State supplies — with a different apportionment regime. The effect is that post-2017, Article 269's direct assignment mechanism applies only to goods and transactions outside the GST net, most importantly petroleum products and alcohol for human consumption, which remain subject to the older CST framework.

Understanding Article 269 today requires holding two timelines together: its pre-GST life as the constitutional foundation of the Central Sales Tax regime and its post-GST residual life covering commodities still outside the GST net. Both reflect the same structural logic, even if the fiscal stakes have changed with the introduction of GST.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) sets out the core rule: taxes on inter-State sale or purchase of goods, and taxes on consignment of goods in the course of inter-State trade, are levied and collected by the Union but deemed assigned to the States from 1 April 1996 onward. The Explanation confines the assignment to goods other than newspapers and only for transactions that qualify as inter-State commerce — a qualification that has generated an extensive body of case law on what makes a transaction "in the course of inter-State trade." Critically, the article does not allow the Union any discretion over whether to assign; the assignment is constitutionally compelled the moment the tax is levied.

Clause (2) refines the assignment rule: the net proceeds attributable to Union Territories are excluded (since there is no separate State government to receive them), but all remaining proceeds are assigned to the States within which the tax is leviable. Parliament may prescribe principles for distributing proceeds among the States — this was the basis for the Central Sales Tax Act and its distribution provisions. The language "shall not form part of the Consolidated Fund of India" is emphatic: these are pass-through receipts in constitutional terms, not Union revenues that are later redistributed.

Clause (3) gives Parliament the power to formulate principles for determining when a sale, purchase, or consignment takes place "in the course of inter-State trade or commerce." This is a necessary complement to the assignment rule: the line between intra-State transactions (taxed by States under Entry 54 of the State List) and inter-State transactions (taxed by the Union and assigned back under Article 269) is economically significant and constitutionally contested. Parliament exercised this power through Section 3 of the Central Sales Tax Act, which defines inter-State sales. The Supreme Court has had to interpret this section in hundreds of cases across several decades.

## Key cases

**Bengal Immunity Company v. State of Bihar (1955)** — One of the earliest and most foundational cases on the distinction between inter-State and intra-State commerce. The Supreme Court rejected a narrow reading of "in the course of inter-State trade" and held that the movement of goods across State borders, directly linked to the transaction, is the critical indicator. This interpretation determined the scope of transactions that attract Union levy and State assignment under the predecessor provisions to Article 269, and remains the baseline for CST characterisation.

**State of Madras v. Radio and Electrical Mfg. Co. (1959)** — The Court applied the Bengal Immunity principle to refine when a sale terminates the inter-State character of a movement, clarifying that transactions integrated with cross-border movement remain inter-State even if the formal contract is executed within one State. The holding defined the outer boundary of Article 269 assignment for manufacturing and supply contracts.

**Gannon Dunkerley v. State of Rajasthan (1993)** — The Supreme Court's broad reading of works contracts as having a separable goods component brought large-scale construction and supply agreements within the scope of the CST levy, with consequences for which State received the assigned proceeds. The judgment shaped the Article 269 regime for capital goods transactions and infrastructure contracts.

**State of Kerala v. Union of India (CST Matters)** — The Court examined whether the Union was correctly accounting for CST proceeds as pass-through assignments rather than retaining them as Consolidated Fund income, affirming that Article 269(2)'s exclusion from the Consolidated Fund is absolute. The Union has no discretion over the quantum to be assigned once Parliament's distribution formula operates; it acts as a collecting agent, not a beneficial owner of these revenues.

## Current significance

Since the GST rollout in July 2017, the fiscal architecture that Article 269 underpinned has substantially shifted. The Central Sales Tax Act has been largely displaced for goods and services now within the GST net. Article 269A governs IGST on inter-State supplies — a broader, more complex apportionment framework that distributes proceeds between the Union and the States rather than assigning them entirely to the originating State. Article 269's assignment mechanism now operates principally for goods outside GST: petroleum products (crude oil, petrol, diesel, aviation turbine fuel, and natural gas), tobacco and alcohol for human consumption, and certain other excluded commodities.

The ongoing GST Council debate about bringing petroleum products within the GST net has direct constitutional implications for Article 269. If petroleum is brought within GST, the residual assignment mechanism of Article 269 shrinks further, and the IGST apportionment route under Article 269A becomes the dominant instrument. States that are large petroleum-producing or consuming states — including Rajasthan, Gujarat, Assam, and Tamil Nadu — continue to receive significant CST-assigned revenues under Article 269 for petroleum transactions, and watch this debate closely.

The broader legacy of Article 269 is structural: it established the principle that Union levy power in inter-State commerce does not carry with it an entitlement to retain the fiscal fruit of that levy. This principle, replicated in different form in Articles 268 and 269A, is fundamental to preventing the Union's regulatory role in interstate commerce from being used as a mechanism to accumulate revenue at States' expense. As the GST Council continues to evolve the post-2017 fiscal framework, Article 269's logic remains a reference point for ensuring that inter-State commerce revenue ultimately flows to the States where economic activity takes place.
