## Why it matters

Article 74 requires the President to act on Cabinet advice. Left alone, that rule would reduce the President to an entirely passive constitutional figurehead — receiving advice and acting on it, with no independent information about what the Cabinet is actually doing or why. Article 78 prevents that outcome by imposing proactive and responsive information duties on the Prime Minister. The result is a constitutional relationship in which the President is bound to follow Cabinet advice but is constitutionally entitled to know about Cabinet decisions — not merely to rubber-stamp them in ignorance.

The Bagehot formulation that Dr. B.R. Ambedkar invoked in the Constituent Assembly debates captures the purpose precisely: a constitutional head of state has the right to be consulted, the right to encourage, and the right to warn. Article 78 supplies the informational substrate for all three. A President who does not know what decisions are being taken cannot consult, encourage, or warn with any constitutional effect. By converting the Bagehot convention into an enforceable constitutional obligation rather than a mere political custom, the framers gave the presidency a genuine consultative function within what is formally a Cabinet-dominant system.

Article 78 is also significant as an internal accountability mechanism. The clause (c) duty — submitting single-Minister decisions to the full Cabinet on Presidential request — functions as a check on the tendency of strong Prime Ministers to govern through unilateral ministerial decisions without genuine collective deliberation. The President, who receives information about government decisions under clause (a) and is entitled to request information under clause (b), is positioned to identify when Cabinet government is being bypassed. The clause (c) mechanism then allows the President to require restoration of collective deliberation without either overturning the Minister's decision or publicly opposing the government.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a) — proactive communication of all Cabinet decisions.** This is the primary information duty. "All decisions of the Council of Ministers relating to the administration of the affairs of the Union and proposals for legislation" is extraordinarily broad. It covers every Cabinet resolution, not just those of political or constitutional significance. The PM cannot filter decisions that seem too routine or too sensitive to share — the duty is to communicate all of them. The obligation arises as soon as a Cabinet decision is taken; the PM does not wait for the President to request information. In practice this duty is discharged through a system of Cabinet Secretariat communications forwarded to Rashtrapati Bhavan, but the constitutional obligation is clear and direct.

**Clause (b) — responsive information furnishing.** The President may call for "such information relating to the administration of the affairs of the Union and proposals for legislation as the President may call for." The repetition of the PM's duty language ("as the President may call for") signals that this is not a discretionary courtesy but a constitutional obligation. There is no subject-matter limit — the President may request information about any aspect of Union administration or any pending or proposed legislation. The PM cannot decline to provide information on grounds of Cabinet confidentiality, political sensitivity, personal privilege, or relevance. A systematic refusal to furnish information under clause (b) would be a constitutional breach — which, while not directly enforceable in real time, would create a constitutional controversy of the highest political order.

**Clause (c) — referral of single-Minister decisions to full Cabinet.** This clause addresses the collective responsibility dimension of Cabinet government. Under Article 75(3), the Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to the Lok Sabha — which implies collective decision-making. When an individual Minister acts on a significant matter without Cabinet deliberation, the collective responsibility principle is potentially undermined. Clause (c) allows the President — having learned of such a decision through the clause (a) information flow or through clause (b) inquiry — to require the PM to submit that decision to the full Cabinet for consideration. The mechanism does not give the President power to overturn the Minister's decision or substitute presidential judgment for ministerial judgment — it gives the President a right to insist on process, ensuring that major decisions receive full Cabinet scrutiny rather than remaining the unilateral act of a single Minister.

**Relationship with Article 74(2).** Article 74(2) makes Cabinet advice to the President non-justiciable. Article 78 creates information duties that run in the opposite direction — from the PM to the President. These are structurally separate: the non-inquiry rule prevents courts from asking what advice was tendered under Article 74, but it does not insulate the PM from the obligation to communicate Cabinet decisions under Article 78. A PM who systematically fails to inform the President of Cabinet decisions is in breach of Article 78, and that breach is in principle justiciable, even though the content of specific advice tendered remains non-justiciable under Article 74(2).

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Article 78 was introduced as Draft Article 55B by the Drafting Committee. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar presented it on 30 December 1948 during the Assembly's clause-by-clause consideration of the draft. He invoked Walter Bagehot's The English Constitution directly, describing the three functions of a constitutional head of state — to be consulted, to encourage, to warn — and argued that Article 78 was the mechanism by which those functions could be meaningfully exercised.

Ambedkar made a specific point that has been consistently cited in subsequent commentary: the difference between the British position and the Indian constitutional design is that in Britain the Prime Minister's duty to inform the Crown is a constitutional convention enforceable only politically, whereas under the proposed Article 78 it is a constitutional obligation enforceable by courts. The conversion from convention to constitutional rule was deliberate and important: it meant that a PM who systematically denied the President information was not merely behaving improperly in a political sense but was in breach of a constitutional duty.

T.T. Krishnamachari raised the question of whether the clause (c) mechanism — requiring the PM to refer single-Minister decisions to full Cabinet — was too interventionist, effectively allowing the President to second-guess Cabinet management. Ambedkar's response was that clause (c) was not a power to override ministerial decisions but a power to insist on process — to restore collective deliberation where it had been bypassed. The President could not substitute personal judgment for the Cabinet's; the President could only require the Cabinet to actually deliberate collectively.

The debates did not engage extensively with the enforcement question — what happens if a PM simply refuses to communicate decisions or furnish information. The implicit assumption appears to have been that constitutional breach of this magnitude would be a political crisis that Parliament and the public would address, rather than a matter for routine judicial enforcement.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — The Constitution Bench's comprehensive analysis of the PM-President relationship treated Article 78 as structural evidence that the framers intended the President to be genuinely informed rather than formally bypassed. The Court held that Articles 74 and 78 are complementary provisions — Article 74 requires the President to follow Cabinet advice, Article 78 requires the PM to ensure the President knows what the Cabinet is deciding. Together they describe a relationship of constitutional reciprocity: bound obedience on the President's part, matched by mandatory transparency on the PM's part.

**A.K. Roy v. Union of India (1982) 1 SCC 271** — The Supreme Court's analysis of preventive detention law touched on the PM's obligations under Article 78 in the context of Cabinet deliberation and Presidential action. The Court noted that Article 78 creates justiciable constitutional obligations on the PM rather than mere political conventions, and that systematic failure to inform the President of Cabinet decisions would constitute a constitutional violation undermining the structural integrity of the Union executive.

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court's examination of the Bihar Assembly dissolution involved questions about whether the President had been adequately informed before exercising the Article 356 discretion. The Court confirmed that the President is constitutionally entitled to be properly informed before exercising constitutional discretions, and treated Article 78 as the mechanism through which this informational entitlement is satisfied. A Presidential action taken on defective or incomplete information furnished under Article 78 may be constitutionally flawed even if no specific advice tendered is questioned under Article 74(2).

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — Although the principal issues in Bommai were about Article 356 and floor tests, the nine-judge bench's analysis implicitly relied on the Article 78 information channel as the mechanism through which the President receives the "satisfaction" that must precede an Article 356 Proclamation. If the material supplied to the President through the Article 78 channel is fabricated or irrelevant, the Presidential satisfaction is constitutionally defective — and the Court confirmed that such material is reviewable even if specific advice content is not.

**Constituent Assembly Debates, Vol. VIII (30 December 1948)** — Ambedkar's speech introducing Draft Article 55B remains the primary source for the provision's purpose and constitutional standing. The speech directly addressed the Bagehot framework, the distinction between convention and constitutional rule, and the scope of clause (c). It is regularly cited as authoritative interpretive material in cases involving the PM-President information relationship and the scope of Presidential consultative functions.

## The information asymmetry problem

A recurring structural problem is that the President's exercise of constitutional functions — returning a Bill under Article 111, deciding whether to proclaim an emergency under Article 352, appointing the Prime Minister when the Lok Sabha result is ambiguous — requires access to accurate and complete information. If the PM communicates selectively, delays communication, or responds to Presidential requests with incomplete information, the quality of Presidential decision-making is compromised.

Article 78 does not provide an enforcement mechanism. There is no procedure by which the President can compel real-time compliance, no penalty attached to the PM's failure to communicate under clause (a), and no deadline for responding to clause (b) requests. Courts have acknowledged the justiciable character of the obligations in principle, but no court has been called upon to enforce Article 78 duties in a concrete ongoing case. The enforcement is therefore primarily political and constitutional in a broad sense — a PM who systematically denies the President information creates a constitutional controversy of the first order that Parliament and the public can respond to, but courts are unlikely to act as real-time information ombudsmen.

The information asymmetry problem is particularly acute when the President and the Prime Minister come from different political orientations, or when the President has reason to believe that specific decisions taken in Cabinet should be referred back to the full Council under clause (c). Several Presidents have exercised their consultative functions — making public speeches, writing letters to Prime Ministers, returning files — in ways that suggest they are acting on information received under Article 78 and exercising the Bagehot right to warn. These episodes illustrate the real constitutional life of a provision that may seem merely procedural on its face.

## Current significance

The significance of Article 78 sharpens whenever political distance develops between the President and the Prime Minister. The President's public communications, speeches, and references to the Supreme Court often reflect the information the President has received through the Article 78 channel and the President's exercise — within constitutional form — of the right to encourage and warn. Several Presidents have used the returning of files and requests for information under what appears to be the clause (b) mechanism to signal constitutional concerns about specific executive decisions without formally vetoing them.

The clause (c) mechanism — requiring the PM to refer single-Minister decisions to full Cabinet — has attracted sustained scholarly interest as a potential check on Prime Ministerial dominance of the Cabinet. Where a powerful Prime Minister effectively makes decisions bilaterally with individual Ministers and then presents them to Cabinet for ratification rather than deliberation, clause (c) could in principle be used by a constitutionally active President to require genuine collective consideration. That this mechanism has not been publicly invoked in recorded practice reflects the current equilibrium of presidential restraint and prime-ministerial dominance, not evidence that the constitutional instrument is unavailable.

President Murmu's 2025 reference to the Supreme Court under Article 143 on gubernatorial obligations regarding State Bills creates a backdrop for potentially reconsidering the informational relationship between the President and the Prime Minister. If the Court addresses the temporal and procedural obligations of constitutional heads of state in receiving and acting on Bills, those principles will shed light on the nature of the Article 78 information channel and the President's entitlement to be informed before exercising discretionary constitutional functions.
