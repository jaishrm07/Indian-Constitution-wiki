## What this Article did

Article 144A was inserted by the Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976 — the Emergency-era omnibus amendment — and provided two structural rules governing the Supreme Court's power to strike down laws:

**Minimum bench composition:** No question relating to the constitutional validity of any Central or State law could be decided by the Supreme Court unless the case was heard by a bench of not less than seven judges. Under Article 145, the ordinary rules for Constitution Bench proceedings required only five judges. Article 144A effectively created a super-Constitution Bench requirement for any law's invalidity determination.

**Supermajority for invalidity:** A law could not be declared constitutionally invalid unless a majority of not less than two-thirds of the judges sitting on the bench so held. On a seven-judge bench, this meant five judges would need to find a law unconstitutional. A simple majority (four to three) on a seven-judge bench would not suffice.

Together, these two requirements significantly raised the threshold for judicial invalidation of legislation. A law that could previously be struck down by a majority of a five-judge Constitution Bench (three to two) would under Article 144A require agreement of at least five of seven judges — and if fewer than seven were sitting, no invalidity finding could be made at all.

## The Emergency context and purpose

The 42nd Amendment was enacted when the Supreme Court had just completed the Kesavananda Bharati case (1973), in which a thirteen-judge bench had by a narrow majority (seven to six) upheld the basic structure doctrine as a limit on Parliament's amending power. Article 144A was designed in part to ensure that any future assault on Emergency legislation would face a virtually insurmountable judicial threshold: the government of the day needed only to keep a majority of one third of a super-bench opposed to invalidity, rather than losing a simple majority of an ordinary bench.

The provision was also a response to earlier Supreme Court constitutional decisions that the Emergency government found adverse. By requiring a larger bench, the government may have calculated that assembling a seven-judge bench in the compressed timeframes of Emergency-era litigation would create practical delays. By requiring a two-thirds majority, it created a buffer against what the government perceived as a judiciary inclined to protect rights over executive power.

## Omission by the 43rd Amendment

The Constitution (Forty-third Amendment) Act, 1977 omitted Article 144A by section 5, effective 13 April 1978. This was part of the Janata government's systematic reversal of Emergency-era judicial curtailments: Articles 131A (exclusive SC jurisdiction over Central law validity) and 226A (bar on HC examination of Central law validity) were simultaneously omitted. The ordinary rules were restored: a minimum five-judge Constitution Bench under Article 145 for constitutional validity questions, with a simple majority sufficient to declare invalidity.

The Supreme Court has since held in Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019) that all constitutional validity questions — particularly challenges to amendments — should be heard by Constitution Benches of at least five judges, but this is a judge-made rule of procedure, not a supermajority requirement like Article 144A had imposed.

## Why this Article matters as constitutional history

Article 144A is among the most extreme examples of the 42nd Amendment's assault on judicial independence. It attacked not just the substance of judicial review (as other provisions did by shielding legislation from challenge) but the structural conditions under which review could succeed: the composition of the bench and the threshold for a binding invalidity ruling.

The provision's effect, had it endured, would have been to make judicial review of Emergency-period legislation effectively impossible. A government that had appointedEMergency-era judges, controlled the appointment process, and had detained opposition politicians would face a two-thirds supermajority requirement — a constitutional design that effectively placed legislation beyond judicial reach unless an overwhelming judicial consensus formed.

The 43rd Amendment's restoration of ordinary judicial review procedures was therefore not merely a technical correction but a constitutional statement that the rule of law, including effective judicial review with reachable thresholds, is not negotiable even in periods of political emergency.
