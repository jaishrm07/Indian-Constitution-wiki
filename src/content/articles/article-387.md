## What this Article did

Article 387 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that made special provision for determining population for the purposes of certain elections. It has been omitted.

The Constitution contains various provisions that allocate seats in legislatures and determine electoral arrangements partly on the basis of population. At the time of commencement, the 1941 Census was the most recent census available. The 1951 Census had not yet been completed. Article 387 addressed the transitional problem of which population figures should be used for certain elections during this period, likely specifying the use of 1941 Census data or other available population estimates for electoral purposes.

This was a technical but important provision: electoral rolls, seat allocations, and delimitation of constituencies all depend on reliable population data. During the transition period before the 1951 Census results were available, Article 387 provided clarity on the population figures to be used.

## Why it was omitted

Article 387 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. By that time, the 1951 Census results had been published and used for the delimitation of constituencies for the first general elections. The transitional uncertainty about which population figures to use had been resolved, making Article 387 entirely spent.

## Historical context

The first general elections of 1951-52 were an extraordinary logistical undertaking — the largest democratic exercise in world history at that time, with 173 million eligible voters, over 489 Lok Sabha seats, and around 3,280 State Assembly seats to be filled. Determining population for constituency delimitation was a critical step in organising these elections. Article 387 ensured that there was constitutional clarity about the population figures to be used, despite the lag between the 1941 and 1951 Censuses.

The Delimitation Commission constituted for the first elections used population data derived from the 1941 Census, supplemented by estimates, for the initial delimitation of parliamentary and State Assembly constituencies.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge based on population determination under Article 387 is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 387 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in providing the technical constitutional framework for population-based electoral arrangements during the transition to universal adult franchise elections, a critical step in India's democratic consolidation.

The first general elections of 1951-52 remain a landmark in democratic history. Held over five months (October 1951 to February 1952), they involved over 173 million voters, 489 Lok Sabha constituencies, and simultaneous State Assembly elections across the country. The logistical and constitutional preparations for these elections — including the delimitation of constituencies using the population data provisions that Article 387 helped address — were managed by the Election Commission under Sukumar Sen, India's first Chief Election Commissioner. The successful conduct of those elections was in part built on the constitutional foundations laid by Article 387 and its companion transitional provisions.

The Delimitation Commission established for the first elections had to work with imperfect population data, relying on the 1941 Census as the primary source while the 1951 Census was being conducted. Article 387's provisions for special determination of population for electoral purposes gave constitutional sanction to the use of available data rather than waiting for the 1951 Census, making it possible for the first elections to be held on a timely basis. This technical accommodation had important political consequences — it enabled India to make the critical transition from provisional to elected government within two years of the Constitution's commencement.
