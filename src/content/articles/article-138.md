## What this Article does

Article 138 is an enabling provision that authorises Parliament to expand the Supreme Court's jurisdiction beyond what is expressly conferred by the Constitution. Clause (1) allows Parliament to confer further jurisdiction and powers on the Court in relation to matters falling in the Union List (Seventh Schedule, List I). Clause (2) goes further and allows jurisdiction to be extended to any matter — including matters not in the Union List — if the Union and one or more State Governments enter a special agreement and Parliament enacts enabling legislation.

The two clauses serve different constitutional purposes. Clause (1) operates unilaterally: Parliament alone, acting in relation to a Union List subject, can legislate additional Supreme Court jurisdiction. Clause (2) requires federal cooperation — a special agreement between the Union and at least one state — before Parliament can extend jurisdiction to matters outside the Union List. This distinction reflects the federal compact: Parliament should not be able to unilaterally draw state-subject matters into the Supreme Court's orbit without state consent.

## The Union List constraint in Clause (1)

Clause (1)'s limitation to Union List matters is significant. It means Parliament cannot invoke Article 138(1) to give the Supreme Court jurisdiction over, for example, a purely state subject such as land law or local government. For such subjects, the Article 138(2) special-agreement route is required. This preserves the federal distribution of legislative competence even as Parliament enlarges judicial competence.

In practice, Parliament has used Article 138(1) authority to give the Supreme Court additional jurisdiction over Union List subjects where national uniformity in judicial interpretation is particularly important — for example, commercial disputes involving companies incorporated under central legislation and matters arising under central banking and securities laws.

## The special-agreement route in Clause (2)

Clause (2) provides a flexible federal mechanism. The Union and a state may agree to confer additional jurisdiction on the Supreme Court over any matter, and Parliament then legislates to give effect to that agreement. This route has been used, for example, in inter-state water disputes where a specific agreement between riparian states and the Union sets the jurisdictional framework and Parliament's legislation translates it into judicially enforceable jurisdiction.

## Basic-structure limits

Parliament's Article 138 power is bounded by the basic structure. The most significant constraint established by case law is that Article 138 cannot be used to deprive High Courts of their Article 226 writ jurisdiction, because that jurisdiction is itself a basic-structure feature. Parliament cannot route a matter to the Supreme Court while simultaneously stripping litigants of recourse to High Courts — the two layers of judicial protection must coexist.

## Key cases

**L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997):** The Court held that Parliament cannot use enabling provisions such as Article 138 to confer jurisdiction that effectively bypasses or excludes the constitutional jurisdiction of High Courts under Article 226. The basic-structure doctrine constrains how far Parliament can go in enlarging the Supreme Court's jurisdiction by routing matters away from High Courts.

**Union of India v. Delhi High Court Bar Association (2002):** The Supreme Court affirmed that Article 138 is a source of legislative conferral — Parliament must positively legislate the expanded jurisdiction, and such legislation must be consistent with the constitutional scheme of courts. The article does not create a latent or inherent jurisdiction that the Court can exercise without parliamentary conferral.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019):** The Constitution Bench addressing the tribunal system raised the relationship between parliamentary power to create tribunals and route their appeals (drawing on Article 138 authority and other Union List powers) and the need to preserve the supervisory role of High Courts. The judgment reinforced L. Chandra Kumar's constraint and articulated further conditions for valid tribunal jurisdiction.

## Tribunals and Article 138

The contemporary significance of Article 138 is most visible in the tribunal framework. When Parliament creates a specialised tribunal under a Union List subject and channels appeals from that tribunal to the Supreme Court (rather than through the High Court hierarchy), it is drawing on Article 138(1) authority. The NCLAT-to-Supreme-Court appellate chain under the Companies Act, the NCLT appeals framework, and the Supreme Court's jurisdiction under the Competition Act all involve Article 138-type conferral, even if not always explicitly acknowledged. The L. Chandra Kumar constraint means that High Court supervisory jurisdiction over such tribunals must be preserved, which limits but does not eliminate Parliament's ability to create efficient, streamlined appellate routes to the Supreme Court.

## Current significance

Article 138 is likely to become more operationally significant as India develops specialised adjudication frameworks for complex commercial, environmental, and regulatory matters. Calls for Parliament to use Article 138 to create dedicated intellectual property and competition-law Supreme Court panels, to establish inter-state commercial dispute facilities, or to provide a direct appellate route from major regulatory tribunals all depend on the article's continued availability as a legislative conferral tool. The basic-structure and L. Chandra Kumar constraints define the outer limits, but within those limits there remains considerable scope for Parliament to calibrate the apex court's jurisdiction to meet 21st-century governance needs.
