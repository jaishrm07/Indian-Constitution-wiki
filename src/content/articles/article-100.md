  100. Voting in Houses, power of Houses to act notwithstanding vacancies and quorum.—(1) Save as otherwise provided in this Constitution, all questions at any sitting of either House or joint sitting of the Houses shall be determined by a majority of votes of the members present and voting, other than the Speaker or person acting as Chairman or Speaker. The Chairman or Speaker, or person acting as such, shall not vote in the first instance, but shall have and exercise a casting vote in the case of an equality of votes.

[(3) Until Parliament by law otherwise provides, the quorum to constitute a meeting of either House of Parliament shall be one-tenth of the total number of members of the House.

## Why it matters

Parliamentary democracy functions only if the rules for counting votes are clear and immune from manipulation. Article 100 settles three foundational questions: how many members must be present, how the vote is taken, and whether a House's past business can be undone by after-the-fact challenges to the status of those who participated. Without these rules, every legislative outcome would be vulnerable to procedural attack.

The quorum requirement — one-tenth of total membership — is deliberately low. The framers recognised that requiring a majority to be physically present before any business could be transacted would paralyse an assembly whose members have large constituencies and heavy travel demands. The rule instead places the onus on the presiding officer to act once the absence of quorum is formally noticed.

The simple majority rule ("present and voting") is the constitutional default for all legislative decisions unless the Constitution specifies otherwise. Constitutional amendment Bills under Article 368 require a special majority; ratification of a State of Emergency under Article 352 requires a special majority; removal of judges under Articles 124 and 218 requires a special majority. All other votes — including most Bill passages, confidence motions, no-confidence motions, and resolutions — are governed by Article 100's plain majority standard. Understanding Article 100 is therefore the prerequisite for understanding every other voting rule in the Constitution.

The retroactive-validity saving clause in clause (2) is less discussed but constitutionally important. It provides that neither vacancies in membership nor the subsequent discovery of unauthorised participation vitiates completed parliamentary proceedings. This provision is the constitutional foundation for the finality of legislative acts — without it, legislation could be challenged years after passage on the grounds that one of the voting members was subsequently found to have been disqualified from membership at the time of the vote.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the voting rule:** All questions are decided by a majority of members present and voting. Members who abstain or are absent are not counted in the denominator — this is a critical feature of the "present and voting" standard. A Bill opposed by 100 members, supported by 101, and abstained from by 50 others passes, even though fewer than a majority of total membership voted for it. The presiding officer (Speaker in Lok Sabha, Chairman in Rajya Sabha) is excluded from all first-instance voting, preserving the appearance of impartiality. If votes are exactly equal, the presiding officer casts a casting vote — this is not a second vote but the only vote the presiding officer gets.

**Voice voting and division:** Under the Rules of Procedure, a vote may initially be taken by voice (ayes and noes). If the result is contested, or if a member demands it, the presiding officer must call a division — a formal, counted vote recorded in the parliamentary record. In a division, each member votes by pressing a button in their seat or by entering the Yes, No, or Abstain lobby. The result is recorded with names attached, creating the formal record used in anti-defection proceedings under the Tenth Schedule.

**Clause (2) — validity notwithstanding vacancies:** A House may transact business even when seats are vacant. More importantly, if it later emerges that a person who sat and voted was not entitled to do so — for instance, because a disqualification is discovered retrospectively — that does not vitiate the proceedings or unravel legislation already passed. This protects the finality of legislative outcomes against collateral attacks based on participant eligibility.

**Clauses (3) and (4) — quorum:** The quorum is one-tenth of the total membership: 55 for the Lok Sabha (total strength 550) and 25 for the Rajya Sabha (current maximum strength 250). If quorum is lost mid-sitting, the Chairman or Speaker must either adjourn the House or suspend the sitting until quorum is restored. Parliament may by law set a different quorum threshold but has not exercised this power in the seven decades since the Constitution came into force.

## The simple majority rule and the hierarchy of voting thresholds

Article 100's simple majority rule ("present and voting") is the constitutional baseline, but it interacts with a range of specially calibrated voting requirements elsewhere in the Constitution. Understanding Article 100 requires understanding this hierarchy.

| Vote type | Standard |
|---|---|
| Ordinary Bill passage | Simple majority of present and voting (Art. 100) |
| Constitutional amendment (Art. 368) | 2/3 of present and voting plus absolute majority of total membership |
| No-confidence motion | Simple majority of present and voting |
| Speaker/Deputy Chairman removal (Art. 94/90) | Absolute majority of total membership |
| Ratification of Emergency proclamation (Art. 352) | Special majority of each House |
| Presiding officer's casting vote | Used only on equality of votes |
| Quorum to transact business | 1/10 of total membership |

The "present and voting" denominator has been litigated in constitutional amendment challenges. When the Constitution requires a two-thirds majority of "members present and voting" plus an absolute majority of total membership, courts must determine whether both conditions are independently met. A constitutional amendment passed with a two-thirds majority on low attendance may satisfy the "present and voting" fraction but fail the absolute majority of total membership requirement. The two conditions are cumulative, and both must be independently satisfied.

## Quorum as a procedural weapon

The quorum provisions in clauses (3) and (4) are routinely invoked by oppositions as a procedural weapon. When attendance falls below 55 in the Lok Sabha or 25 in the Rajya Sabha, a member may raise a quorum call — formally drawing the presiding officer's attention to the absence of quorum. The presiding officer is then constitutionally obliged to either adjourn the House or suspend the sitting until quorum is restored.

In practice, presiding officers have developed conventions for managing quorum calls without immediately adjourning: ringing bells to summon members, allowing a brief period for members to assemble, and warning the government that the House cannot transact business without adequate attendance. But if the opposition is determined and the government cannot muster enough members quickly, a quorum call can halt government business entirely. In a session with a tight legislative agenda, repeated quorum calls can deprive the government of the floor time needed to pass priority Bills.

The government's counter-strategy is to maintain party whips on members during important legislative days, ensuring that enough members remain in or near the Chamber. The whipping system works in conjunction with Article 100's quorum requirements to create a structural incentive for ruling-party members to maintain parliamentary attendance even when attendance is personally inconvenient.

## The no-confidence motion and Article 100

One of the most significant applications of Article 100's simple majority rule is the no-confidence motion. A vote of no-confidence requires the support of a majority of members present and voting — not a majority of the total membership of the Lok Sabha. This means a government can survive a no-confidence motion even if a majority of members abstain or are absent, provided those who vote are more in favour of the government than against it.

The practical implication is that a government's survival on a no-confidence vote depends not only on the number of members supporting it but on the calculation of who attends and votes. A government that can persuade potential opponents to abstain rather than vote against achieves the same constitutional result as one that converts opponents to supporters. The S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) judgment by the nine-judge bench firmly established that the only constitutionally valid method of testing majority is a floor vote under Article 100 — a Governor cannot pre-judge the outcome or refuse to call a floor test because they believe the government has already lost its majority.

The casting vote in clause (1) has not, to date, been used by the Speaker of the Lok Sabha in a no-confidence vote, because no such vote has resulted in an exact tie at the national level. But at the State level, the equivalent provisions have been decisive: a Speaker's casting vote has determined the outcome of floor tests when the assembly was exactly evenly divided.

## The retroactive-validity saving clause and anti-defection

Clause (2)'s retroactive-validity saving clause has taken on renewed relevance in the context of disqualification litigation under the Tenth Schedule. When a government falls and members are later disqualified from the date of a past vote, the saving clause ensures that the vote — and any legislation or resolution that depended on it — is not undone retroactively. This creates a structural asymmetry: the disqualification takes effect prospectively for the member's seat but cannot unwind completed legislative business, which may mean that legislation passed with the votes of members who should have been disqualified remains valid law.

The Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2019) case directly addressed this interaction. The Supreme Court reiterated that votes cast by members even after defection notices are issued remain valid for the purposes of the proceedings until disqualification is formally declared, applying the clause (2) principle. This has significant practical implications: a government that can delay disqualification proceedings through the Article 96/Nabam Rebia mechanism can enact legislation using the votes of members who are simultaneously facing disqualification, and that legislation will be valid under clause (2) even if those members are subsequently disqualified.

## The voting record as anti-defection evidence

The voting-by-division procedure, available under the Rules of Procedure when any member requests it, creates a formal record of how each member voted. Whipped votes on important government Bills are typically decided by division, creating a paper trail that can be used in anti-defection proceedings to demonstrate how a member voted contrary to party direction. This linkage between Article 100's division procedure and the Tenth Schedule's anti-defection consequences makes voting a consequential rather than merely procedural act.

The formal record of a division serves as the primary evidence in anti-defection proceedings. When a disqualification petition alleges that a member voted contrary to the party whip, the divisional record of the House is the documentary evidence on which the presiding officer adjudicating the petition relies. Article 100's requirement that votes in a division be individually recorded is thus foundational to the operation of the anti-defection law — without recorded individual votes, the anti-defection mechanism would have no reliable evidentiary basis.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench engaged Article 100 while examining the constitutional validity of the Arunachal Pradesh Assembly proceedings conducted under contested conditions. The Court held that the quorum requirement and proper notice conditions are mandatory constitutional prerequisites, not mere procedural courtesies, and that the presiding officer's duty under clause (4) to adjourn when quorum is lost is non-discretionary. The bench also addressed whether a Deputy Speaker who is himself the subject of a removal notice retains the authority to exercise the presiding officer's functions, including quorum management.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** — The Constitution Bench's analysis of the Tenth Schedule engaged Article 100(2) on the question of votes cast by members facing disqualification proceedings. The Court's treatment of the retroactive-invalidity saving clause shaped the doctrine that completed legislative or voting acts are not undone merely because a participant's entitlement to vote is subsequently questioned.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — The nine-judge bench's discussion of floor tests and majority verification underscored that the constitutional majority under Article 100 is the relevant standard for confidence votes, and that the Governor cannot pre-judge a vote of confidence without testing it on the floor where Article 100 rules apply. The Court held that the only constitutionally valid way to test majority is a floor vote conducted under Article 100 rules — no other method, such as a count of letters or a claim of mathematical majority, satisfies the constitutional requirement.

**Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2019)** — The Supreme Court reiterated that votes cast by members even after defection notices are issued remain valid for the purposes of the proceedings until disqualification is formally declared, applying the clause (2) principle that proceedings cannot be retroactively invalidated. The case directly addressed the interface between Article 100 and the Tenth Schedule in conditions of contested membership.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — The Supreme Court, while dealing primarily with delay in anti-defection adjudication, reaffirmed Article 100's role as the constitutional standard for all voting in legislative chambers, and clarified that the quorum requirements are mandatory minimum conditions, not merely directory guidelines.

## Current significance

Quorum calls remain a regular opposition tactic. In both Houses, members raising a quorum call when attendance falls below the constitutional threshold effectively force the presiding officer to suspend the sitting, disrupting government business. Presiding officers have developed conventions — including bell-ringing to summon members — to manage quorum crises without formally adjourning, though critics argue this delays rather than resolves the constitutional problem.

The casting-vote rule has had decisive consequences in State Assemblies during close floor tests and Speaker elections. At the national level, the interaction between Article 100's simple-majority rule and the special-majority requirements for constitutional amendments has been tested in several amendment challenges, with courts confirming that the "present and voting" denominator (not total membership) governs the two-thirds fraction unless the Constitution expressly specifies otherwise.

Article 100(2)'s retroactive-validity saving clause continues to have significant practical implications as anti-defection and disqualification litigation proliferates. The clause's protection of completed legislative acts — including votes of confidence and no-confidence — means that even a government that later loses its majority and is disqualified cannot have the legislation it enacted during its tenure invalidated on retroactive grounds. This provides a measure of legislative stability that the Constitution deliberately prioritises over the principle of ensuring that only properly entitled members shape legislation.

The quorum provisions in clauses (3) and (4) apply equally to joint sittings under Article 108, where the combined membership of both Houses determines the quorum. A joint sitting of both Houses combined has a quorum of one-tenth of the total combined membership. This has not presented practical difficulties since the only joint sitting ever held — during the deadlock over the Prevention of Terrorism Act — attracted significant attendance. But the constitutional principle that Article 100's quorum rules apply to joint sittings means that a joint sitting used to resolve a bicameral deadlock could itself be disrupted by a quorum call, creating the possibility of a procedural impasse at the highest level of legislative process.
