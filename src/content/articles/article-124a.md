  [124A. National Judicial Appointments Commission.—(1) There shall be a Commission to be known as the National Judicial Appointments Commission consisting of the following, namely:—

  (a) the Chief Justice of India, Chairperson, ex officio;

  (b) two other senior Judges of the Supreme Court next to the Chief Justice of India——Members, ex officio;

  (c) the Union Minister in charge of Law and Justice——Member, ex officio;

  (d) two eminent persons to be nominated by the committee consisting of the Prime Minister, the Chief Justice of India and the Leader of Opposition in the House of the People or where there is no such Leader of Opposition, then, the Leader of single largest Opposition Party in the House of the People——Members:

  Provided that one of the eminent person shall be nominated from amongst the persons belonging to the Scheduled Castes, the Scheduled Tribes, Other Backward Classes, Minorities or Women:

  Provided further that an eminent person shall be nominated for a period of three years and shall not be eligible for renomination.

  (2) No act or proceedings of the National Judicial Appointments Commission shall be questioned or be invalidated merely on the ground of the existence of any vacancy or defect in the constitution of the Commission.

## What this article does

Article 124A inserted the National Judicial Appointments Commission (NJAC) into the Constitution as a formal constitutional body. Its composition reflected a deliberate departure from the collegium model: three sitting Supreme Court judges (the CJI and the next two senior-most), the Law Minister, and two eminent persons selected by a committee of the Prime Minister, CJI, and Leader of the Opposition. The eminent persons clause included a diversity mandate requiring that at least one nominee come from a Scheduled Caste, Scheduled Tribe, Other Backward Class, minority community, or be a woman.

The article was inserted by the Constitution (Ninety-ninth Amendment) Act, 2014, which simultaneously inserted Article 124B (functions of the NJAC) and Article 124C (power to make regulations). All three provisions were operative only briefly before being struck down.

## The NJAC judgment (2015)

In *Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India* (2015), a five-judge Constitution Bench struck down the Ninety-ninth Amendment and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act, 2014, by a 4:1 majority. The Court held that the amendment violated the basic structure of the Constitution because it threatened judicial independence, which the majority treated as an unenumerated but essential element of the basic structure.

The majority reasoned that the inclusion of the Law Minister as a permanent member gave the executive a structural foothold in judicial appointments that could not be cured by procedural safeguards alone. The eminent persons provision drew further criticism: the Court found that two lay nominees, acting in concert, could veto any appointment, handing potential veto power to persons who lacked the institutional accountability of judges. Justice Chelameswar wrote a notable dissent, arguing that the collegium system itself lacked transparency and that the amendment should have been allowed to operate.

## Insert-and-strike dynamic

Article 124A now occupies an unusual position in Indian constitutional law: it is part of the formal text of the Constitution but has no operative legal effect. Parliament inserted it through a valid constituent process; the Supreme Court invalidated it as unconstitutional. Unlike provisions formally repealed or omitted by subsequent amendment, the text of Article 124A (and Articles 124B and 124C) remains printed in authoritative editions of the Constitution, accompanied by editorial notes flagging the judicial nullification.

This dynamic illustrates an important structural feature of the Indian constitutional order: the basic structure doctrine allows courts to treat formally enacted constitutional amendments as void without requiring Parliament to go through the formality of repeal. The provision thus stands as a monument to a failed reform rather than as operative law.

## Current significance

The collegium system, governed by the Second Judges case (1993) and subsequent Supreme Court memoranda of procedure, remains the operative framework for Supreme Court and High Court appointments. Debates over transparency, delays in filling vacancies, and the role of the executive in the collegium process continue. The NJAC episode made clear that any future reform must navigate the basic structure constraint on judicial independence, a requirement that has so far foreclosed purely legislative or presidential solutions.
