## Why it matters

Article 286 was one of the most litigated provisions in Indian constitutional history during the
fifty years before GST. Every State wanted to tax as many commercial transactions as possible; every
merchant wanted to establish that the sale occurred outside the taxing State. The article's
prohibition on taxing out-of-State sales and import/export transactions was the constitutional wall
against which the entire edifice of inter-State commerce taxation was built. The Central Sales Tax
Act 1956 — Parliament's exercise of the clause (2) power — determined the situs of a sale and, by
doing so, decided which State could tax it and at what rate. The result was decades of litigation
about the precise point in time and space at which a sale "took place," making Article 286 the focal
point of Indian commercial tax law for half a century.

The article reflects a fundamental design choice by the framers: in a federation where multiple
States might each claim taxing jurisdiction over the same transaction, a constitutional rule was
needed to assign jurisdiction and prevent cascading double-taxation. Without Article 286, a single
sale of goods crossing State borders could in principle be taxed by the origin State, the
destination State, and any transit State — each asserting that the transaction, or a part of it,
took place within their territory. The prohibition in clause (1) draws a firm line: a State may tax
only the supplies that occur within its own borders, and it may not reach transactions occurring
outside, or those that form part of international trade.

The 101st Amendment's substitution of "supply of goods or services or both" for "sale or purchase
of goods" updated Article 286's vocabulary to align with GST's supply-based framework, but the
underlying constitutional logic — territorial jurisdiction over tax, prohibition on extra-territorial
reach, and Parliament's power to set the situs rules — remains intact. For the excluded commodities
still outside GST, that logic continues to operate under the pre-GST framework.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)(a) — outside the State.** A State cannot tax a supply that takes place outside its
own territory. This prevents each State from reaching transactions located in a neighbouring State.
The determination of where a supply takes place — the situs question — was, under the pre-GST
regime, governed by the rules formulated by Parliament under clause (2) and codified in the Central
Sales Tax Act 1956. Section 3 of that Act defined an "inter-State sale" as one that occasions the
movement of goods from one State to another; the State from which the movement begins was the
taxing State. Once a sale was classified as inter-State under the CST Act, neither the destination
State nor any transit State could levy its own tax on it — they were barred by clause (1)(a).

**Clause (1)(b) — import and export immunity.** A State cannot tax a supply that forms part of an
import into India or an export out of India. This protects cross-border trade from being obstructed
by State levies. The "in the course of export" immunity generated particularly rich litigation:
courts drew a distinction between a sale that is itself the direct cause of export (immune) and a
sale that merely precedes an export by another party (not immune). The Ben Gorm Collieries principle
— that the immunity requires a direct nexus between the sale and the export, not merely temporal
proximity — was applied across hundreds of cases involving mining companies, tea estates, and
port-based exporters.

**Clause (2) — Parliament's formulation power.** Parliament may formulate principles for determining
when a supply falls within the restrictions in clause (1). Parliament exercised this power through
the Central Sales Tax Act 1956, which created detailed rules for identifying the "sale outside the
State," the "sale in the course of inter-State trade," and the "sale in the course of import or
export." The CST Act further imposed a uniform Central levy on declared inter-State sales and
provided a mechanism for revenue sharing between States. The interpretation of those statutory rules
generated an enormous volume of Supreme Court and High Court decisions.

**Clause (3) — deleted.** The original clause (3) contained provisions about "declared goods" and
parliamentary power to reduce the rate of tax; it was omitted by the 101st Amendment as part of
the GST restructuring.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's discussion of what became Article 286 was shaped by awareness of the
acute practical problem of overlapping State tax claims. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar and T.T. Krishnamachari
both emphasised that the Constitution needed to prevent the fragmentation of the internal market
through competing State tax jurisdictions. The original formulation drew on expert advice from
economic advisers who had studied the problems of inter-State taxation in Australia and Canada.
The decision to give Parliament the clause (2) power to formulate situs principles, rather than
constitutionalising the rules directly, was a deliberate choice — it allowed flexibility as India's
commercial law developed, and it placed the technical determination of situs in the domain of
parliamentary legislation rather than constitutional text. This flexibility proved important: the
Central Sales Tax Act went through multiple amendments as the Supreme Court refined the meaning of
"sale outside the State" in successive decisions from 1955 onwards.

## Key cases

**Bengal Immunity Co. v. State of Bihar (1955) AIR 1955 SC 661** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench
overruled an earlier decision and held that a State cannot tax a sale that takes place outside its
territory even when goods are delivered within it. The Court established the "situs of sale" doctrine
— the location at which the transaction takes effect determines which State may tax it — which became
the bedrock of inter-State sales tax law for the next six decades. Bengal Immunity also read clause
(1)(b) broadly to protect the entire chain of transactions in the course of export, not merely the
final delivery to an overseas buyer.

**Tata Iron and Steel Co. v. State of Bihar (1958) SCR 1355** — The Supreme Court applied Bengal
Immunity to inter-State sales of "declared goods" (goods of special importance in inter-State trade,
listed under the Central Sales Tax Act), reinforcing that Parliament's clause (2) formulation must
operate consistently with the constitutional prohibition rather than circumvent it. The judgment is
significant for confirming that the CST Act's situs rules, when correctly applied, protect traders
from double-taxation across State lines.

**Ben Gorm Collieries v. Secretary, Sales Tax (1964) AIR 1964 SC 1413** — The Court examined the
meaning of a sale "in the course of export," drawing a distinction between a sale that is the direct
cause of export and a sale that merely precedes an export by another party. The judgment refined the
export immunity in clause (1)(b) and was applied repeatedly in disputes involving mining companies,
tea estates, and port-based exporters. It established the "direct nexus" test that governed
export-immunity claims for decades.

**State of Travancore-Cochin v. Bombay Co. (1952) AIR 1952 SC 366** — An early decision that
grappled with the meaning of an "outside the State" sale before Parliament had enacted the Central
Sales Tax Act, illustrating the difficulty courts faced in applying the constitutional prohibition
in the absence of statutory situs principles. The case prompted the demand for Parliament to exercise
its clause (2) power promptly, which it did through the CST Act 1956.

**K.G. Khosla & Co. v. Deputy Commissioner of Commercial Taxes (1966) SCR (2) 362** — The Supreme
Court considered the situs of a sale where the goods were in one State, the contract was concluded
in another, and delivery was taken in a third. The Court's analysis of the CST Act's rules in light
of the constitutional text of Article 286 reinforced that the situs question is ultimately a
constitutional one, and that Parliament's formulation power under clause (2) operates within the
constitutional framework rather than displacing it.

## Current significance

With GST covering most goods and services since July 2017, the active dispute territory under Article
286 has contracted sharply. The integrated GST mechanism — with the IGST Act determining when a
supply is inter-State and allocating the tax between the origin State, destination State, and Centre
— has replaced the pre-GST situs-of-sale litigation for the vast majority of transactions. The most
important residual area involves petroleum products (crude oil, high-speed diesel, motor spirit,
natural gas, and aviation turbine fuel) and alcohol for human consumption, which remain outside GST.
For these excluded commodities, each State still levies its own sales tax or VAT, and the Article 286
restriction — as interpreted through the Central Sales Tax Act 1956 — continues to govern inter-State
trade. The question of when petroleum products will be brought under GST, which the GST Council has
repeatedly deferred, will determine how long the pre-GST architecture retains practical significance.

The GST's constitutional embedding in Articles 246A and 279A has not formally replaced Article 286 —
it sits alongside it, with the 101st Amendment having updated Article 286's vocabulary while
preserving its structure. For the excluded commodities and for any future non-GST supply that States
might attempt to tax, Article 286 remains the constitutional anchor. Its continuing relevance is a
reminder that India's tax architecture, even after the GST transformation, remains a layered federal
structure in which constitutional prohibitions on State extra-territorial taxation retain genuine
operative force.
