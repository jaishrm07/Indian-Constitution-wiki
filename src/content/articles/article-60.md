## Why it matters

Article 60's oath is the constitutional moment at which the abstract authority of the presidential office becomes personally binding on its holder. Every constitutional function the President may exercise — assenting to Bills under Article 111, proclaiming emergencies under Article 352, appointing the Prime Minister under Article 75, granting clemency under Article 72 — flows from the office that is activated by this oath. An unsworn President has no constitutional authority; the oath is not merely an entry requirement but the condition that constitutes the office's power in a particular person.

The choice of the Chief Justice of India as the oath-administering authority is the provision's most symbolically charged decision. In most parliamentary systems, the head of state takes an oath before Parliament, the Speaker, or some other legislative authority. India's framers chose the head of the judiciary — a deliberate statement that the President's primary constitutional obligation runs to the Constitution rather than to the government of the day or the parliamentary majority. The judicial administration of the oath creates a constitutional triangle: the President, chosen by Parliament and State Assemblies under Articles 54–55, takes the oath of constitutional fidelity before the most senior judicial officer in the country, creating an institutional link between the executive headship and the constitutional guardian function of the judiciary.

The content of the oath is constitutionally more demanding than the Vice-President's oath under Article 69. The Vice-President swears allegiance and faithful discharge of duties — passive fidelity. The President swears to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution and the law" — a formulation that carries an active protective mandate. The difference has practical constitutional consequences. Courts have invoked the presidential oath to argue that the President is not merely a passive rubber-stamp for Cabinet decisions but retains a residual duty to scrutinise whether a proposed action would place the President in breach of the constitutional commitment to preserve and protect the Constitution.

The inclusion of "the law" alongside "the Constitution" in the oath text is significant. It extends the protective obligation beyond the Constitution's four corners to India's entire legal order — statutes, judicial precedents, treaties and customary international law — giving the oath a broad scope that reinforces the rule of law dimension of the presidential office. A President who assents to a Bill that is on its face repugnant to settled law, or who signs a proclamation that violates not only the Constitution but established statutory procedure, might be in breach of the oath's protective mandate even if the specific constitutional provision is not clearly violated.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "every President and every person acting as President or discharging the functions of the President" extends the oath requirement beyond the elected President. It applies to the Vice-President acting under Article 65, to the Chief Justice of India discharging functions under the Presidential (Discharge of Functions) Act, 1969, enacted under Article 70, and to anyone else who might constitutionally assume presidential functions. The oath follows the function: whoever exercises presidential power must first commit to its constitutional obligations. This prevents a gap in the constitutional protective mandate when the presidential office passes from one holder to another — the commitment is renewed with each transfer.

"Before entering upon his office" establishes the strict temporal condition: the oath must be taken before any exercise of presidential functions. The words "make and subscribe" require both an oral making and a written signing of the oath — both components are constitutionally mandatory. A President who has been elected, notified, and even publicly announced but has not yet taken and subscribed the oath exercises no constitutional authority.

The administering authority — the Chief Justice of India, or in the Chief Justice's absence the most senior available Supreme Court judge — is constitutionally specified, not left to convention or statute. This specificity prevents any government from substituting a more convenient or more compliant authority in an emergency. If both President and Vice-President need to take oaths simultaneously (a constitutional impossibility in practice, but imaginable in edge cases), the provision ensures a judicially administered ceremony in each case.

The oath's "to the best of my ability" qualifier on the preservation mandate is a standard constitutional formulation that acknowledges human limitation without diluting the obligation. It means the President must genuinely attempt to fulfil the protective mandate — passivity, deliberate ignorance, or calculated non-enforcement of the constitutional commitment would not satisfy the "best of ability" standard.

## The oath as a standard for presidential discretion

The most practically important dimension of Article 60's oath is how it interacts with Article 74's requirement that the President act on Cabinet advice. The two provisions create a constitutional tension: the Cabinet directs, but the President is personally committed to the Constitution's preservation. Most constitutional scholarship and judicial doctrine resolves this tension by accepting Cabinet primacy in ordinary matters while preserving the President's right — and perhaps duty — to scrutinise whether a proposed act would violate the oath's protective mandate in a material and fundamental way.

This residual discretion is not a licence for presidential policy-making or political resistance to Cabinet decisions with which the President personally disagrees. The oath is a constitutional standard, not a political one. A President who refuses to act on Cabinet advice because the President dislikes the policy makes a political decision that is inconsistent with Article 74. A President who returns a Bill or seeks reconsideration of a Cabinet recommendation because it appears to violate a specific constitutional provision exercises the Article 60 oath's protective mandate — a constitutional decision that is fully consistent with Article 74(1)'s requirement that advice be "aid and advice," not unconditional command.

## Key cases

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court, while examining the dissolution of the Bihar Assembly, invoked the President's oath under Article 60 as one basis for the proposition that the President cannot be a passive instrument of the Cabinet when asked to act in a manner that is plainly unconstitutional. The oath's command to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution" implies a residual duty of independent constitutional scrutiny that goes beyond mechanical compliance with Cabinet recommendations. The Court confirmed that the President's constitutional obligations under Article 60 are genuine and enforceable, not merely ceremonial.

**S.R. Bommai v Union of India (1994)** — The nine-judge bench's extended discussion of Article 356 included observations that the President's oath obligates the head of state to satisfy herself that the exercise of emergency powers under Article 356 is constitutionally justified, not merely to accept the Cabinet's political judgment that State constitutional machinery has broken down. The oath provides the constitutional basis for the President to examine the factual foundation for an Article 356 proclamation rather than treating Cabinet "satisfaction" as unreviewable — a position the Court endorsed in holding Article 356 proclamations subject to judicial review.

**ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla (1976)** — Justice H.R. Khanna's famous dissent — the sole dissent in the Emergency case — drew on the President's oath under Article 60 to argue that even during a proclaimed Emergency under Article 352 the constitutional order could not be suspended entirely. Khanna reasoned that the President's personal commitment to preserve and protect the Constitution was incompatible with a reading of the Constitution that allowed the President's own Proclamation to strip citizens of all legal protection against arbitrary detention. The oath provided Khanna's dissent with a constitutional anchor that operated independently of the unamended Article 21 — a foundational argument that the Constitution Bench ultimately vindicated in its 2017 nine-judge bench in the Privacy case, which overruled ADM Jabalpur.

**Kehar Singh v Union of India (1989)** — The President's power of clemency under Article 72, and the obligation to apply it according to constitutional standards rather than political preference, was examined in the context of the Article 60 oath. The Court held that the President's oath to preserve, protect and defend the Constitution and the law requires that the clemency power be exercised on the basis of relevant considerations, not arbitrarily or for purely political reasons. The oath's connection to "the law" as well as the Constitution was invoked to establish that the mercy power must be exercised lawfully.

**Constituent Assembly Debates (November 1949)** — The deliberate choice to require the oath before the Chief Justice rather than before Parliament was debated extensively in the Constituent Assembly. Proponents of a parliamentary administration argued that the President's accountability ran to Parliament through the electoral college. Proponents of judicial administration argued that the President's primary obligation was to the Constitution — a text that the judiciary interprets — rather than to any majority in Parliament. The framers adopted the judicial-administration model, a choice that has directly shaped every subsequent debate about presidential discretion under Article 60.

## Current significance

The Article 60 oath has acquired practical significance at the interface of presidential discretion and Cabinet advice. Whenever a President has returned a Bill for reconsideration under Article 111, declined to sign a proclamation immediately, or sought detailed reasons for a Cabinet recommendation, the oath's command to protect the Constitution has been invoked — by both defenders and critics of presidential action — as the constitutional anchor for the President's behaviour.

The oath's applicability to "every person acting as President or discharging the functions of the President" has become practically relevant whenever the Vice-President steps up during a presidential absence. Each such step-up requires the Vice-President to take a fresh Article 60 oath before the Chief Justice. The 1977 episode in which Vice-President B.D. Jatti took the Article 60 oath to act as President after Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed's death, and the 2022 transition in which the outgoing Vice-President briefly acted as President, both involved Article 60 oath ceremonies that renewed the constitutional protective mandate at moments of executive transition.

President Droupadi Murmu's 2025 referral of constitutional questions about gubernatorial delays to the Supreme Court under Article 143 renewed debate about how actively the President may invoke the protective mandate of Article 60 to scrutinise executive and legislative actions. The referral was read by some commentators as an exercise of the President's oath-based constitutional responsibility — that Article 60's mandate to preserve and protect the Constitution required seeking judicial clarity on a disputed constitutional question, rather than either deferring to the government's position or unilaterally resolving the question in the presidential office.
