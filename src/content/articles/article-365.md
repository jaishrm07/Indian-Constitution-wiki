  President's Rule. If a State Government fails to follow a direction that

## Why it matters

Article 365 is constitutionally unusual in that it is a deeming provision embedded in
the Constitution itself. It does not confer a fresh power; it deems a factual situation
to satisfy the condition for exercising an existing power (Article 356). Deeming
provisions in statutes are common, but a constitutional deeming provision that creates
a legal fiction of breakdown on the basis of a specific fact — non-compliance with
a Union direction — is a distinctive constitutional technique.

Article 365 occupies a precise and important position in the constitutional architecture
of emergency provisions. Article 355 imposes on the Union a duty to ensure that every
State government is carried on in accordance with the Constitution. Article 356 gives
the President the power to proclaim President's Rule when that constitutional governance
has broken down. Article 365 bridges the two by specifying one particular route through
which the breakdown condition can be treated as having arisen: a State's failure to
comply with Union executive directions.

Without Article 365, the President would always need to assess directly whether a State
government's conduct amounted to a breakdown of constitutional machinery — a complex,
contextual judgment requiring evidence about the overall state of governance in the
State. Article 365 simplifies this by nominating a specific, defined event — non-compliance
with a Union direction — as a sufficient basis for the President to make the breakdown
finding. The constitutional language is "it shall be lawful for the President to hold"
— making the failure to comply a sufficient but not necessarily exclusive basis for
the Article 356 trigger.

This efficiency has a shadow side. Articles 256 and 257, which contain the Union's
principal direction-giving powers over State executive action, are framed broadly. A
determined Centre that wished to manufacture grounds for dismissing an inconvenient
State government could therefore issue a direction it confidently expected the State to
refuse, wait for the refusal, and invoke Article 365 to trigger Article 356. The
potential for this abuse was apparent to the framers, but they judged that the
parliamentary accountability requirement — every President's Rule proclamation must
be approved by Parliament within two months — provided sufficient safeguard.

The Constituent Assembly debates show that the framers were aware of the potential for
abuse. Several members expressed concern that giving the Union the power to issue
directions and then penalise non-compliance through President's Rule gave the Centre
too much control over State governance. The eventual inclusion of the provision was
justified on the basis that the parliamentary approval requirement would discipline
the Centre's use of the provision. This justification proved inadequate in the 1970s
and 1980s when single-party majorities in Parliament gave the Centre a free hand, and
the real check only arrived with Bommai's judicialisation of the Article 356–365 link.

The SR Bommai judgment of 1994 substantially strengthened the safeguards by making the
Article 356 proclamation, including one premised on Article 365, judicially reviewable.
Courts will now examine not only whether the State actually failed to comply with a
direction, but also whether the direction was constitutionally valid, whether the
non-compliance could rationally be characterised as a constitutional breakdown, and
whether the invocation of Article 356 via Article 365 was proportionate and in good
faith. This three-stage review makes the manufactured-crisis route significantly more
difficult to sustain.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 365 is a single sentence, but its constitutional implications
ramify through the entire federal structure. The key interpretive questions are: what
counts as a Union executive direction; what counts as failure to comply; and what is
the significance of the phrase "it shall be lawful for the President to hold." Courts
have addressed each of these questions in the context of specific factual situations,
building up a body of doctrine that significantly constrains the provision's potential
for misuse.

The operative structure of Article 365 can be broken into four elements, each of which
courts have examined in litigation about President's Rule proclamations premised on
this provision.

The **first element** is the existence of a "direction given in the exercise of the
executive power of the Union under any of the provisions of this Constitution." The
direction must be a genuine exercise of Union executive power, given under an identified
constitutional provision. A mere request, suggestion, or political communication from
the Centre to a State government is not a "direction" for Article 365 purposes. The
constitutional provision under which the direction is given is relevant because that
provision itself defines the scope of the direction's legitimate subject matter — a
direction that goes beyond what the constitutional provision authorises is not a valid
direction and cannot support an Article 365 invocation.

The **second element** is failure "to comply with, or to give effect to" the direction.
This is a factual question: did the State refuse to comply, or did it fail to implement
the direction in any meaningful way? The distinction between refusal and partial
compliance, between disagreement expressed through channels and outright disobedience,
matters for the constitutional assessment. Courts have noted that the Centre must
establish actual non-compliance, not merely dispute about the direction's merits.

The **third element** is the President's assessment that "a situation has arisen in
which the Government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions
of this Constitution." Article 365 does not say that non-compliance automatically
creates this situation — it says "it shall be lawful for the President to hold" that
such a situation has arisen. This formulation preserves a degree of presidential
discretion. The President may, but need not, treat non-compliance as a constitutional
breakdown. A single direction refused on specific grounds is unlikely to constitute
the kind of systemic failure of constitutional governance that Article 356 contemplates.

The **fourth element**, required by judicial doctrine rather than the text of Article
365 itself, is the judicial reviewability of the entire chain. Post-Bommai, courts will
ask: Was the direction constitutionally valid? Was non-compliance established? Was the
President's holding that a constitutional breakdown had occurred rational and bona fide?
If any link in the chain fails this scrutiny, the resulting Article 356 proclamation
can be struck down.

The relationship between Article 365 and Articles 256 and 257 defines the substantive
scope of the direction-giving power whose breach can trigger Article 365. Article 256
requires States to exercise their executive power in such a way as to ensure compliance
with laws made by Parliament. Article 257 requires States not to impede or prejudice
the exercise of the Union's executive power. Directions given under Articles 256 and 257
extend to how the State deploys its administrative machinery in relation to Union laws,
but not to purely State subjects or matters exclusively within State legislative
competence. A direction that exceeds the constitutional scope of Articles 256–257 is
not a valid Union executive direction and cannot ground an Article 365 finding.

The relationship between Article 365 and Article 356 is one of necessary precondition,
not mechanical linkage. Article 365 supplies a factual basis for the Article 356
satisfaction; it does not mandate a proclamation whenever non-compliance is shown.
Conversely, an Article 356 proclamation can rest on grounds entirely independent of
Article 365 — the Governor's report about governance failure need not involve any
Union direction at all. Article 365 is one of multiple pathways to the Article 356
trigger, not the only one.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — In the pre-Bommai era, the
Supreme Court rejected a challenge brought by Congress-governed States against the
Janata government's plans to impose President's Rule through Article 356. The Court
held, by a six-to-one majority, that the President's satisfaction under Articles 355,
356, and 365 was primarily a political matter and that judicial review was very limited
in scope. The permissive approach effectively allowed the Centre to treat political
divergence between Centre and States as near-equivalent to constitutional breakdown —
a position substantially overruled by Bommai seventeen years later.

**S R Bommai v Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench
fundamentally recharacterised the constitutional position of Articles 356 and 365. The
Court held that Presidential satisfaction under Article 356 — including satisfaction
reached through the Article 365 gateway — is subject to judicial review for the
existence of relevant material, for rationality, and for bona fides. The bench held
that Article 365 cannot be used as a mechanical trigger: the President's "holding" that
constitutional machinery has failed must represent a genuine constitutional assessment,
not a predetermined political conclusion. The judgment is the foundational authority
for all subsequent litigation about Article 365's use as a route to President's Rule.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court struck down
the proclamation of President's Rule in Bihar in 2005. The Governor's report had cited
the political situation in the State — post-election horse-trading and instability — as
the constitutional basis for the proclamation. The Court applied Bommai and held that
the Governor's report was not based on relevant material demonstrating an actual
breakdown of constitutional governance. The Bihar proclamation was particularly
significant because it pre-empted the assembly from ever meeting; the Court held that
constitutional governance could not be said to have failed in a State whose government
had never been given the opportunity to function.

**Arunachal Pradesh President's Rule (2016) — Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker** — The
Court found that the Governor had acted unconstitutionally in advancing the Assembly
session, thereby precipitating the political crisis that was then used as the predicate
for President's Rule. The judgment reinforces the principle that the Union cannot use
its constitutional instruments — including Governors exercising executive power under
Article 163 — to manufacture the non-compliance or breakdown that Article 365 then
uses to trigger Article 356.

**In re Proclamation of Emergency in Punjab (extension litigation, 1987–92)** — The
multiple constitutional challenges to the extended imposition of President's Rule in
Punjab engaged, among other issues, whether Article 365's gateway was available once
the State had been under President's Rule for several years and whether directions given
during President's Rule itself could support a continued invocation of Article 356. The
cases produced doctrine on the outer temporal and substantive limits of using Articles
365 and 356 in combination.

**Tamil Nadu Governor case (2025) — Governor v State of Tamil Nadu** — The Supreme
Court's ruling on the Governor's refusal to grant assent to Bills reinforced the broader
constitutional principle that the Governor is a constitutional office-holder bound by
the rules of constitutional conduct. Actions by the Governor that are themselves
unconstitutional cannot provide the factual foundation for an Article 365 finding —
a Governor who manufactures a breakdown by refusing to perform constitutional functions
cannot then invoke Article 365 on the basis of the resulting dysfunction.

**West Bengal and CBI directions (2021–24)** — Disputes between the West Bengal State
Government and the Union about CBI investigations and central agency deployments raised
Article 365-adjacent questions about whether non-cooperation with central agencies
could constitute failure to comply with Union executive directions. Courts examining
these disputes noted that not every Centre-State disagreement about law enforcement
cooperation constitutes non-compliance for Article 365 purposes — the provision requires
failure to comply with specific directions given under constitutionally identified
executive power, not a general atmosphere of political non-cooperation.

## Current significance

Article 365 retains constitutional salience in the contemporary period of heightened
Centre-State friction. Several ongoing disputes involve States resisting or refusing to
implement Union executive directions in areas including law enforcement, deployment of
central agencies, implementation of centrally sponsored schemes with attached conditions,
and the exercise of concurrent legislative power. In each of these contexts, the
question of whether a State's resistance to central direction constitutes non-compliance
for Article 365 purposes is a live constitutional issue.

Post-Bommai, any Article 356 proclamation premised on Article 365 grounds must survive
judicial review at three levels: the validity of the underlying direction, the factual
establishment of non-compliance, and the rationality and proportionality of
characterising the non-compliance as a constitutional breakdown. This multi-layer review
significantly raises the threshold for a successful Article 365 invocation and has
deterred the most obvious forms of manufactured-crisis governance.

The constitutional tension between the direction-giving powers in Articles 256 and 257
— which are broadly framed to preserve Union superintendence over State executive action
in relation to Union laws — and the federal principle established as a basic structure
element in Bommai remains an unresolved structural tension. A Union that issues directions
calibrated to maximise the risk of non-compliance, in areas where State governments have
principled reasons to resist, tests the outer limits of what Article 365 can legitimately
support. Courts have signalled that such manufactured non-compliance would not satisfy
Bommai's scrutiny, but the exact line between legitimate direction-giving and coercive
manufacture of Article 365 conditions has not been judicially drawn with precision.

The Punchhi Commission on Centre-State Relations (2010) recommended that Article 365
should not be invoked without a prior warning to the State, an opportunity for the State
to explain or remedy the non-compliance, and a parliamentary examination of the direction
whose breach is alleged. These procedural safeguards would add an additional
constitutional layer between non-compliance and proclamation. The recommendation has
not been implemented by amendment, leaving Bommai's post-hoc judicial review as the
primary constitutional check.
