## Why it matters

Article 304 is the principal constitutional channel through which State Legislatures may legitimately
restrict the free flow of trade that Article 301 guarantees. It is the primary battleground of Part
XIII litigation. Clause (a) permits a State to impose on imported goods any tax that it also imposes
on comparable domestically-produced goods — parity taxation is allowed, discriminatory taxation is
not. Clause (b) permits a State to impose "reasonable restrictions" on trade in the public interest,
but only after obtaining the prior sanction of the President for the bill before it is even introduced
in the State Legislature. Without such sanction, the bill cannot be introduced, and any law passed
on the basis of such an unsanctioned bill is a constitutional nullity.

The presidential-sanction requirement in the proviso to clause (b) is a federalism safeguard: it
ensures that State-level trade restrictions with inter-State effects receive central scrutiny before
being enacted. This is one of the few instances in the Constitution where a State Legislature's power
to introduce legislation is conditioned on prior executive clearance at the national level. The
design reflects the framers' awareness that individual States have strong incentives to protect local
industry under the banner of "public interest" — the presidential-sanction filter exists precisely to
check that impulse before it crystallises into law.

The opening words — "notwithstanding anything in article 301 or article 303" — are constitutionally
significant. They signal that Article 304 is an exception carved out from both the freedom guarantee
of Article 301 and the non-discrimination mandate of Article 303. A State law that satisfies Article
304 is valid even if it would otherwise violate Articles 301 and 303. This carve-out structure means
Article 304 must be read as an exhaustive statement of the circumstances in which State Legislatures
may restrict trade freedom — there is no residual power outside its two clauses.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a) — parity taxation.** The clause permits a State to impose on goods imported from other
States or Union territories any tax to which "similar goods manufactured or produced in that State
are subject." The operative concept is similarity — courts ask whether the imported good and the
locally-produced good are functionally equivalent, serve the same use, and compete in the same
market. The constitutional standard is parity: the imported goods must not be subjected to a heavier
burden than comparable local goods. Small incidental differences in tax administration are tolerated,
but discriminatory outcomes are not. Clause (a) does not require presidential sanction — it is a
self-executing permission grounded in the equality principle, not in a public-interest justification
that needs central scrutiny.

**Clause (b) — reasonable restrictions in public interest.** This clause is the broader power, but
it is subject to the most demanding constitutional conditions. The restriction must be "reasonable,"
it must be "required in the public interest," and the bill embodying it must receive the President's
prior sanction before introduction. The Supreme Court has developed a multi-factor reasonableness
test: the restriction must have a genuine public purpose, must be proportionate to that purpose, and
must not be a disguised attempt to protect local industry at the expense of inter-State trade.
Restrictions that are in substance protectionist — even if they carry a public-interest label —
fail the test. Courts scrutinise whether the public interest invoked is real or pretextual, and
whether the burden imposed on inter-State trade is proportionate to the legitimate regulatory object.

**The presidential-sanction proviso.** The proviso is unequivocal: no Bill or amendment for the
purposes of clause (b) shall be introduced or moved in the Legislature of a State without the
previous sanction of the President. Three points follow. First, the sanction must be prior — obtained
before introduction, not ratified after passage. Second, the sanction is for the bill, not for the
law, meaning an amended bill may need fresh sanction. Third, courts have held that presidential
sanction obtained under one legislative session does not automatically carry over to a fresh bill in
a subsequent session. The practical consequence is that States wishing to impose trade restrictions
under clause (b) must route the draft legislation through the Ministry of Home Affairs to the
President for clearance — a process that introduces both delay and central-executive oversight.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's discussions on Article 304 (then Draft Article 277) were closely tied to
the broader debate on whether to adopt the Australian model of a "free trade" chapter or the American
model of a commerce clause. T.T. Krishnamachari argued for a framework that preserved State fiscal
autonomy — allowing States to tax imports at parity with local goods — while preventing the kind
of protectionist barriers that had fragmented the colonial economy. The presidential-sanction
requirement was introduced as a compromise to satisfy those members who feared that clause (b)'s
"public interest" power would be used by States to smuggle in protectionist measures. The sanction
mechanism was designed to ensure that the Union government could review any proposed State
restriction before it became law and could refuse sanction if the restriction was in substance a
competitive barrier rather than a genuine public-interest measure.

## Key cases

**State of Mysore v. H. Sanjeeviah (1967) AIR 1967 SC 1507** — The Supreme Court held that a State
forest regulation that effectively restricted the movement of timber across State boundaries required
the prior sanction of the President under the proviso to Article 304(b) to be valid. A law enacted
without such sanction is a constitutional nullity — void from inception, not merely voidable. The
Court confirmed that the presidential-sanction requirement is not a procedural technicality but a
substantive constitutional condition. This ruling has been consistently followed to strike down State
laws imposing inter-State trade restrictions where the presidential-sanction filter was not cleared.

**Video Electronics Pvt. Ltd. v. State of Punjab (1990) 3 SCC 87** — The Court struck down a Punjab
levy on television sets brought from other States that exceeded the levy on locally-manufactured
sets, holding that it violated Article 304(a)'s parity requirement. The decision clarified that
"similar goods" under clause (a) must be assessed by functional use and market substitutability,
and that any differential that disadvantages imported goods is unconstitutional discrimination. The
Court refused to accept that minor administrative differences in the tax machinery justified a
heavier effective burden on imported goods.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. Sriram Granites (1994) 1 SCC 591** — The Court examined a State regulation
restricting the movement of granite quarried within the State to outside buyers, and held that such
a restriction — effectively an export restriction on goods produced within the State — falls within
the ambit of Article 304(b) if it restricts trade with other States, and must comply with both the
reasonableness standard and the presidential-sanction requirement. The judgment clarified that
Article 304 governs not only measures that target imports but also measures that restrict the outward
movement of goods when those measures directly affect inter-State trade.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd. v. State of Haryana (2017) 12 SCC 1** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench
comprehensively overhauled the "compensatory tax" doctrine from Automobile Transport (1962), which
had allowed States to justify entry taxes as the price of using State infrastructure without needing
to comply with Article 304(b). The bench held the compensatory-tax test unworkable and unpredictable
and replaced it with a direct inquiry: does the levy directly and immediately restrict the free flow
of trade? If yes, it must be validated under Article 304. The ruling directly affects every State
that continues to levy entry taxes on petroleum and other non-GST items — States can no longer avoid
the Article 304(b) presidential-sanction requirement by characterising their levy as compensatory.
This is the most significant reconfiguration of Part XIII doctrine in decades.

**M/s Firma A.T.B. Mehtab Majid & Co. v. State of Madras (1963) SCR (3) 85** — An early Constitution
Bench decision applying the parity principle under clause (a) to a State sales tax that imposed a
heavier burden on imported goods than on locally-produced goods in the same category. The Court
struck down the discriminatory differential, establishing the principle that even a formally
neutral tax structure that produces discriminatory outcomes violates clause (a).

**Atiabari Tea Co. v. State of Assam (1961) SCR (1) 809** — The foundational decision that
interpreted the scope of Article 301 and confirmed that any tax directly and immediately impeding
trade must be validated under Article 304 or another Part XIII gateway. Atiabari set up the problem
that Article 304 must solve — the constitutional tension between State fiscal autonomy and the
freedom of inter-State trade — and subsequent Article 304 cases have all operated within the
framework Atiabari established.

## Current significance

Article 304 remains intensely litigated in the post-GST era for two primary categories of levies.
First, entry taxes on petroleum products: since petrol, diesel, aviation turbine fuel, and natural
gas are excluded from GST, States continue to levy entry taxes and VAT on their inter-State movement.
These levies face regular constitutional challenge under Articles 301 and 304, and the post-Jindal
Stainless framework now provides the operative test — the compensatory-tax defence is no longer
available, so States must either show that their levy does not directly and immediately restrict trade
or obtain presidential sanction for a clause (b) restriction. Second, infrastructure cess and
development levies imposed at State borders on commercial vehicles: several States maintain
toll-like charges that are challenged as indirect restrictions on freedom of trade, requiring
justification under Article 304(b) and prior presidential sanction.

The Jindal Stainless ruling has also prompted a broader re-examination of State-level agricultural
marketing regulations and municipal levies on goods entering city limits. The nine-judge bench's
clear preference for a straightforward "direct restriction" test over the complex compensatory-tax
inquiry has simplified — though not eliminated — the litigation landscape under Article 304. APMC
Acts that restrict inter-State movement of agricultural produce, State government orders requiring
compulsory procurement from local sources, and differential licensing fees for out-of-State traders
all now face a more direct constitutional scrutiny — they must either qualify as parity taxation
under clause (a) or satisfy the reasonableness and presidential-sanction conditions of clause (b).

The ongoing debate over whether petroleum products should be brought under GST would, if resolved
in favour of inclusion, largely remove the remaining Article 304 battleground for the highest-value
commodity category. Until that decision is made by the GST Council — a body whose unanimous-consent
requirement has thus far prevented petroleum's inclusion — Article 304 will remain the central
constitutional provision governing inter-State trade disputes in India's most economically
consequential commodity sector.
