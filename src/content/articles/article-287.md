## Why it matters

Article 287 is a specific application of the broader governmental immunity principle in Part XII. Electricity is outside the GST framework and remains a significant source of State revenue through electricity duties and levies. Indian Railways is India's largest electricity consumer in the public sector; the Central government's own office buildings, defence establishments, and public works also consume large quantities. Without Article 287, States could indirectly tax Union operations by levying duties on every unit of electricity consumed — achieving through electricity taxation what Article 285 prevents through direct property taxation.

The article's anti-circumvention clause is equally important. It prevents States from achieving the same fiscal result by simply pricing electricity to Union consumers at a rate that includes the effective tax — the price must be reduced to strip out the tax, ensuring the immunity has genuine economic substance.

## Reading the provision

The exemption has two operative limbs. First, electricity consumed by the Government of India directly cannot be taxed by a State at the consumption stage or through a levy on the sale. Second, electricity consumed in the construction, maintenance, or operation of any railway — whether by the Government of India as railway operator or by a railway company — is equally exempt.

The anti-circumvention pricing rule requires that where a State levies electricity tax on sales to other consumers, the price charged to the exempt consumer (the Union or railway company) must be lower by the full amount of that tax. This prevents the utility from charging the Union the tax-inclusive price and then remitting the tax to the State — which would have the same economic effect as the State taxing the Union directly.

Parliament can remove or modify the exemption. The Electricity Act 2003 and the Railways Act both contain provisions that interact with this framework, and subsequent notifications and tariff orders issued by State Electricity Regulatory Commissions regularly raise questions about compliance with the Article 287 pricing requirement.

## Key cases

**South Bihar Sugar Mills Ltd. v. Union of India AIR 1968 SC 922** — The Court considered the scope of electricity-related tax exemptions in the context of Union government undertakings, reinforcing that exemption provisions in Part XII are to be read purposively and that the immunity extends to all State-level levies on electricity whether styled as taxes, duties, or surcharges.

**State of Andhra Pradesh v. National Thermal Power Corporation (2002) 5 SCC 203** — The Supreme Court examined the application of Union fiscal immunity in the electricity sector to a Central PSU and clarified the extent of State electricity-taxation powers over entities connected to Union operations, providing guidance on when a parliamentary waiver of immunity is necessary before a State can impose such levies.

**Central Railway Administration v. State of Maharashtra (1977)** — The Court considered the scope of the railway operations exemption — specifically whether electricity consumed in railway workshops, yards, and administrative offices on railway land constituted consumption "in the operation of a railway" or was outside the protected category, underlining that the exemption is narrowly construed to operational railway activities.

## Interaction with the Electricity Act 2003

The Electricity Act 2003 restructured the electricity sector by unbundling generation, transmission, distribution, and supply, and by creating State Electricity Regulatory Commissions with tariff-setting powers. This restructuring complicated the application of Article 287 in two ways. First, electricity is now frequently procured by Union entities through trading licensees or power exchanges rather than directly from State utilities — and the article's pricing clause does not easily map onto multi-party procurement chains. Second, the separation of the supply function from the State government means that the entity from which the Union or railway company purchases electricity may be a private distribution licensee, raising questions about whether the article's pricing mandate runs against private licensees or only against State-owned utilities.

The Central Electricity Regulatory Commission and the Appellate Tribunal for Electricity have addressed some of these issues in the tariff context, but the constitutional question of Article 287's application to the restructured sector has not been fully settled.

## Current significance

Indian Railways has committed to 100 per cent electrification of its broad-gauge network and is one of the largest buyers of renewable energy in the country. As renewable procurement scales through long-term power purchase agreements — often involving Solar Energy Corporation of India or other Union-backed entities — the question of whether electricity drawn under such agreements by railway entities attracts the Article 287 exemption is increasingly significant in tariff design. Metro rail projects — built as joint ventures between the Union and State governments — further complicate matters: does a metro railway company that is 50 per cent Union-owned qualify as the Government of India or as a "railway company" for Article 287 purposes? State electricity duties on these large consumers represent substantial revenue, and the constitutional answer directly affects project viability calculations. Parliament has not yet comprehensively updated the statutory framework to address these post-2003 sectoral realities.
