## Why it matters

Article 46 occupies a pivotal position in the constitutional architecture of social transformation. The Constituent Assembly inserted it as a direct acknowledgment that political equality alone — the one-person-one-vote principle of universal suffrage — cannot undo centuries of structured exclusion. Ambedkar and his colleagues understood that Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes had been locked out of education, property ownership, and economic participation by a combination of caste hierarchy, untouchability, and colonial indifference. The directive requires the State not merely to refrain from discrimination but to affirmatively promote — with "special care" — the interests of those groups. That language of special care distinguishes Article 46 from the general non-discrimination norms in Article 14 and situates it alongside the positive obligations in Articles 15(4) and 16(4).

The article's scope is deliberately broad. "Weaker sections" is not limited to SCs and STs; the phrase embraces all groups whose educational and economic marginalisation makes them vulnerable to exploitation. Courts have read this to include Other Backward Classes, minority communities in certain contexts, and even women in specific economic settings. The reference to protection from "social injustice and all forms of exploitation" reaches bonded labour, trafficking, discriminatory wage practices, and denial of land rights — making Article 46 a constitutional warrant for a wide range of protective legislation that might otherwise face challenge on grounds of over-classification or unreasonable restriction.

Because Article 46 sits in Part IV, it is not directly enforceable as a Fundamental Right. But it has proven to be a powerful interpretive tool. Courts read it as the constitutional purpose behind Articles 15(4) and 16(4), so legislation implementing Article 46's mandate is assessed with significant deference. It is also invoked to resolve apparent conflicts between equality norms and affirmative-action measures: the directive confirms that the Constitution itself contemplates and commands differential treatment for historically excluded groups.

## Reading the provision

The primary judicial use of Article 46 is as an aid to construing the scope of affirmative-action provisions. When the validity of reservation statutes is challenged, courts routinely treat the directive as confirming Parliament's constitutional authority and as evidence of the constitutional value that the statute is designed to advance. In Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992), the nine-judge bench treated Article 46 as one of the core constitutional commitments that justifies a system of reservations, and analysed the 50 percent ceiling on reservations against that background rather than as an absolute rule divorced from constitutional purpose.

Courts have also used Article 46 to interpret the word "backward" in Articles 15(4) and 16(4), holding that social backwardness rooted in caste discrimination is precisely what the directive contemplates. When challenges arise to the inclusion of new communities in backward-class lists or to the sub-classification of SC/ST categories, Article 46 provides the affirmative constitutional basis for the legislative exercise. The Supreme Court in State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024) drew on the underlying purpose of Articles 46 and 15(4) to uphold sub-classification within the SC list, recognising that some groups within broad categories face even deeper disadvantage.

## Key cases

**State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951)** — In the first major post-Constitution challenge to reservations, the Supreme Court struck down a Madras Government order reserving seats in state medical and engineering colleges on the basis of religion, race, and caste, holding it violated the then-unamended Article 29(2). The decision prompted the First Constitutional Amendment (1951), which inserted Article 15(4) to allow special provisions for socially and educationally backward classes and for SCs and STs. Article 46's directive was the constitutional value that Parliament invoked when inserting that amendment, cementing the link between the Part IV directive and the Part III enabling clause.

**Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992)** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Mandal Commission's 27 percent reservation for Other Backward Classes in central government services, while imposing a 50 percent ceiling on total reservations and excluding the "creamy layer" from OBC benefits. The Court treated Article 46 as a foundational constitutional commitment that provides the purposive basis for the entire reservation architecture under Articles 15(4) and 16(4). The judgment also drew on Article 46's reference to protection from social injustice to hold that reservations are not a deviation from equality but an instrument for achieving it among groups for whom formal equality has historically been meaningless.

**Ashoka Kumar Thakur v. Union of India (2008)** — The Constitution Bench upheld the Central Educational Institutions (Reservation in Admissions) Act, 2006, which mandated 27 percent reservations for Other Backward Classes in centrally funded higher-education institutions. The majority expressly relied on Article 46 to hold that the constitutional directive to promote the educational interests of weaker sections extends to higher education and provides a wide legislative discretion in designing the reservation programme. The Court also required the exclusion of the "creamy layer," drawing on Article 46's focus on those who remain deprived despite being nominally within a backward category, to ensure that the directive's benefit reaches those who most need it.

**State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench overruled E.V. Chinnaiah v. State of Andhra Pradesh and upheld the power of States to sub-classify within the Scheduled Caste list to extend preferential treatment to the most disadvantaged sub-groups. The Court grounded the ruling in the purpose of Articles 46 and 16(4) — to ensure that the constitutional mandate of special care translates into tangible educational and economic advancement for those at the bottom of the social hierarchy, and not merely into benefits captured by relatively better-placed members of the broad SC category.

## Current significance

Article 46 remains central to ongoing controversies about the design and limits of reservation policy. Debates about whether to extend reservations to the private sector, whether economic criteria alone can justify affirmative action under the Economically Weaker Sections quota inserted by the 103rd Amendment, and whether the 50 percent ceiling is constitutionally absolute all involve the directive's mandate. The Supreme Court's 2022 judgment upholding the EWS quota (Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India) generated sharp debate about whether affirmative action motivated solely by economic status, without reference to social discrimination, is consistent with Article 46's framework.

The directive is also active in litigation about the Prevention of Atrocities Act, bonded labour, land reform, and forest rights of tribal communities. Courts invoke it to assess the adequacy of State action in implementing welfare programmes for SCs and STs, and it underpins constitutional arguments about the government's positive obligations to provide scholarships, pre-matric and post-matric educational support, and hostel facilities. As debates about data-collection on caste and the demand for a caste census intensify, Article 46's mandate of special care grounded in accurate identification of disadvantage becomes more rather than less significant.
