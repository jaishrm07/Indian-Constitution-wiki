## Why it matters

Fundamental rights are meaningless without an enforcement mechanism, and enforcement requires a defendant. Article 12 identifies who that defendant can be. The provision is not simply administrative housekeeping — it is the gate through which every Part III claim must pass. A citizen who has been dismissed from a statutory corporation, denied admission to a government-funded institution, or had their contract cancelled by a public-sector enterprise cannot invoke the equality guarantee of Article 14, the anti-discrimination mandate of Article 15, or the liberty protection of Article 21 unless the body they are challenging qualifies as "the State." Article 12 is therefore, in practical terms, one of the most consequential provisions in the entire fundamental rights chapter.

The framers drafted Article 12 with the obvious cases in mind — the Government of India, Parliament, state governments, state legislatures, and local bodies like municipal corporations and panchayats are all expressly included. The difficulty has always been the residual clause: "all local or other authorities within the territory of India or under the control of the Government of India." As India built its post-independence state through statutory corporations, public-sector enterprises, autonomous regulatory bodies, registered societies receiving state funds, and public-private hybrid entities, courts were repeatedly asked whether these bodies fell within that residual phrase. The answer required a test, and developing that test has been one of the most important ongoing projects in Indian constitutional adjudication.

The stakes are high not just for the individuals who seek to invoke Part III against these bodies, but for the constitutional design of the welfare state itself. If a statutory electricity board, an oil corporation, or a financial institution is not "the State," its employees cannot directly challenge dismissal as a violation of Article 21's due process protections. They cannot invoke Article 14's anti-arbitrariness standard against arbitrary service conditions. They are thrown back onto ordinary employment law, which may or may not offer equivalent protection. The decision about whether a body is "the State" therefore determines whether constitutional values of equality, fairness, and dignity penetrate the workplace or remain at the gate.

With the liberalisation of the Indian economy from 1991 onward, the practical importance of this question has grown rather than diminished. Privatisation, disinvestment, and contracting-out of public services have created new institutional forms — private companies with government minority stakes, special purpose vehicles for infrastructure, statutory regulators overseeing privatised sectors — none of which fit neatly into the Article 12 categories. Whether these entities are or can become "the State" for fundamental rights purposes is a live question that courts have not yet definitively resolved.

## Reading the provision

The text uses the word "includes" rather than "means," signalling that the enumerated categories are not exhaustive. The Supreme Court has consistently interpreted this as an invitation to examine whether unlisted bodies nevertheless fall within the constitutional concept of "the State." The word "includes" does significant legal work — it prevents a purely textualist reading that would confine the State to the four explicitly named categories and freeze the constitutional definition at the time of drafting.

The phrase "other authorities" is the pivotal expression. Early decisions borrowed from the ejusdem generis canon — that "other authorities" should be read as similar in kind to the specifically listed authorities — which would have confined the definition to bodies exercising governmental powers of the traditional kind. The Supreme Court in Rajasthan State Electricity Board rejected this narrow reading, and subsequent cases have moved progressively toward a functional and contextual analysis rather than a category-based one.

"Under the control of the Government of India" extends Article 12 beyond territorial jurisdiction to cover entities that may be registered abroad or may not operate within India's physical territory but are substantively controlled by the Union government. This extension has modest practical importance today but reflects the framers' awareness that the state might exercise power through formally non-governmental channels.

The phrase "unless the context otherwise requires" at the opening of the definition acknowledges that the word "State" in other parts of the Constitution may have a different meaning — in Part VI, for example, "State" refers to a constituent unit of the federation, not to all the entities covered by Article 12. The Article 12 definition is specifically for Part III purposes.

## Key cases

### The early expansion: statutory corporations as State

**Rajasthan State Electricity Board v. Mohan Lal (1967)** — The Electricity Board dismissed employees and argued that, as a statutory corporation, it was not "the State" and its employees therefore had no direct access to Part III. The Supreme Court rejected this argument emphatically. Justice Shah, writing for the Court, held that the expression "other authorities" in Article 12 is wide enough to include all authorities created by the Constitution or statute on whom powers are conferred by law, not merely those bodies that are agents of the government or exist under the direct administrative hierarchy of the Union or a State. The Electricity Board was held to be "the State" because it was created by statute, performed public functions, was financed by the state government, and was subject to state control. This decision opened the definition of State to statutory corporations generally, a major expansion of the right to invoke Part III.

**Sukhdev Singh v. Bhagatram Sardar Singh Raghuvanshi (1975)** — Three statutory corporations — ONGC, LIC, and IFC — were challenged to show whether they counted as "the State." The Court confirmed that they did. Justice Mathew elaborated a rationale: these corporations are "public authorities" discharging functions of public importance, and their power to determine the conditions of service of large numbers of employees — a power effectively backed by state authority — must be subject to constitutional discipline. The judgment deepened the instrumentality rationale: when a corporation's powers flow from a statute rather than from the consent of private parties, the exercise of those powers should be subject to the same constitutional constraints as the exercise of statutory power by any other government entity.

### The instrumentality test: Ajay Hasia

**Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib Sehravardi (1981)** — The Regional Engineering College, Srinagar, was a registered society substantially funded and controlled by the Union and state governments. Students challenged its admission process as violating Article 14. The college argued that, as a private registered society, it was not "the State." The Supreme Court, in one of the most consequential Article 12 decisions, held that it was. Justice Bhagwati articulated a multi-factor instrumentality test: if the government has pervasive financial and functional control over a body, if the body is performing a public function rather than a private commercial activity, if its management is effectively appointed or controlled by the government, and if the government's involvement goes beyond mere regulation to actual direction, then the body is an instrumentality or agency of the state and falls within Article 12. The test is not applied mechanically — no single factor is conclusive — but the combination of deep government involvement and public function character is the core of the analysis.

**Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002)** — This Constitution Bench decision arose from a challenge by a scientist employed by the Indian Institute of Chemical Biology, a registered society under the Council of Scientific and Industrial Research. CSIR was funded almost entirely by the central government, and its council was chaired by the Prime Minister. Earlier decisions had produced inconsistency — some single-judge benches had excluded registered scientific societies from Article 12 automatically. The Constitution Bench settled the law by applying the Ajay Hasia factors and holding that IICB fell within Article 12. More importantly, the Court clarified that the Ajay Hasia test is not a checklist to be applied mechanically but a holistic inquiry into whether the body is, in substance, financially, functionally, and administratively an instrumentality or agency of the government. The formal legal status — company, society, trust, or statutory corporation — is a relevant consideration but not determinative.

### The limits of the test: BCCI and private bodies

**Zee Telefilms Ltd. v. Union of India (2005)** — Zee Telefilms, a broadcaster, challenged the Board of Control for Cricket in India's grant of television rights, arguing that the BCCI exercised a public function — the control and management of cricket in India — and should therefore be treated as "the State." The Supreme Court, by three to two majority, held that the BCCI is not "the State" under Article 12. The majority emphasised that BCCI is a voluntary private association, its members are clubs and individuals, it receives no government funding and no government nominees on its board, and the government exercises no pervasive control over it. The fact that BCCI is the de facto monopoly manager of cricket in India, and that cricket is effectively a public interest activity, was not enough — the absence of structural financial and functional government control meant the Ajay Hasia threshold was not crossed. The two dissenters, Justices Santosh Hegde and S B Sinha, held that any body exercising a monopoly public function and accountable to no other public law should be treated as "the State" regardless of formal government involvement.

The BCCI case has become a significant limitation on the reach of Article 12. It confirmed that the exercise of public power — even a monopoly public function — does not by itself attract Article 12 status if government financial and functional control is absent. The consequence is that large non-state actors, from professional sports boards to accreditation bodies, may exercise enormous effective power over people's lives and opportunities without being subject to constitutional discipline under Part III.

**Federal Bank Ltd. v. Sagar Thomas (2003)** — The Court held that a private commercial bank, even one regulated by the Reserve Bank of India under the Banking Regulation Act, is not "the State" under Article 12. RBI regulation does not amount to the kind of pervasive financial and functional control that Article 12 requires. The fact that banks are heavily regulated in the public interest — and that regulation may impose obligations similar to those that apply to state entities — does not transform the bank into a state actor. The decision drew a clear line between regulation (which does not make the regulated entity "the State") and instrumentality (which does).

## Current significance

The boundary-drawing exercise required by Article 12 has never been more pressing. The past three decades have produced a proliferation of institutional forms that sit awkwardly between the state and the private sector: public-private partnership entities managing airports, ports, and highways; special purpose vehicles created by statute to deliver infrastructure projects; statutory regulators managing privatised sectors; entities in which the government holds a minority stake but exercises effective operational control through contractual arrangements. Whether these bodies are "the State" for Article 12 purposes is litigated regularly and answered inconsistently.

The privatisation of formerly public-sector enterprises — airlines, banks, telecommunications companies — raises a distinct question that courts have not yet addressed squarely: when a previously nationalised entity that was unquestionably "the State" is divested to private ownership, does it lose its Article 12 status immediately, or does some period of transition or retained public function character maintain the status? The employees of Bharat Aluminium Company, Hindustan Zinc, or other divested entities who were hired under conditions shaped by Article 14 and Article 21 protections have a real interest in the answer.

The Ajay Hasia test, despite its age, remains the operating framework. Its multi-factor character means that no single answer is possible in advance for any new institutional form — bodies must be evaluated individually, and the inquiry is inherently fact-intensive. This creates uncertainty for both institutions seeking to plan their governance structures and for individuals considering whether to invest the time and cost of constitutional litigation. There is an argument, made with increasing frequency by constitutional scholars, that the test should be revisited to take account of the changed institutional landscape — and that the exercise of monopoly public power should be treated as a stronger indicator of "State" status, even in the absence of traditional government control.

The Supreme Court's larger jurisprudence on constitutional compliance in administrative action — the proportionality doctrine, the right to reasons, the prohibition on manifest arbitrariness — has in practice crossed the strict Article 12 boundary to some extent through statutory interpretation and common law review. But these routes are less powerful than direct constitutional rights enforcement, and the Article 12 boundary still determines whether a person can frame their claim as a fundamental rights violation and access the more potent remedies of Article 32 or Article 226.
