  56. Term of office of President.—(1) The President shall hold office for a term of five years from the date on which he enters upon his office:

  Provided that—

  (a) the President may, by writing under his hand addressed to the Vice-President, resign his office;

  (b) the President may, for violation of the Constitution, be removed from office by impeachment in the manner provided in article 61;

  (c) the President shall, notwithstanding the expiration of his term, continue to hold office until his successor enters upon his office.

  (2) Any resignation addressed to the Vice-President under clause (a) of the proviso to clause (1) shall forthwith be communicated by him to the Speaker of the House of the People.

## Why it matters

Article 56 provides the temporal architecture of the presidential office. A fixed five-year term insulates the President from the continuous political pressure that would attend a tenure subject to revocation at the pleasure of the ruling party. Without a constitutionally defined term, the executive's ability to threaten replacement would undermine the President's ability to exercise even the limited constitutional discretion the office retains — such as returning a Bill for reconsideration under Article 111 or scrutinising whether a Cabinet recommendation to proclaim an emergency under Article 352 meets constitutional thresholds. The security of tenure is not a privilege of office but a structural precondition for independent constitutional functioning.

The provision channels early termination into two legitimate routes — voluntary resignation and impeachment for constitutional violation — while closing off any third route of executive dismissal. The President cannot be removed because the government of the day has changed, because a new Parliament has been elected with a different majority, or because the incumbent has fallen out of political favour. This insulation is deliberate. India's constitutional framers, drawing on the experiences of constitutional systems where heads of state could be dismissed at will, concluded that the presidency required protection against arbitrary political removal. The only legitimate forced exit is through the Article 61 impeachment process, which requires a two-thirds majority in each House of total membership — a threshold so high it functions as much as a deterrent as a genuine removal mechanism.

The holdover clause in proviso (c) — requiring the President to remain in office past the formal expiry of the term until a successor takes the oath — is a constitutional continuity guarantee. Elections take time; swearing-in ceremonies must be scheduled; there is always a potential gap between the last day of one term and the first day of the next. Proviso (c) collapses that gap constitutionally. At no moment can India find itself without a President. This provision has been invoked on multiple occasions when general election results were delayed, or when the new President's swearing-in ceremony fell a few hours or days after the outgoing President's term formally ended.

The resignation channel is carefully routed through the Vice-President rather than the Prime Minister or the Speaker. This routing reflects a structural constitutional logic: under Article 65, the Vice-President is the person who steps in when the President's office is vacant. The Vice-President is therefore the most immediately affected constitutional officer when a resignation is tendered, and the most appropriate recipient of the written notice. The obligation to communicate the resignation immediately to the Lok Sabha Speaker serves as a parliamentary notification mechanism — Parliament is informed, but it cannot veto or delay the resignation.

## Reading the provision

The five-year term runs from "the date on which he enters upon his office" — the date of the oath under Article 60, not the date of the election or the date of notification of the result. This is a standard constitutional drafting technique: the clock starts at the actual assumption of constitutional responsibility, not at any earlier administrative stage.

The word "for" in "removed from office by impeachment for violation of the Constitution" is significant. It confines the impeachment ground to constitutional violation and excludes general political disagreement, personal misconduct unrelated to constitutional obligations, and policy differences with the government. The restriction means that an unhappy Parliamentary majority cannot fabricate a constitutional-violation charge as a pretext for political removal — though determining what counts as a constitutional violation is ultimately a political judgment made by Parliament, not a judicial determination, since Article 61 does not require any prior court finding.

Clause (2)'s requirement of "forthwith" communication from the Vice-President to the Speaker establishes an immediate notification obligation. The Vice-President has no discretion to delay, withhold, or condition the communication. The word "forthwith" has been construed in Indian statutory and constitutional contexts to mean as promptly as is practicable without any unnecessary delay. This removes the risk that the Vice-President — who would benefit from a presidential vacancy by assuming functions under Article 65 — might have any motive or authority to suppress or delay the notification.

## Key cases

**B.R. Kapur v State of Tamil Nadu (2001)** — The Supreme Court's examination of the Governor's tenure (Article 157) drew on the parallel structure of Articles 56 and 157 to hold that fixed terms for constitutional heads are a structural feature of the Indian constitutional design. The Court observed that removal outside the prescribed procedure would be constitutionally impermissible, and that the parallelism between presidential and gubernatorial tenure protections is not coincidental — it reflects a consistent constitutional philosophy about insulating heads of state from arbitrary dismissal.

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The seven-judge Constitution Bench noted that the five-year fixed term, combined with the impeachment-only route for removal, insulates the President from arbitrary dismissal by the executive. This security of tenure reinforces the distinction between the ceremonial head's independence and the political accountability of the Council of Ministers, which is continuously accountable to Parliament under Article 75 but enjoys no equivalent security.

**Union of India v Jyoti Prakash Mitter (1971)** — The Court affirmed in a related constitutional context that offices with prescribed terms and specific removal procedures cannot be terminated except in strict conformity with those procedures. The principle is directly applicable to Article 56: the constitutional prescription of a five-year term with two specific early-exit routes is an exhaustive enumeration, not a minimum floor.

**Constituent Assembly Debates on Article 56** — The debate on the term length considered both shorter terms (three years) and longer ones (seven years, as in several European republics). The five-year term was chosen as aligning with the typical parliamentary electoral cycle, so that presidential and parliamentary electoral rhythms would roughly coincide, reducing the risk of prolonged misalignment between a President elected in one political climate and a Parliament elected in a radically different one. The framers also explicitly rejected a life tenure or tenure during good behaviour, both of which would have removed presidential accountability entirely.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court confirmed in the context of gubernatorial and presidential removal that Article 56 is exhaustive: the President cannot be removed except through the Article 61 impeachment procedure. Any executive attempt to pressure a President into resignation, or to declare the office vacant for any reason not specified in Article 56, would be constitutionally invalid. The security of tenure guaranteed by Article 56 is a structural feature, not merely a convention.

**Kehar Singh v Union of India (1989)** — In considering presidential powers under Article 72, the Court noted that the President exercises office throughout the full five-year term guaranteed by Article 56 and that this tenure security gives the President the constitutional standing to perform functions like reviewing mercy petitions without fear of political reprisal for any particular decision. The term guarantee and the impeachment-only removal route together create the institutional independence necessary for the President to act as a constitutional check.

## Current significance

India has never witnessed a presidential resignation or a completed impeachment, making the five-year cycle the practical default. The only motion resembling impeachment proceedings was initiated in 1977 against President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed posthumously — a symbolic gesture reflecting outrage over the Emergency, not a formal Article 61 proceeding. The impeachment threshold — requiring a two-thirds majority of each House's total membership — is deliberately set so high that it functions more as a constitutional deterrent than a realistic political tool.

The holdover clause of proviso (c) gained direct relevance in 2022 when the outgoing President's term ended close to the date of swearing in Droupadi Murmu as her successor. The constitutional machinery ran smoothly, illustrating the practical importance of what might appear to be a merely technical safeguard. Any future scenario where a presidential election is contested before the Supreme Court under Article 71 and the litigation extends beyond the swearing-in date would test both the holdover clause and the de facto validation provision of Article 71(2) simultaneously.

The non-diminution guarantee for presidential emoluments in Article 59(4) complements Article 56's security of tenure: both provisions together ensure that the President cannot be coerced either by threat of removal or by financial pressure. This dual insulation is the constitutional foundation for whatever residual independence the presidential office retains in scrutinising Cabinet recommendations.
