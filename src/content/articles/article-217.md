  217. Appointment and conditions of the office of a Judge of a High Court.—(1) Every Judge of a High Court shall be appointed by the President by warrant under his hand and seal [on the recommendation of the National Judicial Appointments Commission referred to in article 124A], and the Governor of the State, and, in the case of appointment of a Judge other than the Chief Justice, the Chief Justice of the High Court, [shall hold office, in the case of an additional or acting Judge, as provided in article 224, and in any other case, until he attains the age of [sixty-two years:]]

  Provided that—

  (a) a Judge may, by writing under his hand addressed to the President, resign his office;

  (b) a Judge may be removed from his office by the President in the manner provided in clause (4) of article 124 for the removal of a Judge of the Supreme Court;

  (c) the office of a Judge shall be vacated by his being appointed by the President to be a Judge of the Supreme Court or by his being transferred by the President to any other High Court within the territory of India.

  (2) A person shall not be qualified for appointment as a Judge of a High Court unless he is a citizen of India and—

  (a) has for at least ten years held a judicial office in the territory of India; or

  (b) has for at least ten years been an advocate of a High Court *** or of two or more such Courts in succession.2***

## Why it matters

Article 217 is the principal provision governing who becomes a High Court judge
and on what terms. Its importance goes beyond the mechanics of appointment: the
qualifications it prescribes, the tenure it guarantees, and the removal
procedure it incorporates together define the institutional character of the
High Court bench. An independent and professionally qualified bench is a
prerequisite for the High Court to perform its constitutional functions —
hearing writ petitions under Article 226, exercising criminal and civil
appellate jurisdiction, and superintending the subordinate judiciary under
Article 227.

The retirement age of sixty-two — five years younger than the sixty-seven
applicable to Supreme Court judges — has been a recurring source of debate.
Critics argue the gap encourages High Court judges to make elevation to the
Supreme Court a career goal, creating subtle dependence on those who control
that process. Defenders of the gap argue that the bench benefits from judges
at the height of their energy and that regular turnover improves institutional
freshness.

## Reading the provision

Article 217(1) requires three consultees before the President can appoint a
High Court judge: the Chief Justice of India, the Governor of the State, and
— for appointments other than the Chief Justice — the Chief Justice of the High
Court concerned. After the Second Judges Case (1993), the collegium's
recommendation is binding; the President may return it for reconsideration but
must ultimately act on a reiterated recommendation.

The qualification requirements in Article 217(2) set a minimum bar but not a
ceiling. A person must be an Indian citizen with at least ten years in judicial
office or as a High Court advocate. The explanation to clause (2) allows periods
of judicial office, advocacy, and positions requiring special legal knowledge to
be combined in computing the ten-year threshold. There is no maximum age for
appointment, though the collegium would not recommend a person appointed a day
before the sixty-two-year retirement age.

The reference to the NJAC in the current official text of Article 217 reflects
the 99th Amendment, which was struck down in 2015 in the NJAC Case. In legal
effect, the collegium process governs appointments. The official text therefore
presents a provision that is constitutionally inoperative in its current form —
a technical anomaly not corrected by formal re-amendment.

## Key cases

**S.P. Gupta v Union of India (First Judges Case, 1981)** — A seven-judge bench
held that the executive held primacy in judicial appointments under Article 217
and that the Chief Justice of India's opinion did not bind the government. The
decision facilitated the government's practice of displacing inconvenient judges
through transfers and declining to confirm additional judges, triggering the
judicial response that followed in the Second Judges Case.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (Second Judges
Case, 1993)** — A nine-judge bench overruled S.P. Gupta and established that
the collegium has binding authority over High Court appointments under Article
217. The President may return a recommendation for reconsideration but is bound
by a reiterated recommendation. This case established the collegium system that
continues to govern appointments.

**In Re Special Reference No. 1 of 1998 (Third Judges Case)** — The Presidential
Reference clarified the composition of the collegium for High Court appointments
as the Chief Justice of India and the four senior-most Supreme Court judges.
A recommendation made without proper collegium consultation has no constitutional
validity.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (NJAC Case,
2015)** — A five-judge bench struck down the 99th Constitutional Amendment and
the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act, 2014. The Court held that
judicial primacy in appointments under Article 217 is part of the basic
structure and that the NJAC, by giving the executive an effective veto, violated
that basic structure.

## Current significance

Article 217 sits at the centre of ongoing debates about judicial accountability,
the transparency of the collegium process, and the pace at which High Court
vacancies are filled. With more than a quarter of sanctioned High Court posts
frequently vacant, delays in the Article 217 appointment process have a direct
effect on case pendency. The government's power to hold collegium
recommendations in abeyance without formally rejecting them — sometimes for
years — is a recurring friction point between the executive and the judiciary.

The controversy over the transfer of High Court judges has been closely linked
to Article 217. The First Judges Case revealed that the government had used
the non-reappointment of additional judges as a pressure mechanism. Chief
Justice A.N. Ray's supersession of three senior judges for elevation to Chief
Justice of India in 1973 — a direct catalyst for the judicial independence
litigation that followed — cast a long shadow over the Article 217 process.

The five-year gap between the High Court retirement age of sixty-two and the
Supreme Court retirement age of sixty-seven creates a potential structural
problem for judicial independence. A judge who retires from a High Court is
eligible for post-retirement appointments to tribunals, commissions, and other
bodies by the executive. If the prospect of such appointments influences
judicial behaviour, the protections of Article 217 are partially neutralised —
a concern that courts themselves have acknowledged without fully resolving.
