  The Government of India can, by agreement with a foreign government, take on executive, legislative, or judicial functions that legally belong to that foreign territory. This is not a transfer of territory — the foreign land does not become part of India. Rather, India agrees to administer it on the foreign government's behalf or pursuant to a bilateral arrangement. ## Why it matters

Article 260 is one of the most quietly important provisions in Part XI — a constitutional mechanism designed for the messy realities of post-colonial territorial administration that no clean legislative formula can entirely resolve. India emerged from Partition inheriting a complex patchwork of sovereignty: enclaves of Pakistani or Bangladeshi territory within India's borders, Indian enclaves within Bangladesh, Portuguese and French colonial territories still operating within the subcontinent, and disputed princely-state boundaries that had not been cleanly settled at Partition.

Article 260 gives the Government of India the constitutional authority to administer foreign territory — to exercise the functions of governance there — without that territory being annexed into India or becoming a State. It separates the exercise of governmental functions from sovereignty over territory, enabling cooperative arrangements where India governs foreign-held land by agreement. This separation is crucial: territorial cession or acquisition requires constitutional amendment and parliamentary process under Article 3. Article 260 covers the intermediate case — India doing the governing without acquiring the territory.

The article also reflects a broader constitutional recognition that the Government of India may need to exercise authority outside India's formal borders — whether for pragmatic border-management reasons, for humanitarian purposes in adjacent territories, or pursuant to international treaties. The provision interacts with the Union's treaty-making power under Article 73, the executive power of the Union to conduct foreign affairs, and the parliamentary law on foreign jurisdiction that every Article 260 agreement must comply with.

## Reading the provision

**The agreement requirement:** Article 260 requires a bilateral agreement between the Government of India and the government of the foreign territory. The Government of India cannot unilaterally exercise functions in foreign territory under this article — there must be a consensual arrangement. "Government of any territory not being part of the territory of India" is broad: it includes foreign sovereign states, territories administered by foreign states but not sovereign in their own right, and potentially territories in a transitional sovereignty status. The framers drafted this language broadly to accommodate the diverse constitutional situations that existed in 1950 — Portuguese Goa, French Pondicherry, independent-but-uncertain Sikkim, and the numerous enclave situations with East Pakistan.

**The functions that may be undertaken:** The Government of India may undertake "executive, legislative or judicial" functions. This is a comprehensive grant — all three branches of governmental power in the foreign territory may be taken over by agreement. In practice, the most common forms are executive (administering public services, maintaining registers, issuing documents) and judicial (extending Indian court jurisdiction to residents of the enclave). Legislative functions — making binding law for the territory — are more politically sensitive and are less likely to be the subject of Article 260 agreements, since they would effectively amount to de facto annexation.

**The foreign jurisdiction law constraint:** Every Article 260 agreement is "subject to, and governed by, any law relating to the exercise of foreign jurisdiction for the time being in force." This is a significant limiting clause. The Government of India does not have a free hand; Parliament must have provided a legal framework for exercising foreign jurisdiction, and any specific Article 260 agreement must operate within that framework. The relevant background law includes the Foreign Jurisdiction Act 1947 (inherited from the colonial period), which regulated the exercise of jurisdiction by the Crown in territories not under direct British sovereignty. Parliament retains the power to legislate the terms on which foreign jurisdiction may be exercised, and the Government's agreements are constrained by whatever Parliament has authorised.

## The enclave problem and Article 260's context

The primary historical context for Article 260 was the enclave problem — pockets of territory that belonged to one sovereignty but were physically surrounded by the territory of another. At Partition and Independence, India inherited a complex situation of Bangladeshi (then East Pakistani) enclaves within Indian territory and Indian enclaves within Bangladesh (then East Pakistan). There were 162 such enclaves in total — 111 Indian enclaves within Bangladesh and 51 Bangladeshi enclaves within India, many with counter-enclaves and counter-counter-enclaves. Administering these patches of foreign territory — or reaching agreement on their administration — required a constitutional mechanism that Article 260 provided.

The Nehru-Noon Pact of 1958 attempted to resolve the enclave problem between India and Pakistan (covering the territory that became Bangladesh) and proposed exchange of most enclaves, but it was never implemented. The Bangladesh Land Boundary Agreement of 1974 provided for the exchange of enclaves, but the Indian government could not implement it without a constitutional amendment, since the territories involved included Indian enclaves whose transfer to Bangladesh required Parliamentary approval. After decades of negotiation, the Constitution (100th Amendment) Act, 2015, amended the First Schedule to give constitutional effect to the exchange, formally incorporating the acquired territories into India and relinquishing the Indian enclaves that were transferred to Bangladesh. Article 260 had provided the intermediate constitutional basis for the decades-long administrative arrangement in the enclaves pending this final resolution.

## Territorial exercise versus territorial cession: the Berubari distinction

Article 260 must be carefully distinguished from territorial cession or acquisition under Article 3. Article 3 deals with changes to the territory of India — forming new States, adjusting State boundaries, and changing State names. It requires parliamentary legislation. Article 260 deals with the exercise of governmental functions in territory that remains formally outside India: the territory itself does not change hands, only the governmental administration changes.

The Supreme Court's Berubari Union opinion (Re the Berubari Union and Exchange of Enclaves (1960) 3 SCR 250) drew precisely this distinction: ceding Indian territory — such as the Berubari Union, a tract of territory the Government proposed to transfer to Pakistan under the Nehru-Noon Pact — required parliamentary legislation under Article 3 or (as the Court ultimately held) a constitutional amendment. Merely exercising governmental functions in foreign territory by agreement, without altering the territorial sovereignty of either country, falls under Article 260 and the foreign jurisdiction law framework. The distinction defines the constitutional process required: Article 3 requires parliamentary legislation or constitutional amendment; Article 260 requires a governmental agreement and compliance with existing foreign jurisdiction law.

## Interaction with Article 73 and the treaty power

Article 73 gives the Union executive power extending to all matters on which Parliament has power to legislate and to the conduct of foreign affairs. The Union's power to enter into treaties with foreign states — including Article 260 agreements — flows from Article 73 read with the Union executive power. However, the Constitution does not require parliamentary ratification of treaties as a general matter (unlike in some other constitutional systems), which means that Article 260 agreements, once entered into by the Government, take effect as executive arrangements without necessarily requiring fresh parliamentary approval, so long as they comply with the existing foreign jurisdiction law. Where an agreement requires implementation through legislation — for example, extending Indian civil or criminal law to the residents of an enclave — Parliament must enact that legislation; the executive agreement alone does not create justiciable rights for individuals.

## Key cases

**Re the Berubari Union and Exchange of Enclaves (1960) 3 SCR 250** — The Supreme Court's advisory opinion on the Berubari Union and the exchange of enclaves is the foundational judicial text for understanding the constitutional treatment of territorial arrangements. While the Court's central holding addressed Article 3 and the process required for ceding Indian territory, it touched extensively on the boundary between territorial cession and exercise of governmental functions in foreign territory. The Court's analysis of how India could exercise governmental functions in territories of contested or transitional sovereignty provides the closest judicial framework for understanding Article 260's scope and its relationship to the more demanding process required for territorial alteration.

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India (1963) 1 SCR 371** — In examining the scope of Union power over territory, the Court affirmed the general principle that the Government of India's extraterritorial arrangements must have parliamentary grounding. Article 260's express foreign-jurisdiction-law requirement operationalises this principle for the specific category of extraterritorial governance arrangements. The judgment confirmed that Parliament's role in authorising the framework for foreign jurisdiction is not merely procedural but constitutionally mandatory.

**Maganbhai Ishwarbhai Patel v. Union of India (1970) 3 SCC 400** — The Supreme Court considered whether the Kutch boundary settlement with Pakistan required parliamentary approval. While the Court ultimately held that the executive power to enter into boundary agreements was within the executive's competence without parliamentary approval in that case, the judgment's analysis of the limits of executive treaty-making power and the point at which parliamentary authorisation is constitutionally required is directly relevant to the scope of Article 260 agreements. Agreements that merely adjust administrative arrangements in border areas without altering India's constitutional territory may proceed under Article 260 and Article 73 without parliamentary approval; agreements that cede Indian territory require the Article 3 legislative process or a constitutional amendment.

## Current significance

Article 260 has ongoing significance for India's administrative agreements with Bhutan, Nepal, and Bangladesh — especially the management of residual cross-boundary issues arising from the 2015 enclave exchange. As India's international legal engagements in areas such as maritime jurisdiction, cybersecurity cooperation, and cross-border environmental regulation grow more complex, Article 260 provides a constitutional foothold for arrangements under which Indian governmental agencies exercise jurisdiction or perform functions outside India's formal territory.

The provision interacts with India's treaty obligations under international law and with the scope of parliamentary law on foreign jurisdiction — a framework that has not been legislatively updated since the colonial-era Foreign Jurisdiction Act, prompting discussion about whether modern extraterritorial arrangements need a comprehensive statutory update. India's growing governance responsibilities in the Indian Ocean region — maritime patrol coordination, joint customs and immigration arrangements with SAARC neighbours, and disaster relief governance — raise Article 260 questions about whether a clearer parliamentary framework is needed for the executive to enter into comprehensive cooperative governance agreements.

The article also has relevance in situations of disputed territorial sovereignty — such as the status of certain islands in the Indian Ocean, or arrangements with territories that are not fully sovereign states. Article 260's broad language ("territory not being part of the territory of India") does not require the other party to be a recognised sovereign state, which may be relevant in future cooperative governance arrangements with sub-national entities or territories in transitional status.
