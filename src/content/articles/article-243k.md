  Article 243K does for Panchayat elections what Article 324 does for Parliament and State Assembly elections — it removes the conduct of elections from the control of the State government and vests it in an independent constitutional body. The State Election Commission has sole superintendence, direction, and control of electoral rolls and election conduct for all Panchayats. The State Election Commissioner cannot be removed except in the manner and on the grounds applicable to a High Court judge, and service conditions cannot be worsened after appointment. officialText: |-
  243K. Elections to the Panchayats.—(1) The superintendence, direction and control of the preparation of electoral rolls for, and the conduct of, all elections to the Panchayats shall be vested in a State Election Commission consisting of a State Election Commissioner to be appointed by the Governor.

  (2) Subject to the provisions of any law made by the Legislature of a State, the conditions of service and tenure of office of the State Election Commissioner shall be such as the Governor may by rule determine:

  Provided that the State Election Commissioner shall not be removed from his office except in like manner and on the like grounds as a Judge of a High Court and the conditions of service of the State Election Commissioner shall not be varied to his disadvantage after his appointment.

  (3) The Governor of a State shall, when so requested by the State Election Commission, make available to the State Election Commission such staff as may be necessary for the discharge of the functions conferred on the State Election Commission by clause (1).

  (4) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, the Legislature of a State may, by law, make provision with respect to all matters relating to, or in connection with, elections to the Panchayats.

## Why it matters

Article 243K creates the State Election Commission (SEC) as a constitutionally independent authority for the conduct of Panchayat elections. It is modelled on the Election Commission of India (ECI) under Article 324, applying the same design principle — an independent constitutional body insulated from executive interference — to the sub-State tier of democratic government. The provision answers a specific problem documented across decades of pre-Amendment Panchayati Raj experience: State governments had near-total control over the timing and conduct of Panchayat elections, and they used that control to extend the tenures of favoured bodies, delay elections in constituencies where they expected poor results, and otherwise subordinate local democracy to State-level political calculations.

Clause (1) vests the superintendence, direction, and control of electoral rolls and election conduct exclusively in the SEC. The word "vested" is important: it is the same word used in Article 324 for the ECI and in Article 53 for the executive power of the Union. Courts have consistently read it as a plenary and exclusive grant of authority, not a delegation subject to executive withdrawal. The SEC is not a subordinate body of the State government for election purposes; it is an independent constitutional institution with primary authority over its domain.

Clause (2) protects the institutional independence of the SEC through personal security of tenure for the State Election Commissioner. The removal process mirrors that applicable to a High Court judge: an address of the State Legislature supported by a specified majority, followed by investigation and report, prevents summary executive dismissal. The companion protection — that conditions of service shall not be varied to the Commissioner's disadvantage after appointment — closes a secondary route of pressure: a State government cannot reduce salary, strip support staff, relocate the office, or remove perquisites to make the Commissioner's position untenable without formally going through the removal process.

Clause (3) imposes a positive obligation on the Governor: when the SEC requests staff for election conduct, the Governor must make available what is necessary. This matters because the SEC has no independent cadre of election officials — it relies on State government personnel for polling staff, security coordination, and administrative support. A State government's refusal to deploy adequate staff is a practical veto on timely elections, and clause (3) converts that refusal from an administrative discretion into a constitutional breach.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "superintendence, direction and control" in clause (1) is a term of art that has been interpreted through extensive Article 324 jurisprudence and applied by analogy to Article 243K. Courts have held that it covers not merely the mechanical process of counting votes but the full range of decisions about when elections are held, how constituencies are divided for election purposes, how nomination and voting procedures are administered, and how disputes about electoral rolls are resolved. The State government's retained authority under clause (4) to legislate on election-related matters operates within, and subject to, this vested superintendence: State election laws are applicable to SEC operations, but the SEC administers those laws independently.

The Governor's appointment power in clause (1) is formally independent of State cabinet advice in constitutional text, but in practice the appointment reflects the advice of the State cabinet just as all other gubernatorial appointments do. This means that a newly elected State government may — and frequently does — appoint an SEC Commissioner who is expected to be sympathetic to the government's scheduling preferences. The security-of-tenure provision in clause (2) is designed to make such appointments relatively irreversible: once an SEC Commissioner is in post, the government cannot dismiss them for inconvenient independence.

The relationship between the SEC and the Election Commission of India is not hierarchical: the ECI has no superintendence over the SEC for Panchayat elections, and the SEC has no supervisory role over State Assembly elections conducted by the ECI. However, in practice there is considerable information-sharing between the two bodies, and in several States election schedules for Panchayat elections are coordinated with the ECI to avoid conflicts with the model code of conduct for Assembly or Lok Sabha elections.

Clause (4) reserves substantial legislative space for State governments: they may legislate on "all matters relating to, or in connection with, elections to the Panchayats" subject to the Constitution. This means State governments retain authority over candidate eligibility, disqualification grounds, reservation rosters, delimitation processes, and electoral dispute resolution — all within the framework the SEC administers. The boundary between permissible State legislation on election procedure and impermissible interference with the SEC's superintendence has been a recurring subject of constitutional challenge.

## Key cases

**Rajendra Singh Rana v. Swami Prasad Maurya (2007) 4 SCC 270** — The Supreme Court affirmed the constitutional independence of the State Election Commission, holding that the SEC's authority over the conduct of elections flows directly from Article 243K and cannot be subordinated to executive direction from the State government. The Court drew explicit parallel with the ECI's independence under Article 324 jurisprudence and held that the security-of-tenure provisions for the State Election Commissioner are structural guarantees of institutional autonomy that courts must actively enforce.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010) 7 SCC 202** — While primarily about OBC reservation under Article 243D, the five-judge bench's reasoning confirmed that the SEC retains constitutional authority to proceed with elections on the schedule mandated by Article 243E even when State governments seek postponement on grounds of pending reservation exercises. The ruling confirmed that the SEC's superintendence of election conduct is not displaced by unresolved legislative or policy questions at the State level, and that the SEC's constitutional mandate requires it to exercise independent judgment rather than await executive clearance.

**State of Uttarakhand v. Balwant Singh Chaufal (2010) 3 SCC 402** — The Supreme Court emphasised that the SEC's constitutional mandate is not fulfilled by merely receiving instructions from the State government: it requires the SEC to exercise independent judgment about when and how elections are conducted, consistent with Part IX's constitutional framework. The Court held that an SEC that defers indefinitely to State government preferences about election timing is not discharging its constitutional function.

**Anugrah Narayan Singh v. State of UP (High Court, 2019)** — The Allahabad High Court addressed an attempt by the State government to alter the service conditions of the incumbent State Election Commissioner after appointment by reducing the administrative support and budgetary allocation of the SEC office. The Court held that this constituted a variation of service conditions to the Commissioner's disadvantage after appointment, in violation of the proviso to Article 243K(2), and directed the State to restore the prior position.

**Ashok Kumar Yadav v. State of Haryana — SEC staffing disputes** — In a series of cases, High Courts have ordered State governments to deploy the staff requested by State Election Commissions for election conduct, holding that clause (3)'s obligation to make staff available when requested is not discretionary. The refusal to deploy adequate police or administrative personnel for election duty — a common State government tactic to create practical obstacles for the SEC — has been held to be a constitutional breach separate from any question of formal dissolution or delay.

## Current significance

The independence of State Election Commissions has come under sustained pressure in several States. The most common pattern involves State governments delaying the finalisation of OBC reservation rosters or ward delimitation exercises, which they then present to the SEC as an impediment to holding elections within the Article 243E window. Courts have repeatedly held that the SEC must proceed with elections even without completed reservation rosters if the constitutional timeline is about to be breached, but State governments have in some cases declined to provide the necessary administrative support unless their preferred election schedule is accepted.

A second recurring controversy involves the appointment and removal of State Election Commissioners. Because appointment is by the Governor on cabinet advice, newly elected governments have sometimes sought to replace incumbent SECs whose independence is perceived as politically inconvenient. The High Court removal protection in Article 243K(2) has provided the primary defence against such removals, but the protection has occasionally been tested through indirect means — including attempts to downgrade the Commissioner's rank, relocate the office, or constitute the SEC with additional members who dilute the incumbent Commissioner's authority.

A third pressure point involves the relationship between State governments and the SEC over model code of conduct. Unlike the ECI, which has developed an extensive and well-tested model code for Parliamentary and Assembly elections, SECs operate under less uniform procedural frameworks. When Panchayat elections overlap with Assembly or Lok Sabha election schedules, States have argued that the ECI's model code takes precedence and displaces the SEC's authority — an argument courts have generally rejected, holding that the two commissions operate over different elections and neither has supervisory authority over the other.
