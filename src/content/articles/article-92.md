officialText: |-
  92. The Chairman or the Deputy Chairman not to preside while a resolution for his removal from office is under consideration.—(1) At any sitting of the Council of States, while any resolution for the removal of the Vice-President from his office is under consideration, the Chairman, or while any resolution for the removal of the Deputy Chairman from his office is under consideration, the Deputy Chairman, shall not, though he is present, preside, and the provisions of clause (2) of article 91 shall apply in relation to every such sitting as they apply in relation to a sitting from which the Chairman, or, as the case may be, the Deputy Chairman, is absent.

  (2) The Chairman shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the Council of States while any resolution for the removal of the Vice-President from his office is under consideration in the Council, but, notwithstanding anything in article 100, shall not be entitled to vote at all on such resolution or on any other matter during such proceedings.

## Why it matters

Article 92 solves a structural conflict that the Constitution creates by making the Vice-President the Chairman of the very chamber that must vote on the Vice-President's removal. Without Article 92, the Chairman would control every aspect of the debate on their own removal — who speaks, how long they speak, whether points of order are sustained, and whether proceedings can be adjourned. A constitutional mandate for recusal ensures that removal proceedings under Article 67(b) can be conducted fairly, with a neutral presiding officer determined by the Article 91(2) mechanism. The article thus preserves the integrity of the upper chamber's most significant accountability act.

The natural-justice principle at the core of Article 92 is one of the oldest and most universal requirements of fair process: a person should not judge their own cause. The Constitution does not leave this principle to convention in the context of presiding officers — it codifies the recusal requirement explicitly, making the obligation mandatory rather than a matter of discretionary good conduct. This reflects the framers' recognition that presiding officers, by virtue of the powers they hold, are peculiarly capable of subverting proceedings directed against them if not expressly constrained.

The article is also important for what it reveals about the constitutional theory of presiding-officer powers. The removal proceedings under Article 92 are among the very few parliamentary proceedings in which a presiding officer's conduct is directly subject to constitutional override — the affected officer is not merely expected to exercise their powers impartially but is constitutionally stripped of those powers for the duration. This is a constitutional acknowledgment that the appearance of impartiality, not merely its substance, is required when a presiding officer's own removal is at stake.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) applies the recusal rule to two distinct situations — removal of the Vice-President (requiring the Chairman to step aside) and removal of the Deputy Chairman (requiring the Deputy Chairman to step aside). Both are governed by the same principle: the affected officer "shall not, though he is present, preside." The recusal is mandatory and self-executing — it does not depend on the affected officer's own judgment about impartiality, nor does it require a ruling from the House or from a court. The word "shall" imports an absolute obligation.

In both cases, Article 91(2)'s ordinary-absence succession mechanism fills the presiding gap, treating the recusal as equivalent to the presiding officer being absent from the sitting. This means the Deputy Chairman steps in when the Chairman is recused under clause (1), and the Rules-of-Procedure substitution mechanism activates when the Deputy Chairman is the one recused. The House never needs to make an ad hoc appointment or conduct a vote on who shall preside — the succession is constitutionally predetermined.

Clause (2) specifies what the Chairman retains during Vice-Presidential removal proceedings — the right to speak and participate (including making submissions, responding to allegations, and defending the conduct in office) but absolutely no right to vote. The phrase "notwithstanding anything in article 100" makes this an overriding restriction: even the Chairman's casting vote in a tie is eliminated. The provision thus allows the Chairman to be heard but not to control the arithmetic of the removal vote.

The asymmetry between clause (1) and clause (2) is notable. Clause (1) covers both the Chairman and the Deputy Chairman facing removal, but clause (2) — which specifies the retained right to speak and the elimination of voting rights — mentions only the Chairman. This is because the Deputy Chairman, facing removal under Article 90, is an elected Rajya Sabha member who would in any case retain voting rights as a member. The Deputy Chairman who is not presiding can participate in the proceedings in the same way as any other member, subject to the standard parliamentary rules. The special treatment in clause (2) is needed only for the Chairman because the Chairman is the Vice-President, who is not otherwise a voting member of the Rajya Sabha and whose participation and voting rights during removal proceedings need to be explicitly addressed.

## The connection to Vice-Presidential removal under Article 67

Article 92 is the procedural implementation of Article 67(b)'s removal mechanism. Removing the Vice-President requires the Rajya Sabha to pass a resolution by absolute majority and the Lok Sabha to agree. The Rajya Sabha's deliberation on that resolution would be fatally compromised if the person being removed were also controlling the deliberation. Article 92 prevents that outcome by mandating recusal and specifying precisely what the affected officer may and may not do.

The absolute-majority requirement in Article 67(b) ensures that only a genuine, sustained political majority can remove the Vice-President. Article 92 ensures that this majority is assembled and counted in a procedurally fair environment. The combination of the substantive threshold (absolute majority) and the procedural protection (mandatory recusal) creates a removal mechanism that is both difficult and fair — appropriately calibrated for the removal of a constitutional officer who holds significant institutional authority.

The Vice-President's removal requires Rajya Sabha initiation with fourteen days' notice and then Lok Sabha concurrence. During the Rajya Sabha's deliberation, Article 92 operates to keep the Chairman from controlling the process. During the Lok Sabha's concurrence stage, the Vice-President as Chairman has no role at all — the Lok Sabha is not subject to Article 92 in the same way, though the natural-justice principle underlying the article would apply by analogy.

## Rajya Sabha practice during removal proceedings

In India's constitutional history, no Vice-President has ever been removed from office, and removal resolutions have been rare. The practical operation of Article 92 has therefore been worked out more in theory and in State-level analogues than in direct Rajya Sabha experience. The article's requirements have been tested in State Legislative Council contexts, and the principles developed there — including the procedural consequences of a presiding officer's mandatory recusal — apply by constitutional analogy to the Rajya Sabha.

The Rajya Sabha's Rules of Procedure do address the scenario of the Chairman's recusal under Article 92, directing that the Vice-Chairman or a member of the panel of Vice-Chairpersons shall preside. This procedural specification ensures that the Article 91(2) succession mechanism is operationalised in a principled way even in the unusual removal-proceeding context.

## The Nabam Rebia extension

The Supreme Court's 2016 judgment in Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker developed the constitutional logic of Articles 92 and 96 into a broader principle. The Court held that once a notice for removal of a presiding officer is admitted, the presiding officer cannot exercise discretionary, quasi-judicial powers — specifically the Tenth Schedule disqualification power — that could affect the composition of the House and thereby the outcome of the removal vote. This extension operates from the moment the notice is admitted, not only during the formal consideration of the removal resolution in a sitting.

The Nabam Rebia principle generalises Article 92's specific recusal requirement into a broader constraint on presiding officers facing accountability proceedings. The Court's reasoning was that the purpose of the recusal rule — ensuring that a presiding officer cannot use procedural power to defeat the mechanism of accountability — would be defeated if the constraint applied only to the physical act of presiding but not to substantive decisions taken before the formal removal sitting. A presiding officer who disqualifies the members most likely to vote for removal, before the removal sitting occurs, could effectively pre-empt the accountability mechanism.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Constitution Bench held that once a notice for removal of a presiding officer is admitted, the presiding officer cannot exercise discretionary powers — particularly the power to disqualify members under the Tenth Schedule — that could affect the composition of the House and thereby the fate of the removal resolution. This principle generalises the specific Article 92 recusal rule into a broader constitutional constraint on presiding officers facing accountability proceedings, applicable even before the formal debate on removal begins.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** — The bench noted that the constitutional design of presiding-officer articles, including Article 92, establishes that presiding officers have a constitutional role distinct from partisan interests, and that the removal mechanism is part of that accountability structure. The existence of Article 92 was one element of the bench's reasoning for why the extensive powers vested in presiding officers under the Tenth Schedule did not violate fundamental rights — the office-holders remain accountable to the chamber they preside over.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Court's extended analysis of presiding officer powers and their constitutional limits engaged with recusal obligations as part of the accountability architecture, including the Article 96 Lok Sabha counterpart to Article 92. The judgment affirmed that constitutional recusal requirements are structural protections for the integrity of parliamentary proceedings, not mere courtesies.

**Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court's engagement with the Maharashtra political crisis and the Speaker's disqualification jurisdiction applied and elaborated on the Nabam Rebia doctrine, working out when and how the constraints derived from Article 92 and its Lok Sabha counterpart apply in conditions of contested parliamentary arithmetic. The case confirmed that these constraints have real-time operational significance during political crises.

## Current significance

Article 92 has not been directly litigated frequently because Vice-Presidential and Deputy Chairman removal resolutions are rare events in Indian parliamentary history. But the Nabam Rebia doctrine — which extends Article 92's underlying logic far beyond its literal text — has made the provision's principles among the most frequently invoked in contemporary parliamentary disputes. The question of when a removal notice constrains a presiding officer's discretionary powers has arisen in multiple State legislature contexts and has important implications for the Rajya Sabha whenever a Deputy Chairman faces a removal notice while anti-defection or related proceedings are pending before the chair.

The broader significance of Article 92 lies in its articulation of the principle that constitutional accountability mechanisms must be procedurally insulated from the power of the person being held accountable. This principle — that the process of accountability cannot be controlled by its subject — is one of the foundational requirements of legitimate governance. By constitutionalising it in the specific context of presiding officers, Article 92 has become the textual source from which courts have drawn and extended a general principle of Indian constitutional law about the integrity of removal and accountability procedures.

Reform proposals for India's presiding-officer framework have noted the tension between the broad adjudicative powers conferred by the Tenth Schedule and the mandatory recusal requirement of Article 92. If the Deputy Chairman's disqualification jurisdiction is suspended whenever a removal notice is pending, and if removal notices can be strategically used to prevent disqualification proceedings from moving forward, the constitutional design of using presiding officers as disqualification adjudicators may need to be reconsidered. The alternative of an independent tribunal would eliminate the Article 92 conflict of interest by removing the presiding officer from the adjudication role entirely.
