## What this Article does

Article 147 is a transitional interpretation clause that extends the meaning of "substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution" — a phrase used in Articles 132, 133, 134, and in the parallel High Court appellate jurisdiction provisions in Part VI Chapter V — to include substantial questions about the interpretation of the Government of India Act, 1935, the Indian Independence Act, 1947, and Orders in Council or orders made thereunder. Without this deeming provision, parties seeking to raise questions about the constitutional predecessor statutes might have been unable to access the special appellate routes reserved for constitutional questions.

The article reflects the reality that in 1950 a large body of legislation, executive instruments, and judicial precedent still operated under the 1935 Act framework, and that constitutional interpretation questions concerning the new Constitution would frequently be bound up with questions about the pre-Constitution legal order. Article 147 ensures that neither type of question falls into a jurisdictional gap.

## Reading the provision

The operative phrase in the articles it serves — Articles 132, 133, 134, and their High Court counterparts — is "substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution." This phrase does the work of defining the class of questions that attract the constitutionally-prescribed appellate routes rather than ordinary civil or criminal appeal routes.

A question is "substantial" if it is of general public importance or if the decision in the case depends on the answer to it, and if the question is not already settled by a binding Supreme Court precedent. It is a question "as to the interpretation" if the outcome turns on the meaning, scope, or application of a constitutional provision — not merely the application of ordinary law to facts.

Article 147's extension of this phrase to cover the Government of India Act, 1935 and the Indian Independence Act, 1947 means that a party who needed to argue before the early Supreme Court that a provision of the 1935 Act had been incorrectly understood — affecting rights that carried over into the new constitutional order — could do so within the certification framework of Articles 132 and 133, rather than being confined to ordinary civil appellate routes that did not have the same protections.

The extension to Orders in Council and orders made thereunder captured the vast body of secondary colonial legislation: from provincial acts and executive orders to adaptation orders and continuation ordinances that kept inherited law alive during the transition period. Many of these instruments raised genuine interpretive disputes about their scope and continued validity, and Article 147 ensured these were treated with appropriate constitutional seriousness.

## The concept of a "substantial question" in practice

The Supreme Court has developed a substantial body of case law interpreting what makes a question "substantial" for the purposes of the articles that Article 147 interprets. Key principles include:

**Not frivolous or already settled** — A question is not substantial if there is a direct and binding precedent of the Supreme Court resolving it. The question must be live and open in law.

**General public importance or case determinative** — The Court in Sir Chunilal Mehta and Sons Ltd. v. Century Spinning and Manufacturing Co. Ltd. (1962) held that a question is substantial if it is of general public importance or one the decision of which in the case would affect parties other than those immediately before the court, or if it involves a question of law that has not been settled by the Supreme Court.

**Not merely a question of fact** — The certification mechanism under Articles 132 and 133 is confined to legal questions; a dispute about facts, even if it turns on the application of constitutional provisions, does not attract certification. Article 147 imports this restriction into the predecessor-statute context as well.

**Constitutional nexus required** — For Article 147's extended meaning, the question must relate to the interpretation of the predecessor statutes in the sense of their constitutional character — what the 1935 Act conferred, what it preserved, what it displaced. Ordinary commercial disputes arising under a pre-1950 contract, even if governed by legislation enacted under the 1935 Act, do not become constitutional questions simply because the legislation was made under colonial authority.

## Key cases

**Garikapati Veeraya v. N. Subbiah Choudhry (1957) SCR 488** — The Supreme Court interpreted the scope of pre-Constitution appellate rights in light of the transitional framework including Article 147, confirming that the right of appeal is a vested right that accrues at the commencement of the suit and is governed by the law in force at that time. Questions about the 1935 Act regime for appellate jurisdiction were properly treated as constitutional questions falling within the Article 147 extended definition.

**State of Bombay v. United Motors (India) Ltd. (1953) SCR 1069** — An early case in which the Supreme Court was required to interpret provisions carried over from the 1935 Act into the Constitution. The Court's willingness to engage with the pre-Constitution legislative history illustrated that Article 147's deeming provision had immediate practical operation in the first years after commencement, enabling the Court to treat questions about the predecessor statute's meaning as constitutional questions.

**Sir Chunilal Mehta and Sons Ltd. v. Century Spinning and Manufacturing Co. Ltd. (1962) Supp 3 SCR 549** — The Supreme Court laid down authoritatively the criteria for what constitutes a "substantial question of law," the concept that Article 147 extends to cover the predecessor statutes. The judgment established the two-limb test — general public importance or case determinativeness — that courts apply when deciding whether to certify an appeal on a substantial question.

**Bhagwati Prasad v. Chandramaul (1966) 2 SCR 286** — The Court reiterated that the substantial-question standard is not satisfied merely because a legal point arises that is debatable; there must be a genuine difficulty or doubt about the applicable law, and it must matter to the outcome of the case. This standard, interpreted by Article 147 to extend to predecessor statutes, thus filters out routine appeals dressed up as constitutional questions.

## Current significance

Article 147 is today of primarily historical importance because the Government of India Act, 1935 has been comprehensively displaced and very few live disputes turn on its interpretation as such. However, it retains latent relevance in cases involving pre-1950 land grants, princely-state accession instruments, inam tenures, and Orders in Council that continue to have legal effect through saving provisions in Articles 372 and 374 of the Constitution.

Constitutional historians and comparative lawyers also value the article as evidence of how the Constituent Assembly handled the transition from colonial constitutional law to the new republican framework, choosing continuity over rupture wherever possible. The framers were aware that an abrupt legal rupture — treating the 1935 Act as if it had never existed — would have created enormous uncertainty about vested rights, pending appeals, and continuing statutory obligations. Article 147 was one of several transitional provisions designed to ensure that the new constitutional order absorbed, rather than annihilated, the pre-existing legal order to the extent consistent with constitutional principles.

For contemporary constitutional theory, Article 147 also illustrates that the concept of a "substantial question of law as to the interpretation of the Constitution" is not confined to the text of the 1950 Constitution but extends to the constitutional tradition of which it forms part — a reminder that constitutional interpretation is always partly an exercise in historical continuity.
