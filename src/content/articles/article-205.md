  205. Supplementary, additional or excess grants.—(1) The Governor shall—

  (a) if the amount authorised by any law made in accordance with the provisions of article 204 to be expended for a particular service for the current financial year is found to be insufficient for the purposes of that year or when a need has arisen during the current financial year for supplementary or additional expenditure upon some new service not contemplated in the annual financial statement for that year, or

## Why it matters

The annual budget is an estimate made months before the financial year begins and covers twelve months of future government activity. It cannot be a perfect prediction. Disaster relief costs, public salary revisions following Pay Commission awards, policy changes requiring new expenditure, unexpected debt service obligations, and a hundred other contingencies routinely make the original appropriation insufficient. Article 205 provides the constitutional mechanism for managing this reality without compromising the legislature's control over the public purse: the government must return to the Legislature, present its revised requirements, and obtain fresh legislative authorisation.

This is the essential complement to Article 204's absolute prohibition on non-appropriated withdrawals from the Consolidated Fund. Without Article 205, a State government that needed more money than its original appropriation would have no constitutional mechanism to spend it mid-year; with Article 205, it has a structured, transparent mechanism that keeps the legislature informed and in control of all departures from the original budget. The mechanism's value is precisely that it makes mid-year budget adjustments visible and legislatively sanctioned, rather than achieved through executive discretion or quiet accounting adjustments.

The excess grant mechanism — for cases where money has already been spent in excess of the grant — performs a different but equally important accountability function. By requiring post-facto legislative approval of overspending, it completes the constitutional audit loop: the CAG identifies excess expenditure in its annual audit report, the Public Accounts Committee examines the circumstances, the Assembly must vote an excess grant to regularise the position, and the executive faces legislative scrutiny of why it spent more than it was authorised to. In practice this process often runs with significant delay, but the constitutional requirement remains operative and has been confirmed by the Supreme Court as a constitutional obligation, not a discretionary housekeeping exercise.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)(a) — supplementary and additional demands**: Two situations trigger the clause (1)(a) mechanism. First, where the appropriation for an existing service is insufficient for the current year — the government needs more money for a service it is already providing. Second, where a new service has arisen during the year that was not contemplated in the annual financial statement — the government needs to start spending on something entirely new. In both cases, the Governor causes a supplementary financial statement to be laid before the Legislature, and the full budget procedure of Articles 202, 203, and 204 applies to that statement. The Governor's recommendation is required, demands must be voted by the Assembly, and a Supplementary Appropriation Bill must be passed.

The distinction between supplementary (more money for an existing service) and additional (money for a new service not previously contemplated) is important for understanding the scope of legislative oversight. A new service represents a policy departure from the original budget — a decision to spend public money on something the Legislature did not approve when it voted the original demands. The Legislature's opportunity to scrutinise and potentially refuse a new service demand is therefore particularly significant as a check on mid-year executive policy-making.

**Clause (1)(b) — excess demands**: This is constitutionally distinct from supplementary demands. Here, the money has already been spent — in excess of the grant voted by the Assembly. The government is coming to the Legislature after the fact, seeking ratification of expenditure that was incurred without legislative authority. The Governor presents a demand for the excess to the Assembly, and the Assembly votes on whether to regularise it. The Assembly may refuse to vote an excess grant, though in practice governments with a majority ensure these are passed. The constitutional significance is that excess expenditure is not simply forgiven administratively — it requires a specific legislative act to become constitutionally valid.

**Clause (2) — full budget procedure applies**: The supplementary and excess demands are subject to the same constitutional requirements as the original budget. This means the financial statement under clause (1)(a) must satisfy the requirements of Article 202; demands under Article 203 must receive the Governor's recommendation; and a Supplementary Appropriation Bill under Article 204 must be passed before the additional money is drawn from the Consolidated Fund. There is no abbreviated procedure for supplementary demands — each one is a mini-budget requiring full legislative processing.

## Comparison with Article 115 (Union)

Article 205 is the State equivalent of Article 115 covering supplementary, additional, or excess grants for the Union. The three categories — insufficient appropriation, new service, excess expenditure — are identical. The procedural requirements — Governor or President recommendation, demand-for-grant procedure, Appropriation Bill — mirror the Union process. The State Finance Minister presents the supplementary budget in the Assembly, as the Union Finance Minister presents supplementary demands in the Lok Sabha. The main practical difference is in the scale and frequency: Union supplementary demands typically run to multiple tranches across the financial year, while State supplementary demands vary considerably by State budgetary discipline.

## The excess grant mechanism and public accountability

The excess grant mechanism in clause (1)(b) is the constitutional response to a specific form of executive overreach: spending more public money than the legislature has authorised. Its constitutional significance is that it treats this overreach not as an administrative irregularity to be quietly regularised within the finance department, but as a matter requiring formal legislative action — a demand that must be presented, scrutinised, and voted upon.

The process depends on the CAG's audit function. The Comptroller and Auditor General examines the accounts of the State Government and prepares an audit report that is laid before the State Legislature. If the CAG finds expenditure in excess of the appropriation, that finding triggers the Article 205(1)(b) obligation: the Governor must cause a demand for the excess to be presented to the Assembly. The Public Accounts Committee then examines the excess and the circumstances that caused it before the demand comes to a vote.

In practice, the regularisation process frequently runs years behind the financial year in which the excess was incurred. States with large volumes of excess expenditure may present omnibus regularisation demands covering multiple years. This delay reduces the accountability value of the mechanism — the executive management decisions that produced the excess are remote in time from the legislative review — but the constitutional requirement remains, and courts have confirmed that indefinite delay in presenting excess demands is a breach of the constitutional obligation.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor General of India v Kamlesh Vadilal Mehta (2003) 2 SCC 349** — The Supreme Court held that excess expenditure without legislative sanction is a constitutional violation, not merely a financial irregularity, and that the CAG's duty to report such excess and the legislature's obligation to regularise it through the excess grant mechanism of Article 205 are constitutional requirements. The judgment established that the audit-accountability chain — CAG report, Public Accounts Committee scrutiny, legislative regularisation — is constitutionally mandated, not optional, and that the State government's failure to present excess demands within a reasonable time is itself a constitutional default.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v Synthetics and Chemicals Ltd (1991) 4 SCC 139** — The Court's observations about the binding character of appropriation law on the executive reinforce the principle underlying Article 205's excess grant mechanism: if spending exceeds authorised amounts without a valid excess grant, it cannot be retrospectively validated by executive action. The legislature must formally regularise excess expenditure through the constitutional mechanism this article provides, and the executive cannot treat excess spending as automatically valid simply because it has acknowledged the excess in its accounts.

**Re Allocation of Spectrum (2G Spectrum Case) (2012) 3 SCC 1** — Although arising under the Union framework, the Supreme Court's insistence that all public finance decisions must have proper legislative authorisation reinforces the principle that departures from the original budget — whether in amount or in the nature of service — must be brought back to the legislature for approval under the equivalent of Article 205. Executive decisions that change spending priorities without supplementary demand approval risk constitutional invalidity of the resulting expenditure.

**Karnataka Public Accounts Committee Reports (various, Karnataka High Court)** — Karnataka High Court and CAG proceedings arising from PAC reports have confirmed that the delay in presenting excess demands does not extinguish the constitutional obligation — the demand for excess must still be presented and voted, and the longer the delay, the more seriously the failure engages the legislative accountability function of Article 205. Courts have held that states cannot treat systematic excess expenditure as a routine administrative matter when it engages the constitutional requirement of legislative regularisation.

**Andhra Pradesh v Comptroller and Auditor General (various High Court proceedings)** — Andhra Pradesh High Court cases examining the relationship between the CAG's audit findings and the State government's obligation to present regularisation demands under Article 205 confirmed that the audit-legislative accountability relationship created by Article 205 is constitutionally mandated. The government cannot simply note audit objections and respond administratively — the constitutional mechanism requires the legislature to formally authorise or refuse the excess.

## Current significance

Supplementary demands are a regular and substantial feature of State budgets, often presented in two or three rounds during the financial year. Their aggregate volume across Indian States has grown considerably, reflecting both the dynamic character of State expenditure needs and, in some States, a pattern of systematic under-budgeting in the original estimates. When supplementary demands consistently exceed a large fraction of the original budget, questions arise about the quality of initial budgeting and the adequacy of legislative oversight — if the original budget is known to be an underestimate, the legislature's scrutiny of it at the start of the year is meaningfully reduced.

The constitutional significance of Article 205 in contemporary governance extends beyond the purely technical. State governments that use supplementary demands to fund large policy decisions — welfare scheme expansions, salary revisions, emergency relief — after the legislative session where the original budget was scrutinised may be effectively bypassing meaningful Assembly debate on those policy choices. The procedural equivalence between supplementary demands and original demands under clause (2) is the constitutional answer to this concern: the Assembly's scrutiny powers are the same, even if the political reality of passing supplementary demands before a government with a majority is different from the public scrutiny surrounding the original budget.

The excess grant mechanism, when invoked, serves as an important accountability check requiring post-facto legislative approval of overspending. States maintaining current excess grant regularisation procedures — with demands presented promptly after CAG audit and examined by an active Public Accounts Committee — create a genuine accountability loop. States that allow excess demands to accumulate over years without presentation undermine the constitutional architecture, and the Supreme Court's clarification in Kamlesh Vadilal Mehta that this is a constitutional obligation rather than an administrative discretion provides the basis for judicial intervention where the obligation is systematically ignored.
