## Why it matters

Maharashtra and Gujarat are among India's largest and most economically diverse States. Both contain sub-regions whose historical trajectories, infrastructure levels, and access to government services differ markedly from the dominant metro-coastal areas. Article 371 is one of the few constitutional provisions that mandates the creation of intra-state regional development institutions and requires annual legislative accountability for their work. It reflects the founding insight that formal State boundaries, drawn on linguistic lines in 1960, do not automatically produce equitable development across all parts of a new State.

The political context of the article is essential. Bombay State was reorganised on 1 May 1960 into Maharashtra and Gujarat under the Bombay Reorganisation Act. The reorganisation was contested bitterly — it followed years of agitation and the police firing on the Samyukta Maharashtra movement — and the new States inherited deep internal disparities. Vidarbha had been part of the Central Provinces and Berar before 1956 and had its own distinct administrative culture; Marathwada came from the Hyderabad State. The demand that these regions not be absorbed invisibly into a Pune-Mumbai axis produced the constitutional guarantee that the Governor would have standing responsibility for sub-regional equity. In Gujarat, Saurashtra and Kutch had their own princely-states histories and legitimate anxieties about investment being drawn exclusively to Ahmedabad and the mainland.

The demand for a separate Vidarbha State has never fully subsided, and Article 371 is central to the political argument both for and against it. Those opposing separate statehood argue the existing constitutional mechanism is sufficient if properly implemented; those favouring it argue that the mechanism has failed and that only the incentives that come with a separate State will produce genuine equity. This live political contestation gives Article 371 a continuing salience that many Part XXI provisions lack.

## Reading the provision

The original Article 371 addressed Bombay State before its bifurcation; clause (1) has been omitted and is represented by asterisks in the official text. The operative provision is clause (2), which is enabling rather than self-executing. It allows the President, by order, to impose a special responsibility on the Governor of Maharashtra or Gujarat across three domains.

**Domain 1 — institutional (clause 2(a)):** The Governor must ensure the establishment of separate development boards for each recognised sub-region: Vidarbha, Marathwada, and the rest of Maharashtra for that State; Saurashtra, Kutch, and the rest of Gujarat for that State. Each board must place an annual report before the State Legislative Assembly, creating a formal accountability link between the sub-regional body and the legislature. "Separate" boards matters — a single integrated board could subsume regional voices; separate boards give each sub-region a distinct institutional identity.

**Domain 2 — fiscal (clause 2(b)):** The Governor is to ensure equitable allocation of developmental expenditure across these sub-regions, subject to the State's overall requirements. This "subject to overall requirements" qualification means the provision does not mandate a fixed formula or ring-fenced sub-regional budget share. It imposes a duty to pay attention to sub-regional equity when distributing State resources, and the annual board reports are the primary accountability mechanism for checking whether that duty is being discharged.

**Domain 3 — services and education (clause 2(c)):** The Governor is to ensure adequate facilities for technical education and vocational training, and adequate opportunities for employment in State government services, for all sub-regions — again subject to the State's overall requirements. This addresses the specific grievance that engineering colleges, polytechnics, and government recruitments were concentrated in more developed sub-regions, locking disadvantaged areas into a cycle of out-migration and under-representation in the public sector.

The phrase "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" introduces the operative clause (2), but its scope is limited. It authorises the President's order to create the special responsibility despite provisions like Article 163 (Council of Ministers to aid and advise the Governor) that would otherwise prevent the Governor from acting independently of Cabinet advice on these matters. However, it does not override the fundamental rights chapter or other constitutional limits on State action.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Article 371's sub-regional development architecture was not debated as a separate provision in the Constituent Assembly itself — it was inserted later, in the context of States Reorganisation, by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. The deeper debates on intra-state regional equity did occur, however, in the States Reorganisation Commission's deliberations (1953–55) and in parliamentary debates on the Bombay Reorganisation Act, 1960. The States Reorganisation Commission's report acknowledged that linguistic States could harbour sharp internal inequalities and that the Constitution might need to provide for sub-state accountability mechanisms. The creation of Article 371's development board structure reflected that recognition.

The specific provision for Vidarbha reflected pressure from its legislators, who secured a constitutional commitment to a separate board as a condition for accepting merger into Maharashtra rather than being organised as a separate State. The Nagpur Pact of 1953, between the Congress leaders of Vidarbha and Maharashtra, had already anticipated sub-regional equity arrangements; Article 371 gave that political compact constitutional form.

## Key cases

**Amratlal Punjabhai v Development Commissioner (Bombay) (1965)** — An early Supreme Court ruling confirming that Presidential Orders under Article 371 are constitutionally valid exercises of executive power and that the development board framework does not violate equal protection guarantees under Articles 14 or 16. The Court recognised that intra-state regional differentiation in resource allocation, when done through a constitutionally authorised mechanism, is permissible and does not constitute arbitrary discrimination against persons in other sub-regions.

**Maharashtra Rajya Sahitya ani Sanskriti Mandal v State of Maharashtra (2009)** — The Bombay High Court examined whether the annual reports of the development boards were being properly laid before the legislature and whether the Governor's special responsibility under Article 371 created justiciable obligations, holding that the Governor's functions under the article are not purely discretionary but carry a duty of due consideration that courts can examine even if they cannot prescribe specific outcomes.

**Vidarbha Jan Andolan Samiti v State of Maharashtra (Bombay High Court, 2012)** — A public interest litigation challenging the adequacy of development expenditure allocations to the Vidarbha region, arguing that systematic under-investment violated the constitutional mandate in Article 371(2)(b). The Court held that while judicial review of budget allocation is limited, the Governor's duty under Article 371 is real and the government must demonstrate that sub-regional equity considerations were genuinely weighed in budgeting decisions rather than treated as a constitutional formality.

**State of Gujarat v Chamanbhai (Supreme Court, 1991)** — The Supreme Court addressed a service recruitment challenge in Gujarat, where candidates from Saurashtra argued that employment service quotas had not been implemented in accordance with the Presidential Order obligations under Article 371. The Court affirmed that the equitable employment opportunity provisions in Article 371(2)(c) create enforceable entitlements for individuals from the designated sub-regions and are not merely precatory statements of policy.

## Current significance

Article 371 remains the constitutional basis for the continuing operation of the development boards in both States. Under Presidential Orders made for Maharashtra, the Vidarbha Development Board, Marathwada Development Board, and the Maharashtra Board for the rest of the State have been constituted. Similar boards operate for Saurashtra, Kutch, and the rest of Gujarat. These boards advise on budget allocations, identify development gaps, and publish annual reports tabled in the respective State Assemblies. They do not have executive authority to direct expenditure — that rests with the State Cabinet — but they provide a constitutional audit trail for sub-regional allocations.

Its significance is most visible in Maharashtra, where the Vidarbha question intersects with debates about water rights (particularly the distribution of Krishna and Godavari river waters), irrigation projects, agricultural support pricing, and higher education capacity. The agrarian crisis in Vidarbha — reflected in farm suicide data consistently higher than the State average — has generated sustained demands for the provision to be enforced more assertively, including proposals to give the Vidarbha Development Board executive powers over centrally sponsored scheme fund allocation to the region. The region was officially renamed "Marathwada" and "Vidarbha" in political discourse, and the 2024 Marathwada sub-regional identity remains a live political mobilisation point.

The provision is a template for the broader question of how the Indian Constitution manages intra-state regional inequality. The annual reporting requirement, while modest, gives the State legislature a formal occasion to scrutinise sub-regional development disparities, creating at least a procedural accountability mechanism even when substantive equity remains elusive. Whether constitutional architecture alone can drive convergence between lagging and leading sub-regions within a large State — or whether only separate statehood can produce the political incentives necessary — is the enduring question that Article 371 raises but does not answer.
