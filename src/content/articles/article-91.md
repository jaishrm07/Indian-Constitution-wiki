  Article 91 ensures the Rajya Sabha always has a presiding officer by establishing a cascading chain of substitution — Deputy Chairman when the Chairman's office is vacant (including when the Vice-President is acting as President), a Presidential appointee if both offices are vacant, and a panel or House-determined substitute for ordinary absences. officialText: |-
  91. Power of the Deputy Chairman or other person to perform the duties of the office of, or to act as, Chairman.—(1) While the office of Chairman is vacant, or during any period when the Vice-President is acting as, or discharging the functions of, President, the duties of the office shall be performed by the Deputy Chairman, or, if the office of Deputy Chairman is also vacant, by such member of the Council of States as the President may appoint for the purpose.

  (2) During the absence of the Chairman from any sitting of the Council of States the Deputy Chairman, or, if he is also absent, such person as may be determined by the rules of procedure of the Council, or, if no such person is present, such other person as may be determined by the Council, shall act as Chairman.

## Why it matters

The Rajya Sabha cannot function without a presiding officer — someone to maintain order, recognise speakers, rule on points of order, and certify the conduct of proceedings. Article 91 provides the constitutional insurance that ensures this function is never interrupted, regardless of what happens to the primary and secondary presiding officers. The article is structurally significant because it determines when the executive (via Presidential appointment) may intervene in the legislature's management — a constitutional exception to the general principle that each House manages its own affairs.

The article reflects the Constitution's calibrated approach to executive-legislative separation. In ordinary circumstances, the legislature manages its own presiding functions through a hierarchy of elected officers and rules-based substitutes. Only when the House's own mechanisms are exhausted — because both the Chairman and the Deputy Chairman are unavailable — does the Constitution permit the President (acting on the advice of the Council of Ministers) to appoint a member to perform the Chairman's duties. This exception is narrow and deliberately structured to be the last resort rather than a default mechanism.

Article 91 has acquired renewed practical importance because the position of Deputy Chairman has been left vacant for extended periods in recent Parliaments. This vacancy means that the first fallback in clause (1) — the Deputy Chairman — is unavailable, and the subsequent fallback is a Presidential appointment. Understanding when clause (1) applies, what triggers a Presidential appointment, and what powers the appointed person holds is therefore a live constitutional question rather than an abstract contingency.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) governs structural vacancies — not mere absences from a sitting but genuine gaps in the office itself. There are two scenarios in clause (1). First, when the office of Chairman is vacant: since the Chairman is the Vice-President, this would occur if the Vice-President dies, resigns, or is removed during the interval before a new Vice-President takes office under Article 66. Second — and more commonly — when the Vice-President is acting as President under Article 65. When the Vice-President discharges presidential functions, the Vice-President constitutionally cannot simultaneously perform the Rajya Sabha chairmanship. Clause (1) treats this as a structural vacancy and directs that the Deputy Chairman step in.

If the Deputy Chairmanship is also vacant, clause (1)'s fallback to Presidential appointment is an exceptional constitutional mechanism. The President — in practice, on the advice of the Council of Ministers — appoints a Rajya Sabha member to perform the Chairman's duties. This is the only situation in which the executive may directly place a person in the Rajya Sabha's presiding role, and it is intentionally designed as a residual provision rather than a routine one. The political sensitivity of such an appointment is significant: the executive effectively chooses who presides over the upper house, at least temporarily.

Clause (2) governs ordinary absences — the Chairman is simply not at a particular sitting. The succession here runs entirely through parliamentary mechanisms. The Deputy Chairman fills in first. If the Deputy Chairman is also absent, a person determined by the Rules of Procedure acts as Chairman — in practice, this means the panel of Vice-Chairpersons, senior members nominated in advance to preside. If no such person is present, the Council itself may determine who presides. The President has no role in this scenario whatsoever; the House's own procedural framework is constitutionally adequate for managing routine presiding gaps.

## The acting Chairman's powers

A person substituting under Article 91 holds the full constitutional authority of the Chairman for the period of substitution — including all powers that the Chairman would have exercised. This includes ruling on points of order, maintaining order, recognising members to speak, and certifying the conduct of proceedings. Whether it extends to anti-defection adjudication (a power vested in the Chairman under the Tenth Schedule) is more contested, particularly under the Nabam Rebia doctrine, which holds that certain discretionary powers cannot be exercised by a presiding officer facing a removal notice.

The question of whether an Article 91 substituting officer can exercise Tenth Schedule powers has not been definitively resolved for the Rajya Sabha context, but the logic of the Nabam Rebia principle applies: a substituting Chairman who is not themselves the subject of a removal notice can generally exercise the full range of Chairman functions. The concern in Nabam Rebia was specifically about a presiding officer adjudicating the composition of the House while their own removal was pending — a concern that does not arise when the substitution is prompted by an ordinary vacancy or absence unconnected to any removal proceeding.

## When the Vice-President acts as President

The most constitutionally significant application of Article 91 is the scenario in Article 65 — the Vice-President acting as President when the President is unable to discharge functions due to absence, illness, or any other cause. During such periods, the Vice-President is not merely performing dual roles; the Constitution treats the assumption of presidential functions as requiring the Vice-President to step out of the Rajya Sabha chairmanship entirely. The Deputy Chairman steps into the Chairman's role for the full duration, not just for the sittings at which the Vice-President is absent.

This design reflects a constitutional logic that the presiding officer of the Rajya Sabha must be politically independent of the executive, and a person simultaneously exercising presidential authority — which includes presidential assent to Bills, proclamation of emergency, and executive command — cannot simultaneously be an impartial presiding officer of a legislative chamber. Article 91(1)'s clean transfer of the Chairman's duties during presidential substitution preserves this independence in constitutional structure, even when the same individual is involved.

## The Deputy Chairman vacancy problem

In recent Parliaments, the Rajya Sabha has gone without a Deputy Chairman for extended periods — a situation that the Constitution's framers did not anticipate as a regular occurrence. Article 89(2) provides that the Rajya Sabha "shall, as soon as may be, choose a member of the Council to be Deputy Chairman." The phrase "as soon as may be" is directory rather than mandatory, but its repeated non-fulfilment has been argued to be a constitutional default. No court has yet ordered the election of a Deputy Chairman, and the question of whether such a judicial direction would be appropriate remains open.

The practical consequence of the vacancy is that clause (1)'s first fallback is unavailable. If the Vice-President acts as President, a Presidential appointment becomes the immediate necessity. The appointed member — chosen by the executive — would then preside over the Rajya Sabha, raising questions of institutional independence that are absent when a constitutionally elected Deputy Chairman fills the role. The Deputy Chairman vacancy has thus created a structural vulnerability in the Rajya Sabha's constitutional independence.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The five-judge bench's analysis of presiding-officer succession and the exercise of powers during vacancy or pending removal proceedings, developed in the context of the analogous State legislature provisions, applies directly to the Article 91 framework. The Court emphasised that substitution provisions must be read as enabling orderly legislative function, not as opportunities to install sympathetic presiding officers to defeat pending proceedings. The judgment confirmed that a substituting presiding officer cannot be used strategically to circumvent the accountability mechanism of removal proceedings.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1964 (Keshav Singh case)** — The Supreme Court's examination of parliamentary privileges and the role of presiding officers engaged the question of who legitimately exercises the powers of a presiding officer — a question directly connected to the succession mechanism in Article 91. The Court's analysis of what constitutes acting as a presiding officer confirmed that substitution under constitutional authority creates full legal authority for the period of substitution.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Court's comprehensive analysis confirmed that constitutional succession provisions for presiding officers create fully authorised substitutes with the same constitutional standing as the primary office-holder, so that proceedings conducted by a substituting presiding officer have the same constitutional validity as those conducted by the substantive office-holder. The principle applies equally to Rajya Sabha substitution under Article 91.

**Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court's extended engagement with Maharashtra's political crisis and the Speaker's disqualification jurisdiction touched on what happens to presiding functions when the primary and substitute officers are both entangled in removal proceedings — a question that echoes the structural concerns addressed by Article 91's hierarchy of substitution.

## Current significance

Article 91 became practically live when the Rajya Sabha did not elect a Deputy Chairman for extended periods. The absence of a Deputy Chairman means that if the Chairman (Vice-President) becomes structurally unavailable — for instance, by acting as President — the only recourse under Article 91(1) is Presidential appointment of a Rajya Sabha member, a constitutionally authorised but politically sensitive executive intrusion into the legislature. This vulnerability has given the competition for the Deputy Chairmanship renewed urgency, and has raised academic questions about whether the constitutional obligation to elect a Deputy Chairman "as soon as may be" under Article 89(2) can be made enforceable through judicial direction.

The broader constitutional question raised by Article 91 is about the relationship between executive and legislative authority in House management. The Constitution's preference is clearly for parliamentary self-management — the Vice-Chairpersons panel under Article 91(2) is entirely a creation of the House's own rules. The Presidential appointment fallback under Article 91(1) is a constitutional admission that self-management cannot always succeed, not an invitation to the executive to exercise influence over legislative proceedings. The appropriate interpretation of Article 91 must therefore keep the Presidential appointment power narrow, confined to genuine constitutional emergencies rather than treated as a convenient alternative when the House's internal selection mechanisms are merely slow.

The intersection of Article 91 with the Tenth Schedule's anti-defection provisions continues to generate constitutional questions. If the person appointed by the President under Article 91(1) to perform the Chairman's duties is called upon to adjudicate anti-defection petitions, the constitutional legitimacy of that adjudication is far more fragile than when an elected Deputy Chairman exercises the same power. The executive's role in placing the adjudicator would create an appearance of bias that the constitutional design of the Tenth Schedule — which vests the power in the elected presiding officer precisely to maintain distance from the courts and the executive — is designed to avoid.
