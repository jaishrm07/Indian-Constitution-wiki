## Why it matters

Caste, religion, sex, race, and place of birth are the fault lines along which India's deepest inequalities run. Article 15 addresses them directly, not as aspirations but as constitutional obligations. The prohibition in Article 15(1) — that the State shall not discriminate on these grounds — is an equality guarantee with immediate bite: any government policy, administrative rule, or statutory provision that singles out citizens for disadvantage on account of their religion, caste, sex, or origin can be challenged directly.

But Article 15 is more than an anti-discrimination provision. It is also the constitutional foundation of India's affirmative action framework in education. The enabling clauses — (3) for women and children, (4) for backward classes and SCs/STs, (5) for backward class admissions to private educational institutions, (6) for economically weaker sections — represent successive constitutional choices that the State may, and must at least be permitted to, take positive measures to advance disadvantaged groups. These clauses are not exceptions to Article 15(1) in the derogatory sense of special pleading — they reflect a constitutional understanding that equality requires more than formal neutrality when centuries of structural discrimination have produced unequal starting points.

The history of Article 15 is also the history of constitutional amendment driven by judicial decisions. When the Supreme Court in 1951 struck down the Madras communal reservation system for medical colleges, Parliament responded within months with the First Amendment that added Article 15(4). When the Court in 2002 held that unaided private educational institutions had a right to determine their own admissions, Parliament responded in 2005 with Article 15(5) to ensure that reservation could extend to unaided private institutions. When the ruling party in 2019 wished to create a new category of reservation for economically weaker sections, the 103rd Amendment added Article 15(6). Article 15 is therefore not a static text but one that has been reshaped repeatedly in response to the interaction between judicial decisions and political will.

The tension between the anti-discrimination principle in clauses (1) and (2) and the enabling provisions in clauses (3)-(6) is never fully resolved. It is managed through doctrine — the fifty-percent ceiling on aggregate reservations, the requirement to exclude the creamy layer from OBC benefits, the conditions under which states may identify backward classes, and the question of whether sub-classification within SC/ST lists is constitutionally permissible. Each of these doctrinal choices is a response to the underlying constitutional tension, and each remains contested.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) uses the word "only" — discrimination on grounds "only" of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. This word was the subject of early interpretive controversy. Does "only" mean that the State may discriminate on one of these grounds if the discrimination is also based on some additional consideration? The Court's approach has been that the word "only" prevents the provision from catching cases where the discriminatory ground is incidental to a legitimate classification, but it does not permit a State to evade Article 15 simply by pointing to some additional, minor differentiating factor. If the essential or dominant reason for the discrimination is one of the prohibited grounds, Article 15(1) applies.

Clause (2) is remarkable because it imposes a constitutional obligation on private actors, not just the State. No citizen — not just no state authority — may deny another citizen access to shops, restaurants, hotels, entertainment places, public wells, ghats, or roads on the prohibited grounds. This horizontal application of the anti-discrimination norm is limited to the specific categories listed, but within those categories it creates a direct constitutional right as against private discrimination. This was significant at the time of drafting as a constitutional response to caste-based exclusion from public spaces.

The phrase "special provision" in clauses (3)-(6) is broader than "reservation." The enabling clauses permit reservations but also permit other forms of targeted assistance — fee concessions, coaching programmes, lower qualifying criteria for admissions. Reservation is the most contested application, but the enabling power is not limited to reservation.

The requirement in clause (4) that a class be "socially and educationally backward" sets conditions on the identification of beneficiary classes that have generated extensive litigation. A class that is economically poor but not socially backward does not qualify under clause (4) — the social backwardness must be demonstrated. The dominant caste affirmative action cases (Maratha, Jat, Gujjar) have required courts to examine whether these communities, despite economic disadvantage in some members, satisfy the constitutional test of social backwardness. Clause (6), added in 2019, addresses pure economic disadvantage and is a separate, not a relaxed version of, the clause (4) criterion.

## Key cases

### The first crisis: Champakam and the First Amendment

**State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951)** — The Government of Madras operated a Communal Government Order that reserved seats in state medical and engineering colleges on the basis of religion and caste, allocating specified numbers of seats to Brahmins, non-Brahmins, Muslims, Harijans, Anglo-Indians, and Indian Christians. Champakam Dorairajan, a Brahmin woman, was denied admission to a medical college despite scoring higher marks than some admitted candidates from other communities. The Supreme Court held that the Communal Government Order violated Article 15(1) and Article 29(2). The Court rejected the Government's argument that the Directive Principles in Part IV authorised the communal reservation — it held that Directive Principles cannot override fundamental rights.

The immediate constitutional consequence was the First Constitutional Amendment Act 1951, which added Article 15(4), enabling the State to make special provision for the advancement of any socially and educationally backward classes or for the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes. The amendment effectively nullified the ratio of Champakam as applied to reservation policy and inaugurated the constitutional architecture for affirmative action in Indian education.

### Defining the limits: Indra Sawhney and the fifty-percent ceiling

**Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992)** — The nine-judge bench assessment of the Mandal Commission's OBC reservations of 27 percent in central government services is the foundational case for the entire affirmative action system. Though primarily decided under Article 16(4), the Court's reading of Articles 15(4) and 16(4) as reflecting a common constitutional philosophy is essential to understanding Article 15. The Court upheld the OBC reservations in principle but imposed three critical limitations: first, reservations in aggregate cannot exceed fifty percent of available seats, a constitutional ceiling derived from the equality guarantee of Articles 14 and 15(1); second, the creamy layer — the more prosperous members of OBC communities who have risen above social backwardness — must be excluded from the benefits of Article 15(4) and 16(4); and third, the identification of backward classes cannot be based on religion alone.

**TMA Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — An eleven-judge bench held that private unaided educational institutions have a constitutional right under Article 19(1)(g) to determine their own admissions, limited only by a prohibition on capitation fees and unfair practices. The State could not impose its reservations on unaided private institutions. This created a gap in the affirmative action framework — students from SC/ST/OBC communities had reservation rights only in state-maintained and aided institutions. Parliament responded with the 85th and 93rd Constitutional Amendments, which added Article 15(5) specifically to enable reservation in admissions to private educational institutions (excluding minority institutions under Article 30). The response illustrates the interactive dynamic between judicial interpretation and constitutional amendment that has shaped Article 15.

### The 93rd Amendment and private educational institutions

**Ashoka Kumar Thakur v. Union of India (2008)** — This case tested the Central Educational Institutions (Reservation in Admission) Act 2006, which implemented Article 15(5) by requiring private aided and unaided institutions (other than minority institutions) to provide OBC reservations in admissions. The Court by a four-to-one majority upheld Article 15(5) itself as constitutionally valid — the enabling clause does not violate the basic structure — but held that the exclusion of minority institutions under Article 30 from its operation was a permissible constitutional choice, not a violation of equality. The minority judgment by Justice Raveendran took the view that subjecting only OBCs (and not forward-class students) to Article 15(5) requirements engaged the very equality question Article 15 was designed to address.

### The Naz Foundation connection

**Naz Foundation v. Government of NCT of Delhi (Delhi High Court, 2009)** — While primarily an Article 21 and Article 14 case, the Delhi High Court's decision decriminalising same-sex conduct also engaged Article 15. The Court held that "sex" as a ground of non-discrimination in Article 15(1) extends to sexual orientation — discrimination against a person on account of their sexual orientation is discrimination on grounds of sex. This reading of Article 15 was not directly addressed by the Supreme Court in Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India (2018), which struck down Section 377 primarily under Articles 14, 19, and 21, but the argument that sexual orientation discrimination falls within Article 15's "sex" prohibition continues to influence legal scholarship and lower court reasoning.

### Affirmative action for EWS: the 103rd Amendment

**Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (2022)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Constitution (103rd Amendment) Act, which inserted Articles 15(6) and 16(6) to permit ten percent reservation for economically weaker sections. The majority (3:2) held that economic disadvantage is a constitutionally permissible criterion for affirmative action, that the EWS reservation falls outside the fifty-percent ceiling applicable to reservations under clauses (4) and (5) of Article 15, and that the exclusion of OBCs, SCs, and STs from EWS benefits — because they have their own reservation categories — does not violate the equality guarantee.

The two dissenting justices (D Y Chandrachud and Bela Trivedi) took sharply different positions. Justice Chandrachud held that the 103rd Amendment violates the basic structure of the Constitution by treating reservation as a tool of economic upliftment divorced from the social justice rationale that underlies the entire affirmative action framework, and that excluding the most historically disadvantaged communities from EWS benefits is inherently discriminatory. Justice Trivedi also dissented but on narrower grounds. The majority opinion and the dissents together constitute the most searching examination of the constitutional philosophy underlying Article 15 since Indra Sawhney.

### Sub-classification within SCs and STs

**State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held, overruling a previous five-judge decision, that states may sub-classify within the Scheduled Caste list to provide preferential quotas for the more deprived sub-groups within the list, as long as the sub-classification is based on empirical data on inadequate representation. The case has profound implications for Article 15(4) — it holds that treating all Scheduled Castes as a homogeneous group when some are far more socially deprived than others is itself inconsistent with the equality and social justice values that Article 15(4) is designed to serve. Sub-classification is therefore not only permissible but may be constitutionally required to give meaningful effect to the enabling clause.

## Current significance

Article 15 sits at the centre of several simultaneously active constitutional debates. The Davinder Singh sub-classification framework is being implemented by several states — Punjab, Tamil Nadu, and others have enacted sub-classification legislation — and these laws face constitutional challenges that require courts to apply the Davinder Singh criteria to specific state contexts. The inquiry into whether sub-classification is supported by adequate data on representation is factually intensive, and the standard for what constitutes adequate data has not yet been definitively settled.

The question of whether Article 15(1)'s prohibition of discrimination on grounds of sex extends to gender identity and sexual orientation has been raised in the context of the Supreme Court's NALSA judgment on transgender rights and the subsequent Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Act 2019. If discrimination against transgender persons is discrimination on grounds of sex within Article 15(1), the anti-discrimination guarantee becomes a powerful tool against institutional discrimination in health, education, and employment — areas not directly addressed by the transgender legislation.

The Naz Foundation connection — whether "sex" in Article 15 encompasses sexual orientation — remains live in the context of the ongoing litigation about same-sex marriage rights and relationship recognition, which explicitly raises Article 15 as one of the constitutional bases for recognising equal rights for same-sex couples.

The boundary between Articles 15(4) and 15(6) is also producing new litigation. Several communities seek to move between backward class categories or argue that economic disadvantage within already-reserved communities should entitle them to EWS reservation in addition to community-based reservation. These claims test the internal logic of the Article 15 framework and the extent to which courts will permit cumulative reservations to approach or exceed the overall constitutional ceiling.
