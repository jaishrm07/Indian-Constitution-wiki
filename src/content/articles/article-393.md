## What this Article does

Article 393 is the short title provision of the Constitution. It simply declares that the document shall be called "the Constitution of India." This is a standard provision in legislative drafting that gives a law its formal name.

Although brief, the short title provision is not merely ceremonial. It establishes the official name by which the Constitution is cited in legal proceedings, referenced in other legislation, and invoked in courts and official documents. Every reference in any statute or judicial decision to "the Constitution of India" derives its authority from this naming provision.

## Why it matters — the choice of name

The choice of the title "Constitution of India" carries significant implications. It is a republican document — not styled as the "Constitution of the Dominion of India" or the "Constitution of the Union of India" (though the latter phrase appears as "Union of India" elsewhere in the text). The title signals a clean break from the dominion status framework and asserts the document as the foundational law of an independent, sovereign republic.

The name also affirms that the document is a Constitution in the fullest sense — a supreme law, not merely a constitutional statute passed by Parliament. The Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed the unique constitutional status of this document, contrasting it with ordinary parliamentary legislation that can be amended or repealed through regular legislative processes. The short title "Constitution of India" encapsulates this supreme status.

## Historical context

The Constituent Assembly debated the Constitution's title along with many other provisions. The choice to call it the "Constitution of India" rather than the "Constitution of the Republic of India" was deliberate — "Republic" is affirmed in Article 1 (which declares India to be a "Sovereign Democratic Republic") and in the Preamble, making it unnecessary to repeat it in the short title. The simplicity of the title also reflects a decision to create a foundational document that would be known by the name of the nation itself, without qualifying adjectives.

The Constitution was signed by members of the Constituent Assembly on 24 November 1949 and came into force on 26 January 1950 — the date chosen in commemoration of the Purna Swaraj (Complete Independence) declaration of 26 January 1930 by the Indian National Congress.

## Key cases

No constitutional case has turned on the short title provision itself. However, the naming of the document has been referenced in cases about the Constitution's supreme status and about constitutional interpretation.

**Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala AIR 1973 SC 1461** — The Supreme Court's landmark judgment affirming the basic structure doctrine analysed the Constitution as a whole, treating it as a supreme document whose fundamental features Parliament cannot abrogate. The title "Constitution of India" is the textual anchor for this analysis.

## Current significance

Article 393 continues to be operative and significant. Every citation of the Constitution invokes this provision. Its simplicity belies its importance: it is the provision that gives the Constitution of India its name and, with that name, its identity as the supreme law of the land.

The short title also has practical legal significance in that it is the name by which the Constitution is cited in the Interpretation Acts and in all legislation that refers to constitutional provisions. Section 3 of the General Clauses Act, 1897, as adapted, defines "Constitution" to mean the Constitution of India — a definition anchored in the title given by Article 393. The apparent simplicity of this eight-word provision thus underpins a large body of interpretive law that depends on the Constitution being unambiguously identified as a specific, named document.

The naming of the document as the "Constitution of India" rather than the "Constitution of the Republic of India" or "Constitution of the Union of India" also reflects a conscious choice about the document's relationship to Indian identity. The Constitution belongs to India — to its people, its territory, its history — not merely to a particular form of government (Republic) or structural arrangement (Union). This choice has been cited by scholars as reflecting the inclusive, national character of the constitutional enterprise undertaken by the Constituent Assembly.

Article 393 is also notable for its position in the text. As the first article of Part XXII (Short Title, Commencement, Authoritative Text in Hindi, and Repeals), it occupies the structural role that introductory provisions traditionally occupy in legislative drafting. Its simplicity — a single sentence establishing a name — contrasts sharply with the complexity of the document it names, but that simplicity is itself meaningful: the Constitution does not need a complex title because the nation whose fundamental law it embodies is itself the most important descriptor.
