officialText: |-
  59. Conditions of President's office.—(1) The President shall not be a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any State, and if a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any State be elected President, he shall be deemed to have vacated his seat in that House on the date on which he enters upon his office as President.

  (2) The President shall not hold any other office of profit.

  (3) The President shall be entitled without payment of rent to the use of his official residences and shall be also entitled to such emoluments, allowances and privileges as may be determined by Parliament by law and, until provision in that behalf is so made, such emoluments, allowances and privileges as are specified in the Second Schedule.

  (4) The emoluments and allowances of the President shall not be diminished during his term of office.

## Why it matters

Article 59 gives the presidential office its structural independence by ensuring that the President owes no competing loyalties — to a constituency, to a parliamentary party, or to an employer — and is immune from financial coercion by the executive or legislative branches. These conditions are not personal privileges. They are institutional safeguards for the functioning of the office in a constitutional democracy where the head of state must, at least formally and sometimes actually, exercise independent constitutional judgment.

The incompatibility with legislative membership in clause (1) is the first and most important condition. A President who retained a parliamentary seat would simultaneously have obligations to a constituency, a political party's parliamentary discipline, and the constitutional office — three sets of competing loyalties that are irreconcilable. The automatic vacation of the seat upon entering office removes all three conflicts at once. It also prevents a President from being simultaneously subject to parliamentary party whips and to the constitutional obligation to act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers — which may not always align with the President's original parliamentary party.

The prohibition on holding any other office of profit in clause (2) extends the incompatibility principle beyond the legislature. The President cannot simultaneously serve as a paid university chancellor, a statutory commissioner, or any other position that draws public remuneration. This ensures that the President's sole institutional identity is the constitutional office — no secondary salary, no secondary employer, no secondary institutional loyalty. The condition is permanent throughout the term and operates independently of the Article 58(2) qualification bar that applies to candidates before election.

The financial security provisions in clauses (3) and (4) are structural rather than merely remunerative. The guarantee against diminution of emoluments during the term directly parallels the equivalent guarantee for Supreme Court judges under Article 125(2) and High Court judges under Article 221(2). The parallel is not coincidental — both sets of office-holders are required to exercise constitutional functions that may bring them into conflict with the executive, and both are insulated from financial retaliation by the same constitutional mechanism. A President who could be reduced to a lower salary by a parliamentary resolution would be a President who faced a standing incentive to avoid conflict with the parliamentary majority.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) operates automatically — "shall be deemed to have vacated." There is no process, no formal resignation, no acceptance of resignation. The vacation happens by constitutional operation on the date the President enters office. The seat becomes vacant at that moment and triggers the constitutional machinery for by-elections under Article 151. The President has no discretion to delay the vacation or to seek permission from the Speaker or any other authority.

Clause (2)'s prohibition on "any other office of profit" is complementary to the automatic vacation in clause (1). Having given up the legislative seat, the President is also prohibited from filling that void with any remunerative public position. The word "other" is important — it presupposes that the Presidency itself is, for constitutional purposes, an office but not one to which the "office of profit" prohibition applies in the ordinary sense.

Clause (3) separates the housing entitlement — explicitly granted as a use without rent — from the broader emoluments and privileges, which are determined by Parliament by law or, in default, by the Second Schedule. Parliament has enacted the President's Emoluments and Pension Act, 1951 (and subsequent amendments) to specify the salary, allowances, and post-retirement pension and facilities. The Second Schedule backstop ensures that even if Parliament fails to enact fresh legislation, the President is not left without legally defined entitlements.

Clause (4)'s non-diminution guarantee operates only in one direction — it prevents reduction, but does not prevent increase. Parliament may at any time enhance the President's emoluments, even mid-term. This asymmetric protection reflects the constitutional concern: the danger is that a hostile parliamentary majority might reduce presidential emoluments as a punitive or coercive measure, not that it might enhance them. The guarantee is time-limited to "his term of office" — once a new presidential term begins, Parliament could in theory set new, lower rates, though the political consequences would be considerable.

## Key cases

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court's analysis of Articles 53–59 as a coherent scheme confirmed that the conditions of Article 59 are designed not merely as personal obligations but as structural features protecting the independent functioning of the office. The prohibition on holding office of profit reinforces the President's role as a non-partisan constitutional head, while the financial security provisions ensure that the independence of the office is backed by material insulation.

**H.H. Maharajadhiraja Madhav Rao Scindia v Union of India (1971)** — In examining the financial protections extended to constitutional dignitaries and the validity of executive orders affecting those protections, the Supreme Court drew upon the logic of non-diminution clauses. The Court treated such clauses as constitutional commitments that bind even Parliament and cannot be circumvented through indirect reductions — whether through special taxation, withdrawal of facilities, or redesignation of entitlements under different budgetary headings.

**Union of India v Jyoti Prakash Mitter (1971)** — The Court held that conditions of a constitutional office — including financial entitlements — are not subject to executive modification during the tenure of the incumbent. This principle directly supports the reading of Article 59(4) as protecting against not only direct legislative reduction but also executive action that has the effect of diminishing the emoluments the President receives.

**Presidential Salary (Income Tax) — legislative practice** — The question of whether the non-diminution guarantee prevents Parliament from levying income tax on presidential salary was addressed by specific legislation amending the President's Emoluments and Pension Act. The constitutional resolution was that Parliament could impose income tax on presidential income by expressly providing for it in advance — since prospective taxation is not a mid-term diminution of the emoluments but a general fiscal burden applied equally. This legislative solution was consistent with the non-diminution guarantee while allowing Parliament's taxing power to apply to presidential income.

**B.R. Kapur v State of Tamil Nadu (2001)** — The Court examined the parallel structure of presidential and gubernatorial office conditions, noting that Article 159's conditions of gubernatorial office (including the non-diminution guarantee for the Governor's emoluments under Article 158(4)) mirror Article 59's structure. The parallelism confirms that the insulation from financial coercion is a general feature of constitutional head-of-state offices at both Union and State levels.

## Current significance

The automatic vacation of legislative seats under Article 59(1) has practical relevance whenever a sitting Rajya Sabha member, Lok Sabha member, or State MLA is elected President. The vacation triggers a by-election in the vacated constituency, creating administrative obligations for the Election Commission and political uncertainty in close parliamentary arithmetic situations. When the President-elect is a Rajya Sabha member whose vacancy is filled by the State government in the interim, the resulting appointment can affect the Rajya Sabha's political balance.

The non-diminution guarantee of clause (4) has continued significance in debates about subjecting high constitutional office-holders to general fiscal measures. The 1986 and 2008 amendments to the President's Emoluments Act, and the question of whether benefits-in-kind or facility withdrawals constitute "diminution" within the meaning of clause (4), remain areas of interpretive uncertainty. If Parliament were ever to introduce a significant across-the-board pay reduction for constitutional positions during a fiscal crisis, Article 59(4) would require that the sitting President be exempted or that the reduction take effect only from the next presidential term.

The broader significance of Article 59 is that it completes the architecture of the presidential office by ensuring that whoever holds it is fully committed to the office and financially secure within it. Together with the fixed five-year term of Article 56, the oath of office under Article 60, and the emoluments structure of the Second Schedule, Article 59 creates the institutional conditions for the kind of constitutional independence the President needs to fulfil even the limited but real functions — scrutinising legislation, appointing the Prime Minister in an uncertain political situation, exercising mercy under Article 72 — that the Constitution assigns to the office.
