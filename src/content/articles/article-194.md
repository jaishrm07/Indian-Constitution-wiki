  194. Powers, privileges, etc., of the Houses of Legislatures and of the members and committees thereof.—(1) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution and to the rules and standing orders regulating the procedure of the Legislature, there shall be freedom of speech in the Legislature of every State.

  (2) No member of the Legislature of a State shall be liable to any proceedings in any court in respect of anything said or any vote given by him in the Legislature or any committee thereof, and no person shall be so liable in respect of the publication by or under the authority of a House of such a Legislature of any report, paper, votes or proceedings. ## What this Article does

Article 194 is the State Legislature's counterpart of Article 105,
conferring on each House and its members freedom of speech in the
legislature, immunity from court proceedings for anything said or any vote
given, and immunity in respect of authorised publications. Beyond these
specific immunities, clause (3) preserves the general body of
parliamentary privileges — with their content fixed by reference to those
prevailing immediately before the Forty-Fourth Amendment came into force
in 1978 — until the State Legislature defines them by law. These
privileges include the power to commit for contempt, to regulate internal
proceedings without judicial interference, and to control the entry of
strangers.

Clause (4) extends the protection to all persons constitutionally entitled
to participate in proceedings — such as Ministers attending under Article
177 and the Advocate-General — ensuring that the privilege regime covers
the full range of authorised participants and not only elected members.

## Reading the provision

### Clause (1): Freedom of speech in the Legislature

The freedom of speech conferred by clause (1) is subject to two
limitations built into the text itself: the provisions of the Constitution
(including, most prominently, the anti-defection schedule under the Tenth
Amendment) and the rules and standing orders of the House. A member's
speech is therefore not unconditionally free; it must comply with
procedural rules. This clause operates in the domain of what a member
says within the House, while clause (2) supplies the immunity from
external legal proceedings that makes that speech truly free in practice.

### Clause (2): Immunity from court proceedings

Clause (2) is the core protective provision. No member is liable to "any
proceedings in any court" in respect of anything said or any vote given in
the Legislature or any committee thereof. The phrase "proceedings in any
court" is wide and covers civil and criminal proceedings alike. The
immunity extends to publications authorised by a House — Hansard,
committee reports, and official notices — but not to unauthorised
republications by newspapers or other parties.

The immunity is functional rather than personal: it attaches to the act
(speaking, voting, authorised publication), not to the individual's status
as a member. A member who does not cast a vote, or whose speech is made
outside the House, receives no protection under clause (2).

### Clause (3): Residual privileges and the 44th Amendment baseline

Before the 44th Amendment, clause (3) defined the residual privileges of
State Legislatures by reference to those of the House of Commons of the
United Kingdom as they existed at the commencement of the Constitution.
The 44th Amendment replaced that reference with a more indigenous
baseline: the privileges that the House possessed "immediately before the
coming into force of section 26 of the Constitution (Forty-fourth
Amendment) Act, 1978." This was a deliberate choice to anchor privileges
in Indian legislative practice rather than the evolving common law of
Westminster.

### Privilege versus fundamental rights

A recurring question is whether legislative privilege can override
fundamental rights under Part III. The Supreme Court has consistently held
that privileges are themselves constitutional rights guaranteed by Article
194 and must be reconciled with fundamental rights by harmonious
construction, but that the protection of core legislative functions —
particularly free speech in debate and the right to regulate internal
proceedings — takes precedence within its proper domain. Where a
privilege claim is used to suppress an outsider's fundamental rights,
courts have shown greater readiness to intervene.

## Key cases

**M.S.M. Sharma v. Sri Krishna Sinha (Searchlight Case) (1959)** — The
Supreme Court held that a newspaper editor could be committed for contempt
of the Bihar Legislative Assembly for publishing a speech that the House
had directed to be expunged from the proceedings. The case established
that the Article 194 contempt power is insulated from challenge under
Article 19(1)(a) because the privilege itself flows from the Constitution.
The Searchlight case anchored the principle that the Legislature's
internal discipline is a constitutional right, not a derogation from
fundamental rights.

**Keshav Singh v. Speaker, Legislative Assembly (1965)** — In one of the
most dramatic constitutional confrontations of independent India, a member
of the Uttar Pradesh Legislative Assembly was committed for contempt by
the House, and the Allahabad High Court admitted his habeas corpus
petition. The Assembly then held the High Court judges themselves in
contempt for entertaining the petition, producing a standoff between the
judiciary and the legislature. The President referred the matter to the
Supreme Court under Article 143. The Full Court — all the judges of the
Supreme Court sitting together — held that although Article 194 gives the
legislature power to commit for contempt, courts retain jurisdiction under
Article 226 to examine whether the committal falls entirely outside the
legislature's powers. The ruling drew the outer boundary of legislative
privilege: the House may control its own proceedings and punish contempt,
but courts are not entirely excluded when the committed person is a
stranger rather than a member or when jurisdiction is genuinely disputed.

**P.V. Narasimha Rao v. State (CBI/SPE) (1998)** — A five-judge
Constitution Bench, by a 3:2 majority, held that a Member of Parliament
(and by the same reasoning a State legislator) who actually voted in the
House as agreed when accepting a bribe could claim immunity under Article
105(2) (and its Article 194(2) equivalent) because the immunity attached
to the vote. The majority held that where the vote was the subject matter
of the criminal charge, the immunity covered it. The decision was widely
criticised as converting an immunity designed to protect legislative
independence into a shield for corruption.

**Sita Soren v. Union of India (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench
unanimously overruled P.V. Narasimha Rao. The Court held that Article
194(2) protects the legislative act of speaking and voting but does not
immunise the antecedent and independent criminal act of accepting a bribe.
Accepting a bribe is complete as an offence at the moment of acceptance;
it is not part of the legislative act and falls entirely outside the
scope of the privilege. The ruling restores coherence to the doctrine by
confining immunity to acts intrinsic to the legislative function and
refusing to extend it to conduct that merely precedes or accompanies that
function.

## Current significance

The Sita Soren overruling is the most consequential development in
legislative privilege doctrine since Keshav Singh. Its immediate practical
effect for State Legislatures under Article 194 is that no member can
invoke clause (2) as a bar to prosecution for bribery connected with
legislative acts, including floor-crossing during confidence votes or
budget sessions — a scenario that had been a structural vulnerability in
State politics since the PV Narasimha Rao decision.

More broadly, the decision reflects a judicial trend of reading legislative
immunity purposively: the immunity exists to enable fearless legislative
speech and voting, not to create a zone of impunity for corruption. This
purposive reading has potential implications for other edges of privilege
doctrine — for instance, whether a member who is paid to ask a planted
question in committee proceedings, or who abuses the authorised-
publication immunity to defame a private party, can similarly be denied
privilege protection on the ground that the conduct is extrinsic to the
legislative function.

The unresolved tension between Article 194 privileges and fundamental
rights under Articles 19, 20, and 21 continues to generate litigation,
particularly in cases where members are expelled or suspended and claim
that the House has violated their constitutional rights. Courts have
generally been reluctant to interfere with internal disciplinary
proceedings but have reserved the power to act where the House acts wholly
without jurisdiction or in manifest violation of natural justice.
