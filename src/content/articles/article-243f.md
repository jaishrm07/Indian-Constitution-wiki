  243F. Disqualifications for membership.—(1) A person shall be disqualified for being chosen as, and for being, a member of a Panchayat—

  (a) if he is so disqualified by or under any law for the time being in force for the purposes of elections to the Legislature of the State concerned:

  Provided that no person shall be disqualified on the ground that he is less than twenty-five years of age, if he has attained the age of twentyone years;

  (b) if he is so disqualified by or under any law made by the Legislature of the State.

  (2) If any question arises as to whether a member of a Panchayat has become subject to any of the disqualifications mentioned in clause (1), the question shall be referred for the decision of such authority and in such manner as the Legislature of a State may, by law, provide.

## Why it matters

Article 243F defines the boundary between eligibility and disqualification for Panchayat membership. Its two-track structure is important. The first track (clause 1(a)) imports the entire body of State-legislature-election disqualifications directly into the Panchayat context — covering convictions for specified offences, holding offices of profit, unsound mind, insolvency, and other grounds under the Representation of the People Act and equivalent state laws — with the one modification that the minimum age floor is 21 rather than 25. This modification reflects the purpose of Panchayati Raj to draw in younger citizens from rural areas who might not otherwise enter public life.

The second track (clause 1(b)) is the more contested element. It gives State legislatures an open-ended power to prescribe additional disqualifications by law, subject only to constitutional constraints on fundamental rights and equality. Several states have used this power innovatively — and controversially — to impose disqualifications based on educational qualifications, indebtedness to cooperative societies, non-payment of electricity dues, non-maintenance of toilets, and most visibly, having more than two children. These additional disqualifications have generated substantial litigation about whether they constitute reasonable conditions for public office or unreasonable restrictions on the democratic right to stand for election.

## How courts use it

The Supreme Court has had to balance two competing considerations when reviewing Article 243F(1)(b) disqualifications. On one side stands the principle of universal political participation underlying the 73rd Amendment's democratisation project. On the other stands the principle that states may legitimately require candidates for public office to satisfy minimum conditions that reflect public policy goals such as population control, good governance, or financial accountability.

The leading judgment in this area, Javed v. State of Haryana (2003), upheld the two-child norm disqualification. Courts distinguish between disqualifications that are tied to the conduct or status of the person (such as having more than two children) and disqualifications based on immutable characteristics (such as caste, religion, or gender) that would be constitutionally impermissible. The Article 243F(2) framework for adjudicating disqualification disputes — requiring referral to a designated authority — has also been tested, with courts holding that the authority must be genuinely impartial and its process must satisfy natural justice requirements.

## Key cases

**Javed v. State of Haryana (2003)** — The Supreme Court upheld the Haryana Panchayati Raj Act provisions disqualifying a person with more than two living children from contesting Panchayat elections. The petitioners argued that the provision violated Articles 14, 16, and 21 of the Constitution. The Court, by a majority, rejected all these contentions, holding that the two-child norm was a rational policy choice within the permissible scope of Article 243F(1)(b), that it applied equally to all persons regardless of religion, and that it did not infringe the right to privacy under Article 21 in a manner disproportionate to the state's legitimate aim of population stabilisation. This case remains the most significant interpretation of the extent of the State legislature's power under Article 243F(1)(b).

**Rajbala v. State of Haryana (2015)** — The Supreme Court upheld Haryana legislation imposing minimum educational qualifications for Panchayat election candidates (specifically, requiring candidates for Gram Panchayat to have passed Class VIII, and higher qualifications for higher tiers). The Court held that educational qualifications are a permissible exercise of the Article 243F(1)(b) power, that they do not violate the right to equality, and that the State legislature is entitled to determine what minimum conditions are appropriate for persons who will manage local self-government. The judgment attracted criticism for its potential to exclude disadvantaged communities — particularly women, SC/ST populations, and the poor — who historically have lower educational attainment.

**Vijayalakshmi v. District Collector (2006)** — The Madras High Court addressed the procedural requirements for adjudicating disqualification disputes under Article 243F(2), holding that the designated authority must follow a fair process and cannot determine disqualification without giving the affected member an opportunity to be heard. The case reinforced that the procedural framework for disqualification is itself a constitutional requirement, not a purely statutory matter.

## Current significance

The intersection of Article 243F with the right to equality remains contested terrain. The two-child norm disqualifications upheld in Javed have been criticised by demographers and rights advocates on the ground that the policy disproportionately affects Muslim women — who, if divorced after a third child, lose their political rights even though the decision to have children was not exclusively theirs. Several states have also enacted disqualifications tied to non-payment of local-body dues, which critics argue penalise poverty rather than misconduct.

The educational qualification disqualifications upheld in Rajbala have significantly affected Panchayat composition in states like Haryana and Rajasthan, where large proportions of potential candidates — especially women in reserved seats — have been found ineligible on educational grounds. This has created a tension within the constitutional design of Part IX itself: the reservation framework of Article 243D was intended to bring marginalised communities into Panchayat governance, but educational disqualifications under Article 243F can undercut that purpose.
