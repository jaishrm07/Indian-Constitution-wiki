## Why it matters

Article 307 is the institutional-completion clause of Part XIII. It recognises that the constitutional guarantee of a common internal market — spread across Articles 301 to 304 — may require an ongoing regulatory body to monitor, adjudicate, and enforce the free-trade and anti-discrimination norms of Part XIII, rather than leaving all enforcement to case-by-case judicial review. The article gives Parliament express power to create such a body and to vest it with whatever powers and duties are needed. This is not a minor enabling clause: it reflects a constitutionally contemplated institutional design in which a specialised, expert body continuously polices the boundaries between legitimate State regulation and unconstitutional trade fragmentation.

The provision reflects a constitutional philosophy familiar from other parts of the document: where a complex ongoing regulatory task is contemplated, the framers provided not just the substantive rule but also a mechanism for institutional implementation. Just as Article 279A provides for the GST Council to coordinate indirect-tax policy, Article 280 provides for the Finance Commission to address vertical fiscal imbalances, and Article 263 provides for the Inter-State Council to resolve Centre-State disputes, Article 307 provides for a potential inter-state trade authority with jurisdiction over the specific freedom guaranteed by Articles 301 to 304. However — unlike those other bodies — Parliament has never exercised this power. No Article 307 authority exists.

The framers' intent, as recorded in Constituent Assembly debates, was that an Article 307 body would function somewhat like the Interstate Commerce Commission in the United States — a permanent, expert regulatory commission with investigative, adjudicatory, and rule-making powers over inter-state commerce. The decision not to create such a body at the outset reflected both practical constraints (India was newly independent and building basic state capacity) and the belief that the Supreme Court could handle Part XIII enforcement adequately. Seventy-five years later, neither of those conditions holds with the same force: the Court's Part XIII docket is substantial, and the absence of a body with investigative powers means that many trade-barrier violations never reach adjudication at all.

The significance of Article 307's non-activation is that the entire enforcement of Part XIII has fallen to the judiciary. Every trader who faces an unconstitutional inter-state trade restriction must individually litigate to the High Court or Supreme Court — an expensive and slow process that deters enforcement and allows systemic barriers to persist. An Article 307 authority could investigate complaints, take suo motu action, issue interim relief, and build an institutional record of trade barriers, fundamentally changing the enforcement landscape.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 307 is notably brief and permissive. Parliament "may" create such an authority — the power is discretionary, not obligatory. Parliament chooses the institution's design, powers, and duties, subject only to the constraint that they must be directed towards "carrying out the purposes of Articles 301, 302, 303 and 304." This is a broad mandate. The purposes of those four articles include upholding the freedom of internal trade, permitting public-interest restrictions on that freedom, prohibiting discrimination between states, and regulating State-level trade restrictions. An Article 307 body could accordingly be invested with powers to investigate State levies and regulations, adjudicate trader complaints, issue certifications of compliance, and advise Parliament and State legislatures on the constitutional validity of proposed trade measures.

Constitutionally, an Article 307 authority would have a status analogous to other constitutional-statutory bodies like the Competition Commission of India or the Securities and Exchange Board of India: created by Parliament under an express constitutional authorisation, with powers that derive ultimately from the Constitution. Its decisions on trade-freedom questions would presumably be reviewable by the High Courts under Article 226, but the authority's constitutional anchoring would give its determinations significant weight.

The reference to "purposes" rather than "powers" or "provisions" of Articles 301-304 suggests that the Article 307 body's mandate is substantive rather than merely procedural. Parliament could give it not just the power to enforce existing rules but a broader mission to advance the integrated internal market as a constitutional objective — including through advisory reports, recommended legislative changes, and monitoring mechanisms.

## Key cases

No direct case law turns on Article 307 itself, since no authority has been created under it. The provision features in academic commentary and in deliberations of the Law Commission of India, which has periodically recommended that Parliament exercise its Article 307 power to establish a permanent inter-state trade regulator. In the Constituent Assembly debates, the inclusion of Article 307 was defended as a forward-looking provision — a constitutional invitation for Parliament to build the institutional infrastructure for a common market as India's economy matured and as the limitations of purely judicial enforcement became apparent.

In comparative constitutional law, scholars routinely contrast Article 307's non-activation with the active institutional structures in comparable federal systems. The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission investigates State-level trade restrictions under the Competition and Consumer Act. The European Commission monitors compliance with single-market rules across member states. Canada's internal trade agreements are administered by a permanent committee secretariat. The United States Interstate Commerce Commission, though now defunct in its original form, was operational for more than a century. India alone among comparable federal systems has a constitutional provision for an inter-state trade authority and has left it entirely unused.

The Jindal Stainless (2017) nine-judge bench ruling, which overhauled the compensatory-tax doctrine and increased the number of State levies exposed to Part XIII scrutiny, has created a large volume of constitutional uncertainty about which levies are valid. An Article 307 authority could provide authoritative guidance on this question without the need for individual litigation in each case — but the absence of such a body means that the uncertainty must be worked out case by case in the courts.

## Current significance

The case for activating Article 307 has grown stronger in the post-GST era rather than weaker. The GST Council has effectively become a cooperative-federal body managing indirect taxation, but its mandate is tax coordination, not trade-freedom enforcement. The Article 307 body contemplated by the framers would have a different and broader remit: investigating State-level trade barriers, adjudicating disputes between private parties and State governments about the legality of trade restrictions, and advising Parliament on whether existing laws comply with Articles 301 to 304.

Two developments make this especially relevant today. First, the continued exclusion of petroleum, alcohol, and real estate from GST means that these large sectors remain subject to fragmented State-level regulation with significant inter-state trade implications. Petroleum prices vary by State not only because of State taxes but also because of entry restrictions and licensing requirements that impede the free movement of fuel across borders. Alcohol distribution is effectively segregated by State. Real estate transactions face different stamp duties, registration requirements, and approval processes in different States, creating significant friction for inter-state investment flows. All of these are precisely the kinds of systemic trade barriers that an Article 307 authority would be designed to address.

Second, the Jindal Stainless ruling has created uncertainty about which State levies on non-GST goods are constitutionally valid. A statutory Article 307 authority equipped with investigative and economic-analysis capacities could provide authoritative guidance, reduce the Supreme Court's docket burden in Part XIII matters, and create a predictable framework for State governments seeking to understand which regulatory measures they can lawfully adopt. The continued non-creation of such a body represents a significant gap in India's constitutional implementation — not a deficiency of constitutional design, but a failure of legislative will to complete the institutional architecture the framers envisioned.
