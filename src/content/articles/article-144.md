## What this Article does

Article 144 imposes a constitutional obligation on every civil and judicial authority within the territory of India to act in aid of the Supreme Court. The mandate is absolute in its terms — "shall act in aid" — placing compliance beyond the realm of administrative discretion. It transforms the Supreme Court from a body that merely pronounces law into one whose orders carry behind them the constitutional compulsion of every arm of the State.

The article serves two functions simultaneously. First, it is a command directed at civil and judicial authorities: they have no power to refuse, obstruct, or ignore the Court's directions. Second, it is a source of constitutional authority for the Court itself: it justifies the Court in issuing directions to bodies that are not parties to the litigation before it, because those bodies are constitutionally obligated to act in aid of the Court's exercise of jurisdiction. Without Article 144, the Court's power to issue mandamus or other prerogative-style writs to bodies not before it would rest on shakier foundations.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "all authorities, civil and judicial" is deliberately comprehensive. "Civil authorities" encompasses the executive at every level — the Union, States, Union Territories, local bodies, statutory corporations, and non-statutory bodies discharging public functions. "Judicial authorities" includes every court and tribunal in the country, from district courts upward, ensuring that High Courts cannot defy Supreme Court directions and that subordinate courts are bound to implement the apex court's law under Article 141 as reinforced by Article 144.

"Act in aid" does not simply mean compliance with specific orders. It connotes an active facilitative obligation: an authority must take whatever steps lie within its power to enable the Supreme Court to exercise its jurisdiction. This broader reading supports the Court's practice of issuing institutional directions — setting up monitoring committees, directing the creation of databases, requiring filing of compliance affidavits — to authorities that are instrumentally necessary to give effect to the Court's jurisdiction even though they are not the primary respondents.

The article does not specify sanctions for non-compliance, but the Court's power to punish for contempt under Article 129 provides the enforcement backstop. Read together, Articles 129 and 144 create a system in which every civil and judicial authority can be commanded, and defiance can be punished, ensuring that the Court's constitutional supremacy is not merely theoretical.

## How courts use it

Article 144 is most visibly engaged in three types of situations:

**Structural injunctions and continuing mandamus** — In environmental, prison reform, and housing cases, the Court issues continuing mandamus requiring regular compliance reports. Article 144 is the constitutional basis on which the Court asserts authority to direct State agencies that may not be formal parties to file status reports, constitute task forces, or implement specific programmes.

**Enforcement against investigative agencies** — When investigative agencies are directed to proceed or refrain from proceeding in a certain manner, Article 144 supplies the constitutional compulsion. The CBI, state police forces, and regulatory bodies like SEBI and the ED have all been directed under the Court's supervisory jurisdiction, with Article 144 underpinning the legitimacy of those directions.

**Cross-jurisdictional orders** — Where a Supreme Court order must be implemented across multiple States simultaneously, Article 144 eliminates any argument that a State government is not bound by an order addressed to a different State or to the Union.

## Key cases

**Vineet Narain v. Union of India (1998) 1 SCC 226** — The Supreme Court directed the CBI and other investigating agencies to continue the Jain Hawala diaries investigation free from executive interference. The Court grounded its authority to issue binding directions to the CBI — a Union government body not formally the subject of a writ petition — in part on Article 144, which obligates civil authorities to act in aid of the Court's jurisdiction to ensure the rule of law.

**Union Carbide Corporation v. Union of India (1989) 3 SCC 38 and (1991) 4 SCC 584** — In the Bhopal gas tragedy litigation, the Court approved a settlement and then supervised its implementation. It invoked Article 144 to confirm that both the Union and State governments were constitutionally bound to take the administrative steps necessary to give effect to its orders, including channelling compensation and setting up claims tribunals.

**T.N. Godavarman Thirumulpad v. Union of India (1997) 2 SCC 267** — The ongoing forest conservation matter, running for decades, required simultaneous directions to State governments, the Union Ministry of Environment, forest departments, and district collectors across India. Article 144 provided the constitutional foundation for the Court to treat all these bodies as obligated actors rather than merely requesting their cooperation.

**Paramvir Singh Saini v. Baljit Singh (2020) 10 SCC 471** — The Court directed all States and Union Territories to install CCTV cameras in police stations and magistrates' courts, relying on its power under Article 144 read with Article 32 to issue binding directions to civil authorities that have a constitutional duty to act in aid of its oversight jurisdiction.

## Current significance

Article 144 is the constitutional foundation for the Supreme Court's authority to enforce its own orders — authority that would be hollow without the obligation it creates. In the era of public interest litigation and continuing mandamus, the article is regularly engaged when governments delay or resist implementation of Court directions on issues from air pollution to prison overcrowding to electoral rolls.

A significant unresolved question is whether the article extends to high constitutional functionaries: the President of India, Parliament, or the Election Commission. The Court has issued directions to the Election Commission and has been obeyed, suggesting the obligation reaches all authorities regardless of constitutional standing. However, a situation involving a direct conflict between a Court direction and a Presidential act or Parliamentary legislation has not been definitively resolved. The outer boundaries of Article 144 — at the interface of judicial supremacy and constitutional separation of powers — remain one of Indian constitutional law's most important open questions.
