plainEnglish: >-
  Article 22 has two distinct parts that pull in opposite directions. Clauses
  (1) and (2) protect a person arrested under ordinary criminal law — they must
  be informed of the grounds of arrest, given access to a lawyer of their
  choice, and produced before a magistrate within 24 hours. These are minimum
  procedural guarantees that operate as a constitutional floor beneath the
  criminal procedure code. Clauses (3) through (7) then carve out a parallel
  constitutional universe for preventive detention — the power to detain
  someone not because they have committed an offence but because the state
  believes they will do something harmful. In that universe the ordinary
  safeguards of clauses (1) and (2) do not apply, and the protections are
  considerably weaker — the grounds of detention must be communicated, an
  opportunity to make a representation must be given, and an Advisory Board
  must approve detention beyond three months — but there is no right to know
  the full evidence, no right to legal representation before the Advisory Board,
  and no requirement of a public trial. The preventive detention clauses have
  made Article 22 one of the most controversial provisions in the Constitution,
  enabling a form of imprisonment that bypasses the criminal justice system
  entirely.
officialText: |-
  22. Protection against arrest and detention in certain cases.—(1) No person who is arrested shall be detained in custody without being informed, as soon as may be, of the grounds for such arrest nor shall he be denied the right to consult, and to be defended by, a legal practitioner of his choice.

  (2) Every person who is arrested and detained in custody shall be produced before the nearest magistrate within a period of twenty-four hours of such arrest excluding the time necessary for the journey from the place of arrest to the court of the magistrate and no such person shall be detained in custody beyond the said period without the authority of a magistrate.

  (3) Nothing in clauses (1) and (2) shall apply—

  (a) to any person who for the time being is an enemy alien; or

  (b) to any person who is arrested or detained under any law providing for preventive detention.

  (4) No law providing for preventive detention shall authorise the detention of a person for a longer period than three months unless—

  (a) an Advisory Board consisting of persons who are, or have been, or are qualified to be appointed as, Judges of a High Court has reported before the expiration of the said period of three months that there is in its opinion sufficient cause for such detention:

  Provided that nothing in this sub-clause shall authorise the detention of any person beyond the maximum period prescribed by any law made by Parliament under sub-clause (b) of clause (7); or

  (b) such person is detained in accordance with the provisions of any law made by Parliament under sub-clauses (a) and (b) of clause (7).

  (5) When any person is detained in pursuance of an order made under any law providing for preventive detention, the authority making the order shall, as soon as may be, communicate to such person the grounds on which the order has been made and shall afford him the earliest opportunity of making a representation against the order.

  (6) Nothing in clause (5) shall require the authority making any such order as is referred to in that clause to disclose facts which such authority considers to be against the public interest to disclose.

  (7) Parliament may by law prescribe—

  (a) the circumstances under which, and the class or classes of cases in which, a person may be detained for a period longer than three months under any law providing for preventive detention without obtaining the opinion of an Advisory Board in accordance with the provisions of subclause (a) of clause (4);

  (b) the maximum period for which any person may in any class or classes of cases be detained under any law providing for preventive detention; and

  (c) the procedure to be followed by an Advisory Board in an inquiry under sub-clause (a) of clause (4).

## Why it matters

When a state arrests someone, it exercises the most coercive power that government possesses — the power to physically remove a person from their family, their work, and their community and place them in a cell. Article 22 exists to impose constitutional minimums on how that power is exercised. The requirement that grounds be communicated, that a lawyer be available, and that a magistrate review the detention within 24 hours are not bureaucratic formalities — they are the constitutional barriers between a rule of law state and arbitrary imprisonment.

For most arrests under ordinary criminal law, Article 22's clauses (1) and (2) function as enforceable constitutional standards alongside the Code of Criminal Procedure. They provide a constitutional remedy where the ordinary procedural code fails — a person who is arrested and held incommunicado without being told why, or who is denied a lawyer, or who is kept for days without magistrate production, can seek a writ of habeas corpus directly, invoking both Article 22 and Article 21.

The second part of Article 22 — the preventive detention clauses — represents an accommodation that many constitutional scholars consider a fundamental compromise of the liberty values the framers sought to enshrine. The Constituent Assembly's debates show that Jawaharlal Nehru and others argued that the new state needed the power to detain without trial those who threatened the security of the nation — a response to the violence of partition and the fears of communist insurgency. The framers of the preventive detention clauses sought to constrain that power by building advisory board review and the communication of grounds into the constitutional text. But the constraints have often proved weak in practice, and the coexistence of normal criminal procedure and preventive detention within the same article has always been constitutionally uneasy.

The consequences of preventive detention's constitutional legitimacy have been far-reaching. Laws like the Maintenance of Internal Security Act (MISA), the National Security Act (NSA), the Public Safety Act (PSA) in Jammu and Kashmir, and the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA) all derive at least some of their constitutional legitimacy from Article 22. The individuals detained under these laws — political opponents, separatist activists, journalists, students — often spend months or years in detention while challenge proceedings make their way through courts.

## Reading the provision

Clauses (1) and (2) set three specific requirements for arrests under ordinary (criminal) law. First, grounds of arrest must be communicated "as soon as may be" — the phrase creates a temporal obligation that is flexible but real; unreasonable delay in communicating grounds violates Article 22(1). Second, the right to consult and be defended by a lawyer of one's choice is constitutionally guaranteed — the right to choose one's own lawyer (not merely any lawyer) is explicit. Third, production before the nearest magistrate within 24 hours (excluding travel time) is mandatory — this requires the physical presentation of the person before a judicial officer with authority to decide on further detention or release.

The word "arrested" in Article 22 refers to formal arrest under penal or criminal law, not to every form of state-compelled restriction of movement. A person summoned by a tax authority or detained by immigration officials at the border is not necessarily "arrested" within Article 22 — the provision targets criminal arrests. Courts have, however, given the word a broad rather than narrow construction, looking at the substance of the restriction rather than its formal legal label.

Clause (3) creates the critical exception: the safeguards in clauses (1) and (2) do not apply to enemy aliens or to persons arrested under preventive detention laws. This exception is complete — there is no modified application; the safeguards simply do not apply. The justification at the time of drafting was that preventive detention, being non-punitive and protective rather than criminal, required different constitutional treatment. Critics have always argued that the practical experience of preventive detention — which involves all the physical realities of imprisonment — makes this distinction constitutionally unsustainable.

Clause (4) limits preventive detention to three months without Advisory Board approval. The Advisory Board must consist of persons who are or have been or are qualified to be High Court judges — an attempt to ensure judicial-quality oversight. However, the Advisory Board's proceedings are not required to be public, the detainee does not have a constitutional right to legal representation before it (though many statutes now provide this), and the Advisory Board's finding is advisory rather than binding in all circumstances.

Clause (5) requires communication of grounds of detention and an earliest opportunity to make a representation. But clause (6) immediately qualifies this: the detaining authority need not disclose facts that it considers to be against the public interest to disclose. The combination of clauses (5) and (6) means that the detainee receives only a partial account of the case against them — the state can withhold exactly the facts that are most central to the decision to detain. Courts have required that the detaining authority not make the grounds so vague or general as to be meaningless, but the scope of the public interest exception has given the state considerable latitude.

## Key cases

### The founding era and the Gopalan framework

**A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950)** — The Constitution's first major liberty case arose just weeks after it came into force. Gopalan, a communist leader, was detained under the Preventive Detention Act 1950 and challenged his detention as violating Articles 13, 19, 21, and 22. The Supreme Court, by a five-to-one majority, upheld the detention and established a framework that would govern liberty jurisprudence for nearly three decades. The majority held that each article in Part III covers its own specific subject matter; Article 21 covers personal liberty against criminal procedure, and Article 22 provides the specific constitutional framework for preventive detention. The Court refused to read Articles 19, 21, and 22 together — each was a self-contained provision. If the detention law complied with Article 22, no separate scrutiny under Articles 19 or 21 was required. The result was to restrict review of preventive detention to the specific procedural requirements of Article 22 and to insulate the substantive decision to detain from broader liberty scrutiny. Justice Fazl Ali's famous dissent argued that Articles 21 and 22 should be read together and that "procedure established by law" in Article 21 requires fairness, a view vindicated 28 years later in Maneka Gandhi.

**Hadibandhu Das v. District Magistrate, Cuttack (1969)** — The Supreme Court held that the grounds of detention communicated to a detainee must be clear, specific, and sufficient to enable the detainee to make an effective representation. A detention order that gives only vague or general grounds — "in the interest of public order" without specifying any concrete act or apprehension — fails the constitutional requirement of clause (5) and is invalid. This decision established that courts will look behind the detention order and examine whether the grounds, as communicated, are substantive rather than formulaic. It was a significant step toward substantive review of preventive detention within the Article 22 framework.

**Golam Hossain v. Commissioner of Police, Calcutta (1974)** — The Court held that where the grounds of detention were communicated in a language the detainee did not understand, the constitutional requirement of clause (5) was not satisfied. The right to receive grounds and to make a representation is meaningless if the detainee cannot understand the document they receive. This principle — that the communication must be in a language comprehended by the detainee — has been consistently applied and is one of the most frequently successful grounds of challenge to detention orders.

### The Emergency and its aftermath

**ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla (1976)** — The Emergency case, decided at the nadir of Indian constitutionalism. The Supreme Court, by four to one, held that during a proclaimed emergency with the right to move courts suspended under Article 359, there was no right to challenge preventive detention in court — not even on grounds of mala fides, or that the detention was directed at the wrong person. The majority held that the Presidential Order under Article 359 effectively extinguished the right to invoke Article 22 before courts. Justice H R Khanna's solitary dissent — that the right to life and liberty exists independently of any constitutional provision and cannot be suspended by presidential order — is considered one of the great moments of Indian judicial courage. The decision was overtaken by the 44th Amendment and subsequent doctrine but stands as the starkest example of how Article 22's preventive detention framework can be used to deny all judicial protection for liberty.

**Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)** — The doctrinal revolution primarily about Article 21, but directly affecting the standard of review applicable to detention and arrest. By holding that Article 21's "procedure established by law" must be fair, just, and reasonable — and that Articles 14, 19, and 21 form an integrated framework — Maneka Gandhi effectively overruled the Gopalan approach of treating Article 22 as a self-contained code. Post-Maneka Gandhi, a detention that complies formally with Article 22 may still be challenged under Article 21 for procedural unfairness and under Article 14 for arbitrariness. The two provisions no longer operate in separate silos.

### Post-Emergency consolidation

**A.K. Roy v. Union of India (1982)** — A Constitution Bench considered the National Security Act 1980, enacted after the Emergency, and the constitutional validity of its provisions including extended detention and the scope of the Advisory Board. The Court upheld the Act but held that the Advisory Board proceedings must comply with natural justice principles to the extent consistent with the statutory framework, and that the Board's composition requirement — judges or persons qualified to be judges of High Courts — must be strictly maintained. The judgment confirmed that post-Emergency preventive detention legislation continues to operate within the Article 22 framework but is subject to substantive scrutiny going beyond mere procedural compliance.

**Kartar Singh v. State of Punjab (1994)** — The Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act (TADA) came before a Constitution Bench for a comprehensive examination of its compatibility with Articles 21 and 22. The Court upheld TADA's extended detention provisions and special court procedures against Article 22 challenges, holding that the gravity of the terrorist threat and the exigencies of evidence gathering justified departure from normal procedural timelines. However, the Court imposed conditions — confessions must be recorded before a magistrate, confessions made under TADA's special provisions must be subject to some form of judicial scrutiny, and the Advisory Board process for extended detention must be genuine rather than pro forma. The case illustrated the Court's approach of upholding stringent security legislation while imposing conditions designed to ensure that the constitutional safeguards are not entirely illusory.

**Sayed Taher Ahemad Naqshbandi v. State of Jammu and Kashmir (2023)** — In the ongoing stream of habeas corpus petitions challenging PSA detentions in J&K, the Supreme Court reiterated that grounds of detention must be specific and must have a direct nexus to the acts or apprehensions that justify the detention order. Reliance on stale or remote events as grounds for detention was held to be constitutionally impermissible, and the Court set aside the detention order. The case is representative of the Court's post-2019 engagement with J&K preventive detention, where the volume of detention orders tested the judicial review mechanism intensively.

## Current significance

Preventive detention laws remain among the most litigated provisions in Indian constitutional practice. The National Security Act 1980, applicable across India, and the Public Safety Act 1978, applicable in Jammu and Kashmir, generate habeas corpus petitions in large numbers before High Courts and the Supreme Court. The grounds most frequently succeeding in these challenges are: failure to communicate grounds in a language the detainee understands; reliance on grounds that are vague, general, or insufficient to constitute genuine grounds; failure to supply the detainee with the documents relied on; and unreasonable delay between the order and its communication.

The Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act raises distinct constitutional questions under Article 22 because it combines elements of both criminal prosecution (the offences are tried by special courts under the normal adversarial process) and preventive detention (bail is extremely difficult to obtain, especially for offences attracting section 43D(5), which requires denial of bail if there is a prima facie case). The de facto preventive character of UAPA detention — where accused persons spend years in custody before trial — generates Article 22 challenges on the ground that the substance, if not the form, of the detention is preventive rather than punitive.

The Advisory Board mechanism, which is the constitutional check on extended preventive detention, has been the subject of persistent criticism. The Board's proceedings are not public, the detainee does not have a constitutional right to legal representation before it in all cases, the Board does not give reasons for its conclusions, and its decision is not directly amenable to appeal. The question of whether the Advisory Board, as currently constituted and functioning, provides the genuine judicial-quality review that Article 22(4)(a)'s composition requirement implies has been raised in scholarly commentary and some judicial opinions, but has not been squarely addressed by the Supreme Court.

The relationship between Article 22 and bail jurisprudence under special laws — including the question of whether denial of bail in prolonged pre-trial custody violates Article 21 independently of Article 22 — has become increasingly active. The Supreme Court has in recent years shown greater willingness to grant bail in long-duration UAPA and PMLA cases on the ground that Article 21's right to speedy trial is being violated, using the Maneka Gandhi integrated framework rather than Article 22 alone. This development illustrates how the doctrinal separation between ordinary arrest (governed by Articles 21 and 22(1)-(2)) and preventive detention (governed by Articles 22(3)-(7)) has become less rigid in practice, as courts increasingly apply Article 21's substantive fairness standard across both domains.
