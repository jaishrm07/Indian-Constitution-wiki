## What this Article does

Article 36 performs a structural function that is easy to underestimate. Without it, one might argue that "the State" in Part IV — which is primarily hortatory and non-justiciable — should be read more narrowly than in Part III, or that local bodies, statutory corporations, and other authorities are not duty-bearers under the directives. By explicitly cross-referencing Article 12, the Constitution ensures that the same expansive definition applies in both Parts. This matters practically whenever courts read directive principles into the content of fundamental rights: the obligation created is owed by the same comprehensive set of state actors in both cases.

Part IV was the first Part in the Constitution to contain its own definition section, which indicates how carefully the framers were attending to the institutional question of who the directives bind. The directives address obligations of extraordinary social and economic breadth — equal pay for equal work, adequate means of livelihood, just conditions of labour, provision of legal aid, organisation of village panchayats, promotion of educational and economic interests of weaker sections. Each of these directives generates obligations that must fall somewhere in the institutional structure. Article 36 ensures the answer is the same answer as in Part III: the full extended definition of State under Article 12.

## Article 12's extended definition and its reach

Article 12 defines "the State" to include the Government and Parliament of India, the Government and Legislature of each State, and all local or other authorities within the territory of India or under the control of the Government of India. The Supreme Court has, over decades, expanded the "other authorities" category well beyond its literal text to include statutory corporations, government companies exercising significant public functions, and bodies funded or substantially controlled by the State. The operative tests developed by the Court ask whether the entity is an instrumentality or agency of the State — whether the State's financial, functional, and administrative control over the body is pervasive enough to treat it as part of the State apparatus rather than a genuinely independent institution.

Article 36's cross-reference means every extension of Article 12's definition that courts have developed applies equally in the Part IV context. When a court considers whether a public-sector undertaking has complied with DPSP-related obligations — for example, whether it provides equal pay for equal work under Article 39(d), or maintains just and humane conditions of work under Article 42 — the question of whether it is "the State" for Part IV purposes is answered by the same tests used for Part III.

## The instrumentality of State test

The most significant doctrinal contribution to Article 12 — and therefore to Article 36 — is the "instrumentality of State" test developed through a series of cases beginning in the 1970s. The test emerged because the literal text of Article 12 did not clearly resolve whether bodies that were not formally government departments or legislatures could be part of the State. The Court developed a functional test that looks past the formal legal structure to ask whether the body, in substance, exercises a public function under government control.

**Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib Sehravardi (1981)** — A Constitution Bench identified a multi-factor test for determining whether a body is an instrumentality or agency of the State. The relevant factors include: whether the entity was created by statute or statutory instrument; whether the State holds the entire share capital or a dominant financial interest; whether the entire funding comes from the government; whether the entity enjoys a monopoly status conferred by the State; whether the functions performed are governmental or public functions; whether the government has deep pervasive control over the management and policies of the body; and whether the functions transferred to the corporation were previously performed by a government department. No single factor is conclusive; the test is holistic and the presence of several indicators of State character will ordinarily suffice. The Ajay Hasia test is the foundation on which courts assess whether any entity — however described formally — is the State for both Part III and, via Article 36, Part IV purposes.

**Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench reconsidered the position of autonomous scientific and research bodies registered as societies but substantially funded and controlled by the Government. The Court held that the "instrumentality of State" test must be applied contextually and that CSIR laboratories, despite their society registration, are part of the State because of the depth of government financial and functional control over them. The judgment refined the Ajay Hasia framework by emphasising that the question is whether the entity is financially, functionally, and administratively dominated by or under the control of the government. If it is, the entity is the State regardless of its formal legal form.

## How courts use Article 36

Article 36 rarely appears as the principal ratio of a decision, but it functions as a background assumption in every case that reads directive principles alongside fundamental rights. When the Court held in Unni Krishnan J.P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993) that the right to education is part of Article 21, it was simultaneously directing both the Central government and state governments as duty-bearers — a coherent obligation that the Article 12/36 framework makes possible. Similarly, when public-health directives under Article 47 are used to reinforce Article 21 rights, the obligation falls on state health ministries, local bodies, and state-controlled hospitals all at once because Article 36 ensures they are all "the State" for Part IV purposes.

In public interest litigation addressing bonded labour, environmental pollution, prison conditions, or access to public services, courts frequently issue directions against multiple respondents at different levels of government simultaneously. The ability to do this without separately establishing the State character of each respondent rests on the Article 12/36 framework creating a unified definition across both Parts.

## Key cases

**State of Madras v. V.G. Row (1952)** — One of the earliest cases to trace the relationship between Parts III and IV, the Court confirmed that "the State" in Part IV carries the same expansive meaning as in Part III, so the legislature, executive, and local authorities are all within the reach of the directives. This reading has been taken as settled constitutional doctrine in all subsequent cases harmonising the two Parts.

**Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib Sehravardi (1981)** — The Constitution Bench's articulation of the instrumentality of State test provides the principal analytical framework for determining who is "the State" for Article 36 purposes when the entity in question is not a traditional government department. The multi-factor test — examining financial dominance, functional control, public purpose, and administrative supervision — applies equally to determine whether a body bears directive principle obligations under Part IV.

**Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002)** — The seven-judge bench settled the position for autonomous institutions created by or substantially dependent on the government, confirming that the formal legal form of the entity (society, company, statutory body) is not determinative. The contextual assessment of control and public function extends the Article 36 definition to cover entities that might not appear obviously governmental.

**Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India (1984)** — In directing the Central and state governments to identify and rehabilitate bonded labourers under Articles 21, 23, and the directive principles, the Court treated both levels of government as co-obligors under the Constitution's rights and directives framework — an approach that Article 36's definitional consistency makes doctrinally straightforward.

**Zee Telefilms Ltd. v. Union of India (2005)** — The Court held that the Board of Control for Cricket in India is not the State for Article 12 purposes because it lacks sufficient government control. This decision illustrates the outer limit of the Article 12/36 definition: entities that regulate a private activity, even with statutory recognition, do not become part of the State without the requisite degree of governmental domination. The BCCI ruling is the reference point for arguments about extending the definition to private platform or infrastructure entities.

## Federalism implications

Article 36's incorporation of Article 12's "other authorities" category has significant federalism implications. State enterprises, municipalities, panchayats, and statutory bodies funded by state governments are all duty-bearers under both the fundamental rights and the directive principles frameworks. In practice, state governments are the primary implementers of most directive principle programmes — food security, education, health care, rural employment — and Article 36's definition ensures there is no gap between who the directive addresses and who the courts can direct to act.

The consequence is that state-government corporations and statutory bodies established under state law are as fully bound by directive principle obligations as the central government, provided they meet the instrumentality test. A State Electricity Distribution Company, a State Housing Board, or a State Road Transport Corporation — all entities through which directive principles relating to employment conditions, equal pay, and social security are relevant — are part of "the State" by virtue of Article 36 read with Article 12.

## Current significance

Article 36's definitional role becomes more significant as courts expand the justiciable content of fundamental rights through directive principles. Every time a court holds that Article 21 encompasses a socio-economic entitlement — health care, housing, a pollution-free environment, dignified working conditions — the state obliged to deliver that entitlement is the state as defined by Article 36, and the convergence of duty-bearers between Parts III and IV ensures directions can be issued comprehensively across all tiers of government.

The question of who counts as "the State" is being actively revisited in the context of digital infrastructure, telecommunications, and large platform intermediaries. Where private entities are heavily regulated, licensed, or financially supported by the government, or where they exercise functions that have historically been governmental — public communications, universal service obligations, data infrastructure — arguments are made that they should be drawn within the Article 12 definition and therefore within Article 36's scope for Part IV purposes. Courts have so far declined to extend the definition to purely private actors, even large ones, but the ongoing expansion of the regulatory state and the government's growing financial and regulatory entanglement with digital infrastructure creates continuing pressure on the outer edge of the test. The intersection of Article 36's definitional reach with constitutional obligations imposed on hybrid public-private entities is likely to generate significant litigation as the Indian State's relationship with digital infrastructure and platform governance develops.
