## Why it matters

Article 220 operationalises the constitutional principle that judicial office is a trust, not merely an employment. A judge who has exercised power over litigants, advocates, and lower court officers for years cannot simply return to professional practice in those same courts without creating acute risks of real or perceived advantage. The bar applies for life after retirement from permanent appointment — it is not a cooling-off period of fixed duration but a permanent restriction on the forums in which the former judge may appear as a professional advocate.

The calibration of the exception is equally significant. Allowing practice before the Supreme Court and other High Courts acknowledges that distinguished advocates who served as judges should not be entirely excluded from further legal life. The conflict-of-interest concern is concentrated in the specific court where the judge sat and its subordinate hierarchy — not in co-equal High Courts or in the apex court — and the exception targets that concern precisely.

## Reading the provision

The key phrase is "plead or act in any court or before any authority." "Plead" covers appearing as advocate or counsel; "act" covers a broader range of representative functions. "Any authority in India" has been read widely to include statutory tribunals, regulatory bodies, and quasi-judicial authorities — reaching well beyond traditional courts into the increasingly important landscape of specialised adjudicative bodies.

The restriction applies only after tenure as a permanent judge. During any period of service as an additional judge under Article 224, the bar has not yet attached. This creates an asymmetry — a judge who retires after serving only as an additional judge faces no Article 220 restriction, while one who was ever made permanent faces a lifetime bar in specified forums.

Article 220 must be read alongside Article 124(7), which imposes a stricter restriction on retired Supreme Court judges: they may not plead or act before any court or authority in India at all. The Article 220 bar is thus less absolute — it still permits practice before the Supreme Court and other High Courts — reflecting that the conflict-of-interest concern for a High Court judge is specific to the court where they sat, not universal across all jurisdictions.

The Explanation clarifies that High Courts in former Part B States prior to the Seventh Amendment are excluded from the definition for historical transitional reasons tied to the 1956 reorganisation of States along linguistic lines.

## Key cases

**A. K. Kraipak v. Union of India (1969)** — The Court's reasoning about judicial disqualification based on prior relationships affirmed the broad constitutional principle underlying Article 220 — that a judge who has personal or professional connections with parties before the court is subject to disqualification, and that post-tenure restrictions on the judicial community serve the constitutional value of institutional impartiality.

**Supreme Court Bar Association v. Union of India (1998)** — The Court observed that Article 220 represents a specific constitutional judgment that judicial independence and institutional integrity are worth protecting through restrictions on post-judicial professional life. The restriction cannot be challenged as an unconstitutional restraint on trade or profession under Article 19(1)(g) because the constitutional provision is itself the source and the limit.

**S. P. Gupta v. Union of India (1981) (First Judges Case)** — The Supreme Court's expansive discussion of judicial independence provides the most important constitutional backdrop for Article 220. The Court observed that the credibility of the judiciary depends not only on the conduct of judges while in office but on the perception that judges have no incentive to decide cases in favour of those who might engage them after retirement.

**L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997)** — In holding that the power of judicial review is part of the basic structure, the Court reinforced the importance of maintaining institutional independence at all levels of the judicial hierarchy. Articles 220 and 124(7) are understood as constitutional expressions of that independence requirement extended into the post-retirement sphere.

## Current significance

Article 220's prohibition is confined to pleading or acting in courts and before authorities. It does not restrict retired High Court judges from accepting appointments to executive bodies, commissions, arbitration panels, or government committees. This narrow scope has been criticised as inadequate in an era when retired judges routinely move into high-paying commercial arbitration practices, join corporate advisory boards, or accept government-nominated positions on statutory bodies.

A significant contemporary debate concerns whether Article 220 covers private commercial arbitration at all. Arbitration is not practice "in any court," and an arbitral tribunal is arguably not "any authority in India" in the traditional sense. The counter-argument draws on the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, which gives arbitral awards the force of a court decree, and notes that parties before an arbitrator who is a former High Court judge enjoy advantages in proceedings that may return to that court for enforcement or challenge. The Law Commission's Report No. 230 (2009) flagged the arbitration gap as requiring legislative attention, but Parliament has not acted.

Critics argue that the revolving door between the judiciary and the executive or private sector undermines the perception of judicial independence — particularly when judges near retirement preside over matters involving parties or governments that may later engage them. The contrast with other common law jurisdictions is often cited: in the United Kingdom, strong convention limits post-retirement appearances; Canada and Australia impose various cooling-off periods. India's constitutional text is more directly restrictive within its scope but silent on the expanding post-judicial world of arbitration, regulation, and executive appointment.
