## Why it matters

The Indian Constitution distributes legislative power through three Lists — the Union List, the State List, and the Concurrent List — but no enumeration can be exhaustive. Article 248 ensures that every possible subject of regulation is covered: if a matter is not in List II or List III, Parliament has exclusive power over it. This prevents regulatory gaps and maintains a coherent federal hierarchy where the Union is the default legislator for genuinely new subjects of governance.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) places residuary power with Parliament, subject to Article 246A (which introduced the GST framework and carved out goods and services tax as a concurrent taxing power). The reference to matters "not enumerated in the Concurrent List or State List" — rather than not enumerated at all — is significant: Parliament's full power over List I entries is established by Article 246, not by Article 248. Residuary power under Article 248 is the fallback for matters absent from both List II and List III.

Clause (2) explicitly extends residuary power to taxation, confirming that Parliament may impose taxes on subjects not listed in either List. This was consequential in the pre-GST era for subjects such as service tax, which was levied by Parliament under the residuary head for decades before being absorbed into GST.

## Residuary power in practice

Entry 97 of the Union List is the formal textual location of residuary power, cross-referencing Article 248. Courts have consistently held that a challenge to Union tax legislation must first test whether the subject falls under any List I entry, then List III, before invoking Article 248 residuary power. The residuary head is truly a last resort in legislative competence analysis.

Subjects that have been held to fall under residuary power include: television broadcasting (not enumerated when the Constitution was adopted), service tax on commercial services (before the 101st Amendment), offshore development activities, and aspects of cybercrime not covered by specific List entries. Cryptocurrency and artificial intelligence regulation are current candidates for residuary treatment.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. H.S. Dhillon (1972) 2 SCC 44** — The Supreme Court held that a challenge to a Union tax law must first exhaust List I before turning to Article 248. The Court ruled that wealth tax on agricultural land was validly covered by residuary power since it fell outside the State List entry for land revenue. The judgment established the analytical sequence: List I first, then residuary — a State cannot claim that a matter belongs to its legislative domain simply because Parliament has not placed it in List I.

**Indian Express Newspapers v. Union of India (1985) 1 SCC 641** — The Court reaffirmed that regulatory schemes for print media and advertisement taxes could be grounded in List I and residuary entries, demonstrating that Parliament's residuary competence under Article 248 can reach economically significant activities even when specific List I entries do not precisely cover them.

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India** — The Court reaffirmed that the Seventh Schedule Lists must be interpreted broadly and purposively, and that Article 248 fills any gap that remains. A State cannot claim a residual field of its own because India's constitutional design places all residuary power squarely with Parliament.

## How courts use it

When the constitutional validity of a Union law on a subject without a clear List I entry is challenged, courts first examine whether a broad reading of any List I entry covers the subject. If not, they turn to List III. Only if both fail does residuary power under Article 248 come into play. This sequence ensures that the Lists are given full effect before the residuary head is engaged. Courts have generally been reluctant to strike down Union laws on residuary subjects as long as the subject is genuinely absent from the State and Concurrent Lists.

## Current significance

Emerging regulatory domains — cryptocurrency, artificial intelligence governance, and certain aspects of space commerce — have no dedicated entry in any Schedule List, making Article 248 the live constitutional hook for Union legislation on these subjects. The question of whether a central cryptocurrency bill draws power from List I entries on currency and foreign exchange or from the residuary head is actively contested in policy and legal circles. The interaction between Article 248 and Article 246A (GST) also continues to generate litigation over which tier of government may tax novel digital supplies.
