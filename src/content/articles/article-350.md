## Why it matters

Article 350 is where the Constitution's language policy touches the individual citizen most directly. All the provisions of Part XVII about official languages at the Union and State levels, about inter-governmental communication, and about the language of courts and legislation address how government works internally. Article 350 addresses how citizens communicate with government. It guarantees that a person cannot be shut out of the administrative process simply because they do not speak the official language of the authority they are addressing.

The provision has particular importance for three overlapping groups: linguistic minority communities within States whose official language is different from their mother tongue; migrant workers who have moved to States whose official language they do not speak; and tribal communities whose languages may be recognised neither as Eighth Schedule languages nor as State official languages. For all three groups, Article 350 is the constitutional guarantee that their voices will reach the government even when their language does not match the official medium.

The article's practical significance has grown considerably with the expanding scope of government: as the State has inserted itself into more aspects of citizens' lives through welfare programmes, regulatory systems, and digital infrastructure, the number of occasions on which a citizen must submit a representation to a government authority has multiplied. Each of those occasions is governed by the Article 350 guarantee. When grievance portals accept complaints only in Hindi or in the State official language, when RTI applications in minority languages are rejected without processing, when revenue records petitions are returned for not being in the official script — each such instance raises an Article 350 question.

## Reading the provision

The article uses the formulation "any of the languages used in the Union or in the State." This is deliberately broader than "official language." The languages "used in the Union" encompass all the languages in which the Union government and its officials communicate — including English, Hindi, and the constitutional languages used in official correspondence. The languages "used in the State" encompass any language actually in use within that State, not merely the State's official language under Article 345 or the Eighth Schedule languages. A representation submitted in a tribal language actively spoken within a State is protected by Article 350, even if that language is not an official language of the State or of the Union.

The right to submit a representation is an active obligation on authorities: they must receive and process the representation, not merely not refuse it. An authority that receives a representation in a minority language and takes no action — treating the language barrier as a sufficient reason for inaction — violates the spirit of Article 350 even if it does not formally refuse to receive the document.

Article 350 does not, however, guarantee a response in the same language. Courts have consistently held that the right under Article 350 is the right to submit; whether the authority must respond in the same language depends on other provisions including State official language laws and the Official Languages Act 1963. An authority that receives a Tamil-language petition and responds in Hindi or English has not necessarily violated Article 350, as long as the response genuinely engages with the substance of the representation.

The article covers "any officer or authority" of the Union or a State — a deliberately comprehensive formulation. It covers district collectors, sub-divisional officers, public-sector undertakings, statutory commissions, police authorities, and every other arm of the State. It is not limited to formal grievance redressal forums or to officers above a particular level. A person submitting a complaint at the ward office of a municipal corporation, a petition to a passport authority, or a representation to a district administration land records office is covered by Article 350's guarantee.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's discussion of Article 350 was relatively brief but the provision reflected a genuine consensus that the language compromise at the Union level — designating Hindi while preserving English — should not be used to shut citizens out of their government. The concern was practical: if a citizen in Tamil Nadu or Andhra Pradesh submitted a complaint to a central government office in Tamil or Telugu, and that office refused to process it because the official language was Hindi or English, the citizen would be effectively denied access to government. Members across the linguistic divide agreed that this outcome would be unjust and constitutionally unacceptable.

The formulation "any of the languages used" rather than "the official language or any of the official languages" was chosen deliberately to provide maximum coverage. It reflected the Assembly's awareness that the list of official languages was a political construct and that the lived linguistic reality of India's citizens extended well beyond any list. A citizen should not have to know the distinction between an official language and a merely "used" language when submitting a grievance to a government office.

Members from minority communities pressed for the provision to be coupled with an obligation on government to respond in a language the petitioner could understand. That demand was ultimately not incorporated in Article 350, leaving the response-language question to other provisions and statutory arrangements. The failure to include a response-language obligation has been a persistent limitation on the article's practical effectiveness, as an authority can technically comply with Article 350 by receiving a representation while responding in the official language — which the petitioner may not be able to read.

## Key cases

**Gurmail Singh v. State of Punjab (Punjab and Haryana HC)** — The court held that the right under Article 350 to submit representations in any language used in the State is enforceable, and that an authority's refusal to receive or process a representation solely because it was not in the official State language violates the constitutional guarantee, particularly where the language used is widely spoken within the State.

**T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The Supreme Court's discussion of the constitutional language framework included the observation that Article 350's right to petition in one's own language is part of the broader guarantee of linguistic respect and non-subordination, confirming that Part XVII as a whole protects the individual's linguistic identity in dealings with government, not only the State's power to set an official language.

**Lok Prahari v. Union of India (PIL on grievance access)** — Public-interest litigation concerning access to grievance redressal mechanisms in rural areas raised Article 350 as a constitutional floor: State grievance portals and district collector complaint systems that function only in the State's official language were challenged as failing to honour the constitutional guarantee for linguistic minority residents.

**Urdu Representation Cases (Allahabad HC, various)** — Multiple cases before the Allahabad High Court concerning the right of Urdu speakers to submit representations to revenue and district administration authorities in Urdu have confirmed that the Article 350 right covers representations in Urdu in Uttar Pradesh, where Urdu is widely used, notwithstanding that Hindi is the State official language. These cases have also confirmed that the presidential direction under Article 347 recognising Urdu in certain districts reinforces the Article 350 right by clarifying that Urdu is a language "used in the State" for Article 350 purposes.

**Right to Information Act complaints in minority languages (various High Courts)** — RTI applications filed in Urdu, Tamil, and other non-official languages in States where those languages are spoken have generated administrative and judicial guidance affirming that the Article 350 right extends to RTI applications and that public information officers cannot reject applications solely on the basis that they are not in the official State language.

## Article 350 in the digital governance era

The article's significance has grown sharply with the digitisation of government grievance mechanisms. Online portals, mobile applications, and helpline systems designed for grievance redress have typically been built in the State's official language and, at the Union level, in Hindi and English. A citizen who cannot operate in those languages faces a de facto barrier to accessing grievance mechanisms, even though Article 350 guarantees a language-neutral right of access.

Several High Courts and the Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities have flagged this problem. The constitutional guarantee of Article 350 requires that digital grievance systems be accessible in the languages actually used in the State, not only the official language. Implementation of multilingual grievance portals under the RTI, the Consumer Protection Act, and major welfare scheme grievance systems has been identified as an Article 350 obligation. The National e-Governance Plan's commitment to multilingual digital services is the administrative response to this constitutional requirement, but implementation has been uneven.

The intersection of Article 350 with the new Digital Personal Data Protection Act is also relevant: requirements that privacy notices, consent forms, and data-subject rights requests be available in languages citizens understand overlap with the Article 350 baseline for citizen-government communication. As government increasingly relies on digital infrastructure for service delivery and regulatory compliance, Article 350's language-access guarantee must be read as extending to the digital interfaces through which citizens now interact with the State.

## Current significance

Article 350's practical force is tested wherever government services are digitised in a single language, where helplines and complaint systems operate only in the official State language, or where district-level authorities informally reject non-official-language petitions. Right to Information requests, consumer complaints to State commissions, and revenue records petitions are areas where the right under Article 350 is most commonly invoked. The article also intersects with the Right to Fair Compensation Act and other statutory grievance mechanisms that require notifications and representations to be filed in prescribed formats, raising questions about linguistic access that Article 350 is meant to resolve in favour of the citizen.

The Commissioner for Linguistic Minorities' annual reports consistently flag implementation gaps: authorities in linguistically diverse States routinely process representations only in the official language, leaving minority-language petitioners without effective recourse. The gap between the constitutional guarantee and administrative practice is the central challenge Article 350 faces. Whether stronger judicial enforcement, administrative training, or legislative elaboration of the article's requirements is the right response remains an open question in constitutional practice.
