officialText: |-
  243ZF. Continuance of existing laws and Municipalities.—

  Notwithstanding anything in this Part, any provision of any law relating to Municipalities in force in a State immediately before the commencement of the Constitution (Seventy-fourth Amendment) Act, 1992, which is inconsistent with the provisions of this Part, shall continue to be in force until amended or repealed by a competent Legislature or other competent authority or until the expiration of one year from such commencement, whichever is earlier:

  Provided that all the Municipalities existing immediately before such commencement shall continue till the expiration of their duration, unless sooner dissolved by a resolution passed to that effect by the Legislative Assembly of that State or, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, by each House of the Legislature of that State.

## Why it matters

When a new constitutional regime takes effect, there is an inevitable gap between what the new framework requires and what pre-existing law provides. At the commencement of the Seventy-Fourth Amendment Act on 1 June 1993, every State had its own municipal legislation — Bombay Municipal Corporation Act, Karnataka Municipalities Act, Madhya Pradesh Municipal Corporation Act, and so on — many of which were inconsistent with Part IXA's requirements on seat reservation, term of office, ward committees, State Finance Commissions, and election management. A rule of immediate invalidity for all inconsistent State laws would have created a legislative vacuum and administrative chaos: municipal bodies operating under those laws would suddenly have had no legal basis for their continued functioning.

Article 243ZF provides the transitional solution. It creates a grace period of one year — ending on 1 June 1994 — during which inconsistent municipal law continued to operate. This gave State legislatures a defined window to enact conforming municipal legislation. The article is not a permanent exemption; it is time-limited. Once the year expired, Part IXA's requirements applied directly and any still-inconsistent State law ceased to have effect. In practice, several States were slow to amend their municipal laws, leading to disputes about the effect of pre-existing legislation after the one-year period expired.

## The term-protection proviso

The proviso protects existing municipalities from being dissolved simply because the constitutional framework changed. A municipality that was validly constituted before the Seventy-Fourth Amendment and was in the middle of its term at commencement could not be dissolved merely to bring the new Part IXA framework into effect earlier. Only a formal legislative resolution — passed by the Legislative Assembly, or in the case of bicameral Legislatures, by each House — could end the term early. This is a significant protection: it prevents the executive from using the transition to the new framework as cover for suppressing an inconvenient opposition-controlled municipal body.

The proviso reflects a general constitutional principle running through the panchayat and municipality provisions: once elected, a local body's term cannot be cut short by executive action. The same principle appears in Articles 243E and 243U, which prohibit dissolution of panchayats and municipalities except by legislative resolution. The transitional context of Article 243ZF extends this principle backward in time to cover bodies elected under pre-constitutional-amendment law.

## Comparison with Article 243N

Article 243ZF is the municipal equivalent of Article 243N, which provides the same transitional protection for existing panchayats and panchayat laws under Part IX (the Seventy-Third Amendment). The two provisions use virtually identical language, reflecting the consistent constitutional architecture of the twin local-government amendments. The one-year window in both cases ran from 24 April 1993 (for Article 243N under the Seventy-Third Amendment) and 1 June 1993 (for Article 243ZF under the Seventy-Fourth Amendment).

## Litigation after the one-year period

Courts in several States dealt with the question of whether State municipal laws that had not been amended within the one-year window were rendered ineffective by Article 243ZF's expiry. The general position taken by High Courts was that specific inconsistent provisions lost effect but that the broader municipal law framework remained in force to the extent it was consistent with Part IXA — courts read down inconsistent provisions rather than treating the entire State municipal law as void. This avoided the governance vacuum that would have followed from a strict invalidity reading, while ensuring that Part IXA's requirements were given effect. The more persistent difficulty was with State laws that had not made the mandatory reservations, established the State Finance Commission, or transferred the required functions to municipalities: courts issued mandamus directions requiring States to comply with Part IXA obligations even where the underlying State legislation had not been updated.

## Current significance

By 2025, the transitional window of Article 243ZF has long expired: its operative provisions discharged their function in the mid-1990s. The article retains historical and interpretive importance — it explains why post-1993 litigation about the validity of pre-1993 municipal laws was decided the way it was — but it creates no ongoing legal obligations. Its continuing significance is as a marker of constitutional design: the framers of the Seventy-Fourth Amendment were careful to ensure that the shift to a new constitutional order for urban governance did not create disruption for existing elected bodies or produce a legal vacuum that could be exploited to keep urban governance in suspended animation.
