## Why it matters

Article 34 is the Constitution's most extreme emergency provision in Part III — the point at which the framers acknowledged that an organised civil breakdown could require military authority to displace civil governance and judicial oversight entirely. Unlike Articles 352 to 360, which create elaborate procedural structures for emergencies, Article 34 says nothing about how martial law is proclaimed or ended. Its silence is intentional: the article is not a grant of power to proclaim martial law but a mechanism for managing the legal aftermath when martial law, if validly proclaimed under common law or prerogative, has occurred.

The parliamentary-sanction requirement is the article's core safeguard. Every colonial instance of martial law in India — after the Amritsar massacre of 1919, during the Quit India suppression of 1942 — involved subsequent legislative indemnification of officers whose actions would otherwise have been unlawful. The framers preserved this tool but made it unambiguously dependent on Parliament: the executive cannot immunise military conduct by executive order, by notification, or by administrative circular. Only an Act of Parliament can provide the indemnification or validation that Article 34 contemplates.

The existence of Article 34 as a conceptual ceiling also helps define the constitutional space available to lesser emergency measures. AFSPA operates under a statutory regime that provides qualified immunity — requiring prior government sanction for prosecution — without the total indemnification Parliament could grant under Article 34. The Armed Forces Tribunal adjudicates claims against armed forces personnel. These mechanisms are understood by courts to fall well below the Article 34 maximum. The article's dormancy has not prevented it from serving as a constitutional reference point for understanding what the Constitution would and would not permit if the threshold for formal martial law were ever crossed.

## Reading the provision

The non-obstante clause — "notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Part" — positions Article 34 as a potential override on all the fundamental rights in Articles 12 to 33, including the right to equality (Article 14), the right to life (Article 21), and the right to move courts (Article 32). However, the override is not self-executing: Parliament must pass a specific law. The article does not itself suspend any right; it authorises Parliament to grant indemnity by legislation.

"Any person in the service of the Union or of a State or any other person" is deliberately broad. It covers not only military officers and police personnel but also civilians acting under military direction — private contractors, civilian auxiliaries, intelligence officers, or any individual who participated in restoring order under martial law authority. The breadth prevents the technical argument that a particular actor is not "of the Union" from defeating indemnification.

"Validate any sentence passed, punishment inflicted, forfeiture ordered or other act done" extends the provision's reach beyond prospective indemnity to retrospective curative validation. If a military tribunal conducted summary proceedings that were legally defective — applying martial law procedures not authorised by civil law — Parliament can by statute validate those proceedings as if they had been properly conducted. Courts could otherwise review and nullify such proceedings. The validation power forecloses that review for acts Parliament specifically covers.

The provision covers only "any area within the territory of India where martial law was in force" — it does not apply to actions taken by Indian forces outside India, and it does not apply to actions taken in disputed territories or across borders.

## Martial law versus emergency powers: the constitutional distinction

Indian constitutional law draws a sharp distinction between the formal emergency powers in Articles 352 to 360 and the martial law context of Article 34. An Article 352 national emergency does not by itself invoke martial law or Article 34 — the two regimes have different triggers, different procedures, and different constitutional footprints.

A national emergency under Article 352 requires a written declaration by the Cabinet, Parliamentary approval within one month, and periodic continuation approvals. It has defined constitutional consequences for fundamental rights (suspension of Articles 19 and 20-21 under specified conditions under Articles 358 and 359), for Centre-State relations (Article 353), and for Parliament's powers. Judicial review of emergency proclamations was confirmed by Minerva Mills and Kesavananda as available in principle, even if limited in scope.

Martial law — the complete displacement of civil administration and judicial process by military authority in a defined territory — has no constitutional procedure in Part XVIII. Its authority derives from necessity and prerogative. What Article 34 does is create a parliamentary mechanism for managing the legal consequences of martial law after the fact, not a licence to proclaim it. The absence of a formal martial law procedure in the Constitution reflects the framers' view that formal martial law should remain a constitutional last resort with no regularised pathway — Parliament's power to validate it retrospectively exists, but the absence of proclamation procedure keeps it judicially opaque.

## The Colonial Precedents

The framers drew directly on the Indian experience of colonial martial law when drafting Article 34. Two episodes were particularly salient.

In April 1919, following the Jallianwala Bagh massacre at Amritsar, Lieutenant General Dyer ordered the massacre and Brigadier General O'Dwyer governed Punjab under martial law conditions. The Indemnity Act subsequently passed by the Indian Legislative Council protected officers from civil liability, causing Dr. B.R. Ambedkar and others to note that legislative indemnification was the standard colonial technique for retrospectively legalising military violence. The Indian Independence Indemnity Act 1920 was a specific instance of this pattern.

During the Quit India Movement of 1942, the colonial government deployed the Indian Army to suppress civilian resistance in parts of Bengal, Bihar, and the United Provinces under conditions that approximated martial law without formal proclamation. Orders were given to shoot, bombard villages from the air, and use force that would have been unlawful without emergency authorisation. Subsequent colonial legislation provided protection from prosecution. The framers deliberately preserved this retrospective parliamentary mechanism — believing a post-facto legislative check was better than no check at all — while insisting that the executive could not act without it.

## The AFSPA distinction

The Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act 1958, and its later version for Jammu and Kashmir, provides an intermediate constitutional regime for armed force deployment in "disturbed areas" notified by the Central or State government. AFSPA grants officers of the armed forces powers to search, arrest, and use potentially lethal force against persons suspected of acting against public order. It provides statutory immunity from prosecution without prior government sanction.

AFSPA is constitutionally grounded in Article 355's duty to protect States against internal disturbance, not in Article 34. The Supreme Court in Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v Union of India (1998) upheld AFSPA while expressly distinguishing it from formal martial law under Article 34. The Court noted that AFSPA does not displace civil administration or suspend judicial process; civilian courts continue to function and can receive petitions against armed forces conduct. The statutory immunity AFSPA provides is qualified — it requires prior government sanction for prosecution — whereas parliamentary indemnification under Article 34 would be absolute.

The Extra Judicial Execution Victim Families Association case (2016) reinforced this distinction, confirming that armed force personnel do not enjoy blanket immunity in disturbed areas and that every use of force must be in conformity with the Army's own rules of engagement. Article 34's absolute indemnification would eliminate this judicial oversight; AFSPA immunity does not.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The framers' discussion of Article 34 was brief but principled. Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar noted that the article was not a licence for the executive but a mechanism ensuring parliamentary accountability — if military action had been taken under martial law, Parliament would have to own it publicly through legislation. B.R. Ambedkar's view was that the provision was a regrettable necessity: in extreme situations involving armed insurrection or total civil breakdown, it might be necessary to allow military governance and subsequent parliamentary ratification, but the requirement of actual parliamentary legislation was the only constitutional safeguard available short of absolute prohibition of the power.

Several members noted that formal martial law would be judicially unreviable once declared, given its origins in common-law prerogative beyond the Constitution's text. The decision to leave martial law procedure undefined — rather than constitutionalising it as Article 352 had constitutionalised emergency — reflects the framers' judgment that some residual power in extremis was unavoidable, but that it should not be given a regularised constitutional home.

## Key cases

**Re Bhagat Singh (1931, Privy Council)** — The colonial-era Privy Council addressed the scope of military authority during the pre-Independence period. Though not binding on independent India's courts, this decision informed the framers' understanding of what martial law entailed and why parliamentary indemnification was its standard legal sequel.

**Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v Union of India (1998) 2 SCC 109** — The Supreme Court upheld AFSPA, drawing an explicit distinction between the AFSPA deployment regime (Article 355 basis, qualified immunity, judicial oversight maintained) and the formal martial law context of Article 34 (post-facto parliamentary indemnification, judicial oversight suspended). The Court held that AFSPA did not invoke Article 34 because civil administration and courts continued to function.

**Extra Judicial Execution Victim Families Association v Union of India (2016) 14 SCC 536** — The Supreme Court, in a major ruling on alleged fake encounter killings by the Army in Manipur, confirmed that armed force personnel have no blanket immunity even in disturbed areas and that every use of force must comply with military law on use of force. The Court's refusal to treat AFSPA as equivalent to Article 34 indemnity reinforced the distinction and opened systematic judicial accountability for armed force conduct that would not survive Article 34 indemnification.

**General Officer Commanding, Rashtriya Rifles v CBI (2005) 12 SCC 559** — The Court addressed the intersection of the AFSPA prior-sanction requirement with the Central government's prosecution powers. While declining to find a blanket immunity, the Court treated the prior-sanction requirement as a meaningful procedural filter, distinguishing it from the absolute Article 34 indemnity.

**A.K. Roy v Union of India (1982) 1 SCC 271** — Though primarily about the National Security Act and preventive detention, the judgment's discussion of the relationship between emergency powers and Part III rights bears on Article 34's position in the constitutional architecture. The Court confirmed that fundamental rights restrictions during emergency require constitutional warrant and cannot be achieved by administrative action alone.

## Current significance

Article 34 has remained entirely dormant in post-Independence India — no formal martial law has been proclaimed and no parliamentary indemnification law under Article 34 has been enacted. The article is significant primarily as a conceptual ceiling and as a reference point for debates about AFSPA, armed force accountability, and the constitutional basis for extraordinary security measures.

The Supreme Court's active jurisprudence on AFSPA accountability — particularly the Extra Judicial Execution case and subsequent litigation about encounter deaths in Manipur — has made Article 34's distinction from AFSPA immunity constitutionally important. As long as courts insist that AFSPA immunity falls short of Article 34 indemnification, they retain jurisdiction to examine alleged abuses. Parliamentary indemnification under Article 34 would categorically foreclose such review.

Academic debate continues about whether a future constitutional crisis — involving armed secessionist movement in a major State, internal breakdown of the kind that might require the total displacement of civil administration — could justify formal martial law and the subsequent Article 34 indemnification pathway. The article's existence answers that question: the Constitution contemplates such a contingency but requires Parliament to own it. In a polity with an increasingly active judiciary and a fundamental rights culture, the practical and political cost of invoking Article 34 and seeking parliamentary indemnification is understood to be enormous — which is why lesser instruments like AFSPA continue to bear the constitutional load that, at the extreme, only Article 34 could carry.
