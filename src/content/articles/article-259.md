## What this Article originally did

Article 259 was a transitional provision of the original Constitution that dealt with the armed forces maintained by Part B States — the former princely States and Rajpramukh-governed units that formed a distinct category in the First Schedule until the States Reorganisation Act 1956. In its original form, it allowed Part B States to maintain their own armed forces subject to the Union's supreme command, and provided for the eventual integration or disbanding of those forces as the Union directed.

The provision was omitted entirely by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956, which came into force on 1 November 1956 — the same date as the States Reorganisation Act. With the reorganisation of States on linguistic lines and the abolition of the Part A / Part B / Part C / Part D distinction, Part B States ceased to exist and there was no longer any constitutional category to which Article 259 could apply.

## The Part A / Part B / Part C / Part D framework

To understand Article 259, one must understand the constitutional classification of States that existed at the Constitution's commencement in 1950. The First Schedule originally divided India's territory into four parts:

- **Part A States** were the former British Indian provinces, including Madras, Bombay, West Bengal, UP, Bihar, and others — governed through normal elected governments under the full federal framework.
- **Part B States** were the former princely States with their own constitutions and Rajpramukhs (the head of state, analogous to a Governor) — including Hyderabad, Mysore, Travancore-Cochin, Madhya Bharat, Saurashtra, and others.
- **Part C States** were smaller territories, including former Chief Commissioner's Provinces and some smaller princely States, under more direct Central governance.
- **Part D** was Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

Part B States occupied a distinctive constitutional position: they had agreed to accede to India under the terms negotiated by V.P. Menon and Sardar Patel, and many retained their own Rajpramukhs (often the former rulers), administrative frameworks, and — crucially — their own armed forces. Article 259 provided the constitutional framework for managing those forces under Union command until their integration was complete.

## The integration process

The process of integrating the armed forces of the princely States into the Indian Army began before the Constitution came into force. The most prominent example was the Police Action in Hyderabad (Operation Polo, September 1948), which ended the Nizam's resistance to accession and led to the disbanding of the Razakars and integration of the Hyderabad State Forces. By January 1950, the armed forces of most major princely States had already been absorbed into the Indian Army or State Armed Police, with former State units sometimes forming the nucleus of new Indian Army regiments.

Article 259 formalised this process: it acknowledged the residual existence of Part B State forces while subordinating them to Union command, and gave the Union government the authority to direct their ultimate integration or disbanding. By November 1956, the task was substantially complete, and the article was no longer needed.

## Key cases

**No live case law** — Since Article 259 was omitted before substantive constitutional litigation on its terms could develop, there is no Supreme Court jurisprudence directly interpreting the article. Historical accounts of the integration are found in V.P. Menon's works on Indian integration and in the administrative records of the Ministry of States rather than in court judgments.

**Background — Berubari Union and Exchange of Enclaves (1960) 3 SCR 250** — Though not directly about Article 259, the Supreme Court's analysis of how the Constitution managed territorial and sovereignty transitions in the post-Independence period provides the closest judicial framework for understanding the transitional provisions of which Article 259 was a part.

## Constitutional design lessons

The omission of Article 259 illustrates a pattern in Indian constitutional design: transitional provisions are included in the text to manage specific historical transitions, and when the transition is complete, the provision is omitted by amendment rather than left as dead letter. Other examples include Articles 313 (transitional service provisions), 314 (omitted by the 28th Amendment), and Articles 369 and 370 (transitional provisions for legislative competence and Jammu and Kashmir respectively). The willingness to formally omit spent provisions reflects a constitutional culture of textual housekeeping that keeps the document's operative content aligned with current reality.

## Current significance

Article 259 is a spent provision with no contemporary operational significance. Scholars of constitutional history use it as a window into the mechanics of the Sardar Patel-led integration of the princely States and the provisional federal arrangements that bridged 1947 and 1956. The article documents the constitutional architecture that managed the transition from a patchwork of sovereign and semi-sovereign territories into a unified federal republic — one of the most remarkable state-building achievements of the twentieth century.
