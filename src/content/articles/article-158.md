  158. Conditions of Governor's office.—(1) The Governor shall not be a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any State specified in the First Schedule, and if a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any such State be appointed Governor, he shall be deemed to have vacated his seat in that House on the date on which he enters upon his office as Governor.

  (2) The Governor shall not hold any other office of profit.

  (3) The Governor shall be entitled without payment of rent to the use of his official residences and shall be also entitled to such emoluments, allowances and privileges as may be determined by Parliament by law and, until provision in that behalf is so made, such emoluments, allowances and privileges as are specified in the Second Schedule.

  (3A) Where the same person is appointed as Governor of two or more States, the emoluments and allowances payable to the Governor shall be allocated among the States in such proportion as the President may by order determine.

  (4) The emoluments and allowances of the Governor shall not be diminished during his term of office.

## What this Article does

Article 158 sets out the conditions that define the independence and integrity of the Governor's office. The article operates on two complementary axes: first, it insulates the Governor from political entanglements by prohibiting legislative membership and other profitable offices; second, it insulates the Governor from financial pressure by guaranteeing rent-free official residence, constitutionally secured emoluments, and protection against any reduction in those emoluments during the term.

By prohibiting the Governor from holding membership of any legislature or any other office of profit, the Constitution seeks to ensure the Governor acts in a sphere separate from party-political contestation. The automatic vacation of a legislative seat upon assuming gubernatorial office (clause 1) reinforces this expectation: the moment of appointment severs the new Governor's formal connections to the legislative base from which political identity is ordinarily drawn.

## Clause-by-clause analysis

**Clause (1) — No legislative membership.** A serving MP or MLA who is appointed Governor vacates that seat automatically on the date of assuming office. There is no need for a formal resignation; the vacation is constitutionally self-executing. This prevents the anomaly of a single person simultaneously exercising executive authority at the State level and legislative authority in Parliament or another Assembly.

**Clause (2) — No other office of profit.** The Governor cannot hold any other office from which monetary or other benefit flows. This is a forward-looking prohibition that applies throughout the tenure, not merely at the time of appointment. A Governor who is offered and accepts any such office must be treated as having violated the conditions of the gubernatorial office itself.

**Clause (3) — Emoluments and official residence.** The Governor is entitled to rent-free use of official residences — the Raj Bhavan in each State capital — and to emoluments and allowances determined by Parliament by law. Until Parliament legislates, the Second Schedule's provisions apply. Charging the Governor's emoluments on the Consolidated Fund of the State rather than the Union's fund reflects the constitutional expectation that the State bears the cost of its own constitutional head.

**Clause (3A) — Shared emoluments.** Inserted to address the practice of one person serving as Governor of two or more States simultaneously (permitted by Article 153's proviso), this clause provides that emoluments are allocated among the States concerned in a proportion determined by presidential order.

**Clause (4) — Protection against diminution.** Mirroring the analogous protections for judges under Articles 125 and 221, this provision prevents the Union or State governments from using financial pressure to influence the Governor's conduct. A government that disapproves of a Governor's constitutional judgments cannot reduce the emoluments as a disciplinary or coercive measure.

## Key cases

**B.P. Singhal v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court, reading Articles 155, 156, and 158 together, emphasised that the conditions of office in Article 158 constitute a constitutional guarantee of the Governor's institutional dignity and independence. The Court noted that the prohibition on holding any other office of profit under clause (2) reflects the constitutional intent that the Governor should be a full-time constitutional authority free from conflicting obligations.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)** — The Constitution Bench underscored that the conditions laid down in Article 158 reflect the Governor's position as a non-political head of the state executive. The Court's finding that the Governor acts on ministerial advice in all but specified discretionary matters must be read alongside Article 158's independence conditions: the Governor is structurally free of legislative or electoral entanglement precisely so that the advice-following role can be performed without personal political interest.

**Hargovind Pant v. Dr. Raghukul Tilak (1979)** — The Court held that the Governor is not an employee of the Union government but a constitutional authority. Reading Article 158 in context, it confirmed that the office's conditions of service — including the guarantee against diminution of emoluments — place the Governor in a constitutional rather than an employment relationship with the Union.

## Current significance

The practice of appointing the same person as additional charge Governor of two or more States simultaneously, addressed by clause (3A), has attracted criticism as an economy measure that dilutes the constitutional attention a Governor owes to each State. Several opposition-governed States have argued that charge-holding arrangements reduce the Governor's functional presence at moments of constitutional importance — such as when summoning or proroguing the Assembly becomes contentious. The question of whether a charge-holding Governor can simultaneously discharge the Article 158(2) obligation of holding no other office of profit has occasionally been raised, though courts have consistently treated the concurrent appointment as a constitutionally authorised exception rather than a violation.
