## Why it matters

The Indian Constitution requires prior procedural steps — recommendations and sanctions — before certain categories of Bills may be introduced or proceeded with. These requirements serve substantive constitutional purposes: they protect federal financial interests, ensure that legislation affecting the Union's taxation powers or the States' financial autonomy is subject to appropriate prior scrutiny at the executive level, and prevent hasty or uninformed legislative action on constitutionally sensitive matters. But procedures can go wrong. A legislature may inadvertently omit a step, a presiding officer may overlook a requirement, or the sequence may be disrupted by a political crisis or administrative oversight.

Without Article 255, a missed recommendation could potentially void an entire Act of Parliament or a State legislature — no matter how thoroughly it had been debated, how constitutionally sound its substance, and how carefully the relevant constitutional authority had considered and granted assent. Such an outcome would be disproportionate: using a procedural sequencing error to invalidate legislation that the relevant authority had ultimately approved would elevate technicality over substance. Article 255 prevents this by treating the missing recommendation as a procedural deficiency cured by the ultimate act of assent.

The provision also reflects a deeper constitutional logic about the relationship between preliminary recommendations and final assent. A recommendation at the introduction stage is a lesser act — it signals that the relevant authority has no objection to the Bill proceeding. Assent at the end of the process is a greater act — the relevant authority has considered the Bill in its final form and affirmatively approved it into law. If the greater act has been performed, it would be anomalous to void the law because the lesser act was omitted. Article 255 gives constitutional expression to this logic, and the courts have consistently applied it as a principle of purposive construction that favours legislative finality.

## Reading the provision

**The triggering condition**: The article applies when "some recommendation or previous sanction required by this Constitution was not given." The Constitution requires recommendations or sanctions at various points: Article 207 requires the Governor's recommendation for demands for grants and financial Bills at the State level; Article 274 requires Presidential sanction for Bills affecting Union taxation; various other provisions require Presidential recommendation or assent for Bills reserved for Presidential consideration. Article 255 applies across all these requirements whenever the prescribed preliminary step was omitted but assent was subsequently given.

**The curative condition — three scenarios**: Clause (a): where the Governor's recommendation was required, assent from either the Governor or the President cures the omission. This includes cases where Presidential assent is given because the Governor has reserved the Bill for the President's consideration under Article 200. The logic is that Presidential assent represents a higher level of constitutional authority than the Governor's recommendation would have been, and so the omission of the lesser act is cured by the performance of the greater one.

Clause (b): where the Rajpramukh's recommendation was required — a pre-1956 provision relating to the rulers of Part B States (former princely states that were administratively governed by Rajpramukhs pending full integration into the Indian Union) — assent from either the Rajpramukh or the President cures the omission. This clause is spent; Part B States were abolished by the States Reorganisation Act 1956 and no Rajpramukh exists today. Clause (b) remains in the constitutional text as an artifact of the integration process.

Clause (c): where Presidential recommendation or previous sanction was required, only Presidential assent provides the cure. Presidential sanction requirements — for instance under Article 274 for Bills affecting central taxes — protect federal financial interests. The cure is confined to Presidential assent because only the President can provide the equivalent level of constitutional authority when the President's own prior involvement was required.

**"By reason only" — the scope of the cure**: The most important phrase in the article is "by reason only." Article 255 cures invalidity arising solely from the missing recommendation or sanction. It does not cure invalidity arising from any other ground — lack of legislative competence, violation of fundamental rights, conflict with the basic structure, failure to follow other mandatory constitutional procedures, or substantive constitutional defects. If a law is challenged on multiple grounds and the missing recommendation is just one of them, Article 255 cures the recommendation ground only. The other grounds remain open, and the law may still be struck down on those grounds. The limiting phrase is not a minor qualification — it is the essential boundary of the cure.

**What Article 255 does not cover**: The provision does not cure mis-certification of a Bill as a Money Bill by the Speaker; that is a classification decision, not a recommendation or sanction requirement. It does not cure failure to follow Article 254(2) procedure for States to legislate on concurrent list subjects — that is a substantive competence requirement, not a recommendation requirement. It does not cure the absence of Presidential assent to a Bill reserved under Article 200 — that is a condition of validity, not a preliminary sanction. The article is confined to the specific category of preliminary recommendations and sanctions that the Constitution prescribes as prerequisites to the introduction or proceeding of a Bill.

## The Rajpramukh clause and India's constitutional integration

The presence of clause (b) — the Rajpramukh clause — in a provision otherwise concerned with the ordinary legislative process is a reminder of the constitutional history that surrounded the integration of princely states into the Indian Union. At independence, India comprised not only British India but hundreds of princely states, many of which acceded to India while retaining internal autonomy under various instruments of accession and merger agreements.

The Part B States created by the Constitution were governed by their former rulers, now styled Rajpramukhs, who exercised functions analogous to the Governor's functions in other States. Constitutional provisions applicable to these States required the Rajpramukh's recommendation or sanction in ways that paralleled the Governor's role elsewhere. Article 255(b) ensured that legislative business in Part B States was not inadvertently voided by the failure to obtain the Rajpramukh's prior recommendation, provided Presidential assent had been given. With the reorganisation of 1956, Part B States ceased to exist, and the Rajpramukh clause became constitutionally spent while remaining in the text as a record of the integration period.

## Key cases

**M P V Sundararamier and Co v State of Andhra Pradesh AIR 1958 SC 468** — In an early and foundational judgment, the Supreme Court confirmed the distinction between substantive invalidity (lack of legislative competence, which Article 255 cannot cure) and procedural invalidity (failure to follow constitutionally required sequencing, which Article 255 does cure). An Act enacted without the required Presidential recommendation but with Presidential assent was upheld under Article 255. The Court held that the article reflects a constitutional preference for legislative finality over technical procedural challenges that do not go to the substance of the legislative power.

**State of Bihar v Kameshwar Singh AIR 1952 SC 252** — An early Constitution Bench examined whether the omission of procedural requirements rendered a State Act void, and held that where the Constitution provides a procedural cure, courts should not void legislation for defects the Constitution itself has deemed non-fatal. The principle that procedural requirements protecting federal coordination should be read purposively — not as technical trip-wires — was affirmed and has informed subsequent application of Article 255. This case established the baseline of judicial approach to the curative provisions in Part XI.

**Hoechst Pharmaceuticals Ltd v State of Bihar (1983) 4 SCC 45** — The Supreme Court examined the conditions under which prior Presidential recommendation requirements could be treated as procedural rather than substantive, and clarified that Article 255 applies specifically to the category of preliminary recommendations — not to sanctions that go to the substantive constitutional validity of the measure. The judgment draws the critical distinction between procedural sequencing requirements (curable under Article 255) and substantive competence requirements (not curable), and is the clearest statement of Article 255's doctrinal limits.

**R C Cooper v Union of India (1970) 1 SCC 248 (Bank Nationalisation Case)** — The Court's treatment of constitutional requirements for legislation affecting banking and financial rights included analysis of what procedural and recommendation requirements must be satisfied and which, if unsatisfied, affect the substantive validity of the legislation. The judgment contextualises Article 255 by clarifying what falls outside its curative scope — requirements that go to substantive competence, not procedural sequence, cannot be cured by subsequent assent.

**Rai Sahib Ram Jawaya Kapur v State of Punjab AIR 1955 SC 549** — Observations in this foundational case about the constitutional role of executive recommendation in the legislative process provide the doctrinal background against which Article 255's cure of recommendation omissions operates. The recommendation requirements exist to serve substantive constitutional purposes; Article 255 accepts that those purposes can be served by assent when the preliminary step has been missed, provided the final constitutional authority has itself given informed approval.

## Current significance

Article 255 operates quietly but importantly as a constitutional safety net that prevents purely technical sequencing errors from unravelling otherwise valid legislation. The clause relating to Rajpramukhs is spent; the Governor and President clauses remain operative whenever a recommendation requirement is inadvertently missed in the legislative process. In the ordinary course of legislative business, these situations are uncommon — legislative drafting offices and presiding officers are generally attentive to recommendation requirements — but they arise occasionally, particularly in States with high legislative output or political instability that disrupts normal legislative procedure.

The article's relevance has been indirectly engaged in a broader constitutional debate about the relationship between procedural requirements and substantive legislative validity. The Supreme Court's consideration of whether Finance Bills passed as Money Bills — thereby bypassing the Rajya Sabha — are constitutionally valid raised an analogous question: when a classification or certification decision at the beginning of the legislative process is contested, does assent at the end cure the problem? Article 255's "by reason only" limiting phrase provides the structural answer: the Constitution distinguishes between procedural sequencing defects (curable by assent) and substantive constitutional defects (not curable by any subsequent step). The Money Bill classification controversy falls outside Article 255's scope — it involves a constitutional decision about the nature of the legislation, not simply a missed preliminary recommendation — but the article's purposive logic informs the broader analysis.

The provision also matters as a statement of constitutional values: the framers' judgment that elected legislatures should not have their work undone by procedural technicalities when the substantive safeguards — including the consideration of a politically accountable constitutional head who formally grants assent — have been fully satisfied. This preference for legislative finality over technical invalidation, expressed in Article 255, continues to inform the Court's approach to challenges based on procedural irregularities in the legislative process and is a constitutional principle of genuine practical importance in a system where procedural complexity creates real opportunities for technical challenge.
