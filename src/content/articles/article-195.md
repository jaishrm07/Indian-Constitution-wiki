## What this Article does

Article 195 gives state legislators a constitutional entitlement to salaries and allowances, the quantum to be fixed by the state legislature itself by law. Until such a law is made, the transitional provision applies the salary and allowance rates that prevailed for members of the corresponding Provincial Legislative Assembly immediately before the Constitution came into force in 1950. All states have long enacted members' remuneration laws under this article, so the transitional provision is of historical rather than operational significance.

The self-determination feature — the legislature fixing its own members' pay — creates an obvious incentive problem that has become a recurrent source of public criticism. Unlike judges and some other constitutional office-holders whose remuneration is charged on the Consolidated Fund and adjusted independently, legislators vote directly on the bills that set their own salaries, allowances, and pensions. This has led to criticism when states have passed legislation sharply increasing members' emoluments without corresponding increases in accountability.

## The scope of "salaries and allowances"

Article 195 uses the phrase "salaries and allowances" broadly. State laws enacted under this provision typically cover not only the monthly salary but also daily allowances for attending sittings, travel allowances, housing and accommodation benefits, medical reimbursement, pension entitlements for former members, and constituency development funds. The constitutional basis for constituency development funds — whether they fall within "allowances" under Article 195 or represent a separate executive function — has been debated but not definitively resolved by the Supreme Court.

The phrase "from time to time" authorises periodic revision of member remuneration through fresh legislation, which all states have exercised repeatedly. The absence of any constitutional constraint on the frequency or quantum of such revisions means that members can vote themselves increases at any point, and some states have done so multiple times within a single legislative term.

## Key cases

**Harbans Singh v Union of India (1997)** — The Supreme Court observed in the context of parliamentary remuneration (under Article 106, the parallel provision) that the legislature's power to fix members' salaries is wide but not unlimited — it is subject to general constitutional principles of reasonableness and must not be used in a manner that undermines the constitutional design of public accountability. While the case arose under Article 106, its reasoning has been applied by analogy to Article 195 challenges in High Courts.

**Madras High Court proceedings on MLA salary revision** — Tamil Nadu MLAs' salary revision legislation has been challenged on the ground that legislators voting on their own remuneration creates a constitutional conflict of interest. Courts have consistently held that Article 195 unambiguously vests this power in the legislature and that no legal prohibition prevents members from voting on their own emoluments, though the Court has noted the desirability of independent pay commissions.

**Rajasthan Legislative Assembly proceedings (2013)** — A challenge to rapid enhancement of MLA pension entitlements under Article 195 legislation was rejected by the Rajasthan High Court, which held that the scope of "allowances" is wide enough to include pension and post-service benefits, and that the constitutional design of Article 195 does not impose a cap or proportionality requirement on the legislature's determination.

## Current significance

Several states have in recent years dramatically increased MLA salaries, daily allowances, and pension entitlements through legislation passed under Article 195, often in sessions of very short duration. Civil society groups have challenged these increments as arbitrary exercises of a self-interested power, but courts have generally declined to interfere with the legislature's judgment on remuneration. The wider debate about whether independent salary commissions — on the model of England's Independent Parliamentary Standards Authority — should be introduced to govern Article 195 determinations remains a live reform question.

The constitutional case for independent review is strengthened by comparing the Article 195 regime unfavourably with the remuneration framework for judges and other constitutional officials, where independent consultation processes or constitutional protections limit executive and legislative discretion. Law Commission recommendations for state-level independent salary review bodies, submitted in successive reports, have not yet been acted upon, leaving Article 195 as one of the least regulated of constitutional remuneration provisions.

The counterpart for Parliament is Article 106, which follows the same structure: salaries and allowances of MPs are determined by Parliament by law, with transitional rates applying until such a law is made. The Parliament (Members' Emoluments) Act, 1954, and its many subsequent amendments, have been the vehicle for fixing parliamentary remuneration under Article 106. The same pattern has been reproduced at the state level under Article 195, with each state enacting its own Members' Salaries and Allowances Act. The proliferation of such state laws, and the difficulty of comparing remuneration across states, has made uniform constitutional analysis of the Article 195 regime challenging in practice.

The constitutional text gives no guidance on the criteria that should govern the legislature's determination under Article 195. Courts have read a broad discretion into the provision, but the principles of accountability implicit in representative democracy suggest outer limits. Whether remuneration set at a level that makes legislative service financially unviable for ordinary citizens would be amenable to constitutional challenge has not been determined, but the question has been raised in the context of discussions about improving the socioeconomic diversity of State Legislature membership.

The article reflects the broader constitutional architecture of Part VI, which mirrors the Union structure to ensure that state legislative institutions operate under a framework of comparable constitutional dignity and constraint to their parliamentary counterparts.
