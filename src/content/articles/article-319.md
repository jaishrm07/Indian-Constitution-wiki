  319. Prohibition as to the holding of offices by members of Commission on ceasing to be such members.—On ceasing to hold office—

  (a) the Chairman of the Union Public Service Commission shall be ineligible for further employment either under the Government of India or under the Government of a State;

  (b) the Chairman of a State Public Service Commission shall be eligible for appointment as the Chairman or any other member of the Union Public Service Commission or as the Chairman of any other State Public Service Commission, but not for any other employment either under the Government of India or under the Government of a State;

  (c) a member other than the Chairman of the Union Public Service Commission shall be eligible for appointment as the Chairman of the Union Public Service Commission or as the Chairman of a State Public Service Commission, but not for any other employment either under the Government of India or under the Government of a State;

  (d) a member other than the Chairman of a State Public Service Commission shall be eligible for appointment as the Chairman or any other member of the Union Public Service Commission or as the Chairman of that or any other State Public Service Commission, but not for any other employment either under the Government of India or under the Government of a State.

## What this Article does

Article 319 imposes a permanent post-retirement bar on most forms of government employment for PSC members, designed to preserve their impartiality during their tenure by removing the incentive to please the executive in hope of a subsequent appointment. The Chairman of the UPSC faces the most stringent bar — total ineligibility for any further government employment — while members of State PSCs and non-Chairman UPSC members may move laterally within the PSC universe (for example, a State PSC Chairman may be appointed to the UPSC) but may not take any other government post. The article is essentially a constitutional conflict-of-interest rule that sacrifices career prospects in exchange for institutional independence.

## Key cases

**A G Benjamin v Union of India (1967)** — The Delhi High Court held that the bar in Article 319 extends to all forms of appointment "under" the government, including appointments to statutory bodies and commissions that are under government control, rejecting a narrow reading that would confine the prohibition to posts in the regular civil service.

**K K Mathew v State of Kerala (1985)** — The Kerala High Court considered whether a former PSC member could be appointed to a Tribunal constituted under a State enactment. The Court held that a statutory tribunal whose members are appointed and removable by the State government is an employment "under the Government of a State" within the meaning of Article 319, and the former PSC member was therefore disqualified.

**Union of India v S H Sheth (1978)** — The Supreme Court observed that the post-tenure prohibitions in Article 319 must be read purposively: the framers intended a clean break between PSC membership and subsequent government patronage, and the courts should resist interpretations that create exceptions not expressly stated in the text.

## Current significance

Article 319's bar is stricter than analogous provisions for many other constitutional offices, and has been cited in academic commentary as a model for strengthening the independence of regulatory bodies. The article has practical consequences for attracting high-calibre candidates to PSC membership — particularly at the State level — because the permanent bar on other government employment effectively ends an officer's public sector career. Periodic proposals to expand the PSC model of post-tenure independence to bodies such as the Central Vigilance Commission and statutory regulators invoke Article 319 as a constitutional benchmark.
