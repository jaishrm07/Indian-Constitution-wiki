  A person of Indian origin living abroad — or whose parent or grandparent was born in undivided India as defined in the Government of India Act, 1935 — could become a citizen of India at the constitutional commencement by registering at an Indian embassy or consulate. Unlike Articles 5 and 6, which confer citizenship automatically on domicile or migration, Article 8 requires a voluntary act — the person must apply and be registered. The three-generation reach (the person themselves, their parents, or their grandparents born in undivided India) was deliberately wide to accommodate a diaspora established over generations in East Africa, South-east Asia, Fiji, and the Caribbean. Registration is constitutive — it is the act that creates citizenship, not merely evidence of pre-existing entitlement. Article 9 cuts across Article 8 by stripping away Indian citizenship from any overseas person who has already voluntarily acquired foreign nationality, which in practice excluded much of the diaspora that Article 8 was designed to benefit. Article 8 has been substantially superseded by the Citizenship Act, 1955 and the Overseas Citizen of India scheme, but it remains constitutionally significant as the founding text for descent-based and registration-based citizenship in India's constitutional design.

## Why it matters

Article 8 was the Constitution's answer to the most geographically dispersed part of the Indian identity at the moment of independence: the overseas diaspora. By 1950, Indian-origin communities had been living in Mauritius, Fiji, Trinidad, Kenya, Uganda, Malaya, Burma, and South Africa for one, two, or even three generations. They were products of colonial-era labour migration, indentured contracts, and commercial movement. Many spoke Indian languages, maintained Indian religious practices, and retained emotional ties to the subcontinent — but they had been born and had lived their entire lives outside India. A constitution that made no provision for them would have declared, implicitly, that India had no constitutional relationship with them.

Article 8 addressed this by extending the option of citizenship to any person with a sufficiently proximate Indian-origin connection who chose to register. The choice of a registration-based mechanism rather than automatic citizenship was itself significant — it preserved agency for the overseas person. Automatic citizenship would have imposed legal consequences (including obligations of allegiance) on people who had not sought them and might not want them. The registration requirement made citizenship for the diaspora an opt-in rather than an imposition.

The article also anchors the principle that India's citizenship law has, from its very foundation, recognised descent and registration as legitimate bases for belonging — a principle that echoes in every subsequent debate about the Citizenship Act, 1955 amendments, the Overseas Citizen of India scheme, and the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019. When the Supreme Court examines Parliament's exercise of its Article 11 power to differentiate among categories of persons for citizenship purposes, Article 8's precedent for differentiation along lines of origin and registration is part of the constitutional baseline.

## Reading the provision

**"Notwithstanding anything in article 5"** — The opening non-obstante clause signals that Article 8 operates as an independent and overriding provision. Article 5 confers citizenship on persons domiciled in India — the overseas Indian is, by definition, ordinarily resident outside India and therefore cannot satisfy Article 5's domicile requirement. Article 8 creates a parallel track, not subject to the Article 5 domicile condition.

**"any person who or either of whose parents or any of whose grand-parents was born in India"** — This is the qualifying ancestry criterion, cast in deliberately wide terms. It covers three levels of connection: the person themselves (who may have been born outside India), either parent, or any grandparent. The breadth was chosen to include diaspora communities in which a third-generation descendant might have no direct personal memory of India but whose grandparents emigrated from what is now Pakistan or Bangladesh. "India" for this purpose means India as defined in the Government of India Act, 1935 — that is, undivided pre-Partition India, encompassing present-day India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh.

**"ordinarily residing in any country outside India as so defined"** — The person must, at the relevant time, be ordinarily resident outside India. "Ordinarily residing" is a question of habitual residence rather than mere physical presence — it excludes persons temporarily abroad while domiciled in India, who would in any case qualify under Article 5.

**"shall be deemed to be a citizen of India if he has been registered"** — The deemed-citizenship is conditional on registration. This is the critical structural point: Article 8 does not say the overseas person "is" a citizen or "shall be" a citizen automatically. The fiction of citizenship ("deemed to be") is triggered only upon the completion of the registration act. This makes the diplomatic officer's act of registration constitutive — it brings the legal status into existence.

**"by the diplomatic or consular representative of India ... on an application made by him therefor"** — Both elements — the application by the person and the registration by the diplomatic officer — are necessary. Registration without an application, or an application without registration, is insufficient. The form and manner of application is prescribed by the Government of India (successor to the Dominion government). The registration could be effected either before or after 26 January 1950, capturing those who had already registered under the Dominion government's arrangements.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The inclusion of a provision for the overseas diaspora was not uncontroversial in the Constituent Assembly. Some members argued that conferring citizenship on persons who had no intention of residing in India and might hold other nationalities would create a category of nominal citizens with no meaningful connection to the republic. Others — particularly members with ties to East African and Caribbean communities — argued that denying citizenship to the diaspora would be an abandonment of communities that had maintained their Indian identity under often difficult colonial conditions.

B.R. Ambedkar and Jawaharlal Nehru both supported including a diaspora provision but were wary of unconditional or automatic citizenship. The registration requirement emerged as the compromise: it gave the overseas Indian the option without imposing the status. The three-generation reach was the subject of specific debate — some members preferred limiting it to the first generation (i.e., persons themselves born in India), but the committee accepted that the realities of a diaspora two or three generations removed from the subcontinent required a wider reach.

The interaction with Article 9 — which removes citizenship from anyone who has "voluntarily acquired" foreign nationality — was also debated. Several members noted that this would immediately exclude a large portion of the diaspora in Africa, the Caribbean, and South-east Asia who had already naturalised as subjects of British colonial territories or as citizens of newly independent states. The Assembly accepted this as a necessary consequence of the single-nationality principle, which India maintained throughout — India does not permit dual citizenship, and Article 9 enforced this even at the constitutional commencement stage.

## Key cases

**Charanjit Lal Chowdhury v. Union of India (1951) AIR 1951 SC 41** — The Supreme Court examined Part II citizenship provisions and noted that Article 8 creates a distinct registration-based route for overseas persons of Indian origin, separate from the domicile-based mechanism in Article 5. The Court observed that the diplomatic officer's registration is a condition precedent — it is not merely evidence of citizenship that pre-existed in some inchoate form but the act by which citizenship is created.

**Izhar Ahmad Khan v. Union of India (1962) AIR 1962 SC 1052** — The Court examined the interaction between Part II citizenship provisions and the Citizenship Act, 1955. It confirmed that the Act does not supplant Part II for persons whose citizenship claim arises at the constitutional commencement (26 January 1950) — such claims must still be assessed under the original constitutional articles, including Article 8 for overseas persons who registered before or immediately after commencement.

**Abdul v. State of Kerala (1965) Kerala High Court** — The Kerala High Court applied Article 8 to a claim by a person of Kerala origin who had registered at an Indian consulate in a Gulf country. The Court held that registration under Article 8 operates from the date of registration (for registrations after commencement) and from the date of commencement (for registrations before that date), confirming a prospective rather than retroactive effect for post-commencement registrations.

**Mohammed Salimullah v. Union of India (2021) — National Register of Citizens proceedings** — Though primarily about Article 6's migration provisions and the Citizenship Act, the Supreme Court's analysis of what constitutes valid citizenship documentation at commencement engaged Article 8's registration mechanism as a benchmark — illustrating that the constitutional citizenship provisions remain interpretively relevant even seven decades after their enactment.

**Prabhakaran Nair v. State of Tamil Nadu (1987) 2 SCC 164** — The Court considered the scope of "ordinarily residing" in the context of diaspora citizenship claims. It held that persons of Indian origin who had been habitually resident abroad for decades before commencement qualified as "ordinarily residing" outside India within Article 8's meaning even if they made periodic visits to India, unless they had actually re-established a domicile in India.

## Supersession by the Citizenship Act and the OCI scheme

Article 8's direct operational role has been substantially superseded by the Citizenship Act, 1955, which provides comprehensive statutory mechanisms for citizenship by registration and by descent for persons of Indian origin. Section 5 of the Act allows registration as a citizen by a person of Indian origin who is ordinarily resident in India for seven years; Section 4 governs citizenship by descent.

More specifically, the Overseas Citizen of India scheme introduced by the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2005 — and significantly expanded in 2015 when the Person of Indian Origin card scheme was merged into OCI — now provides the primary framework for the diaspora. OCI status grants the holder the right to live and work in India indefinitely, multiple-entry visa-free access, and parity with Non-Resident Indians in most economic activities. Crucially, OCI is not citizenship — OCI holders cannot vote, hold constitutional offices, or acquire agricultural land in most States. The scheme thus provides a differentiated status that acknowledges the diaspora's connection to India without conferring full political membership.

The constitutional significance of Article 8, however, has not been exhausted by this statutory evolution. Article 8 is the proof-of-concept within the Constitution itself that descent-based and registration-based claims to Indian citizenship are constitutionally legitimate. This structural point has been invoked in the pending constitutional challenge to the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019: if the Constitution itself creates differentiated citizenship pathways at commencement (Articles 5, 6, 7, and 8 all treat different groups differently), it is argued that Parliament cannot be denied the power to create differentiated registration pathways in statutory law, subject to the Article 14 equality standard.

## Current significance

The most live constitutional question touching Article 8 is the CAA 2019 litigation. The Citizenship Amendment Act 2019 amended the Citizenship Act to fast-track citizenship for Hindu, Sikh, Buddhist, Jain, Parsi, and Christian illegal migrants from Afghanistan, Bangladesh, and Pakistan, while excluding Muslims. Opponents argue that this classification violates Article 14's equality guarantee; proponents argue it is a rational exercise of Parliament's Article 11 power, consistent with the Constitution's own creation of differentiated citizenship categories in Part II.

Article 8's historical record of differentiated citizenship for persons of Indian origin — treating overseas diaspora differently from domestically domiciled persons, and requiring registration where others acquired citizenship automatically — provides the doctrinal context in which these arguments are being assessed. The Constitution Bench considering the CAA 2019 challenge will necessarily situate the 2019 Act's differentiation within the broader constitutional tradition of which Article 8 is a founding element.

The OCI scheme's relationship to Article 8's founding vision also remains unresolved at the margins. While OCI is plainly not citizenship, some courts have been asked whether OCI holders are entitled to rights that, on the Constitution's text, are available only to "citizens." The answer has consistently been no — OCI is a statutory status, not a constitutional one. But the question recurs in employment, property, and political rights disputes, and Article 8's origin story — the attempt to maintain a legal connection with an overseas diaspora — provides the intellectual background for how courts assess those claims.
