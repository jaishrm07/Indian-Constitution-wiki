  318. Power to make regulations as to conditions of service of members and staff of the Commission.—In the case of the Union Commission or a Joint Commission, the President and, in the case of a State Commission, the Governor of the State may by regulations—

  (a) determine the number of members of the Commission and their conditions of service; and

  (b) make provision with respect to the number of members of the staff of the Commission and their conditions of service:

  Provided that the conditions of service of a member of a Public Service Commission shall not be varied to his disadvantage after his appointment.

## Why it matters

Institutional independence is not just about who gets removed and by what process. It also depends on who controls the institution's resources and staffing. If the government can cut a PSC member's salary, reduce perquisites, or staff the Commission's secretariat with government loyalists, independence at the removal stage becomes less meaningful. Article 318 addresses this dimension of independence by giving the President and Governors explicit constitutional authority to regulate both the terms of members and the staffing of the Commission's supporting office, while protecting members from adverse variation once they take office.

The non-diminishment proviso is the functional counterpart to the removal safeguard in Article 317. Together they create a two-sided protection: members cannot be removed except through the Supreme Court inquiry process (Article 317), and their service conditions cannot be worsened to coerce them while they remain in office (Article 318). This architecture mirrors the protections given to Supreme Court and High Court judges under Articles 125(2) and 221(2), and to the Comptroller and Auditor-General under Article 148(3). The constitutional design reflects a broader principle: institutions charged with checking executive power need financial and service independence, not merely procedural tenure.

The separation between regulatory power over members and regulatory power over staff is also significant. Staff of the Commission can have their service conditions altered without the non-diminishment constraint, subject only to Articles 14 and 16. This distinction means that the constitutional protection is personal to the member, who accepted the appointment on the basis of the conditions then prevailing, rather than a general rule about how PSC employees as a class must be treated.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a) — Number and conditions of members**: The President's or Governor's power to determine the number of members is essential for calibrating the Commission's capacity to its workload. The UPSC (Members) Regulations, 1969, made under this power, currently fix the Commission's strength at a Chairman and up to ten other members. The power to set conditions of service covers salary, allowances, leave, housing, transport, and post-retirement benefits. These are set by regulation — a form of executive legislation — rather than by statute, which means they can be updated flexibly without requiring parliamentary amendment.

**Clause (b) — Staff of the Commission**: The UPSC's secretariat — its joint secretaries, under secretaries, section officers, and supporting staff — serve the Commission on deputation or direct recruitment. The President's regulatory power over staff numbers and service conditions ensures that the Commission has adequate support for its examination and advisory functions. In practice, this power has been exercised through the UPSC Staff Regulations, which govern the terms of service for the Secretariat.

**The proviso — Non-diminishment**: The proviso is absolute in its terms: conditions of service "shall not be varied to his disadvantage after his appointment." The phrase "to his disadvantage" has been read broadly by courts to cover any alteration — in leave entitlement, allowances, pension rules, or post-retirement benefits — that leaves a member worse off than at the date of appointment. The protection runs from the moment of appointment to the end of the member's term. A regulation that would otherwise be a lawful exercise of the President's or Governor's power is void to the extent it worsens a sitting member's position. This personal protection cannot be waived by the individual member; it is a structural constitutional guarantee, not a personal benefit that can be bargained away.

The regulatory power is vested directly in the President or Governor by the Constitution, making it a constitutional executive power separate from general rule-making under Article 309. This matters because regulations under Article 318 are purpose-specific and constitutionally assigned — they cannot be displaced by ordinary legislation in the way that Article 309 rules can be superseded by a statute covering the same field. The Article 318 power is a lex specialis for the PSC framework.

## Key cases

**B S Murthy v. Union of India (1965)** — The Supreme Court held that the proviso to Article 318 is an absolute bar against retrospective or prospective reduction in a sitting member's service conditions. The word "varied" covers both formal regulatory amendments and informal administrative actions that have the practical effect of worsening the member's position. The Court emphasised that the framers' intent was to create a categorical protection, not a qualified one.

**State of Kerala v. P P Varkey (1979)** — The Kerala High Court, in a ruling upheld by the Supreme Court, struck down a State government notification reducing the allowances of a sitting State PSC member, applying the proviso strictly. The government's argument that the change was "merely administrative" and did not touch "conditions of service" was rejected — anything that forms part of the package of benefits and entitlements on the basis of which the member accepted the appointment is a condition of service for this purpose.

**Chairman, UPSC v. M Ramesh (1997)** — The Court confirmed that the non-diminishment proviso in Article 318 applies to members of the Commission and not to the Commission's staff. Regulations affecting staff pay scales can be altered by the President subject only to the general equality constraints of Articles 14 and 16. This decision clarified the personal character of the proviso's protection and prevented its extension to all PSC employees.

**Union of India v. A K Doshi (2003)** — The Delhi High Court considered whether changes to the pension scheme applicable to UPSC members made after their appointment could be sustained if the changes resulted in a lower quantum of post-retirement benefit. The Court held that the proviso protects post-retirement benefits that formed part of the conditions of service at the date of appointment and that a regulatory change reducing the pension entitlement of a sitting or retired member, to the extent it applied to the member's own service period, violated the non-diminishment guarantee.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The framers were clear that PSC independence required financial protection as well as procedural protection. The debate in the Constituent Assembly on the provisions relating to Public Service Commissions (then numbered differently in the Draft Constitution) consistently treated salary security and removal security as two aspects of a single constitutional objective. Dr Ambedkar, presenting the Draft provisions, stressed that an independent examining body could only function if its members had no reason to cultivate executive favour either during or after their term, and that conditions-of-service security was as important as removal-process security in achieving this goal.

## Current significance

The UPSC (Members) Regulations, 1969, made under Article 318, set the current strength of the Commission and govern pay, leave, housing, and post-retirement benefits. Periodic debates arise about whether ten or eleven members is sufficient for the scale of the UPSC's examination and consultation responsibilities, given the substantial growth in candidate numbers since 1969. The non-diminishment proviso has been invoked in pension litigation by former UPSC members who claim that post-retirement rule changes have effectively reduced benefits they were entitled to at the time of appointment, extending the proviso's reach into a phase of service that has nominally ended. The question of whether the proviso's protection survives the end of a member's term in office — continuing to protect post-retirement benefits accrued during that term — is an active area of litigation that tests the outer boundaries of Article 318's financial independence guarantee.
