## What this Article did (and why it was omitted)

Article 131A was inserted by the Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976 — the most extensive constitutional amendment in Indian history, enacted during the Emergency under Prime Minister Indira Gandhi. Among its many provisions curtailing judicial power, Article 131A provided that only the Supreme Court would have jurisdiction to determine whether a Central law was constitutionally invalid. High Courts, which had always possessed this power as part of their jurisdiction to issue writs under Article 226 and to adjudicate civil and criminal cases requiring constitutional interpretation, were stripped of it.

The practical effect was significant. When a citizen challenged a Central law in a High Court — whether on Article 14 grounds (arbitrary or discriminatory), Article 19 grounds (violation of freedom of speech, occupation, etc.), or any other constitutional ground — the High Court could not decide the constitutional validity question. Under Article 131A, it would have been required to refer the question to the Supreme Court, creating delays and concentrating constitutional adjudication in a single tribunal that already had enormous docket pressures.

For the Emergency government, the provision had clear advantages: High Courts across the country were the primary forum for writ petitions challenging government action, and concentrating constitutional validity review in Delhi reduced the risk of adverse rulings from regional benches with differing political orientations.

## The Emergency context

Article 131A was part of a cluster of 42nd Amendment provisions designed to shift the balance of power toward the Executive and away from the judiciary. The same amendment inserted Article 226A (barring High Courts from considering constitutional validity of Central laws in writ proceedings) and Article 228A (requiring a minimum five-judge bench for validity questions about State laws). Together, these provisions effectively paralysed High Courts as venues for constitutional litigation about parliamentary legislation.

The 42nd Amendment also amended Article 368 to declare that Parliament's amending power was unlimited and no amendment could be questioned in any court — a provision that the Supreme Court struck down in Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India (1980) as itself violating the basic structure.

## Omission by the 43rd Amendment (1977)

After the Emergency ended and the Janata Party government came to power in the 1977 elections, Parliament enacted the Constitution (Forty-third Amendment) Act, 1977. Section 4 of that Amendment omitted Article 131A, effective 13 April 1978. The omission was part of a systematic reversal of the Emergency constitutional changes. Article 226A and 228A were simultaneously omitted by sections 8 and 10 of the same Amendment.

The restoration of High Courts' power to assess the constitutional validity of Central laws was treated as an essential element of restoring the rule of law and judicial independence after the Emergency, and was among the 43rd Amendment's least controversial provisions.

## Constitutional significance

Article 131A's brief existence and omission illuminate several important constitutional principles. The provision demonstrated that restricting judicial review of legislation to a single apex court, however superficially reasonable as a matter of judicial efficiency, is constitutionally problematic when it eliminates access to regional judicial forums and creates practical barriers to constitutional challenge.

More fundamentally, the episode confirmed that judicial review of legislative validity — including the power of High Courts to examine whether Central laws comply with the Constitution — is not a mere statutory assignment that Parliament can re-route by constitutional amendment. The restoration of this power in 1977 was treated as a constitutional imperative, not a policy choice.

The 43rd Amendment's reversal of the Emergency-era judicial curtailments, of which Article 131A was one, stands as a significant moment in Indian constitutional history: an example of Parliament using the constituent power to restore rather than reduce judicial independence. This stands in contrast to the more usual concern about Parliament using the amending power to entrench executive dominance.

## Reading this Article today

Article 131A is today of purely historical and doctrinal interest. It appears in the constitutional text as a tombstone entry noting its omission. Reading it alongside Articles 226A and 228A — also omitted by the 43rd Amendment — provides a concentrated view of the Emergency's approach to judicial power and the constitutional response when democratic governance was restored. Anyone studying the limits of the amending power, the basic structure doctrine's engagement with judicial review, or the institutional design of constitutional courts in India will find these three omitted articles an instructive comparative study.
