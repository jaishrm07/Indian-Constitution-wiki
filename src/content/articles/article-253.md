## Why it matters

Article 253 is the constitutional bridge between India's international obligations and its domestic statute book. In a federal system where legislative competence is divided between Parliament and State Legislatures, the power to bind India internationally through treaties sits entirely with the Union executive under Article 73. But treaty-making without a corresponding legislative power to implement those treaties would be practically hollow: India could ratify international conventions but lack Parliamentary competence to enact the domestic legislation they require if the subject matter fell on the State List. Article 253 closes this gap definitively. Its "notwithstanding" clause overrides the entire ordinary scheme of legislative distribution in Chapter I of Part XI — meaning Parliament can legislate on any State List subject, without State consent, whenever the purpose is to implement a treaty, international agreement, convention, or decision of an international body.

The scope of this power is genuinely transformative. India's international obligations span subjects that are squarely within the State List: forests, fisheries, local industries, public health, agriculture, water, education. Without Article 253, each of these subjects would require either State legislation or the cumbersome Article 252 consent mechanism to produce Central legislation. With Article 253, a decision of an international conference or an obligation under a bilateral or multilateral treaty can ground Parliamentary legislation on any of these subjects. India's wildlife protection laws, biodiversity legislation, major environmental statutes, and legislation implementing UN anti-trafficking conventions all trace their Parliamentary competence partly through Article 253.

The provision also functions as a judicial interpretive tool in a way that makes it more than a simple legislative power grant. The Supreme Court has held, beginning with Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan, that where Parliament has not discharged its Article 253 implementing obligation, courts may themselves draw on India's unimplemented treaty commitments to fill gaps in constitutional protection. This judicial use of Article 253 has significantly expanded the content of fundamental rights in areas from gender equality to transgender rights to environmental protection — making Article 253, indirectly, a source of judge-made law as well as a grant of Parliamentary legislative competence.

For federal theory, Article 253 represents one of the most significant structural asymmetries in the Indian Constitution. While States retain their State List competence as a general matter, the Union's treaty-making power — an executive power exercised without parliamentary approval and without State consent — can in effect expand Parliament's legislative jurisdiction over State List subjects whenever an international obligation can be shown. The breadth of Article 253's override and the permissive interpretation courts have given to the "implementing" requirement means that the provision can be used to justify Central legislation on a wide range of State-domain subjects with relatively thin international law justification. This tension between State autonomy and India's obligations as a member of the international community runs through the entire body of Article 253 jurisprudence.

## Reading the provision

The provision is unusually compact — a single sentence — but each element carries significant weight.

**"Notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Chapter"** — This is a blanket override of Articles 245 through 252, which together constitute the ordinary legislative competence framework. It means all of those provisions — including Article 246's three-list division of legislative subjects, and the territorial limits in Article 245 — are subordinated when Parliament acts under Article 253. Parliament can legislate on State List subjects, Concurrent List subjects, or Union List subjects for the purpose of implementing international obligations. The override operates without requiring any State consent or Rajya Sabha resolution — unlike Articles 249 and 252, Article 253 conditions Parliament's extended competence on nothing beyond the existence of an international obligation.

**"Parliament has power to make any law for the whole or any part of the territory of India"** — The territorial scope of Article 253 legislation can be India-wide or partial. Parliament may implement a treaty obligation for specific States or territories if the obligation is geographically limited. This flexibility allows, for example, implementation of border-specific bilateral agreements or regional conventions that apply only to certain parts of India.

**"For implementing any treaty, agreement or convention with any other country or countries"** — The trigger is implementation of an international instrument. Three categories of bilateral and multilateral instruments are expressly named: treaties, agreements, and conventions. The distinction between these terms is primarily one of formality and form — a treaty is typically the most formal, ratified instrument; an agreement may be concluded by executive exchange; a convention is usually a multilateral framework instrument opened for signature. The inclusion of all three means the form of the international instrument is not decisive. Even a bilateral executive agreement that has not been ratified by Parliament can ground Article 253 legislation if it satisfies the "implementing" test.

**"Or any decision made at any international conference, association or other body"** — This extension is of great practical importance. It means that decisions of international bodies — resolutions of the UN General Assembly, decisions of the Conference of the Parties to multilateral environmental agreements, recommendations of WHO, ILO Conventions — can all serve as the basis for Article 253 legislation, even when they are not formal treaties. The phrase "or other body" is deliberately residual and broad, capturing the full range of intergovernmental and international institutional outputs that India's executive might commit to implement. Courts have read this broadly, accepting that India's obligations under soft-law international instruments such as the Stockholm and Rio Declarations can ground Article 253 implementing legislation.

**The "implementing" requirement — breadth of the nexus test:** The critical question under Article 253 is how close the nexus between the international obligation and the domestic legislation must be. Must the legislation be strictly necessary to fulfil a specific treaty obligation, or is a loose connection sufficient? The Supreme Court's approach has been consistently permissive. In Maganbhai Patel v. Union of India, the Court held that Parliament's power under Article 253 is wide and that legislation is valid under Article 253 as long as it can reasonably be said to implement the international obligation — a tight correspondence between specific treaty clauses and specific statutory provisions is not required. The "implementing" test is one of reasonable connection, not strict necessity. This has been interpreted to mean that a law enacted to give effect to the general objects and purposes of a treaty or convention, even if not required word-for-word by the treaty text, is valid Article 253 legislation.

## Key cases

**Gramophone Company of India Ltd. v. Birendra Bahadur Pandey (1984) 2 SCC 534** — The Supreme Court confronted the relationship between international treaty law and domestic municipal law. Examining India's obligations under the Universal Copyright Convention in the context of importing infringing records, the Court held that international treaties form part of Indian law to the extent they are not inconsistent with domestic statutes, and that courts must construe Indian laws in conformity with treaty obligations wherever possible. The judgment established the principle of treaty-conforming interpretation — the presumption that Parliament does not intend to legislate contrary to India's international obligations — as a canon of statutory construction. Although the case turned on interpretive methodology rather than the scope of Article 253 legislative competence directly, it is foundational for understanding how international law enters domestic Indian law and why Article 253 is not the only avenue for treaty norms to influence domestic outcomes.

**Maganbhai Ishwarbhai Patel v. Union of India (1970) 3 SCC 400** — In this landmark five-judge Constitution Bench decision, the Court examined whether Parliament had competence to legislate on matters arising from the Rann of Kutch boundary agreement with Pakistan. The State of Gujarat argued that the cession of territory involved State-domain matters requiring State consent. The Court held that Parliament's power under Article 253 to implement international agreements is wide and is not subject to the restrictions of the ordinary Seventh Schedule distribution. The judgment established that the "implementing" requirement under Article 253 does not demand a clause-by-clause correspondence between the treaty text and the domestic law — a reasonable nexus between the legislation and the international obligation is sufficient. This broad interpretation has governed Article 253 analysis ever since and explains why Central environmental, wildlife, and biodiversity legislation has been upheld under Article 253 even when specific statutory provisions go beyond the precise treaty obligations.

**Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997) 6 SCC 241** — The Supreme Court was called upon to address the constitutional void created by Parliament's failure to enact legislation on workplace sexual harassment, which India's obligations under CEDAW (the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women, ratified by India in 1993) and the Beijing Platform for Action required. In the absence of domestic legislation, the Court invoked Article 253 read with Articles 14, 15, and 21 to derive enforceable guidelines binding on all employers. The Court held that international conventions ratified by India, when not inconsistent with the Constitution and domestic law, could be used to expand the content of fundamental rights, and that where Parliament had not yet fulfilled its Article 253 obligation to implement these conventions, courts could fill the gap through interpretation. Vishaka created binding employer guidelines — in effect, judge-made sub-constitutional law — that operated for sixteen years until Parliament enacted the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act in 2013. The case is the single most cited authority for the proposition that Article 253 is not only a legislative competence grant but also an interpretive basis for courts to enforce unimplemented treaty obligations as part of fundamental rights.

**Novartis AG v. Union of India (2013) 6 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court upheld Section 3(d) of the Patents Act 1970 (inserted by the 2005 amendment) against Novartis's challenge that it violated India's obligations under the TRIPS Agreement (the Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights). The Court held that the Patents Act, as amended, was a valid exercise of Parliament's Article 253 power to implement TRIPS and the WTO Agreement, and that the TRIPS Agreement itself left member states a significant "policy space" to calibrate patentability standards in accordance with their public health objectives. The case is important for Article 253 because it demonstrates that implementing legislation need not adopt the most restrictive interpretation of treaty obligations — Parliament may implement an international agreement in a manner that reflects its own policy priorities within the space the agreement allows. The judgment also represents the most significant encounter between Article 253-based legislation and judicial scrutiny of whether India's domestic law actually meets its treaty obligations, a question the Court resolved in favour of Parliamentary discretion.

**People's Union for Civil Liberties v. Union of India (1997) 3 SCC 433** — In a challenge to the right to privacy in the context of telephone tapping, the Supreme Court drew on the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR), ratified by India, to interpret Article 21's right to life and personal liberty broadly. While the case did not directly turn on Article 253 legislative competence, it confirmed the wider principle — articulated first in Vishaka — that unimplemented treaty obligations ratified by India can inform the interpretation of fundamental rights. The PUCL judgment extended the Vishaka approach beyond women's rights to general civil liberties, cementing the role of Article 253 (and the international obligations underlying it) as an interpretive resource across the full range of fundamental rights jurisprudence.

## Current significance

Article 253 underpins a substantial body of Central legislation on State-domain subjects. The Wildlife Protection Act 1972 draws competence from India's obligations under the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES). The Biological Diversity Act 2002 implements the Convention on Biological Diversity. The Environment (Protection) Act 1986 derives partly from India's obligations under the Stockholm Declaration on the Human Environment. The Protection of Human Rights Act 1993 implements India's UN Charter obligations. The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act 2012 gives effect to the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child. Each of these laws extends Parliamentary competence into subjects — forests, wildlife, water, child welfare — that would otherwise fall in the State List.

A persistent structural tension surrounds the breadth of the "implementing" nexus requirement. If any reasonable connection to an international obligation suffices, Article 253 can effectively become a route to centralise almost any regulatory subject by securing a treaty commitment or joining an international body whose decisions touch that subject. Critics of this view argue that requiring a genuine implementing purpose — not merely any connection to an international instrument — is essential to preserve meaningful federal boundaries. The judicial record is not entirely consistent: Maganbhai supports a wide view, while the Novartis judgment suggests courts will examine whether the legislation actually falls within the space the international agreement provides, implying at least a threshold of genuine implementation.

The Paris Agreement on climate change and the net-zero commitments India has made at successive COP sessions have intensified the relevance of Article 253. India lacks comprehensive domestic climate legislation equivalent to the UK's Climate Change Act. As climate litigation begins to arrive before Indian constitutional courts — with petitioners invoking Articles 21 and 48A alongside international climate obligations — the question of whether Article 253 requires Parliament to legislate to implement COP decisions, and whether courts can use unimplemented climate commitments to expand environmental rights under the Vishaka principle, is an emerging constitutional frontier. The answer will depend heavily on whether COP decisions count as decisions of "any international conference, association or other body" under Article 253 and whether India's NDC (Nationally Determined Contribution) commitments are sufficiently specific to ground implementing legislation claims.

The Article 253 implementing gap — between India's ratified treaty obligations and domestic legislation — remains wide on several subjects. The Convention Against Torture (signed but not ratified), the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights obligations on economic and social rights legislation, and numerous ILO Conventions on labour rights remain partially or wholly unimplemented. Courts have been willing to draw on these obligations under the Vishaka principle but have also been careful to note that judicial gap-filling under Article 253 is a second-best substitute for Parliamentary legislation, not a permanent substitute for it. The Article 253 story is therefore as much about Parliament's implementing failures as about the Courts' creative responses to them.
