## Why it matters

Article 69 prescribes a constitutionally mandated condition precedent to the exercise of Vice-Presidential authority: the oath of allegiance and faithful discharge of duties, taken before the President or a Presidential nominee. Every detail of the provision — the administering authority, the form of the oath, and its content — is constitutionally deliberate and contrasts pointedly with the presidential oath under Article 60. These contrasts illuminate the constitutional hierarchy between the two offices and the different nature of the obligations each office-holder accepts on entering.

The most immediate significance of Article 69 is operational: no one can begin exercising Vice-Presidential functions — presiding over the Rajya Sabha, performing any function under Article 64, or acting as presidential successor under Article 65 — until the oath is taken. The oath is not a ceremonial formality that can be performed after assuming office; it is the condition that constitutionally enables the assumption. Any act purportedly taken in a Vice-Presidential capacity before the oath would lack constitutional foundation and would be subject to challenge as void.

The choice to have the oath administered by the President — or someone the President nominates — rather than by the Chief Justice of India (as for the presidential oath) reflects a considered constitutional hierarchy. The presidential oath is administered by the head of the judiciary because the President's primary obligation is to the Constitution itself, independent of any other constitutional officer. The Vice-Presidential oath is administered by the head of the executive because the Vice-President is a subordinate constitutional officer whose obligations run through the constitutional framework the President formally heads. The President administering the oath is a constitutional expression of institutional rank.

The content difference from the presidential oath is equally significant. Under Article 60, the President pledges to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution and the law" — an active, protective mandate. Under Article 69, the Vice-President pledges only "true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India as by law established" and "faithful discharge" of duties — a passive, fidelity-based commitment without the active constitutional guardianship that the President's oath implies. This narrower formulation has practical constitutional consequences: the Vice-President, as Rajya Sabha Chairman, does not have an independent constitutional mandate to review Cabinet decisions, return Bills, or otherwise act as a constitutional check on the government in the way that the President's active oath arguably supports.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "before entering upon his office" makes the oath a strict temporal precondition. Entry upon office is identified with the moment of taking and subscribing the oath — not with the election result, the notification, or any administrative formality. "Subscribe" means the oath-taker must sign the document on which the oath is recorded. The combination of making (speaking) and subscribing (signing) ensures that the oath has both oral and written authentication.

The alternative of "President, or some person appointed in that behalf by him" gives the administering authority flexibility. In the 2022 succession, where the incoming Vice-President was sworn in before the outgoing President was approaching the end of the term, the sitting President administered the oath. In any scenario where the President is incapacitated or unavailable, a Presidential nominee — typically a senior Union Minister designated by the President for this purpose — can perform the administration. This flexibility ensures that Article 69's condition precedent can always be satisfied even in unusual administrative circumstances.

The oath text uses the dual formula "I, A.B., do swear in the name of God / solemnly affirm" to accommodate both religious oath-taking and secular affirmation. This parallel formulation is standard in Indian constitutional oaths, reflecting the secular character of the Republic and the right of conscience of individual office-holders.

## The two-oath sequence when acting as President

When a Vice-President steps up to act as President under Article 65 — either because of a presidential vacancy or the President's temporary incapacity — the Article 69 oath that the Vice-President has already taken is insufficient for presidential functions. The more demanding presidential oath under Article 60 must be separately taken before the Chief Justice of India before any presidential function is exercised. Article 69's oath of allegiance does not incorporate the protective mandate ("preserve, protect and defend the Constitution") that the presidential oath requires.

This two-oath structure creates a constitutionally precise succession sequence. The incoming Vice-President takes the Article 69 oath before the President. If the presidential term then expires before a new President is elected, the same individual must take the Article 60 oath before the Chief Justice to act as President under Article 65(1). Two separate ceremonies, before two separate administering authorities, creating two separate sets of constitutional obligations — the second superseding the first for the period of presidential substitution without erasing it. When the acting Presidency ends and the Vice-President resumes the Vice-Presidential role, no fresh Article 69 oath is required: the original oath remains in force throughout the entire Vice-Presidential term.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Constitution Bench's comparative analysis of the President's and Vice-President's constitutional roles, drawing on Articles 60 and 69, noted that the different oath formulations reinforce the hierarchical and functional distinction between the two offices. The Vice-President's oath of allegiance is a parliamentary officer's commitment, whereas the presidential oath encompasses a broader and active constitutional guardianship. The Court drew on this distinction to resist arguments that the Vice-President has independent constitutional authority co-equal to the President's.

**Constituent Assembly Debates on Article 69** — The framers explicitly considered whether the Vice-President's oath should mirror the President's in full. Dr B.R. Ambedkar explained that the narrower formulation was deliberate: the Vice-President is primarily a legislative presiding officer rather than the head of the executive, and the oath should reflect that role. Imposing the full presidential protective obligation on the Vice-President would have implications for how the Vice-President exercises the Rajya Sabha chairmanship — implications that the framers considered constitutionally inappropriate for a parliamentary presiding officer.

**B.D. Jatti's two-oath sequence (1977)** — When Vice-President B.D. Jatti acted as President following President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed's death, he took a fresh presidential oath under Article 60 before the Chief Justice of India before performing any presidential function. This confirmed in practice that the Article 69 Vice-Presidential oath does not substitute for the Article 60 presidential oath, and that the two-oath succession sequence is constitutionally required rather than merely conventional. Jatti's administration of presidential functions for over five months under the Article 60 oath, before reverting to the Vice-Presidency when Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy was sworn in, validated the entire constitutional chain.

**2022 succession ceremonies** — When Jagdeep Dhankhar was sworn in as Vice-President on 11 August 2022, the Article 69 oath was administered by outgoing President Ram Nath Kovind, whose term had formally expired the previous day. The holdover provision of Article 56(1)(c) meant Kovind remained formally in office until Droupadi Murmu's swearing-in as President later that day. The sequencing of the two ceremonies — Dhankhar's Article 69 oath followed by Murmu's Article 60 oath — illustrated the constitutional interlocking of Articles 60, 65, 68, and 69 in a real-world transition.

## Current significance

The Article 69 oath requirement has acquired practical significance in the context of debates about the independence and impartiality of the Rajya Sabha Chairman. Since the Vice-President's oath is one of allegiance and faithful discharge of duties rather than one of constitutional guardianship, critics of partisan chairmanship conduct have argued that the oath's narrow scope leaves the Vice-President without a strong constitutional foundation for acting as a non-partisan constitutional officer. Defenders respond that faithful discharge of duties implicitly requires impartiality in the Chair, since biased procedural rulings would not constitute "faithful" discharge.

The distinction between the two oaths — and their different administering authorities — has also been invoked in academic debates about judicial independence. The President's oath before the Chief Justice symbolises the judiciary's role as the constitutional guarantor. The Vice-President's oath before the President symbolises the legislature's subordination to the executive hierarchy. These symbolic dimensions are not merely ceremonial — they shape the institutional culture and self-understanding of the offices concerned.

The 2022 constitutional succession demonstrated that the interlocking oath requirements of Articles 60, 65, and 69 function smoothly as a practical matter, even under the time pressure of back-to-back ceremonies within a few hours. This smooth operation is a testament to careful advance constitutional planning rather than happy accident.
