officialText: |-
  243ZM. Audit of accounts of co-operative societies.—(1) The Legislature of a State may, by law, make provisions with respect to the maintenance of accounts by the co-operative societies and the auditing of such accounts at least once in each financial year.

  (2) The Legislature of a State shall, by law, lay down the minimum qualifications and experience of auditors and auditing firms that shall be eligible for auditing accounts of the co-operative societies.

  (3) Every co-operative society shall cause to be audited by an auditor or auditing firms referred to in clause (2) appointed by the general body of the co-operative society:

  Provided that such auditors or auditing firms shall be appointed from a panel approved by a State Government or an authority authorised by the State Government in this behalf.

  (4) The accounts of every co-operative society shall be audited within six months of the close of the financial year to which such accounts relate.

  (5) The audit report of the accounts of an apex co-operative society, as may be defined by the State Act, shall be laid before the State Legislature in the manner, as may be provided by the State Legislature, by law.

## Why it matters

Weak or captured audit processes were a central governance failure in the co-operative sector. In many States, co-operative societies were audited by government audit officers who were institutionally reluctant to issue adverse findings against politically connected societies. Audit qualifications were inconsistent, audit appointments were controlled by boards rather than members, and reports were either not produced on time or not made available to members. The result was that the co-operative sector's accounts were opaque even to their nominal owners — the members — let alone to the wider public.

Article 243ZM constitutionalises the basic framework for independent, timely audit. Several elements of the design are worth examining. The general body — not the board — appoints the auditor: this prevents the board from controlling who audits it. The auditor must come from a State government-approved panel — this ensures minimum quality standards and prevents shell-firm appointments. The qualification requirements that the State must legislate are a mandatory constitutional obligation (clause (2) uses "shall"), not a discretionary power. The six-month audit completion deadline prevents the perpetual deferral that had allowed some societies to avoid effective auditing for years.

## Apex society transparency — clause (5)

The requirement that audit reports of apex co-operative societies be laid before the State Legislature is a significant transparency mechanism. Apex co-operative societies — typically State co-operative banks, apex dairy federations, or State marketing federations — are among the largest financial institutions in their States, managing resources that affect hundreds of thousands of farmers, dairy producers, and other rural members. Requiring their audit reports to be tabled in the Legislature creates public accountability for these institutions and allows elected legislators to scrutinise their management. This is the co-operative equivalent of the requirement for public sector undertaking accounts to be laid before the Legislature.

## Interaction with Registrar audit powers

Pre-97th Amendment co-operative law typically vested audit authority in the Registrar of Co-operative Societies, a government officer. This meant audits were conducted by the State government rather than by independent professionals appointed by the members. Article 243ZM shifts the audit framework from a government-audit to a panel-professional model: audit must be done by qualified private auditors or auditing firms from an approved panel, appointed by the general body. The State's audit role shifts from conducting audits to setting qualification standards and approving auditor panels — a regulatory rather than operational role. This structural shift is designed to enhance both independence and professional quality, while maintaining minimum standards through State regulation of the auditor qualification framework.

## Post-Rajendra N. Shah position

Following the 2021 ruling, Article 243ZM's requirements are constitutionally mandatory for multi-State co-operative societies. For State co-operatives, the binding force depends on what State law provides — States are not constitutionally compelled to follow the Part IXB audit framework for their own sector. However, the Part IXB audit standards have influenced co-operative sector reform legislation in several States, and the NABARD-administered model co-operative societies act reflects norms broadly consistent with Article 243ZM. The provision also serves as a constitutional benchmark in litigation challenging the adequacy of audit frameworks in specific co-operative societies.
