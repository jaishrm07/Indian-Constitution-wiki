## What this Article does

Article 177 gives Ministers and the Advocate-General of a State the right to speak and participate in the proceedings of the State Legislature — including its committees — even if they are not elected members of the House in question. This ensures the executive arm retains a voice on the floor of the legislature for purposes of accountability, explanation, and policy exposition. A Minister who is a member of the Legislative Council, for example, may speak in the Legislative Assembly on matters affecting the government, and vice versa.

The article expressly withholds the right to vote from persons attending under its authority, drawing a clear line between participation in debate and exercise of the franchise. This mirrors the parallel provision for Parliament under Article 88 and reflects a consistent constitutional design in which formal presence in proceedings does not automatically confer voting rights.

## Who is covered

The article covers two categories:

**Ministers**: Every member of the State Council of Ministers, whether holding a portfolio or not, whether a member of the Assembly or the Council. A Minister appointed from the Council can speak in the Assembly, and a Minister appointed from the Assembly can speak in the Council. This cross-chamber speaking right is particularly important in bicameral states (Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, Uttar Pradesh), where a coalition government may have Ministers drawn from both Houses.

**The Advocate-General**: The State's chief law officer, appointed under Article 165, has the same right to speak and participate in either House. This provision allows the government's legal advisers to brief the legislature on constitutional or legal aspects of pending legislation — a function that becomes important when contentious bills raise fundamental rights or federalism concerns that require professional legal explication.

## Committee proceedings

The right extends to committee proceedings of which the Minister or Advocate-General "may be named a member." This language allows for Ministers and the Advocate-General to be formally nominated to select committees or standing committees without necessarily being elected members of the House to which the committee belongs. Their participation there is also subject to the same no-voting rule: they can contribute expertise but cannot determine committee outcomes by vote.

## The no-voting rule

The explicit denial of voting rights is constitutionally significant. It means that Ministers who cross-speak under Article 177 cannot affect the numerical result of any division in the other House. A Council Minister speaking in the Assembly under Article 177 during a money bill debate — where the Council has no power — cannot try to influence the outcome by voting. A Minister who has lost an election and is temporarily holding office under Article 164's six-month grace period has no vote in the Assembly during that period when appearing under Article 177, though in that case the Minister is not even a member and the article specifically governs the participation right.

## Key cases

**In re: Kerala Legislative Assembly (Speaker's rulings, various)** — Speaker's rulings in several states have interpreted Article 177 to mean that a Minister speaking under this article is subject to the same rules of procedure and discipline as an elected member, including the power of the presiding officer to call the Minister to order. The articles of legislative privilege apply equally to Ministers exercising the Article 177 right.

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court's affirmation that Ministers function collectively and under cabinet discipline has implications for Article 177: Ministers attending the other House do so as spokespersons for the cabinet, and their statements can bind the government on the floor of the legislature.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — Though concerned with the Speaker's powers, the judgment's broad analysis of the relationship between the executive and legislature under Part VI confirms that the Article 177 right must be exercised consistently with constitutional norms of legislative propriety and cannot be used to manipulate House proceedings for partisan advantage.

## Current significance

The Article has gained renewed relevance in bicameral states where coalition governments routinely include Ministers who are members of the Council rather than the Assembly, or who have lost elections and hold office temporarily under Article 164. In such situations Article 177 is the only constitutional basis on which those Ministers can defend their ministries' estimates, answer calling-attention motions, or participate in legislative debate in the Assembly. The Advocate-General's participation rights under the same provision are particularly significant in states where major constitutional litigation — over ordinances, Bills returned by the Governor, or Article 356 actions — requires the government's law officer to brief the legislature directly.
