  Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, the Legislature of a State may, by law, endow the Panchayats with such powers and authority as may be necessary to enable them to function as institutions of self-government and such law may contain provisions for the devolution of powers and responsibilities upon Panchayats at the appropriate level, subject to such conditions as may be specified therein, with respect to—

## Why it matters

Article 243G is the devolution engine of the 73rd Amendment. It requires state legislatures to endow Panchayats with the powers and authority necessary to enable them to function as institutions of self-government — not merely as administrative agencies of the state. The article is deliberately framed as a constitutional direction to devolve, and it links that devolution to two specific purposes: preparation of plans for economic development and social justice, and implementation of schemes relating to the twenty-nine subjects listed in the Eleventh Schedule. Those subjects range from agriculture and land improvement to primary and secondary education, poverty alleviation, and social forestry. Article 243G is significant because it transforms Panchayats from bodies exercising delegated administrative discretion into constitutional institutions with a mandate to plan and implement in their own right.

The gap between this constitutional aspiration and the reality of state-level devolution — or the absence of it — is the central unresolved question of Indian local governance. The 73rd Amendment was a political landmark when enacted in 1992, but its transformative promise rested entirely on what state legislatures would actually choose to do under Article 243G. The article uses the permissive word "may" rather than "shall": the Legislature of a State "may, by law, endow" Panchayats with powers. This choice of language has proved consequential. It means that the Constitution creates the institutional structure — mandatory elections, reservations, finance commissions — but places the substance of actual governance authority at the discretion of state governments, which have powerful political and administrative incentives to retain centralised control over the schemes and funds that Article 243G envisions being devolved.

This dynamic has generated what analysts call the "3Fs problem": finance, functions, and functionaries must all be devolved together for Panchayats to be genuinely empowered, but states have typically devolved some combination of these in partial and inconsistent ways. A Panchayat that receives the function of maintaining primary schools but not the funds to pay teachers, or one that receives funds but whose gram sevak reports to the district collector rather than to the elected Gram Sabha, is not a functioning institution of self-government in any meaningful sense. Article 243G's twenty-nine Eleventh Schedule subjects only acquire operational meaning when the full triad of functions, funds, and functionaries is transferred. Most states have devolved some functions on paper, almost none have transferred all three consistently across all subjects.

The 73rd Amendment was meant to be a paradigm shift in Indian governance philosophy — away from the Nehruvian model of development administered from above, and toward a model in which elected local bodies at village, intermediate, and district levels plan and implement programmes for their own communities. Article 243G is the constitutional provision that makes this shift operational. Its failure to be implemented fully is not merely a governance problem; it is a constitutional failure that calls into question whether India's formal commitment to democratic decentralisation is matched by the political will to make it real.

## Reading the provision

The opening phrase — "Subject to the provisions of this Constitution" — preserves the supremacy of the Constitution as a whole, including fundamental rights. Devolution under Article 243G cannot, for instance, authorise a Panchayat to take action that infringes on Part III rights, and State laws made under this article remain subject to judicial review on constitutional grounds.

The phrase "the Legislature of a State may, by law" is the source of the permissive character of Article 243G. Devolution is not self-executing. A State legislature must pass a positive law. Until it does, no Panchayat has constitutional authority over Eleventh Schedule subjects simply because the subject is listed. This contrasts with the mandatory obligations elsewhere in Part IX — such as the requirement to hold elections under Article 243E and the mandatory reservation requirements under Article 243D — which operate independently of State legislative choice.

"Such powers and authority as may be necessary to enable them to function as institutions of self-government" sets the constitutional benchmark. The purpose is not merely to give Panchayats some tasks to perform but to give them what is necessary for genuine self-government. This language has been used by courts and by the Second Administrative Reforms Commission to argue that token devolution — handing over functions without accompanying funds and staff — does not satisfy the constitutional standard. The word "necessary" implies that the devolution must be functionally sufficient, not merely nominal.

"The preparation of plans for economic development and social justice" in clause (a) confers planning authority — a power historically jealously guarded by state planning departments and by the erstwhile Planning Commission and its successor NITI Aayog. Genuine implementation of this clause would require district-level and village-level planning processes to feed upward into state and national plans rather than, as is customary, national and state plans being disaggregated downward for local implementation.

Clause (b) — "implementation of schemes for economic development and social justice as may be entrusted to them including those in relation to the matters listed in the Eleventh Schedule" — contains the Eleventh Schedule reference. The twenty-nine subjects in the Eleventh Schedule are illustrative of the scope of potential devolution, not mandatory minimums. A state may devolve fewer subjects or more, with or without conditions. The phrase "as may be entrusted to them" emphasises that entrusting must be an affirmative act; default retention by the state is always permissible.

"At the appropriate level" recognises that Panchayats exist at three levels under Article 243B — village, intermediate, and district. Article 243G allows devolution to be calibrated to the appropriate tier for each function. Village sanitation may be a Gram Panchayat function; secondary roads an Intermediate Panchayat function; district hospital maintenance a Zila Panchayat function. The failure of most states to specify this calibration clearly has led to overlapping mandates and functional ambiguity.

## Key cases

**State of Uttarakhand v. Balwant Singh Chaufal (2010)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of Panchayat governance directly engaged Article 243G's devolutionary mandate and found that it creates constitutional accountability for states to give Panchayats genuine functional authority. The Court stressed that the constitutional design treats Panchayats as institutions of self-government with planning and implementation responsibilities, not as subordinate administrative extensions of the state, and that governance accountability flows from this design. The ruling is significant because it treated Article 243G as creating an enforceable constitutional standard rather than an unreviewable legislative discretion.

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004)** — The Supreme Court's interpretation of Part IX in this case acknowledged Article 243G as the normative foundation for understanding what Panchayats are constitutionally intended to do. The case reinforced that restrictions on Panchayat functioning imposed by state governments must be assessed against the constitutional purpose of enabling genuine local self-governance, not merely formal electoral participation. The Court's reasoning has been applied in subsequent cases to question whether administrative orders that bypass Panchayat bodies in the delivery of government schemes are constitutionally sustainable.

**Gram Panchayat of Village Jamalpur v. Malwinder Singh (1985)** — Decided before the 73rd Amendment but significant for establishing the legal personality and autonomous domain of village Panchayats as local governing bodies. The Court's recognition that Panchayats exercised distinct governmental functions, not merely delegated ministerial tasks, provided part of the foundation for the 73rd Amendment's constitutional elevation of Panchayats to institutions of self-government under Article 243G. Post-amendment courts have returned to this ruling to emphasise that the 1992 changes built on an existing jurisprudential tradition of treating Panchayats as genuine governing institutions.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court's comprehensive analysis of the 73rd and 74th Amendment framework addressed the obligations imposed on State governments by Part IX, including the devolution mandate in Article 243G. The Court held that the constitutional design of local self-government — including the Eleventh Schedule, State Finance Commissions, and the election machinery — constitutes an integrated scheme that cannot be partially implemented. Selective compliance, such as holding elections without devolving functions or funds, was characterised as inconsistent with the constitutional design.

**State of U.P. v. Pradhan Sangh Kshettra Samiti (1995)** — The Supreme Court held that the constitutional mandate to constitute local bodies and hold elections was not merely directory, and extended this reasoning to the broader institutional framework of Part IX. The ruling laid the groundwork for treating the Article 243G devolution mandate as a constitutional obligation of substance, not merely of form, and has been cited by State Finance Commissions and advocacy bodies as authority for requiring measurable devolution progress rather than accepting token compliance.

## Current significance

The central structural failure identified by most analyses of the 73rd Amendment is that states have been slow and incomplete in implementing Article 243G's devolution mandate. As of the early 2020s, no state has transferred all twenty-nine Eleventh Schedule subjects to Panchayats in a comprehensive and operationally meaningful way. The Second Administrative Reforms Commission, the National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution, and every Central Finance Commission since the Eleventh have documented this devolution deficit and recommended remediation. The consequence is that Panchayats exist as constitutional institutions without the functional authority that Article 243G envisages. They hold elections, maintain minimum quorums, pass resolutions, and formally receive some grants — but the substantive planning and implementation role that Article 243G envisions remains with district bureaucracies, state line departments, and central scheme implementing agencies.

The second dimension of current significance involves the relationship between Article 243G and Centrally Sponsored Schemes. When the Union government channels funds for local development through state governments rather than directly to Panchayats, and when states then administer those schemes through district bureaucracies rather than Panchayat bodies, the Article 243G vision of Panchayat-led implementation is bypassed. Efforts to direct scheme funds through Gram Panchayat Development Plans — a mechanism with direct constitutional roots in Article 243G — remain unevenly implemented. The Fifteenth Finance Commission made a portion of rural grants conditional on Panchayats maintaining audited accounts and preparing development plans, creating a fiscal incentive for states to move toward the GPDP model, but implementation remains patchy and the underlying political economy of centralisation has not changed.

A third, emerging dimension concerns the relationship between Article 243G and new administrative arrangements such as District Development Coordination and Monitoring Committees (DISHA), Aspirational Districts programmes, and other district-level convergence mechanisms introduced by the Union government. These mechanisms place elected Members of Parliament and state politicians at the centre of district-level development coordination, often displacing or marginalising elected Panchayat bodies in the same geographic area. Critics argue that this is structurally inconsistent with the Article 243G vision, which places the Gram Panchayat — not the Member of Parliament or the district collector — at the centre of local planning and implementation. The tension between the constitutional devolutionary logic of Article 243G and the administrative centralisation logic of most Union government programmes represents the live frontier of local governance jurisprudence in India.
