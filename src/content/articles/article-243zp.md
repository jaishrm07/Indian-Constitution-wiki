officialText: |-
  243ZP. Returns.—Every co-operative society shall file returns, within six months of the close of every financial year, to the authority designated by the State Government including the following matters, namely:—

  (a) annual report of its activities;

  (b) its audited statement of accounts;

  (c) plan for surplus disposal as approved by the general body of the co-operative society;

  (d) list of amendments to the bye-laws of the co-operative society, if any;

  (e) declaration regarding date of holding of its general body meeting and conduct of elections when due; and

  (f) any other information required by the Registrar in pursuance of any of the provisions of the State Act.

## Why it matters

Mandatory annual returns serve a multi-layered governance function. First, they create a formal accountability record: the co-operative society must annually put on file the evidence that it is operating in compliance with its constitutional and statutory obligations — elections have been or will be conducted on time, the general body meeting has been held, accounts have been audited, and members have approved the surplus distribution plan. Second, they allow the Registrar and the State government to monitor co-operative governance health across the sector without conducting individual inspections of each society. Third, they provide a basis for regulatory action: a society that fails to file returns, or files returns disclosing non-compliance, provides the Registrar with a documented predicate for enforcement action.

The six-month deadline aligns with the audit and general body meeting deadlines in Articles 243ZM and 243ZN. The sequencing is logical: audit is completed within six months of year-end; the annual general body meeting is held within six months (at which audited accounts are presented and the surplus disposal plan is approved by members); and the annual return — which includes the audited accounts and the surplus disposal plan as approved by the general body — is filed within six months. In practice, all three obligations can be discharged in sequence within the six-month window.

## Content of the return — element by element

The annual report of activities gives a narrative account of what the society has done during the year — production, sales, credit deployed, services delivered. The audited accounts provide financial accountability. The surplus disposal plan — importantly, as "approved by the general body" — documents that members have actually exercised their ownership right to decide how any surplus is distributed between dividends, reserves, and social purposes. The bye-law amendment list ensures that the Registrar's records of the society's governing documents are current. The election declaration is a governance integrity checkpoint: the society must confirm that its general body meeting was held and state when elections will be (or have been) conducted. This declaration requirement makes it difficult for a society to simply not conduct elections without that non-compliance appearing in the regulatory record.

## Interaction with Articles 243ZQ (offences)

Article 243ZP interacts closely with Article 243ZQ, which specifies the offences that State law must cover. One of the enumerated offences under Article 243ZQ(a) is wilfully making a false return. The annual return framework of Article 243ZP is thus backed by a penal backstop: an officer or member of a co-operative society who knowingly files a false or misleading return — for example, by stating that elections have been conducted when they have not — commits an offence under the State co-operative law that Parliament's 97th Amendment required States to enact. This penal backstop strengthens the annual return's role as a governance accountability mechanism.

## Post-Rajendra N. Shah position

Article 243ZP uses mandatory language ("every co-operative society shall file returns") without the "the Legislature of a State may, by law" qualification used in some other Part IXB provisions. This suggests the framers intended it as a direct constitutional obligation. After the 2021 ruling, this obligation applies in full to multi-State co-operative societies under the Article 243ZR modification. For State co-operatives, the constitutional compulsion has been removed by Rajendra N. Shah, but State co-operative acts typically contain equivalent returns requirements that operate independently of the constitutional provision.
