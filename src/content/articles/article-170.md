## Why it matters

Article 170 establishes the democratic foundation of each State Legislature by prescribing how the directly elected house is composed and how its constituency boundaries are drawn. The requirement of direct election from territorial constituencies links every member of the Assembly to a defined geographical community whose residents choose their representative. This direct mandate gives the Assembly its authority as the primary lawmaking and government-forming institution in the State, and it is why the Assembly alone (not the Council, if one exists) determines the fate of the State government through confidence votes.

The equality principle embedded in clause (2) — that constituencies must be drawn to maintain as nearly as practicable equal population-to-seat ratios across the State — is a constitutional guarantee of equal representation. A State government cannot manipulate constituency boundaries to favour incumbents or penalise opposition-held areas while maintaining formally equal numbers of seats. The Delimitation Commission process, established by Parliament under clause (3), operationalises this principle after each census. Its orders are protected from judicial challenge by Article 329, reflecting a constitutional choice to treat electoral boundary-drawing as a technical exercise insulated from political litigation.

The most significant current aspect of Article 170 is the delimitation freeze. Successive constitutional amendments — culminating in the provision that the 1971-census seat totals and 2001-census constituency boundaries shall remain in force until after the first census taken after 2026 — have created a situation in which the Assembly compositions of all States are based on demographic data that is now many decades old. This freeze was designed to protect states with better population management (largely in the south and west) from losing seats relative to high-growth northern states. But the freeze cannot last indefinitely, and its eventual end will produce a major redistribution of political weight.

The size constraints — a minimum of sixty and a maximum of five hundred members — reflect a pragmatic judgment about the range within which a legislature can function effectively. The minimum ensures that even small states like Goa and Sikkim have a meaningful deliberative body. The maximum prevents the most populous states from creating assemblies so large as to be ungovernable. Both limits are constitutional floors and ceilings rather than determinate numbers; the actual size of each Assembly is set by the Delimitation Commission within these bounds.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Direct election and size limits**

All members of a State Legislative Assembly are chosen by direct election — voters in each constituency elect a representative directly, without electoral college intermediaries. This contrasts with the Legislative Council under Article 171, where some members are elected by local bodies, some by graduate and teacher electorates, some by the Assembly itself, and some nominated by the Governor. The Assembly's directly elected character gives it democratic primacy in the constitutional scheme of the State Legislature.

The size range of sixty to five hundred members is a constitutional constraint on the Delimitation Commission. Current Assembly sizes range from Sikkim (32, below the minimum but protected by a special provision) to Uttar Pradesh (403). States are not free to set their own Assembly sizes; the number is determined by delimitation orders made under Parliamentary law.

**Clause (2) — Equal representation principle**

The requirement that the population-to-seat ratio be "as nearly as practicable" equal throughout the State means that some deviation from perfect mathematical equality is permissible where geography, administrative boundaries, or practical considerations make it unavoidable. The Delimitation Commission uses census data to calculate target populations per constituency and then draws boundaries that approximate the target while respecting physical geography and existing administrative units.

The Explanation in clause (2) defines "population" for delimitation purposes as population at the last census whose figures have been published, but provides that until the first census after 2026, this reference is to be read as the 2001 census. This is the mechanism through which the delimitation freeze operates for constituency boundaries — current boundaries are calculated on 2001 data even as the country's actual population has grown substantially in the intervening quarter-century.

**Clause (3) — Delimitation after each census**

Clause (3) requires that after each census, both the total seat numbers and the constituency boundaries must be readjusted. The readjustment is to be done by an authority and in a manner prescribed by Parliamentary law — currently the Delimitation Act and the Delimitation Commission constituted under it. The proviso in clause (3) protects sitting assemblies from disruption by providing that readjustment does not affect the sitting assembly's composition until dissolution.

The freeze proviso — that until the first census after 2026, no readjustment of the 1971-census seat totals or 2001-census constituency boundaries is required — currently suspends the mandatory delimitation obligation. When this freeze lifts, the Delimitation Commission will be required to carry out a comprehensive readjustment for all States, and that process will be one of the most consequential constitutional exercises in contemporary India.

## Key cases

**Meghraj Kothari v Delimitation Commission (1966)** — The Supreme Court upheld the wide powers of the Delimitation Commission in drawing constituency boundaries under Article 170, holding that delimitation orders are final and cannot be challenged in courts. This finality is now codified in Article 329, which bars courts from questioning the validity of delimitation proceedings. The case established that the Article 170 equality principle is enforced through the delimitation process itself, not through post-election judicial review.

**Mohd. Abdul Hafiz v Shah Nawaz Khan (1996)** — The Court confirmed that delimitation exercises must adhere to the equality of representation principle in clause (2) and examined challenges to Assembly composition arising from changes in state boundaries following a reorganisation. States carved from larger units inherit the delimitation obligations under Article 170, and any interim arrangement must move expeditiously toward full compliance with the equality principle.

**Kishanrao Shankarrao Wankhede v Chintamanrao Ramchandra Deshmukh (1960)** — An early case in which the Court examined whether constituencies drawn under the predecessor provisions complied with the population-to-seat ratio requirement. The Court held that substantial deviation from equality of representation could vitiate a delimitation, establishing that the "as nearly as practicable" standard is not merely aspirational but imposes a genuine legal obligation on the delimitation authority.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India (challenges to delimitation freeze)** — Multiple petitions have challenged successive extensions of the delimitation freeze on the ground that maintaining constituencies based on outdated census data violates the equality principle of Article 170(2). Courts have consistently held that the constitutional amendments effecting the freeze are valid exercises of Parliament's amending power, but the challenges have kept the tension between democratic equality and federal balance in the public constitutional discourse.

## Current significance

The delimitation freeze under Article 170 is among the most consequential unresolved constitutional issues in Indian politics. When the freeze lifts after the post-2026 census, states in the south and west — Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana — that have achieved significantly lower population growth than states in the north and east face the prospect of losing relative representation both in their own assembly compositions and in the Lok Sabha (which faces the same freeze under Article 81). This prospect has generated strong political resistance from southern political parties and state governments.

The Kovind Committee report (2024), which recommended constitutional amendments to facilitate simultaneous elections to Parliament and all State Assemblies, directly engaged with Article 170 as one of the provisions that would need to be addressed. Synchronising elections would require either early dissolution of some Assemblies (cutting their five-year terms) or extensions (using the emergency proviso in Article 172), and any such restructuring interacts with the delimitation schedule under Article 170.

The equal representation principle also intersects with the reservation system for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes constituencies, which are readjusted after each delimitation to reflect changes in the population distribution of reserved communities. The freeze has therefore also deferred the readjustment of reserved constituencies, maintaining a distribution based on decades-old data about SC and ST population concentrations. When delimitation eventually resumes, the reservation geography of all State Assemblies will shift alongside the general constituency boundaries.
