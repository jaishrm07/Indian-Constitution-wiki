## Why it matters

Article 106 places parliamentary remuneration entirely in Parliament's own hands. This is a deliberate constitutional choice — unlike judicial salaries, which are charged on the Consolidated Fund under Article 125 to insulate judges from executive pressure, MPs' pay is set by ordinary legislation that Parliament can revise at any time. The design reflects the principle that elected representatives should determine the terms of their own service, subject to the accountability that elections provide. In practice, the self-determination feature has generated persistent questions about whether political accountability adequately substitutes for independent scrutiny.

The contrast with judicial independence provisions is instructive. Article 125 charges judicial salaries directly on the Consolidated Fund and requires a special process to reduce them, preventing executive manipulation of judicial pay. Article 106 imposes no such protection, but equally imposes no such restriction on self-revision — Parliament can increase, decrease, or restructure its members' remuneration through ordinary legislation passed by the same people who benefit from it.

## Reading the provision

Article 106 has two operative parts. The substantive rule is that members are entitled to such salaries and allowances as Parliament from time to time determines by law — an open-ended grant of legislative power to fix remuneration with no constitutional ceiling or floor. The transitional rule preserved the allowances that applied to members of the Constituent Assembly of the Dominion of India until Parliament enacted its own law — a gap-filler quickly superseded by the Members of Parliament (Salaries and Allowances) Act 1954.

The 1954 Act has been amended numerous times to revise basic salary, daily allowances during sessions, constituency allowances, office expenses, travel entitlements, housing, medical facilities, and pension rights. Because Parliament legislates on its own remuneration, the arrangement has attracted recurring criticism that members can increase their pay without the independent scrutiny applied to civil service or judicial salary revisions.

The Act covers members of both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha equally. Pension entitlements for former MPs were introduced separately and expanded over the years, with a former member now entitled to pension even after a single term. The salary structure is supplemented by a wide range of non-salary benefits — subsidised accommodation in Delhi, heavily subsidised meals in Parliament House, free train and air travel, and office-expense allowances — many provided by executive order or House rules rather than the 1954 Act itself.

## Key cases

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)** — While primarily an election law and constitutional amendment case, the Supreme Court's examination of parliamentary accountability and the relationship between parliamentary privilege and external legal scrutiny touched on the broader question of whether Parliament's self-regulatory functions — including setting its own remuneration — are shielded from judicial oversight. The Court confirmed that courts retain jurisdiction over constitutional questions touching on Parliament's composition and the validity of parliamentary legislation, but that procedural matters internal to Parliament are protected by Article 122.

**Amarinder Singh v. Special Court (1992)** — In cases examining parliamentary immunity under Article 105, the Court drew a distinction between acts done in the exercise of a member's functions (protected by privilege) and personal financial entitlements such as salary and allowances (which arise from statute and are in principle enforceable as legal rights). This confirms that a member's right to salary under the 1954 Act is a statutory entitlement, not a matter of parliamentary privilege, and is in principle subject to ordinary legal enforcement.

**P. V. Narasimha Rao v. State (1998)** — The five-judge bench's examination of Article 105's speech and vote immunity considered at the margins whether financial benefits received by members in connection with parliamentary participation could be characterised as matters within parliamentary privilege. The Court's analysis confirmed that the salary regime under Article 106 is a statutory, not a privilege-based, entitlement.

## Current significance

The self-determination feature of Article 106 — Parliament voting on its own pay — periodically attracts public debate, particularly when salary revisions are passed quickly or without public deliberation. Several Salary and Allowances (Amendment) Acts have been passed with minimal scrutiny, raising questions about whether the Constitution should instead require an independent pay commission to recommend MP salaries, as many Commonwealth legislatures now use.

The Seventh Pay Commission and subsequent executive pay rounds for civil servants have in practice served as reference points for parliamentary salary revisions, but the link is informal and not constitutionally required. The broader package of non-salary benefits means that the headline salary figure understates the total cost to the public exchequer of each member's remuneration. Periodic Right to Information requests and parliamentary questions have illuminated the full extent of these entitlements, generating calls for comprehensive disclosure and for an independent statutory body to recommend, rather than Parliament to self-determine, the overall remuneration package.
