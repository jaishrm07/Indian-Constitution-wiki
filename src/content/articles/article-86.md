  Article 86 gives the President a discretionary but convention-governed right to address either House of Parliament or both together, and to send written messages to either House — whether about a pending Bill or on any other matter. officialText: |-
  86. Right of President to address and send messages to Houses.—(1) The President may address either House of Parliament or both Houses assembled together, and for that purpose require the attendance of members.

  (2) The President may send messages to either House of Parliament, whether with respect to a Bill then pending in Parliament or otherwise, and a House to which any message is so sent shall with all convenient despatch consider any matter required by the message to be taken into consideration.

## Why it matters

Article 86 establishes a constitutional channel of communication from the executive to Parliament that operates outside the normal legislative process. While the President does not ordinarily participate in debates or move amendments, the address and message powers give the constitutional head a formal presence in parliamentary proceedings beyond the mandatory special addresses under Article 87. The provision is structurally significant because it reflects the Constitution's design of the President as a component of Parliament under Article 79 — not merely an approver of its output but an institution with a right to speak to it directly.

The distinction between Article 86 and Article 87 is important and frequently overlooked. Article 87 is mandatory — the President must address both Houses at the opening of the first session after a general election and at the opening of the first session of each year. Article 86, by contrast, is discretionary. The President "may" address either House separately or both together, and "may" send messages. This discretionary character means Article 86 addresses are neither routine nor constitutionally compelled; they represent a deliberate choice to use a constitutional instrument for a specific communicative purpose. In practice this means Article 86 addresses are rare, while the message power under clause (2) has found limited but identifiable use in specific legislative contexts.

The article also carries significance as a marker of the President's role within the constitutional schema of parliamentary government. The framers of the Constitution drew heavily on the Westminster model, in which the Crown retains a formal right to communicate with Parliament. Article 86's address and message powers are the Indian Constitution's adaptation of that convention — translated from a hereditary monarchy to an elected republican head, and disciplined by Article 74's requirement that the President act on Cabinet advice. The result is a power that belongs constitutionally to the President but is exercised politically by the Cabinet, with the constitutional form giving the communication a weight and solemnity that an ordinary government statement would not carry.

The provision gains particular relevance in periods of friction between a President who holds independent political convictions and a government that controls the parliamentary agenda. In such moments, the question of whether the President can initiate an Article 86 address without specific Cabinet advice — or whether the Cabinet can instruct the President to deliver an address on a topic of its choosing — becomes a live constitutional question touching on the limits of Article 74 and the degree of discretion the President retains in constitutional-head functions.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) is the broader power — the President may address either House separately or both together in joint assembly, and may require members to attend for that purpose. The phrase "require the attendance of members" is constitutional language for a formal summons. It reflects the dignity of a presidential address rather than a coercive attendance mechanism: the Constitution provides no specific sanction against a member who does not attend such a sitting, and the more plausible consequence of non-attendance would be a matter of parliamentary discipline under the Rules of Procedure rather than a constitutional penalty. The clause does not restrict the President to addressing both Houses jointly — unlike Article 87, which specifies an address to "both Houses assembled together," Article 86(1) explicitly permits addressing either House alone, making it a more flexible instrument.

Clause (2) is the message power — more targeted and operational than the address. A message under clause (2) may concern a pending Bill (for instance, drawing Parliament's attention to a constitutional concern about pending legislation, or signalling the executive's interest in a particular amendment) or "any other matter." The phrase "any other matter" is broad enough to encompass communications on policy, public interest issues, or matters of legislative urgency that the executive wishes to formally register with Parliament outside the normal ministerial channels. The obligation that a House "shall with all convenient despatch consider any matter required by the message to be taken into consideration" imposes a mild but real constitutional duty. A House that simply ignores a Presidential message without deliberation would be in arguable breach of the article's terms, though no court has been asked to enforce this obligation directly.

Both powers operate within the Article 74 framework. All executive action of the President — including communications to Parliament — is understood to be exercised on the advice of the Council of Ministers. This means the address and message powers are, in the ordinary run of constitutional practice, instruments of government communication rather than independent presidential expression. The constitutional significance of this constraint is that it simultaneously disciplines the use of these powers and raises the constitutional temperature when a President chooses to communicate something to Parliament that the Cabinet has not formally advised.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — The nine-judge bench elaborated on the constitutional relationship between the President, the Council of Ministers, and Parliament. The Court reinforced that presidential communications to Parliament under provisions like Article 86 operate within a framework of parliamentary executive accountability — the President communicates to Parliament as part of a system in which the elected executive is accountable through Parliament, not one in which the President acts as an independent competing authority. The judgment is the most important source of constitutional doctrine on when the President acts on advice and when residual discretion might exist.

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006)** — In the Bihar dissolution case, the Supreme Court's wide-ranging examination of presidential action and constitutional conventions addressed when the President acts on advice versus when residual discretion is available. While the case turned principally on Article 356, its reasoning provides the governing framework for understanding that Article 86 powers are within the Article 74 discipline — the President communicates what the Cabinet advises, and a President who refuses to communicate what the Cabinet has formally tendered as advice would be acting outside constitutional bounds.

**Presidential messages on Bills — Legislative practice** — Though not litigated, the practice of the President sending messages under Article 86(2) about pending Bills has arisen on several occasions when the President's Secretariat has communicated concerns about legislation. The most constitutionally debated variant involves Bills that have already been passed by Parliament and are awaiting presidential assent under Article 111. Whether the President can use the Article 86(2) message power to signal concerns to Parliament about such a Bill — rather than formally returning it under Article 111 — remains constitutionally unresolved. If permissible, it would give the President an informal influence mechanism beyond what Article 111 explicitly provides; if not permissible, it would risk turning the message power into a device to circumvent the constitutional procedure for assent.

**Conventions under the Westminster model** — The Indian practice draws on the broader convention derived from the British constitutional tradition, where the Crown's right to communicate with Parliament has existed for centuries. The Indian framers explicitly chose to retain this structure in Article 86, but subjected it to the republican constraints of Articles 74 and 79. The convention's content — that presidential addresses and messages should not be used to campaign against the government's own policy — has developed through practice rather than adjudication.

## Current significance

Article 86 has generated limited direct litigation but is constitutionally significant at the margins of presidential-government relations. In periods of constitutional tension — when a President publicly signals disagreement with government policy, or when a government seeks to use the presidential address to Parliament as a platform for a contested political message — the question of whether the President may refuse to address Parliament or decline to send a message requested by the Cabinet arises as a matter of constitutional convention rather than enforceable law. The answer most constitutional commentators give is that the President cannot constitutionally refuse to act on Cabinet advice in a matter like this, but the political cost of compelling a reluctant President to address Parliament may deter a government from pressing the point.

The article also provides the constitutional basis for a practice that has occasionally been used but is not well understood: the President sending a message about a pending Bill that the President has already received for assent. Whether this is a permissible mechanism for the President to flag concerns without formally returning a Bill under Article 111's return procedure remains contested. Article 111 provides a structured procedure for the President to return a Bill with a message requesting reconsideration; using Article 86(2) to send a message about the same Bill before exercising or declining to exercise the Article 111 power introduces a constitutional ambiguity about the scope and sequencing of presidential powers over legislation.

The rarity of Article 86 addresses — as distinct from the mandatory Article 87 addresses — means that when the President does exercise this power, it carries disproportionate constitutional and political weight. A presidential address to a single House on a specific subject would be an extraordinary constitutional event in contemporary Indian practice, signalling a level of executive-legislative engagement that the ordinary parliamentary calendar does not provide for. This rarity makes Article 86 a provision whose significance is better understood by its potential and its limits than by its routine exercise.
