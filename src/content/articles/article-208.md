## Why it matters

A legislature that cannot control its own procedure is not fully independent. The power to set the rules of business — how long debates run, when questions are asked, what business has priority, how committees are formed and empowered, what counts as notice and what as default — is fundamental to the legislature's institutional character. If these rules were set by the executive or by the courts, the legislature would be subordinate in its own proceedings. Article 208 prevents this by vesting the rule-making power in each House itself.

This self-governance principle is not absolute — Article 208's rules must be made "subject to the provisions of this Constitution," meaning they cannot override fundamental rights, anti-defection provisions, or other constitutional requirements. But within that outer boundary, the House has plenary authority to organise its proceedings as it sees fit. The courts' primary role is to enforce the constitutional boundary, not to supervise procedural choices within it.

The practical significance of Article 208 is immense. The Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business of a State Legislature run to hundreds of rules covering every aspect of legislative life: introduction of Bills, the three-reading process, committee referral, question formats, time allocation, adjournments, privilege motions, no-confidence procedures, and the conduct of elections by the House. These rules shape the daily functioning of the legislature far more than any individual constitutional provision. Poorly drafted or inadequately updated rules can obstruct effective scrutiny; well-designed rules can enable genuine accountability. The power under Article 208 to make and amend these rules is therefore a key institutional resource for legislatures that wish to strengthen their oversight and accountability functions.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the rule-making power**: Each House "may make rules for regulating, subject to the provisions of this Constitution, its procedure and the conduct of its business." Three features are significant. First, "may make" — the power is permissive, not obligatory (though the transitional default in clause (2) fills the gap until rules are made). Second, "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" — this is the outer limit, not a general subordination to any superior authority. The rules must not violate constitutional provisions, but within that limit the House has full discretion. Third, "its procedure and the conduct of its business" — the scope is broad, covering all aspects of how the House organises and conducts proceedings.

**Clause (2) — transitional provincial rules**: Until a House makes its own rules, the pre-constitutional rules of the corresponding provincial legislature apply — adapted by the Speaker or Chairman as needed. This provision ensured that no legislative proceedings were disrupted in the early years after the Constitution came into force, when the new State Legislatures needed time to draft their own comprehensive rules. Most State Legislatures made their own rules relatively quickly, but the transitional provision remains operative for any House that has not yet done so.

**Clause (3) — inter-House communication rules in bicameral States**: In States with a Legislative Council, the two Houses must communicate about Bills, messages, and other legislative business. The Governor may make rules for this inter-House procedure after consulting both the Speaker and the Chairman. The consultation requirement prevents the Governor from acting unilaterally in designing communication procedures that affect both Houses. These inter-House communication rules become particularly significant when there is disagreement between the two Houses about a Bill, or when the Council delays or rejects Assembly legislation.

**"Subject to the Constitution" — the constitutional boundary**: The phrase "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" is not merely hortatory. Rules made under Article 208 cannot override the anti-defection provisions of the Tenth Schedule, cannot curtail the fundamental rights of members or the public, cannot circumvent the constitutional requirements for Money Bills or Appropriation Bills, and cannot deprive the courts of jurisdiction to review constitutionally impermissible legislative action. Within these limits, however, rules made under Article 208 have constitutional grounding and courts will not readily substitute their procedural judgments for those of the House.

## Key cases

**Special Reference No 1 of 1964 (Keshav Singh Reference) AIR 1965 SC 745** — In this landmark presidential reference, the Supreme Court affirmed the constitutional status of legislative rules of procedure made under the predecessor provision of Article 208 and confirmed that legislative bodies possess broad powers over their own procedure. The Court held that while the legislature's procedural powers are wide, they operate within the constitutional framework — rules cannot override fundamental rights or constitutional provisions. The judgment is the foundational authority on the constitutional status and limits of legislative procedure rules.

**Raja Ram Pal v Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007) 3 SCC 184** — Decided under Article 118 (the Union counterpart of Article 208), the Supreme Court comprehensively restated the principle that legislative rules of procedure have constitutional grounding and that courts exercise jurisdiction over legislative procedural action only at the outer constitutional margins. The Court confirmed that judicial review is available where a legislature's procedural action violates a specific constitutional provision, transgresses fundamental rights, or amounts to a patent constitutional impropriety — but not merely because the procedure chosen is unusual or contentious.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court reviewed procedural actions taken under the Arunachal Pradesh Assembly's Rules of Procedure and held that the Speaker's exercise of procedural authority — including the scheduling of notice for a no-confidence motion against the Deputy Speaker — is subject to judicial review when it transgresses constitutional limits, in this case the anti-defection provisions of the Tenth Schedule. The judgment confirmed that Article 208 rules cannot be used as procedural cover for constitutionally impermissible actions.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023) 2 SCC 73** — The five-judge bench's review of the Maharashtra political crisis included examination of how the rules of procedure of the Maharashtra Assembly governed the conduct of the no-confidence proceedings and the floor test. The Court's analysis confirmed that procedural rules under Article 208 must operate consistently with constitutional requirements for fair and transparent House proceedings, and that manipulation of procedure to produce a constitutionally impermissible outcome is itself justiciable.

**Bimolangshu Roy v State of Assam (2017)** — The Gauhati High Court addressed a challenge to the Assam Legislative Assembly's Rules of Procedure in the context of the Speaker's disqualification powers under the Tenth Schedule and found that Article 208 rules cannot confer on the Speaker procedural powers that are inconsistent with the constitutional scheme of the Tenth Schedule. The judgment illustrates how the "subject to the Constitution" qualification in Article 208 operates to limit procedural rules that would otherwise extend institutional power beyond constitutional bounds.

## Current significance

The rules of procedure made under Article 208 are being tested by contemporary pressures on State legislative functioning. Several concerns have been raised by legislators, civil society organisations, and parliamentary reform bodies. First, question hour — the mechanism through which members can hold the executive accountable on specific issues — is frequently curtailed by restrictions in State legislative rules that limit the number of questions listed, the frequency of starred questions that require oral answers, and the supplementary questions that may be asked. Second, the committee system through which detailed legislative scrutiny is conducted varies enormously in effectiveness across States, and the rules governing committee referral of Bills, evidence gathering, and reporting are inconsistent in their rigour. Third, the rules governing Private Members' Business — the constitutional right of non-Cabinet members to introduce and debate their own legislation — are often structured to ensure that government business always takes priority and that private member legislation rarely progresses.

Reform of the rules of procedure under Article 208 is therefore a practical mechanism through which State Legislatures can strengthen their accountability functions without constitutional amendment. Expanding question hour, strengthening committee referral requirements, creating dedicated slots for opposition business, and modernising the procedural rules for electronic voting and virtual attendance are all within the rule-making power of each House under Article 208.

In bicameral States, the inter-House communication rules made by the Governor under clause (3) have become relevant as State governments have sometimes attempted to use the Council (whose composition may differ from the Assembly's political arithmetic) to delay or modify legislation. The rules governing the communication process between the two Houses shape how such conflicts are managed procedurally, and their adequacy is part of the broader question of whether bicameral State Legislatures are functioning effectively.
