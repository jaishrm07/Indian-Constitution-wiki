## Why it matters

Article 75 converts the abstract principle that India is a parliamentary democracy into a concrete operational framework. It specifies who appoints the Prime Minister, how other Ministers are appointed, how long Ministers serve, what the constitutional foundation of collective Cabinet responsibility is, and what size the Cabinet may reach. Together these provisions form the skeletal framework of the Union executive's day-to-day functioning. Nothing in the Constitution is more directly felt in ordinary governance — every executive decision, every Cabinet resolution, every vote of no-confidence flows through Article 75.

The most important single provision is clause (3): "The Council of Ministers shall be collectively responsible to the House of the People." Collective responsibility has two inter-related dimensions. First, accountability — the government must resign if it loses the confidence of the Lok Sabha, whether through a formal no-confidence vote, the defeat of a Money Bill, or the sustained collapse of majority support. Second, solidarity — individual Ministers must publicly support and defend Cabinet decisions regardless of private disagreement; a Minister who cannot maintain that solidarity must resign. This convention, elevated to a constitutional obligation, prevents the executive from fracturing between Ministers with competing electoral mandates or ideological commitments. Coalition governments particularly depend on collective responsibility to present a united front while managing internal disagreements.

The 91st Amendment's insertion of clauses (1A) and (1B) addressed two specific pathologies of coalition-era politics that had emerged by the early 2000s. Cabinet bloat — Prime Ministers awarding ministerial posts as political currency to keep coalition partners satisfied — had produced Cabinets of 70-80 Ministers in some States. The anti-defection bar closed a logical gap in the Tenth Schedule by ensuring that a disqualified defector could not continue exercising executive power even after being stripped of legislative membership.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — appointment of Prime Minister.** The text says only that "the Prime Minister shall be appointed by the President." It does not specify who the President must appoint. The constitutional convention that supplies the missing criterion — appoint the person most likely to command the confidence of the Lok Sabha — has been consistently observed since 1947 but is nowhere stated in the text. When a party or pre-election alliance commands a clear majority, the President's discretion is nominal; the constitutional convention leaves no real choice. Where no clear majority exists, the President has genuine, if constrained, discretion: the discretion to assess parliamentary arithmetic and invite the person most capable of demonstrating majority support within a reasonable time. This is not a free-ranging personal discretion — it is a discretion bounded by parliamentary logic.

**Clause (1) — appointment of other Ministers.** Other Ministers are appointed by the President "on the advice of the Prime Minister." This is an unqualified obligation — the President appoints whoever the Prime Minister advises. The President cannot refuse to appoint a Minister recommended by the Prime Minister (except perhaps in the most extreme cases, such as a nominee against whom criminal proceedings have been framed — see B.R. Kapur below — but even this is disputed). Portfolio allocation, the size of the Cabinet below the 15 per cent ceiling, and the relative seniority of Cabinet Ministers are all matters of Prime Ministerial prerogative, not Presidential discretion.

**Clauses (1A) and (1B) — 91st Amendment additions.** Clause (1A) caps the total Council of Ministers — including the Prime Minister — at 15 per cent of total Lok Sabha membership. With the Lok Sabha at 543 elected members, this permits a maximum of approximately 81 Ministers (including the Prime Minister). Clause (1B) extends the Tenth Schedule's anti-defection disqualification to ministerial eligibility. A member disqualified under paragraph 2 of the Tenth Schedule (for voluntarily giving up party membership or voting against party direction) is also disqualified from ministerial appointment for the remainder of the Parliament's term, or until the member is re-elected after contesting a fresh election, whichever is earlier.

**Clause (2) — the pleasure doctrine.** "Ministers shall hold office during the pleasure of the President" is the formal text. On its face it gives the President unlimited power to dismiss any Minister at will. In constitutional practice, the President exercises this pleasure only on the Prime Minister's advice. The President dismisses individual Ministers when the PM recommends it; the President dismisses the entire Council when the PM advises dissolution or when the government has lost Lok Sabha confidence. A President who unilaterally dismissed a Minister contrary to the Prime Minister's advice would be acting contrary to Article 74's binding advice structure — since the advice of how to run the Cabinet belongs to the Prime Minister, not to the President's independent judgment.

**Clause (3) — collective responsibility.** "Collectively responsible to the House of the People" means the Cabinet must maintain the confidence of the Lok Sabha as a body. It does not mean each Minister is individually responsible to the House — individual ministerial responsibility is a separate constitutional convention. Collective responsibility has been judicially confirmed as a constitutional norm, not merely a political convention, meaning that a government that has demonstrably lost Lok Sabha confidence has a constitutional obligation to resign or seek a fresh mandate. The Speaker's role in no-confidence motions and the President's role in dissolving the Lok Sabha are both conditioned by the operation of this clause.

**Clause (4) — oath of office and secrecy.** The oath of secrecy taken before assumption of ministerial office has constitutional implications for Article 78's information duties. A Minister is bound by constitutional oath not to disclose Cabinet deliberations — this is the counterpart of Article 74(2)'s non-justiciability rule and the basis on which Cabinet documents are ordinarily protected from disclosure under public records legislation, subject to court orders.

**Clause (5) — six-month non-membership rule.** The rule is mandatory and self-executing. A Minister who is not a member of either House for any period of six consecutive months automatically ceases to be a Minister at the end of that period. No formal order is needed. The rule creates a limited window for the Prime Minister to appoint technocrats, former civil servants, economists, or other specialists to the Cabinet — but requires them to enter Parliament by winning a Lok Sabha by-election, winning a Rajya Sabha seat (by election from State legislatures), or being nominated to the Rajya Sabha by the President. Many prominent Finance Ministers and Defence Ministers have been appointed under this window and have subsequently been elected to the Rajya Sabha.

**Clause (6) — ministerial salaries.** The second schedule sets default salaries, with Parliament empowered to vary them. This is a lesser-litigated provision but important because it prevents the government from rewarding politically loyal Ministers with higher salaries outside parliamentary appropriation — the quantum is always Parliament's determination.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debates on Article 75 centred primarily on the appointment of the Prime Minister and the nature of collective responsibility. Dr. Ambedkar confirmed that the appointment clause — while formally leaving the choice to the President — was to be read in light of the parliamentary convention requiring appointment of the majority leader. He explicitly rejected the view that the President had any meaningful personal discretion in this appointment except in the exceptional hung-parliament case.

On collective responsibility, K.M. Munshi and Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar argued that the provision should clarify the distinction between collective and individual ministerial responsibility. Ambedkar's response was that collective responsibility to the Lok Sabha was the constitutionally mandated minimum — that a government lacking Lok Sabha confidence could not continue — but that individual responsibility was a convention of parliamentary practice that did not require constitutional text to be enforceable.

The pleasure doctrine in clause (2) was debated in light of earlier constitutions that had given heads of state stronger removal powers. Ambedkar confirmed that "during the pleasure of the President" was to be read as "during the pleasure of the Cabinet" in practical terms — the President would dismiss Ministers only on Cabinet advice, not independently.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — The Constitution Bench comprehensively analysed Articles 74 and 75 together and held that the President exercises the pleasure doctrine in clause (2) only on ministerial advice. The judgment confirmed that India's Union executive is parliamentary in the fullest Westminster sense — the President appoints and dismisses Ministers as a constitutional act, but the substance of those acts is always determined by the Prime Minister's advice. The case involved a challenge to the Governor's dismissal of a probationary civil servant, but its analysis of the President-Council relationship under Articles 74-75 remains the foundational authority.

**U.N.R. Rao v. Indira Gandhi (1971) 2 SCR 63** — The Supreme Court confirmed that dissolution of the Lok Sabha does not terminate the Council of Ministers' constitutional status. The caretaker Ministry continues under Article 75 until a new Council is formed, because Article 74 requires a Council to exist at all times. Collective responsibility in clause (3) continues in caretaker form, but the convention is that a caretaker government avoids major policy commitments or controversial appointments pending the formation of a new government with fresh Lok Sabha confidence.

**Rajendra Singh Rana v. Swami Prasad Maurya (2007) 4 SCC 270** — The Supreme Court directly addressed the operation of clause (1B) and held that disqualification under the Tenth Schedule takes effect from the moment the disqualifying event occurs, not from the date of the Speaker's formal order. The case was decided in a State context (under Article 164's equivalent provisions) but the reasoning applies equally to clause (1B) of Article 75 at the Union level. A member who has crossed the floor is constitutionally ineligible for ministerial appointment even before the Speaker has formally adjudicated the disqualification.

**B.R. Kapur v. State of Tamil Nadu (2001) 7 SCC 231** — The Supreme Court held that a person against whom a court has framed charges of a type that would attract disqualification under the Representation of the People Act, 1951 is constitutionally ineligible for appointment as Chief Minister (under Article 164). Although decided in the State context, the Court's constitutional reasoning — that ministerial eligibility has implicit constitutional limits beyond the express textual requirements — applies equally to Article 75 Central ministerial appointments. The case remains contested in its breadth, and subsequent decisions have not uniformly followed it.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 2002 (Gujarat Assembly case) (2002) 8 SCC 237** — The Supreme Court's advisory opinion on the dissolution of the Gujarat Legislative Assembly and the subsequent election raised questions about the legitimate scope of the caretaker Ministry's authority under Article 75(3)'s collective responsibility requirement. The Court held that a government that has recommended dissolution is in caretaker status and its collective responsibility obligation limits — though does not eliminate — its ability to take major governmental decisions pending the election outcome.

**Anil Kumar Jha v. Union of India (1982)** — The Patna High Court held that the six-month non-membership rule in clause (5) is mandatory and self-executing, operating automatically at the end of the prescribed period without any need for a formal notification from the President or the Prime Minister. The automatic operation mirrors the similar mandatory language in other constitutional provisions on parliamentary qualification and disqualification.

## Current significance

The 15 per cent ceiling in clause (1A) has repeatedly created political arithmetic problems in coalition governments. When a coalition government includes six or seven distinct parties, distributing Cabinet positions within the ceiling while maintaining majority support requires Prime Ministerial skill. The 2004-2014 UPA governments and subsequent NDA governments both navigated this by creating a tiered Cabinet — full Cabinet Ministers, Ministers of State with independent charge, and Ministers of State without independent charge — with differing levels of policy authority and political weight. The ceiling has arguably increased the strategic value of full Cabinet membership as a reward for coalition loyalty, without eliminating the underlying dynamic of coalition management through ministerial appointments.

The constitutional question that most urgently remains unresolved is the precise moment at which a government defeated on a no-confidence vote legally ceases to have authority under Article 75(3). The convention is that collective responsibility requires immediate resignation, but the government controls the executive machinery during the transition. Can a defeated government make major appointments, promulgate ordinances, or enter into international commitments during the period between the no-confidence vote and the new government's oath of office? The constitutional answer — implicit in Article 74's structure and clause (3)'s requirement — is that a caretaker government acts as a custodian, not as a policy-making executive, but this constraint is conventional rather than legally enforceable in real time.

The OCI and NRI diaspora dimension of Articles 73-75 has grown with the expansion of India's overseas community. The question of whether persons holding OCI cards who return to India and are elected to the Lok Sabha as citizens can be appointed as Ministers — and whether the resumption of Indian citizenship by OCI cardholders is fully effective for ministerial eligibility purposes — has not been the subject of direct judicial scrutiny but has been raised in academic commentary as the overseas community's political participation grows.
