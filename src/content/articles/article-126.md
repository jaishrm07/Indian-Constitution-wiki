## What this Article does

Article 126 provides a constitutional mechanism to ensure continuity in the administration of the Supreme Court. Whenever the office of the Chief Justice of India is vacant — through death, retirement, resignation, or removal — or whenever the Chief Justice is temporarily unable to perform duties by reason of absence, illness, or any other cause, the President may appoint one of the other Judges of the Court to perform the duties of the office. The article does not specify which judge must be appointed, leaving the choice formally with the President, though by long-established convention the senior-most available judge is designated.

The provision is deliberately broad in its trigger — "absence or otherwise" — ensuring that no administrative lacuna can arise from an unexpected incapacity. Because the Chief Justice exercises administrative functions (constituting benches, listing matters, chairing the collegium for appointments) as well as judicial ones, the continuity guarantee in Article 126 is indispensable to the Court's day-to-day functioning.

## Vacancy versus temporary inability

Article 126 covers two distinct situations with different causes and durations:

**Vacancy:** The office is formally unoccupied — the previous Chief Justice has retired, died, resigned, or been removed, and a new one has not yet been sworn in. During this period, which is typically short, the acting Chief Justice exercises all the administrative and judicial functions of the office, including collegium functions.

**Temporary inability:** The incumbent Chief Justice is still in office but is temporarily unable to perform duties — due to illness, absence on official travel abroad, or any other cause. The acting designation lasts only as long as the inability continues.

The constitutional wording "or otherwise" in the second limb is intentionally open-ended, ensuring coverage of any reason for inability without requiring prior categorisation.

## The seniority convention

Although Article 126 vests the appointment discretion in the President, the consistent practice since the Constitution came into force has been to designate the senior-most puisne judge — the judge next in seniority to the Chief Justice — as acting Chief Justice. This practice has never been departed from and has hardened into a constitutional convention of the first order. Its effect is to remove the President's formal discretion in substance, converting what the text frames as a presidential choice into an automatic succession rule similar to that operating in the collegium for substantive Chief Justice appointments.

The convention protects judicial independence by preventing the executive from passing over the senior-most judge and designating a more junior judge who might be more amenable to executive preferences. Any departure from the seniority convention would be constitutionally controversial and would immediately raise the spectre of executive manipulation of the judicial hierarchy.

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (1993):** The Second Judges case confirmed that the office of the Chief Justice carries unique institutional responsibilities that make uninterrupted discharge of those duties a constitutional necessity. The collegium mechanism and the seniority norm in appointments flow from the same concern: preventing executive interference in the judicial hierarchy. Article 126's operation in practice reflects the same logic applied to temporary vacancies.

**In re: Appointment of Acting Chief Justice (constitutional convention):** There has been no litigated challenge to the seniority convention under Article 126 — the convention is so firmly established that it has never been tested. This itself is constitutionally significant: a convention this durable and universally observed functions as a constitutional rule even in the absence of formal legal enforceability.

## Whether an acting CJI can exercise collegium powers

A question that has arisen in academic and institutional discussion — though not yet decided by the Court — is whether an acting Chief Justice appointed under Article 126 can exercise all the collegium powers of the substantive Chief Justice, including initiating recommendations for judicial appointments to the Supreme Court or High Courts. The prevailing view, consistent with the broad wording of Article 126 ("the duties of the office shall be performed"), is that the acting Chief Justice is vested with the full suite of duties, not just routine administrative functions. However, in practice, major collegium decisions tend to be deferred until the substantive Chief Justice returns or a new one is sworn in, a convention that acknowledges the difference between acting and substantive authority even where the constitutional text does not draw the line.

## Current significance

Article 126 is invoked whenever the incumbent Chief Justice is on leave, abroad on official visits, hospitalised, or during the gap between a retiring Chief Justice and the swearing-in of a successor. In a court that lists hundreds of matters daily across multiple benches, even brief administrative discontinuity could disrupt urgent proceedings. The article's significance was underlined in discussions about bench constitution during collegium deliberations in 2023, as any absence of the Chief Justice raised quorum questions for collegium meetings. The provision also intersects with debates about whether an acting Chief Justice can participate in collegium deliberations as a full member or only in a caretaker capacity, a question that the NJAC judgment's emphasis on collegium primacy makes particularly acute.
