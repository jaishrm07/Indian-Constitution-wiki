  250. Power of Parliament to legislate with respect to any matter in the State List if a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation.—(1)

  Notwithstanding anything in this Chapter, Parliament shall, while a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation, have power to make laws for the whole or any part of the territory of India with respect to [goods and services tax provided under article 246A or] any of the matters enumerated in the State List.

  (2) A law made by Parliament which Parliament would not but for the issue of a Proclamation of Emergency have been competent to make shall, to the extent of the incompetency, cease to have effect on the expiration of a period of six months after the Proclamation has ceased to operate, except as respects things done or omitted to be done before the expiration of the said period.

## Why it matters

Article 250 is the most sweeping of all the Union override mechanisms in Part XI. Unlike Article 249, which requires a formal Rajya Sabha resolution supported by two-thirds of members, Article 250 is automatic and total: the moment a National Emergency under Article 352 is proclaimed, Parliament acquires the power to legislate on every State List subject simultaneously, without any further procedural step. It reflects the constitutional logic that during a genuine national crisis the federal division of powers must yield to unified national governance.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) uses "notwithstanding anything in this Chapter" to signal that all ordinary limits on Parliament's legislative competence — Articles 246, 247, and the Seventh Schedule allocation — are overridden while the Emergency is in force. The 101st Amendment added "goods and services tax provided under article 246A" to clause (1), meaning even the cooperative GST framework can be centralised under Emergency authority.

Clause (2) provides the sunset mechanism: a law made under Article 250 power lapses six months after the Emergency ceases to operate, except for things already done or omitted under it. This saves completed legal relationships and prevents retrospective disruption while ensuring that the emergency-era expansion of Parliament's power does not outlast the emergency itself.

## The Emergency-period legislative record

India has invoked Article 352 three times: in 1962 (Chinese aggression), 1971 (Bangladesh war), and 1975–77 (internal Emergency). The 1975–77 Emergency produced the most consequential use of Article 250-type authority. Parliament enacted legislation on subjects such as industrial relations, land ceiling implementation, and preventive detention schemes that encroached deeply into State subjects. Several of these laws had lasting institutional effects even after they lapsed under the six-month saving period, because State governments chose to re-enact them or because the legal relationships created under them continued.

The Forty-second Amendment of 1976, enacted during the Emergency, also amended the Constitution itself to shift several subjects from the State List to the Concurrent List — a more permanent expansion of Union power that did not depend on Article 250 continuing in operation.

## Key cases

**Makhan Singh Tarsikka v. State of Punjab AIR 1964 SC 381** — The Supreme Court considered the validity of detention orders made under legislation enacted during an earlier Emergency when the Emergency was subsequently revoked. The Court upheld acts done within the six-month saving period, giving practical content to Article 250(2)'s transitional rule and confirming that rights and obligations accrued under emergency-period laws survive the Emergency itself.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — While this case primarily addressed Article 356, the Court's analysis of federal relations during Emergency confirmed that Articles 250 and 353 together produce a near-unitary governance structure during a proclaimed Emergency, with States' ordinary legislative autonomy substantially suspended. The Court treated this as a deliberate constitutional choice for crisis periods rather than a defect in federal design.

**Bhut Nath Mate v. State of West Bengal AIR 1974 SC 806** — The Court examined laws that continued in force after the saving period and held that a State Legislature cannot be treated as having ratified or adopted a Parliament-made Emergency-period law merely by inaction; positive State legislative action is required for the subject matter to be governed by State law again after the six months elapse.

## How courts use it

Courts apply a two-stage analysis for Article 250 legislation: first, whether a valid Emergency proclamation was in force when the law was enacted; second, whether the law falls within the State List subjects opened up by the proclamation. The substantive content of the Emergency proclamation itself is subject to judicial review after S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994), which confirmed that Presidential satisfaction must rest on relevant material. An Emergency struck down after the fact would retroactively affect Parliament's competence to have made laws under Article 250 during that period.

## Current significance

India's last National Emergency ended in 1977, and no Article 352 proclamation has been made since. Contemporary debates about internal security legislation, disaster management laws, and pandemic-response frameworks have revived questions about whether Parliament might claim Article 250-type authority through non-Emergency legal routes, or whether ordinary List I entries on defence and public order suffice. The interaction of Article 250 with Article 246A means that even GST administration could theoretically be centralised during an Emergency — a concern flagged in fiscal federalism scholarship as a potential lever for bypassing the cooperative consensus built into the GST regime.
