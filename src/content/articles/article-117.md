## Why it matters

The Indian Constitution creates a three-tier classification for legislation with financial implications. Pure Money Bills under Article 110 effectively bypass Rajya Sabha. Ordinary Bills under Article 107 go through both Houses with full co-equal status. Article 117 creates the intermediate tier: financial Bills that contain provisions drawn from the Article 110 list but are not exclusively confined to those matters. This middle category preserves some executive control over fiscal proposals — through the Presidential recommendation requirement — while ensuring Rajya Sabha retains full co-legislative status.

The practical importance of Article 117 has grown as governments have sought to classify legislation as Money Bills rather than financial Bills, because the Money Bill route gives the executive a guaranteed legislative path when it lacks a Rajya Sabha majority. If Article 117's intermediate tier is properly maintained, legislation that mixes financial and non-financial provisions must go to Rajya Sabha for genuine scrutiny.

The Presidential recommendation requirement in Article 117 also serves a separation of powers function: it ensures that only the executive — acting through the President on Cabinet advice — can initiate legislation that imposes new taxes, alters borrowing authority, or commits the Consolidated Fund to new purposes. Private members cannot by themselves bring such legislation to the floor without the executive's endorsement. This reflects the constitutional design that fiscal policy is an executive prerogative which Parliament scrutinises and approves, rather than a legislative initiative that the executive must implement regardless of its fiscal consequences.

Understanding Article 117 requires reading it alongside Article 109 (Money Bills), Article 110 (Money Bill definition), and Article 113 (demands for grants). Together, these provisions define the architecture of financial legislation — determining which chamber originates which type of Bill, what role the President must play, and how much co-equal bicameral scrutiny applies to each category.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Category A financial Bills:** A Bill that makes provision for any of the matters in Article 110(1)(a) to (f) — the same categories that define a Money Bill — is a Category A financial Bill if it also contains provisions on matters outside those categories (because if it contained "only" provisions on those matters it would be a Money Bill). Two special rules apply:
- (i) such Bills cannot be introduced in Rajya Sabha; and
- (ii) a Presidential recommendation is required before the Bill is introduced or an amendment to it is moved.

However — critically — Rajya Sabha can amend these Bills and must pass them as a co-equal legislator. The Article 108 joint-sitting mechanism applies if the two Houses disagree.

The proviso to clause (1) carves out one important exception: a member may move an amendment to reduce or abolish any tax without needing a Presidential recommendation. This preserves parliamentary freedom to propose tax cuts even without executive support.

**Clause (2) — what is excluded from the definition:** The same exclusions that appear in Article 110(2) apply here: fines, licence fees, local authority taxes, and service fees do not make a Bill a Category A financial Bill merely because they have a financial element. This prevents routine regulatory legislation from being treated as a financial Bill.

**Clause (3) — Category B financial Bills:** A broader second category covers Bills that, if enacted and brought into operation, would involve expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of India — even if those Bills do not deal with any Article 110 matter at all. A social welfare scheme, a public sector expansion, or a regulatory programme that imposes costs on the Consolidated Fund falls here. These Bills may originate in either House, but neither House may pass them without a Presidential recommendation having been made to that House. The Presidential recommendation is to "consider" the Bill — it is not a blank cheque of approval.

**The Presidential recommendation — substance or formality?** Courts have consistently held that the Presidential recommendation under Article 117 is a mandatory constitutional precondition, not a procedural formality. A Bill passed without the requisite recommendation is constitutionally invalid. The recommendation does not, however, bind Parliament: it is a condition for Parliament to consider the Bill, not an instruction to pass it.

**Difference between clause (1) and clause (3) recommendations:**

| Feature | Clause (1) — Category A Bills | Clause (3) — Expenditure Bills |
|---|---|---|
| Covers | Bills making provision for Art. 110(a)–(f) matters | Bills that would involve Consolidated Fund expenditure |
| Origin | Only Lok Sabha | Either House |
| Recommendation | Required before introduction or amendment | Required before passage in either House |
| Rajya Sabha role | Full co-legislator | Full co-legislator |

## Key cases

**Mangalore Ganesh Beedi Works v. Union of India (1974):** The Supreme Court examined what qualifies as a Bill "making provision for" Article 110 matters under Article 117(1). The Court held that the test is substantive: whether the financial provisions are the primary subject matter of the Bill or merely incidental to a non-financial purpose. Courts look to the Bill as a whole, not to individual clauses in isolation.

**Hingir-Rampur Coal Co. Ltd. v. State of Orissa (1961):** A foundational case on Presidential recommendation for financial Bills (under the analogous State provision), in which the Supreme Court held that the recommendation is a mandatory constitutional precondition. A Bill or amendment moved without the requisite recommendation is void regardless of the substance of the measure, and the defect cannot be cured retrospectively.

**State of Bihar v. Kameshwar Singh (1952):** An early Supreme Court authority establishing that Presidential or Gubernatorial recommendations for financial legislation are substantive constitutional conditions, not ministerial formalities. A Bill passed without the recommendation lacks constitutional validity even if its content is otherwise within the legislature's competence.

## Current significance

The collapse of the Article 117 intermediate tier into the Money Bill route has been the defining controversy of recent Indian parliamentary practice. When governments classify Bills with significant non-financial provisions as Money Bills by relying on Article 110(1)(g)'s incidental-matters gateway, they simultaneously render Article 117's intermediate category irrelevant — because the Bill that should have been a Category A financial Bill (going to Rajya Sabha with full amendment rights) is instead treated as a Money Bill (going to Rajya Sabha for recommendations only). The pending seven-judge bench reference in Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019) will determine whether the Article 117 intermediate tier retains constitutional vitality, and what test distinguishes a Bill that genuinely belongs in the Money Bill category from one that should go through the fuller bicameral process that Article 117 preserves.

The clause (3) Presidential recommendation requirement for expenditure Bills has occasionally been used by opposition parties to challenge legislation passed without the recommendation or passed before the recommendation was communicated to one of the Houses. These challenges have generally failed on the merits — courts have found that the recommendation was in fact given — but the cases confirm that the requirement is justiciable and that a legislative record that does not clearly show the President's recommendation was given before passage could, in principle, be fatal to the legislation's validity.

The distinction between a Bill that "makes provision for" Article 110 matters and a Bill that merely "involves expenditure from the Consolidated Fund" under clause (3) is also constitutionally significant for private member's Bills. A private member's Bill that proposes a new social programme — creating an entitlement to state support — falls under clause (3) and cannot be passed without a Presidential recommendation. This effectively gives the executive a veto over private legislative initiatives that would create fiscal obligations, even when such Bills enjoy majority support in both Houses.
