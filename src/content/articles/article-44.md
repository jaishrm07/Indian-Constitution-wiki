## Why it matters

Article 44 directs the State to work towards a uniform civil code covering marriage, divorce, maintenance, succession, adoption, and inheritance for all citizens regardless of religion. It represents the Constitution's aspiration to overcome the fragmentation of personal law along religious lines — a fragmentation that, in the framers' view, produced discriminatory outcomes for women, entrenched communal identity over citizenship, and prevented the development of a common national legal culture.

The provision was placed in Part IV rather than Part III deliberately. The Constituent Assembly debates show that Dr. B.R. Ambedkar and others who favoured a uniform code recognised that its immediate implementation was politically impossible given the composition of the Assembly and the sensitivity of the subject. Placing it in the Directive Principles was a compromise: it preserved the constitutional aspiration without making it immediately justiciable. This compromise has defined the UCC debate ever since — the directive is real and binding on the State as a policy direction, but its enforcement pathway runs through Parliament and State legislatures, not the courts.

The fundamental tension that Article 44 generates is between two constitutional values: the aspiration for a common citizenship expressed through uniform law, and the religious and cultural autonomy guaranteed to all communities under Articles 25 and 26. The Constitution simultaneously permits religious communities to manage their own affairs according to their own religious law and directs the State to eventually create a uniform law for everyone. Courts have consistently refused to resolve this tension by ordering implementation — recognising that the judgment about timing, scope, and form is a political one — but have used it repeatedly to express frustration with legislative inaction and to signal that the directive imposes a real constitutional obligation.

## Reading the provision

The text is brief: the State "shall endeavour" to secure a uniform civil code "throughout the territory of India." The word "endeavour" is the softest formulation in Part IV, softer even than "shall strive" in Article 38 or "shall take steps" in Article 40. This reflects the political sensitivity of the subject: the framers wanted to record the aspiration without tying any particular government to a fixed timetable. But the Supreme Court has consistently treated "endeavour" as imposing a genuine obligation rather than a purely aspirational wish — an obligation that requires the State to demonstrate progress, even if it does not permit courts to mandate a particular legislative outcome.

"Throughout the territory of India" emphasises uniformity: the code must apply to all citizens in all parts of the country. This has implications for Goa, which has a common civil code inherited from the Portuguese civil administration — occasionally cited as proof that a UCC is constitutionally feasible and politically manageable. It also has implications for personal law systems operating in different northeastern states under constitutionally protected customary law regimes, raising the question of whether a UCC could be applied uniformly without impinging on constitutional protections for tribal customs under Articles 371 and the Sixth Schedule.

A UCC would constitutionally require Parliament to legislate a single set of rules on marriage (including validity, registration, grounds for dissolution), divorce (grounds, procedures, maintenance), succession (intestate and testamentary), adoption, and inheritance. Such a code would supersede the Hindu Marriage Act, Muslim Personal Law Application Act, Parsi Marriage and Divorce Act, Indian Christian Marriage Act, and the personal law-based rules that currently govern each community. The constitutional difficulty is whether such legislation can be designed in ways that respect Articles 25 and 26's protections for religious practice and denominational autonomy while eliminating discriminatory outcomes — particularly for women — within each personal law system.

The gender-equality argument for a UCC is particularly powerful. Critics of the current personal law regime argue that it allows, and in some cases requires, women to be treated as legally inferior to men in matters of marriage, divorce, and inheritance — and that this discrimination is given constitutional shelter by the personal law exception. Article 44 is read in this tradition as an equality provision: the State's duty to work toward uniformity is driven by the constitutional demand that citizens receive equal treatment before the law regardless of the accident of their religious community. This reading makes the UCC debate not merely a question of secularism but a question of whether women across communities are entitled to equal rights in personal law.

The judicial treatment of Article 44 has also grappled with the question of personal choice. If a citizen freely chooses to be governed by their religious personal law, does the State's Article 44 obligation extend to overriding that choice? Courts have not resolved this question, but the direction of constitutional argument — particularly in cases involving forced marriage, unequal inheritance, and discriminatory dissolution rules — suggests that the Article 44 mandate is increasingly understood as a demand for minimum equality standards within personal law, not merely an aspiration toward a single uniform code. This interpretation allows progressive personal law reform within each community's framework to count as partial compliance with the Article 44 directive.

## Key cases

**Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum (1985)** — The most consequential personal law case in Indian constitutional history and the defining moment for the modern UCC debate. The Supreme Court held that a divorced Muslim woman was entitled to maintenance under Section 125 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, which the Court held applied to all citizens regardless of religion. Chief Justice Chandrachud's majority opinion made extensive observations urging the enactment of a uniform civil code under Article 44, stating that a common civil code would help the cause of national integration. The political reaction was immediate: the Muslim community mobilised against the judgment, the Congress government under Rajiv Gandhi reversed the decision through the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act 1986, and the UCC became a flashpoint in communal politics for a generation. The political aftermath illustrated the precise tension Article 44 embodies — between the constitutional aspiration for uniformity and the political reality of minority community resistance.

**Sarla Mudgal v. Union of India (1995)** — The Court addressed the practice of Hindu men converting to Islam for the purpose of contracting a second marriage, circumventing the Hindu Marriage Act's prohibition on bigamy. Justice Kuldip Singh, writing for the bench, held that a Hindu husband who converts to Islam to contract a bigamous second marriage remains guilty of bigamy under the Indian Penal Code. The judgment included a ringing call for Parliament to enact a uniform civil code, the Court directing that its order be forwarded to the Prime Minister and Law Minister. The judgment provoked a fresh controversy about judicial overreach into legislative domain and the relationship between the directive principle and minority religious autonomy, and was revisited in Lily Thomas v. Union of India (2000) which upheld the bigamy conviction while distancing the Court from any order directing the legislature to enact the UCC.

**John Vallamattom v. Union of India (2003)** — Striking down a provision of the Indian Succession Act that imposed disproportionate restrictions on Christians making charitable bequests, the Court again invoked Article 44, expressing regret that Parliament had not yet moved toward a uniform code and that differential treatment of citizens in personal law matters on the basis of religion was inconsistent with constitutional values. The case illustrated how Article 44 continues to function as a standard against which discriminatory personal law provisions are measured, even when courts cannot directly enforce uniformity.

## Current significance

The adoption of a Uniform Civil Code by Uttarakhand in 2024 made it the first state to enact such a code, and the Law Commission of India's consultations on the subject have renewed national debate about the pace and form of implementation. The constitutional tension between Article 44 and minority rights under Articles 25, 26, and 29 remains intensely contested. Muslim, Christian, and other communities continue to argue that their personal law regimes fall within the protected sphere of religious denomination autonomy, and that a coercive uniformity would itself violate the Constitution's commitment to plurality.

Constitutional scholars debate whether a UCC, to be constitutionally valid, must be genuinely neutral — equally transforming all religious personal laws — or whether selective reform that targets only Muslim personal law (as some political actors have proposed) would itself violate Article 14's equality guarantee. The 21st Law Commission in its 2018 consultation paper took the view that a UCC is neither necessary nor desirable at this stage, and that reform of personal laws within each community's framework is a more constitutionally appropriate path. The 22nd Law Commission, constituted in 2022, re-opened consultations and took a different approach, but its final recommendations remained contested.

The question of whether the Constituent Assembly's placement of Article 44 in Part IV rather than Part III was a principled choice about the relationship between cultural pluralism and civic uniformity, or merely a political compromise to be overcome, remains the deepest constitutional argument surrounding the provision. Proponents of a UCC argue that equality demands uniformity: citizens should not have different rights in marriage, divorce, and inheritance on the basis of religion. Opponents argue that the Constitution's plural framework, which permits personal law diversity as an expression of religious and cultural identity, is itself a constitutional value of the same order as formal equality.

The Supreme Court in Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017) struck down the practice of instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) as unconstitutional, a decision followed by the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019 criminalising the practice. This targeted reform to Muslim personal law, enacted without a comprehensive UCC framework, illustrates the incremental path to personal law reform that has characterised Indian legislative practice — and raises the question whether targeted legislative intervention is a constitutionally sufficient discharge of the Article 44 directive.

The constitutional debate about the UCC has also been shaped by the question of who should enact it. Article 246 read with Entry 5 of the Concurrent List gives both Parliament and State legislatures jurisdiction over "marriage and divorce, infants and minors, adoption, wills, intestacy and succession." Uttarakhand's 2024 UCC legislation demonstrates that State governments can enact personal law codes under this entry, opening the possibility of a patchwork of State-level codes rather than a single national code. Whether such State-level variation satisfies the Article 44 mandate for a uniform code "throughout the territory of India" is a question that courts have not yet addressed directly but that will inevitably arise as the Uttarakhand code is challenged and as other states consider similar legislation.

The personal law reform debates of the 2020s have also raised the question of what "uniform" means in Article 44. Some scholars argue that uniformity requires identical rules for all citizens regardless of religion, gender, or custom. Others argue that a code can be "uniform" in the sense of being universally applicable while incorporating graduated rules that account for cultural diversity — much as secular personal law in France and other civil law jurisdictions includes provisions for diversity within a single legislative framework. This interpretive question has profound implications: a broader definition of "uniform" would allow a code that retains community-specific provisions (provided they do not discriminate) to satisfy the Article 44 directive, reducing the political costs of implementation significantly.

The Supreme Court's consistent observation that the UCC directive imposes a real obligation on the State — without ordering its implementation — reflects a constitutional posture of moral pressure without legal mandate. In Jose Paulo Coutinho v. Maria Luiza Valentina Pereira (2019), the Court again lamented Parliament's inaction on the UCC, continuing a decades-long pattern of judicial exhortation without enforcement. Some scholars have argued that this pattern itself represents a constitutional failure: a court that acknowledges a constitutional obligation but refuses to enforce it over decades risks reducing the directive principle from a binding constitutional norm to a rhetorical device — a risk that Article 37's instruction that directive principles are "fundamental in the governance of the country" is designed to prevent.

The question of the UCC and tribal communities deserves separate attention. The Fifth and Sixth Schedules protect tribal customary law and governance in scheduled areas. A UCC applied to tribal communities without their free, prior, and informed consent would potentially conflict with these constitutional protections and with the Panchayat (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act's recognition of tribal customary governance. The most recent Uttarakhand UCC contains exemptions for tribal communities, reflecting the constitutional constraint that Article 44's uniformity directive operates within — it cannot be implemented in ways that override the Constitution's parallel commitments to tribal autonomy and customary law protection.

Article 44 ultimately asks the deepest question about the kind of constitutional community India is: a community of citizens governed by common secular law in their private lives, or a plural community of communities, each entitled to live by its own personal law with equal constitutional recognition. The tension between these two visions is not resolvable by the directive alone; it requires a political and legislative judgment that the Constitution has deliberately left open, placing the directive in Part IV rather than Part III, and leaving its implementation to democratic deliberation. The UCC debate is thus not merely a legal question about personal law uniformity; it is a constitutional debate about the meaning of citizenship, equality, and pluralism in a diverse democracy.
