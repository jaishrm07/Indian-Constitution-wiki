## Why it matters

The geographic location of the apex court matters for access to justice in a country of India's size and diversity. A litigant who cannot afford to travel to Delhi to pursue a constitutional right is practically denied that right, regardless of what the Constitution says. Article 130's significance lies precisely in the gap between what it permits — regional sittings of the Supreme Court at any place the Chief Justice and President agree — and what has actually occurred, which is the consistent concentration of all Supreme Court activity in a single building in New Delhi.

The access-to-justice problem created by this concentration is not trivial. India has an area of over 3.2 million square kilometres. The distance from Chennai to Delhi is approximately 2,200 km; from Guwahati, approximately 2,300 km; from Kochi, approximately 2,900 km. For litigants from these regions — who must appear in the Supreme Court in person for oral hearings, urgent matters, or cases involving voluminous documentary evidence — the cost of travel and accommodation in Delhi adds substantially to the burden of litigation. Senior advocates who appear regularly in the Supreme Court are themselves concentrated in Delhi, creating a further structural disadvantage for parties who cannot afford to instruct Delhi-based counsel.

Article 130's continuing constitutional significance is therefore not as a provision that has been much litigated — it has generated relatively little case law — but as the provision through which the most significant unrealised access-to-justice reform in India's judicial architecture could be implemented, without constitutional amendment, by the simple act of the Chief Justice and the President agreeing to designate additional places of sitting.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 130 is short but its structure rewards careful reading.

**"The Supreme Court shall sit in Delhi":** Delhi is the default seat of the Court — not merely one option among equals. The Court must sit in Delhi unless the Chief Justice, with Presidential approval, designates an additional place. The provision does not authorise the Court to move its seat away from Delhi entirely; it authorises the designation of additional places. The Delhi seat is permanent; any other designated place is in addition to, not in substitution for, Delhi.

**"Or in such other place or places":** The plural "places" confirms that the Chief Justice can designate multiple additional locations, not just one. There is no constitutional limit on the number of additional sitting places that could be designated — the Chief Justice and President could, in theory, designate bench locations in every major regional centre. The choice of which places to designate, and how many, is entirely within the Chief Justice's institutional discretion.

**"As the Chief Justice of India may, with the approval of the President, from time to time, appoint":** Three elements are embedded here. First, the discretion is the Chief Justice's — the President's role is to approve, not to initiate or override. Second, "with the approval of the President" (meaning the Cabinet under Article 74) ensures that any designation is a joint institutional decision with the executive's concurrence, preventing unilateral judicial restructuring of the Court's sitting arrangements. Third, "from time to time" makes the power a continuing one: the Chief Justice could designate a location for a specific case or hearing, for a periodic sitting schedule, or for a permanent regional bench, and could later modify or revoke the designation.

**Non-justiciability:** The Chief Justice's decision on where to sit is an institutional judgment — an exercise of the Chief Justice's powers in relation to the administration of the Supreme Court — rather than an administrative function subject to mandamus. Petitions seeking a court direction to the Chief Justice to exercise Article 130 power have been dismissed consistently, on the ground that this is a matter of internal judicial administration not amenable to judicial review.

## The access-to-justice problem

The geographic concentration of the Supreme Court in Delhi has three distinct adverse effects on access to justice.

**The cost barrier:** Pursuing a Supreme Court matter requires the services of an advocate enrolled with the Supreme Court. The vast majority of Supreme Court advocates practise from chambers in Delhi. A party from Tamil Nadu or Assam who needs to engage senior counsel — for a constitutional challenge, a significant civil appeal, or an Article 32 writ — must either retain a Delhi-based lawyer (whom they may meet only once or twice before hearing) or make repeated trips to Delhi to instruct local counsel. In either case, the cost premium on distance is substantial.

**The information barrier:** Litigants in remote States have limited access to information about Supreme Court filing requirements, practice directions, and procedural norms. The Bar Associations of High Courts in southern and north-eastern States have consistently reported that their members have difficulty navigating the Supreme Court's filing requirements without a permanent Delhi connection, and that the cost of establishing such a connection makes meaningful Supreme Court practice impossible for all but the largest law firms.

**The doctrinal barrier:** Because Supreme Court practitioners are disproportionately concentrated in Delhi, and because Delhi-based advocates are better positioned to build relationships with senior colleagues, the doctrinal development of Indian constitutional law has been disproportionately shaped by a Delhi-centric litigation culture. This is not merely an access problem for individual litigants — it affects the range of constitutional arguments that are made and considered, and therefore the constitutional jurisprudence that emerges.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debated the location of the Supreme Court at some length. Several southern members, led by T.T. Krishnamachari and others from Madras, advocated for rotating benches or multiple seats from the outset, arguing that concentration in Delhi would make the Court effectively inaccessible to large parts of the population. The counterargument — which prevailed — was that a single Supreme Court at the national capital would develop more consistent jurisprudence, that the new Court needed to establish itself as a national institution from one central location, and that the Chief Justice's power to appoint additional places of sitting was a sufficient flexibility mechanism. The eventual compromise — Delhi as default with the CJI-and-President power to add other places — was presented to the Assembly as a practical solution that left the door open to regional sittings without mandating them.

B.R. Ambedkar, who piloted the Draft Constitution through the Assembly, expressed sympathy with the access-to-justice concern but emphasised the importance of the Supreme Court developing a unified and consistent national jurisprudence, which he considered most likely if the Court sat as a single institution in one place. The debate anticipated the precise tension — judicial unity versus judicial access — that has structured the regional bench controversy ever since.

## Law Commission recommendations

The Law Commission of India has recommended the establishment of regional benches of the Supreme Court on at least four occasions, each time concluding that Article 130 provides sufficient constitutional authority without amendment.

The 229th Law Commission Report (2009) on "Need for Division Benches of the Supreme Court at Regional Level" is the most detailed examination. The Commission proposed four regional sittings in addition to Delhi — at Chennai or Hyderabad (for the southern region), at Mumbai (for the western region), at Kolkata (for the eastern region), and at Delhi retaining the constitutional and original jurisdiction. Each regional sitting would exercise appellate jurisdiction for High Courts in its region, with the Delhi bench retaining the constitutional bench, Article 32 original jurisdiction, and special leave jurisdiction involving the most significant constitutional questions.

The Commission's analysis concluded that no constitutional amendment was required — only exercise of Article 130 power by the Chief Justice with Presidential approval. The report noted that the Chief Justice's power to "from time to time appoint" additional places was expressly designed for this purpose by the framers. No government or Chief Justice has acted on this recommendation in the fifteen years since it was made.

The 95th Law Commission Report (1984) had made an earlier recommendation for circuit benches. The 120th Report (1987) examined the feasibility of regional sittings in detail. The persistence of these recommendations — and their consistent non-implementation — suggests that the obstacle to regional benches is institutional will, not constitutional authority.

## Key cases

**Writ Petition (C) No. 217 of 2014 and related regional bench petitions** — Petitions filed before the Supreme Court seeking a direction that the Chief Justice exercise the Article 130 power to establish regional benches were dismissed. The Court affirmed that the decision to establish additional seats of the Court is an institutional matter within the Chief Justice's exclusive discretion and is not justiciable by mandamus. The Chief Justice is not subject to a writ of mandamus in relation to a power that is expressly permissive ("may appoint") and is an internal judicial administration matter.

**Supreme Court of India v. Subhash Chandra Agarwal (2020) 5 SCC 481** — In the context of transparency and institutional access, the Constitution Bench noted that the physical seat of the apex court affects both access to information and to justice itself, implicitly acknowledging that the Delhi-centric model under Article 130 has systemic access-to-justice implications. While the case was about RTI applicability to the Supreme Court, the Court's discussion of its own institutional nature addressed the relationship between physical accessibility and constitutional accountability.

**State of West Bengal v. Committee for Protection of Democratic Rights (2010) 3 SCC 571** — The Court's discussion of its institutional authority and supervisory jurisdiction affirmed that the Supreme Court is a unitary institution — its jurisdictional reach extends to all of India regardless of where it physically sits. This principle addresses the concern that regional benches could fragment the law: the Court confirmed that it functions as a single judicial institution even if it sits in multiple places, and that judgments given at any designated location would be binding across India.

## Virtual hearings and partial mitigation

The expansion of video-conferencing infrastructure from 2020 onwards — initially as an emergency response to the COVID-19 pandemic — has partially mitigated the access problem created by Article 130's default Delhi siting. The Supreme Court's e-court initiatives allow litigants and advocates in distant States to appear remotely in many proceedings. The Court has made video-conferencing facilities available at High Court premises across India, and most routine appeals, admission matters, and miscellaneous applications can now be argued by advocates appearing from their home jurisdictions.

However, virtual hearings do not fully substitute for physical attendance in several categories of matter. Constitutional bench hearings of the highest importance, cases involving live documentary evidence, and urgent matters where physical presence gives advocates better ability to respond to bench observations — all of these still typically require or strongly favour physical presence in Delhi. The virtual hearing infrastructure has reduced the pressure for regional benches without eliminating the underlying access-to-justice concern, and some critics argue that the availability of virtual hearings has become an institutional rationale for continuing to defer the regional bench reform.

## Current significance

The Article 130 debate reflects a deeper tension between judicial unity — a single court sitting in Delhi enforces uniform national law and develops consistent constitutional doctrine — and judicial access — litigants everywhere should have practical access to the apex court at manageable cost. Successive Chief Justices have come down firmly on the side of judicial unity, with the practical argument supplemented in recent years by the point that virtual hearings provide an alternative access mechanism that earlier generations did not have.

The constitutional provision, however, has not been rendered obsolete by virtual hearings. For States that feel their constitutional interests are under-represented in a Delhi-centric judicial culture — States from the south and north-east whose lawyers, litigants, and legal traditions are less visible at the Supreme Court — Article 130 remains the provision through which the most significant structural reform of access to the apex court could be implemented. The provision requires only the institutional will of the Chief Justice and the assent of the President. No statute, no constitutional amendment, and no parliamentary approval is needed. The persistence of the regional bench proposal — through four Law Commission reports, multiple Bar Association petitions, and decades of advocacy — is itself evidence that Article 130's potential has not been exhausted by the current practice of permanent Delhi siting.
