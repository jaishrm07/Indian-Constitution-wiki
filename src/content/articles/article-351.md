## Why it matters

Article 351 is the final provision of Part XVII and, in many ways, the most politically charged. It is the constitutional authority that Hindi promoters invoke when pressing for mandatory Hindi in competitive examinations, central government offices, IIT/IIM campuses, and national broadcasting. It is also the provision that non-Hindi States contest most forcefully: every political demand for expanding Hindi's footprint in national life finds its constitutional hook in Article 351, and every rejection of that demand is grounded in the argument that Article 351's directive runs to the Union alone and cannot impose Hindi on States, institutions, or individuals who do not choose it.

The article is also a constitutional statement about what kind of Hindi India was choosing to promote. The reference to "Hindustani" — the colloquial, Persianised-inflected spoken form — alongside "other languages specified in the Eighth Schedule" as enrichment sources, and the prescription to draw vocabulary "primarily on Sanskrit and secondarily on other languages," reflects a deliberate attempt to describe a Hindi that would be culturally inclusive rather than narrowly north-Indian or exclusively Sanskrit-derived. This vision of composite Hindi was the price non-Hindi members paid for accepting Hindi's constitutional elevation: they extracted a commitment that the Hindi being promoted would be the Hindi of the streets and of Urdu's literary tradition, not the Sanskritised bureaucratic Hindi that many Hindi nationalists actually wanted.

Whether this constitutional vision has been honoured in practice is sharply contested. Critics argue that the Hindi promotion institutions funded under Article 351 have produced an increasingly Sanskritised administrative Hindi that is further from the Hindustani of millions of native speakers than it is from the composite vision the Constitution describes. Supporters argue that developing Hindi as a full-spectrum national language necessarily involves building a formal register that draws on Sanskrit's terminological precision for technical and legal usage.

## Reading the provision

The directive is addressed to "the Union" — not to States, not to private institutions, not to individuals. This limitation is constitutionally significant. The Union may promote the spread of Hindi, but it cannot use Article 351 to require States to make Hindi their medium of administration, to require private employers to use Hindi in the workplace, or to require minority educational institutions to adopt Hindi as their medium of instruction. The directive's subject is the Union's own conduct and the institutions the Union funds and controls.

The phrase "promote the spread of the Hindi language" encompasses Union-funded Hindi teaching programmes, All India Radio and Doordarshan content in Hindi, promotional campaigns for Hindi literacy, and financial support for Hindi publishing and literary organisations. "Develop it so that it may serve as a medium of expression for all the elements of the composite culture of India" is the transformative mandate: Hindi is not merely to be preserved but to be developed into a language capable of expressing the full range of India's cultural, scientific, and literary traditions.

"Assimilating without interfering with its genius, the forms, style and expressions used in Hindustani" — the reference to Hindustani is a direct nod to the Gandhi-era preference for a blended, accessible Hindi. Hindustani was the spoken form — Persianised, colloquial, shared between Hindu and Muslim communities — that Gandhi had championed as a potential national link language. After the Constituent Assembly's rejection of Hindustani as the constitutional official language (in favour of Sanskrit-inflected Hindi), the framers salvaged the Hindustani compromise by encoding it in Article 351: Hindi must draw on Hindustani's forms and expressions as it develops. This was a substantive constitutional constraint on the future direction of Hindi's linguistic evolution, not merely an aesthetic preference.

The vocabulary prescription — "primarily on Sanskrit and secondarily on other languages" — establishes a hierarchy but not an exclusivity: Sanskrit is the primary source, all other languages are secondary. This is the provision most contested by Urdu and regional language advocates, who argue that it effectively marginalises Perso-Arabic vocabulary that is organically part of spoken Hindi/Urdu and elevates a Sanskritic standard that is less accessible to ordinary speakers.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debate on what became Article 351 was one of the most prolonged and emotionally charged of the entire constitution-drafting exercise. Hindi nationalists, led by members from Uttar Pradesh and other Hindi-belt States, wanted an unambiguous directive for Hindi's promotion as the eventual national language, to be developed through Sanskrit vocabulary and Devanagari script. Non-Hindi members — particularly from the south, the east, and the Urdu-speaking communities of the north — resisted a formulation that would produce an exclusionary, Sanskritised Hindi far removed from the spoken language of most Hindi users.

The key battleground was the reference to Hindustani. Gandhi's preference for Hindustani as the link language of independent India had been represented in early drafts of the language provisions. After Gandhi's assassination, the Hindi nationalists gained the upper hand in pushing for Sanskrit-inflected Hindi over the composite Hindustani form. The compromise in Article 351 — mandating that Hindi must assimilate Hindustani forms and expressions — was the residue of the Hindustani position: Hindi would be the official language, but it would be constitutionally directed to remain connected to its colloquial, Urdu-influenced spoken reality.

T.T. Krishnamachari and other southern members who had fought the hardest against Hindi's elevation accepted the language provisions partly on the basis of the composite-culture formulation in Article 351. They argued that if Hindi was to be promoted, it must at minimum be promoted as a genuinely all-India cultural medium capable of expressing Tamil poetry, Bengali literature, and the philosophical traditions of the south — not as a vehicle for north-Indian cultural dominance. The "composite culture" language was their safeguard against cultural imperialism disguised as language policy.

Seth Govind Das and other Hindi nationalists, by contrast, accepted the Hindustani reference and the secondary Sanskrit caveat as acceptable concessions because they expected that in practice, the Hindi promotion institutions would develop the Sanskritic register that their scholarly community preferred. The subsequent history of Hindi's development by official institutions — producing a heavily Sanskritised bureaucratic Hindi increasingly at odds with spoken Hindustani — suggests that the nationalist expectations were more accurately fulfilled than the pluralist ones.

## The 1965 anti-Hindi agitations

The most dramatic test of the tension between Article 351 and non-Hindi linguistic identity came in January–February 1965, when the fifteen-year transitional period for English under Article 343(2) was set to expire. The expectation that Hindi would replace English as the sole official language triggered widespread and violent protests in Tamil Nadu, causing dozens of deaths and prompting Prime Minister Lal Bahadur Shastri to give assurances — subsequently enacted in the Official Languages Amendment Act 1967 — that English would continue as an associate official language alongside Hindi as long as non-Hindi States wished.

The 1965 events permanently shaped the political space within which Article 351 operates: any aggressive implementation of the Hindi promotion directive risks triggering the kind of linguistic nationalism in non-Hindi States that the Constituent Assembly's compromise was designed to manage. Tamil Nadu's formal two-language policy (Tamil and English only, with no Hindi in State government) remains the most visible State-level assertion of the limits of Article 351, and it is constitutionally grounded precisely in the argument that Article 351's directive runs to the Union alone. The State of Tamil Nadu has consistently maintained that it bears no constitutional obligation under Article 351 and that the Union's duty to promote Hindi does not translate into any obligation on non-Hindi States to accommodate that promotion in their administrative or educational systems.

The 1965 agitations were also a turning point in the interpretation of the "composite culture" mandate in Article 351. Non-Hindi members argued that a Hindi that could serve as the medium of expression for "all the elements of the composite culture of India" would need to be accessible and welcoming to Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, and Malayalam cultural expressions — and that the agitations demonstrated that the Hindi then being promoted was experienced by south Indians as alien, not composite. This critique of Article 351's implementation became a recurring theme in the subsequent decades' language controversies.

## Key cases

**T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The Supreme Court's discussion of the Union's language policy acknowledged that Article 351's promotion of Hindi must coexist with the constitutional recognition of India's linguistic plurality. The directive cannot override fundamental rights of linguistic minorities under Articles 29 and 30, and it cannot be used to subordinate regional languages constitutionally recognised in the Eighth Schedule.

**Government of Andhra Pradesh v. J.B. Educational Society (2005)** — The Court held that Article 351 justifies Union programmes and grants for Hindi teaching but does not impose an obligation on State governments or private educational institutions to adopt Hindi as a medium of instruction. The directive runs to the Union; it does not extend the Union's power over States or private entities in the language domain.

**Aruna Roy v. Union of India (2002)** — A Constitution Bench confirmed that the Article 351 directive must be read alongside, and balanced against, the other language provisions of Part XVII. It does not allow the Union to marginalise regional languages or subordinate them to Hindi; the composite-culture formulation implies that all of India's linguistic traditions contribute to the culture Hindi is meant to express.

**Rajbhasha Samiti Report challenges (various High Courts, 2000s–2020s)** — Parliamentary Committee on Official Language recommendations invoking Article 351 to justify mandatory Hindi in higher education and government employment have been challenged in High Courts on the basis that Article 351's directive to the Union does not authorise the Parliament or the executive to impose Hindi on State governments, public sector units operating in non-Hindi States, or private citizens. Courts have generally agreed that Article 351 is a directive to the Union government in respect of its own activities, not a licence for general Hindi imposition.

**Nainital Bank Employee Union v. Nainital Bank (2001)** — The Allahabad High Court considered whether a public-sector bank could require employees to use Hindi under Article 351, holding that the article is a directive to the Union government and does not by itself create enforceable obligations on public-sector undertakings to impose Hindi on employees or customers beyond what the Official Languages Act and its rules require.

## Current significance

Article 351 is the constitutional underpinning for the Kendriya Hindi Samiti (chaired by the Prime Minister), the Central Hindi Directorate, the Sahitya Akademi's Hindi work, and Hindi promotion drives in public-sector enterprises and government offices. The Parliamentary Committee on Official Language routinely invokes it when recommending stronger Hindi mandates in recruitment and education. Non-Hindi States contest these recommendations on the ground that Article 351 is a directive to the Union alone, not a limitation on their own language choices or a licence for the Union to impose Hindi on their populations.

The controversy over Hindi imposition resurfaces regularly: debates over the three-language formula in school education, mandatory Hindi content in All India Radio and Doordarshan broadcasts, Hindi-only signage in central government offices, and the use of Hindi in Parliamentary debates all invoke Article 351 on one side and federalism, linguistic pluralism, and Articles 29 and 350A on the other.

The article's vision of Hindi as a language enriched by contact with all of India's linguistic traditions — rather than a Sanskritised administrative imposition — is frequently invoked by those who argue that the constitutional directive has been misimplemented. Scholars like Ganesh Devy and politicians like M. Karunanidhi have argued that the genuine fulfilment of Article 351 would produce a more inclusive, composite Hindi that non-Hindi speakers might engage with on terms of linguistic dignity rather than subordination — a Hindi that assimilated Tamil's logical grammar, Bengali's literary sensibility, and Urdu's expressive vocabulary, as the Constitution actually directs. That vision remains largely unrealised, and the gap between the constitutional text of Article 351 and the political reality of Hindi promotion continues to be one of India's most consequential unresolved constitutional questions.
