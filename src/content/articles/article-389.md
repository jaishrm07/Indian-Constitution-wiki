## What this Article did

Article 389 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that made provision for Bills pending in the Dominion Legislatures and in the Legislatures of Provinces and Indian States. It has been omitted.

When the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950, there were Bills at various stages of passage in the pre-constitutional legislative bodies — the Dominion Legislature (Constituent Assembly sitting as Legislature), the provincial legislatures, and the legislatures of the Indian States. These Bills had been introduced, debated, and in some cases read a second time, but had not yet received presidential or royal assent and become law.

Article 389 provided rules for the fate of these pending Bills. It determined which stage in the legislative process they had reached and what would happen to them under the new constitutional framework — whether they could be continued by the new provisional legislative bodies or would need to be reintroduced.

## Why it was omitted

Article 389 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. By that time, all pending Bills from the pre-constitutional period had either been enacted, lapsed, or been reintroduced in the new legislative bodies. There was no further need for the transitional provision.

## Historical context

Legislative continuity across the constitutional transition was practically important. Laws were being made continuously, and the disruption of legislative processes would have created uncertainty. Article 389 ensured that work done in the old legislative bodies on pending legislation was not wasted, but could be carried forward appropriately in the new framework.

The provision illustrated a broader principle in Part XXI: the transition from the dominion to the republic was to be managed with maximum continuity and minimum disruption at every level of government — legislative, executive, and judicial.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge based on the status of transitional Bills under Article 389 is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 389 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in securing the continuity of the legislative process across the constitutional transition, preventing the loss of legislative work in progress at the moment of commencement.

The transition from the Dominion Legislature and provincial Legislatures to the new provisional Parliament and State Legislatures involved not just a change in constitutional status but also a restructuring of the legislative bodies themselves. Bills that had been introduced under the Dominion framework but not yet enacted represented significant legislative work. Article 389 preserved this investment by providing a mechanism for these Bills to be carried forward, contributing to the legislative output of the transitional period. The article illustrates how the framers thought carefully not just about institutional continuity but about continuity of the legislative pipeline itself.

The Government of India Act, 1935 legislative framework that governed the Dominion Legislature had its own rules about Bills, their stages, and lapsing. When the new Constitution came into force, a Bill that had passed its first and second readings under the 1935 framework but not received assent was in a legal limbo — it had been generated by a legislature that no longer existed. Article 389 resolved this limbo by prescribing rules for treating such Bills in the new framework, preventing the loss of months of parliamentary work and enabling the new provisional Parliament to take up where the Dominion Legislature had left off.
