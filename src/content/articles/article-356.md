  356. Provisions in case of failure of constitutional machinery in States.—(1) If the President, on receipt of a report from the Governor of a State or otherwise, is satisfied that a situation has arisen in which the Government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of this Constitution, the President may by Proclamation—

  (a) assume to himself all or any of the functions of the Government of the State and all or any of the powers vested in or exercisable by the Governor or any body or authority in the State other than the Legislature of the State;

  (b) declare that the powers of the Legislature of the State shall be exercisable by or under the authority of Parliament;

  (c) make such incidental and consequential provisions as appear to the President to be necessary or desirable for giving effect to the objects of the Proclamation, including provisions for suspending in whole or in part the operation of any provisions of this Constitution relating to any body or authority in the State:

  Provided that nothing in this clause shall authorise the President to assume to himself any of the powers vested in or exercisable by a High Court, or to suspend in whole or in part the operation of any provision of this Constitution relating to High Courts.

  (2) Any such Proclamation may be revoked or varied by a subsequent Proclamation.

  (3) Every Proclamation under this article shall be laid before each House of Parliament and shall, except where it is a Proclamation revoking a previous Proclamation, cease to operate at the expiration of two months unless before the expiration of that period it has been approved by resolutions of both Houses of Parliament.

  (4) A Proclamation so approved shall, unless revoked, cease to operate on the expiration of a period of six months from the date of issue of the Proclamation. Provided that if and so often as a resolution approving the continuance in force of such a Proclamation is passed by both Houses of Parliament, the Proclamation shall, unless revoked, continue in force for a further period of six months — but no such Proclamation shall in any case remain in force for more than three years.

  (5) Notwithstanding anything contained in clause (4), a resolution with respect to the continuance in force of a Proclamation approved under clause (3) for any period beyond the expiration of one year from the date of issue of such Proclamation shall not be passed by either House of Parliament unless — (a) a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation in the whole of India or in the whole or any part of the State, and (b) the Election Commission certifies that the continuance of the Proclamation is necessary on account of difficulties in holding general elections to the Legislative Assembly of the State concerned.

## Why it matters

Article 356 is the most abused emergency provision in the Indian Constitution. Dr. B. R. Ambedkar, introducing the provision in the Constituent Assembly, called it a "dead letter" that would never be used except in cases of extreme necessity — he could not anticipate that in the first forty-five years after the Constitution came into force, it would be invoked over ninety times. The provision was designed to be an instrument of last resort, used to restore constitutional government in a State that had collapsed into violence, unconstitutionality, or genuine political paralysis. In practice, it became the principal tool through which the Central Government — whichever party was in power — dismissed inconvenient State governments, destabilised opposition-controlled administrations, and reasserted Union control during political transitions.

The provision exemplifies a fundamental tension in the Indian federal design. India's constitution-makers, shaped by the experience of Partition and the challenge of integrating hundreds of princely states, created a federation that is deliberately tilted toward the Union. Article 355 imposes a duty on the Union to protect States against external aggression and internal disturbance, and to ensure that every State government is carried on in accordance with the Constitution. Article 356 is the enforcement mechanism for that duty — but it is also a loaded weapon that gives the Union the power to subjugate any State government it chooses, subject only to the safeguard of parliamentary approval. When the same party controls both the Union and Parliament, that safeguard is minimal.

The Sarkaria Commission, appointed in 1983 to review Centre-State relations, documented the systematic misuse of Article 356 and recommended strict standards that should govern its use. The Commission's report — particularly its recommendation that the Governor's report be placed before Parliament and that the floor test be used to resolve doubts about majority — profoundly influenced the Supreme Court's reasoning in SR Bommai v Union of India (1994), the landmark nine-judge bench decision that constitutionalised restraints on the use of Article 356. Post-Bommai, the article has been used more carefully; overt partisan misuse has become rarer; but disputes about Governor conduct and the conditions for imposing President's Rule continue to generate constitutional litigation.

The interaction between Article 356 and the federal principle — which the Court has held to be part of the basic structure of the Constitution — is the central constitutional problem. If Parliament could amend Article 356 to make the President's satisfaction entirely unreviewable, that would potentially destroy the federal principle (since the Centre could then dismiss any State government at will). The basic structure doctrine provides the ultimate constitutional limit: no amendment can be used to effectively destroy the States' right to democratic self-governance.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the trigger**: The President must be "satisfied that a situation has arisen in which the Government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of this Constitution." This is the operative trigger, and it was historically construed as a purely subjective standard — the President's satisfaction, not any objective constitutional breakdown. The Governor's report is one potential source of the President's satisfaction, but clause (1) says "or otherwise," meaning the President can act on other information as well (including Cabinet intelligence reports, independent assessment, or political events). The three possible consequences of the proclamation — assuming executive functions, bringing the legislature under parliamentary control, and making incidental provisions — can be deployed separately or together.

**What cannot be done**: The proviso to clause (1)(c) is a critical constitutional safeguard. The President cannot assume the powers of the High Court and cannot suspend any provision of the Constitution relating to High Courts. This ensures that the judicial check on President's Rule remains intact — the High Court continues to function, continues to exercise its ordinary jurisdiction, and continues to review executive action taken during President's Rule. This is the provision that prevented the Emergency-era model of complete judicial subjugation from being replicated at the State level through Article 356.

**Clauses (3) and (4) — parliamentary time limits**: Every Proclamation must be laid before both Houses within two months. If it is not approved, it lapses. An approved Proclamation operates for six months at a time, extendable by further six-month parliamentary resolutions, but cannot in any case remain in force for more than three years. The three-year outer limit is itself subject to a further restriction under clause (5): after the first year, continued imposition requires both a national emergency to be in operation in the State and the Election Commission's certification that elections cannot be held.

**Clause (5) — the Punjab exception**: The three-year limit and the clause (5) requirements were specifically extended for Punjab, where President's Rule was imposed in May 1987 during the height of the militancy crisis and continued until January 1992. Parliament extended the Proclamation for a total of five years and exempted it from the clause (5) requirements by a specific constitutional proviso — illustrating that even the Article 356 framework can be amended to address specific security situations, though the amendment was itself constitutionally controversial.

**The Governor's role**: Article 356 proclamations do not issue automatically when a State government loses its majority or falls into difficulties. They require a Governor's report (in most cases) and a Cabinet recommendation to the President. The Governor's decision to send a report recommending President's Rule is itself a high-stakes constitutional act, and the Governor's conduct in the period leading up to a proclamation — refusing to invite the leader of the largest party to form a government, refusing to give a government an opportunity to prove its majority, or advancing the date of the Assembly session to precipitate a crisis — has been the subject of Supreme Court scrutiny.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v Union of India (1977)** — The newly elected Janata Party government at the Centre sought to impose President's Rule on States still governed by the Congress party, relying on reports from Congress-appointed Governors. The Supreme Court by a six-to-one majority rejected the Congress State governments' challenge, holding that the President's satisfaction was primarily a political matter and that courts would not interfere with it. Only Justice Bhagwati, in dissent, held that there must be some material for the President's satisfaction that is reasonably connected to the constitutional conditions. The case illustrated the pre-Bommai era's extreme judicial deference to Article 356 proclamations, and it remains the clearest example of the provision being used for partisan reasons under the guise of constitutional concern.

**A K Roy v Union of India (1982)** — The Court confirmed that the President's satisfaction under Article 356 is not entirely beyond judicial review, though it deferred to the Government on the merits. The judgment was transitional — it acknowledged the justiciability of Article 356 proclamations while declining to articulate clear standards for reviewing them, leaving the doctrinal development for Bommai.

**S R Bommai v Union of India (1994)** — The most consequential Article 356 case, and one of the most important federalism decisions in Indian constitutional history. A nine-judge Constitution Bench held unanimously that the proclamation imposing President's Rule is subject to judicial review; that the Court can examine whether there was any material before the President capable of supporting the satisfaction that constitutional machinery had failed; that if the material is irrelevant or non-existent, the proclamation can be struck down as unconstitutional; that the President's satisfaction, though not fully justiciable, must have a rational nexus to the actual constitutional conditions in the State; and that the correctness of that satisfaction is a constitutional matter that courts will review.

The Court also held that the floor of the House is the only constitutional way to determine whether a government commands majority — the Governor cannot form an independent judgment about whether a ministry has lost its majority by counting letters or conducting his own inquiry. If there is doubt, the Governor must advise a floor test. Dismissing a government without a floor test, when a floor test was feasible, is itself a ground for setting aside the proclamation. This holding fundamentally changed the Governor's role in political crises, shifting the constitutional burden of verification back to the legislature.

The Court further held that the federal principle — the constitutional guarantee that States will have democratic self-governance — is part of the basic structure. This means that Article 356 cannot be used to destroy federalism even if Parliament amends the provision; any amendment that makes Article 356 completely unreviewable or that allows the Union to dismiss any State government for any reason would be unconstitutional.

Most dramatically, the Court indicated (though only in dicta, not as a binding ratio) that if a proclamation was found to be unconstitutional, the reinstated government's position would be revived from the date of the illegal proclamation — meaning Acts passed by Parliament during the proclamation period could be invalidated. This dictum significantly raised the consequences of an unconstitutional proclamation.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court applied Bommai and struck down the proclamation imposing President's Rule in Bihar in May 2005 as unconstitutional. Bihar's Assembly had just been elected but was hung, and the Governor had reported that he had been informed by defectors and others that the coalition majority was being manufactured through horse-trading. The Court held that the President's satisfaction based on a Governor's report that itself relied on defections and shifting loyalties — rather than on an actual floor test — was not based on relevant material. The case was the most significant post-Bommai application of the justiciability principle.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — While not an Article 356 case in its primary holding, this five-judge Constitution Bench judgment addressed the lead-up to a President's Rule proclamation. The Court held that the Governor had acted unconstitutionally by advancing the session of the Arunachal Pradesh Assembly from the scheduled date in order to facilitate the opposition's no-confidence motion against the Speaker, and that this advance summoning was itself an unconstitutional exercise of gubernatorial power. The consequent proclamation of President's Rule was made against a backdrop of unconstitutional Governor conduct. The case reinforced the principle that the Governor's actions leading to a proclamation are themselves reviewable.

**Tamil Nadu Governor case (2025)** — The Supreme Court ruled that the Governor of Tamil Nadu had unconstitutionally withheld assent to Bills passed by the State Legislature and had acted contrary to the constitutional scheme under Articles 200 and 201. While the case was primarily about Governor conduct on Bills rather than Article 356, the Court's reasoning about the Governor as a constitutional office-holder bound by the advice of the Council of Ministers — not a political actor with independent discretion — is directly relevant to the Article 356 context.

## The history of Article 356 misuse

The scale of Article 356's misuse before Bommai is staggering by any standard. Between 1950 and 1994, the provision was invoked over ninety times. The pattern was consistent: when a State government became politically inconvenient for the party at the Centre, the Governor would send an adverse report (or the Centre would act "otherwise"), and the President would issue a proclamation. The Union government would then use the President's Rule period to hold fresh elections, often at a time when they calculated their State-level fortunes would be better.

The most blatant instances came in periods of political transition: after the 1967 elections when the Congress lost several States and tried to reassert control through Article 356; during the 1975-77 Emergency when Article 356 was used against non-Congress governments as part of the Emergency apparatus; after 1977 when the Janata government dismissed Congress-held States; and after 1980 when Indira Gandhi returned to power and dismissed nine State governments in a single week.

The Sarkaria Commission catalogued this abuse and recommended that Article 356 be used only as a last resort, after the Centre has exhausted other constitutional options; that the Governor's report be required to contain specific material; that the State government be given an opportunity to explain the situation before a proclamation is issued; that the Lok Sabha not be dissolved pending the State Assembly's revival; and that the Inter-State Council be activated to provide a consultative forum for Centre-State grievances. Bommai incorporated the core of these recommendations into constitutional doctrine, and post-Bommai misuse of Article 356, while not eliminated, has been substantially reduced.

## Current significance

Article 356 remains highly contentious in contemporary constitutional politics, though its locus of controversy has shifted. Direct dismissals of elected State governments have become rarer since Bommai, because the Supreme Court has made clear it will review the constitutional basis for any proclamation. The new battlefield is Governor conduct in the period before a proclamation might be imposed — Governors refusing to invite the leader of the largest party to form a government after an election, withholding assent to Bills for months or years, summoning or refusing to summon Assembly sessions at inconvenient moments, and sending adverse reports to the Centre based on contested assessments of political situations.

Active litigation in several States involves Governor-Cabinet standoffs where the Governor has delayed action on Bills or is refusing to act on Council of Ministers' advice on various matters. The Supreme Court's 2025 ruling in the Tamil Nadu Governor case has strengthened the constitutional position that Governors must act on the advice of the Council of Ministers except in specified circumstances, and the Court has been willing to issue mandamus to Governors who refuse to act.

The interaction between Article 356 and the anti-defection provisions of the Tenth Schedule has also produced new constitutional tensions. When State governments fall following defections, the question of whether a new government can be formed or whether President's Rule should be imposed depends on the Governor's assessment of the post-defection arithmetic — and Governor assessments in these situations have themselves been challenged as partisan.

The Sarkaria Commission's recommendation that Article 356 be made significantly harder to invoke has never been implemented through constitutional amendment. Academic debate about whether the provision should be repealed entirely, or replaced with a judicial trigger mechanism, continues. The current constitutional position — post-Bommai judicial review plus political accountability — provides meaningful but imperfect restraint on the provision's abuse.
