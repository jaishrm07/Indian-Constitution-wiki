  68. Time of holding election to fill vacancy in the office of VicePresident and the term of office of person elected to fill casual vacancy.—

  (1) An election to fill a vacancy caused by the expiration of the term of office of Vice-President shall be completed before the expiration of the term.

  (2) An election to fill a vacancy in the office of Vice-President occurring by reason of his death, resignation or removal, or otherwise shall be held as soon as possible after the occurrence of the vacancy, and the person elected to fill the vacancy shall, subject to the provisions of article 67, be entitled to hold office for the full term of five years from the date on which he enters upon his office.

## Why it matters

Article 68 supplies the constitutional timing rules for Vice-Presidential elections. Its structural importance lies in ensuring that the office of Vice-President — which under Article 64 is simultaneously the ex officio chairmanship of the Rajya Sabha, and under Article 65 is the first line of presidential succession — is never left vacant for longer than constitutionally permissible. An extended vacancy in the Vice-Presidency would have dual consequences: the Rajya Sabha would be without its constitutionally designated presiding officer (relying instead on the Deputy Chairman under Article 91), and the presidential succession line would be effectively broken, forcing reliance on the contingency provisions of Article 70.

The two-clause structure of Article 68 maps onto two fundamentally different situations. Clause (1) handles the routine case: a Vice-President whose five-year term under Article 67 is about to expire. The Constitution's requirement here is unambiguous — the fresh election must be "completed before the expiration of the term." This is a hard constitutional deadline, not a target. The Election Commission is under a constitutional obligation to schedule and conduct the election so that the results are declared, the new Vice-President is elected, and the transition can happen before the sitting term ends. Proviso (c) of Article 67 provides the safety net — the incumbent holds over until the successor takes the oath — but Article 68(1) makes clear that this safety net should not be the primary plan.

Clause (2) handles casual vacancies: those caused by death, resignation, removal, or "otherwise." Here the timing standard is less precise — "as soon as possible" — but the standard is mandatory rather than aspirational. The phrase "as soon as possible" in Indian constitutional law has been consistently read as imposing a genuine obligation of expedition, not merely a good-faith aspiration. The Election Commission cannot treat a casual Vice-Presidential vacancy as a low-priority matter to be scheduled at convenience. The urgency is structural: every day without a Vice-President is a day without the constitutionally designated Rajya Sabha Chairman and a day on which the presidential succession line is incomplete.

The full-term entitlement for casual vacancy successors — a person elected to fill a casual Vice-Presidential vacancy gets a full five-year term, not merely the remainder of the predecessor's term — is a deliberate constitutional design choice. It means that the Vice-President elected after a casual vacancy will have a five-year mandate that may run significantly beyond the ordinary five-year electoral cycle, potentially outlasting Parliaments that were seated when the predecessor's term began. This design ensures that the incoming Vice-President has a full, independent constitutional mandate rather than the truncated authority of a caretaker completing someone else's term.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1)'s requirement that the election be "completed before the expiration of the term" raises the question of what "completed" means. In the context of the Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952, which governs the mechanics of the election, the election is "completed" when the returning officer declares the result. The new Vice-President is elected from that moment, though they do not "enter upon office" until taking the Article 69 oath. The election can therefore be completed within the meaning of Article 68(1) even if the new Vice-President has not yet been sworn in — and Article 67(c)'s holdover provision bridges the gap between the expiry of the old term and the new Vice-President's oath.

Clause (2)'s "as soon as possible" standard contrasts directly with the six-month maximum prescribed by Article 62(2) for presidential casual vacancies. The absence of a specific maximum duration for Vice-Presidential casual vacancy elections is notable. The most plausible explanation is that the framers regarded the Vice-Presidential office as slightly less urgent than the Presidency for electoral continuity purposes — a vacancy in the Vice-Presidency, while consequential, does not prevent the entire Union executive from functioning, whereas a prolonged presidential vacancy would require the Council of Ministers to operate without the constitutional head of the executive. The Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha can perform the chairmanship function, and the Presidential (Discharge of Functions) Act, 1969, covers the worst-case presidential succession gap. But "as soon as possible" still imposes a genuine constitutional obligation on the Election Commission.

The full-term entitlement in clause (2) is explicitly "subject to the provisions of article 67." This means the casual vacancy successor's five-year term is still subject to the same resignation, removal, and holdover provisions that govern any Vice-President. The cross-reference also confirms that the five-year term runs from the date the casual vacancy successor "enters upon his office" — the date of the Article 69 oath — not from the date of election or the date the vacancy occurred.

## Key cases

**Election Commission of India — Vice-Presidential election schedules (1997, 2002, 2007, 2012, 2017, 2022)** — The Election Commission has consistently applied Article 68(1) as a mandatory constitutional obligation, scheduling each Vice-Presidential election so that the result is declared before the sitting Vice-President's term expires. The Commission's approach treats the article's deadline as a hard constitutional limit. In each cycle since 1952, the election has been completed before the term's expiry, and Article 67(c)'s holdover clause has been used only to bridge the brief gap between the election result and the new Vice-President's oath ceremony.

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — While the case's primary focus was the nature of presidential and vice-presidential power rather than electoral timing, the Court's analysis of the constitutional indispensability of the Vice-Presidential office confirms the rationale for Article 68's strict timing regime. The Court observed that the Vice-President's dual constitutional role — Rajya Sabha Chairman and presidential successor — gives the office a significance that makes any prolonged vacancy a constitutional problem, not merely a political inconvenience.

**B.D. Jatti's acting Presidency and the 1977 Vice-Presidential election** — When Vice-President B.D. Jatti stepped up to act as President following President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed's death in February 1977, the Vice-Presidential office became vacant simultaneously under Article 65(1)'s succession mechanism. Article 68(2) was immediately triggered. The 1977 Vice-Presidential election that followed — in which B.D. Jatti himself was subsequently elected Vice-President after leaving the acting Presidency on Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy's swearing-in — provided a practical illustration of the cascade: presidential death triggered Article 65(1), which vacated the Vice-Presidency, which triggered Article 68(2), which required an expedited Vice-Presidential election, all while the Deputy Chairman presided over the Rajya Sabha under Article 91.

**Gopal Narain v Returning Officer, Vice-Presidential Election (1969)** — The Supreme Court entertained a petition challenging the 1969 Vice-Presidential election under Article 71, affirming that all doubts and disputes about Vice-Presidential elections — including challenges relating to the conduct of the election and its timing under Article 68 — fall within the Supreme Court's exclusive jurisdiction. The Court confirmed that Article 68's election-timing requirements interact with Article 71's dispute-resolution framework: a challenge to the validity of a Vice-Presidential election on grounds of timing defects would be adjudicated by the Supreme Court alone. This exclusive jurisdictional structure means there is no High Court recourse for any challenge relating to compliance with Article 68's deadlines.

**V.V. Giri's Vice-Presidential resignation and the 1969 casual vacancy** — When V.V. Giri resigned the Vice-Presidency on 3 May 1969 to contest the presidential election, Article 68(2) was triggered immediately. The Vice-Presidential office was vacant for a period while the electoral machinery was mobilised. Gopal Swarup Pathak was subsequently elected as Vice-President. Pathak's five-year term ran from the date of his oath — not from the date the vacancy occurred or from the point where Giri's original term would have ended — confirming in practice the full-fresh-term guarantee that Article 68(2) provides for casual vacancy successors.

## Current significance

Article 68 has operated smoothly in every Vice-Presidential election cycle. The Election Commission's careful scheduling has ensured that Article 68(1)'s pre-expiry completion requirement has been satisfied in each routine transition. The main constitutional significance of Article 68 today lies not in past controversies but in future risk scenarios: any situation in which the Vice-President unexpectedly vacates office — through death, resignation, removal, or stepping up to act as President under Article 65(1) — would immediately activate the "as soon as possible" obligation under Article 68(2).

The absence of a specific maximum time limit in clause (2), unlike the six-month cap in Article 62(2) for presidential casual vacancies, has attracted scholarly commentary. Some constitutional scholars have argued that the open-ended "as soon as possible" standard creates an enforcement gap: there is no provision in the Constitution or in the 1952 Act for a court to compel the Election Commission to act within a particular deadline for Vice-Presidential casual vacancies. The closest analogy is the Supreme Court's approach to electoral schedules under Article 324 — the Court has in other contexts indicated that constitutional timing obligations are justiciable — but no case has directly tested the enforceability of Article 68(2)'s "as soon as possible" standard.

The full-term entitlement in clause (2) will acquire practical significance if a casual Vice-Presidential vacancy ever occurs in the middle of a term. A successor elected under clause (2) would hold office for a full five years from their oath date, potentially straddling multiple general election cycles. This could produce a Vice-President whose term and political mandate are significantly misaligned with the parliamentary majority that currently forms the government, creating institutional tensions of the kind Article 64's ex officio chairmanship design does not always handle gracefully. Whether such misalignment would intensify or moderate the political conflicts that have characterised the Vice-President's Rajya Sabha chairmanship role in recent years remains a question for future constitutional practice.
