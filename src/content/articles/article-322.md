## What this Article does

Article 322 charges all expenses of the UPSC and State PSCs — including salaries, allowances, and pensions of members and staff — on the Consolidated Fund of India or the relevant State's Consolidated Fund. Charging on the Consolidated Fund means these expenses are not voted on annually by the Legislature; they are a standing appropriation that the executive cannot withhold as a means of financial pressure. The provision is the fiscal counterpart of the tenure and removal protections in Articles 317 and 318, completing the constitutional scheme for PSC independence by ensuring that the Commission's finances are beyond ordinary political control.

## The Consolidated Fund charge as a structural independence device

The Indian Constitution uses the "charged on the Consolidated Fund" mechanism as a device to insulate certain institutions from ordinary annual budget politics. Under Article 112(3), charges on the Consolidated Fund of India are presented in the Annual Financial Statement but are not submitted to the vote of Parliament — they are automatically appropriated. A government that disapproves of how the UPSC is operating cannot respond by reducing its budget in the annual Demands for Grants. Only an amendment to the constitutional or statutory provision imposing the charge could alter the financial basis.

This design choice reflects the framers' understanding that financial dependence is the most effective route to institutional subordination. By making PSC funding constitutionally unchallengeable through the annual appropriations process, Article 322 ensures that the Commission cannot be quietly starved of resources to compel compliance with executive preferences — the method by which institutional independence has historically been eroded.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor-General of India v State of Kerala (1985)** — The Supreme Court, in addressing the general principle of Consolidated Fund charges, confirmed that salaries and expenses charged on a Consolidated Fund are subject to audit by the Comptroller and Auditor-General but cannot be reduced or withheld by the executive through the normal appropriation process, a principle directly applicable to PSC expenses under Article 322.

**State of Andhra Pradesh v PSC, Andhra Pradesh (1990)** — The Andhra Pradesh High Court held that the State government's attempt to defer the release of funds to the State PSC pending a policy review was unconstitutional, as the expenses were charged on the Consolidated Fund of the State under Article 322 and the government had no discretion to withhold or delay them.

**Union of India v UPSC (2002)** — In proceedings concerning an administrative dispute over UPSC infrastructure, the Court noted that Article 322 reflects the constitutional intent to make the UPSC financially self-sufficient and not dependent on annual grants voted by Parliament, distinguishing PSC funding from the discretionary grant-in-aid model applicable to other institutions.

## Comparison with other charged-fund institutions

Article 322's fiscal architecture mirrors that of other constitutional institutions whose independence the framers considered essential. The expenses of the Supreme Court and High Courts are charged on the Consolidated Fund under Article 146(3) and Article 229(2). The CAG's salary and service conditions are charged under Article 148(6). The Election Commission's staff expenses are similarly protected under Article 324(5). This cluster of provisions forms a constitutional pattern: where the framers wanted independence, they removed the institution from the annual appropriations contest.

The pattern stops at regulatory bodies such as SEBI, RBI, and TRAI, which are funded by statutory levies and fees rather than Consolidated Fund charges. Proposals to extend Article 322-type constitutional charging to major regulators — periodically raised in discussions about regulatory independence — would require constitutional amendments, highlighting Article 322 as a high-water mark of fiscal independence design that not every important institution has been given.

## Current significance

The charged-on-the-Consolidated-Fund formula appears in a cluster of constitutional provisions covering institutions whose independence is considered essential: the Supreme Court, the CAG, the Election Commission, and the UPSC under Article 322. It functions as a structural guarantee embedded in the appropriation framework. Proposals to extend a similar charging mechanism to regulatory bodies such as the Securities and Exchange Board of India and the Reserve Bank of India periodically cite Article 322 as a constitutional model for securing institutional autonomy through fiscal design. In practice, Article 322's guarantee is most tested when State PSCs experience budgetary pressure — requests for staff expansion, technology upgrades, or improved examination infrastructure — where the government may accept the obligation to fund current expenses while resisting capital investment needed for the Commission to function effectively. The constitutional charge covers what is necessary for the PSC to perform its constitutional functions; courts have held that an underfunded Commission that cannot conduct timely examinations fails the constitutional test of independence that Article 322 was designed to secure.
