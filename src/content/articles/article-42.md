## Why it matters

Article 42 is the constitutional basis for the entire body of Indian labour welfare law dealing with working conditions and the protection of women workers. Its two components — just and humane conditions of work, and maternity relief — address complementary dimensions of workplace dignity. The first reflects the Constitution's insistence that labour is not a commodity and that the terms under which people work must meet a minimum standard of humanity, regardless of the formal bargain struck in the employment contract. The second reflects a specific recognition that women workers bear the biological burden of reproduction and that the State must ensure they are not disadvantaged, disciplined, or dismissed because of it.

The placement of maternity relief in the Constitution itself — rather than leaving it entirely to Parliament and state legislatures — is significant. It signals that maternity protection is not a concession from employers or a legislative favour but a constitutional obligation. This framing has shaped how courts approach attempts to exclude categories of women workers from statutory maternity benefits: such exclusions face the constitutional presumption that Article 42's mandate extends to all women in employment, not just those in the most formally protected segment of the workforce.

Article 42 operates in close relationship with Article 21. The right to work in safe and humane conditions, and the right of women workers not to be economically penalised for pregnancy, have both been read by the Supreme Court as aspects of the right to life and dignity. This connection has made Article 42 a productive source of doctrinal support for expanding the scope of labour welfare legislation in an era of increasing informal employment and contractualisation.

## Reading the provision

Courts invoke Article 42 primarily as an interpretive guide when construing labour welfare statutes. When a statute is ambiguous about whether a benefit — maternity pay, safe working conditions, occupational health facilities — applies to a particular category of worker, the Court reads the ambiguity in the direction that advances the constitutional mandate. This approach has been used to extend statutory protections to casual, contractual, and muster-roll workers who might otherwise be excluded by narrow definitions of "employee" in legislation drafted with formal employment in mind.

The directive has also been read into Article 21 to create constitutional entitlements that courts can enforce even in the absence of specific statutory provision. In cases involving bonded labour, hazardous occupations, and the health of workers in unregulated industries, the Court has treated Article 42 as establishing a constitutional floor: whatever the gaps in legislation, the State cannot permit working conditions that fall below the minimum standard of humanity that the directive requires.

In the area of maternity benefits, courts have been particularly willing to use Article 42 as an interpretive anchor to resist contractual exclusions, pro-employer interpretations, and administrative practice that effectively penalise women for taking maternity leave. The article has informed rulings about whether maternity benefit periods count for service and seniority purposes, whether contract and daily-wage workers are covered, and whether arbitrary denial of maternity leave violates the right to equality under Article 14.

## Key cases

**Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997)** — The Supreme Court relied on Article 42 alongside Articles 14, 15, and 21 to hold that sexual harassment at the workplace violates women workers' constitutional rights, including the right to work in conditions that do not subject them to degrading treatment. The judgment treated workplace dignity — the freedom from harassment and coercion — as an element of the humane conditions of work that the State is constitutionally required to secure. The Vishaka guidelines, which remained operative law until the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act, 2013, were thus partly grounded in Article 42's mandate.

**Municipal Corporation of Delhi v. Female Workers (Muster Roll) (2000)** — The Supreme Court held that women employed as daily-wage and muster-roll workers by the Municipal Corporation of Delhi were entitled to maternity benefits under the Maternity Benefit Act, 1961, notwithstanding their informal employment status. The Court rejected the argument that the Act's coverage was limited to regular employees, reading the statute in light of Article 42's constitutional command that maternity relief must extend to women in employment. The judgment directly established that informal and contractual women workers cannot be excluded from maternity protections, and is the principal authority for the proposition that Article 42 requires a broad reading of "employee" in welfare legislation.

**Consumer Education and Research Centre v. Union of India (1995)** — The Court explicitly invoked Article 42 alongside Article 21 to require asbestos and other hazardous industries to provide health and safety infrastructure for workers, including regular medical examinations, compensation for occupational disease, and safe working environments. It held that the directive for humane conditions of work becomes an enforceable constitutional standard when read through Article 21, and that employers operating under statutory licence cannot treat the directive as inapplicable to their operations. The ruling is one of the most direct uses of Article 42 to generate positive occupational health obligations.

**Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India (1984)** — In the bonded labour case, the Supreme Court held that the conditions in which bonded and migrant workers were kept — confined, unpaid or underpaid, subjected to physical coercion — constituted a violation of Articles 21, 23, and 42. Justice Bhagwati's majority opinion treated Article 42 as a constitutional benchmark for evaluating the State's omissions in labour law enforcement: the directive that working conditions must be just and humane is not discharged by legislation that is never enforced. The Court directed the State to systematically identify, release, and rehabilitate bonded labour, treating the directive as generating an obligation of active vigilance.

## Current significance

Article 42 has become increasingly relevant as the Indian workforce shifts toward contractual, gig, and platform-based employment, where workers often lack the formal employment relationships that qualify them for statutory protections. The Code on Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions (2020) and the Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act (2017) — which extended paid maternity leave to 26 weeks for certain categories of women — both draw constitutional legitimacy from Article 42, but the coverage gaps for informal workers, domestic workers, and gig workers remain significant points of constitutional concern.

Courts and labour advocates invoke Article 42 in litigation about whether platform companies' classification of workers as "independent contractors" rather than employees is consistent with constitutional welfare obligations, and whether the State's failure to extend occupational safety and maternity protections to unclassified workers violates the directive. The article also informs the ongoing debate about domestic workers — a predominantly female and overwhelmingly informal workforce — whose exclusion from comprehensive welfare legislation sits in visible tension with Article 42's mandate to secure humane conditions of work and maternity relief for all workers.
