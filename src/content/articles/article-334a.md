  334A. Reservation of seats for women to come into effect after an exercise of delimitation.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Part or Part VIII, the provisions of the Constitution relating to the reservation of seats for women in the House of the People, the Legislative Assembly of a State and the Legislative Assembly of the National Capital Territory of Delhi shall come into effect after an exercise of delimitation is undertaken for this purpose after the relevant figures for the first census taken after commencement of the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 have been published and shall cease to have effect on the expiration of a period of fifteen years from such commencement. ## What this Article does

Article 334A is the operative and temporal engine of the women's reservation system created by the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023. While Articles 330A and 332A state the substantive reservation obligations for Parliament and State Assemblies, Article 334A determines when those obligations actually take effect, how long they last, how reserved seats rotate, and what happens to existing legislatures when the reservation commences.

Without Article 334A, the obligations in Articles 330A and 332A would be immediately operative. Article 334A deliberately defers them, making the reservation constitutionally real but practically conditional on future events — the census and delimitation — that are themselves subject to political and logistical timing.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Trigger and sunset.** No seat is reserved for women in the Lok Sabha, any State Assembly, or the Delhi Assembly until two preconditions are satisfied sequentially. First, the census taken after the 106th Amendment's commencement must be published — specifically, the "relevant figures" from the first such census. Second, a delimitation exercise must be undertaken specifically for the purpose of implementing women's reservation on the basis of those figures. Only then does the reservation commence.

The sunset runs for fifteen years from the date the reservation commences — not from the date of enactment. If delimitation does not occur until 2034, for example, the initial reservation period runs to 2049. The clause opens with "notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Part or Part VIII," signalling its overriding character across all other provisions of Parts XVI and VIII.

**Clause (2) — Extension by Parliament.** Once the reservation commences, it continues until Parliament by law determines an end date. This structure mirrors Article 334's long history with SC/ST reservations: Parliament must affirmatively act to extend or allow expiry. The clause prevents automatic lapse and places the political responsibility for renewal squarely on Parliament.

**Clause (3) — Rotation of reserved seats.** Reserved constituencies for women must rotate after each subsequent delimitation exercise. The mechanism prevents any constituency from becoming permanently a women-reserved seat. The detailed rotation methodology — how seats are assigned, whether sequentially or by lot — is left to Parliament to prescribe by legislation. The rotation design borrows from the SC/ST reserved-seat model, where delimitation periodically reassigns which constituencies are designated.

**Clause (4) — Continuity of existing legislatures.** No existing Lok Sabha, State Assembly, or Delhi Assembly is dissolved upon commencement of the reservation. The reservation operates only from the next general election following the delimitation conducted under clause (1). This prevents a mid-term disruption and protects the mandate of sitting members who were elected before the reservation framework existed.

## Comparison with Article 334

Article 334 is the earlier sunset provision governing SC/ST reserved seats in Parliament and State Assemblies, originally set to expire in 1960 and subsequently extended eight times by amendment — most recently by the 104th Amendment (2020) which extended SC/ST reservations to 2030. Article 334A follows the same structural logic: a time-limited reservation that requires periodic legislative renewal. The design choice reflects a constitutional philosophy that political reservations should be justified afresh at each renewal rather than made permanent, and that the legislature — not the Constitution's text — should bear ongoing political accountability for the continuation of preferential representation.

The key difference is mechanism: SC/ST reservation extensions under Article 334 required constitutional amendments, while Article 334A's clause (2) allows Parliament to extend the women's reservation by ordinary law rather than the Article 368 amendment procedure. This is a significant reduction in the procedural bar for extension — Parliament can renew women's reservation by simple majority without state ratification.

## The deferred commencement debate

The conditionality in clause (1) has attracted sustained criticism. The 2021 decennial census was not conducted due to the COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent administrative delays; as of 2026, it has yet to be conducted. Delimitation under Article 82 is itself constitutionally frozen until after the first census following 2026. The practical result is that the 106th Amendment, passed unanimously in September 2023, is unlikely to produce any change in legislative composition before approximately 2034.

Critics argue that the trigger conditioned on census and delimitation was a deliberate parliamentary device to pass the reform politically while indefinitely deferring its practical consequence. Supporters contend that delimitation was a necessary precondition to accurately map women-reserved constituencies against population data, and that any alternative design risked litigation about the fairness of constituency assignments made without current census data.

## Key cases

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — The Supreme Court's ruling established that reservations in public employment are subject to a 50 percent ceiling under Article 16, but distinguished political reservations for electoral representation. Article 334A's reservation framework operates under the distinct constitutional space carved out for Part XVI provisions, and the Indra Sawhney ceiling has been understood not to apply to electoral seat reservations.

**Writ petitions on 106th Amendment (2023-24)** — Multiple public interest petitions were filed in the Supreme Court challenging the deferred commencement as defeating the object of the amendment and seeking directions to accelerate census and delimitation. The Supreme Court did not issue substantive directions on the timing, affirming that the scheduling of census operations and the conduct of delimitation are executive and legislative functions not subject to mandamus in the ordinary course.

**Krishnamurthy v Union of India (2022)** — In the context of delimitation of Lok Sabha constituencies, the Supreme Court examined the constitutional constraints on the Delimitation Commission's exercise of discretion. Its reasoning about the justiciability of delimitation decisions is likely to be revisited when the first delimitation under the women's reservation regime takes place, particularly on questions of constituency selection and rotation methodology.

## The OBC sub-quota question and its relationship to Article 334A

Article 334A is silent on the OBC sub-quota within the women's reservation. The political controversy about whether a separate sub-quota for Other Backward Classes women should be carved out of the one-third reservation was resolved at the time of the 106th Amendment by omission — no such sub-quota was inserted. The argument made during the Rajya Sabha debates was that Parliament could insert an OBC sub-quota by ordinary law once the framework was operational, without the need for a further constitutional amendment.

Article 334A's structure leaves this open in theory: clause (2) says the reservation continues until Parliament determines by law, and Parliament could potentially legislate a sub-quota as part of an extension or modification. Whether an OBC sub-quota within the women's reservation would itself require a constitutional amendment (because it modifies the constitutional scheme created by Articles 330A and 332A) or could be achieved by statute is a question that constitutional scholars and political parties are likely to contest when the reservation approaches operational commencement.

## Current significance

Article 334A is the mechanism that converts the aspirational language of Articles 330A and 332A into an operative constitutional programme. Its deferred trigger, combined with a fifteen-year sunset renewable by ordinary law, creates a reservation regime that is constitutionally guaranteed but practically contingent on political will across two distinct thresholds: first, the conduct of a census and delimitation; and second, parliamentary action to extend the reservation once it begins.

The provision should be read as part of a multi-tier architecture alongside Articles 330A, 332A, 334, 243D, and 243T. When operative, it will affect the composition of approximately 4,000 directly elected legislative seats across Parliament and all State Assemblies — the most extensive single transformation of Indian electoral representation architecture since the Constitution came into force in 1950. The combination of a trigger (census plus delimitation), a fifteen-year duration, and a parliamentary-law extension mechanism makes Article 334A an experiment in constitutionalising a major social reform while retaining democratic flexibility over its long-term operation.
