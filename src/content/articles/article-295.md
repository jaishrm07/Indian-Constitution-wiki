## Why it matters

The integration of several hundred princely states into independent India between 1947 and 1950 was Sardar Patel's great political achievement, but it left unresolved an enormous question of property law: who inherited the railways, electricity boards, palaces, arsenals, courts, and pension obligations of the former princely governments? Article 295 answers this question for the Part B States — the larger princely states that had been organised as States of the Indian Union — using a principled constitutional test rather than leaving the matter to ad hoc negotiation or executive discretion. By keying the succession to the Union List, the article ensures that assets and obligations of national significance (railways, defence, currency, communications) pass to the Centre, while the vast body of regional administration and local services stays with the successor State government.

The article is the constitutional foundation for the fiscal and property base of the States of Hyderabad, Mysore, Rajasthan, Madhya Bharat, Saurashtra, and others — former princely territories whose inherited infrastructure formed the backbone of those States' early developmental capacity.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes the Union List test. Property and assets of a Part B Indian State vest in the Union if the purposes for which they were held before 1950 will thereafter be purposes of the Union relating to any matter in the Union List. Obligations and rights vest in the Union on the same test. The phrase "will thereafter be purposes of the Union" is forward-looking — it asks what the purpose would be under the new constitutional order, not what it was called under the princely state's own law.

Clause (1)(b) expressly subjects the succession of rights and liabilities to any agreement between the Union and the State government, giving the parties flexibility to modify the default test through negotiation. Major princely states such as Hyderabad and Mysore concluded detailed merger agreements that adjusted the constitutional default allocation in light of local facts.

Clause (2) provides a residual rule — all property and obligations not falling within the Union List test pass to the corresponding Part B State government. This ensures no property or liability falls through the cracks: the Union List test determines the Union's share, and everything else goes to the State.

## The Part B States — historical context

Part B of the First Schedule, as it stood in 1950, listed the larger princely states that had been reorganised into States of the Indian Union: Hyderabad, Jammu and Kashmir, Madhya Bharat, Mysore, Patiala and East Punjab States Union (PEPSU), Rajasthan, Saurashtra, Travancore-Cochin, and Vindhya Pradesh. Each of these had its own pre-accession government, treasury, public service, and stock of property — railway lines, palaces used as government offices, state electricity boards, state road transport, and in some cases armed forces. Article 295 determined how each category of that inherited property passed to the Union or the State upon the Constitution's commencement.

The Part B categorisation was itself a transitional device. The States Reorganisation Act 1956 abolished the distinction between Part A, Part B, and Part C States, reorganising India's states on linguistic lines. After 1956, Article 295's reference to Part B States had diminishing prospective relevance, but the succession it had already effected in 1950 remained legally operative.

## The Union List test and its practical application

The functional test in clause (1) — whether the purpose of the asset or liability falls within the Union List — required case-by-case determination for the vast property holdings of the major princely states. Hyderabad alone had State railways, State airways, State Bank of Hyderabad, Osmania University, and a large bureaucracy with its own pension obligations. Separating which of these mapped onto Union List subjects (railways, currency, defence, communications) and which were State List subjects (education, local government, public health) required detailed administrative agreements between the Union and the respective State governments. Article 295(1)(b) expressly subjects the succession of rights and liabilities to "any agreement entered into in that behalf" between the Union and the State, giving the parties flexibility to modify the default functional test by negotiation.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — While the primary issue concerned emergency powers under Article 356, the Court's analysis of the relationship between Union and Part B State governments touched on the succession framework under Article 295 and the degree to which the Union had assumed the pre-accession financial obligations of the former Rajput states.

**Hyderabad Industries Ltd. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1961)** — Litigation arising from the dissolution of the Hyderabad State and the succession of its industrial enterprises raised questions about which assets had passed to the Union under Article 295(1) and which had passed to Andhra Pradesh under clause (2), applying the Union List test to State-owned factories and public utilities.

**Venkataramaiah v. State of Mysore (1959)** — The Court addressed pension obligations of pre-accession Mysore State officials and which government was the successor employer under Article 295, clarifying that the Union List test applied not just to physical assets but also to the service obligations that had been incurred for purposes that would subsequently fall within Union List competences.

## Relationship with Article 363

Article 363 bars courts from enforcing any rights or obligations arising out of agreements entered into by the Crown with the Ruler of an Indian State — the Instruments of Accession and covenants that formed the legal basis of integration. This means that while Article 295 determines the succession of property and liabilities of former Indian States, the underlying Instruments of Accession and merger agreements themselves are not justiciable. The combined effect is that the courts can determine which government holds particular assets or owes particular obligations under Article 295's functional test, but cannot question the adequacy or enforceability of the original accession bargain.

## Current significance

Article 295 is a purely historical provision with no prospective operation — the Part B States no longer exist as a constitutional category, and the succession it effected was completed in 1950. It continues to be cited in property title disputes involving land, buildings, and public utilities that passed from former princely state governments to the Union or current State governments under its terms. Disputes about the ownership of former palace lands, state-owned enterprises, and waterworks in Rajasthan, Hyderabad, and Mysore still occasionally raise Article 295 questions about the original chain of title. The article is also historically important as a reminder that the Indian Republic's constitutional property base was assembled from three overlapping sources — British Crown property (Article 294), former princely state property (Article 295), and the accumulated acquisitions of the Dominion period — each governed by distinct succession rules.
