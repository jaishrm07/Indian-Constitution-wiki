## What this Article did

Article 385 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that addressed provisional Legislatures in States in Part B of the First Schedule. It has been omitted.

The Part B States in the original First Schedule were the former acceding princely states that had been integrated into the Indian Union. They included major States like Hyderabad, Mysore, Travancore-Cochin, Rajasthan (a merger of multiple former princely states), and others. Their legislative bodies had very different histories from the provincial Legislatures of Part A States — some had Constituent Assemblies, some had consultative bodies, and some had limited representative institutions.

Article 385 provided for the continuation of these existing legislative bodies as provisional Legislatures of the Part B States under the new Constitution, pending fresh elections. This was more complex than the Part A State Legislatures because the Part B States had more varied institutional histories and because their integration into India had been more recently completed (many had been acceded to India only in 1947-1949).

## Why it matters

The constitutional validation of provisional legislatures in the former princely states was particularly sensitive. Many of these states had not had representative legislative institutions at all before their integration into India — their "legislatures" were often nominated bodies or consultative councils with limited powers. Article 385's treatment of these bodies as provisional constitutional legislatures represented an immediate democratic upgrade: these assemblies were now operating under a constitutional framework that included fundamental rights, judicial review, and the prospect of early elections under adult franchise. The provision thus accelerated the democratic transformation of the former princely states.

## Why it was omitted

Article 385 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. The States Reorganisation Act, 1956 abolished the Part B and Part C State categories entirely and reorganised India's States on linguistic lines. After that reorganisation, the Part B States ceased to exist as such, and the transitional provisions relating to them became doubly spent — spent both because the provisional assemblies had been replaced by elected assemblies after the 1951-52 elections, and spent because the very States they referred to had been reorganised or dissolved.

## Historical context

The integration of the princely states into India was one of the most complex constitutional and political processes of 1947-1950. Under the leadership of Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel and V P Menon, over 500 princely states were integrated through instruments of accession and merger agreements. The largest of these — Hyderabad, Junagadh, and Kashmir — were integrated through processes that involved negotiations, military action (in the case of Hyderabad), or ongoing dispute (in the case of Kashmir).

Article 385 gave constitutional sanction to the legislative arrangements that existed in these States at the time of their integration, treating their existing legislatures as provisional constitutional legislatures of the new Part B States.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge to the provisional Legislatures of Part B States is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 385 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in the constitutional integration of the former princely states and in the recognition that their existing legislative institutions were valid provisional Legislatures under the new Constitution, providing continuity of governance during the transition period.
