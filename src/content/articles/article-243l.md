## Why it matters

Article 243L performs the constitutional translation work necessary to apply Part IX in Union Territories. The Panchayati Raj framework was designed for states — where a Governor heads the executive and a State Legislature enacts laws. Union Territories have a different constitutional architecture: they are governed by the President through an Administrator (Lieutenant Governor or Administrator) under Article 239, and only some have legislatures at all. Without Article 243L's adaptation, the constitutional text of Part IX would simply not fit the UT context.

The article's approach is to substitute actors, not to create a separate framework. The result is that the full substantive obligations of Part IX — the three-tier structure, the reservation requirements, the Finance Commission, the election commission — apply to UTs in the same way they apply to states, with the Administrator exercising the functions the Governor exercises in a state and the UT legislature (where one exists) exercising the functions of the State legislature. For UTs without a legislature (such as Ladakh and Lakshadweep), the powers of the State legislature would vest in Parliament, making the Centre both the governing authority and the law-making authority for Panchayats in such territories.

The Presidential proviso is a significant source of flexibility — and potential dilution. The President may by notification direct that Part IX applies to any UT "subject to such exceptions and modifications" as the President specifies. This power has been used in relation to newly created and reorganised Union Territories to phase in or adjust the Panchayati Raj framework in light of administrative realities.

## How courts use it

Courts applying Part IX in the context of Union Territories rely on Article 243L to establish which executive and legislative authority has the relevant obligations. Challenges to delayed Panchayat elections in Union Territories, for instance, are framed against the Administrator rather than a Governor, and the State Election Commission analogue for UTs is treated as a constitutional institution with the same independence as its state-level counterpart. High Courts exercising jurisdiction over UTs have applied the full range of Part IX protections — including the mandatory election timeline of Article 243E — to UT Panchayats, reading Article 243L as making no substantive reduction in these constitutional obligations.

The proviso's Presidential notification power has been tested when notifications have modified or delayed the application of specific Part IX provisions. Courts have treated these notifications as executive actions subject to constitutional review, not as legislative amendments, meaning they must be within the scope of what the proviso authorises and cannot derogate from the fundamental constitutional structure of Panchayati Raj.

## Key cases

**NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018)** — While principally about the governance relationship between the Delhi Government and the Lieutenant Governor, the Supreme Court's analysis of the constitutional position of elected institutions in a Union Territory with a legislature is directly relevant to Article 243L. The Court's emphasis on the primacy of the elected legislature in areas within its legislative competence informs how Article 243L should be read in UTs with legislatures: the UT legislature exercises genuine constitutional authority over Panchayat matters, not merely delegated administrative authority.

**Pratap Singh v. State of Jharkhand (2005)** — In addressing Panchayat election obligations, the Supreme Court emphasised that the constitutional mandate for timely elections under Part IX applies without exception across different administrative territories. The reasoning supports the Article 243L position that UTs have the same constitutional obligations as states regarding Panchayat elections and cannot use their different governance structure as a basis for non-compliance.

## Current significance

Article 243L has acquired particular contemporary relevance following the bifurcation of Jammu and Kashmir into two Union Territories — Jammu and Kashmir (with a legislature) and Ladakh (without a legislature) — in 2019. The application of Part IX to these territories under Article 243L, including the timing and manner of Panchayat elections and the establishment of a State Election Commission equivalent, became a live constitutional and administrative question in the reorganisation's aftermath. Panchayat elections were held in Jammu and Kashmir in 2018, before reorganisation, and the subsequent constitutional framework for Panchayati Raj in both UTs has been determined through the Article 243L mechanism.
