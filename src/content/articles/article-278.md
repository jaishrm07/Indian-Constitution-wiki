## What this Article originally provided

Article 278 originally authorised the Union to enter into agreements with States listed in Part B of the First Schedule — the former princely states and their successors that had acceded to or merged with India but retained distinct financial arrangements pending full integration — to cover matters such as the continuation of pre-accession tax arrangements, payments in lieu of ceded tax powers, and special grants.

Part B States occupied an intermediate constitutional status: they had their own Rajpramukhs (appointed by the President) instead of Governors, and their integration into the general financial framework of the Constitution was to be phased in through negotiated agreements rather than uniform application. Article 278 was the legal basis for these transitional financial compacts. These agreements could address the handover of State tax administration from the former princely state revenue systems to the new constitutional framework, guaranteed payments to successor governments in lieu of tax powers that were now Union subjects, and special fiscal adjustments needed to equalise the financial position of newly integrated territories.

The Part B States included territories such as Hyderabad, Mysore, Travancore-Cochin, Madhya Bharat, and Saurashtra — large and economically significant areas whose financial systems were entirely distinct from the British provincial system that formed the template for the original Constitution.

## Why it was omitted

The States Reorganisation Act 1956 and the Seventh Constitutional Amendment abolished the Part B/Part C/Part D categorisation and brought all States into a uniform tier. With no Part B States remaining, Article 278 became redundant and was omitted by the Seventh Amendment itself, effective 1 November 1956 — the date the new State structure came into force. The omission was a constitutional housekeeping measure: once the category of "Part B State" no longer existed, the provision enabling agreements with such States had no subject matter and could not be invoked.

The financial arrangements that had been negotiated under Article 278 were either absorbed into the general grants-in-aid framework under Article 275 or extinguished as the formerly distinct princely state fiscal systems were unified into the national revenue architecture. The First Finance Commission (1952) had to account for these transitional Article 278 arrangements in its recommendations for grants-in-aid, making the transition from fragmented pre-accession fiscal compacts to a uniform devolution framework a live constitutional challenge during the Constitution's first decade.

## How courts use it

Article 278 generated no significant litigation before its omission in 1956, partly because the agreements it authorised were executive compacts rather than legislative acts and partly because the short time between the Constitution's commencement (1950) and the article's omission (1956) limited the opportunity for disputes to mature into litigation. Post-omission, courts encountering claims arising from pre-1956 Article 278 agreements have treated them as historical contracts whose enforceability depends on whether they were absorbed into successor legislation or executive orders, rather than as continuing constitutional obligations.

## Key cases

No reported judicial decisions directly interpreted Article 278 on its merits. The financial arrangements concluded under the Article were documented in Ministry of Finance records and States Reorganisation Commission materials. Post-omission, the Supreme Court has referenced Article 278 in constitutional history discussions when tracing the evolution of fiscal federalism from the princely-state integration period to the present unified framework.

**States Reorganisation Commission Report (1955)** — While not a judicial decision, the Commission's report is the primary analytical document examining Article 278 arrangements. It recommended abolishing the Part B/C/D category structure on the ground that differentiated fiscal arrangements were becoming administratively unworkable and were inconsistent with the goal of a uniform federal financial system.

## Current significance

Article 278 has no current operational significance. It serves as a constitutional history marker illustrating how the framers managed the extraordinary diversity of fiscal arrangements inherited from the colonial period and the princely state system. The broader lesson — that the Constitution was designed with flexibility to accommodate transitional arrangements and then sunset them — informs ongoing debates about whether similar phased-integration mechanisms could be used for territories that accede to or reorganise within India in the future, such as provisions for fiscal transition following potential statehood grants to Union Territories. Article 278's history is also cited by scholars examining the constitutional basis for special fiscal provisions for North-Eastern States and for Jammu and Kashmir's pre-2019 financial arrangements, as examples of the Constitution's capacity to accommodate differentiated fiscal relationships within an otherwise uniform federal framework.

The contrast between Article 278's sunset nature and Article 371 (special provisions for North-Eastern and other States) is instructive: the framers chose a temporary route for the princely-state integration problem and a permanent one for regional asymmetry in development levels and cultural identity. This choice illustrates the distinction between transitional fiscal arrangements (which Article 278 represented) and ongoing differentiated federalism (which Article 371 represents), a distinction that continues to inform constitutional debates about special categories of statehood.
