## What this Article does

Article 134A, inserted by the Constitution (Forty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, governs the procedure by which a High Court grants a certificate of fitness for appeal to the Supreme Court under Articles 132, 133, and 134. It creates two routes for the certificate to be considered:

- **Sua sponte (clause a):** The High Court may, on its own motion, consider whether to grant the certificate, without waiting for any application.
- **On oral application (clause b):** If the aggrieved party makes an oral application immediately after judgment, the High Court is obliged to determine the certificate question as soon as may be after the judgment is pronounced.

The "immediately after" requirement is procedurally significant. The aggrieved party must make the oral application at the time the judgment is delivered — not days later by written application. This ensures that the certificate question is addressed while the bench is still assembled and fresh, and prevents parties from strategically delaying the application.

## The three certificate routes

Article 134A is a procedural provision that serves three substantive appellate routes:

**Article 132 (constitutional questions):** An appeal lies to the Supreme Court from a High Court judgment, decree, or final order in a civil, criminal, or other proceeding, if the High Court certifies that the case involves a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of the Constitution.

**Article 133 (civil appeals):** An appeal lies in civil proceedings if the High Court certifies that the case involves a substantial question of law of general importance, and that in the High Court's opinion the question needs to be decided by the Supreme Court.

**Article 134(1)(c) (criminal appeals):** An appeal lies in criminal proceedings if the High Court certifies that the case is a fit one for appeal to the Supreme Court — a broader and less prescriptive standard than the civil and constitutional routes.

Article 134A is the procedural gateway through which all three of these certificate routes are accessed. Without a certificate (or without an application under Article 136 for special leave), there is no appeal to the Supreme Court as of right.

## The gatekeeping function

By requiring the High Court itself to certify fitness for appeal, the Constitution places a two-tier filter on certificate-based appeals to the Supreme Court: first, the substantive standard (substantial question of law, etc.) and second, the procedural requirement that the aggrieved party apply immediately after judgment. This design reflects the framers' concern about managing the Supreme Court's caseload — requiring an immediate oral application disciplines parties to identify the appeal question at once, and the "as soon as may be" requirement for the High Court's determination disciplines the court itself.

The High Court's decision to grant or refuse a certificate is a judicial act and is itself open to challenge — though typically through Article 136 (special leave petition) rather than by direct review, since a refusal of the certificate does not preclude the Supreme Court from granting special leave on the same or different grounds.

## Key cases

**Garikapati Veeraya v. N. Subbiah Choudhry (1957):** The Supreme Court established the foundational principle that the right of appeal is a vested right governed by the law in force at the commencement of the suit or proceeding. This principle governs how Article 134A operates for proceedings that straddled the amendment's insertion date and informs the general approach to High Court certificates.

**Central Bank of India v. Ram Narain (1955):** The Court clarified the standard for "substantial question of law of general importance" under the civil appeal certificate route (now Article 133), holding that not every point of law is substantial — the question must be one whose resolution will have general applicability and is genuinely uncertain. This standard applies when High Courts consider certificates under Article 134A for civil appeals.

**Balak Ram v. State of Punjab (1975):** The Court addressed the certificate requirement in criminal cases and the consequences of a High Court granting a certificate. The judgment clarified that a certificate under Article 134(1)(c) opens the appeal as of right, unlike Article 136 which confers a discretionary jurisdiction on the Supreme Court.

**Ram Kishan v. Tarun Bajaj (2014):** The Supreme Court addressed the procedural requirement in Article 134A(b) — specifically the "immediately after" requirement for oral applications — holding that it means promptly upon pronouncement of judgment and not at some later point in time. Parties who miss the immediate application window must instead approach the Supreme Court under Article 136.

## Relationship with Article 136

Article 134A certificate appeals and Article 136 special leave petitions coexist in the Supreme Court's appellate framework. A party who fails to obtain a certificate under Article 134A (either because they did not apply immediately, the High Court refused, or the matter does not meet the certification standard) may still petition the Supreme Court under Article 136 for special leave. Article 136 confers a discretionary jurisdiction that covers the entire field of appellate jurisdiction and is not limited by the specific grounds in Articles 132, 133, and 134. In practice, most appeals to the Supreme Court now come through Article 136 rather than by certificate, partly because the Article 134A "immediately after" requirement is easily missed.

## Current significance

Article 134A is rarely the subject of independent litigation but its procedural requirements shape the flow of appeals from High Courts to the Supreme Court. The practical dominance of Article 136 (special leave petitions) as the route to the Supreme Court has reduced the certificate mechanism's operational significance, but Article 134A retains constitutional importance as the prescribed procedure for certificate-based appeals as of right. The Law Commission and academic commentators have noted that the "immediately after" requirement can cause inadvertent loss of a right of appeal in criminal cases where a death sentence or life imprisonment has been imposed, and have suggested that the procedural requirement should be applied liberally in such cases.
