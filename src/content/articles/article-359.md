## Why it matters

Part III of the Constitution confers a catalogue of fundamental rights, and Article 32 gives every person the right to move the Supreme Court for the enforcement of those rights. During a National Emergency, Article 359 allows the President to suspend this right of access to courts — but only for rights specifically named in the presidential order, and never for Articles 20 and 21 after the 44th Amendment.

The distinction between Article 358 and Article 359 is fundamental. Article 358 automatically suspends Article 19 itself — the rights simply do not exist during a war or external aggression emergency. Article 359, by contrast, does not suspend the rights themselves; it suspends the right to move courts for their enforcement. In principle, a right not enforced in court still exists, but in practice, a right with no judicial remedy is a right with no protection.

Before the 44th Amendment of 1978, the President's Article 359 order could suspend enforcement of any Part III right, including Articles 20 and 21. During the 1975 Emergency, a presidential order did exactly that — it suspended the right to move courts under Article 21, which was then used to prevent habeas corpus petitions from detainees challenging their preventive detention. The Supreme Court majority in ADM Jabalpur (1976) held that this was constitutionally valid and that courts could not look behind the detention order. The result was that tens of thousands of persons were held in preventive detention with no judicial remedy whatsoever.

The 44th Amendment permanently reversed this. Article 359 now expressly excludes Articles 20 and 21 from the scope of any presidential order. No matter how severe the emergency, no matter what order the President issues, the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21 and the protection against double jeopardy and self-incrimination under Article 20 remain judicially enforceable.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the suspension order.** The President may by order declare that the right to move any court for the enforcement of specified Part III rights shall remain suspended for the duration of the proclamation or a shorter specified period. The order must name the rights; a general suspension of all Part III rights (other than Articles 20 and 21) is possible in principle but the naming requirement at least forces specificity.

**Clause (1A) — the legislative analogue.** Once a suspension order is in operation, it operates not only procedurally (barring court access) but also substantively: nothing in the suspended Part III rights restricts the State's power to make laws or take executive action in relation to those rights. This makes Article 359, when a presidential order is in force, functionally similar to Article 358 — the right effectively ceases to constrain the State. Clause (1A) was added to make this explicit, avoiding any argument that the right survives in substance even if its enforcement is suspended.

**The recital requirement.** As with Article 358, a law must contain a recital linking it to the emergency proclamation and executive action must be taken under such a law, for Article 359(1A) protection to apply. This prevents the government from using a general suspension order to immunise legislation that has nothing to do with the emergency.

**Clause (2) — geographic scope.** The order may extend to the whole or any part of India. Where the emergency operates only in part of India, the order cannot extend beyond that part unless the President is satisfied that the security of India is threatened by activities connected to the emergency zone. This mirrors the geographic limitations in Articles 353 and 358.

**Absolute exclusion of Articles 20 and 21.** The bracketed text — "(except articles 20 and 21)" — inserted by the 44th Amendment is the constitutional resolution of the most acute abuse of the emergency power. These protections against arbitrary criminal conviction and arbitrary deprivation of life or liberty are treated as non-derogable regardless of the nature or severity of the emergency.

## Key cases

**ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla (1976)** — The Supreme Court was asked during the 1975 Emergency whether habeas corpus petitions could be maintained despite a presidential order suspending the right to move courts under Article 21. The majority of four judges held that when Article 21 (right to life and personal liberty) was suspended by presidential order, no court had jurisdiction to question a detention, regardless of whether it was authorised by law. Justice H R Khanna's solitary dissent — that the executive cannot deprive a person of life or liberty without the authority of law, even during an emergency, and that the common law and natural law principle of legality survives any Article 359 order — was vindicated by the 44th Amendment. The case is widely regarded as the low point of the Supreme Court's commitment to constitutional values, and Khanna's dissent as one of the finest judicial statements in Indian legal history. The majority's reasoning has been progressively abandoned in subsequent constitutional jurisprudence.

**Makhan Singh Tarsikka v State of Punjab (1964)** — Decided during the 1962 Emergency prompted by the India-China war, the Supreme Court drew a clear procedural and substantive distinction between Articles 358 and 359. The Court confirmed that Article 359 operates only through a specific presidential order, that rights not named in the order remain enforceable, and that the order must be in force at the time the court is moved for the suspension to apply. The judgment provided the constitutional map used (and abused) in the 1975 Emergency.

**State of Maharashtra v Bhaurao Punjabrao Gawande (2008)** — The Court reaffirmed in the post-Emergency period that any suspension of enforcement of rights under Article 359 must be strictly within the terms of the presidential order, that the courts retain jurisdiction over claims falling outside those terms, and that Articles 20 and 21 remain enforceable regardless of any order. The judgment reinforced the post-44th Amendment constitutional settlement.

## Current significance

The permanent exclusion of Articles 20 and 21 from Article 359 orders is the single most important safeguard against a repetition of the ADM Jabalpur outcome. It is now settled constitutional doctrine, reinforced by the nine-judge bench's privacy judgment in Justice K S Puttaswamy v Union of India (2017), which treated human dignity and personal liberty as inalienable constitutional values that no emergency can erase.

Nevertheless, significant concerns remain. First, Articles other than 19, 20, and 21 — including the equality guarantees of Articles 14 and 15, property rights, and procedural rights — can still be suspended by a presidential order under Article 359. A government facing an emergency it declares to be on armed rebellion grounds could, without the Article 358 automatic suspension, still obtain a broad Article 359 order suspending Article 14 and 15 protections, effectively removing equality and non-discrimination guarantees. Second, the combination of Article 359 suspension and the broad preventive detention powers available under Entry 9 of the Union List means that even with Articles 20 and 21 protected, the practical scope for arbitrary detention remains wide. Third, ADM Jabalpur was only "overruled" in its reasoning, not formally in a case turning on the same facts; the formal constitutional question of what precisely remains of Article 359 in a future emergency has not been definitively settled by a constitutional bench after the 44th Amendment.
