## Why it matters

Article 28 is where secularism, education, and individual conscience meet in the most direct way the Constitution allows. Every child who attends a government school, every student in a grant-in-aid college, every teacher compelled to lead a morning prayer assembly — each is affected by the constitutional line Article 28 draws between education and confessional instruction. The provision addresses a concrete human vulnerability: that a student dependent on a state-funded institution for access to knowledge has diminished practical capacity to refuse religious demands made by that institution. The Constitution recognises this power asymmetry and responds by prohibiting religious instruction in fully state-funded schools and by requiring genuine consent in aided or recognised institutions.

The stakes extend beyond individual conscience. Education is a site where identity is formed and where communities reproduce their culture across generations. Majority communities with the power to shape state-funded curriculum can, if unchecked, embed their religious worldview in public education in ways that marginalise students of minority faiths. Article 28(1)'s absolute prohibition on religious instruction in fully state-maintained institutions responds precisely to this risk. It tells the legislative majority that public schools are secular space, not space for the transmission of any particular faith, however widely held.

The three clauses of Article 28 create a layered structure that reflects the institutional complexity of Indian education. Fully state-maintained institutions face an absolute prohibition. State-administered institutions established under endowments requiring religious instruction receive a tailored exception. Recognised or aided institutions — by far the largest category in Indian education — may retain their religious character but cannot conscript students into it without consent. This graduated approach is sophisticated: it does not flatten all educational institutions into uniform secularism, but it calibrates the constitutional constraint to the degree of state involvement.

The provision matters beyond the classroom in a more general way. It establishes that the state's secular character has institutional consequences. When the state runs a school, it must run it as a school for all citizens regardless of faith. The principle is not confined to education in a narrow sense: it informs how public institutions of all kinds should handle religious expression and how the state should relate to the religious diversity of its citizen body. Article 28 is therefore a specific application of a broader constitutional commitment whose implications extend to the entire public institutional sphere.

The contemporary relevance of the article is visible in recurring controversies. Government school assemblies that include specific prayers, yoga instruction presented as a form of spiritual practice, temple-opening ceremonies at government-aided universities, and curricula that draw on the sacred texts of one tradition while ignoring others — each raises Article 28 questions. As states become more openly assertive about the religious character of their cultural policies, the structural separation Article 28 mandates between state educational machinery and confessional instruction becomes an increasingly important line of constitutional defence.

Article 28 is also the provision through which the right of students from religious minorities to participate in majority-faith institutions without surrendering their own identity is most directly protected. The consent requirement in Article 28(3) is not merely procedural: it reflects the constitutional judgment that faith must be embraced freely to be constitutionally legitimate. A child compelled to attend prayers, memorise scripture, or participate in religious ceremonies by an institution funded from public money is in a position the Constitution explicitly refuses to countenance.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes the strictest rule: no religious instruction in any institution wholly maintained out of State funds. "Wholly maintained" is a term of art that has required careful judicial attention. An institution funded entirely by the state, without any private endowment or community contribution, falls squarely within this prohibition. But most Indian educational institutions have mixed funding: state grants, student fees, private donations, and community contributions combine in varying proportions. Courts have generally held that "wholly maintained" means that the state bears the entire financial burden of maintaining the institution, so that even a small private contribution may take an institution outside the absolute prohibition of clause (1) and into the consent-based regime of clause (3). This interpretation is not universally accepted, but it reflects the dominant judicial approach.

Clause (2) creates a carefully bounded exception for a historically specific category: institutions administered by the state but established under an endowment or trust that requires religious instruction. The classic example is a school or college that was founded by a religious community, endowed with funds dedicated to religious education, and later taken over by or brought under state administration. The state's administrative role does not extinguish the founding obligation to provide religious instruction where the endowment expressly requires it. This clause reconciles the secular principle with the pre-existing institutional landscape that the Constitution inherited; it does not create a general license for state-administered institutions to provide religious instruction. The exception is narrow: the endowment or trust must specifically require religious instruction, not merely permit it or associate the institution with a religious tradition.

Clause (3) addresses the largest category: institutions recognised by the state or receiving state aid. These institutions may provide religious instruction and conduct religious worship, but no person may be required to participate unless that person — or, if a minor, their guardian — has given consent. The consent requirement has several important features. First, it is individual and specific: institutional practice cannot substitute for individual choice. Second, it is protective of minors: the framers recognised that children are especially vulnerable to institutional pressure and provided for guardian consent rather than expecting children to assert their own objections. Third, consent must be genuine and prior: a student who feels pressure to consent because of the institution's religious character, or who is admitted on terms that include compulsory religious participation, has not given constitutionally adequate consent.

The term "religious instruction" in clause (1) and clause (3) has required judicial definition. The Supreme Court has distinguished between religious instruction — the inculcation of doctrine, practice, and commitment associated with a particular faith — and the academic study of religion as a cultural, historical, or comparative phenomenon. Teaching comparative religion in a secular, scholarly manner does not constitute "religious instruction" within Article 28. Nor does including material about major world religions in a value education curriculum, provided the treatment is objective and non-confessional. The line between instruction and education is inherently unstable, however: a presentation of Hindu mythology as history, or of Islamic ethics as universal morality, can cross from the academic into the confessional without any single identifiable moment of transition.

"Religious worship" in clause (3) encompasses any ceremonial religious practice, including daily prayers, hymn-singing, puja, namaz, or similar acts of devotion conducted by the institution. The "premises attached thereto" formulation extends the protection beyond the classroom to the school compound, chapel, or any other space associated with the institution, preventing institutions from circumventing the consent requirement by relocating religious activities to adjacent but formally distinct spaces.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The debate in the Constituent Assembly on what became Article 28 reflected the sharp tensions between the secular vision of the new state and the claims of religious communities — particularly Christian missionary bodies and Hindu educational trusts — to continue providing religious education in institutions they had established and continued to support. The initial drafts generated considerable controversy. Some members from the Christian minority argued that institutions established by missionary effort, even where receiving state aid, should retain the unrestricted right to provide religious instruction as part of their foundational character. Others argued that any use of public money to fund religious instruction was inconsistent with the secular state the Constitution was building.

The final structure of Article 28 emerged from a compromise brokered largely through the Drafting Committee's work. The absolute prohibition in clause (1) was accepted because it applied only to institutions wholly dependent on the state — a category that in practice would be limited to government-run schools and colleges with no private contribution. The exception in clause (2) addressed the specific case of endowed institutions under state administration, satisfying members who were concerned about disrupting pre-existing arrangements. The consent mechanism in clause (3) addressed aided and recognised institutions, preserving the right of religious communities to maintain their educational character while protecting individual students from compulsion. The compromise was explicitly designed to accommodate the existing landscape of mission schools, gurukuls, madrasas, and denominational colleges without requiring any of them to abandon their religious character, provided individual consent was respected.

The Constituent Assembly also debated whether the prohibition on religious instruction should extend to "religious education" more broadly, or whether it should be confined to formal doctrinal instruction associated with a specific faith. Ambedkar and other members of the Drafting Committee argued for a narrower formulation focused on instruction rather than all religious content, partly to avoid the practical impossibility of entirely de-religionising education in a society where religious culture permeates history, literature, and ethics. The final text — "religious instruction" — reflects this narrower choice, and the boundary between instruction and education that the Court would later be asked to draw was in some respects anticipated by the Assembly's deliberate use of the more specific term.

## Key cases

**Aruna Roy v. Union of India (2002) 7 SCC 368** — The petitioner challenged the National Curriculum Framework's inclusion of value education incorporating material about world religions, arguing that teaching about religion in government schools violated Article 28(1). The Supreme Court rejected the challenge and held that teaching about religion in an academic, objective, and multi-faith manner does not constitute "religious instruction" within Article 28(1). The Court drew a principled line: Article 28(1) prohibits the inculcation of particular religious beliefs and practices; it does not prohibit the scholarly study of religion as a cultural and historical phenomenon. The ruling was significant because it gave the state considerable latitude to include religious and ethical content in public school curricula provided the approach was comparative and non-confessional.

**D.A.V. College v. State of Punjab AIR 1971 SC 1731** — The Court considered the status of DAV colleges receiving state aid under Article 28(3) and confirmed that the requirement of prior consent before students are required to attend religious instruction is mandatory and constitutionally enforceable. The Court held that aided institutions retain their religious character and may continue to provide religious instruction and conduct religious worship, but cannot treat participation as a condition of admission, academic standing, or continued enrollment. The judgment clarified that the consent requirement protects individual students even within institutions whose founding purpose is religious, establishing that institutional identity cannot override individual constitutional protection.

**Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala (1986) 3 SCC 615** — Three Jehovah's Witnesses children refused to sing the national anthem at their government school on religious grounds, relying on their sincere belief that singing the anthem was a form of worship inconsistent with their faith. The Supreme Court held that expelling the students for this refusal violated their fundamental rights, drawing explicitly on the non-compulsion principle underlying Article 28(3) as part of the constitutional culture that prohibits the state from coercing religious observance in educational settings. The case established that even acts of quasi-religious or patriotic observance can attract constitutional protection where the objection is based on genuinely held religious conviction, not mere refusal of discipline.

**Bal Patil v. Union of India (2005) 6 SCC 690** — The petitioners challenged the provision of government grants to institutions providing religious instruction in the Jain tradition, arguing this violated Article 27 and Article 28. The Supreme Court held that general grants-in-aid to minority educational institutions do not violate Article 28(1) provided the grants are for educational rather than specifically religious purposes, and that the consent requirement in Article 28(3) adequately protects non-Jain students who might attend aided Jain institutions. The case reinforced the distinction between an institution's religious identity — which is protected under Articles 29 and 30 — and state-funded confessional instruction, which Article 28 restrains.

**St. Xavier's College v. State of Gujarat AIR 1974 SC 1389** — The Supreme Court upheld the right of a minority-run institution to maintain its distinctive religious and educational character while acknowledging that Article 28(3) places consent-based limits on what religious observance it may require of aided students. The Court confirmed that the guarantee of institutional autonomy under Article 30 and the individual protection of Article 28(3) operate simultaneously: a minority institution may preserve its ethos, but it cannot use its Article 30 autonomy to override the consent requirement that protects students who are not members of the founding community.

**Dayanand Anglo Vedic Trust v. State of Gujarat (2016)** — A challenge to state education authority directives requiring uniformity of secular curriculum content in aided schools, including Arya Samaj schools with a longstanding tradition of Vedic instruction. The Gujarat High Court applied Article 28(3) to hold that the school could continue Vedic religious instruction as part of its institutional character provided prior consent was obtained from students and guardians, and that the state's regulatory power over curriculum did not extend to eliminating religious instruction in aided institutions that had been providing it as a central feature of their founding purpose.

## Current significance

Article 28 is at the centre of intensifying debates about the relationship between state, education, and religion across India. Government school morning assemblies that include prayers from one religious tradition, mandatory yoga sessions presented as spiritual practice, and regional-language textbooks drawing heavily on one community's scriptural tradition have all attracted Article 28(1) challenges. State governments that have introduced mandatory Sanskrit instruction or Vedic mathematics in government school curricula face arguments that the specific religious associations of those subjects bring them within the prohibition on religious instruction in fully state-maintained schools.

The question of where secular education ends and religious instruction begins has become more urgent as states with BJP governments have introduced reforms that critics characterise as Hinduising the public school curriculum. Conversely, the same doctrinal line is invoked by defenders of madrasas — state-aided institutions providing Islamic instruction — against those who argue that such aid violates the secular principle. Article 28's layered structure accommodates this diversity: madrasas receiving state aid are within clause (3)'s consent framework, not clause (1)'s absolute prohibition. But the question of whether significant state aid to an institution whose primary purpose is religious instruction eventually transforms that institution into one "maintained by the state" for Article 28(1) purposes is legally unsettled.

The introduction of the National Education Policy 2020 has generated fresh Article 28 questions. The NEP's emphasis on Indian knowledge systems — including Vedic mathematics, classical texts, and traditional practices — raises the question of how those subjects can be taught in public schools without constituting "religious instruction" within Article 28(1). The Court's approach in Aruna Roy — asking whether the treatment is academic and multi-faith rather than confessional — provides the doctrinal framework, but its application to specific syllabus content remains contested. Individual school principals and teachers are often left without clear guidance on exactly which practices are prohibited and which are permissible in the government school day.

The open constitutional question Article 28 poses is whether the consent mechanism in clause (3) provides genuinely effective protection in the context of residential schools, scholarship-dependent students, and communities where the social pressure to conform to institutional religious practice is overwhelming. Formal consent given by economically dependent or socially pressured guardians may not constitute the genuine, free consent that the Constitution requires. Whether courts should scrutinise the conditions under which consent is obtained, rather than merely its formal existence, is a question that the existing case law has not fully addressed.
