plainEnglish: >-
  Article 84 sets the positive entry requirements for parliamentary membership — citizenship with a formal oath before the Election Commission, minimum age (25 for the Lok Sabha, 30 for the Rajya Sabha), and any additional qualifications Parliament prescribes by law. These positive qualifications work alongside the disqualifications in Article 102, which bar those who otherwise qualify. Parliament has used its power under clause (c) through the Representation of the People Act 1951 to add requirements such as electoral-roll enrolment. The age differential between the two Houses reflects the framers' view of the Rajya Sabha as a more deliberative, senior chamber. Ongoing debates about educational qualifications for elected representatives — following the Supreme Court's 2015 upholding of panchayat-level educational requirements — raise the question of whether similar prescriptions for Parliament under Article 84(c) would be constitutionally valid.
officialText: |-
  84. Qualification for membership of Parliament.—A person shall not be qualified to be chosen to fill a seat in Parliament unless he— [(a) is a citizen of India, and makes and subscribes before some person authorised in that behalf by the Election Commission an oath or affirmation according to the form set out for the purpose in the Third Schedule;]

  (b) is, in the case of a seat in the Council of States, not less than thirty years of age and, in the case of a seat in the House of the People, not less than twenty-five years of age; and

  (c) possesses such other qualifications as may be prescribed in that behalf by or under any law made by Parliament.

## Why it matters

Article 84 defines the floor of eligibility for Parliament. It is the positive side of a two-sided
constitutional gate — Article 102 provides the disqualifications that close the gate on those who
would otherwise meet the Article 84 threshold. Together they establish who may sit in Parliament.
The article is constitutionally significant both for what it requires (citizenship, age, oath) and
for what it delegates to Parliament (the power to prescribe further qualifications under clause (c)),
making Parliament itself the arbiter of additional entry requirements for its own membership — a
structural self-regulation power with democratic accountability implications.

The citizenship and oath requirement under clause (a) connects the right to stand for election to
the same foundational act that connects members to the constitutional order once elected. The oath
or affirmation set out in the Third Schedule binds the candidate, at the threshold stage of
nomination, to uphold the Constitution and the law. This means the formal act of becoming
constitutionally eligible is simultaneously an act of constitutional commitment — a distinction
from ordinary citizenship rights, where no such formal act is required to exercise civil liberties.

The age thresholds in clause (b) reflect the framers' deliberate stratification of the two chambers
by experience and seniority. The Constituent Assembly's debates show that the Rajya Sabha was
conceived as a revising chamber composed of persons with substantial experience in public life — a
body that would deliberate rather than merely respond to immediate popular pressures. Raising the
entry age by five years was a structural device to make that conception real. The fact that this
stratification has persisted without challenge for over seven decades reflects both the enduring
constitutional consensus around the bicameral design and the political difficulty of amending
provisions whose basic logic continues to commend itself.

The delegation of further qualification power under clause (c) is one of the Constitution's cleaner
examples of structured self-regulation. Parliament, as the institution, holds the power to alter its
own entry requirements — subject to constitutional limits — through ordinary legislation. This
creates a dynamic constitutional provision: what counts as a "qualification" in practice can change
as Parliament legislates, making Article 84 open-ended in a way that most constitutional eligibility
provisions are not. The accountability check is electoral — if Parliament prescribes qualifications
that are seen as self-serving or exclusionary, the electorate's remedy is to change the Parliament
that made the rule.

## Reading the provision

Clause (a) pairs citizenship with a formal act — the oath or affirmation before the Election
Commission in the form prescribed by the Third Schedule. This is not merely a declaration of
identity; it is a constitutional commitment to the values of the oath. The fact that the oath must
be made and subscribed before a designated authority means that citizenship alone is insufficient
— a citizen who has not gone through the formal process cannot stand. The Election Commission's
role here is ministerial: it designates authorised persons before whom the oath is taken, but it
does not adjudicate citizenship disputes, which remain matters for the competent authority under
the Citizenship Act 1955.

Clause (b) sets the age differential — 25 for the Lok Sabha, 30 for the Rajya Sabha. The higher
threshold for the Rajya Sabha reflects the framers' conception of it as a deliberative, experienced
body; the lower threshold for the Lok Sabha reflects its character as a directly democratic
institution where fresh mandates and younger voices are less constrained. The age is calculated at
the time of nomination, not at the time of election. A candidate who is 24 at the time of nomination
but turns 25 before the election is held remains disqualified, because the constitutional threshold
applies at the moment of being "chosen," which the Election Commission rules treat as the date of
scrutiny of nominations.

Clause (c) is the delegation provision. Parliament may prescribe additional qualifications by or
under law. Under this power, the Representation of the People Act 1951 requires candidates to be
enrolled as an elector in any constituency in India — a requirement that was challenged and upheld.
Parliament could, in theory, prescribe educational qualifications, financial disclosure conditions,
or other requirements under clause (c), though constitutional limits under Articles 14 and 19 would
constrain arbitrary or disproportionate conditions. The phrase "by or under any law made by
Parliament" means that Parliament can also sub-delegate: it may empower another body (such as the
Election Commission) to prescribe additional qualifications through subordinate legislation,
provided the primary law from Parliament is in place.

The interaction between Article 84's positive qualifications and Article 102's disqualifications is
important. A person may satisfy every Article 84 requirement but still be barred by Article 102 —
for instance by holding an office of profit, being of unsound mind as determined by a competent
court, or being disqualified under any law made by Parliament on the grounds of corrupt practices
or electoral offences. The Representation of the People Act 1951, which operationalises both
Articles 84 and 102, contains the most comprehensive list of these disqualifications, including the
criminal conviction bar that the Supreme Court substantially reshaped in the Lily Thomas judgment
of 2013.

## Key cases

**Lily Thomas v. Union of India (2013)** — This is the most consequential case on parliamentary
qualification in the post-liberalisation era. The Supreme Court struck down Section 8(4) of the
Representation of the People Act 1951, which had allowed convicted members of Parliament and State
Legislatures to continue in office while their appeals were pending. The Court held that once a
member is convicted of an offence carrying a sentence of two years or more, disqualification
attaches immediately under Article 102(1)(e) read with Section 8 of the RPA — the convicted
person loses their seat without waiting for the disposal of any appeal. The Court reasoned that
Parliament cannot, through ordinary legislation, dilute the constitutional standard for
disqualification; it can only supplement it.

**Manoj Narula v. Union of India (2014)** — The Supreme Court examined whether persons against
whom criminal charges are framed (as distinct from conviction) can be appointed as Ministers.
While the judgment held that there is no constitutional bar on a chargesheeted person being a
Minister, the Court's observations about Article 75(1) and the Prime Minister's duty to advise
the President to appoint only persons of integrity are directly relevant to the Article 84 debate.
The case illustrates the distinction between the threshold of eligibility (Article 84) and the
exercise of political judgment about fitness for office — a distinction the Constitution largely
leaves to electoral accountability rather than legal disqualification.

**Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)** — Though primarily about personal liberty under
Article 21, the Supreme Court's holding that citizenship rights must be read broadly and that
restrictions on citizens' entitlements must satisfy Articles 14, 19, and 21 has influenced how
courts approach the citizenship and oath requirement under Article 84(a). Any action that strips
citizenship — and thereby parliamentary eligibility — is a severe civil consequence requiring
full procedural safeguards.

**Subramanian Swamy v. Election Commission of India (2013)** — The Supreme Court examined
Parliament's power to prescribe qualifications under Article 84(c), holding that Parliament has
wide discretion but that conditions which arbitrarily or disproportionately restrict the right to
contest elections may face constitutional scrutiny under Article 14. The right to contest an
election is a constitutional right, not a mere statutory privilege.

**Rajbala v. State of Haryana (2015)** — The Supreme Court upheld minimum educational requirements
for candidates to panchayat and municipal bodies, reasoning that the legislature could reasonably
treat basic literacy as a qualification for elected office. The judgment raised the question of
whether a similar prescription under Article 84(c) for Parliament would be upheld, a question left
open by the Court. The five-judge bench took care to distinguish the panchayat context — where
candidates deal with specific local development functions — from the parliamentary context, where
the range of subjects is vastly wider. Whether that distinction is constitutionally significant
for the validity of any future educational qualification for Parliament remains unresolved.

## Current significance

The age thresholds in Article 84 have been debated in the context of youth political participation.
India lowered the voting age to 18 by the 61st Amendment but kept the parliamentary eligibility age
at 25, creating a seven-year gap between when a citizen can vote and when they can stand for the
House they elect. Whether this gap is constitutionally or normatively coherent — and whether
reducing the Lok Sabha eligibility age to 21 or 18 would require a constitutional amendment — has
been discussed but not legislated. The Law Commission's 2015 report on electoral reforms noted the
anomaly but did not make a formal recommendation for amendment.

The Lily Thomas judgment's immediate-disqualification rule has had lasting political consequences.
Several sitting Members of Parliament have lost their seats upon conviction since 2013, and the rule
has made criminal accountability in electoral politics a constitutional rather than merely political
question. The Tenth Schedule's anti-defection mechanism — which results in disqualification decided
by the Speaker — operates as a separate disqualification track alongside Article 102, one that has
grown in importance as coalition politics and floor-crossing have remained persistent features of
Indian parliamentary life.

The power under clause (c) has also been at issue in debates about criminal antecedents. Following
a series of Supreme Court directions requiring disclosure of criminal cases by candidates (under the
Association for Democratic Reforms line of cases), the question of whether Parliament could prescribe
conviction-based bars or disclosure requirements beyond those in Article 102 under clause (c) is
constitutionally live, intersecting with the Court's supervision of electoral integrity. The Court
has repeatedly nudged Parliament to legislate more stringent disqualification rules, and Parliament's
consistent inaction on these nudges reflects the political economy of incumbency rather than any
constitutional incapacity to act.
