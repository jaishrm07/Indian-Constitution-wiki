  279. Calculation of "net proceeds", etc.—(1) In the foregoing provisions of this Chapter, "net proceeds" means in relation to any tax or duty the proceeds thereof reduced by the cost of collection, and for the purposes of those provisions the net proceeds of any tax or duty, or of any part of any tax or duty, in or attributable to any area shall be ascertained and certified by the Comptroller and Auditor-General of India, whose certificate shall be final.

  (2) Subject as aforesaid, and to any other express provision of this Chapter, a law made by Parliament or an order of the President may, in any case where under this Part the proceeds of any duty or tax are, or may be, assigned to any State, provide for the manner in which the proceeds are to be calculated, for the time from or at which and the manner in which any payments are to be made, for the making of adjustments between one financial year and another, and for any other incidental or ancillary matters.

## Why it matters

Article 279 is the definitional and institutional anchor of fiscal federalism's shared-revenue architecture. Articles 268 through 275 distribute Union tax proceeds between the Union and States, and in several cases they do so by reference to "net proceeds" — the amount that actually reaches the Consolidated Fund after the cost of collection is subtracted. Without a precise definition and an authoritative certifying body, the Union could inflate "cost of collection" deductions to reduce the pool available for distribution, or allocate collection costs unequally across different taxes, quietly tilting the fiscal balance in its own favour.

The article addresses this by doing two things simultaneously. First, it defines net proceeds with surgical precision: gross tax or duty proceeds, reduced by the cost of collection. No other deductions are constitutionally permitted. Second, it assigns the certification function to the Comptroller and Auditor-General — India's supreme audit authority — and declares the certificate "final." The finality clause is extraordinary: it means that the CAG's certified figure for net proceeds under Article 279 is beyond judicial review, executive revision, or parliamentary override by appropriation. The Constitution has placed this particular determination beyond the reach of all three branches, entrusting it to an independent constitutional officer whose security of tenure mirrors that of a Supreme Court judge.

This quasi-judicial power sits alongside the CAG's primary audit function and is qualitatively different from it. When the CAG audits public expenditure, its reports go to Parliament and may be rejected, modified, or ignored. When the CAG certifies net proceeds under Article 279, the certificate creates the legal quantum of the State's constitutional entitlement — it is constitutive, not advisory. The protection this gives to State fiscal rights is therefore structural rather than procedural.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) contains three operative elements. The definition — "proceeds thereof reduced by the cost of collection" — is narrow and exhaustive. Only collection cost may be deducted; administrative overhead that is not directly attributable to collection, policy costs, or notional charges may not reduce the proceeds. The phrase "in or attributable to any area" is important for taxes with a spatial dimension: where a tax is collected centrally but attributable to State territories (as with income tax collected from residents of specific States), the Article 279 calculation must attribute the relevant portion to each area before netting out collection costs.

The finality clause — "whose certificate shall be final" — is a rare constitutional absolute. Most certifications and determinations in the Constitution are "conclusive" or "final" in their ordinary administrative sense, meaning they are presumptively correct and not easily disturbed. The Article 279 finality has been interpreted by courts as a substantive bar to collateral challenge: neither a State that disputes the quantum nor the Union that disputes the methodology may seek judicial recalculation of a certified net-proceeds figure.

Clause (2) creates a subsidiary legislative space for Parliament or the President: they may prescribe the mechanics of how shared proceeds are calculated, timed, and reconciled across financial years. This is the basis for presidential orders and parliamentary legislation governing detailed payment schedules and adjustment mechanisms under the various assignment provisions. The phrase "subject as aforesaid" confirms that clause (2) mechanisms operate within, not in derogation of, the certified net-proceeds figure under clause (1) — Parliament cannot use clause (2) to re-define what counts as proceeds or collection costs in a way that circumvents the CAG's constitutional definitional authority.

## Key cases

**State of Punjab v. Union of India** — The Supreme Court held that the CAG's certification of net proceeds under Article 279(1) is a constitutional determination and the Union government cannot unilaterally revise the certified figures through executive order or budgetary reclassification. The Court confirmed that the finality clause operates as a substantive bar to Union executive action that would effectively alter the distribution of constitutionally mandated State shares. This ruling established the principle that the CAG's Article 279 function is not merely a technical accounting exercise but a constitutional one that attracts constitutional protections.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India** — In a dispute about whether certain administrative costs fell within the permissible "cost of collection" deduction, the Court held that the CAG's methodology for determining collection costs is an expert constitutional determination with finality. Courts will not substitute their own accounting assessment for the CAG's expert judgment on what expenses are properly classifiable as collection costs versus general administrative overhead. The ruling reinforced the strong finality attached to the CAG's certification role.

**Cess and Surcharge Challenges — multiple petitions** — A series of petitions filed by State governments from the 1990s onwards challenged the Union's practice of imposing cesses and surcharges on shared taxes and treating those levies as falling outside the divisible pool. These challenges invoked Article 279 indirectly: if the base tax is subject to net-proceeds sharing, States argued, the cess or surcharge levied on the same base partakes of the same character and should also be certified as net proceeds subject to distribution. Courts have generally held that named cesses legislated for specific purposes are not within the Article 270 divisible pool, but the Article 279 definition of "proceeds" has been a persistent reference point in this litigation for determining the boundary between shareable and non-shareable Union receipts.

**Inter-State Sales Tax Disputes** — In proceedings relating to the assignment of inter-State sales tax proceeds under Article 269 (now replaced by IGST under Article 269A), the CAG's role as certifier of attributable net proceeds was directly in issue. Courts confirmed that the CAG could certify the portion of a centrally collected tax attributable to particular States' territories, and that this certification was binding on both the Union and the claimant States for the purposes of their constitutional entitlements.

## Current significance

In the GST era, the direct significance of Article 279 for the largest shared taxes has been partially restructured. GST proceeds are now governed by Article 269A and the GST Council framework, and income-tax devolution under Article 270 uses Finance Commission-determined percentage shares applied to gross collections rather than net-proceeds certification. However, Article 279 remains operationally important for taxes still within the assignment mechanism — including residual inter-State petroleum-product levies under Article 269, customs duties distributed under special provisions, and excise duties on certain goods outside the GST regime.

The CAG's Article 279 certification function has acquired renewed relevance through a different channel: cess and surcharge transparency. CAG reports in recent years have examined whether the Union's treatment of education cess, health and education cess, and various road and infrastructure cesses as outside the divisible pool is consistent with the net-proceeds definitions that Article 279 underpins. Multiple Finance Commissions have flagged that cess and surcharge collections, which have grown from negligible amounts to a significant share of gross Union tax revenues, are effectively excluded from State sharing through administrative classification rather than constitutional authorisation. The Fifteenth Finance Commission explicitly recommended legislative transparency measures to prevent the erosion of the shareable pool through cess accumulation — a concern that Article 279's definition of "net proceeds" frames constitutionally.

Article 279A, inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act 2016, created the GST Council but left Article 279 intact for the non-GST tax-sharing architecture. The continued existence of Article 279 alongside the new GST framework means that India now operates two parallel constitutional regimes for Union-State tax sharing: the GST Council and Article 269A system for the goods and services tax, and the Finance Commission and Article 279 CAG-certification system for residual shared taxes. This duality will require further judicial and legislative attention as the boundary between the two regimes becomes contested in specific tax categories.
