plainEnglish: >-
  Article 236 defines two terms that govern the entire Chapter VI framework for subordinate courts — "district judge" and "judicial service." District judge is defined by an inclusive list of functional equivalents including sessions judges, chief presidency magistrates, additional district judges, and chief judges of small cause courts, preventing States from evading the constitutional appointment requirements by creating senior judicial posts under different names. Judicial service is defined as a service consisting exclusively of persons intended to fill civil judicial posts at or below district judge level — the "exclusively" qualifier means a mixed service containing both judicial and non-judicial officers does not qualify for constitutional protection. Together these definitions determine whether a post falls under Article 233's appointment requirements, Article 234's recruitment requirements, or Article 235's High Court control — making Article 236 the constitutional sorting mechanism for the entire landscape of civil judicial appointments in the States.
officialText: |-
  236. Interpretation.—In this Chapter—

  (a) the expression "district judge" includes judge of a city civil court, additional district judge, joint district judge, assistant district judge, chief judge of a small cause court, chief presidency magistrate, additional chief presidency magistrate, sessions judge, additional sessions judge and assistant sessions Judge;

  (b) the expression "judicial service" means a service consisting exclusively of persons intended to fill the post of district judge and other civil judicial posts inferior to the post of district judge.

## Why it matters

Definitional articles can be among the most practically consequential provisions in a constitutional chapter. Article 236 determines the threshold for the entire Chapter VI framework — whether a given post attracts the constitutional appointment consultation requirements, whether a particular service falls within High Court control, and whether a judicial officer is entitled to the constitutional protections the chapter provides. Every dispute about whether a State has properly filled a judicial post, and every challenge to a post-holder's career management, begins by establishing where their post falls relative to Article 236's definitions.

The definitions serve an anti-avoidance function. Without the inclusive definition of "district judge," a State could create senior judicial posts under new names and staff them through ordinary administrative channels without High Court consultation. Without the "exclusively" qualifier in the definition of "judicial service," States could merge judicial officers into general administrative services, removing them from the constitutional framework. Article 236 closes those potential evasion routes.

## Reading the provision

The inclusive definition of "district judge" in clause (a) is deliberately broad. It covers judges of city civil courts and small cause courts in large urban centres, additional and joint district judges who share the senior civil judicial workload, sessions judges who exercise the senior criminal court function, and presidency magistrates — the colonial-era title used in the three presidency towns of Calcutta, Bombay, and Madras. The variety of titles in the list reflects India's historically diverse court structures inherited from British India's different provinces.

The definition operates by inclusion, not exhaustion. It sets out a list of posts that "includes" these categories, meaning analogous posts created under different names may also fall within the definition if they exercise equivalent functions. This allows the constitutional framework to capture newly created posts without amendment.

The definition of "judicial service" in clause (b) uses the word "exclusively." This qualifier is the key filter. A service must consist exclusively of persons filling civil judicial posts — it cannot include both judicial and non-judicial officers. Revenue court officers, rent controllers, and other adjudicative functionaries who are part of general administrative services do not constitute a "judicial service" within Article 236, even if they exercise judicial functions. Their officers are not automatically subject to Chapter VI's protections.

The definition refers to "civil judicial posts." Criminal court posts below sessions judge level are not automatically within the definition — they may be brought within the framework through Governor notifications under Article 237 but are not covered by Article 236(b) itself. This is the gap that Article 237 was designed to fill.

## Key cases

**Chandra Mohan v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1966)** — The Supreme Court interpreted the definition of "district judge" to cover sessions judges, foreclosing attempts by States to treat sessions judge appointments as falling outside the constitutional framework. The inclusive definition captures all specified categories regardless of the precise title used.

**Satya Narayan Sharma v. State of Rajasthan (1966)** — The Rajasthan High Court applied the Article 236 definition of "judicial service" to determine whether a particular category of civil judicial officer was within the constitutional framework of High Court control. States cannot exclude posts by creating parallel service structures.

**Sushanta Kumar Bose v. High Court of Orissa (1968)** — The Orissa High Court relied on Article 236's definitions to identify which officers were covered by Articles 233 and 235. The "exclusively" qualifier means a service cannot be partly judicial and partly executive — it must consist exclusively of judicial officers to qualify for constitutional protection.

**State of Assam v. Ranga Muhammad (1967)** — The Supreme Court used the Article 236 definitions to determine the constitutional status of magistrates and their relationship to the judicial service. The Court's analysis of who falls within "judicial service" versus who requires a Governor notification under Article 237 rests directly on these definitions.

## Current significance

The definitions in Article 236 are of ongoing importance as States create new judicial posts or rename existing ones in response to changing caseloads. The question of whether a newly created post falls within the "district judge" category or within the "judicial service" below that level determines the constitutional framework for appointment — and failures to observe the correct framework render appointments void.

The emergence of specialised courts — family courts, commercial courts, motor accident claims tribunals — has created definitional questions about whether their presiding officers are within the Chapter VI judicial service. The majority view is that such posts are part of the civil judicial hierarchy and attract the chapter's framework. The Commercial Courts Act, 2015 staffs commercial court benches by designating existing district judges and additional district judges — officers already within the Article 236 definition — preserving the constitutional continuity of the framework.

Any challenge to an appointment that bypasses the Chapter VI framework begins by establishing that the relevant post falls within Article 236's definitions. This threshold showing is the constitutional starting point for virtually every dispute about the legitimacy of judicial appointments in the States. Only a court with authority to interpret the Constitution can draw that line — and in doing so, it determines whether the constitutional protections of Chapter VI apply or whether the appointment is left to ordinary executive employment discretion.
