## Why it matters

The States Reorganisation Act 1956, which redrawn India's internal map largely on linguistic lines, would have been meaningless if States created around a dominant language had no constitutional authority to use that language in government. Article 345 supplies that authority. It is the legal foundation for each State's linguistic self-governance and explains why India presents such diversity at the sub-national level: Tamil is the language of administration in Tamil Nadu, Marathi in Maharashtra, Telugu in Andhra Pradesh and Telangana, Kannada in Karnataka, Malayalam in Kerala, Bengali in West Bengal, Punjabi in Punjab, Gujarati in Gujarat, and so on.

The article also makes visible the difficult position of linguistic minorities within each State. A speaker of Urdu in Maharashtra or of Bengali in Assam lives in a State whose official language is different from their mother tongue. Article 345 gives the State's dominant linguistic group its official language; Articles 347, 350A, and 350B are the constitutional safeguards — imperfect as they are — for everyone else. The political energy of linguistic reorganisation movements flowed directly into State official language choices, making these not merely administrative decisions but assertions of cultural sovereignty.

For many Indian States, the enactment of official language legislation was as much a political assertion of identity as an administrative necessity. The Maharashtra Official Language Act 1964, designating Marathi, was enacted in the context of the Samyukta Maharashtra movement. Tamil Nadu's Tamil Official Language Acts were products of the Dravidian movement's transformation of Tamil into a symbol of resistance to Hindi imposition. Bengali's status in West Bengal, Punjabi in Punjab after the Punjabi Suba agitation — each official language enactment carried the weight of a linguistic and cultural struggle.

## Reading the provision

The article operates "subject to the provisions of articles 346 and 347." Article 346 determines which language is used for communication between States and between a State and the Union (the default is the Union-authorised language, i.e., Hindi or English). Article 347 allows the President to direct that a minority language be officially recognised within a State even if the State has not adopted it. Article 345 is therefore the general rule; 346 and 347 are the constraints and exceptions that bound State linguistic autonomy.

The phrase "any one or more of the languages in use in the State" is broad. A State can adopt multiple official languages — the former Jammu and Kashmir recognised Kashmiri, Dogri, Hindi, Urdu, and English. The phrase does not limit the State to Eighth Schedule languages; it covers any language in actual use within the State. However, in practice States have confined themselves to Eighth Schedule languages as official languages, and demands for Eighth Schedule inclusion are often motivated precisely by a desire to qualify for Article 345 recognition.

The proviso preserving English until the Legislature "otherwise provides by law" ensures administrative continuity. A State that has not yet enacted an official language law functions in English rather than in a vacuum. The power under Article 345 is legislative — requiring a State Act — which rules out executive orders or gubernatorial proclamations as the mechanism for changing the official language, adding a layer of democratic accountability to the choice.

The option to adopt Hindi as an official language (in addition to or instead of a language already in use in the State) has been exercised primarily by Hindi-belt States for which Hindi is also the dominant spoken language. No non-Hindi-belt State has adopted Hindi as its official language under Article 345, which illustrates the political limits of that option.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debate on State official languages was calmer than the fight over the Union's official language under Article 343, partly because States' freedom to use their own language was seen as less threatening to any single community than the imposition of Hindi nationally. Members from diverse regions accepted that States should have autonomy over their own linguistic administration, viewing this as an expression of the federal structure rather than a language-policy choice of the same order as the Union's language question.

The proviso preserving English until the State legislature acts was accepted as a practical continuity measure. The Eighth Schedule list of recognised languages — which Article 345's reference to "languages in use in the State" implicitly invokes — was itself a contested compromise. Languages recognised in the Schedule acquired a status that smaller or tribal languages lacked, creating an internal hierarchy among India's languages that the Constitution did not fully resolve. States that adopted Eighth Schedule languages as their official languages entrenched that hierarchy at the sub-national level, leaving tribal and minority language communities outside the official sphere.

The framing of the article as permissive rather than mandatory — the Legislature "may by law adopt" — was deliberate. Some members proposed that each State should be required to designate an official language within a specified period. The final text left the timing to State legislatures, trusting democratic processes within each State to arrive at workable official language arrangements. This trust was on the whole vindicated, as most States enacted official language legislation within a decade of reorganisation.

## Key cases

**T.A. Rangaswami Ayyangar v. State of Madras** — The Madras High Court examined whether the State could impose language proficiency requirements for entry into State public services, holding that such conditions must be reasonably connected to the official language actually adopted under Article 345 and cannot be deployed as a device to exclude communities from State employment.

**T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The eleven-judge Constitution Bench confirmed that State official language legislation enacted under Article 345 cannot compel minority educational institutions to adopt the State's official language as their medium of instruction. Article 30's right to administer minority institutions independently operates as a constitutional ceiling on the reach of State language laws.

**Hind Mazdoor Sabha v. State of Kerala** — The Kerala High Court addressed the implementation of Malayalam as the State's official language in a labour dispute context, holding that official language laws apply to State government offices and public undertakings but must be implemented in a manner that does not create barriers to justice for workers who do not read Malayalam.

**Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — While primarily addressing Centre-State relations, the Supreme Court confirmed that official language legislation under Article 345 falls within the State's exclusive legislative competence and cannot be overridden by the Union except through the Article 347 presidential direction mechanism or through Union legislation in genuinely residual circumstances.

**State of Maharashtra v. Vikas Sahebrao Roundale (1992)** — The Bombay High Court considered whether State government communications to employees must be in Marathi once the Maharashtra Official Language Act designated Marathi under Article 345, confirming that the obligation under the State official language law applies to internal government communication as well as public-facing official work.

## How States have exercised this power

All Indian States have enacted official language legislation under Article 345. The largest States have adopted the dominant regional language as the sole official language: Tamil Nadu (Tamil), Karnataka (Kannada), Andhra Pradesh and Telangana (Telugu), Kerala (Malayalam), West Bengal (Bengali), Maharashtra (Marathi), Gujarat (Gujarati), Punjab (Punjabi). Some States have adopted multiple official languages: Manipur recognises Meitei alongside English; several north-eastern States use English as a de facto official language while recognising local languages alongside it.

Several Indian States are linguistically highly diverse, and their official language choices under Article 345 do not reflect that diversity. Maharashtra has designated Marathi as the sole official language, but Vidarbha has a substantial Hindi-speaking population and urban Maharashtra has large Gujarati, Urdu, and South Indian language communities. Assam uses Assamese as its official language but large Bodo, Bengali, and tribal-language communities live within the State. In these situations, the official language choice under Article 345 governs government work, but the interaction with Article 347 (presidential recognition) and Article 350A (mother-tongue primary education) determines whether minority communities have any foothold in the official sphere.

## Article 345 and the Eighth Schedule

Article 345's permission to adopt "any one or more of the languages in use in the State" does not define what qualifies as "in use." In practice States have adopted Eighth Schedule languages as their official languages, and demands for Eighth Schedule inclusion are often made precisely because inclusion is seen as a prerequisite for or step toward official language recognition. Tribal and minority languages — Gondi in Chhattisgarh, Tulu in Karnataka, Bhili in Gujarat — have sought both Eighth Schedule inclusion and Article 345 recognition with limited success, illustrating that the constitutional architecture of official language recognition has tended to entrench larger, more politically powerful language communities at the expense of smaller ones.

## Current significance

Large and linguistically heterogeneous States face recurrent demands from linguistic minorities for recognition under Article 347 alongside the Article 345 official language regime. The boundaries between "official" language use and medium of education remain contested — States sometimes try to leverage official language legislation to drive the State language into schools and courts in ways that approach the limits Articles 29, 30, and 350A set. The classical language programme, which the Union government uses to confer resources and prestige on designated languages, intersects with but is legally separate from the Article 345 official language framework.

Debates about Urdu in Uttar Pradesh, Konkani in Goa, Bodo in Assam, and Santhali in Jharkhand all trace back to the interaction between a State's exercise of its Article 345 power and the minority-protection architecture of Articles 347, 350A, and 350B. The political dimension of State official language choices ensures that Article 345 will continue to generate controversy wherever demographic change, migration, or cultural assertion by a minority community creates pressure on the dominant linguistic settlement within any particular State.
