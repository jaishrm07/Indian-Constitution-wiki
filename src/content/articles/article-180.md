  180. Power of the Deputy Speaker or other person to perform the duties of the office of, or to act as, Speaker.—(1) While the office of Speaker is vacant, the duties of the office shall be performed by the Deputy Speaker or, if the office of Deputy Speaker is also vacant, by such member of the Assembly as the Governor may appoint for the purpose.

  (2) During the absence of the Speaker from any sitting of the Assembly the Deputy Speaker or, if he is also absent, such person as may be determined by the rules of procedure of the Assembly, or, if no such person is present, such other person as may be determined by the Assembly, shall act as Speaker.

## What this Article does

Article 180 provides a constitutionally layered succession mechanism for the Speaker's functions. The article addresses two distinct scenarios: vacancy in the office (clause 1) and absence from a particular sitting (clause 2). Together they ensure the Assembly is never without a competent presiding authority, regardless of what befalls the Speaker.

The article is the State-level counterpart of Article 95, which governs the Lok Sabha's arrangements in the same circumstances. Both articles use an identical structure: designated successors (Deputy Speaker), followed by a residual mechanism (Governor appointment for vacancy; rules or Assembly choice for absence).

## Clause (1): Vacancy in the office

When the Speaker's office is vacant — whether because the Speaker has died, resigned, been removed under Article 179, or ceased to be a member — the Deputy Speaker performs the duties of the office. This is not merely "acting as Speaker" for a single sitting; the Deputy Speaker exercises the full continuing authority of the Speaker until a new election under Article 178 is held.

If both the Speaker and Deputy Speaker offices are simultaneously vacant, the Governor appoints a member of the Assembly to discharge the duties. This Governor-appointment mechanism is activated only in this narrow situation — it does not give the Governor any routine power over the presiding office. The appointed member's authority is temporary and expires upon the election of a new Speaker or Deputy Speaker.

## Clause (2): Absence from a sitting

When the Speaker is merely absent from a particular sitting — travelling, ill, or otherwise unavailable for that session — the Deputy Speaker takes the chair. If the Deputy Speaker is also absent, the rules of procedure of the Assembly designate a member to act as Speaker for that sitting. If no such person is present or rules make no provision, the Assembly itself determines who acts.

The key distinction between clauses (1) and (2) is the scope of authority: a person "acting as Speaker" under clause (2) exercises the Speaker's powers only for that particular sitting. Once the Speaker returns, the speaker resumes. A person performing the "duties of the office" under clause (1) has a continuing authority that spans multiple sittings and covers all the Speaker's functions, including the Tenth Schedule disqualification power.

## The Governor's appointment power under clause (1)

The Governor's residual power to appoint a member when both offices are vacant requires careful constitutional reading. This power is exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers — it is not a discretionary gubernatorial act. The Governor cannot use this appointment to install a political ally or to affect the composition of the presiding office for partisan purposes. The person so appointed exercises the Speaker's constitutional functions including, controversially, the Tenth Schedule disqualification power — a situation that has given rise to litigation about whether a Governor-appointed interim Speaker has the same institutional legitimacy as an elected Speaker for the purposes of the anti-defection law.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of the Deputy Speaker's powers during the Arunachal Pradesh Assembly crisis relied in part on the Article 180 framework, noting that a Deputy Speaker discharging the Speaker's duties in a vacancy situation does not acquire any greater powers than the Speaker would have had, including in relation to disqualification proceedings where the Speaker's authority was itself contested. The Court confirmed that derivative authority is limited to what the original office-holder could have done.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — When the Maharashtra Speaker resigned mid-crisis, questions arose about the Acting Speaker's powers under the equivalent of Article 180(2). The Court's analysis clarified that an Acting Speaker's decisions on routine proceedings are valid but that decisive constitutional actions — such as disqualification orders or decisions about the floor test — require scrutiny of whether the acting authority was properly constituted and authorised.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v The Hon'ble Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — The Constitution Bench's analysis of the Speaker's obligations under the Tenth Schedule applies by extension to any person performing the Speaker's duties under Article 180: the obligation to decide disqualification petitions without unreasonable delay attaches to the office, not to the particular individual, and whoever holds the Article 180 authority bears the same constitutional obligation.

## Current significance

Article 180 becomes operationally important in the recurring Indian phenomenon of political crises accompanied by Speaker vacancies or strategic resignations. When Speakers resign at moments of constitutional sensitivity — during floor-test disputes, disqualification proceedings, or session-summoning controversies — the cascade of authority under Article 180 determines who makes potentially decisive constitutional decisions. Courts have needed to clarify repeatedly that persons acting under Article 180 exercise derived and limited powers, cannot exceed what the elected Speaker could have done, and are subject to the same constitutional constraints and judicial review as a substantive Speaker. The article thus functions as both a continuity mechanism and a constraint on the expansion of executive influence over House proceedings during leadership vacuums.
