  296. Property accruing by escheat or lapse or as bona vacantia.— Subject as hereinafter provided, any property in the territory of India which, if this Constitution had not come into operation, would have accrued to His Majesty or, as the case may be, to the Ruler of an Indian State by escheat or lapse, or as bona vacantia for want of a rightful owner, shall, if it is property situate in a State, vest in such State, and shall, in any other case, vest in the Union:

## Why it matters

Under the common law and the personal law of Indian princes, property without an owner or heir reverted to the sovereign — in British India to the Crown, in the princely states to the Ruler personally. When India abolished the monarchy and the privy purse arrangement, a question arose about this residuary ownership: who inherits property that has no private claimant? Article 296 provides the constitutional answer, ensuring that there is no gap in the ultimate ownership of ownerless property in the Indian Republic. Without it, the demise of the Crown could have been argued to extinguish the State's residuary ownership claim entirely, requiring fresh legislation in every State to recreate the escheat entitlement.

## Reading the provision

Article 296 converts the common law doctrines of escheat, lapse, and bona vacantia from a royal prerogative into a republican public entitlement. Under pre-Constitution law, property without a legal heir or owner accrued to the Crown — in British India, to the Crown in right of the government; in the princely states, to the Ruler personally. Article 296 replaces the Crown or Ruler as the ultimate owner with the State or the Union: property situated within a State vests in that State; property not situated in any State (for example, property in the continental shelf or in Union territories without State status) vests in the Union. The proviso adjusts this rule where the property at the time of accrual was already in possession or control of the Government of India or a State government — in which case the purposes test from Article 295 applies, vesting property used for Union purposes in the Union and property used for State purposes in the State.

## Escheat, lapse, and bona vacantia — the doctrinal background

Escheat traditionally refers to the reversion of property to the State when a person dies intestate without any surviving heirs. Lapse was a doctrine applied particularly in the princely states by the British, under which a state "lapsed" to British control when its ruler died without a natural heir and the British declined to recognise an adopted heir — a policy famously used by Lord Dalhousie in the 1840s-50s. Bona vacantia ("ownerless goods") is the broader concept covering property that has no ascertainable owner due to dissolution, abandonment, or the complete failure of succession.

Article 296 does not alter the substantive conditions under which escheat, lapse, or bona vacantia arise — those continue to be governed by the Hindu Succession Act, the Indian Succession Act, the Companies Act (for dissolved companies), and other applicable laws. What the article does is specify the governmental recipient: State government for property within a State, Union for property elsewhere.

## Key cases

**State of Bihar v. Radha Krishna Singh (1983)** — The Supreme Court considered the application of escheat law to ancestral property in Bihar, applying Article 296's rule that property within a State vests in the State upon escheat. The judgment discussed the relationship between Article 296 and the State's escheat legislation, confirming that the constitutional provision sets the beneficiary but leaves the trigger conditions to the applicable succession law.

**Controller of Estate Duty v. State of Kerala (1961)** — Litigation involving the treatment of escheated estates for estate duty purposes raised the question of whether the State's acquisition of property under Article 296 was an acquisition for public purposes and how it interacted with the estate duty liability of the deceased's estate.

**Union of India v. Krishnamurthy (1972)** — A case concerning the dissolution of a company incorporated in British India whose assets had not been distributed, raising the question of whether the bona vacantia provision in Article 296 vested the company's property in the State where it was situated or in the Union as the successor to the Crown in right of the Dominion government.

## Bona vacantia and dissolved companies

A significant contemporary application of the bona vacantia doctrine under Article 296 arises when companies are dissolved under the Companies Act without having distributed all their assets. Upon dissolution, a company ceases to exist and its remaining property passes to the Crown or its successor under bona vacantia. Under Article 296, this means that property of a dissolved company situated in a State passes to the State government, while property not in any State passes to the Union. The Ministry of Corporate Affairs and State governments have published guidelines for handling such properties, and the Registrar of Companies routinely deals with claims by State governments over the assets of struck-off or dissolved companies under this framework.

## Current significance

Article 296 has continuing practical relevance in several contexts. First, intestate succession without heirs remains a regular occurrence; State governments' revenue departments regularly handle escheat proceedings for agricultural land, urban plots, and immovable property where the succession has failed completely. Second, the dissolution of companies — particularly shell companies struck off under the Ministry of Corporate Affairs' clean-up drives — generates bona vacantia claims over bank accounts, immovable property, and other assets left in the dissolved company's name. Third, the article has constitutional importance in confirming that the abolition of the monarchy did not leave a gap in the ultimate ownership of ownerless property — the republican successor governments seamlessly inherited the Crown's position as the ultimate residuary owner of property without a private claimant.
