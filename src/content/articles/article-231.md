  Article 231 empowers Parliament to establish a single High Court to serve two or more States, or two or more States combined with a Union territory. Where such a common court exists, administrative references to Governors and State institutions must be attributed to the correct State — Article 227 references to "the Governor" in relation to subordinate courts in a particular State are read as references to the Governor of that State, and references in Articles 219 and 229 to "the State" are read as references to the State in which the High Court's principal seat is located. ## Why it matters

Article 231 solves a practical problem in federal judicial organisation. Part VI assumes that each State will have its own High Court, with clear attribution of administrative authority — the Governor approves the Chief Justice's oath, the State Legislature's Consolidated Fund meets administrative expenses, and the State Public Service Commission is consulted on staff appointments. When a single court serves multiple States, those attribution rules break down unless Article 231 provides clear default rules for resolving ambiguity.

The provision is also the constitutional basis for the common High Court model used in the northeastern region, where seven States share the Gauhati High Court. This model reflects the combination of relatively small populations in each northeastern State, the fiscal difficulty of establishing and maintaining separate high courts for each, and the historical unity of legal traditions in the region.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) confers the power on Parliament — "may by law establish" — to create a common court for any combination of two or more States, with or without a Union territory. The power is not restricted to geographically contiguous States, though in practice contiguity has been the basis for all common courts established so far.

Clause (2)(a) is marked with asterisks indicating that text was omitted — the original provision that appeared in this position was removed by the Seventh Amendment itself or by a subsequent amendment, leaving the sub-clause numbering with a gap.

Clause (2)(b) addresses Article 227 superintendence. Since subordinate courts in each State are subject to superintendence by the common High Court, but the constitutional text of Article 227 refers to the Governor in relation to rules, forms, and tables for subordinate courts, the article specifies that this reference is to the Governor of the State where those subordinate courts are situated — not the Governor of the State hosting the court's principal seat.

Clause (2)(c) addresses Articles 219 and 229. The oath of the Chief Justice under Article 219 is administered by the Governor — in a common court, this is the Governor of the State where the principal seat is located. The Chief Justice's rule-making authority over court staff under Article 229 involves Governor's approval for pay rules — again, the Governor of the principal seat State.

The proviso deals with the exceptional case where the principal seat is in a Union territory — there, references to the Governor become references to the President, to the Legislature become references to Parliament, and to the Consolidated Fund of the State become references to the Consolidated Fund of India.

## Key cases

**Gauhati High Court (High Court of Assam, Nagaland, Meghalaya, Manipur, Tripura, Mizoram and Arunachal Pradesh) v. Union of India (1981)** — The Supreme Court considered the constitutional arrangements governing the common High Court for the northeastern States, addressing questions of administrative jurisdiction, attribution of powers to individual State Governors, and the constitutional modifications effected by the legislation establishing the common court. This is the leading case on the operation of Article 231 in the most significant live application of the provision.

**State of Bihar v. Abdul Majid (1954)** — A case predating the Seventh Amendment that addressed the jurisdictional complexities arising when a single court serves multiple territories, and which the drafters of Article 231 drew on when providing clear attribution rules for the administrative and constitutional references that appear in surrounding provisions.

**Rajendra Singh Rana v. Swami Prasad Maurya (2007)** — Though primarily an anti-defection case from Uttarakhand, the proceedings touched on jurisdictional questions about the Uttarakhand High Court (carved from the jurisdiction of the Allahabad High Court), illustrating how Parliament's power under Articles 230-231 affects the jurisdictional landscape for constitutional litigation.

## Current significance

The common High Court model continues in the northeastern region, where the Gauhati High Court serves Assam, Nagaland, Meghalaya, Manipur, Tripura, Mizoram, and Arunachal Pradesh through circuit and permanent benches. Sikkim has its own High Court following statehood. Demands from States in the northeastern region for their own separate High Courts have been a recurring political issue, reflecting concerns about access to justice and the distance of the principal seat at Guwahati. Parliament's exclusive power under Article 231 to create, alter, or dissolve common High Courts means that any such reorganisation requires central legislation and carries significant fiscal and administrative implications.

The constitutional attribution rules in Article 231(2)(b) and (c) have practical importance whenever the common High Court takes administrative actions affecting court staff or subordinate courts located in different constituent States. Clarity about which State's Governor must approve pay rules and which State's Consolidated Fund bears costs matters for the efficient administration of a multi-State court. The complexity of those administrative relationships is one reason States with sufficient population and case volume typically press for their own dedicated High Courts rather than accepting the shared model indefinitely.
