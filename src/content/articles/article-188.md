## What this Article does

Article 188 requires every member of a State Legislature — whether Assembly or Council — to take an oath or affirmation in the prescribed form before taking their seat. The oath, set out in the Third Schedule, commits the member to bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India and to faithfully discharge their duties. Administration of the oath before the Governor, or an authorised person, gives the act a formal constitutional character and marks the moment at which membership rights and obligations begin.

The requirement is not merely ceremonial. A member who sits or votes before complying with Article 188 is subject to the penalty prescribed by Article 193 — a financial penalty for each day of unauthorized sitting or voting, recoverable as a debt due to the state. This creates a direct enforcement mechanism for the oath requirement.

## The Third Schedule form and its significance

The Third Schedule specifies the exact form of the oath or affirmation for members of State Legislatures. The member swears or solemnly affirms to bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India as by law established, to uphold the sovereignty and integrity of India, and to faithfully discharge the duty upon which they are about to enter. The option to affirm rather than swear was included to respect the religious diversity of India and to accommodate members for whom swearing on a religious text would be either inaccessible or objectionable.

The Constituent Assembly debated the form of the oath carefully, choosing language that emphasises constitutional allegiance over allegiance to any particular party, programme, or leader. This makes the Article 188 oath substantive rather than merely procedural: it is an undertaking of constitutional fidelity that persists through the member's term and that shapes the interpretation of their subsequent obligations, including anti-defection duties under the Tenth Schedule.

## Key cases

**Article 193 penalty proceedings** — High Courts in several states have addressed the consequences of technical non-compliance with the Article 188 oath requirement, consistently holding that the penalty under Article 193 is civil rather than criminal in nature and that courts have jurisdiction to recover it as a debt, which presupposes that the Article 188 requirement is a justiciable precondition to lawful participation in legislative proceedings.

**Constituent Assembly Debates (1949)** — The framers explicitly chose the Third Schedule form of oath to include a specific commitment to the Constitution, distinguishing it from looser forms of parliamentary pledge used in some provincial legislatures under the Government of India Act 1935. This history is relevant to the interpretation of Article 188: the oath is substantive, not merely procedural, and a member who takes it while intending to act against the Constitution's basic commitments may be seen as in constructive breach.

**Kihoto Hollohan v Zachillhu (1992)** — The Constitution Bench noted that the oath taken under Article 188 creates a constitutional bond between the member and the Constitution that is separate from, but reinforces, the anti-defection obligations in the Tenth Schedule. A member's duty to vote in accordance with the party whip must be assessed against the overriding obligation of constitutional fidelity undertaken in the Article 188 oath.

## Current significance

The Article 188 oath has become relevant in contemporary anti-defection jurisprudence: members who take the oath of allegiance to the Constitution and then allegedly act on instructions from party leadership against their constituent duties have been examined under the Tenth Schedule. Courts have noted that the oath taken under Article 188 to uphold the Constitution must be read alongside the anti-defection obligations in the Tenth Schedule, and that these together define the full constitutional commitment of a member of a State Legislature.

Questions about whether deliberate breach of the Article 188 oath — for example, by voting knowingly against a constitutional provision at party direction — could have independent legal consequences beyond the Tenth Schedule procedure have been raised in academic commentary. While courts have not yet imposed consequences distinct from the disqualification process, the debate signals the growing recognition that the Article 188 oath is not a mere formality but a substantive constitutional undertaking with potential justiciable dimensions.

The parallel provision for Members of Parliament is Article 99, which similarly requires an oath before a Member can take their seat in either House of Parliament. The structural identity between Articles 99 and 188 reflects the Constitution's consistent commitment to oath-taking as a condition precedent to the exercise of legislative authority at every level of the federal structure. Both provisions trace the same Third Schedule form, ensuring that every elected legislator in India — from a Member of the Lok Sabha to a member of a State Legislative Assembly — undertakes the same fundamental constitutional commitment.

The administration of the oath before the Governor — or an authorised person — rather than before the Chief Justice or another judicial officer, reflects the constitutional position of the legislature as an institution separate from the judiciary. The Governor is the constitutional head of the State government, and the presentation of the oath before the Governor marks the integration of the newly elected member into the constitutional framework of State governance. The Governor's role is formal and mandatory: there is no discretion in the administration, and no refusal to administer the oath to a duly elected member would be constitutionally tenable.

The article reflects the broader constitutional architecture of Part VI, which mirrors the Union structure to ensure that state legislative institutions operate under a framework of comparable constitutional dignity and constraint to their parliamentary counterparts.
