## Why it matters

Article 63 is a single declaratory sentence, but its constitutional significance cannot be measured by its length. By placing the creation of the Vice-Presidency in the Constitution rather than in ordinary statute, the framers achieved three things simultaneously: they made the office permanent (subject only to constitutional amendment); they signalled that its powers and election procedure must also be in the Constitution; and they established a constitutional succession mechanism that could not be altered by parliamentary whim.

The structural importance of this declaration is understood by comparing it to what would have existed without it. In the absence of Article 63, Parliament could create a Vice-President by statute, modify the office at any time, or abolish it entirely by simple majority. The succession vacuum that would follow any presidential vacancy would have to be filled by whatever Parliament had arranged. By constitutionalising the office, the framers ensured that India always has a designated constitutional successor to the President — a person whose authority, powers, and method of appointment are all determined by the Constitution itself, not by the political preferences of the government of the day.

The Vice-Presidency in India serves two distinct constitutional functions that are not usually combined in a single office. The primary day-to-day function is serving as the ex officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha under Article 64 — a legislative leadership role with significant procedural authority over the conduct of the upper House of Parliament. The secondary but constitutionally weightier function is serving as the designated presidential successor under Article 65 — stepping in when the President is unable to act, whether due to death, resignation, removal, or temporary incapacity. No other constitutional democracy combines the Senate Presidency with executive succession in quite the same way as the Indian Vice-Presidency, though the analogy with the United States Vice-President as Senate President is the closest comparative model.

The framers' choice of this dual design reflects a practical judgment about constitutional efficiency. Rather than creating two separate offices — a Rajya Sabha Chairman elected by that House and a presidential successor designated separately — they combined both functions in a single institution, elected by the full Parliament. This gives the Vice-President a national parliamentary mandate (from both Houses) while simultaneously providing the Rajya Sabha with a presiding officer whose authority derives from a broader electoral base than the House's own members.

## Reading the provision

The brevity of Article 63 is a drafting technique. The provision creates the office; the succeeding articles provide its content. This two-step structure — bare creation followed by elaboration — mirrors Article 52 (President declared) followed by Articles 53–61 (powers, election, term, qualification, conditions, oath). The constitutional architecture of Part V is built on this pattern of declaratory creation followed by operational detail.

The word "shall" in "there shall be a Vice-President" is mandatory. It does not merely permit the existence of the office — it requires it. At every moment in India's constitutional life, there must be a Vice-President in office. If the Vice-President's office falls vacant because the incumbent steps up to act as President under Article 65(1), Article 68 immediately requires an election to fill that vacancy "as soon as possible." The mandatory character of Article 63 thus generates a continuous obligation of constitutional maintenance — the office cannot be allowed to remain vacant for any extended period.

The implication of this mandatory character is that Parliament cannot — by not holding Vice-Presidential elections, by defunding the office, or by any other statutory mechanism — allow the Vice-Presidency to wither out of existence. Any attempt to do so would contradict the constitutional command of Article 63. The office must exist, must be filled, and must function. Courts would have jurisdiction to enforce this obligation if Parliament were to default on holding Vice-Presidential elections within the timeframes set by Article 68.

## The Vice-President as Rajya Sabha Chairman

The most significant constitutional function of the Vice-President flows from Article 64: the Vice-President is the ex officio Chairman of the Council of States. This dual role — constitutional successor to the President and presiding officer of the upper House — makes the Vice-Presidency unique among constitutional offices. No other officer in the Union government simultaneously carries succession authority for the executive and procedural authority for the legislature.

As Chairman of the Rajya Sabha, the Vice-President exercises sweeping procedural powers: deciding points of order, maintaining order in the chamber, recognising speakers, authenticating Bills passed by the Rajya Sabha, and ruling on procedural questions. The Chairman's rulings are not subject to appeal within the House and can only be reversed by a resolution of the Rajya Sabha itself. This authority has given Vice-Presidents and the occupants of the chairmanship significant influence over the legislative process, particularly during periods when the government lacks a majority in the upper House and Opposition blocking strategies are most likely.

The relationship between the Article 63 office and the Article 64 chairmanship is constitutionally inseparable: every Vice-President is automatically the Rajya Sabha Chairman, without any additional election or confirmation by the House. This ex officio character distinguishes the Chairman's authority from that of a Speaker elected under Article 93: while the Speaker is formally chosen by the House, the Chairman's authority derives directly from the constitutional office created by Article 63 and given function by Article 64.

## The Vice-President as presidential successor

Article 65 provides that the Vice-President shall act as President when a vacancy arises in the presidential office, or when the President is unable to discharge functions due to absence, illness, or any other cause. When acting as President, the Vice-President exercises all presidential powers under the Article 60 oath — a fresh oath that must be taken before the Chief Justice of India, going beyond the Article 69 oath administered before the President on entering the Vice-Presidency. Critically, the Vice-President while acting as President does not simultaneously perform the duties of the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha — those duties pass to the Deputy Chairman under Article 91.

This separation ensures that the constitutional functions of both the Presidency and the Rajya Sabha chairmanship are fully covered even when a single person is constitutionally required to temporarily occupy the presidential role. The interlocking design of Articles 63, 64, 65, and 91 reflects careful constitutional planning for governance continuity.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court's comprehensive examination of Part V treated Articles 63–69 as a coherent cluster defining the Vice-Presidential office's nature. The Court observed that the Vice-President, like the President, is a constitutional head who exercises powers within constitutional constraints, and that the bare existence of the office mandated by Article 63 carries the full constitutional weight elaborated in the succeeding provisions.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — Though directly concerning a State legislative speaker, the Constitution Bench's analysis of the separation between the presiding officer's personal interests and institutional duties engaged with the broader constitutional design of presiding offices, including the Vice-President's role as Chairman of the Rajya Sabha under Articles 63–64. The Court's observations on the institutional independence of presiding officers have implications for the Vice-President's chairmanship function.

**In re Presidential Poll (1974)** — The Court's discussion of the electoral college under Articles 54–55 touched on the Vice-President's role in the succession chain, confirming that Article 63's creation of the office is the foundational provision for the entire set of succession and electoral provisions that follow in Articles 64–69.

**Subhash C. Agarwal v Union of India (2019)** — The Supreme Court's RTI ruling on constitutional offices confirmed that the Vice-President, in the capacity of Rajya Sabha Chairman under Article 64, functions as a public authority and is subject to the transparency obligations of the Right to Information Act. The Court's analysis of the Vice-Presidential office's constitutional derivation from Articles 63–64 placed it clearly within the category of constitutional institutions carrying public-accountability obligations.

## Current significance

The single-sentence structure of Article 63 has been invoked in academic debate about whether Parliament could, by statute, create deputy or acting Vice-Presidential positions, or whether Article 70's contingency power is the only route for addressing situations where both the President and Vice-President are unavailable. The consensus constitutional view is that any statutory creation of a Vice-Presidential substitute without a corresponding constitutional amendment would conflict with the mandatory character of Article 63's requirement that there shall be a Vice-President.

The Vice-President's role as Rajya Sabha Chairman has made the position politically significant during periods of sharp divisions between the ruling coalition and the Opposition over upper-house procedure. The election of Jagdeep Dhankhar as Vice-President in 2022 and his subsequent conduct of Rajya Sabha proceedings generated constitutional debate about the independence of the Chair from the executive, the scope of the Chairman's powers to suspend opposition members, and the standard of review available for Chairman's procedural rulings. These debates trace directly to the constitutional architecture built on Article 63's creation of the Vice-Presidency as a single institution combining executive succession and legislative presiding authority.
