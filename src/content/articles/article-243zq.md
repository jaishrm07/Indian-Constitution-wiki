officialText: |-
  243ZQ. Offences and penalties.—(1) The Legislature of a State may, by law, make provisions for the offences relating to the co-operative societies and penalties for such offences.

  (2) A law made by the Legislature of a State under clause (1) shall include the commission of the following act or omission as offences, namely:—

  (a) a co-operative society or an officer or member thereof wilfully makes a false return or furnishes false information, or any person wilfully not furnishes any information required from him by a person authorised in this behalf under the provisions of the State Act;

  (b) any person wilfully or without any reasonable excuse disobeys any summons, requisition or lawful written order issued under the provisions of the State Act;

  (c) any employer who, without sufficient cause, fails to pay to a co-operative society amount deducted by him from its employee within a period of fourteen days from the date on which such deduction is made;

  (d) any officer or custodian who wilfully fails to handover custody of books, accounts, documents, records, cash, security and other property belonging to a co-operative society of which he is an officer or custodian, to an authorised person; and

  (e) whoever, before, during or after the election of members of the board or office bearers, adopts any corrupt practice.

## Why it matters

Constitutional provisions for co-operative governance are effective only if backed by enforceable sanctions. Article 243ZQ creates a constitutional mandate for State legislatures to make the enumerated forms of co-operative misconduct into criminal offences. This is unusual: the Constitution rarely specifies categories of conduct that States must criminalise. The provision reflects the 97th Amendment's recognition that previous co-operative legislation had weak or inconsistently applied penal provisions, and that governance reform required not merely structural changes to board composition and election procedures but enforceable accountability for specific misconduct.

The five enumerated categories address the most practically significant forms of co-operative misgovernance. False returns (Article 243ZQ(2)(a)) back up Article 243ZP's mandatory annual return requirement. Disobedience of lawful orders (Article 243ZQ(2)(b)) ensures that co-operative officers cannot simply ignore regulatory directions from the Registrar or other authorised persons. The employer wage-deduction offence (Article 243ZQ(2)(c)) addresses a specific and widespread abuse: employers who deduct amounts from their employees' wages for co-operative loan repayments or share contributions but then fail to remit those amounts to the society, effectively misappropriating the deducted sums. Failure to hand over property (Article 243ZQ(2)(d)) addresses the situation where outgoing officers or superseded board members refuse to cooperate with transition — withholding records, preventing access to bank accounts, or concealing assets. Corrupt election practices (Article 243ZQ(2)(e)) give the co-operative election framework its penal backstop.

## The employer wage-deduction provision

Clause (2)(c) deserves particular attention as the most technically specific of the enumerated offences. Many co-operative credit societies, particularly salary-based credit societies for government employees, use salary deduction at source as their primary loan repayment mechanism: the employer deducts the instalment from the employee's salary and remits it to the society. The system depends entirely on employer compliance. Where employers — often government departments or large employers — fail to remit deductions on time or at all, they effectively use society members' loan repayment amounts as interest-free short-term finance at the society's expense. Article 243ZQ(2)(c) makes this failure an offence if the remittance is not made within fourteen days of the deduction. The fourteen-day window provides a reasonable grace period for normal payment processing while creating a clear bright-line rule for enforcement.

## Corrupt election practices

The inclusion of corrupt election practices under Article 243ZQ(2)(e) is significant: it extends the constitutional commitment to clean co-operative elections (established through Article 243ZK's independent electoral authority requirement) into the penal domain. The broad temporal scope — conduct "before, during or after the election" — captures the full range of electoral misconduct: pre-election vote-buying, impersonation and booth-capturing during the election, and post-election coercion or intimidation to prevent elected members from taking office. State law must make all of these forms of conduct criminal, giving the co-operative election framework a penal backbone equivalent to the Representation of the People Act's provisions for parliamentary and State elections.

## Post-Rajendra N. Shah position

After the 2021 ruling, the obligation to enact the enumerated offences under Article 243ZQ applies fully to multi-State co-operative societies (where Parliament performs the legislative role under Article 243ZR). For State co-operative societies, Rajendra N. Shah removed the constitutional compulsion, but most State co-operative acts already contain broadly equivalent penal provisions for false returns, disobedience of regulatory orders, and electoral corruption. The constitutional specification of these offences has influenced model co-operative legislation at the national level and continues to serve as a normative reference in co-operative sector reform discussions.
