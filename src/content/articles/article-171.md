  171. Composition of the Legislative Councils.—(1) The total number of members in the Legislative Council of a State having such a Council shall not exceed [one-third] of the total number of members in the Legislative Assembly of that State:

  Provided that the total number of members in the Legislative Council of a State shall in no case be less than forty.

  (2) Until Parliament by law otherwise provides, the composition of the Legislative Council of a State shall be as provided in clause (3).

  (3) Of the total number of members of the Legislative Council of a State—

  (a) as nearly as may be, one-third shall be elected by electorates consisting of members of municipalities, district boards and such other local authorities in the State as Parliament may by law specify;

  (b) as nearly as may be, one-twelfth shall be elected by electorates consisting of persons residing in the State who have been for at least three years graduates of any university in the territory of India or have been for at least three years in possession of qualifications prescribed by or under any law made by Parliament as equivalent to that of a graduate of any such university;

  (c) as nearly as may be, one-twelfth shall be elected by electorates consisting of persons who have been for at least three years engaged in teaching in such educational institutions within the State, not lower in standard than that of a secondary school, as may be prescribed by or under any law made by Parliament;

  (d) as nearly as may be, one-third shall be elected by the members of the Legislative Assembly of the State from amongst persons who are not members of the Assembly;

  (e) the remainder shall be nominated by the Governor in accordance with the provisions of clause (5).

  (4) The members to be elected under sub-clauses (a), (b) and (c) of clause (3) shall be chosen in such territorial constituencies as may be prescribed by or under any law made by Parliament, and the elections under the said sub-clauses and under sub-clause (d) of the said clause shall be held in accordance with the system of proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote.

  (5) The members to be nominated by the Governor under sub-clause (e) of clause (3) shall consist of persons having special knowledge or practical experience in respect of such matters as the following, namely:— Literature, science, art, co-operative movement and social service.

## Why it matters

Article 171 embodies a constitutional theory of bicameralism that is distinct from the Westminster model. Where the House of Lords historically drew its membership from hereditary aristocracy and appointed clergy, the State Legislative Council under Article 171 draws from local democracy (municipal and district board representatives), educational achievement (graduates and teachers), the confidence of the Assembly itself, and executive nomination for specialist expertise. This pluralist composition was intended to produce a reviewing chamber that represents different societal constituencies from those that dominate the directly elected Assembly — not a rival democratic chamber, but a complementary one.

The provision matters because it determines whether an upper house actually fulfils its constitutional purpose. A Council dominated by the same party that controls the Assembly adds little deliberative value and may simply rubber-stamp legislation. A Council dominated by the opposition can obstruct legislation and create political deadlock, tempting the Assembly to use Article 169 to abolish the upper house. The Article 171 composition is designed to avoid both extremes by distributing representation across multiple electorates — local bodies, professional communities, the Assembly, and gubernatorial nomination — none of which maps perfectly onto the party composition of the Assembly.

The graduate and teacher constituencies under clauses (3)(b) and (3)(c) are constitutionally unusual in global comparative terms. They create electorates defined by educational and professional status rather than by geography or party. The Constituent Assembly included them in the hope that such constituencies would return members with specialist knowledge in fields such as education, public health, and science — a form of functional representation that supplements territorial representation. In practice, these constituencies are often captured by politically organised professional networks rather than genuinely independent experts, and their continued relevance is periodically debated.

The Governor's nomination power under clause (3)(e) — a quota for persons with special knowledge in literature, science, art, the cooperative movement, or social service — is the state-level analogue of the President's Rajya Sabha nominations under Article 80(3). It is intended to bring into the Legislature distinguished individuals who would not or could not stand for election. The controversy in Maharashtra, where the Governor withheld nominations for over two years contrary to Cabinet recommendations, has made this power one of the most litigated aspects of Article 171 in recent years.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Size: one-third cap and forty-member floor**

The maximum size of the Council — one-third of the Assembly's total membership — ensures that the upper house cannot approach the Assembly in size. A state with a 400-member Assembly can have at most a 133-member Council. The forty-member minimum floor means that even in states whose Assembly has fewer than 120 members, the Council must have at least forty members. The floor prevents the Council from becoming so small as to be unrepresentative or unable to conduct serious legislative business.

**Clause (2) — Parliament's power to vary composition**

Clause (2) is architecturally important: it makes the composition in clause (3) the default that applies "until Parliament by law otherwise provides." Parliament therefore has authority to restructure the Council's composition by legislation, potentially altering the fractions allocated to different electoral categories or changing the eligibility criteria for each constituency. This power has not been extensively used, but it means that the Article 171(3) composition is not immutable within the constitutional framework.

**Clause (3)(a) — Local body constituency (one-third)**

The largest single component of the Council — approximately one-third of total membership — is elected by an electorate of municipal and district board members and other local authority members specified by Parliament. This gives local elected representatives a formal channel into the State Legislature, creating a vertical link between the three tiers of government. The local body constituency has often been seen as a proxy for rural and semi-urban interests that may not be adequately represented in urban-dominated Assembly constituencies.

**Clauses (3)(b) and (3)(c) — Graduate and teacher constituencies (one-twelfth each)**

These two constituencies each account for approximately one-twelfth of Council membership. Graduate voters must have held a degree from any Indian university for at least three years; teacher voters must have been engaged in teaching in institutions not below secondary school standard for at least three years. The three-year qualification period prevents newly graduated or newly appointed individuals from immediately influencing Council composition. Elections in these constituencies use single transferable vote with proportional representation, as provided in clause (4), which may allow minority professional viewpoints within these electorates to obtain Council representation.

**Clause (3)(d) — Assembly-elected members (one-third)**

Approximately one-third of Council members are elected by the Assembly itself from among persons who are not Assembly members. This mechanism gives the Assembly a direct role in composing the Council, ensuring some degree of alignment between the two chambers' political orientations. It also provides a route for distinguished public figures to enter the Council through the Assembly's confidence without having to contest a direct election.

**Clauses (3)(e) and (5) — Governor's nominees (remainder)**

The remaining seats — typically around one-sixth of the total — are nominated by the Governor from among persons with special knowledge or practical experience in literature, science, art, the cooperative movement, or social service. These are the only fields specified in the Constitution itself. Unlike the President's Rajya Sabha nominations under Article 80, where the constitutional text does not specify how the nomination power is exercised, Article 171(5) is silent on whether the Governor acts on advice. The Supreme Court's 2023 Maharashtra ruling resolved the ambiguity by confirming that the Governor must act on Cabinet advice.

## Key cases

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1977)** — In the context of inter-governmental disputes, the Court observed that the compositional structure of State Legislative Councils under Article 171 reflects a deliberate constitutional choice about how upper-house representation is to be balanced between elected and nominated elements. Statutory interference with this balance requires clear Parliamentary authority under Article 171(2), and executive action that bypasses the compositional requirements is constitutionally impermissible.

**Biennial Election Disputes in Maharashtra and Karnataka** — Several election petitions before High Courts and the Supreme Court have addressed the computation of the "as nearly as may be" fractions in clause (3). Courts have confirmed that these are directory approximations rather than exact arithmetic requirements but that departures must be minimised and justified by electoral machinery constraints rather than partisan convenience. The fractions are to be computed on the current total number of Council members as fixed under clause (1), not on any theoretical maximum.

**Governor's Nominations — Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court, in proceedings arising from the Maharashtra political crisis, directed the Maharashtra Governor to act on the Cabinet's recommendation of twelve nominees for the Legislative Council under Article 171(3)(e). The Governor had withheld action for over two years. The Court held that the nomination power under Article 171(3)(e) is not a discretionary personal power of the Governor but is exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers, consistent with the general constitutional principle of Cabinet responsibility established in Article 163. The judgment significantly clarified the legal framework for Governor-Cabinet disputes over Council nominations.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The broader Constitution Bench decision on the Maharashtra political crisis engaged with the Governor's relationship with the Council as a chamber of the Legislature, holding that gubernatorial action that effectively cripples the functioning of a constitutionally established legislative body — whether by withholding nominations, refusing to summon sessions, or calling floor tests without constitutional basis — is subject to judicial scrutiny under Article 226 and the supervisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Court.

## Current significance

Article 171's nomination provision has been at the centre of sustained constitutional controversy in Maharashtra, setting a clear precedent that the Governor cannot use the nomination power under clause (3)(e) as a political tool to deny the ruling government its entitled Council composition. The 2023 direction from the Supreme Court to fill the vacancies is now the leading authority on the exercise of this power and will govern future nomination disputes in all states with Councils.

The graduate and teacher constituency model faces periodic calls for reform on the grounds that low voter turnout, narrow electorates, and capture by organised political networks have made these seats unrepresentative of the broader professional community they are meant to represent. The Law Commission and various constitutional reform bodies have noted the anomaly but no amendment has been enacted. The clause (2) power of Parliament to restructure Council composition provides a legislative pathway for reform without a constitutional amendment, but political will to act has been absent.

Broader debates about the role and value of State Legislative Councils continue. States that have recently abolished their Councils (Andhra Pradesh in 2020, with restoration in 2024) and states debating creation of new ones invoke the Article 171 composition structure as a factor in the argument — whether a Council with the Article 171 composition can genuinely serve as an effective reviewing chamber or merely adds delay and cost. The outcome of this debate will shape the future of bicameralism at the State level in India.
