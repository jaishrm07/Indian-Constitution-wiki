## Why it matters

Article 218 closes a potential gap in the constitutional design. Without it,
one might argue that the removal provisions of Article 124 apply only to Supreme
Court judges, leaving the security of tenure of High Court judges to be
determined by ordinary legislation — and therefore potentially at the mercy of
a parliamentary majority. By expressly incorporating Article 124(4) and 124(5)
through the substitution mechanism, Article 218 ensures that High Court judges
enjoy the same constitutional security of tenure as the most senior judges.

The design choice to centralise the removal process at Parliament — rather than,
say, a state legislative procedure — is also significant. A state government
angered by High Court decisions cannot seek the removal of a judge through the
state assembly. Only Parliament, by special majority, in both Houses, in the
same session, can remove a High Court judge, ensuring that state-level political
pressure cannot translate into removal threats.

## Reading the provision

The substitution technique used in Article 218 is deceptively simple. Article
124(4) provides that a Supreme Court judge may be removed by the President after
an address by each House of Parliament supported by a majority of total
membership and not less than two-thirds of members present and voting, on
grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity. Article 124(5) authorises
Parliament to regulate the procedure for the presentation of such an address.
Article 218 makes both clauses applicable to High Courts by substituting "High
Court" wherever "Supreme Court" appears.

The procedural machinery is supplied by the Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968. When
a removal motion is admitted by the Speaker or the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha,
a three-member inquiry committee is constituted — consisting of a Supreme Court
judge, a Chief Justice of a High Court, and a distinguished jurist. The
committee holds an inquiry, gives the judge a hearing, and reports its findings.
Only if the committee finds proved misbehaviour or incapacity does the motion
proceed to the special majority vote in both Houses. The entire process is
deliberately demanding and time-consuming.

No High Court judge has ever been successfully removed through this procedure.
Justice Soumitra Sen of the Calcutta High Court resigned in 2011 after the
Rajya Sabha passed a removal motion — the first time either House had voted to
remove a judge — but before the Lok Sabha could vote. His resignation rendered
the motion moot, maintaining the historical record of zero completed removals.

## Key cases

**K Veeraswami v Union of India (1991)** — The Supreme Court examined whether
a sitting High Court judge could be prosecuted for criminal offences without
going through the Article 218 process. The majority held that presidential
sanction is required before a judge can be prosecuted, reasoning that the
constitutional scheme of removal under Articles 218 and 124 would be undermined
if criminal prosecution could be initiated independently, circumventing the
parliamentary removal mechanism.

**Sub-Committee on Judicial Accountability v Union of India (1991)** — The Court
held that the procedure for initiating removal proceedings under Article 218 is
justiciable to a limited extent. The Speaker or Chairman cannot arbitrarily
refuse to constitute an inquiry committee once a valid notice of removal has
been signed by the requisite number of members. At the same time, the Court
recognised that the process is primarily a parliamentary one.

**In Re Justice C.S. Karnan (2017)** — The Supreme Court initiated suo motu
contempt proceedings against a sitting High Court judge for making public
statements alleging corruption in the judiciary. The case exposed a significant
gap in the constitutional framework: neither the impeachment mechanism under
Article 218 nor the contempt power under Article 215 was designed to deal
efficiently with misconduct by a sitting judge who refused to participate in
proceedings. The case ended with Karnan's conviction for contempt and
imprisonment.

## Current significance

The gap between Article 218's formal but rarely-used impeachment mechanism and
the practical need for judicial accountability has produced two parallel —
and constitutionally problematic — responses. First, the Supreme Court's
In-House Procedure allows complaints against judges to be investigated
internally, with outcomes that may include a recommendation that the judge
resign. This procedure has no statutory basis, its proceedings are not public,
and its outcomes are not legally binding.

Second, contempt proceedings have occasionally been used as a de facto
disciplinary tool, as in the Karnan case, though contempt is not designed for
that purpose. Calls for a Judicial Accountability Commission — a less demanding
but still independent accountability mechanism — have been made in multiple Law
Commission reports. The fundamental constitutional challenge is that any such
mechanism must not compromise the Article 218 protection against executive or
legislative pressure on sitting judges.

Article 218 reflects the framers' deliberate choice to extend the highest
available institutional protection to High Court judges. The requirement that
both Houses of Parliament — not the state legislature — must vote for removal
is the most tangible expression of this intent. High Courts are simultaneously
State institutions subject to state political pressures and constitutionally
protected components of the national judicial system, and Article 218 resolves
that tension firmly in favour of judicial independence.
