## Why it matters

The pleasure doctrine is the constitutional expression of a fundamental choice made by the framers:
that the state must retain ultimate control over who serves in its public offices. An unqualified
property right in a post would make it difficult to discipline, transfer, or remove servants who
underperform or are no longer needed. Article 310 avoids this by denying any common-law contractual
entitlement to continued employment in government service. The holder of a civil post is not a tenant
who can be evicted only on prescribed grounds; he or she is a servant who continues in office at the
sovereign's will. This reflects the older prerogative position in constitutional law, inherited and
deliberately retained by the framers.

However, the framers equally recognised that naked pleasure would leave servants vulnerable to
victimisation, political manipulation, and wholesale purges whenever a new government came to power.
A civil service that held its posts entirely at pleasure would be incapable of giving independent
advice or enforcing the law impartially. Article 311 was therefore designed as the restraining
counterpart to Article 310, and the two must be read together as a constitutional bargain: the state
retains ultimate power to remove, but must do so following a mandatory procedural framework. Article
310 announces the general principle; Article 311 is the specific exception that Parliament and the
courts have spent decades defining and protecting.

The scope of Article 310 is deliberately wide. It covers defence service members, civil service
members, all-India service members (IAS, IPS, IFS), and holders of any civil post under the Union
or a State. The defence services are included by the first clause — a reflection of the unified
character of service to the Union — but they are largely excluded from Article 311's safeguards.
This asymmetry reflects the particular requirements of military discipline, where speed and hierarchy
are more important than the civilian procedural model. For armed forces personnel, Parliament has
enacted its own disciplinary codes under Entry 2 of the Union List.

The "except as expressly provided by this Constitution" saving clause in Article 310(1) is as
important as the pleasure doctrine itself. It carves out a protected zone for constitutional
functionaries whose independence requires insulation from executive will. The judges of the Supreme
Court and High Courts, the Comptroller and Auditor-General, the Chief Election Commissioner, the
Chairman and members of the UPSC, and other specified constitutional officers do not hold office at
pleasure. They have statutory fixed terms and can be removed only through constitutionally specified
processes — typically requiring parliamentary involvement or a Supreme Court inquiry. Article 310
thus creates a two-tier structure: ordinary civil servants subject to pleasure, and constitutional
office-holders insulated from it.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) is categorical and uses the word "every" — the pleasure doctrine admits no partial
application to the categories it covers. Three categories are specified for Union service: defence
service members, civil service members, all-India service members, and holders of any civil post
under the Union. For State service, the covered category is members of a civil service of a State
and holders of any civil post under a State. The use of both "member of a civil service" and "holder
of a civil post" is intentional: it ensures that both regular service appointees and ad hoc or
project-based civil post holders are brought within the doctrine, leaving no gap through which a
claim of contractual tenure could be established.

The phrase "during the pleasure of the President" (or the Governor for State services) does not mean
the President or Governor personally must issue every removal order. As a matter of constitutional
law, the President and Governor act on the aid and advice of their respective Councils of Ministers.
The pleasure doctrine therefore means that the executive government of the day retains the power to
remove civil servants, exercised through the normal machinery of government. This understanding has
significant implications: it means that removal decisions are ultimately political acts, which is
precisely why the procedural safeguards of Article 311 are constitutionally necessary.

Clause (2) carves out a narrow space for compensatory contracts. The clause applies only to persons
who are not members of a defence service, all-India service, or Union or State civil service — that
is, persons appointed to civil posts outside the regular service structure, typically specialists or
domain experts brought in on contract. Where the government needs such a person and that person would
not accept a post held entirely at pleasure, a contract may promise compensation if the post is
abolished or the person is removed for non-misconduct reasons before the agreed term ends. Two
conditions must be met: the President or Governor must consider the contract necessary to secure the
person's special qualifications, and the compensation provision must be triggered either by abolition
of the post or by removal for reasons not connected with misconduct.

This clause does not give the contractor a right to remain in office or to resist termination. It
gives only a contractual right to monetary compensation on early termination in the specified
circumstances. A contractor whose post is abolished still loses the post; the only question is
whether compensation is owed. The clause also implicitly confirms that members of the regular civil
services — IAS, IPS, IFS, and cadre services — cannot rely on this compensatory mechanism: they
hold their posts as servants at pleasure, and their security of tenure derives entirely from Article
311 and applicable service rules.

The pleasure doctrine must be exercised consistently with Article 311. A termination that is in
substance punitive — dismissal dressed as pleasure-based termination, or removal cast as an
administrative order declining to confirm a probationer — will be treated by courts as attracting
Article 311's procedural safeguards regardless of the form of the order. The courts have consistently
applied the test of substance over form: what matters is whether the termination is, in reality, a
penalty for misconduct. If it is, the pleasure doctrine cannot be invoked to bypass the inquiry
requirement.

## Key cases

**Parshotam Lal Dhingra v Union of India (1958)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench drew the
foundational distinction between reversion from a higher to a lower grade (which may not be punitive
and may not attract Article 311) and outright dismissal or removal (which always does). The Court
held that Article 310's pleasure doctrine co-exists with, and does not displace, Article 311
protections for substantively appointed servants. The defining question is whether the action casts a
stigma on the servant or is imposed as a penalty — if so, Article 311 applies regardless of how the
order is framed. This case remains the starting point for every Article 310/311 analysis.

**State of Punjab v Sukh Raj Bahadur (1968)** — The Court held that a servant's lien on a
substantive post — the right to revert to that post if a promotion is lost — is not a tenure right
that the pleasure doctrine extinguishes. The executive cannot invoke pleasure to deprive a servant of
a lien that arises from service rules lawfully made under Article 309. This decision clarifies that
Article 310 deals with tenure in the post, not with accumulated rights in the service that arise
under validly enacted rules.

**Sarvesh Kumar Awasthi v State of Uttar Pradesh (2010)** — The Court reiterated that the pleasure
doctrine is not a licence for arbitrary action. Where a termination order is shown to be a colourable
exercise of power — dismissal disguised as pleasure — courts will look through the form of the order
and apply Article 311 safeguards. The Court emphasised that the constitutional scheme treats Article
310 and Article 311 as a unified regime, not as competing provisions.

**Union of India v Tulsiram Patel (1985)** — A Constitution Bench confirmed that while Article 310
creates no contractual tenure right, it does not allow the state to bypass Article 311's mandatory
inquiry by labelling a dismissal a "pleasure" order. The inquiry can only be dispensed with under
the specific exceptions enumerated in Article 311(2). This decision settled the relationship between
the two articles as a matter of constitutional doctrine, confirming that the pleasure doctrine and
the protections of Article 311 must be reconciled, not played off against each other.

**Moti Ram Deka v General Manager, North-East Frontier Railway (1964)** — The Supreme Court held
that the termination of a railway employee whose service was at pleasure could not bypass Article 311
when the termination was, in substance, a punishment. The Court considered whether a regulation
allowing termination on notice without reasons was consistent with the constitutional requirement
of inquiry when misconduct was the real reason for the action, confirming that form-of-order
arguments cannot defeat substantive constitutional protection.

**Shyam Lal v State of Uttar Pradesh (1954)** — One of the earliest decisions on the pleasure
doctrine, in which the Court considered whether compulsory retirement was a reduction in rank or a
form of dismissal, and affirmed that Article 310 read with Article 311 required careful attention to
what the action was in substance rather than in form. This early decision established the interpretive
tradition that treats formal labels as irrelevant when substance indicates punishment.

## Current significance

The pleasure doctrine continues to generate litigation wherever the government terminates services
without holding a full departmental inquiry, claiming the order is a pleasure order and not punitive.
Courts are regularly called on to determine whether the substance of an order is punitive or
administrative. The cases most frequently arising concern: termination of probationers during or at
the end of probation (where the courts ask whether the termination was consequent on findings of
misconduct, making it punitive); compulsory retirement orders (where courts ask whether retirement
was used as a device to remove a servant on undisclosed misconduct grounds); and orders varying
terms of service in ways that effectively demote a servant without calling it a reduction in rank.

The doctrine has also drawn renewed attention in the context of Governors — who themselves hold
office at the pleasure of the President under Article 156 — and questions about whether displeasure-
based removal of a Governor must be supported by reasons. The Supreme Court in B P Singhal v Union
of India (2010) held that the pleasure doctrine for Governors is not absolute and that a removal
must be for relevant reasons, even if those reasons need not be disclosed. This reading has
implications for understanding the outer limits of Article 310 for ordinary civil servants as well.

Beyond litigation, the pleasure doctrine remains relevant to the ongoing debate about civil service
reform. Advocates of greater security of tenure argue that the pleasure doctrine, even as constrained
by Article 311, creates incentives for compliance with political direction rather than independent
decision-making. Reform proposals have included fixed tenures for senior civil servants (partially
addressed for IAS and IPS officers through the 2016 amendments to service rules), independent boards
to assign postings and transfers, and greater transparency in disciplinary proceedings. All of these
proposals ultimately bump against the constitutional baseline that Article 310 establishes: the
state, acting through the executive, holds the power to terminate services, and the question is only
how much procedural constraint that power must face before being exercised.
