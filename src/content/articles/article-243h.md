## Why it matters

Article 243H is the constitutional foundation for Panchayat fiscal autonomy. Without independent financial resources, a local government cannot perform the functions assigned to it under Article 243G and the Eleventh Schedule: it becomes merely an implementing agency for state programmes rather than a self-governing institution. The 73rd Amendment's vision of genuine self-government at the village level depends critically on whether Panchayats have adequate and predictable funding. Article 243H creates four distinct channels: own-source revenues through levy power, assigned revenues through tax-sharing, grants-in-aid, and the structural mechanism of a dedicated Panchayat Fund.

The permissive character of the provision — "the Legislature of a State may, by law" — is its defining feature and its central weakness. Unlike the mandatory three-tier structure of Article 243B, the fiscal framework of Article 243H imposes no minimum obligation on states. A state may choose to give Panchayats virtually no independent revenue base. In practice, most Indian states have given Panchayats very limited taxing powers (typically property taxes, water rates, and licence fees), assigned modest shares of state taxes, and funded local bodies primarily through conditional grants tied to specific central and state schemes. This grant-dependency has meant that Panchayats often function as extensions of state administrative machinery rather than autonomous local governments.

## How courts use it

Courts have generally been reluctant to enforce Article 243H positively — that is, to require states to devolve specific amounts or types of revenue to Panchayats. The permissive language does not lend itself to a justiciable floor. However, courts have intervened on two related grounds. First, where state law grants Panchayats taxing power under Article 243H(a) but executive action or departmental instructions block the exercise of that power, courts have held that the legislative grant of authority cannot be nullified by administrative inaction. Second, courts have used the overall scheme of Part IX — of which Article 243H is a part — to test whether state actions that centrally control Panchayat finances, or absorb Panchayat revenues into state accounts without redistribution, are inconsistent with the constitutional design.

The State Finance Commission mechanism under Article 243I operates in tandem with Article 243H. The Commission reviews Panchayat finances and makes recommendations on the principles for distributing state revenues to Panchayats. Courts have held that the Governor's obligation to constitute the Finance Commission periodically is mandatory, and that failure to do so is a constitutional default.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Committee for Protection of Democratic Rights (2010)** — While primarily addressing the powers of the CBI in state matters, the Court's observations about the constitutional status of Panchayats as a third tier of government reinforced the principle that Part IX institutions, including their financial architecture, are not mere statutory creatures but have constitutional standing. The judgment contextualised Article 243H within the broader picture of Panchayati Raj as a constitutional mandate.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** — In this OBC reservation case, the Supreme Court discussed the overall scheme of Part IX, noting that the constitutional design requires both political representation (addressed by Articles 243C and 243D) and functional and financial empowerment (addressed by Articles 243G and 243H). The Court emphasised that the 73rd Amendment's objectives cannot be achieved through political representation alone without corresponding devolution of functions and finances.

**Swaraj Abhiyan v. Union of India (2016)** — In the context of drought relief and rural distress, the Supreme Court critically examined the adequacy of funds transferred to local bodies for implementing rural welfare programmes, engaging with the broader framework of Panchayat finances under Part IX. The case highlighted the gap between the constitutional design of fiscal devolution and the reality of conditional central transfers that bypass Panchayat financial autonomy.

## Current significance

Fiscal devolution to Panchayats remains structurally inadequate across most Indian states. The Fifteenth Finance Commission (2021-26) noted that local governments in India receive approximately 4-5 percent of total government expenditure, far below the levels seen in comparable federal systems. The Commission recommended increasing grants to local bodies but also observed that own-source revenue generation by Panchayats is critically low, often because state governments are reluctant to allow meaningful property tax levies that might compete with state revenue interests.

The Panchayat Fund structure required by Article 243H(d) has been implemented unevenly. Several states maintain unified accounts that commingle Panchayat receipts with state funds, making it difficult for Panchayats to know or control their actual financial position. Social audit mechanisms and the public finance transparency goals of Article 243J (accounts and audit) cannot function effectively unless Panchayats have discrete, auditable financial accounts — a basic requirement that Article 243H's Fund provision was intended to ensure but has not reliably delivered.
