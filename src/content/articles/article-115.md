  115. Supplementary, additional or excess grants.—(1) The President shall—

  (a) if the amount authorised by any law made in accordance with the provisions of article 114 to be expended for a particular service for the current financial year is found to be insufficient for the purposes of that year or when a need has arisen during the current financial year for supplementary or additional expenditure upon some new service not contemplated in the annual financial statement for that year, or

  (b) if any money has been spent on any service during a financial year in excess of the amount granted for that service and for that year, cause to be laid before both the Houses of Parliament another statement showing the estimated amount of that expenditure or cause to be presented to the House of the People a demand for such excess, as the case may be.

  (2) The provisions of articles 112, 113 and 114 shall have effect in relation to any such statement and expenditure or demand and also to any law to be made authorising the appropriation of moneys out of the Consolidated Fund of India to meet such expenditure or the grant in respect of such demand as they have effect in relation to the annual financial statement and the expenditure mentioned therein or to a demand for a grant and the law to be made for the authorisation of appropriation of moneys out of the Consolidated Fund of India to meet such expenditure or grant.

## Why it matters

Article 115 closes the gap that would otherwise arise when the original Appropriation Act proves insufficient. Without a mechanism for supplementary and excess grants, the executive would face a stark choice between halting government service mid-year when funds ran out and exceeding constitutionally authorised spending without any parliamentary sanction. Article 115 provides the safety valve — but requires that any such overflow spending go through the same parliamentary scrutiny as the original budget.

The excess grant procedure is constitutionally unusual: it contemplates Parliament ratifying expenditure the executive has already incurred. The CAG's audit reports are the principal mechanism for surfacing excess expenditure, making the CAG's independence a precondition for Article 115's accountability function to operate. If excess spending is never identified or reported, the constitutional requirement to seek parliamentary sanction is effectively unenforceable.

## Reading the provision

Article 115 provides the constitutional basis for supplementary, additional, and excess grants — the mechanisms by which the government can return to Parliament during the financial year to seek authorisation for spending not covered by the original Appropriation Act. A supplementary or additional grant arises when existing allocations prove insufficient or when a new service not originally budgeted is required. An excess grant arises when money has already been spent beyond the authorised amount — a post-facto ratification by Parliament. In all three cases, clause (2) applies the full Article 112–114 procedure, meaning a supplementary statement is laid before Parliament, demands for grants are voted by Lok Sabha, and a supplementary Appropriation Act is passed.

**Three distinct instruments:**
- Supplementary grant — when an existing grant proves insufficient for the current year's purpose
- Additional grant — when a new service has arisen during the year that was not in the original estimates
- Excess grant — when money has already been spent beyond the authorised amount, requiring post-facto parliamentary sanction; this is unique in that the spending precedes the authorisation

The excess grant procedure is particularly significant constitutionally: the appropriation that gives constitutional validity to spending arrives after the fact. The Constitution treats this as permissible provided the prescribed procedure is followed, creating a temporal anomaly that the CAG's audit function is designed to manage.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor-General of India v. K. S. Jagannathan (1986)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the CAG's identification of excess expenditure triggers the Article 115(1)(b) obligation: the President must lay a demand for excess before Lok Sabha, and the executive cannot simply absorb overruns into the next year's budget without parliamentary sanction. Failure to follow this procedure renders the excess expenditure constitutionally unsanctioned.

**Union of India v. Azadi Bachao Andolan (2003)** — While principally a tax treaty case, the Supreme Court's observations on fiscal accountability noted that the supplementary grants mechanism under Article 115 is the constitutional safety valve ensuring that policy changes requiring additional funding receive Parliamentary scrutiny even when they arise mid-year.

**State of Punjab v. Ram Lubhaya Bagga (1998)** — The Court's analysis of state-level analogous provisions confirmed that the excess-grant procedure applies with equal force to the states (under Articles 205–206), and by extension affirmed the constitutional architecture of Article 115 as a universal principle of parliamentary financial control in India's federal system.

## Current significance

Supplementary demands for grants have grown substantially in recent years, with the Union government regularly presenting two or three rounds of supplementary demands during the parliamentary year. Critics argue this dilutes the original budget's significance as a comprehensive financial plan and reduces parliamentary scrutiny since supplementary demands receive far less debate time than the main budget. The CAG's reports have repeatedly flagged excess expenditure on major schemes — particularly in defence, food subsidies, and capital projects — where actual spending has significantly exceeded Appropriation Act ceilings, raising ongoing questions about whether Article 115's excess-grant mechanism is being used as intended or has become a routine post-facto legitimisation exercise.
