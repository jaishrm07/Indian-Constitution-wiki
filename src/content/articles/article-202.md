  202. Annual financial statement.—(1) The Governor shall in respect of every financial year cause to be laid before the House or Houses of the Legislature of the State a statement of the estimated receipts and expenditure of the State for that year, in this Part referred to as the "annual financial statement".

  (2) The estimates of expenditure embodied in the annual financial statement shall show separately—

  (a) the sums required to meet expenditure described by this Constitution as expenditure charged upon the Consolidated Fund of the State; and

  (b) the sums required to meet other expenditure proposed to be made from the Consolidated Fund of the State; and shall distinguish expenditure on revenue account from other expenditure.

  (3) The following expenditure shall be expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund of each State—

  (a) the emoluments and allowances of the Governor and other expenditure relating to his office;

  (b) the salaries and allowances of the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker of the Legislative Assembly and, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, also of the Chairman and the Deputy Chairman of the Legislative Council;

  (c) debt charges for which the State is liable including interest, sinking fund charges and redemption charges, and other expenditure relating to the raising of loans and the service and redemption of debt;

  (d) expenditure in respect of the salaries and allowances of Judges of any High Court;

  (e) any sums required to satisfy any judgment, decree or award of any court or arbitral tribunal;

  (f) any other expenditure declared by this Constitution, or by the Legislature of the State by law, to be so charged.

## Why it matters

Article 202 establishes the constitutional compulsion that makes State legislatures meaningful institutions of financial accountability. A State government cannot spend public money from the Consolidated Fund without an appropriation authorised by the Legislature, and no appropriation can be made without the Legislature first seeing and discussing the estimates of receipts and expenditure that the financial statement provides. Article 202 is therefore the gate through which all State public expenditure must pass — not because it directly authorises spending, but because it requires the executive to present a complete picture of projected income and outgoings before any legislative approval can be sought.

The separation of expenditure into charged and voted categories is the provision's most constitutionally significant structural feature. Charged expenditure — listed exhaustively in clause (3) — is not subject to vote. It is drawn automatically from the Consolidated Fund of the State once included in the financial statement, without the Legislature having the power to refuse or reduce it. This insulates constitutionally essential payments — judicial salaries, debt service, the costs of the Legislature's own presiding officers — from the annual appropriations battle. The protection is strongest for High Court judges' salaries: making these charged expenditure means the Legislature cannot use the budget process to threaten or pressure the judiciary by denying funds. Judicial independence is thus given financial expression through Article 202(3)(d).

Article 202 is the State-level counterpart of Article 112, which requires the President to lay an annual financial statement before Parliament. The two provisions are structurally identical — both require the statement to distinguish charged from voted items, both list the categories of charged expenditure for the respective level of government, and both trigger downstream procedures (demands for grants in Article 203/113, Appropriation Acts in Articles 204/114, and supplementary provisions in Articles 205/115). Reading Article 202 alongside Article 112 helps illuminate the federal fiscal architecture: the same constitutional disciplines that apply to Union public finance apply to State public finance, adapted for the State's scale and the Governor's role in place of the President.

The compulsory nature of the presentation — the Governor "shall" cause the statement to be laid — means that failure to present a budget at all would itself be a constitutional violation. A Governor who withholds or delays the financial statement (for example, in a situation of political dispute between the Governor and the government) would be acting in breach of Article 202's mandatory terms. The provision thus reinforces the Governor's constitutional obligation to facilitate the functioning of the Legislature, not to impede it.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — The mandatory presentation**

The Governor must cause the financial statement to be laid before the Legislature in respect of every financial year. The financial year runs from 1 April to 31 March. The statement must be laid before the House or Houses — in bicameral states, the financial statement goes to both chambers, though Money Bills (including the Appropriation Bill that gives effect to the budget) can only originate in and be ultimately passed by the Assembly. The Council may discuss the financial statement but cannot vote to reduce or refuse any demand for grants.

**Clause (2) — Charged and voted expenditure**

The financial statement must present charged and voted expenditure separately. Charged items are shown in the statement but are not voted on — they are appropriated automatically. Voted items are the subject of the demands-for-grant procedure in Article 203, through which the Assembly scrutinises and approves each ministry's spending requests. The distinction between revenue account expenditure (day-to-day government expenses — salaries, subsidies, maintenance) and other expenditure (capital formation, loan repayments, creation of assets) must also appear in the statement, enabling the Legislature to understand the fiscal character of each spending proposal.

**Clause (3)(a) — Governor's emoluments**

The Governor's emoluments and office expenses are charged. This prevents the Legislature from using the budget to economically pressure the Governor — a protection that mirrors the treatment of the President's emoluments under Article 112(3)(a) and the salaries of Supreme Court and High Court judges under various provisions. The Governor's charged status under Article 202(3)(a) means that a Legislature hostile to a particular Governor cannot deny the Governor funding.

**Clause (3)(b) — Presiding officers' salaries**

The salaries and allowances of the Speaker, Deputy Speaker, and (in bicameral states) the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Council are charged. This protects the independence of the presiding officers of both Houses from legislative interference through budget manipulation. A ruling government that has fallen out with its Speaker cannot threaten the Speaker's pay through the appropriations process.

**Clause (3)(c) — Debt service**

State debt charges — interest, sinking fund contributions, and redemption payments — are charged. This gives constitutional priority to the State's financial obligations over other expenditure, protecting the State's creditworthiness and the interests of lenders. A Legislature in political crisis cannot use the budget to default on the State's lawful debt obligations by voting to cut debt service.

**Clause (3)(d) — High Court judges' salaries**

Expenditure on the salaries and allowances of High Court judges is charged on the State's Consolidated Fund. This is the most constitutionally significant of the charged items: it insulates the financial remuneration of judges from legislative pressure or manipulation, giving the guarantee of judicial independence a concrete financial underpinning. A State Legislature cannot reduce the pay of a High Court judge it dislikes through the budget process.

**Clauses (3)(e) and (3)(f) — Court awards and additional charged items**

Sums required to satisfy court judgments, decrees, and arbitral awards are charged — the State cannot plead appropriations limitations as a reason for non-compliance with lawful court orders. Additional items may be declared charged by the Constitution or by State Legislature by law, allowing the category to expand over time through legislation rather than constitutional amendment.

## Key cases

**In re: Under Article 143 of the Constitution of India (Special Reference No. 1 of 2002)** — The Supreme Court's observations on the constitutional status of the annual financial statement as the foundational document of legislative financial control apply equally to the State framework under Article 202. The Court emphasised that the financial statement is not merely a procedural formality but a constitutional instrument through which the executive is made accountable to the legislature for all public expenditure.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1977)** — The Supreme Court addressed the federal dimensions of State financial autonomy and the relationship between State budgetary processes and Union oversight. The judgment is relevant to understanding why certain State expenditure items are constitutionally protected as charged expenditure and thus insulated from ordinary legislative debate and vote, preserving the financial independence of constitutional institutions from both legislative and executive manipulation.

**High Court of Judicature at Allahabad v. Yashwant Singh Yadav** — The principle that High Court judges' salaries are charged expenditure under Article 202(3)(d) has been affirmed in proceedings before the Supreme Court involving attempts by State governments to delay or defer payment of judicial salaries and pension benefits. The Court has consistently held that the charged status of these items imposes a constitutional obligation of prompt and full payment, and that delay without justification is a breach of Article 202.

**Bhim Singh v. Union of India (2010)** — The Court's treatment of consolidated fund expenditure in the Union context — particularly the principle that charged items represent constitutionally guaranteed financial obligations rather than discretionary spending — informs the interpretation of Article 202(3) listing State charged expenditure. The reasoning confirms that the category of charged expenditure is constitutionally protected against reduction or denial through the appropriations process.

## Current significance

Article 202 forms the constitutional foundation of State-level public finance management, and its significance has grown as State finances have become increasingly strained. The classification of High Court judges' salaries as charged expenditure has been practically tested in states experiencing fiscal stress — the constitutional status of these payments protects them from the budget cuts that discretionary expenditure faces during fiscal consolidation.

Debates about State fiscal autonomy — particularly the tension between State budgetary priorities and the conditions attached to centrally sponsored schemes and Finance Commission grants — frequently invoke the framework established by this article. The constitutional separation between charged and voted items ensures that certain core expenditures are protected regardless of fiscal conditions or political conflicts between the Centre and a State government.

The transparency dimension of Article 202 has been reinforced by parallel developments in fiscal responsibility legislation. Most States have enacted fiscal responsibility and budget management laws that require multi-year fiscal projections, outcome budgeting, and public disclosure of contingent liabilities — all of which build on the constitutional foundation of the annual financial statement. The Reserve Bank of India's annual State Finances report consistently measures State compliance with the Article 202 framework as an indicator of fiscal governance quality.

The provision also anchors protections for independent institutions at the State level. Chief Electoral Officers' expenses, Public Service Commission budgets, and the emoluments of Lokayuktas and other constitutional bodies in States that have constituted them by law may be declared charged under clause (3)(f), extending the Article 202 protection beyond the items specifically enumerated. This flexibility to add items to the charged category by State legislation makes Article 202 a living framework rather than a closed list.
