  127. Appointment of ad hoc Judges.—(1) If at any time there should not be a quorum of the Judges of the Supreme Court available to hold or continue any session of the Court, [the National Judicial Appointments Commission on a reference made to it by the Chief Justice of India, may with the previous consent of the President] and after consultation with the Chief Justice of the High Court concerned, request in writing the attendance at the sittings of the Court, as an ad hoc Judge, for such period as may be necessary, of a Judge of a High Court duly qualified for appointment as a Judge of the Supreme Court to be designated by the Chief Justice of India.

## What this Article does

Article 127 addresses a specific operational contingency: the possibility that the Supreme Court may lack a quorum of Judges to hold or continue a session. In such circumstances, the Chief Justice of India must make a reference, and the body responsible for judicial appointments — currently described in the text as the NJAC, but in practice the collegium since the NJAC was struck down in 2015 — may, with the President's prior consent and after consulting the Chief Justice of the concerned High Court, request a High Court Judge who is qualified for Supreme Court appointment to sit as an ad hoc Judge for as long as necessary.

Clause (2) makes attendance a priority duty: the designated High Court Judge is obliged to attend Supreme Court sittings ahead of their other obligations at the High Court, and while attending exercises the full jurisdiction, powers, and privileges of a Supreme Court Judge. The article provides a constitutional safety valve ensuring that quorum requirements can always be met without delay, protecting litigants from indefinite adjournments caused by vacancies or absences.

## The quorum requirement

The Constitution does not fix a universal quorum for all Supreme Court sittings, but Article 145(3) requires a minimum of five judges for cases involving a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of the Constitution. In practice, regular division benches sit as two-judge benches. Article 127's trigger — "no quorum available to hold or continue any session" — can theoretically be engaged when vacancies and absences reduce the bench below the minimum required for constitution bench hearings, even if two-judge benches remain possible. This has become a live concern given prolonged periods during which the Court operated well below its sanctioned strength of 34 judges.

## The NJAC reference and its post-2015 effect

The article currently refers to the NJAC as the body that acts on the Chief Justice's reference. Following the Supreme Court's striking down of the NJAC in 2015, this reference is constitutionally inoperative. The pre-NJAC collegium process governs by default. No constitutional amendment has been made to substitute the collegium for the NJAC reference in Article 127, creating the same inoperative-text problem that affects Articles 124A, 124B, and 124C.

## Distinction from Article 128

Article 127 (ad hoc judges) and Article 128 (retired judges) are complementary but distinct mechanisms:

- **Article 127** applies to serving High Court judges sitting temporarily at the Supreme Court to address a quorum deficit. The designated judge remains a High Court judge but is directed to prioritise Supreme Court attendance.
- **Article 128** applies to retired Supreme Court, Federal Court, and qualified retired High Court judges, who may voluntarily return to sit. Article 128 can be invoked "at any time" and is not limited to quorum emergencies.

The key differences are: the service status of the person (serving judge versus retired), the trigger (quorum deficit versus any time), and the consent requirement (Article 128 preserves personal autonomy; Article 127 creates a duty once designated, though the designation itself requires the collegium's request and the President's consent).

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (1993):** The Second Judges case examined the full spectrum of judicial appointment mechanisms under Articles 124 to 128, confirming that the ad hoc Judge mechanism is an exceptional measure reserved for quorum failures rather than a general tool for managing caseload. The qualification requirement — that the High Court Judge must be duly qualified for Supreme Court appointment — must be strictly met.

**Union of India v. Sankalchand Himatlal Sheth (1977):** The Court's discussion of judicial independence confirmed that any mechanism for temporarily deploying a judge in a higher court must be insulated from executive caprice. This principle underlies the consent and consultation requirements in Article 127.

## Vacancy pressure and the case for proactive use

The Supreme Court has periodically operated at significantly lower numbers than its sanctioned strength of 34 judges. When effective strength falls to 20 or fewer, constitution bench sittings are disrupted and cases of general public importance are delayed. Academic and bar association commentary has argued that Article 127 could be used more proactively as an interim measure while permanent appointments are processed. The reluctance to invoke the article is attributed to institutional preference for a permanent bench, practical disruption to High Courts losing a judge, and concerns about the optics of calling up junior High Court judges to the apex court.

## Current significance

Article 127 has rarely been invoked in the Supreme Court's history. Its continued relevance is as a constitutional backstop preventing absolute court closure in an extreme vacancy scenario, and as a reminder that the Constitution anticipated judicial capacity problems. The unresolved NJAC-reference in the text means that any future invocation would require clarity on whether the collegium or some other body acts on the Chief Justice's reference — a question that may need to be addressed either by constitutional amendment or by a formal Supreme Court ruling on the operative procedure.
