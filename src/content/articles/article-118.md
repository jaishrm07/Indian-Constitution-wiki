  118. Rules of procedure.—(1) Each House of Parliament may make rules for regulating, subject to the provisions of this Constitution, its procedure and the conduct of its business.

  (2) Until rules are made under clause (1), the rules of procedure and standing orders in force immediately before the commencement of this Constitution with respect to the Legislature of the Dominion of India shall have effect in relation to Parliament subject to such modifications and adaptations as may be made therein by the Chairman of the Council of States or the Speaker of the House of the People, as the case may be.

## Why it matters

Parliamentary self-regulation is a precondition of legislative independence. If the executive could prescribe how Parliament conducts its business, or if courts could second-guess every procedural decision, the legislative branch would cease to be a co-equal branch of government. Article 118's grant of autonomous rule-making power is the constitutional expression of this independence — each House is master of its own procedure within the framework the Constitution itself sets.

The structural limit — "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" — is equally important. Article 118 does not place Parliament above the Constitution; it gives Parliament authority to fill the procedural gaps the Constitution leaves open. Where the Constitution itself speaks — on quorum (Article 100), on financial procedure (Articles 112–116), on language (Article 120) — Parliament's rules must conform, and a rule that conflicts with a constitutional provision is simply invalid. Courts respect this autonomy but have established jurisdiction to review whether the House acted within its constitutional authority, drawing a distinction between non-justiciable procedural irregularity and justiciable constitutional violation.

## Reading the provision

Article 118 grants each House of Parliament the autonomous power to regulate its own procedure and conduct of business through rules, subject only to the provisions of the Constitution itself. Clause (2) provided a transitional bridge at commencement, carrying forward the Dominion Legislature's rules until each House could frame its own. Clauses (3) and (4) vest the President — after consulting both presiding officers — with rule-making power for joint sittings, with the Lok Sabha Speaker presiding at any such sitting.

**The Rules of Procedure in practice:** Both Houses have enacted detailed rules of procedure and conduct of business. Lok Sabha's Rules of Procedure (currently in their latest amendment) and Rajya Sabha's Rules cover everything from notice periods for questions, time allocation for debates, the committee system, financial bill procedure, and member conduct. These rules are not ordinary legislation — they are made by the House under this constitutional power and bind members but cannot override constitutional provisions.

**Joint sitting rules:** The President's joint sitting rules under clause (3) govern the rare occasions when both Houses sit together under Article 108. The Lok Sabha's Speaker presides, giving the larger House's presiding officer authority over the combined sitting. The only joint sitting ever held in Indian parliamentary history was in 1961 (on the Dowry Prohibition Bill).

## Key cases

**In re: Powers, Privileges and Immunities of State Legislatures, Special Reference No. 1 of 1964** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the power of each House to regulate its own procedure is a constitutional privilege that courts respect, but held that this autonomy is not absolute — it operates within the constitutional framework, and courts retain jurisdiction to examine whether a House's conduct has exceeded constitutional limits.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Lok Sabha Speaker (2007)** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench held that while Article 122 protects proceedings from judicial review on grounds of procedural irregularity, the validity of a rule made under Article 118 may itself be judicially examined if it is alleged to violate a constitutional provision. The House's rule-making power under Article 118 is a power conferred by the Constitution, not a power superior to it.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** — The Supreme Court's landmark anti-defection judgment confirmed that the Tenth Schedule — which empowers the Speaker to decide disqualification questions — must be read alongside Article 118's framework, and that procedural rules for disqualification proceedings do not enjoy immunity from constitutional scrutiny merely because they derive from the House's self-regulatory power.

## Current significance

The interaction between Article 118's self-regulatory grant and judicial review under Article 32/226 has become increasingly contested as courts have been asked to examine the conduct of presiding officers in matters ranging from anti-defection rulings to the suspension of members. The Supreme Court's willingness in Raja Ram Pal to look behind the formal shelter of Article 122 and examine whether a House's action had a constitutional basis has effectively established a two-track analysis: procedural irregularity is non-justiciable, but ultra vires conduct or violation of constitutional rights can be reviewed. This line continues to be drawn case by case, making Article 118 a live boundary marker between parliamentary autonomy and constitutional accountability.
