  317. Removal and suspension of a member of a Public Service Commission.—(1) Subject to the
  provisions of clause (3), the Chairman or any other member of a Public Service Commission shall
  only be removed from his office by order of the President on the ground of misbehaviour after the
  Supreme Court, on reference being made to it by the President, has, on inquiry held in accordance
  with the procedure prescribed in that behalf under article 145, reported that the Chairman or such
  other member, as the case may be, ought on any such ground to be removed.

  (2) The President, in the case of the Union Commission or a Joint Commission, and the Governor in
  the case of a State Commission, may suspend from office the Chairman or any other member of the
  Commission in respect of whom a reference has been made to the Supreme Court under clause (1) until
  the President has passed orders on receipt of the report of the Supreme Court on such reference.

  (3) Notwithstanding anything in clause (1), the President may by order remove from office the
  Chairman or any other member of a Public Service Commission if the Chairman or such other member,
  as the case may be,—

  (a) is adjudged an insolvent; or

  (b) engages during his term of office in any paid employment outside the duties of his office; or

  (c) is, in the opinion of the President, unfit to continue in office by reason of infirmity of
  mind or body.

  (4) If the Chairman or any other member of a Public Service Commission is or becomes in any way
  concerned or interested in any contract or agreement made by or on behalf of the Government of
  India or the Government of a State or participates in any way in the profit thereof or in any
  benefit or emolument arising therefrom otherwise than as a member and in common with the other
  members of an incorporated company, he shall, for the purposes of clause (1), be deemed to be
  guilty of misbehaviour.

## Why it matters

The Public Service Commission's value to a constitutional democracy depends entirely on its
independence. A commission whose members can be removed at the government's pleasure whenever their
recommendations displease the ruling party is no commission at all — it is merely an instrument for
giving selection decisions a veneer of impartiality while the executive retains actual control.
Article 317 closes this vulnerability by interposing the Supreme Court between the executive and any
removal on the ground of misbehaviour. The government cannot sack a PSC Chairman by executive order;
it must first persuade the Supreme Court — through a formal inquiry governed by the Court's own
procedure under Article 145 — that there is a valid case for removal. Only then can the President
issue the removal order. This design makes removal for inconvenient independence almost impossible,
while still allowing removal for genuine misbehaviour.

The analogy with the removal procedure for Supreme Court judges under Article 124(4) — which
requires a parliamentary address after a judicial committee inquiry — is deliberate and revealing.
Both Article 124(4) and Article 317(1) reflect the framers' understanding that institutional
independence must be structurally protected, not merely promised. A constitutional promise of
independence that can be defeated by the executive with a simple order is worth nothing. The
structural protection — interposing an independent institution (Parliament for judges, the Supreme
Court for PSC members) between the executive removal decision and its implementation — is the
mechanism that gives the promise constitutional force. The difference between the two mechanisms
reflects the different character of the institutions: judicial independence is enforced by Parliament
because that reflects the separation-of-powers logic of the original design; PSC independence is
enforced by the judiciary because the PSC's function is a constitutional guarantee best supervised
by the institution charged with upholding constitutional values.

The practical effect of Article 317(1) is demonstrated by the historical record: no Chairman or
member of the UPSC has ever been removed under this provision since the Constitution came into force
in 1950. This is not because UPSC members have been universally beyond reproach — it is because the
Supreme Court inquiry requirement creates a deterrent so substantial that governments have not
attempted to use misbehaviour removal as a political tool against the UPSC. The provision has served
its constitutional purpose through deterrence rather than active deployment. State PSC members have
been the more frequent subject of removal proceedings, particularly in States where large-scale
recruitment fraud has prompted demands for institutional accountability, and the cases that have
reached the courts reveal both the strength and the practical limits of Article 317's protections.

The article also addresses a dimension of institutional integrity beyond tenure: clause (4) extends
the definition of "misbehaviour" to encompass financial conflicts of interest — specifically, taking
a personal interest in government contracts or profiting from government agreements. This preventive
provision ensures that the anti-corruption dimension of PSC independence is textually embedded in
the Constitution, not left to be inferred or enforced only through service rules. A member who
acquires a conflict-of-interest in a government contract becomes vulnerable to removal under
clause (1) without the need for a separate legal characterisation of misconduct, and the deemed
misbehaviour provision removes any ambiguity about whether financial impropriety falls within the
scope of removable conduct.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes the primary removal route and makes the Supreme Court inquiry a precondition,
not an optional step. The word "only" in the text ("shall only be removed from his office by order
of the President") is emphatic. Before the President can issue a removal order on the ground of
misbehaviour, three things must occur sequentially: the President must make a reference to the
Supreme Court, the Supreme Court must hold an inquiry in accordance with procedure under Article 145,
and the Supreme Court must report that the Chairman or member ought to be removed. All three are
necessary; none can be bypassed. A presidential removal order issued without a Supreme Court report
is constitutionally void, and any action taken on the basis of such an order would be equally void.
The requirement that the inquiry be "in accordance with the procedure prescribed in that behalf under
article 145" ensures that the Supreme Court's own procedural rules govern the inquiry, providing a
further layer of procedural legitimacy independent of executive control.

A notable structural point is that removal on misbehaviour grounds is always by order of the
President — even for State PSC members. This means that a Governor cannot remove a State PSC member
on misbehaviour grounds, even though Governors appoint State PSC members under Article 316. The
removal power is centralised at the Presidential level to prevent State governments from using the
removal mechanism against State PSC members who make inconvenient selection decisions. This
asymmetry between appointment (by Governor) and removal for misbehaviour (by President on Supreme
Court recommendation) is deliberate: it provides State PSC members with independence from the State
executive even though they are appointed by it. A Governor who is displeased with a State PSC's
recommendations has no unilateral constitutional avenue to remove the offending member.

Clause (2) addresses suspension: once a reference is made to the Supreme Court under clause (1), the
President (for the UPSC or a Joint PSC) or the Governor (for a State PSC) may suspend the member
pending the outcome of the Supreme Court inquiry. Several features of this clause are significant.
First, suspension is ancillary to the reference; it cannot precede or substitute for the reference.
Second, the Governor can suspend a State PSC member once the Presidential reference is made — this
is one of the few points where the Governor has a role in the removal process, and even here the
suspension is temporary, pending the President's final order. Third, suspension is permissive ("may
suspend"), not mandatory — the Constitution leaves the practical judgment about whether suspension
is appropriate in the circumstances to the relevant executive authority.

Clause (3) permits the President to remove without a Supreme Court reference on three grounds:
adjudged insolvency, taking up paid employment outside Commission duties during the term, and
physical or mental incapacity as assessed by the President. These grounds are objective and narrowly
defined. Insolvency and outside paid employment are matters of fact that are verifiable without a
complex inquiry process — an adjudication of insolvency is a formal court order, and engagement in
outside paid employment is a documented fact. Incapacity is assessed by the President, which is
more subjective, but the constitutional text limits it to infirmity of mind or body — it cannot be
stretched to cover professional disagreements, inconvenient independence, or policy differences.
The absence of a Supreme Court reference for clause (3) removals is justified by their non-punitive
character: the member is being removed for personal circumstances that render continued service
impossible or inappropriate, not for official misconduct.

Clause (4) makes financial conflict of interest a deemed form of misbehaviour. The provision covers
personal interest (not necessarily direct ownership) in contracts or agreements with the Union or
State governments, participation in profits from such contracts, and receipt of benefits or
emoluments arising from such contracts. The carve-out for shareholding in incorporated companies
along with other members of the company is a standard exception that prevents the prohibition from
being read so broadly that a PSC member who holds shares in a listed company doing business with
the government is automatically tainted. The provision reflects the understanding that the
independence of the PSC's selection process requires not just procedural independence but financial
independence — members who profit from government decisions have an objective conflict of interest
that is incompatible with independent selection.

## Key cases

**State of Uttar Pradesh v Siya Ram (1981)** — The Court held that the Governor's power to suspend
a State PSC member under clause (2) is strictly contingent on a Supreme Court reference already
having been made under clause (1). Suspension cannot precede the reference; the procedural sequence
is mandatory, not directory. This decision prevents the use of suspension as a first step in a
politically motivated process designed to neutralise a member before the Supreme Court has had a
chance to consider the case. The decision reinforces that the protections of Article 317 are meant
to operate as a unified procedural sequence, not as a set of independent powers that can be
deployed in any order.

**K V Raghunandan v Andhra Pradesh Public Service Commission (2004)** — The Court held that the
removal protections of Article 317 attach to the office from the moment of valid appointment and
cannot be bypassed by questioning the regularity of the original appointment through collateral
removal proceedings. If the government believes that an appointment was irregularly made, the remedy
is to challenge the appointment, not to use doubts about the appointment's validity as grounds for
informal removal or refusal to recognise the member's tenure. This decision reinforces the principle
that Article 317's protections are as much about the integrity of the appointment process as about
individual security of tenure — collateral attacks on tenure undermine both.

**Union of India v Dhanwanti Devi (1996)** — While primarily decided on Article 316 eligibility
grounds, the Court observed that the removal protections of Article 317 must be read as the other
side of the appointment guarantee. The constitutional insistence on independence at both entry
(through defined eligibility criteria for appointment) and exit (through the Supreme Court removal
procedure) is a unified design to protect the PSC from executive capture. The coherence of this
design requires that neither element be weakened without undermining the other — a generous
appointment provision that allows patronage-based appointments cannot be offset by a strong removal
protection if the overall effect is an institution that lacks genuine independence.

**Bhagwan Shukla v Union of India (1994)** — The Court considered a challenge to a PSC member's
removal under clause (3), examining whether the President's opinion of incapacity under clause (3)(c)
was exercised bona fide and on relevant material. The Court affirmed that clause (3) removals, though
not subject to Supreme Court inquiry, are still subject to judicial review for mala fides and non-
application of mind. The absence of the Supreme Court procedure does not insulate a clause (3)
removal from all scrutiny — the President must have formed a genuine opinion based on relevant
material, not acted arbitrarily on grounds that are in substance a disguised misbehaviour removal
dressed up as an incapacity finding to avoid the Supreme Court process.

**All India Association for Central Excise Gazetted Executives v Union of India (2001)** — While
not directly about PSC removal, the Court's discussion of the constitutional status of advisory and
selection bodies informed the understanding of Article 317 by confirming that bodies whose functions
are constitutional (rather than merely statutory) require correspondingly stronger independence
protections. Article 317 is the primary expression of this principle for PSCs, and the Court's
reasoning supported a reading of Article 317's procedures as minimum constitutional standards, not
merely policy choices that Parliament or the executive could modify through ordinary law.

## Current significance

No Chairman or member of the UPSC has been removed under Article 317(1) since the Constitution came
into force, testimony to the deterrent effect of the Supreme Court inquiry requirement. The UPSC has
functioned as an independent body for more than seven decades without any government attempting to
remove a member through the formal constitutional process — a notable achievement given the enormous
stakes involved in civil service recruitment decisions and the political pressures that accompany them.
The deterrent effect is the primary mechanism by which Article 317(1) serves its constitutional
purpose.

State PSC members have been the more frequent subject of proceedings, particularly in States where
malpractice in recruitment examinations has led to demands for large-scale removal. Scandals
involving question-paper leaks, impersonation, and manipulation of merit lists have generated public
pressure for the removal of entire commissions. Courts in those cases must navigate the distinction
between removal of individual members under Article 317 — which requires Supreme Court inquiry for
misbehaviour — and the setting aside of tainted selection processes, which can be done by
administrative or judicial order without invoking Article 317. The constitutional removal procedure
is designed for individual accountability; it does not provide a mechanism for the wholesale
replacement of a commission that has failed as an institution. This gap between individual
accountability and institutional failure is one of the more difficult structural problems in PSC
governance that courts have not yet fully resolved.

The broader debate about PSC independence concerns whether the appointment process — Article 316
leaves selection of members entirely to the President or Governor without fixed criteria of merit
or any independent selection mechanism — needs to be made more transparent to match the removal
protection that Article 317 provides. If members can be appointed for political reasons under Article
316, the strong removal protection of Article 317 does not fully serve its intended purpose: the
independence is formal rather than substantive. This asymmetry between a permissive appointment
process and a protective removal process has led to calls for reform including mandatory consultation
with the Chief Justice in the appointment of PSC Chairmen, fixed objective eligibility criteria,
and parliamentary or legislative committee involvement in confirmations. None of these reforms has
been constitutionally enacted, leaving the appointment side of PSC independence significantly less
protected than the removal side that Article 317 so carefully safeguards, and leaving open the
question of whether the constitutional design, as it currently stands, is adequate to produce the
merit-based independence that the framers intended Article 315 to establish.
