## Why it matters

Article 306 reflects the political and constitutional complexity of India's integration of the princely states after independence. When the Constitution was enacted in 1950, the First Schedule classified states into three categories: Part A states (former British provinces, such as Madras, Bombay, and United Provinces), Part B states (former princely states that had been integrated under Instruments of Accession, including Hyderabad, Mysore, Rajasthan, Saurashtra, Travancore-Cochin, Madhya Bharat, and Vindhya Pradesh), and Part C states (centrally-administered territories). Part B states retained transitional powers that Part A states did not have, including the ability under original Article 306 to impose certain restrictions on trade and commerce that would otherwise have been prohibited by Part XIII's guarantee of freedom of trade, commerce, and intercourse throughout the territory of India.

The rationale was pragmatic. The former princely states had their own fiscal structures, tax systems, octroi arrangements, and trade regulations developed over decades of independent administration under British paramountcy. Several of them — most notably Hyderabad, which had its own currency, and the smaller states of Rajputana — had internal tariff and trade control systems deeply embedded in their revenue structures. Immediate full application of Part XIII's free-trade guarantees to those states would have created administrative disruption and sudden revenue shortfalls in territories that had only recently been integrated into the Indian Union. Article 306 provided a constitutionally-sanctioned transitional window that permitted Part B states to retain certain pre-existing trade restrictions for a limited period.

## Reading the provision

The original text of Article 306 empowered certain Part B states — specifically those listed in sub-clause to the provision — to maintain, during the transitional period, such restrictions on trade and commerce into or out of their territories as were in force immediately before the commencement of the Constitution. These restrictions could only be maintained for a fixed transitional period; they were not a permanent carve-out from Part XIII. The article was closely connected to original Article 305, which preserved existing State laws on trade restrictions subject to adaptation, and to the First Schedule's three-category state classification.

The two states whose transitional trade powers were most significant under Article 306 were Coorg (a Part C territory that had certain retained powers) and Hyderabad. Hyderabad's case was particularly notable because it had been a fully independent sovereign state before its accession, with its own Osmania currency, import duties, and extensive internal trade regulations. These regulations could not simply be abolished on 26 January 1950 without creating administrative chaos. Article 306 provided the legal basis for winding them down in an orderly manner.

When the States Reorganisation Act 1956 and the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956 abolished the three-category classification and merged all states into a unified framework under a single category, the entire basis for Article 306's differentiated treatment of Part B states disappeared. The provision had served its purpose and was formally omitted with effect from 1 November 1956.

## Key cases

No significant case law survives from the brief period (1950–1956) during which Article 306 was in force. Pre-1956 decisions that applied original Article 306 to Part B State trade restrictions are of historical interest only and do not form part of the living constitutional doctrine of Part XIII. In the few cases that arose, courts confirmed that Article 306 was to be construed narrowly as a transitional exception to Part XIII's general guarantees, and that restrictions maintained under it could not be extended beyond the express constitutional period.

**Atiabari Tea Co Ltd v State of Assam AIR 1961 SC 232** — Although decided after Article 306's omission and not directly concerned with it, the Supreme Court's foundational analysis of Part XIII in this case traced the entire architecture of the freedom-of-trade provisions, noting how the transitional exceptions including original Article 306 had been designed and then removed as unnecessary once the integration of the princely states was complete. The Court's analysis of Article 301 as a general declaration of freedom subject only to the articles that follow it remains the definitive reading of Part XIII.

**Automobile Transport (Rajasthan) Ltd v State of Rajasthan AIR 1962 SC 1406** — A Constitution Bench considered the scope of State power to impose regulatory restrictions on trade and commerce under Articles 301 to 304. The judgment's historical survey noted that the omission of Article 306 upon states reorganisation was part of the completion of India's constitutional common market, and that the states formed from the former Part B states were fully subject to Part XIII from 1 November 1956.

## Part XIII and the constitutional common market

Part XIII of the Constitution — Articles 301 to 307 — creates what scholars have called India's constitutional common market: a guarantee that trade, commerce, and intercourse throughout the territory of India shall be free. Article 301 states the general freedom; Articles 302 to 304 set out the permitted exceptions (Parliament may impose restrictions in the public interest; States may impose reasonable restrictions on goods imported from other States if similar restrictions apply to goods produced within the State; no discriminatory taxes on goods from other States). Article 305 saved existing State law. Article 307 empowered Parliament to appoint an authority to carry out the purposes of Articles 301 to 304. Original Article 306 was an exception specifically carved out for Part B states — it operated outside the standard framework of Articles 302 to 305 by allowing Part B states to maintain restrictions that were already in force before the Constitution commenced, without needing to satisfy the conditions imposed on new State restrictions under Articles 302 to 304. This made Article 306 a more permissive exception than the ordinary ones available to Part A states, reflecting the asymmetric constitutional status of the former princely states in the transitional period.

## The 1956 reorganisation and the end of Article 306

The States Reorganisation Commission (1955), chaired by Justice Fazal Ali, recommended the reorganisation of Indian states primarily along linguistic lines. Its recommendations led to the States Reorganisation Act 1956 and the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956, which came into effect on 1 November 1956. The Seventh Amendment abolished the Part A / Part B / Part C distinction, replacing it with a single category of "States" and a separate category of "Union territories". The former Part B states were dissolved: some became new States (Mysore, Rajasthan, Kerala), others were merged into larger States (Madhya Bharat into Madhya Pradesh, Saurashtra into Bombay, Vindhya Pradesh into Madhya Pradesh), and Hyderabad was partitioned between Andhra Pradesh, Mysore, and Bombay. With the disappearance of the Part B category, Article 306's constitutional basis evaporated. The Seventh Amendment therefore omitted Article 306 along with several other provisions specific to the tripartite state classification, completing the constitutional unification of India's internal market under a single Part XIII framework.

## Current significance

Article 306 is a constitutional artefact. Its continued listing in Part XIII with the notation of omission serves as a documentary record of the transitional architecture of India's federal integration — a reminder that the constitutional common market was not established uniformly and instantaneously across all territory on 26 January 1950, but was extended to the former princely states through a graduated process that culminated in the 1956 reorganisation. The article is studied today in the context of comparative constitutional history and the law of states reorganisation.

The omission also illustrates a drafting convention of the Indian Constitution: when a provision is removed by amendment, the article number is retained with a notation of omission rather than being renumbered. This preserves the numerical continuity of the text and keeps the historical record visible on the face of the document. The removal of Article 306 is also a reminder that the Constitution of India has undergone substantial structural revision — not merely policy revision — since its commencement, and that Part XIII's current form as a uniform set of trade-freedom guarantees applying to all states and union territories is the product of that evolution.
