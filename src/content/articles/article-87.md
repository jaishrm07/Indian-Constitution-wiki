## Why it matters

Article 87 institutionalises the formal opening of Parliament as a constitutional act, not merely a ceremonial one. By requiring the President to address Parliament and "inform it of the causes of its summons," the article creates a constitutional link between the executive's agenda and the legislature's mandate. The address is the only moment in the parliamentary calendar where both Houses are constitutionally required to assemble together and to hear — and then debate — the government's programme. Its mandatory character distinguishes it from the discretionary address power in Article 86, and its requirement that time be set aside for debate gives it a functional constitutional purpose beyond pageantry.

The address under Article 87 is among the most politically significant events in the Indian parliamentary calendar. It represents the government's formal statement of intent — not as a Cabinet resolution or a ministerial speech but as a constitutional address delivered by the republic's first citizen. Because the Constitution requires the address at the opening of the first session after a general election, it is also the constitutional occasion on which a newly elected government presents itself and its programme to Parliament for the first time, lending it the character of a formal inauguration of the government's mandate. The debate that follows — the Motion of Thanks on the President's Address — is constitutionally mandated by Article 87(2) and is in practice the widest-ranging debate in Parliament's annual calendar, permitting members to speak on virtually any matter of public importance.

The article captures a fundamental tension in Indian parliamentary government. The President delivers the address, but the Cabinet writes it. The address is titled "President's Address" and delivered in the President's voice, but it expresses the Cabinet's programme, not the President's personal views. This constitutional convention — that the President is the reader and the Cabinet is the author — is well established but also fragile. It depends on the President accepting the Cabinet's text, and on the Cabinet producing a text that the President can deliver with the dignity the constitutional occasion requires. Episodes in which Presidents have visibly diverged from the prepared text, or in which Presidents have publicly distanced themselves from the address's content, expose the limits of the convention's self-enforcing character.

The special address under Article 87 is also constitutionally distinct from royal speeches in the Westminster tradition in one important respect: it is a mandatory constitutional obligation rather than a discretionary act. The President "shall address" — not "may address." This means the question of whether the President can decline to deliver the address, or whether the Cabinet can advise the President to omit an address in any given year, is not open. The address must happen. Only the content is within the Cabinet's control; the occasion itself is constitutionally compelled.

## Reading the provision

The current formulation of Article 87(1) — mandating an address at the first session after a general election and at the first session of each year — replaced the original text, which required an address "at the commencement of every session." The First Amendment Act 1951 narrowed the obligation to its present two-occasion form, reflecting the practical difficulty of requiring a full presidential address at each of the three or more sessions that Parliament may hold in a year. The change reduced the ceremonial frequency but did not alter the constitutional character of the obligation; those two occasions now carry the full weight that every-session addresses once spread across the year.

The phrase "both Houses of Parliament assembled together" establishes this as a joint sitting occasion, but a distinctive one. The joint sittings convened under Articles 108 and 118 for legislative deadlock resolution are quite different in character — they have voting implications for the composition of the Parliament. The Article 87 joint sitting is not a voting occasion; it is a communicative one. The President addresses, the members listen, and each House then separately conducts its Motion of Thanks debate. The joint assembly under Article 87 has no quorum requirement distinct from the general parliamentary quorum, and the arrangements for seating, precedence, and presiding are governed by convention and the Rules of Procedure of both Houses rather than by specific constitutional provision.

Clause (2) requires each House to make procedural provision for debating the address — "the allotment of time for discussion of the matters referred to in such address." In practice, both the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha set aside several days for the Motion of Thanks. The Motion of Thanks is a formal resolution moved by a government backbencher thanking the President for the address, and it is debated before being passed. The debate on the Motion of Thanks is constitutionally anchored in Article 87(2) and is treated as the broadest debate in Parliament's calendar: members may speak on any matter of national concern, the government must respond, and amendments to the Motion can be moved highlighting matters the government's programme has omitted or mishandled. A government that cannot get the Motion of Thanks passed on the floor of the Lok Sabha would face the same constitutional consequences as losing any other confidence vote, making the Motion of Thanks a vehicle of accountability as well as of debate.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court's treatment of the President's constitutional role vis-à-vis Parliament clarified that the President's Address under Article 87 is prepared by and reflects the views of the Council of Ministers. The President reads the address but does not independently author it. The Court reinforced the parliamentary executive model — that the President's role in addressing Parliament is constitutional form, with the Cabinet supplying the substance. The judgment is the principal authority for the proposition that the President's Article 87 address is an exercise of the Article 74 duty to act on Cabinet advice, not an independent presidential communication.

**President Pratibha Patil skipping passages (2012)** — In one of the most significant constitutional incidents relating to Article 87, President Pratibha Patil is reported to have skipped certain passages of the government-prepared address during delivery, particularly references to a controversial policy matter. The episode raised direct constitutional questions: if the President is the constitutional speaker of a Cabinet-prepared text, does the President have any discretion to omit, modify, or add to the address? The consensus view among constitutional commentators is that the President has no such discretion — the text is the Cabinet's communication, and the President's role is to deliver it. However, the incident was never subjected to judicial examination, and the question of whether a President's refusal to read portions of the text constitutes a constitutional wrong, and what remedy would exist, remains open.

**The 1975 Emergency Address** — During the Emergency period, the President's Address to Parliament became a particularly fraught constitutional instrument. The address delivered at the opening of Parliament in 1976 — after the 42nd Amendment extended the Lok Sabha's term and suspended fundamental rights — was a Cabinet-prepared text that presented Emergency measures as national necessities. No President declined to deliver any portion of those addresses. The episode illustrates how the constitutional convention that the President delivers what the Cabinet prepares can be used to lend constitutional legitimacy to programmes that are themselves constitutionally contested.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The five-judge bench's analysis of constitutional address obligations in the State legislature context treated the convening of Parliament and the conduct of business as tightly regulated constitutional acts. While the case concerned a Governor's address under Article 176 (the State-level equivalent of Article 87), the reasoning that constitutional address obligations are not empty formalities but carry substantive consequences for parliamentary procedure applies equally to Article 87. The Court's reasoning supports the view that a failure to provide time for debating the address under Article 87(2) would be a procedural constitutional failing, not merely a parliamentary discourtesy.

## Current significance

The President's Address has become politically contested on two distinct grounds. First, the content of the address — prepared entirely by the government — is often partisan in character, using the constitutional occasion to list the ruling party's achievements and announce its next term's agenda. Opposition parties have responded by staging walkouts from the joint assembly before or during the address, though no court has addressed whether members can constitutionally be required to remain for the address. A boycott of the President's Address has become a periodic feature of Indian parliamentary practice, used by opposition parties to signal their rejection of the government's programme. The constitutional question of whether a boycott of the Article 87 address constitutes a failure to respect a constitutional obligation has not been seriously argued, in part because the Constitution imposes the obligation to address on the President, not the obligation to attend on members.

Second, on several occasions Presidents have signalled discomfort with the content they were asked to deliver, either through public statements before or after the session, or — in one notable episode — by skipping certain passages while reading the address aloud. These incidents raise unanswered constitutional questions about the outer limits of a President's discretion as the constitutional reader of a Cabinet-prepared text. The prevailing convention is that the President has no discretion over the address's content, but there is a residual question about whether the President retains a constitutional duty not to deliver a text that is demonstrably unlawful or constitutionally impermissible, analogous to the obligation the President might have to refuse to sign a manifestly unconstitutional Bill under Article 111.

The requirement in clause (2) that both Houses allocate time for debating the address has been observed more in form than in substance in recent sessions. The Motion of Thanks debates have been progressively shortened, and the quality of debate has shifted from substantive programmatic discussion to political theatre, with government and opposition using the constitutionally mandated occasion for campaign-style messaging rather than parliamentary deliberation. Whether this represents a decline in the constitutional practice that Article 87(2) was designed to protect — the legislative examination of the executive's programme — is a matter of active concern for parliamentary scholars and constitutional practitioners.
