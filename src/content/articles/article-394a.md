## What this Article does

Article 394A was inserted by the Constitution (Fifty-eighth Amendment) Act, 1987. It directs the President to publish an authoritative translation of the Constitution in the Hindi language and to ensure that every subsequent constitutional amendment is also translated into Hindi. The translation, once published, is deemed to be the authoritative text of the Constitution in Hindi for all purposes.

The article operates in three clauses:

Clause (1) requires the President to cause to be published: (a) a Hindi translation of the entire Constitution signed by members of the Constituent Assembly, updated to incorporate all amendments made before publication; and (b) Hindi translations of every future constitutional amendment made in English.

Clause (2) provides that the Hindi translation shall have the same meaning as the original English text. If any difficulty arises in interpretation, the President may cause the translation to be revised appropriately.

Clause (3) declares that the translations published under this article shall be deemed to be the authoritative text of the Constitution in Hindi for all purposes.

## Historical context and significance

The original Constitution of India was drafted and enacted in English. Hindi, despite being the first language listed in the Eighth Schedule and designated as the official language of the Union under Article 343, did not have an authoritative constitutional text until 1987. Before Article 394A was inserted, Hindi translations of the Constitution existed but were not authoritative — they were for reference only, and the English text prevailed in case of doubt.

The demand for an authoritative Hindi text had been present for decades, reflecting both the national language movement and the practical reality that large numbers of Indian citizens, lawyers, and officials who worked in Hindi could not access the primary constitutional text without translation.

The Fifty-eighth Amendment of 1987 resolved this by creating a mechanism for the President to publish an official, authenticated Hindi translation. The translation was to be signed by members of the Constituent Assembly — those still living — to give it the same founding authority as the original English text. This was a symbolic and practical step of considerable significance: it acknowledged that the Constitution of a diverse, multilingual country could not remain accessible only through a colonial language.

The requirement in clause (2) that any difficulty in construing the Hindi translation should be resolved by presidential revision (rather than by automatic recourse to the English text) was also significant. It gave the Hindi text genuine, not merely nominal, authority.

## Languages and the Constitution

Article 394A intersects with the broader constitutional treatment of language. Article 343 makes Hindi in the Devanagari script the official language of the Union. Article 348 requires constitutional proceedings in the Supreme Court and High Courts to be in English (though this can be changed by Parliament or State Legislatures with the President's consent). The Eighth Schedule recognises numerous scheduled languages. Article 394A sits at the intersection of these provisions by creating an authoritative constitutional text in the official language of the Union.

## Key cases

No major constitutional case has turned on the interpretation of Article 394A or on a discrepancy between the English and Hindi texts of the Constitution.

**In re Special Courts Bill 1978 AIR 1979 SC 478** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the English text of the Constitution remains the primary authoritative text in judicial proceedings conducted in English. Article 394A did not alter this; it created an authoritative parallel text in Hindi for the purposes of Hindi-medium institutions and proceedings.

## Current significance

Article 394A continues to be operative. The authoritative Hindi text of the Constitution was published following the Fifty-eighth Amendment. With increasing use of Hindi in legal education, judicial proceedings at subordinate court levels, and public administration, the existence of an authoritative Hindi text is of growing practical significance. The article also reflects a continuing constitutional commitment to making India's foundational document accessible in the language spoken by the largest number of its citizens.
