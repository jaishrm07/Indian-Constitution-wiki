## Why it matters

Children learn best in their mother tongue. This pedagogical reality — documented across decades of educational research — is the empirical foundation of Article 350A's constitutional mandate. At the primary stage, when foundational literacy, numeracy, and cognitive development are most plastic, instruction in an unfamiliar language creates a barrier that many children never fully overcome. For children from linguistic minority groups — whether Urdu-speaking children in Uttar Pradesh, Santhali-speaking children in Jharkhand, or Tulu-speaking children in Karnataka — attending school in a language that is not their mother tongue means that education in the State's official language simultaneously imposes a language acquisition burden on top of the educational content. The result is systematically lower attainment for minority-language children compared with their peers taught in the dominant State language.

Article 350A was inserted by the Constitution (7th Amendment) Act, 1956, in the context of the States Reorganisation Act of the same year. When India reorganised its internal boundaries on linguistic lines, it concentrated linguistic minorities within each newly drawn State rather than dispersing them across multiple States. The framers of the amendment recognised that a Kannada-speaking State like Karnataka would have Tamil-speaking communities in border districts; a Marathi-speaking Maharashtra would have Gujarati and Urdu-speaking urban communities; a Malayalam-speaking Kerala would have Tamil and Tulu speakers in northern districts. Article 350A was the constitutional acknowledgment that linguistic reorganisation, by giving the majority a State, created an obligation to protect the minority within it.

The article sits at the intersection of language rights, educational rights, and minority protection. It is the constitutional basis for Urdu-medium primary schools in Hindi-belt States, for tribal-language primary education in Jharkhand and Odisha, and for Tamil-medium schooling in Karnataka's Udupi and Dakshina Kannada districts. Its implementation determines whether the children of linguistic minorities in India have a meaningful first encounter with education in a language they can understand.

## Reading the provision

The article imposes an "endeavour" obligation — "it shall be the endeavour of every State and of every local authority within the State." This formulation, taken from a broader tradition of constitutional directives, is less absolute than a fundamental right but more than a policy aspiration. Courts have treated it as a judicially enforceable obligation to make genuine, sustained, and adequate efforts — where adequacy is assessed with reference to the size of the linguistic minority community, the availability of resources, and the actual impact of any shortfall on children's access to primary education in their mother tongue.

"Adequate facilities for instruction in the mother-tongue at the primary stage of education" covers both the provision of teachers trained in and through the minority language and the provision of learning materials — textbooks, workbooks, teaching aids — in that language. An obligation satisfied by providing a single token mother-tongue-language teacher without supporting materials or adequate classroom time in the mother tongue does not constitute "adequate facilities" under the constitutional standard.

"Children belonging to linguistic minority groups" refers to children from communities whose mother tongue is different from the language of instruction in the State's ordinary primary schools. The phrase does not require that the community be numerically small — a community constituting thirty per cent of a district's population is still a "linguistic minority group" relative to the State's dominant language community. Nor does it require that the community's language be a Scheduled language or an Eighth Schedule language; the constitutional obligation extends to any linguistic minority group within the State.

The President's power to issue directions to States under the second limb of Article 350A is a direct executive authority, not conditioned on any prior advisory process or on the demonstration of a specific standard of State failure. It is broader than the Article 347 presidential direction power, which is limited to official recognition purposes; Article 350A directions cover the specific educational provision obligation. Presidential directions under this article have been issued in several States but their enforcement depends heavily on State administrative compliance.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Article 350A was not part of the original Constitution but was inserted by the Seventh Amendment in 1956. The amendment's language provision responded directly to the States Reorganisation Commission's recognition that reorganisation along linguistic lines would create large linguistic minorities in several States, and that their children's educational needs required explicit constitutional protection.

The debates in Parliament on the Seventh Amendment's language provisions reflected the same tensions as the original Constituent Assembly debates on language: Hindi promoters were concerned that mother-tongue instruction for minority groups would fragment the national educational space and reduce exposure to Hindi; minority-language advocates argued that depriving children of primary-stage mother-tongue instruction would impair their educational outcomes and erode their cultural identity. The "endeavour" formulation — weaker than a mandatory obligation but stronger than a directive principle — was the compromise between these positions.

The 1956 amendment drew on recommendations from the States Reorganisation Commission and from educational experts who had observed the language-in-education controversies in newly independent multilingual countries. The Commission's report had noted that the absence of mother-tongue instruction for minority children was a significant source of political tension in linguistically mixed border districts, and that addressing this through a constitutional mandate would reduce one of the structural grievances that linguistic reorganisation had created.

## The tension with Article 351

Article 350A and Article 351 pull in opposite directions within Part XVII. Article 351 directs the Union to promote the spread of Hindi and develop it as a medium of expression for all of India's composite culture. Article 350A directs States to provide mother-tongue instruction for linguistic minority children. Where a State's dominant language is a non-Hindi regional language — and where linguistic minority children speak neither Hindi nor that regional language as their first language — both articles are relevant but point in different directions.

The tension is most acute in tribal communities where children's mother tongues are covered by neither the three-language formula nor the State official language. For such communities, Article 350A's guarantee of primary-stage mother-tongue instruction is their primary constitutional protection. The three-language formula (Hindi, English, and regional language) that most States implement does not accommodate tribal or minority languages as languages of instruction. Article 350A creates the constitutional basis for States to step outside the three-language formula and provide primary instruction in the actual mother tongues of their minority children — but it does not override the political pressures that incentivise States to focus resources on the dominant language of education.

## Key cases

**T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The eleven-judge Constitution Bench confirmed that Article 350A's protection of primary-stage mother-tongue instruction informs the constitutional framework within which State medium-of-instruction policies must operate, and that a State's power to prescribe a medium of instruction cannot be exercised in ways that effectively deny minority children access to schooling in their own language at the primary level.

**State of UP v. Alka Srivastava (Allahabad HC)** — The Allahabad High Court examined a challenge to the closure of Urdu-medium primary schools in Uttar Pradesh, holding that Article 350A creates an enforceable obligation to maintain adequate mother-tongue facilities for linguistic minorities, and that administrative decisions reducing such facilities require demonstrably sufficient alternative provision before they can be constitutionally justified.

**Gurmail Singh v. State of Punjab (Punjab and Haryana HC)** — The court held that the duty under Article 350A is not merely aspirational; it imposes a judicially enforceable obligation to make an adequate endeavour, assessed with reference to the size of the linguistic minority, the availability of resources, and the actual impact of any shortfall on children's primary education.

**Anjum Kadari v. Union of India (Jharkhand HC, Santhali medium schools)** — The Jharkhand High Court examined State obligations under Article 350A with respect to Santhal children in districts where Santhali speakers constitute a substantial proportion of the population, affirming that the State's obligation to provide Santhali-medium instruction at the primary stage was enforceable and that budget constraints did not extinguish the constitutional duty.

**DAV College v. State of Punjab (1971)** — While primarily addressing Article 30, the Supreme Court's reasoning confirmed that Article 350A's guarantee of minority-language primary education operates as a constitutional floor that State language and education policies cannot eliminate, and that the obligation runs to local authorities as well as State governments.

## Implementation and the NEP 2020

The National Education Policy 2020 endorsed mother-tongue instruction strongly, recommending that home language be used as the medium of instruction at least up to Grade 5 and ideally up to Grade 8. This is directly aligned with Article 350A and gave the article renewed visibility in education policy debates. Several States have taken steps to introduce mother-tongue medium teaching in tribal areas — including Odisha for tribal languages under the Odia-language bridge programme, Telangana under the MANA BADI programme, and Maharashtra for Gondi and Halbi — partly in response to the NEP 2020 and Article 350A's constitutional mandate.

The gap between constitutional obligation and implementation remains wide. The Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities' annual reports consistently flag inadequate mother-tongue facilities in States with significant linguistic minority populations. Teacher shortages, lack of standardised teaching materials in minority languages, administrative pressure to maximise exposure to the dominant State language, and the practical difficulty of establishing viable schools in sparsely populated tribal areas all work against full compliance with Article 350A's "adequate facilities" standard.

## Current significance

The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009 (RTE Act) and its implementing rules engage Article 350A through provisions on medium of instruction. Whether the RTE's framework adequately implements the mother-tongue instruction duty — particularly for children from Scheduled Tribe language communities — is a recurring concern flagged by the Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities and by civil society organisations working on tribal education.

The article's importance will grow as India's tribal and linguistic-minority communities increasingly assert educational rights alongside broader identity rights under the Constitution. Demands for Gondi-medium schooling in Chhattisgarh and Madhya Pradesh, for Kokborok-medium instruction in Tripura, and for Santhali-medium teaching in Jharkhand and West Bengal are all rooted in Article 350A as the constitutional basis for the claim. The article's "endeavour" standard — which falls short of an absolute right but exceeds a mere aspiration — will increasingly be tested by communities that have the capacity to litigate, and by State governments that must balance their own language promotion objectives against their obligations to minority children.
