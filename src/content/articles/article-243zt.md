## What this Article does

Article 243ZT is the transitional savings provision for Part IXB.
Its function is to prevent an abrupt legal vacuum in the co-operative sector on the day the 97th Amendment came into force.
The article provides that any provision of existing co-operative society law that is inconsistent with Part IXB shall continue to be in force until it is amended or repealed by a competent legislature or authority, or until the expiry of one year from the commencement of the 97th Amendment — whichever is earlier.
The 97th Amendment came into force on 15 February 2012 (the date notified), making the outside transitional deadline 15 February 2013.

Without Article 243ZT, the normal constitutional rule under Article 13 would have operated to invalidate all existing co-operative society law provisions inconsistent with Part IXB on the date of the amendment's commencement, since Part IXB became part of the Constitution from that date.
Given that every State had its own co-operative societies act with provisions touching board composition, election cycles, supersession, audit, and annual general body meetings — many of which would have been inconsistent with Part IXB's new standards — instant invalidity would have produced administrative and legal chaos across a sector encompassing hundreds of thousands of societies and hundreds of millions of members.

## Reading the provision

The "whichever is less" formulation is the key drafting feature.
If a State amended its co-operative law within the one-year window to bring it into conformity with Part IXB, the transitional tolerance terminated at the date of that amendment — the State could not continue using the transitional period to preserve inconsistencies it had already chosen to address.
Conversely, if a State took no action, the one-year deadline on 15 February 2013 became operative and existing inconsistent provisions ceased to have effect to the extent of the inconsistency from that date.

The provision applies to "any provision of any law relating to co-operative societies in force in a State immediately before the commencement" — this covers both primary legislation (State Co-operative Societies Acts) and subordinate legislation (rules, bye-laws, notifications) made under those Acts.
The phrase "competent Legislature or other competent authority" extends the tolled period to include executive or subordinate amendments, not just full legislative repeal.

The "Notwithstanding anything in this Part" opening clause is essential: without it, the provisions of Part IXB would themselves prevail over the saving by virtue of Article 13's inconsistency rule.
The notwithstanding formulation subordinates all of Part IXB's operative provisions to the transitional grace period for the duration of the window, and restores their priority when the window closes.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah (2021) 6 SCC 1** — Although Article 243ZT's operative period had expired by February 2013, the Supreme Court's 2021 ruling that Part IXB's governance standards were constitutionally inapplicable to State co-operative societies retroactively recontextualises the transitional provision.
If Part IXB could never validly bind State co-operatives, then Article 243ZT's transition was, in effect, a grace period toward a destination the Constitution did not permit.
The Court did not revisit the transitional provision directly, but the ruling implies that State co-operative laws that remained inconsistent with Part IXB beyond the transitional period did not actually become invalid in their application to State co-operatives — since the constitutional requirement they were inconsistent with was itself invalid in that application.

**Sarangdhar Nilkanth Mujumdar v. State of Maharashtra (2013)** — Decided in the transitional window, this case considered whether existing Maharashtra co-operative law provisions on board supersession were saved by Article 243ZT pending conforming amendment.
The court applied the transitional provision as written, preserving existing law until the State acted.
It is one of the few decisions to engage directly with the Article 243ZT mechanism during its operative period.

**State of Maharashtra v. Maharashtra Co-operative Societies (various)** — Maharashtra's high courts addressed numerous instances of challenge to existing bye-laws and administrative orders that predated the 97th Amendment on the ground that they conflicted with Part IXB's new constitutional standards.
Article 243ZT was routinely invoked by the State to defend those instruments during the one-year transitional window, and courts consistently applied the provision as a complete answer to challenges brought before the transitional deadline.

## Comparison with Articles 243N and 243ZF

Article 243ZT is the third in a trilogy of transitional provisions:

- **Article 243N** (Part IX — panchayats): existing laws inconsistent with Part IX continue until amended/repealed or one year from commencement, whichever is earlier.
- **Article 243ZF** (Part IXA — municipalities): same structure for pre-existing municipal law.
- **Article 243ZT** (Part IXB — co-operative societies): the same formula applied to pre-existing co-operative law.

The consistent drafting across all three reflects a constitutional philosophy of managed transition: new constitutional mandates take effect at a fixed commencement date, but a reasonable window is provided to avoid the disruption of immediate invalidity across thousands of existing institutions.
The one-year period was chosen as adequate for legislative amendments while being short enough to prevent indefinite delay.
The "whichever is less" structure prevents States from treating the transitional period as a licence for inaction.

## Current significance

By 2025, Article 243ZT has long discharged its operative function.
Its transitional window closed in February 2013 and its effect is entirely historical.

For multi-State co-operative societies — where Part IXB remains valid after Rajendra N. Shah — the relevant pre-existing Central legislation (the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002) was adjusted during the transitional period.
For State co-operatives, the Rajendra N. Shah ruling means Part IXB's substantive standards never had binding constitutional force, which retroactively diminishes the significance of the transitional savings Article 243ZT provided for them.

The article is studied today primarily for three doctrinal contributions:

1. Its role in the doctrine of managed constitutional transition — showing how constitutional amendments can incorporate time-limited derogations from the new constitutional norm.
2. Its structural parallel with Articles 243N and 243ZF — demonstrating a consistent constitutional drafting approach to phased implementation of new constitutional frameworks for self-governance institutions.
3. As an object lesson in how the retroactive invalidation of a constitutional amendment (through the basic structure doctrine in Rajendra N. Shah) can render a transitional savings provision retrospectively purposeless — the transition was toward a destination that turned out to be constitutionally impermissible.

Future constitutional amendments that seek to impose governance standards on State-list institutions will need to grapple with the Rajendra N. Shah principle, and Article 243ZT's transitional structure provides a template for any future attempt to manage the implementation phase of such amendments.
