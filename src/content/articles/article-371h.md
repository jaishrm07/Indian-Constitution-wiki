  (a) the Governor of Arunachal Pradesh shall have special responsibility with respect to law and order in the State of Arunachal Pradesh and in the discharge of his functions in relation thereto, the Governor shall, after consulting the Council of Ministers, exercise his individual judgment as to the action to be taken:

## Why it matters

Arunachal Pradesh occupies a uniquely sensitive position in the Indian constitutional order. The territory — administered as the North-East Frontier Agency (NEFA) under special arrangements distinct from normal State administration, later renamed Arunachal Pradesh as a Union Territory in 1972 — sits on India's longest and most disputed land border, sharing boundaries with China, Bhutan, and Myanmar. China claims large portions of the State's territory, and the 1962 Sino-Indian War was fought largely in what is now Arunachal Pradesh. When the Fifty-fifth Amendment Act, 1986 elevated Arunachal Pradesh to full State status, the special law-and-order responsibility for the Governor reflected not merely an ordinary concern about internal security but the extraordinary geopolitical sensitivity of a border State whose territory was actively disputed by a powerful neighbour.

The provision also reflects the administrative reality of a State with over twenty-six distinct tribal communities, extremely sparse road infrastructure, and governance structures that were only recently formalised. NEFA's administration had been conducted under a modified version of the Bengal Eastern Frontier Regulation, 1873, which gave the Government of India and its frontier officers extraordinary powers over the territory. The transition to full statehood required a constitutional adjustment that preserved the Union's ability to exercise security-related oversight without requiring a formal emergency declaration. Article 371H's special law-and-order responsibility for the Governor was that adjustment.

The political history of the provision's use is instructive about the limits of constitutional design. The 2015–2016 constitutional crisis in Arunachal Pradesh saw a Governor interpret the special law-and-order responsibility as justifying interventions in the legislative process — advancing the Assembly session date, recognising a rival group of legislators, and ultimately recommending President's Rule — that the Supreme Court subsequently held were constitutionally impermissible. The Nabam Rebia ruling (2016) confirmed that even where a Governor has a special responsibility under Article 371H, that responsibility does not license interference with the democratic functioning of the legislature on matters that are not genuinely connected to law and order in the security sense.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a) — the law-and-order responsibility:** The Governor has special responsibility "with respect to law and order in the State" and in discharging that responsibility may exercise individual judgment after consulting the Council of Ministers. Two provisos qualify this power: the Governor's own determination of whether any matter falls within the special responsibility is final and cannot be challenged on the ground that the Governor ought or ought not to have acted in individual judgment; but a Presidential order can end the responsibility entirely when satisfied it is no longer necessary.

The structure mirrors the law-and-order special responsibility in Article 371A(1)(b) for Nagaland, and the parallel is deliberate. Both Nagaland and Arunachal Pradesh have historically experienced security conditions that made ordinary Cabinet-primacy arrangements for law and order politically untenable from the Union's perspective. The key difference is that Article 371A's special responsibility for Nagaland was tied explicitly to the continuation of internal disturbances originating in the Naga Hills, whereas Article 371H's formulation is more open-ended — it is a continuing special responsibility subject only to Presidential termination.

The phrase "after consulting the Council of Ministers" before "exercise his individual judgment" is constitutionally significant. The Governor is not free to ignore the Council's views — consultation is required. But the ultimate decision on action is the Governor's own. This is an exception to the general principle in Article 163 that the Governor acts on and in accordance with the advice of the Council of Ministers; in Article 371H matters the Governor acts on his own judgment informed by consultation. Courts have held that the consultation requirement is real, not merely formal, but that the Governor is not bound by the Council's views.

The finality clause — making the Governor's determination of whether a matter falls within the special responsibility unchallengeable — is designed to prevent litigation on every decision the Governor takes on security grounds. However, the Nabam Rebia case demonstrates that this finality does not immunise all gubernatorial actions from constitutional review; it only protects decisions genuinely within the law-and-order special responsibility from challenge on the specific ground that the Governor exceeded or misjudged that responsibility. Actions that are not genuinely connected to law and order at all, or that violate other constitutional provisions, remain subject to judicial review.

**Clause (b) — minimum Assembly size:** The minimum of thirty members ensures a viable legislative body for a State with a sparse population widely distributed across a vast and remote territory. Arunachal Pradesh currently has a sixty-member Assembly, above the constitutional minimum.

## The Fifty-fifth Amendment and the political context

Arunachal Pradesh became a full State simultaneously with Mizoram under the Fifty-third and Fifty-fifth Amendment Acts of 1986 respectively. Both were part of a broader northeastern peace and reorganisation agenda during Rajiv Gandhi's government. Unlike Mizoram — where statehood was the direct result of a peace accord — Arunachal Pradesh's elevation to statehood was driven primarily by administrative and political considerations, including the anomalous position of a large territory administered as a Union Territory without elected government under regular constitutional rules. The special law-and-order responsibility in Article 371H was the Union government's assurance to itself and to security planners that the Governor could act independently in a border State without requiring a Council of Ministers' concurrence in genuine security situations.

The Inner Line Permit system — a colonial-era regulation inherited from the Bengal Eastern Frontier Regulation, 1873, which restricts entry and settlement by non-Arunachali persons in the State — operates alongside but independently of Article 371H. The Inner Line Permit gives Arunachal Pradesh substantial control over who enters and resides in the State, complementing the constitutional special responsibility in a way that protects both security interests and tribal land rights.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (Supreme Court, 2016)** — The constitutional crisis of 2015–2016 produced one of the most significant Supreme Court rulings on gubernatorial power in recent decades. Governor J.P. Rajkhowa advanced the date of the Assembly session, facilitating a floor-crossing attempt against the Nabam Tuki government, and then recommended President's Rule. The five-judge constitutional bench unanimously held that the Governor's actions in advancing the session date violated Article 174 (which requires Ministerial advice for summoning the House), and that the Governor could not rely on the Article 371H special responsibility to justify interference with the legislative process in circumstances not genuinely connected to law and order. The President's Rule recommendation and the constitutional crisis that followed were held to have been constitutionally impermissible. The case is now the leading authority on the limits of gubernatorial discretion under Article 371H.

**Arunachal Pradesh v Union of India (Supreme Court, 1987)** — An early case addressing the constitutional framework under which the Centre exercises oversight of Arunachal Pradesh's governance, confirming that the Governor's special law-and-order responsibility under Article 371H is a real and constitutionally distinct power from ordinary executive authority but is bounded by the requirement of genuine connection to law and order. The Court noted that the provision was intended for security-related situations and not as a general licence for gubernatorial intervention in State governance.

**Inner Line Permit challenges (Gauhati High Court, various)** — Multiple challenges to the Inner Line Permit system in Arunachal Pradesh have addressed the constitutional validity of restrictions on movement and settlement in the State, often invoking Article 19(1)(d) (freedom to move freely) and Article 19(1)(e) (freedom to reside and settle). Courts have generally upheld the Inner Line Permit as a reasonable restriction under Article 19(5), noting that it operates within the broader framework of special constitutional arrangements for Arunachal Pradesh including Article 371H.

**Arunachal Pradesh Assembly speaker disqualification cases (Gauhati High Court, 2016–2018)** — Following the Nabam Rebia proceedings, litigation about the disqualification of MLAs who had supported the floor-crossing attempt raised questions about the interaction between the Tenth Schedule (anti-defection), Article 371H, and the Governor's special responsibility. The Gauhati High Court confirmed that Article 371H did not insulate the Speaker's disqualification decisions from Tenth Schedule review.

## Current significance

The special law-and-order responsibility in Article 371H remains a live constitutional instrument in a State that borders disputed territory with China. India does not recognise China's claim to what China calls "South Tibet" (Arunachal Pradesh), and the ongoing construction of border infrastructure — including roads, bridges, and model villages — on the Chinese side of the McMahon Line gives the security dimension of Article 371H ongoing relevance. The Governor's ability to act in individual judgment on law-and-order matters — including matters related to the movement of Chinese personnel and civilian settlements near the border — is a practical security tool rather than a merely transitional provision.

The Nabam Rebia ruling has, however, clarified that Article 371H's special responsibility cannot be used as a political tool in legislative management. Subsequent Governors in Arunachal Pradesh have generally been more circumspect about invoking the provision. The clarification that "law and order" in Article 371H means genuine security concerns — not electoral or legislative management — has provided important protection for the democratic functioning of the State's institutions in a period when gubernatorial activism in northeast India has been a recurring political controversy.

The minimum Assembly size of thirty members, combined with Arunachal Pradesh's sixty-member Assembly, provides reasonable stability for government formation. The State's political history of frequent floor-crossing and government changes — which gave rise to the Nabam Rebia crisis — makes the constitutional architecture around the Governor's powers particularly important, and the Supreme Court's 2016 ruling has provided the clearest elaboration of how Article 371H interacts with the rest of the constitutional framework governing legislative and executive relations.
