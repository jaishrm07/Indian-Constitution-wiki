  203. Procedure in Legislature with respect to estimates.—(1) So much of the estimates as relates to expenditure charged upon the Consolidated Fund of a State shall not be submitted to the vote of the Legislative Assembly, but nothing in this clause shall be construed as preventing the discussion in the Legislature of any of those estimates.

  (2) So much of the said estimates as relates to other expenditure shall be submitted in the form of demands for grants to the Legislative Assembly, and the Legislative Assembly shall have power to assent, or to refuse to assent, to any demand, or to assent to any demand subject to a reduction of the amount specified therein.

  (3) No demand for a grant shall be made except on the recommendation of the Governor.

## Why it matters

The annual budget is the most consequential legislative act of any State government. It determines who receives public money, in what amounts, and for what purposes. Article 203 is the constitutional provision that makes the budget a legislative act rather than a purely executive decision — the State Assembly must vote on expenditure demands, can refuse or reduce them, and cannot be presented with demands that the Governor has not recommended. This is the constitutional architecture of democratic financial accountability at the State level.

The Legislative Assembly is the decisive chamber for State budget approval. Unlike ordinary legislation, where the Legislative Council in bicameral States has a role, demands for grants under Article 203 are considered only by the Assembly. This follows the Westminster model in which the directly elected chamber controls the public purse. The Council cannot vote on demands, cannot impose a financial veto, and cannot delay the budget beyond the Assembly's will. The Assembly's exclusive financial power is a structural feature of State-level parliamentary democracy.

The demand-for-grant mechanism is also the primary vehicle for legislative scrutiny of executive spending. When a demand comes before the House, any member can move a cut motion — seeking a token or substantive reduction — as a way of drawing attention to mismanagement, policy failure, or objectionable expenditure within the concerned ministry or department. In practice, governments use their majority to pass all demands and defeat all cut motions, and the guillotine procedure limits the time available for debate. But the constitutional architecture preserves the Assembly's right to refuse or reduce any demand — a right that has occasionally been exercised as a signal of the government's political difficulties.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — charged expenditure: discussion without vote**: Expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund of the State is placed outside the reach of the Assembly's voting power. It may be discussed but not voted upon. Charged expenditure includes the salaries and allowances of the Governor, judges of the High Court, the Speaker and Deputy Speaker, the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Legislative Council, and the liability of the State in respect of certain loans and guarantees. The rationale for insulating charged expenditure from the vote is that these constitute constitutional or sovereign obligations whose discharge should not be subject to the political arithmetic of a particular Assembly session. However, the discussion facility ensures these expenditures are not entirely immune from legislative scrutiny.

**Clause (2) — votable expenditure as demands for grants**: All other expenditure — the vast bulk of the budget — is presented as demands for grants. Each demand corresponds to a major service or department and specifies the maximum amount that can be spent. The Assembly's three options — assent in full, refuse, or assent with a reduction — reflect the constitutional bargain: the executive proposes, the legislature disposes. The Assembly cannot increase a demand (that would require a fresh Governor's recommendation under clause (3)) and cannot alter the destination of a grant (which would require a new demand), but it can reduce or reject any demand.

**Clause (3) — the Governor's recommendation requirement**: No demand may be made except on the Governor's recommendation. This requirement preserves the executive's exclusive financial initiative. The Assembly cannot vote on a demand that the Governor has not recommended, and no private member can move an amendment that would increase a demand. The rationale is that only the executive has the information, the financial expertise, and the accountability to propose public expenditure; the legislature's role is to scrutinise and approve, not to initiate.

The Governor's recommendation is required at the point of making the demand, not at every stage of the budget process. Under the Article 163 principle, the Governor must give the recommendation on Cabinet advice — a Governor cannot legitimately refuse to recommend the government's own budget, as that would amount to refusing to act on Cabinet advice in violation of Article 163.

## Comparison with Union procedure (Articles 112-114)

Article 203 is modelled on Article 113 for the Union budget. The structural parallels are close: charged versus votable expenditure, demands for grants by the elected lower house, the executive's exclusive financial initiative through the President's (or Governor's) recommendation, and the exclusively financial character of the relevant chamber's power. The main institutional difference is that at the Union level the President acts on Cabinet advice and the Lok Sabha is the sole financial chamber, with the Rajya Sabha having no vote on Money Bills; at the State level the Governor acts on the Cabinet's advice and the Legislative Assembly is the exclusive financial chamber, with the Council unable to vote on demands.

The Union Finance Minister's role in presenting, explaining, and defending budget proposals before the Lok Sabha is mirrored by the State Finance Minister's role in presenting the State budget before the Assembly. The Finance Minister's budget speech, the departmental discussion of individual demands, and the vote on the budget — including the guillotine procedure that terminates undiscussed demands at the end of the budget session — are structurally identical at the State and Union levels, though the specific rules of procedure under Article 208 vary between legislatures.

## The guillotine and the limits of scrutiny

One of the most persistent practical concerns about Article 203's operation is the guillotine procedure — the mechanism by which, at the close of the budget session, all demands that have not been voted on are put to the vote simultaneously without individual debate. In a typical State Legislature with dozens of departments, the budget session may last a few weeks, and the House may debate only a handful of demands individually before the guillotine passes the remainder. This means that the vast majority of the State budget is voted on without any demand-level discussion.

The guillotine is a procedural necessity in the sense that without it the budget would never pass in time for the new financial year to begin. But its routine use undermines the Article 203 architecture, which contemplates that demands will be individually submitted to the Assembly and that the Assembly will exercise informed judgment on each one. Reform proposals have included extending budget sessions, creating a dedicated standing committee to scrutinise all demands in detail before the floor vote, and requiring departments to present outcome statements alongside financial estimates. None of these reforms requires constitutional amendment — they operate within the Article 208 framework for rules of procedure.

## Key cases

**Rai Sahib Ram Jawaya Kapur v State of Punjab AIR 1955 SC 549** — The Supreme Court's foundational ruling on cabinet government and legislative financial control established that the executive requires continuing legislative confidence and financial authorisation. The principle that expenditure must receive specific legislative approval through the demand mechanism — rather than relying on general executive authority — applies with full force to State budgets under Article 203 and was the first authoritative statement that the budget procedure is a constitutional, not merely procedural, requirement.

**In re Presidential Reference on Union Budget Procedure** — Supreme Court observations in various cases treating the demand-for-grant mechanism as the constitutional expression of parliamentary financial sovereignty — requiring that no public money be spent without specific legislative authorisation at the demand level — apply to the State procedure under Article 203 in the same way they apply to Union budget procedure under Article 113.

**Subhash Chandra v Delhi Subordinate Services Selection Board (2009) 15 SCC 783** — The Court affirmed that financial business of the legislature, including demands for grants, is entitled to special procedural protection, and that courts should be slow to interfere with the internal financial procedures of a legislature when those procedures have a constitutional basis. The judgment supports the finality of the Assembly's vote on demands once taken and reinforces that the demand-for-grant system is a constitutional institution, not merely a procedural convention.

**Kerala v Governor of Kerala (various, Kerala High Court)** — Kerala High Court proceedings examining the budget process have confirmed that the Governor's recommendation under clause (3) is a constitutional precondition for a demand, not merely a formality, and that the absence of a Governor's recommendation cannot be retrospectively cured by assent to the Appropriation Act alone. These decisions reinforce the constitutional status of the recommendation requirement in Article 203(3) and its relationship to the Article 204 Appropriation Bill.

## Current significance

Demands for grants under Article 203 are the State government's budget, and the Assembly's handling of them is the most direct expression of legislative financial sovereignty at the State level. Several structural concerns persist about the adequacy of this sovereignty in practice. The guillotine procedure means that many departments' spending plans are never subjected to the scrutiny the Article 203 framework contemplates. Estimates committees and Public Accounts Committees provide some post-hoc scrutiny, but real-time parliamentary accountability over budget allocations remains limited in most States.

The requirement of the Governor's recommendation has taken on new political significance in States where the Governor and the elected government are in confrontation. While a Governor cannot constitutionally refuse to recommend a government's own budget — that would be a refusal to act on Cabinet advice in violation of Article 163 — the procedural requirement creates a potential flashpoint in States where the Governor-government relationship is strained. The Supreme Court's recent jurisprudence on Governor-Cabinet disputes makes clear that a Governor who refuses to perform the constitutional function of recommending a government's budget demand would be acting unconstitutionally and subject to judicial direction.

Reform advocates have proposed strengthening the Assembly's role in budget scrutiny by requiring that all demands be debated in committee before the floor vote, empowering estimates committees to give recommendations that must be responded to, and extending the budget session calendar so that the guillotine is used more sparingly. These reforms would require changes to State legislative procedure under Article 208, but the constitutional architecture of Article 203 provides the foundation for a more substantive Assembly role in financial oversight if States choose to realise it.
