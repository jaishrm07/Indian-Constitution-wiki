  315. Public Service Commissions for the Union and for the States.—

  (1) Subject to the provisions of this article, there shall be a Public Service Commission for the
  Union and a Public Service Commission for each State.

  (2) Two or more States may agree that there shall be one Public Service Commission for that group of
  States, and if a resolution to that effect is passed by the House or, where there are two Houses, by
  each House of the Legislature of each of those States, Parliament may by law provide for the
  appointment of a Joint State Public Service Commission (referred to in this Chapter as Joint
  Commission) to serve the needs of those States.

  (3) Any such law as aforesaid may contain such incidental and consequential provisions as may be
  necessary or desirable for giving effect to the purposes of the law.

  (4) The Public Service Commission for the Union, if requested so to do by the Governor of a State,
  may, with the approval of the President, agree to serve all or any of the needs of the State.

  Commission shall, unless the context otherwise requires, be construed as references to the Commission
  serving the needs of the Union or, as the case may be, the State as respects the particular matter

## Why it matters

Democracies can be captured by patronage — governments staffing the bureaucracy with loyalists rather
than meritocrats. The history of public administration in colonial and post-colonial states alike shows
that where civil service recruitment is controlled by the executive without independent oversight,
appointments quickly become instruments of political reward. Article 315 guards against this by making
independent Public Service Commissions a constitutional requirement rather than a policy choice.
Because the Commission is created by the Constitution itself, no ordinary legislation of Parliament
or a State Legislature can abolish it, and its independence is reinforced by security-of-tenure
provisions for its members under Articles 316 and 317, by the prohibition on post-retirement
employment in the government it served under Article 319, and by the mandatory consultation
requirements of Article 320.

The framers modelled the PSC structure on the pre-independence public service commission arrangements
introduced under the Government of India Act 1919 and expanded under the Government of India Act
1935, but gave them an explicitly constitutional status that the colonial versions lacked. The
significance of this elevation cannot be overstated: a statutory body can be modified or replaced by
a simple majority; a constitutional body requires constitutional amendment. Ambedkar in the Constituent
Assembly made it clear that merit-based selection was essential to the equality values of the new
republic — a bureaucracy selected on patronage grounds would inevitably entrench the advantages of
those already powerful, while one selected on merit could serve as an equalising institution. Article
315 is the institutional embodiment of that commitment.

The UPSC and State PSCs are advisory and recommendatory bodies, not appointing authorities. Final
appointment is made by the executive. However, the advisory character of the UPSC does not reduce it
to a rubber stamp. Its constitutional status and the conventions surrounding its operation mean that
executive overrides of UPSC recommendations require justification and are subject to judicial
scrutiny. In practice, the government almost invariably follows UPSC recommendations in the Civil
Services Examination, with the notable exception of decisions about reservation policy and the number
of posts in each category — matters on which the executive has broader discretion. The relationship
between the UPSC's merit-based recommendations and reservation requirements under Articles 15(4) and
16(4) is one of the most complex and litigated intersections in Indian constitutional law.

Article 315 must be read as part of a broader Chapter (Chapter II of Part XIV, Articles 315–323)
that comprehensively regulates the PSC's composition, terms of office, removal, powers, and
relationship with the executive. Reading Article 315 in isolation misses its function: it is the
founding provision of a constitutional institution, and the subsequent articles build out the
institutional design. The chapter as a whole represents one of the most detailed attempts in the
Constitution to create an independent body insulated from executive capture — more detailed, in some
respects, than even the provisions on the judiciary, precisely because the framers understood that
the judiciary had the bench's own self-governing tradition while the PSC needed explicit textual
protection.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) makes the existence of a UPSC and a State PSC for each State mandatory — the word "shall"
is operative. This means a State government cannot simply disband its PSC by passing a State law or
by executive order. Any attempt to do so would be unconstitutional. The existence of the commission
is not discretionary. The clause is, however, subject to the other provisions of Article 315 itself
— meaning that the flexible arrangements for Joint Commissions and UPSC assistance in clauses (2)
and (4) can modify the basic obligation, but only within the parameters those clauses themselves set.
A State cannot avoid the obligation by contracting out to a private body or by creating an exempt
category of "non-civil" posts that effectively replaces the PSC.

Clauses (2) and (3) allow flexibility by permitting two or more States to agree to a Joint State
PSC, with Parliament providing for it by law after the State Legislatures pass the necessary
resolutions. This mechanism is unusual in that it requires both State legislative action (resolution)
and Union legislative action (law by Parliament), reflecting the joint character of the institution
to be created. The law under clause (2) can include any incidental provisions necessary to give
effect to the joint commission's functioning. In practice, early years of the Constitution saw some
use of joint commission arrangements — particularly in what were then Madhya Pradesh and adjacent
States before reorganisation — but most States today have their own PSCs, and the Joint Commission
mechanism is rarely invoked.

Clause (4) enables the UPSC to serve a State's needs if the Governor requests and the President
approves. This is a practical provision that allows States with inadequate infrastructure —
particularly smaller States or newly created Union Territories — to draw on the UPSC's examination
and selection machinery. The UPSC's assistance under clause (4) is consensual and conditional on the
President's approval, ensuring that the UPSC's resources are not stretched in ways that compromise
its primary responsibility to serve the Union's civil service needs.

Clause (5) is an interpretation provision that ensures references in the Constitution to the "Union
Public Service Commission" or "a State Public Service Commission" are read as references to whichever
body is actually serving the relevant needs, whether that is the UPSC acting under clause (4) or a
Joint Commission acting under clause (2). This prevents any gap in constitutional coverage arising
from the flexible arrangements in earlier sub-clauses — the constitutional protections and the
consultation requirements follow the function, not the formal label of the institution.

The UPSC's role is consultative and recommendatory: it selects and recommends candidates, but the
final appointment is made by the executive. Article 320 sets out the matters on which consultation
with the UPSC is mandatory — recruitment, promotions, transfers, disciplinary matters. Courts have
recognised that while the government retains formal power to reject UPSC recommendations, acting
contrary to the UPSC's advice without constitutionally valid grounds and recorded reasons is subject
to judicial review for arbitrariness. The distinction between advisory and binding recommendations
has been one of the primary legal issues in PSC litigation, with courts steadily expanding the
circumstances in which deviations from UPSC recommendations must be explained and justified.

## Key cases

**Union Public Service Commission v S Papaiah (1997)** — The Supreme Court held that the UPSC's
constitutional status gives it independence from executive direction in selection matters, and that
recommendations made after a proper selection process cannot ordinarily be set aside by the
government without constitutionally valid grounds and recorded reasons. The case arose when a State
government sought to reject a UPSC recommendation on grounds that the Court found to be neither
valid nor recorded with sufficient particularity. The decision is the leading authority on the weight
that must be given to UPSC recommendations and on the limits of the executive's power to override
them in individual appointment decisions.

**K Nagaraj v State of Andhra Pradesh (1985)** — The Court discussed the mandatory institutional
character of the PSCs created by Article 315, affirming that the Constitution imposes an obligation
to maintain these bodies as genuinely independent selection institutions. The case also touched on
the relationship between Article 315's institutional requirement and the substantive consultation
obligation under Article 320, confirming that both are constitutional obligations and that compliance
with one does not excuse non-compliance with the other.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v Manbodhan Lal Srivastava (1957)** — An early Constitution Bench decision
that examined the relationship between the institutional existence of the UPSC under Article 315 and
its consultative functions under Article 320. The Court held that the commission must exist as a
meaningful body, not merely a rubber stamp, and that the constitutional scheme requires both
structural independence (guaranteed by Articles 315–317) and functional consultation (guaranteed by
Article 320). Abolishing the commission through legislative indirection — for instance by emptying
Article 320's consultation requirement of content — would be as unconstitutional as directly
abolishing the commission itself.

**K V Raghunath v State of Karnataka** — The Court examined the boundary between advisory and
binding UPSC recommendations, holding that while recommendations in selection matters carry great
constitutional weight, the executive retains the power to make the appointment decision and can, in
appropriate circumstances, depart from the recommendation — but must do so transparently and with
reasons that withstand judicial scrutiny. The decision draws on the principle that a constitutional
advisory body with independent status cannot be treated as irrelevant to the final decision; the
government that consistently ignores PSC recommendations without adequate justification risks a
finding of unconstitutional arbitrariness.

**A K Kraipak v Union of India (1969)** — Although this decision is primarily associated with natural
justice doctrine, its implications for the UPSC are significant. The Court held that selection bodies
— including those conducting recruitment processes under constitutional mandate — are subject to
rules of natural justice, and that a member of a selection committee who is also a candidate before
it cannot participate in the selection process. The principle protects the integrity of the selection
process from internal conflict-of-interest, reinforcing the independence that Article 315 structurally
provides and confirming that constitutional status does not exempt a body from procedural fairness
obligations.

## Current significance

The UPSC administers the Civil Services Examination — among the most competitive examinations in the
world, with hundreds of thousands of candidates competing for a few hundred posts each year — and
its selection process is regularly subject to judicial scrutiny. Recurring legal issues include the
validity of reservation policy decisions, the introduction of new examination patterns or syllabus
changes challenged as arbitrary or discriminatory, age-limit relaxations for specific categories of
candidates, the handling of irregularities discovered after selection (question-paper leaks,
impersonation, falsification of certificates), and the validity of one-time special recruitment
rounds outside the normal Civil Services Examination framework. Each of these matters requires the
Court to calibrate the degree of deference owed to the UPSC's technical expertise against the
constitutional requirement that selections be fair, transparent, and rule-bound.

State PSCs face a different and more severe set of pressures. Recurring scandals over question-paper
leaks — in Bihar, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, and other States — have led to large-scale cancellations
of selections, the appointment of inquiry commissions, and criminal prosecution of officials and
candidates. These scandals raise the question of whether the constitutional design of the PSC is
being effectively implemented at the State level, and whether the removal and appointment provisions
of Articles 316 and 317 are adequate to hold commission members accountable for institutional
failures that are systemic rather than individual.

The proliferation of recruitment boards and commissions outside the PSC structure — justified on the
ground that certain posts are "Group C" or "Group D" and exempt from mandatory PSC consultation under
Article 320's proviso — raises the deeper question of whether the constitutional design of merit-
based independent selection is being hollowed out in practice. The growth of contractualisation in
public employment, the increased use of outsourcing arrangements, and the creation of autonomous
bodies with their own recruitment processes all expand the zone of public employment that falls outside
Article 315's constitutional framework. Whether this represents legitimate administrative flexibility
or a systematic erosion of the constitutional commitment to merit-based selection through independent
commissions remains one of the most important unresolved questions in the governance of India's
public services, and one that courts have thus far addressed only at its edges rather than in its
full constitutional depth.
