  If a High Court sentences someone to death — especially when it reverses an earlier acquittal — the Constitution gives that person an automatic right to appeal to the Supreme Court. No certificate is required, no discretionary leave must be granted. The right is guaranteed because the stakes are the highest possible. officialText: |-
  134. Appellate jurisdiction of Supreme Court in regard to criminal matters.—(1) An appeal shall lie to the Supreme Court from any judgment, final order or sentence in a criminal proceeding of a High Court in the territory of India if the High Court—

  (a) has on appeal reversed an order of acquittal of an accused person and sentenced him to death; or

  (b) has withdrawn for trial before itself any case from any court subordinate to its authority and has in such trial convicted the accused person and sentenced him to death; or

  (c) [certifies under article 134A] that the case is a fit one for appeal to the Supreme Court:

  Provided that an appeal under sub-clause (c) shall lie subject to such provisions as may be made in that behalf under clause (1) of article 145 and to such conditions as the High Court may establish or require.

  (2) Parliament may by law confer on the Supreme Court any further powers to entertain and hear appeals from any judgment, final order or sentence in a criminal proceeding of a High Court in the territory of India subject to such conditions and limitations as may be specified in such law.

## Why it matters

The ultimate human stake in criminal appellate jurisdiction is the difference between imprisonment and freedom, between life and death. Article 134 was designed by the framers with an acute awareness that criminal justice — especially at its most severe — demands the highest level of judicial scrutiny. The automatic right of appeal in death-sentence cases, particularly where a High Court has reversed an acquittal to impose the sentence, reflects a constitutional judgment that no life should be taken on the sole authority of a High Court without the Supreme Court having reviewed the matter. This safeguard is not dependent on the convicted person's resources, the quality of their legal representation, or the persuasiveness of their appeal — the right exists by operation of the Constitution itself.

The provision matters also because Indian criminal justice handles an enormous volume of cases, and the capital sentence — though applied rarely — is imposed on the most heinous crimes. The consistency of death penalty jurisprudence across different High Courts and different benches is a matter of national constitutional concern. When the High Court in one state applies the "rarest of rare" doctrine strictly and another applies it more freely, or when different Supreme Court benches reach conflicting conclusions about what the doctrine requires, the Article 134 appeal channel is the mechanism through which the apex court can attempt to impose doctrinal uniformity. That uniformity is imperfect — the Supreme Court itself has acknowledged inconsistencies — but the appellate channel is at least the constitutional instrument through which the attempt can be made.

The provision interacts with Article 21 — the right to life and personal liberty — in a profound way. Because the death penalty is constitutionally permitted only subject to the "procedure established by law," and because that procedure must itself be fair, just, and reasonable under Maneka Gandhi's reading of Article 21, every aspect of the criminal appellate process in capital cases carries constitutional weight. An appeal under Article 134 is therefore not merely a procedural step; it is itself a component of the constitutionally mandated procedure through which the State may lawfully impose the death penalty.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)(a) — reversal of acquittal with death sentence**: The most significant of the three grounds for automatic appeal. When a person is acquitted by a trial court and then, on the State's appeal, the High Court reverses that acquittal and sentences the accused to death, the convicted person has an immediate constitutional right to appeal to the Supreme Court. The logic is compelling: the accused has already been found not guilty by one court; the High Court has now made the most drastic reversal possible — from acquittal to death. No system that takes due process seriously can allow such an outcome without the apex court's review. The Constitution guarantees this review regardless of whether the accused can afford it.

**Clause (1)(b) — High Court trial with death sentence**: When a High Court exercises its powers to withdraw a case from a subordinate court for trial before itself, and convicts and sentences the accused to death in that trial, an automatic right of appeal lies. This is a less common scenario but equally constitutionally significant — the accused is being sentenced to death in a proceeding that began before the High Court rather than a sessions court, and the Supreme Court's appellate scrutiny is similarly guaranteed.

**Clause (1)(c) — certificate jurisdiction**: For criminal cases that do not involve an automatic right of appeal, a High Court may certify under Article 134A that the case is "a fit one for appeal to the Supreme Court." The certificate standard here is less specific than the civil standard under Article 133 — a "fit" case is one where the High Court considers that the case raises issues of law or fact that merit the Supreme Court's attention. The proviso subjects certificate appeals to rules made under Article 145(1) and conditions the High Court may impose.

**Clause (2) — parliamentary extension**: Parliament may by legislation extend criminal appellate jurisdiction further. This power was exercised through the Supreme Court (Enlargement of Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction) Act, 1970, which provides that an appeal shall lie to the Supreme Court from any judgment, final order, or sentence in a criminal proceeding where (a) the High Court has on appeal reversed an order of acquittal and convicted the accused and sentenced him to imprisonment for life or for a period of not less than ten years, or (b) the High Court has imposed on the accused a sentence of imprisonment for life or for a period of not less than ten years. This legislative extension significantly broadened Article 134's reach beyond the automatic constitutional right in capital cases.

## The death penalty and Article 134 — a constitutional arc

### The original framework

The original Constitution did not itself regulate when the death penalty could be imposed; it left that to Parliament and to the criminal law. Article 134's automatic appeal in capital cases was the constitutional guarantee of Supreme Court scrutiny, but the substantive doctrine governing when death was an appropriate sentence remained largely undeveloped until the 1970s.

### Bachan Singh and the rarest of rare doctrine

The watershed moment came in Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980), a Constitution Bench decision that upheld the constitutionality of the death penalty under Article 21 but simultaneously imposed a rigorous doctrinal constraint on its application. The Court held that the death penalty can be imposed only in the "rarest of rare" cases — a category the Court defined through qualitative factors about the nature of the crime and the character of the criminal. The prosecution bears the burden of establishing aggravating circumstances; the accused has the right to place mitigating circumstances before the court; and the sentence of death is appropriate only when the alternative of life imprisonment is "unquestionably foreclosed."

Bachan Singh reshaped the Article 134 appellate process. After 1980, every death sentence appealed to the Supreme Court — whether under Article 134 or Article 136 — would be assessed against the rarest of rare standard. The High Court's certification in a death case under Article 134(1)(c) would necessarily have to account for the Bachan Singh framework.

### Inconsistency and the Court's own critique

Despite Bachan Singh, the Court acknowledged in later decisions that the rarest of rare doctrine had been applied inconsistently. In Sangeet v. State of Haryana (2013), a two-judge bench made the remarkable observation that the doctrine had "become a patchwork" — that different benches of the Supreme Court had applied it to reach incompatible conclusions on materially similar facts. The judgment called for a comprehensive review of death penalty jurisprudence by a larger bench. That review has not yet been comprehensively completed, and the acknowledged inconsistency remains a live problem in Article 134 criminal appellate practice.

### The 44th Amendment — relevant context

The Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act 1978 made Article 20 and 21 non-suspendable even during a Proclamation of Emergency. This change has significance for Article 134: because Article 21's procedural requirements cannot be suspended even during an emergency, the constitutional protections attaching to criminal proceedings — including the right of appeal in capital cases under Article 134 — cannot be suspended even when other fundamental rights are curtailed. The amendment was a response to the Emergency-era experience in which procedural protections were stripped from criminal proceedings, and it ensures that the right to life is procedurally protected at all times.

## Key cases

**Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)** — The foundational Constitution Bench decision on the death penalty. The Court upheld the constitutionality of the death penalty under Articles 21 and 14 but introduced the "rarest of rare" doctrine as the substantive standard for its application. The judgment was reached through the Article 134 criminal appellate jurisdiction and established the framework within which every subsequent death penalty appeal — whether under Article 134 or Article 136 — is argued and decided. The rarest of rare doctrine is the most consequential contribution of Article 134 jurisprudence to Indian criminal law.

**Machhi Singh v. State of Punjab (1983)** — Following Bachan Singh, the Supreme Court used its criminal appellate jurisdiction to elaborate the factors distinguishing cases warranting the death penalty: the manner of commission of the murder, the motive, the anti-social nature of the crime, and the magnitude of the offence. The case tried to operationalise the rarest of rare framework by specifying categories — mass murders, murders of innocent children, murders involving extreme brutality, murders by a person in a position of dominance. This categorisation has been the primary practical guide for trial and High Courts applying Bachan Singh.

**Swami Shraddananda v. State of Karnataka (2008)** — The Supreme Court, exercising its criminal appellate jurisdiction, substituted a life sentence — with the specific direction that the prisoner would not be released for at least twenty years — for a death sentence. The case is significant because it introduced the concept of a modified life sentence as an intermediate punishment between regular life imprisonment and death, and recognised that in some cases the court may impose life imprisonment with a fixed minimum term to reflect the gravity of the crime without resorting to execution. This modification, available when the appellate court exercises Article 134 or Article 136 jurisdiction, has become an important tool in capital cases.

**Sangeet v. State of Haryana (2013)** — A two-judge bench of the Supreme Court expressed concern about inconsistency in the application of the rarest of rare doctrine across different benches. The judgment observed that the doctrine has not been applied consistently and that the Court's own death penalty jurisprudence contains contradictions that cannot be reconciled without a comprehensive review by a larger bench. This candid acknowledgement of doctrinal incoherence is important for understanding the limits of what Article 134 appellate jurisdiction can achieve when the substantive standard being applied is itself unsettled.

**Santosh Kumar Satishbhushan Bariyar v. State of Maharashtra (2009)** — The Court upheld a death sentence conviction under the Article 134/136 criminal appellate jurisdiction and in the process addressed the procedural obligations of courts in death penalty cases — specifically, that sentencing must be based on evidence, not mere intuition, and that the accused must have a genuine opportunity to place mitigating circumstances before the court at the sentencing stage. The judgment reinforced that the Article 21 right to a fair sentencing procedure is a component of the constitutional process through which the death penalty may be imposed.

**Mohd. Arif v. The Registrar, Supreme Court of India (2014)** — A Constitution Bench held that death row prisoners whose review petitions are dismissed have a right to an oral hearing before a three-judge bench. This procedural guarantee — going beyond what the standard Article 134 and Article 136 appellate processes would otherwise require — was derived from Article 21 and the requirement that a prisoner facing execution be given the maximum procedural protection available. The judgment illustrates how Article 134's appellate framework interacts with Article 21 to require the highest procedural standards in capital cases.

**Shatrughan Chauhan v. Union of India (2014)** — The Supreme Court, in an Article 32 petition arising from the Article 134/136 appellate process, held that undue delay in executing a death sentence can itself be a violation of Article 21 entitling the prisoner to commutation of sentence to life imprisonment. The Court identified factors including the time spent on death row, mental illness, solitary confinement, and delays in the mercy petition process as mitigating circumstances. This case extended the constitutional protection of death row prisoners beyond the trial and appellate stage into the post-conviction executive process.

## Current significance

Article 134 remains constitutionally vital as the formal guarantee that capital sentences receive Supreme Court scrutiny. In practice, death sentence appeals now routinely arrive at the Supreme Court through both Article 134 and Article 136 routes — the procedural distinction between them matters less than the substance of the review. What matters constitutionally is that the review happens at all, that it applies the rarest of rare doctrine rigorously, and that the procedural guarantees of Article 21 are respected throughout.

The pending comprehensive review of death penalty jurisprudence — called for in Sangeet and still awaited — remains one of the most significant unresolved questions in Indian constitutional criminal law. The inconsistency in how the rarest of rare standard is applied means that the availability of the death penalty can vary depending on which bench of the Supreme Court happens to hear a particular appeal. This randomness is in fundamental tension with the equal protection guarantee of Article 14 and with the requirement of Article 21 that procedures affecting the right to life be fair and reasonable.

The growing scholarly and advocacy movement against the death penalty — including the Law Commission's 2015 report recommending its abolition except in terrorism cases — has placed Article 134's constitutional machinery at the centre of a broader debate about whether the Indian State should retain the power to execute people at all. Until that debate is constitutionally resolved, Article 134 will continue to be the provision that ensures the ultimate decision is made by the country's highest court, applying the most exacting standards the Constitution requires.
