## Why it matters

Article 160 is the constitutional safety valve for an unexpected gap in the State's executive headship. Chapter II of Part VI creates a detailed framework for the Governor's appointment (Article 155), qualifications (Article 157), conditions of office (Article 158), oath (Article 159), term and removal (Article 156), and pardon powers (Article 161). But no code of rules can anticipate every contingency. A Governor might be incapacitated mid-term without having formally resigned, or a newly appointed Governor might not have taken the oath when an urgent constitutional act — signing an ordinance, summoning the legislature, accepting a resignation — becomes necessary. Article 160 ensures that the gap is filled not by executive default or improvisation, but by a constitutionally authorised presidential order.

The provision's structural importance lies in what it is not. It is not an alternative route to President's Rule under Article 356. When a Governor is temporarily incapacitated or unavailable, constitutional government in the State is not broken down — the elected ministry continues to govern on behalf of the people. Article 160 merely bridges the administrative gap at the top of the State executive hierarchy by designating a temporary substitute for the Governor's constitutionally defined functions. Any attempt to use Article 160 as a pretext for displacing an elected government or expanding Union control would be constitutionally illegitimate and judicially reviewable.

The provision's significance has grown as the Governor's role has become more politically contested. When a Governor resigns or is incapacitated at a moment of political sensitivity — a pending floor test, a disputed Bill requiring assent, a session of the legislature about to meet — the question of who acts as Governor becomes decisive. The President's choice of a substitute is then not merely administrative but potentially consequential for State political outcomes.

## Reading the provision

**Residual character.** The Article 160 power is explicitly residual — it applies only to contingencies "not provided for in this Chapter" (Chapter II of Part VI). This means that if another provision already covers the situation, Article 160 does not apply. For instance, when the Governor is absent from the State for a short official trip but is not incapacitated, the routine arrangements for official residence and conduct of business already provide for temporary absences in practice, even if no separate constitutional provision expressly covers them. Article 160 operates in the gap between the express provisions.

**The President's power — "as he thinks fit."** The standard is broad: the President "may make such provision as he thinks fit." This is not a ministerially advised power on the article's face, but the general constitutional principle in Shamsher Singh and its progeny — that the President acts on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers in all non-discretionary matters — applies here. The choice of who will discharge the Governor's functions in a contingency is made by the Union Council of Ministers acting through the President, not by the President in personal discretion.

**The designated person's authority.** A person designated under Article 160 exercises the Governor's functions as defined in Part VI — not expanded powers, not Union-delegated administrative authority, but the same constitutional functions a regular Governor would exercise. The designated person must take the Article 159 oath before acting. They do not acquire a five-year term under Article 156. Their authority is temporary and expires either when the substantive Governor resumes functions or when a new Governor is appointed and takes the oath.

**Interaction with Article 163.** The Council of Ministers headed by the Chief Minister aids and advises the Governor (or the person discharging gubernatorial functions) in the same way under Article 163. The designation of a temporary substitute under Article 160 does not alter the constitutional relationship between the State executive and the State's ministerial government. The acting Governor remains as constitutionally bound to follow ministerial advice — in all matters that are not discretionary — as a regularly appointed Governor would be.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Article 160's predecessor provision in the draft Constitution generated relatively brief discussion. The framers' principal concern was ensuring that a temporary gap in the gubernatorial office did not create a power vacuum at the State level. The mechanism was seen as analogous to provisions for contingencies in the Union executive, particularly Article 65, which covers the Vice-President acting as President. The key distinction the framers drew was that at the State level, no single deputy office equivalent to the Vice-Presidency was pre-designated, so the President needed broader discretion to designate whoever was most appropriate in context.

Some members of the Constituent Assembly questioned whether giving the President open-ended discretion to choose who acts as Governor was too broad a grant of Union power over State constitutional arrangements. The response was that the provision was purely functional — it was designed to fill gaps, not to govern States, and the designated person would exercise State executive functions within the same constitutional constraints as any regular Governor. The Assembly accepted this framing, treating the provision as a housekeeping measure rather than a significant constitutional design choice.

## Relationship with Article 356 — a critical distinction

The most important boundary Article 160 must observe is its distinction from Article 356. When a Governor is temporarily unavailable, there is no constitutional breakdown — the elected government of the State continues to function, the legislature continues to meet, and the constitutional machinery is intact. The President's Article 160 order is purely administrative: it designates someone to sign papers and perform constitutional acts that require the Governor's signature or presence.

Article 356, by contrast, authorises the imposition of President's Rule when "a situation has arisen in which the Government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of this Constitution." The two provisions are categorically different in constitutional character, consequence, and the degree of judicial scrutiny they attract. An Article 160 designation is almost certainly not justiciable in the way that an Article 356 proclamation is after Bommai. But any attempt to use an Article 160 arrangement as a pretext to expand Union influence over State governance beyond the purely functional gap it is designed to fill would attract immediate scrutiny under the Bommai federalism framework.

## Acting Governor arrangements in practice

In practice, Article 160 is most commonly invoked by designating a senior judge of the High Court — typically the Chief Justice — to act as Governor when the substantive Governor is unavailable. This has a long history in Indian constitutional practice. In some cases, the Governor of an adjacent State has been given additional charge under the multi-State arrangement authorised by the proviso to Article 153. When neither is feasible, the President issues an order under Article 160 designating a specific member of the Assembly or another appropriate person.

A specific practical scenario that Article 160 covers is the interval between the appointment of a new Governor and the administration of the oath under Article 159. If a constitutional act is required in this window — for instance, if the existing government's term expires and a new government must be sworn in, or if the legislature must be called urgently — the President can designate under Article 160 a person to discharge the specific gubernatorial function until the new Governor takes the oath. This was the scenario the framers had in mind when they described the provision as addressing "any contingency not provided for in this Chapter."

## Key cases

**Hargovind Pant v. Dr. Raghukul Tilak (1979) 2 SCC 179** — The Supreme Court held that the Governor is not an employee or servant of the Union government but a constitutional authority. This characterisation applies with full force to any person designated under Article 160 to discharge gubernatorial functions: the designee exercises constitutional authority and is not an agent of the Centre, even though the Centre made the designation. The judgment established the principle that persons exercising gubernatorial functions under contingency arrangements do so within the constitutional framework of Part VI, not as executive delegates of the Union.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The nine-judge bench confirmed that the President's various powers in relation to State governance — including the residual power under Article 160 — must be exercised consistently with the constitutional scheme of federalism. A presidential order under Article 160 cannot displace an elected government, concentrate Union authority in the State, or serve as a precursor to Article 356 proclamation without the express conditions of Article 356 being met.

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — The Court's discussion of gubernatorial functions confirmed that the constitutional character of the Governor's office — including the duty to act on ministerial advice and the obligation of constitutional good faith — is not diminished when a person acts under Article 160 contingency arrangements. The acting Governor bears the same constitutional obligations as a regularly appointed Governor.

## Current significance

Article 160 is among the least-litigated provisions of Part VI in normal conditions, but it becomes operationally significant at moments of political crisis. When Governors in contested States resign, face serious illness, or are removed close to decisive legislative moments — budget sessions, floor tests, ordinance requirements — the identity of the acting Governor and the scope of their authority become directly relevant to the political outcome. The absence of any statutory or regulatory framework specifying how the President should exercise the Article 160 discretion — particularly on timing, criteria for choosing the designee, and duration of the arrangement — means the choice remains vulnerable to political influence.

Constitutional scholars have repeatedly argued that Article 160 should be supplemented by transparent guidelines. In particular, the choice between designating the High Court's Chief Justice (which preserves judicial independence in the acting role), a neighbouring State's Governor (which may bring that Governor's own political context), and other alternatives should be governed by clear criteria rather than ad hoc presidential discretion. A person who exercises gubernatorial functions when a floor test or Bill assent is imminent makes decisions that can change a State's political history, and the legitimacy of those decisions depends substantially on whether the Article 160 designation was made transparently and without partisan calculation.

The provision also highlights a latent tension in the Centre-state relationship. The President's broad "as he thinks fit" authority over who acts as Governor in a State contingency is an instance of Union supervisory power over State constitutional arrangements that has no direct counterpart in the relationship between States and their own administrative hierarchies. As Centre-state tensions over gubernatorial conduct have intensified, so has scrutiny of every mechanism by which the Centre can influence the composition and conduct of State constitutional offices — including the Article 160 contingency designation.
