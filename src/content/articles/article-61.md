plainEnglish: Article 61 sets out how a President can be removed from office — through impeachment for violating the Constitution. Either House of Parliament can initiate the process, but only after fourteen days' notice signed by at least one-quarter of that House's members. The charge must be passed by a two-thirds majority of the total membership of the initiating House. The other House then investigates, and if it too votes by a two-thirds majority to sustain the charge, the President is immediately removed. India has never used this process. The thresholds are set deliberately high to protect the office from political misuse.
officialText: |-
  61. Procedure for impeachment of the President.—(1) When a President is to be impeached for violation of the Constitution, the charge shall be preferred by either House of Parliament.

  (2) No such charge shall be preferred unless—

  (a) the proposal to prefer such charge is contained in a resolution which has been moved after at least fourteen days' notice in writing signed by not less than one-fourth of the total number of members of the House has been given of their intention to move the resolution, and

  (b) such resolution has been passed by a majority of not less than two-thirds of the total membership of the House.

  (3) When a charge has been so preferred by either House of Parliament, the other House shall investigate the charge or cause the charge to be investigated and the President shall have the right to appear and to be represented at such investigation.

  (4) If as a result of the investigation a resolution is passed by a majority of not less than two-thirds of the total membership of the House by which the charge was investigated or caused to be investigated, declaring that the charge preferred against the President has been sustained, such resolution shall have the effect of removing the President from his office as from the date on which the resolution is so passed.

## Why it matters

Democracies that insulate their heads of state from easy removal risk creating an unaccountable presidency; those that make removal too easy risk turning the presidency into a political football. Article 61 navigates this tension by making impeachment possible but genuinely difficult. The ground — "violation of the Constitution" — excludes ordinary political grievances, while the two-stage process with supermajority thresholds at each stage ensures that removal requires broad parliamentary consensus, not merely a governing majority in one House.

The article also does something subtler: by specifying an elaborate procedure, it makes clear that no other method of removing a President is constitutionally available. A President cannot be asked to resign, dismissed by the Cabinet, or removed by any executive or judicial order. Article 61 is the exclusive and exhaustive method. This exclusivity gives the President security of tenure that reinforces the independence of the office from day-to-day political pressure while ensuring that constitutional accountability is not foreclosed.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "violation of the Constitution" is the central undefined term. The Constitution provides no list of what constitutes such a violation. Commentators have argued that it must mean a substantial, clear breach — not a disputed policy interpretation or a procedural irregularity — because the drafters deliberately chose a narrow ground. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar explained in the Constituent Assembly that the provision was not meant to be a tool for political dissatisfaction with Presidential decisions taken on ministerial advice; it was reserved for genuine dereliction of the constitutional oath under Article 60.

The numerical thresholds deserve close attention. Both the initiating House's resolution and the investigating House's sustaining resolution require two-thirds of "the total membership" — not two-thirds of members present and voting. Vacancies, absent members, and abstentions all work in favour of the President. A House with 543 members must muster at least 362 votes in favour; no amount of absent opposition reduces that floor. This is materially higher than ordinary special majorities used for constitutional amendments, and means impeachment requires cross-party support that has never existed in Indian parliamentary politics.

The President's express right under clause (3) to appear and be represented before the investigating House is a constitutionally guaranteed due-process right. The President can be heard, can challenge the evidence, and can present a defence — unusual protections for what is formally a parliamentary proceeding rather than a judicial one. This provision makes the investigation quasi-judicial in character even though it is conducted within Parliament.

## Key cases

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court, while not adjudicating an impeachment, confirmed that Article 61 is the exclusive constitutional route for removing a President. Any attempt to pressure a President to resign, or to use executive or legislative means to compel departure short of the Article 61 procedure, would be constitutionally irregular. The judgment strengthened the position that the President's security of tenure is a structural guarantee, not a mere political convention.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — The nine-judge bench's examination of Presidential action under Article 356, while dealing with a different subject, reinforced that the President's security of tenure under Articles 56 and 61 is a structural feature of the constitutional order designed to insulate the office from partisan pressure. The high threshold of Article 61 was treated as evidence that the Constitution views the presidency as requiring strong tenure protection.

**Constituent Assembly Debates (Draft Article 55, 1949)** — The debates reveal that the framers consciously modelled the provision on parliamentary accountability traditions while rejecting the American-style impeachment before the Senate. The decision to give the investigating function to the second House, rather than to a judicial body, reflects the parliamentary character of Indian constitutional design, while the requirement of a two-thirds total-membership majority was added specifically to prevent a bare governing majority from weaponising the provision.

**Kehar Singh v. Union of India (1989)** — Though a pardon case under Article 72, the Supreme Court's discussion of Presidential functions noted in passing that once a President acts in a manner that violates the Constitution, the appropriate constitutional remedy is the procedure under Article 61, not judicial intervention to nullify the Presidential act. This comment has influenced academic writing on the relationship between Article 61 and judicial review of Presidential action.

## Current significance

India has never initiated an impeachment proceeding, making Article 61 the Constitution's most dormant major provision. The arithmetic barrier alone effectively ensures that impeachment can only succeed with unanimous or near-unanimous parliamentary support — a coalition that has never formed around any President's conduct, including episodes where Presidents have been publicly criticised for acting without or against ministerial advice.

The unresolved academic question is whether the Supreme Court has jurisdiction under Article 71 to review the procedural validity of an impeachment resolution. Article 71 gives the Supreme Court jurisdiction over disputes relating to the election or removal of the President; whether that extends to reviewing whether the two-thirds threshold was actually met, or whether the fourteen-day notice requirement was properly observed, is not settled. If courts can review the arithmetic of a resolution, they effectively become a second check on Parliament in the impeachment process — a role without precedent. If they cannot, then Parliament is the sole judge of its own compliance with Article 61's requirements, creating a potential accountability gap. Neither question will be answered until India attempts an impeachment, which, given the political and numerical realities, may never occur.
