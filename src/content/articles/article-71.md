## Why it matters

Article 71 is the constitutional framework within which every presidential and vice-presidential election dispute is resolved. Its most fundamental feature is the exclusive jurisdiction it grants to the Supreme Court: no other court — no High Court, no District Court, no special tribunal — has any power to inquire into these disputes. This concentration of jurisdiction in the Supreme Court alone is not accidental. Presidential elections involve the entire nation's elected representatives, span every State and Union territory, and determine the holder of the country's highest constitutional office. Fragmented jurisdiction across multiple courts would produce inconsistent rulings, create race-to-the-courts dynamics, and potentially produce contradictory outcomes across different jurisdictions while the President was in office and acting. Exclusive Supreme Court jurisdiction prevents all of this.

The finality of the Supreme Court's decision under clause (1) is equally important. The word "final" means that the decision cannot be appealed to any other court, though it can be reviewed by the Supreme Court itself under Article 137. This finality gives certainty to an election outcome that must be operationally settled for the executive branch to function. A presidential election cannot remain in a state of perpetual judicial uncertainty while the President performs constitutional duties. The finality clause ensures that once the Supreme Court speaks, the matter is closed — whatever the outcome.

Clause (2) is a constitutional engineering solution to a real problem: what happens to all the presidential acts taken between an election and a subsequent judicial declaration that the election was void? Without clause (2), a voided election would retroactively invalidate every presidential action taken during the challenged period — every Bill assented to, every appointment made, every pardon granted, every proclamation issued. The resulting constitutional chaos would be unmanageable. Clause (2) solves this prospectively: it declares that all such acts remain valid up to the date of the Supreme Court's declaration of voidness. Voidance is forward-looking, not backward-looking.

Clause (4) — the bar on challenges based on electoral college vacancies — was introduced by the 39th Amendment and, though the 44th Amendment repealed most of that Amendment's controversial provisions, clause (4) survived. Its survival reflects practical wisdom: there is no presidential election in which the electoral college is perfectly complete. MLAs die, resign, are disqualified, or are otherwise unavailable at the time of the poll. If any vacancy could invalidate an election, every presidential election would be perpetually vulnerable to challenge on grounds that would be impossible to prevent. Clause (4) draws a sharp line — the existence of vacancies is irrelevant to validity — while leaving other grounds of challenge available.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "all doubts and disputes arising out of or in connection with the election" in clause (1) defines the Supreme Court's jurisdiction broadly. "Election" in Indian electoral law has been interpreted to encompass the entire electoral process — from the issuance of the election schedule through nomination, withdrawal, polling, counting, and the declaration of results. A dispute "in connection with" the election could include challenges to the vote-value calculations under Article 55, objections to the eligibility of a candidate under Article 58, allegations of corrupt practices by electors, or disputes about the validity of the counting process. The breadth of the phrase ensures that no electoral dispute can escape the Supreme Court's reach on technical grounds that it concerns something adjacent to rather than within the election itself.

The Parliamentary law authorised by clause (3) — the Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952 — prescribes the detailed mechanics of the election: the nomination procedure requiring fifty proposers and fifty seconders from the electoral college, the deposit of security, the scrutiny and rejection of nominations, the poll, the count, and the election petition mechanism. The Act also specifies the grounds on which an election petition can be filed, the standard of proof, and the reliefs available. Article 71(3) explicitly preserves the Constitution's supremacy — Parliament's regulatory law operates "subject to the provisions of this Constitution," meaning neither Parliament nor the Election Commission can alter the constitutional framework of Articles 54, 55, 58, and 71 by statutory regulation.

The de facto validation clause in clause (2) creates a temporal boundary at the date of the Supreme Court's declaration, not at the date of the election or the date of the petition. This means that if a petition is filed shortly after an election but the Supreme Court takes five years to decide it, five years of presidential acts are protected by clause (2). The protection is complete and unconditional: it applies to acts done in "the exercise and performance of the powers and duties of the office" — the entire constitutional domain of presidential authority — without any requirement that the acts themselves be legally valid on other grounds.

## Key cases

**Smt. Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain (1975) — the 39th Amendment and its aftermath** — The 39th Amendment (1975) inserted provisions that purported to exclude the Supreme Court's jurisdiction over the election of the Prime Minister and, by extension, the President's election under Article 71. The Indira Gandhi case, decided by a full Constitution Bench, struck down the Prime Minister-specific provisions as a violation of the basic structure of the Constitution — judicial review being a basic structure element. The post-Emergency 44th Amendment then restored Article 71 to its current form, maintaining the Supreme Court's exclusive and unrestricted jurisdiction over presidential elections. Article 71 as it stands today is the product of this constitutional correction of the Emergency-era attempt to make the election of high officers immune from judicial scrutiny.

**In re Presidential Poll, Special Reference No. 1 of 1974** — The Supreme Court exercised its advisory jurisdiction under Article 143 to clarify the validity of a presidential election conducted while State Assembly vacancies existed. The Court's analysis laid the groundwork for what eventually became Article 71(4)'s express vacancy-blindness rule. The Court observed that requiring a fully constituted electoral college before an election could proceed would make every presidential election vulnerable to deliberate disruption through the creation of vacancies, an interpretation the Constitution could not have intended.

**V.V. Giri v D.S. Dora (1970)** — The election petition challenging V.V. Giri's 1969 presidential election was among the first substantive proceedings under Article 71. The Supreme Court's examination of the petition confirmed the procedural architecture: the petition is heard by a bench of not less than five judges, the standard of proof is beyond reasonable doubt for corrupt-practice allegations, and the Court's remedial powers include voiding the election and ordering a fresh election. The case established that the Article 71 jurisdiction is not merely nominal — the Court will genuinely examine evidence and apply the law, though the standard of proof is high.

**A.C. Jose v Sivan Pillai (1984)** — While arising in a State legislative context, the Supreme Court's holding that the Election Commission cannot override validly enacted statutory provisions by issuing inconsistent directions is directly applicable to presidential elections. The Commission's powers in presidential elections are regulated by the Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952, enacted under Article 71(3). Any direction issued by the Commission that contradicts the Act, or that substitutes the Commission's preference for the Parliament-enacted framework, would be constitutionally invalid. The case establishes the hierarchy: Constitution — Parliamentary statute — Election Commission direction.

**Zail Singh v Union of India (1994)** — After the conclusion of his presidential term, Giani Zail Singh raised questions about whether certain acts taken during his presidency — including delays in assenting to Bills — were constitutionally valid. The proceedings did not raise a formal Article 71 election dispute, but the episode illuminated how the post-presidency period can generate constitutional questions that lack a clear Article 71 remedy. The case underlined that Article 71's exclusive jurisdiction covers disputes "arising out of or in connection with the election" — not disputes about the exercise of presidential powers during the term, which would be justiciable through other channels.

**Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy's uncontested election (1977)** — Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy's election as President in 1977 was the only uncontested presidential election in India's history. With no second candidate, no STV count was necessary and no election petition was filed. The absence of litigation in this election confirmed that Article 71 is a dispute-resolution mechanism that activates on demand — it is not an automatic review process. The election is presumptively valid and the Supreme Court's jurisdiction is invoked only when a challenge is actually brought.

## Current significance

Article 71's grant of exclusive Supreme Court jurisdiction has practical consequences for the pace and finality of presidential election resolution. No President-elect has ever been prevented from taking office by pending election litigation, because the Court's process under Article 71 — which involves a full-bench hearing, the examination of evidence, and the application of a high standard of proof — typically takes months or years after the election. The de facto validation clause of Article 71(2) means that even a successful challenge after the President has acted extensively would leave all prior presidential acts intact, protecting the constitutional order from retrospective disruption.

The 39th Amendment episode and its repeal by the 44th Amendment gave Article 71 a specific historical significance in Indian constitutional law. Article 71 is one of the provisions whose text itself records the constitutional struggle over judicial review during the Emergency. The bracketed text of the article (the square brackets around the entire provision) reflects that it was replaced in its entirety by the 44th Amendment, creating a visible textual reminder that this jurisdiction was once targeted for removal. The provision's current form is thus both a statement of constitutional jurisdiction and a monument to the restoration of judicial independence after the Emergency.

The Election Commission's role under Article 71(3) has become more prominent with the increasing complexity of managing the parliamentary and assembly electoral rolls for the presidential poll. Verifying that each proposer and seconder is a valid member of the electoral college, computing vote values for each State's MLAs using the 1971 census data, and administering the STV count all require regulatory precision. The Commission's authority to issue guidelines under Article 71(3) read with the 1952 Act, and the Supreme Court's jurisdiction to review those guidelines for constitutional compliance, creates a two-tier oversight structure that applies constitutional standards to what is ostensibly a technical administrative exercise.
