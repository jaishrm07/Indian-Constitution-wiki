  142. Enforcement of decrees and orders of Supreme Court and orders as to discovery, etc.—(1) The Supreme Court in the exercise of its jurisdiction may pass such decree or make such order as is necessary for doing complete justice in any cause or matter pending before it, and any decree so passed or order so made shall be enforceable throughout the territory of India in such manner as may be prescribed by or under any law made by Parliament and, until provision in that behalf is so made, in such manner as the President may by order1 prescribe.

## Why it matters

Article 142 gives the Supreme Court a constitutional power found in few other jurisdictions: the power to do "complete justice" in any pending case by passing any decree or order necessary, even if no statutory basis exists for that precise relief. It fills the gap between a court's power to identify a legal wrong and its power to actually remedy it. Used expansively, it has produced landmark outcomes in mass tort cases, environmental litigation, electoral accountability, and matrimonial law. Used too freely, it risks the Court substituting its own judgment for Parliament's — a tension that generates ongoing controversy.

This provision is what distinguishes the Supreme Court of India from courts that are entirely creatures of statute. Most courts can only grant relief within the forms of action and remedies that the legislature has authorised. The Supreme Court, because of Article 142, can fashion a remedy that the law has not yet thought of — as long as the relief is necessary to do justice in a case that is already before it. This gives the Court extraordinary flexibility but also places on it a corresponding responsibility not to overstep.

## Reading the provision

Article 142 has two clauses that serve different purposes but both rest on the Court's need for effective enforcement authority.

Clause (1) operates when there is "a cause or matter pending" before the Supreme Court. The power is thus anchored to pending proceedings — it is not a roving commission to intervene wherever the Court perceives injustice. The requirement that an order be "necessary for doing complete justice" imports a standard of necessity, not mere desirability. The Supreme Court has construed this broadly, but the Constitution Bench in Supreme Court Bar Association drew the key limit: Article 142 can supplement existing law by filling gaps; it cannot supplant existing law by creating new remedies that Parliament has consciously withheld.

Clause (2) is a separate, process-related grant: the Court may order attendance of any person, production of documents, or punishment for contempt of itself. This clause underpins the Court's enforcement machinery across the entire territory of India.

The distinction between using Article 142 to supplement law and using it to override law remains the central interpretive question. The Bhopal case showed how thin that line can be: the Court simultaneously achieved a massive tort settlement and extinguished pending criminal proceedings, acting in the interest of comprehensive resolution while generating lasting controversy about whether the victims' rights were adequately protected.

## Key cases

**Union Carbide Corporation v Union of India (1991) — Bhopal Gas Settlement** — The Court used Article 142 to make binding a settlement between the Union of India and Union Carbide for the Bhopal gas tragedy, overriding objections about the settlement's adequacy and the extinguishment of criminal liability. The judgment remains the most consequential and most debated exercise of Article 142, raising fundamental questions about whether the complete-justice power can be used to compromise pending criminal proceedings and override the statutory rights of individual victims. The decision has been criticised as an instance where the Court's invocation of "complete justice" in fact delivered partial justice — clearing Union Carbide's criminal liability in exchange for a civil settlement that many victims found grossly inadequate.

**Supreme Court Bar Association v Union of India (1998)** — A Constitution Bench drew the cardinal limit of Article 142: the complete-justice power supplements law where there are gaps, but cannot supplant a clear statutory scheme. The Court could not use Article 142 to suspend an advocate's licence for contempt because the Advocates Act vested that power in the Bar Council, not the Court. This remains the most authoritative statement of Article 142's boundaries and the case to which litigants invariably return when arguing that the Court has exceeded its Article 142 authority.

**Delhi Judicial Service Association v State of Gujarat (1991)** — The Court exercised Article 142 to issue detailed directions protecting judicial officers arrested by police, in effect creating a protocol that cut across existing criminal procedure. The case illustrated the power's utility in giving real-world effect to constitutional guarantees of judicial independence that statutes had not adequately protected. The directions have since been cited as the model for using Article 142 to issue structural remedies that fill the gap between a constitutional right and its statutory implementation.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India (Electoral Bonds) (2024)** — The Constitution Bench struck down the Electoral Bond Scheme and used Article 142 to direct the State Bank of India and the Election Commission to disclose details of all bonds purchased and redeemed, within tight court-imposed timelines. The case is a recent and high-profile example of Article 142 being used to ensure that a declaration of unconstitutionality produces immediate, measurable transparency outcomes rather than remaining a prospective declaration with no present consequence.

## History and constitutional origins

Article 142 drew on provisions in earlier Indian constitutional instruments. The Government of India Act, 1935 contained no equivalent, and the power to do "complete justice" was articulated by the Constituent Assembly as a conscious addition to give the new Supreme Court the practical authority needed to make constitutional rights meaningful. B R Ambedkar and other framers were aware that a court armed only with the remedies that existing law provided might find itself unable to give full effect to fundamental rights in the face of an executive or legislature that had omitted to provide appropriate remedies. Article 142 fills that gap by giving the Court a residual constitutional warrant to act wherever justice requires it.

The early application of Article 142 was relatively modest — the Court treated it primarily as an amplification of ordinary equitable jurisdiction. The dramatic expansion of its use came with the growth of public interest litigation in the 1980s and 1990s, as the Court began to see itself as a constitutional actor with responsibility for systemic change and not merely for resolution of individual disputes. This shift in institutional self-understanding drove the expansive use of Article 142 that has characterised the Court's jurisprudence since Bhopal.

## Article 142 in matrimonial and family law

One of the most consistent uses of Article 142 has been to dissolve marriages on the ground of irretrievable breakdown — a ground that does not exist in the Hindu Marriage Act or the Special Marriage Act. The Court has exercised this power since the 1990s in cases where both parties consent to dissolution or where a marriage has clearly and completely broken down, holding that waiting for the statutory grounds to be established would itself cause injustice. The Constitution Bench in Shilpa Sailesh v Varun Sreenivasan (2023) confirmed and systematised this practice, holding that Article 142 can be used to waive the mandatory six-month cooling-off period under Section 13B of the Hindu Marriage Act and to grant a decree of divorce on irretrievable breakdown even without mutual consent in exceptional circumstances. This is among the clearest examples of Article 142 supplementing rather than supplanting the law: it fills a gap in the statute without overriding any statutory prohibition.

## Suo motu proceedings and Article 142

A recurring tension arises when the Court exercises Article 142 powers in suo motu proceedings — cases it takes up on its own motion without a private litigant's petition. In these cases, the Court simultaneously acts as the party initiating the proceedings and the tribunal determining the relief. Critics argue that this conflation of roles intensifies the risk that Article 142 will be used as a vehicle for judicial policy-making rather than adjudication. The Court has acknowledged the tension but treated suo motu jurisdiction under Article 32 and Articles 136/142 as constitutionally permissible when the interests of justice clearly require intervention. The COVID-19 pandemic management directions and orders on prison decongestion issued in 2020-21 are the most recent large-scale example.

## Article 142 and the separation of powers

The most persistent structural objection to expansive use of Article 142 is that it erases the boundary between adjudication and legislation. When the Court uses Article 142 to direct the creation of regulatory frameworks, to establish permanent monitoring committees, or to issue detailed operational directions in sectors like the environment or education, it is performing functions that the Constitution assigns to the executive and the legislature. Proponents respond that Article 142 is itself a constitutional provision — Parliament has not constrained it, and the Court's exercise of it is constitutionally authorised even when it may be institutionally imperfect. The debate has sharpened as the volume and detail of Article 142 directions in public interest litigation have grown, and as the Court has grappled with how to ensure compliance with its structural orders without converting itself into a permanent administrative supervisor.

The Court's own doctrine provides some internal limits. The Supreme Court has repeatedly said that Article 142 cannot be used to make orders that are contrary to an express statutory provision or a constitutional requirement; that it operates in the space between the right and the effective remedy; and that it is not available as a general licence to do whatever the Court thinks would be fair and just in the circumstances. How consistently these limits are observed in practice is a question that commentators and practitioners continue to debate, and the limits are litigated in virtually every major Article 142 application.

The enforcement mechanism for Article 142 orders is the same as for any Supreme Court order: Article 144 obliges all civil and judicial authorities to act in aid of the Court, and disobedience of an Article 142 order constitutes contempt under Article 129. This enforcement chain — Article 142 decree, Article 144 duty of compliance, Article 129 contempt sanction — gives the complete-justice power its practical bite and is what makes the Court's structural orders in public interest litigation genuinely coercive rather than merely declaratory.

## Current significance

Article 142 is now a routine feature of major public interest litigation and constitutional cases — the Court regularly issues structural directions that no statute precisely authorises, citing the need for complete justice. The tension between this power and the separation of powers doctrine has sharpened as the Court has used Article 142 to direct policy in areas from forest conservation to prisoner welfare to electoral integrity. Academic and judicial opinion is divided: proponents see it as essential to making the Constitution workable; critics argue that its expansive use converts the Court into an unelected policy-maker. The caution from Attorney-General for India v Amratlal Prajivandas that Article 142 cannot be used to make orders inconsistent with constitutional or statutory provisions is frequently cited but difficult to apply consistently when the Court is acting in a legislative gap.
