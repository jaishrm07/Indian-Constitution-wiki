## Why it matters

Article 52 is a single declaratory sentence, but constitutional architecture depends entirely on sentences like this one. By declaring the office at the level of the Constitution rather than ordinary law, the framers made it impossible for Parliament to abolish the presidency through a simple majority statute. Any change to the existence or character of the office requires the amendment procedure under Article 368 — in many variants requiring a special majority and ratification by State legislatures. The provision thus gives the presidency a permanence and dignity that purely statutory offices do not enjoy.

The choice of a parliamentary presidency rather than an executive one was among the most debated questions in the Constituent Assembly. Proponents of a strong, directly elected president such as K.T. Shah argued that India needed an elected head with real governing authority to lead a young democracy through its early challenges. B.R. Ambedkar and Jawaharlal Nehru countered that parliamentary government — with a head of state bound by Cabinet advice — better suited a country with diverse linguistic, religious, and regional interests because it placed power in a body collectively answerable to the legislature. Article 52, read with the rest of Part V, reflects the Ambedkar-Nehru vision: a President who is head of state but not head of government.

## Reading the provision

The provision operates in two registers simultaneously. At the formal level it creates an office: there shall be a President. At the structural level it signals a design choice — the executive chapter of the Constitution begins by naming its formal head, not its political head. The Prime Minister does not appear in the Constitution until Articles 74 and 75, well after the President has been declared, vested with executive power (Article 53), and equipped with the mechanisms for election (Article 54), qualifications (Article 58), term, and removal. This sequencing is intentional: the architecture insists that power formally belongs to the President and is exercised by the Cabinet only by virtue of the constitutional conventions encoded in Article 74.

The implications are significant. The President's formal ownership of executive power under Article 53 is the source of every executive order, ordinance, appointment, and treaty made in the Union's name. That the Cabinet gives binding advice under Article 74 does not erase the constitutional vesting — it channels it. When the Cabinet's advice is constitutionally defective (given in bad faith, without application of mind, or in breach of constitutional limits), the vesting in the President's office is the textual hook courts use to examine the act.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench authoritatively held that the President must act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers in all ordinary executive matters. Although the judgment turns mainly on Article 74, it begins with Article 52 to define what "President of India" means in a parliamentary democracy: a constitutional head who acts through Ministers and cannot exercise executive power independently. The Court drew a clear line between the formal vesting of power in the President and the real exercise of that power by the Cabinet.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — Nine judges examined Presidential action under Article 356. The Court invoked Article 52 to underline that Presidential satisfaction is a constitutional act subject to judicial review. A President acting without or contrary to Cabinet advice would be acting outside the constitutional order created by Articles 52 and 74 together. The judgment made clear that the formal dignity of the presidential office provides no immunity for unconstitutional executive acts.

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006)** — Testing the dissolution of the Bihar Assembly, the Supreme Court reiterated that Articles 52 and 53 create an office that derives legitimacy from the Constitution and must act within it. The President's constitutional existence does not insulate executive acts from scrutiny; it in fact requires that those acts conform to constitutional standards.

**U.N.R. Rao v. Indira Gandhi (1971)** — The Court confirmed that a Council of Ministers must exist at all times to aid and advise the President, because Article 74 is a structural requirement for the office established by Article 52. Even after the dissolution of the Lok Sabha, the caretaker Ministry continues until a new government is formed; the President cannot be left without a Council of Ministers to advise.

## Current significance

The question of the President's residual discretion continues to generate constitutional debate across several domains. The "pocket veto" — a situation where a President neither assents to nor returns a Bill — exposes a gap in Article 111's deadlines and tests the limits of what the office created by Article 52 may or must do. President Droupadi Murmu's 2025 reference to the Supreme Court seeking clarity on Governors' obligations regarding State Bills indirectly engaged the parallel question of whether Presidential inaction on a Bill can be constitutionally legitimate for an indefinite period.

The hung-parliament scenario raises perhaps the most contested question about the Article 52 office: when no party commands a Lok Sabha majority after a general election, how much personal judgment may the President exercise in deciding whom to invite to form a government? The President's constitutional obligation to find a stable government coexists with the democratic imperative that parliamentary arithmetic, not presidential preference, should determine who governs. Any proposal to move toward a directly elected executive presidency — a periodically debated reform — would require amending or replacing Article 52 itself, a change that would fundamentally alter the character of India's constitutional democracy.
