  175. Right of Governor to address and send messages to the House or Houses.—(1) The Governor may address the Legislative Assembly or, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, either House of the Legislature of the State, or both Houses assembled together, and may for that purpose require the attendance of members.

  (2) The Governor may send messages to the House or Houses of the Legislature of the State, whether with respect to a Bill then pending in the Legislature or otherwise, and a House to which any message is so sent shall with all convenient despatch consider any matter required by the message to be taken into consideration.

## What this Article does

Article 175 empowers the Governor to address the State Legislature — either one House, both Houses, or the Assembly alone — and to send messages to the House or Houses regarding pending bills or any other matter. The right to require the attendance of members for an address is a formal authority that underscores the Governor's constitutional position as a component of the Legislature under Article 168. The messaging power in clause (2) is a channel through which the Governor may communicate constitutional concerns to the legislature without resorting to other more drastic constitutional tools such as withholding assent or recommending dissolution.

The article is the State-level counterpart of Article 86, which gives the President the right to address Parliament and send messages. Both provisions reflect the constitutional role of the executive head as a formal participant in the legislative process — not as a legislator who votes, but as a constitutional voice that can be heard and to which the legislature must attend with reasonable promptness.

## Clause (1): Address power

The Governor's right to address is permissive in form — "may address" — but in practice the annual address at the commencement of the budget session and the post-election address are constitutional expectations governed by Article 176. The clause (1) power is broader than Article 176: it covers any occasion, not just the special mandatory addresses. A Governor could in principle address the House on a constitutional concern arising mid-session, though this is rarely done without ministerial advice.

The power to "require the attendance of members" is a formal constitutional instrument. In practice, the legislature's own rules govern the quorum and attendance requirements; the Governor's right to require attendance is more a statement of the constitutional weight of the address than an independent enforcement mechanism.

## Clause (2): Message power

The message power in clause (2) enables the Governor to send a written communication to the House about any matter — a pending Bill, a constitutional issue, or any other subject — and the House is obliged to consider the matter with "all convenient despatch." This obligation is important: the legislature cannot simply ignore a gubernatorial message. In the context of Bills, the message power provides a constitutionally recognised channel for the Governor to return a Bill with observations rather than withholding assent indefinitely.

The Supreme Court's jurisprudence on Article 200 (assent to Bills) has increasingly treated the Article 175(2) message power as the appropriate mechanism through which a Governor should communicate reservations about pending legislation, rather than allowing indefinite pocket-vetoing. The Court in State of Tamil Nadu v Governor of Tamil Nadu proceedings made clear that the messaging channel must be used constructively and in good faith, not deployed as a delaying tactic.

## Ministerial advice and the address content

Both the address and the message powers are exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. The Governor does not have an independent right to deliver a different address than the one prepared by the ministry, or to send a message without ministerial sanction. Courts have consistently applied the Shamsher Singh principle to these provisions: where the Governor's action is not within a specifically identified constitutional discretion, it must follow ministerial advice.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court's broad holding that the Governor acts on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers in all non-discretionary matters encompasses the address and messaging powers under Article 175.

**Tamil Nadu Governor assent disputes (2023)** — The Supreme Court, in proceedings concerning a Tamil Nadu Governor's prolonged withholding of assent to bills, considered whether the messaging power under Article 175(2) can substitute for or delay the exercise of other constitutional functions. The Court's reasoning confirmed that the Article 175 channel must be used in good faith and cannot become an instrument of legislative obstruction.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — Though primarily decided under Articles 174 and 163, the Court's holding that the Governor cannot act against the advice of a ministry that retains legislative confidence applies equally to the Governor's address and messaging powers under Article 175.

**State of Punjab v Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab (2023)** — The Supreme Court reiterated that the Governor's powers in relation to the legislature, including the address and messaging powers, must be exercised within constitutional bounds and not weaponised against the elected government.

## Current significance

The Governor's power to address the legislature and send messages has gained salience in states where governors have withheld, delayed, or altered the address prepared by the elected government, or have sent messages about Bills in ways that effectively paralysed legislation. The article's interaction with Article 200 (assent) and Article 201 (reservation for the President) is particularly live: courts have needed to clarify the relationship between the Governor's messaging power and the obligation to act on Bills within constitutionally defined timelines, making Article 175 a provision of ongoing operational importance in Centre-state constitutional relations.
