## Why it matters

Article 222 is the provision that has generated the most sustained controversy
about the relationship between judicial independence and executive power at the
High Court level. A transfer — moving a judge from their home court to a distant
one, often with no public explanation — can disrupt a career, separate a judge
from family, and signal professional disfavour in ways that affect the entire
bench's sense of security. Used as a punitive instrument, transfers undermine
the very independence that Articles 217 and 218 are designed to protect. Used
properly — to prevent local affiliations and distribute judicial talent — they
serve a legitimate constitutional purpose.

The constitutional treatment of transfers has shifted dramatically over the
decades. Before the collegium era, the executive could effectively initiate
transfers. After the Second Judges Case, the collegium controls them. The
practical question of whether collegium-driven transfers are more transparent
or less susceptible to abuse than executive-driven transfers remains genuinely
contested.

## Reading the provision

Article 222(1) empowers the President to transfer a judge from one High Court
to any other. No consent of the judge is required — the article does not
mention it, and the Supreme Court has confirmed that transfer is not conditional
on consent. The article refers in its current official text to the NJAC's
recommendation, but that reference is constitutionally inoperative after the
NJAC Case (2015). Transfers are therefore made on the binding recommendation
of the collegium, following the same legal framework as appointments under
Article 217.

Article 222(2) — introduced by the Constitution (Fifteenth Amendment) Act,
1963 — provides a compensatory allowance in addition to salary during service
in the transferred court. Parliament fixes the allowance by law or, until so
fixed, the President does so by order.

The convention that Chief Justices of High Courts are almost always drawn from
outside the state is implemented entirely through the Article 222 transfer
mechanism. This "outsider Chief Justice" policy — designed to prevent the Chief
Justice from having local ties that might affect independence — is one of the
most consistent practices in the collegium's exercise of transfer powers.

## Key cases

**Union of India v Sankalchand Himatlal Sheth (1977)** — The foundational
transfer case, decided before the First Judges Case. The Court held that the
consent of the judge is not required for a transfer under Article 222 but that
the transfer must be in the public interest and cannot be used as a punitive
instrument. The requirement of genuine consultation with the Chief Justice of
India was emphasised. The case foreshadowed the collegium framework that
followed.

**S P Gupta v Union of India (First Judges Case, 1981)** — Article 222 was
extensively examined alongside Article 217. The majority held that the executive
retained substantial discretion in effecting transfers, subject only to
consultation with the Chief Justice of India whose opinion was not binding.
The decision enabled the government's practice of what critics called "punitive
transfers" — displacing judges who had ruled against government interests.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (Second Judges
Case, 1993)** — The Court established that the collegium's recommendation is
binding on the President for transfers under Article 222, just as for
appointments under Article 217. A transfer against the collegium's
recommendation is constitutionally impermissible. This ruling transferred
practical control of judicial transfers from the executive to the collegium.

## Current significance

Transfer decisions under Article 222 remain among the least transparent
exercises of the collegium's power. While the collegium has begun publishing
resolutions for some appointments, transfers — including those of Chief
Justices — are rarely accompanied by published reasons. The legal community
scrutinises transfer patterns for signals about the collegium's assessment of
individual judges, generating speculation that is inevitable when reasoning
is not public.

The Shah Commission Report (1978) documented specific instances in which the
Emergency-era government had used non-confirmation of additional judges and
transfers as instruments of control over the High Court bench. These documented
abuses were a direct impetus for the judicial independence litigation that
produced the First and Second Judges Cases. The constitutional history of
Article 222 is therefore inseparable from the history of the Emergency.

Whether Article 222 transfers can be challenged by the transferred judge in
court has not been finally settled. The Supreme Court has indicated that
transfers are subject to judicial review on very limited grounds — primarily
that the collegium process was not followed — but the substance of a transfer
decision made by the collegium is treated as non-justiciable. This leaves a
significant power largely unreviewable, a position that judicial reform advocates
have argued is inconsistent with the broader principle that all constitutional
powers are subject to the rule of law.
