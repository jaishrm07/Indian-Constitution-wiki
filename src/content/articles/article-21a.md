## What this Article does

Article 21A was inserted by the Constitution (Eighty-Sixth Amendment) Act, 2002, transforming the right to elementary education from a judicially derived extension of Article 21 into an express fundamental right. The Article requires the State to provide free and compulsory education to all children between six and fourteen years of age. "Free" means no fees or charges may be imposed on children attending government schools; "compulsory" means the State must ensure that every child in the age group is enrolled and attends school, not merely that attendance is mandated of parents.

The Eighty-Sixth Amendment made three simultaneous changes. It inserted Article 21A, rewrote Article 45 to cover early childhood care for children below six (converting it from the original fourteen-year directive into a focused under-six directive), and inserted Article 51A(k), which imposes a fundamental duty on parents and guardians to provide opportunities for education to children between six and fourteen. The three provisions together constitute a coherent constitutional scheme for elementary education.

The implementing legislation — the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009 (RTE Act) — came into force in 2010, eight years after the constitutional amendment. The RTE Act specifies teacher-pupil ratios, infrastructure standards, a no-detention policy, and — most consequentially — a requirement under Section 12(1)(c) that unaided private schools reserve 25 per cent of seats in their entry-level classes for children from economically weaker sections and disadvantaged groups, with reimbursement to the school at the government rate per child.

## Before Article 21A — the judicial path

The court-made right to education preceded the constitutional amendment by nearly a decade. In Mohini Jain v. State of Karnataka (1992), the Supreme Court held that the right to education is a fundamental right flowing from the right to life under Article 21, though the ruling was specifically about capitation fees in private medical colleges and generated controversy about its breadth. The more authoritative statement came the following year.

In Unni Krishnan J.P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993), a Constitution Bench held that the right to free education up to the age of fourteen is a fundamental right enforceable under Articles 21 and 32. The Court reasoned that the right to life must be read with the directive principles in Articles 41 and 45, and that a life without education cannot be dignified in any meaningful sense. The Court also constructed an elaborate scheme for regulating private educational institutions — a scheme later substantially reconsidered — but the core holding that elementary education is part of Article 21 was the direct precursor to Article 21A's enactment.

The Tapas Major Committee (1999) and the T.S.R. Subramanian Committee reports drew attention to the legislative gap — the directive-principle right found in Unni Krishnan was not self-executing and required statute. The Eighty-Sixth Amendment gave the right constitutional foundation, and the RTE Act seven years later gave it operational content.

## The 25 per cent reservation in private schools

Section 12(1)(c) of the RTE Act — the provision requiring unaided private schools to admit 25 per cent of their entry-level intake from economically weaker sections — generated the most consequential litigation arising from Article 21A. Schools challenged it as an unconstitutional appropriation of their property and a violation of the freedom to establish and administer educational institutions under Articles 19(1)(g) and 30.

In Society for Unaided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012), a three-judge bench of the Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of Section 12(1)(c) as applied to unaided non-minority private schools. The majority held that the State is entitled to use private school infrastructure as a vehicle for implementing the fundamental right under Article 21A, and that the reimbursement mechanism adequately compensated schools for the public-interest burden. The dissenting judgment of Justice K.S. Radhakrishnan held that compelling private unaided schools to admit state-sponsored children without genuine financial reimbursement amounted to an appropriation of private property.

Minority schools — those established under Article 30 to preserve the distinct language, culture, or religion of a minority — were held to be exempt from the 25 per cent requirement. The exemption was based on the principle that Article 30 rights would be diluted if minority institutions were compelled to admit students through a state-directed quota. This exemption has remained contested, with critics arguing it creates a two-tier system in which the constitutional right to education is effectively enforced only against non-minority private institutions.

## Relationship with Article 45 and the under-six gap

The Eighty-Sixth Amendment deliberately limited the fundamental right to children between six and fourteen, leaving the under-six cohort to the directive-principle sphere under the revised Article 45. This age boundary has been a persistent subject of criticism. Evidence on child development consistently indicates that the first six years — particularly the zero-to-three window — are the most significant for cognitive development and school readiness. Children who arrive at age six without foundational stimulation and language development are already behind peers from more advantaged backgrounds, making the Article 21A guarantee less effective for the most disadvantaged.

Courts have declined to extend the Article 21A right below six, treating the constitutional text as a deliberate limit, but they have used Article 45 alongside Articles 21 and 47 in the right-to-food and child-nutrition proceedings to impose obligations on the State regarding the ICDS and Anganwadi network. The legal pathway to enforceable pre-school rights runs through Article 21 rather than Article 21A, mirroring the pre-amendment strategy that generated Unni Krishnan.

## Key cases

**Mohini Jain v. State of Karnataka (1992)** — The Court held that capitation fees charged by private medical colleges violated the right to education, which it derived from Article 21. The judgment was the first Supreme Court recognition that education is a fundamental right, though its application to all private education was subsequently narrowed in Unni Krishnan.

**Unni Krishnan J.P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993)** — The Constitution Bench held that the right to free and compulsory education until age fourteen is part of Article 21. The ruling gave judicial content to what Article 21A later codified and was the immediate constitutional ancestor of the Eighty-Sixth Amendment.

**Society for Unaided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012)** — The Court upheld the validity of the RTE Act's 25 per cent reservation requirement as applied to unaided non-minority private schools, confirming that Article 21A authorises the State to engage private institutions in delivering the fundamental right. The exemption of minority schools from this requirement was simultaneously confirmed.

**Ashok Kumar Thakur v. Union of India (2008)** — While primarily about reservations in higher education, the Court's discussion of educational rights treated the continuum from early childhood care (Article 45) through compulsory schooling (Article 21A) to higher education as a unified constitutional concern. The judgment situates Article 21A within the broader educational rights architecture.

**Devashayam v. Union of India (2014)** — The Supreme Court addressed the exclusion of children with disabilities from the RTE Act's scope and directed that the Act and Article 21A must be read inclusively. Children with disabilities are entitled to free and compulsory education within the six-to-fourteen age group, and the failure to provide accessible schooling violates both Article 21A and the right to non-discrimination.

## Current significance

More than two decades after the constitutional amendment and fifteen years after the RTE Act came into force, the gap between the right's promise and its delivery remains substantial. Gross enrolment ratios at the elementary level have improved significantly, but learning outcomes have not kept pace: the Annual Status of Education Report (ASER) consistently finds that large proportions of children enrolled in government schools are unable to read basic texts or perform simple arithmetic appropriate for their grade. The formal right to attendance does not guarantee the substantive right to education.

The no-detention policy under the RTE Act — which prohibits failing students before the completion of Class 8 — has been the subject of persistent policy debate and was amended in 2019 to permit state governments to hold students back from Class 5 onwards if they do not meet basic learning standards. The intersection of Article 21A's right to education with the question of whether that right is satisfied by mere enrolment or requires demonstrated learning outcomes is unresolved.

The National Education Policy 2020 proposes extending the scope of compulsory education from the current 6-14 range to a 3-18 range, which would require either a constitutional amendment to Article 21A or a statutory framework reading the broader obligation into Article 21. The NEP 2020 framework also shifts emphasis to foundational literacy and numeracy in the early years, directly engaging the Article 45 gap for under-six children. How these policy ambitions translate into enforceable rights — and how courts will respond to litigation seeking to give NEP 2020 constitutional force — is the next frontier for education-rights jurisprudence under Article 21A.
