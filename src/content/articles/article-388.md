## What this Article did

Article 388 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that made provision for filling casual vacancies in the provisional Parliament and provisional Legislatures of the States. It has been omitted.

The provisional Parliament (under Article 379) and the provisional State Legislatures (under Articles 382 and 385) were composed of members who had been elected under the pre-1950 electoral framework, not under the new Constitution. When vacancies arose in these bodies — through death, resignation, disqualification, or other causes — questions arose about how they should be filled.

Article 388 addressed this by providing rules for filling such casual vacancies in these provisional legislatures during the transitional period. This was a necessary procedural provision to maintain the effective functioning of the provisional legislative bodies pending the first elections under the new Constitution.

## Why it was omitted

Article 388 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. Once the provisional legislatures were replaced by elected legislatures after the 1951-52 elections, vacancy-filling in those bodies was governed by the normal constitutional and statutory framework (the Representation of the People Act and related legislation). The transitional provision had no further purpose.

## Historical context

The provisional Parliament sat from January 1950 until the First Lok Sabha was constituted in April 1952. During this period, some vacancies inevitably arose among its members. Similarly, the provisional State Legislatures, which sat until elections were held in most States in 1951-52, experienced vacancies. Article 388 ensured that these vacancies could be filled in an orderly manner without waiting for the full election machinery under the new Constitution to be activated.

The provisional legislatures were important institutions. Despite their transitional status, they enacted major legislation, approved the national budget, and established the institutional foundations of Indian democracy. Article 388 helped maintain their effective functioning by addressing vacancy-filling.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge based on vacancy-filling in provisional legislatures under Article 388 is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 388 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in ensuring the functional continuity and completeness of the provisional legislative bodies during the critical transitional period of 1950-1952.

The provisional Parliament and provisional State Legislatures were not dormant caretaker bodies. They were active legislative institutions that enacted major laws, passed budgets, and exercised the full constitutional functions of their respective chambers. Article 388, by providing for the orderly filling of casual vacancies in these bodies, ensured that they could function with a full complement of members throughout their transitional existence. This operational completeness was important because the legitimacy of legislation passed by these bodies depended in part on their having been properly constituted at the relevant times.

The practical need for Article 388 arose from the fact that the members of the provisional legislative bodies — drawn from the Constituent Assembly and provincial assemblies — were not freshly elected under the new Constitution. Some were elderly, some were in poor health, and the period between 1950 and 1952 inevitably saw deaths, resignations, and other causes of vacancy. Article 388 provided the procedural mechanism for managing these vacancies, ensuring that the legislative bodies remained functional and quorate throughout the transitional period. Without this provision, vacancies could have accumulated to the point where the provisional legislatures struggled to maintain a working membership.
