## Why it matters

Article 97 provides for the remuneration of the four presiding officers of Parliament — the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha, and the Speaker and Deputy Speaker of the Lok Sabha. The salaries and allowances are to be determined by Parliament by law; pending such legislation, the Second Schedule to the Constitution specifies the applicable amounts. The article ensures that the financial support of presiding officers is constitutionally guaranteed rather than dependent on annual appropriation, reinforcing their independence from the executive and from the ordinary legislative process.

The principle behind Article 97 is identical to that which protects judges under Article 125 and the Comptroller and Auditor-General under Article 148. Constitutional office-holders who are expected to act without fear or favour — presiding officers, judges, and audit officers — must be financially independent of those whose conduct they oversee. For presiding officers, the independence is primarily from the executive and from parliamentary majorities that might otherwise use the threat of reducing or withholding salary as a form of pressure. The Consolidated Fund mechanism, operationalised through implementing legislation, ensures that salary is a standing charge rather than an annual grant that must be approved.

The article is also relevant to the broader question of presiding-officer independence in India's constitutional design. A Speaker or Chairman who is financially comfortable and whose salary is not subject to annual review by the very assembly they preside over is better positioned to rule impartially on matters that displease the government of the day. While the salary quantum itself is modest relative to the complexity of the role, the constitutional structure ensures that the flow of salary is insulated from parliamentary arithmetic in a way that reinforces institutional rather than partisan allegiances.

## The Consolidated Fund guarantee

Although Article 97 does not in its own terms state that salaries are charged on the Consolidated Fund of India, Parliament has done so in the Salaries and Allowances of Officers of Parliament Act, 1953, enacted under this article. Charging salaries on the Consolidated Fund means they are paid without a vote of Parliament — they are a standing charge that does not require annual approval. The Appropriation Act that Parliament passes each year covers Consolidated Fund expenditures in a single omnibus appropriation; there is no occasion for Parliament to separately debate or vote down the salaries of presiding officers.

This structural arrangement is constitutionally deliberate. If presiding officers' salaries were subject to annual parliamentary approval, a dominant party could threaten to reduce or eliminate those salaries as leverage against presiding officers who made procedural rulings unfavourable to the government. The Consolidated Fund charge removes this leverage entirely — Parliament as a collective body fixes the salary quantum by statute, but the individual annual disbursement requires no further authorisation.

The same mechanism applies to the salaries of Supreme Court and High Court judges under Articles 125 and 221, to the salary of the Comptroller and Auditor-General under Article 148, to the salary of the Attorney-General (through convention and practice), and to the salaries of the Election Commissioners under Article 324. All of these are officers whose independence is considered constitutionally important, and all have their salaries charged to the Consolidated Fund rather than voted by Parliament annually.

## The Second Schedule fallback

The Second Schedule originally fixed the salaries of presiding officers at the time the Constitution was adopted in 1950. This fallback is an important constitutional safety valve: it ensures that presiding officers cannot be financially starved out of office by a Parliament that simply declines to legislate about their salaries. If Parliament never enacted the Salaries and Allowances of Officers of Parliament Act, the Second Schedule amounts would continue to apply indefinitely, guaranteed by the constitutional text.

In practice, Parliament has periodically revised salaries and allowances through the Salaries and Allowances of Officers of Parliament Act, 1953, and its subsequent amendments, so the Second Schedule figures are largely of historical significance. But the constitutional safety-net is structurally important: unlike ordinary public servants whose salaries depend entirely on executive notification, presiding officers have a constitutionally guaranteed minimum that the Second Schedule provides.

## Comparison with judicial salary protection

The parallel between Article 97 and Article 125 (judicial salaries) is instructive, though the mechanisms differ. Article 125(2) expressly prohibits Parliament from varying a judge's salary to their disadvantage after appointment — a direct protection against targeted salary reduction of individual judges. Article 97 contains no equivalent prohibition for presiding officers, meaning Parliament could in theory reduce the salary of a presiding officer mid-term by amending the implementing statute. However, this scenario has never arisen in practice, and the Consolidated Fund charge provides protection against the more likely threat — annual budgetary pressure — even in the absence of an anti-reduction guarantee.

Judges derive their primary independence protection from Article 125(2)'s anti-reduction guarantee plus the Consolidated Fund charge. Presiding officers derive their primary independence protection from the Consolidated Fund charge alone, with the Second Schedule as a constitutional floor. This makes presiding-officer financial independence structurally weaker than judicial financial independence, a difference that is partially compensated by the presiding officers' political authority and accountability: they are removable by the House and are themselves elected politicians with independent political standing.

## Salaries, allowances, and post-retirement benefits

The Salaries and Allowances of Officers of Parliament Act, 1953, and subsequent amendments provide for not only basic salary but also sumptuary allowances, house rent allowance, conveyance, medical facilities, secretarial staff, and other perquisites during tenure. Former presiding officers are also entitled to pension and post-retirement benefits, including a pensionary annuity, accommodation or housing equivalent, and travel facilities. These provisions reflect the constitutional intent that presiding officers should be comfortable enough during and after their tenure to devote themselves wholly to institutional duties without financial anxiety.

The post-retirement benefit structure is analogous to that for Supreme Court judges under Article 125 and the First Schedule, though the quantum and terms differ. The pension provisions have occasionally attracted criticism when presiding officers have returned to active party politics after leaving office, since the financial security provided by the state is then combined with partisan political activity — a combination that is not restricted by any post-retirement prohibition analogous to the judicial post-retirement employment restrictions under Article 124(7).

## Article 97 and presiding-officer independence

The financial independence guaranteed by Article 97 is one element of a broader constitutional architecture for presiding-officer independence that also includes tenure protection (removable only by absolute majority on fourteen days' notice), procedural immunity (parliamentary privilege), and constitutional authority (the presiding officer's rulings are generally not subject to judicial review). Together these protections are designed to produce officers who can preside impartially even under political pressure.

The question of whether these protections are sufficient — or whether they should be supplemented by post-retirement restrictions on political activity, by requirements of prior service or judicial training, or by more robust mechanisms for challenging procedurally incorrect rulings — has been a recurring theme in Indian constitutional commentary. The financial element secured by Article 97 is only one component, but it is foundational: an officer who cannot be financially coerced is at least protected against one of the most direct forms of pressure.

## Key cases

**Sub-Committee on Judicial Accountability v. Union of India (1991)** — While the Court was focused on judicial salaries under Article 125, its analysis of constitutionally guaranteed remuneration as a security-of-tenure mechanism applies directly to Article 97. The Court reasoned that charging salaries on the Consolidated Fund insulates constitutional office-holders from financial pressure and is a structural guarantee, not a mere administrative convenience. The principle is directly applicable to presiding officers by analogy.

**P.V. Narsimha Rao v. State (CBI/SPE) (1998)** — The Constitution Bench's extended discussion of the privileges and immunities of Members of Parliament touched on the broader framework of financial and institutional independence designed for constitutional office-holders under Part V, providing context for understanding why Article 97's guarantee matters beyond the purely administrative question of pay scales. The Court's analysis of the relationship between financial security and institutional integrity applies to presiding officers as well as members.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Court's comprehensive analysis of presiding officer powers and institutional design engaged with the independence framework for presiding officers as a whole, noting that salary security under Article 97 is one of several interlocking protections designed to enable impartial presiding. The judgment's broader analysis of constitutional office-holder independence is directly relevant to understanding Article 97's role in the presiding-officer framework.

## Current significance

Article 97 and its implementing legislation are periodically revisited when parliamentary emoluments are revised. The broader question — whether presiding officers, whose salaries are constitutionally secured during tenure, should face post-retirement restrictions on political activity to preserve the appearance of impartiality — remains a matter of political convention rather than enforceable legal obligation. The financial independence guaranteed under Article 97 is structurally analogous to judicial independence, yet presiding officers have not been subject to the post-retirement employment restrictions that apply to judges under Article 124(7).

This distinction has attracted academic scrutiny in the context of recent Speakers' transitions back to active party politics after their tenure. A Speaker who presided over significant parliamentary decisions — including disqualification orders under the Tenth Schedule, rulings on the admissibility of no-confidence motions, and certification of Money Bills — and then immediately returned to partisan activity presents an appearance of conflict that the financial independence secured by Article 97 alone cannot resolve. The constitutional design protects the salary; it does not address the broader question of impartiality in the Speaker's institutional legacy.

The Rajya Sabha's prolonged vacancy in the Deputy Chairmanship has reduced Article 97's practical significance in one respect — the Deputy Chairman's salary and allowances cannot flow to an officer who has not been elected. But it has also highlighted the structural asymmetry between the Rajya Sabha and Lok Sabha in this respect: the Lok Sabha has consistently maintained both its primary and secondary presiding officers, while the Rajya Sabha has operated for extended periods without the Deputy Chairman whose salary and allowances Article 97 and the implementing legislation provide.
