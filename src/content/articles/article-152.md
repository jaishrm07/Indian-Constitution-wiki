## What this Article does

Article 152 is the definitional gateway to Part VI, which sets out the constitutional framework for State Governments. By specifying that "State" in this Part means a State in the First Schedule, the article limits the operative scope of all subsequent Part VI provisions — the Governor's office, the State Legislature, the State executive, and the State judiciary — to the units specifically listed there. It ensures that Union Territories, which are governed by Part VIII and relevant Central legislation rather than by Part VI, are not inadvertently subjected to provisions designed for full States.

The article originally carried a broader exclusion for Jammu and Kashmir, which had its own separate constitutional arrangement under Article 370 and its own Constitution. The text — saying the expression "does not include the State of Jammu and Kashmir" — reflects the residue of that long-standing exclusion. After the revocation of Article 370's operative provisions and the reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir into two Union Territories under the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act 2019, the entity no longer exists as a State in the First Schedule, so the exclusionary clause is now effectively spent rather than actively operative.

## Structural role in Part VI

Article 152 performs the same function for Part VI that the definition clauses in other Parts perform for their respective contexts. It is not a standalone substantive provision but a hermeneutic key: whenever any provision in Part VI refers to a "State," the reader must apply the Article 152 definition unless the particular provision itself indicates a different meaning by context. This keeps Part VI internally coherent and avoids interpretive disputes about whether the word "State" in, say, Article 163 or Article 214 might encompass a Union Territory administered by a Lieutenant Governor.

The exclusion of Jammu and Kashmir historically meant that the special constitutional arrangements for that region were governed by the J&K Constitution and Article 370 rather than by Part VI of the Union Constitution. Part VI's Chapter on the State Judiciary (Articles 214-231) and on the State Legislature similarly did not apply to Jammu and Kashmir in their standard form, reflecting the unique compact negotiated during the Constituent Assembly period.

## Amendment history and 2019 reorganisation

The express exclusion of Jammu and Kashmir was inserted to reflect the political settlement of 1949-1950 that culminated in Article 370. Over decades, Parliament applied individual provisions of the Constitution to Jammu and Kashmir by Presidential orders issued under Article 370(1), creating a complex patchwork of applicability. With the constitutional changes of August 2019, Jammu and Kashmir was divided into the Union Territory of Jammu and Kashmir (with a Legislature) and the Union Territory of Ladakh (without a Legislature). Neither is a State in the First Schedule, so Part VI does not apply to either in any case.

The definitional boundary drawn by Article 152 has practical implications for Union Territories with Legislatures — such as Delhi and Puducherry. Although these territories have elected assemblies, they are governed by Article 239AA and Article 239A respectively, not by Part VI. Their Lieutenants-Governors are not Governors within the meaning of Article 153, and disputes about their constitutional status must be resolved by reference to those special provisions rather than by extending Part VI through the Article 152 definition.

## Key cases

**State of Jammu and Kashmir v Triloki Nath Khosa (1974)** — The Supreme Court clarified that the separate constitutional framework for Jammu and Kashmir flowed from Article 370 and its own Constitution. The Article 152 exclusion was part of the background understanding, and the Court was required to identify applicable law from the J&K Constitution itself rather than from Part VI.

**In re Article 370 (Constitution Bench, 2023)** — The batch of petitions challenging the revocation of Article 370's operative provisions required the Court to assess the status of Jammu and Kashmir post-reorganisation. The Constitution Bench upheld the Presidential actions and confirmed that neither successor Union Territory is a State in the First Schedule, leaving the Article 152 exclusion descriptively accurate even after the formal abrogation.

**GNCT of Delhi v Union of India (2018 and 2023)** — These cases, though decided under Article 239AA rather than Part VI, illuminated the constitutional distinction between a State Governor and a Union Territory Lieutenant Governor. The Court's analysis of what executive powers are available to an elected government in a Union Territory implicitly confirmed that Article 152's limitation of Part VI to First Schedule States is not merely formal but carries real institutional consequences.

## Current significance

As debates about restoring statehood to Jammu and Kashmir continue following the 2023 Constitution Bench judgment, the question of when Part VI will be extended to a reconstituted State gives Article 152 renewed relevance. The article also functions as a quiet reminder that the federal architecture of Part VI — with its full institutional complement of Governor, Council of Ministers, Legislature, and High Court — is a status marker distinguishing States from Territories. Any future statehood grant would bring the new State within Article 152's definition and trigger the full application of Part VI's protections and obligations.
