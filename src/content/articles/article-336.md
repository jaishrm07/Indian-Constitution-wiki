  336. Special provision for Anglo-Indian community in certain services.—(1) During the first two years after the commencement of this Constitution, appointments of members of the Anglo-Indian community to posts in the railway, customs, postal and telegraph services of the Union shall be made on the same basis as immediately before the fifteenth day of August, 1947. ## What this Article does

Article 336 provided the Anglo-Indian community with a phased, declining reservation in the railway, customs, postal, and telegraph services of the Union, mirroring the employment base the community had occupied under the British administration. The reservation operated on a step-down formula: the numbers reserved were to decline by approximately ten per cent every two years from the levels prevailing on 15 August 1947, with full cessation at the end of ten years from the Constitution's commencement — that is, by 26 January 1960. The article was therefore self-executing in its sunset: it required no further legislative or executive action to lapse. Clause (2) preserved the community's right to compete on merit for posts beyond those reserved under clause (1), ensuring that the reservation was a floor, not a ceiling, on Anglo-Indian participation in these services.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "Anglo-Indian community" has its own constitutional definition in Article 366(2), which defines an Anglo-Indian as a person whose father or any of whose other male progenitors in the male line is or was of European descent but who is domiciled within the territory of India and is or was born within such territory of parents habitually resident therein and not established there for temporary purposes only. This is a patrilineal descent-based definition tied to European male ancestry and Indian domicile and birth. It is one of the few places in the Constitution where a community is defined by genealogical descent rather than religious, linguistic, or caste identity.

Under British administration, the railways, customs, telegraph, and postal services had an unusually high proportion of Anglo-Indian employees. The community had historically been directed into these technical and supervisory roles by colonial policy, and had developed professional competencies in these sectors over generations. After independence, the community faced the prospect of displacement as Indianisation of the services proceeded. Article 336 was the Constituent Assembly's response: it guaranteed that this displacement would be gradual and would not destroy the community's employment base overnight, while making clear through the step-down formula that the reservation was transitional and not permanent.

The article must be read alongside Article 337, which provided for grants-in-aid to Anglo-Indian educational institutions (a provision that also contained a sunset clause, reducing grants by ten per cent every three years). Together Articles 336 and 337 represent a constitutionally integrated package of transitional protections for a community that was small — roughly three hundred thousand persons at independence — but economically visible and historically significant in the Indian Union's inherited administrative structure.

The 104th Constitutional Amendment Act 2019, which came into force on 25 January 2020, ended the nomination of Anglo-Indian members to the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies under Articles 331 and 333. Those articles had provided for presidential nomination of up to two Anglo-Indian members to the Lok Sabha and for nomination of Anglo-Indian members to State Legislative Assemblies. The 2019 Amendment did not revive Article 336 (which had already been spent since 1960), but the removal of the Lok Sabha and Assembly nomination rights has been read as the constitutional completion of the community's full transition from special status to ordinary citizenship — removing the last remaining vestige of constitutional differentiation for the Anglo-Indian community. The 104th Amendment is thus the bookend to a process that Article 336 began with the employment reservation phase-out in 1960.

## Key cases

**Frank Anthony v Union of India (1957)** — The Supreme Court considered the scope of Article 336 in relation to posts in the South Eastern Railway, holding that the reservation applied to direct recruitment posts and that the government's discretion in implementing the step-down formula had to be exercised in a manner consistent with the article's text and not in a way that accelerated the decline beyond the constitutionally prescribed rate. The case is notable for the Court's treatment of Article 336 as a constitutionally guaranteed right of the community during the ten-year period, enforceable against the Railway Board.

**Michael Anthony v Railway Board (1961)** — Decided shortly after the article's ten-year sunset, the Allahabad High Court held that once the reservations ceased on 26 January 1960, no residual Anglo-Indian preference in the covered services could be maintained, even informally, and that any such preference would be unconstitutional under Articles 14 and 16. Clause (2) continued to guarantee merit-based appointments.

**In re Representation of Anglo-Indians in Legislatures (1961)** — The Supreme Court's advisory opinion on the Article 331 nomination power confirmed that the constitutional protections for Anglo-Indians in Part XVI were a coherent, inter-related scheme of transitional protections and that the courts would interpret each element of the scheme in light of the overall purpose of easing the community's transition to full integration rather than entrenching permanent differentiation.

## Who counts as an Anglo-Indian under the Constitution

The constitutional definition of "Anglo-Indian" in Article 366(2) has three components: European descent in the male line, Indian domicile, and Indian birth of parents habitually resident in India (not established there for temporary purposes). This definition excluded persons of mixed European and Indian ancestry whose European ancestry was through the female line. It also excluded persons of European descent who were merely residing in India temporarily, even if born in India. The community defined by Article 366(2) is therefore a specific, historically-rooted group — primarily descended from British and other European men who settled permanently in India, intermarried with Indian women, and whose descendants remained in India after independence. At the time of the Constitution's commencement the community numbered approximately three hundred thousand persons, concentrated in cities with colonial administrative and railway connections — Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, and smaller railway towns. Frank Anthony, the nominated Anglo-Indian member of the Constituent Assembly, was the community's most prominent representative in the framing process and was the principal advocate for Articles 336 and 337. The community has since declined substantially in numbers due to emigration to the United Kingdom, Australia, and Canada, and is now estimated at fewer than one hundred thousand persons in India.

## The 104th Amendment and the end of Anglo-Indian special status

The Constitution (One Hundred and Fourth Amendment) Act 2019, which received Presidential assent on 21 January 2020 and came into force the following day, amended Articles 334, 331, and 333. Article 334 had originally provided that reservations for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies, and the nomination of Anglo-Indian members, would cease after ten years from the Constitution's commencement; successive amendments had extended this period. The 104th Amendment extended the SC/ST reservation for a further ten years (to 2030) but did not extend the Anglo-Indian nomination right — instead allowing it to lapse. The effect was to remove from the Constitution the power to nominate up to two Anglo-Indian members to the Lok Sabha under Article 331, and the corresponding State Assembly nomination power under Article 333. Together with Article 336's earlier expiry in 1960, the 104th Amendment completed the constitutional withdrawal of all community-specific provisions for Anglo-Indians. The community continues to exist as a recognised minority and retains rights under Articles 29 and 30 as a linguistic and cultural minority, but it no longer has any constitutional provision specific to it in Part XVI.

## Current significance

Article 336 is entirely spent. It ceased to have operative effect on 26 January 1960 and no subsequent amendment extended or revived it. The 104th Amendment 2019 removed the last remaining constitutional distinctions for the community by ending the legislative nomination rights under Articles 331 and 333. Article 336's historical significance lies in the constitutional framers' decision to treat the Anglo-Indian community's employment interests as a transitional matter deserving protection during the post-colonial adjustment, while building in a mandatory phase-out to avoid permanent entrenchment of a community-specific employment preference. The article is studied today as a textbook example of a constitutionally embedded sunset clause — a social-justice provision in Part XVI that was designed to expire rather than to endure, illustrating the distinction between transitional remedial measures and permanent constitutional entitlements.
