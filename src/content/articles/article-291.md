## Why it matters

The history of Article 291 is one of the most instructive episodes in Indian constitutional law. It demonstrates three things in quick succession. First, the strength of constitutional entrenchment — the Supreme Court, in an eleven-judge bench, struck down the government's attempt to abolish privy purses by executive order, holding that a constitutional guarantee cannot be extinguished without formal amendment. Second, the government's willingness to use constituent power as a response to judicial protection — Parliament passed the Twenty-sixth Amendment within weeks of the judgment, achieving through the amendment process what the Court had said could not be done by executive order alone. Third, the limits of judicial protection — the former Rulers won the legal battle in December 1971 and the amendment wiping out their entitlement came into force on 28 December 1971. The episode fed directly into the Kesavananda Bharati litigation, in which the Supreme Court developed the basic structure doctrine partly as a response to precisely this pattern of using the amendment power to override constitutional protections.

## Reading the provision

Article 291 in its original form placed privy purse payments — guaranteed annual sums paid by the Government of India to the former Rulers of princely states — on a constitutional footing. The payments were charged on the Consolidated Fund of India, meaning they ranked ahead of ordinary appropriated expenditure and could not be reduced or stopped by ordinary parliamentary legislation, only by constitutional amendment. The amounts varied widely by state: the Nizam of Hyderabad received the largest payment and small zamindari states received token sums; in aggregate the annual privy purse bill was approximately Rs 5.5 crore by the early 1970s.

Article 362, which dealt with the recognition of Rulers and the guarantees given at the time of accession, worked alongside Article 291 as a companion provision. Both were omitted simultaneously by the Twenty-sixth Amendment.

## Key cases

**Madhavrao Scindia v. Union of India (1971)** — An eleven-judge Constitution Bench held by a majority that the President's order de-recognising the former Rulers and withdrawing their privy purses was unconstitutional. Article 291 created an enforceable constitutional right, not merely a political assurance, and that right could be extinguished only by formal amendment. The Court held the executive order was void and the privy purse payments remained due. The judgment was delivered on 15 December 1971; the Twenty-sixth Amendment was passed and came into force on 28 December 1971.

**H.H. Maharajadhiraja Madhav Rao Jiwaji Rao Scindia Bahadur v. Union of India (1971)** — The same litigation cited under the parties' full names in earlier reports — the ruling confirmed that recognition by the President was a constitutional prerequisite for the payment of privy purses, and that the executive could not simply revoke recognition as a device to withhold payments that the Constitution guaranteed.

**Post-1971 proceedings on arrears** — After the Twenty-sixth Amendment came into force, former Rulers sought payment of arrears accrued between the unlawful presidential de-recognition order in 1969 and the constitutional omission in 1971. Courts addressed the retroactive effect of the amendment and the extent to which the intervening unconstitutional executive action gave rise to enforceable claims, with decisions holding that the void executive order could not deprive Rulers of accrued payments that had been constitutionally due.

## The Twenty-sixth Amendment and constitutional theory

The abolition of privy purses is studied from several angles. From a pure constitutional-law perspective, it is the starkest example of what has been called "constituent power as a weapon" — the use of the amendment process not to reform the Constitution in a structural sense but to reverse a specific judicial decision. The government could not avoid the Article 291 text; so it removed the text. From a political-economy perspective, the abolition was central to Indira Gandhi's 1971 election platform of "Garibi Hatao" (Remove Poverty) and symbolised the elimination of hereditary privilege at public expense. The constitutional means chosen — omission rather than replacement — left no residual protection for the Rulers and gave no transitional arrangement.

The Kesavananda Bharati bench (1973), which developed the basic structure doctrine as a limit on Parliament's amending power, explicitly discussed the privy purse episode as illustrating the danger of treating the amendment power as unlimited. The doctrine that certain essential features of the Constitution are beyond the reach of even a two-thirds majority in Parliament emerged in part as a jurisprudential response to the aggressive constitutional engineering that Article 291's abolition exemplified.

## Current significance

Article 291 no longer has any operative legal effect. Its significance is entirely historical and theoretical. The episode is a reference point in every discussion of the scope of Parliament's amending power, the limits of judicial protection of constitutionally vested rights, and the relationship between constitutional promises made at the time of State integration and the reforming will of democratic majorities. The privy purse abolition is also invoked whenever the government renegotiates fiscal or autonomy arrangements with particular territories or communities — the argument that constitutional guarantees given at the time of accession are subject to later democratic revision finds both support and counterargument in what happened to Article 291.
