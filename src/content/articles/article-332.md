  332. Reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in the Legislative Assemblies of the States.—(1) Seats shall be reserved for the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes, [except the Scheduled Tribes in the autonomous districts of Assam], in the Legislative Assembly of every State .

  (2) Seats shall be reserved also for the autonomous districts in the Legislative Assembly of the State of Assam.

  (3) The number of seats reserved for the Scheduled Castes or the Scheduled Tribes in the Legislative Assembly of any State under clause (1) shall bear, as nearly as may be, the same proportion to the total number of seats in the Assembly as the population of the Scheduled Castes in the State or of the Scheduled Tribes in the State or part of the State, as the case may be, in respect of which seats are so reserved, bears to the total population of the State. [(3A) Notwithstanding anything contained in clause (3), until the taking effect, under article 170, of the re-adjustment, on the basis of the first census after the year [2026], of the number of seats in the Legislative Assemblies of the States of Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya, Mizoram and Nagaland, the seats which shall be reserved for the Scheduled Tribes in the Legislative Assembly of any such State shall be,—

  (a) if all the seats in the Legislative Assembly of such State in existence on the date of coming into force of the Constitution (Fiftyseventh Amendment) Act, 1987 (hereafter in this clause referred to as the existing Assembly) are held by members of the Scheduled Tribes, all the seats except one;

  (b) in any other case, such number of seats as bears to the total number of seats, a proportion not less than the number (as on the said date) of members belonging to the Scheduled Tribes in the existing Assembly bears to the total number of seats in the existing Assembly.] [(3B) Notwithstanding anything contained in clause (3), until the re-adjustment, under article 170, takes effect on the basis of the first census after the year [2026], of the number of seats in the Legislative Assembly of the State of Tripura, the seats which shall be reserved for the Scheduled Tribes in the Legislative Assembly shall be, such number of seats as bears to the total number of seats, a proportion not less than the number, as on the date of coming into force of the Constitution (Seventy-second Amendment) Act, 1992, of members belonging to the Scheduled Tribes in the Legislative Assembly in existence on the said date bears to the total number of seats in that Assembly.]

  (4) The number of seats reserved for an autonomous district in the Legislative Assembly of the State of Assam shall bear to the total number of seats in that Assembly a proportion not less than the population of the district bears to the total population of the State.

  (5) The constituencies for the seats reserved for any autonomous district of Assam shall not comprise any area outside that district .

  (6) No person who is not a member of a Scheduled Tribe of any autonomous district of the State of Assam shall be eligible for election to the Legislative Assembly of the State from any constituency of that district :

  [Provided that for elections to the Legislative Assembly of the State of Assam, the representation of the Scheduled Tribes and non-Scheduled Tribes in the constituencies included in the Bodoland Territorial Areas District, so notified, and existing prior to the constitution of Bodoland Territorial Areas District, shall be maintained.]

## Why it matters

Article 332 is the constitutional anchor for SC and ST representation in the legislatures that govern most citizens' day-to-day lives. State assemblies control land, police, public health, primary education, agriculture, and local governance — the domains that most directly affect marginalised communities. Without mandatory reserved seats, the structural barriers to SC and ST electoral participation — social intimidation, resource inequality, and candidate-selection gatekeeping by dominant communities — would likely produce near-zero SC and ST representation in assemblies despite their substantial share of the electorate. The proportionality formula ensures that as SC and ST populations grow or as delimitation exercises redraw constituency maps, reserved seats track demographic reality rather than remaining frozen at historically convenient numbers.

The article's north-east provisions reflect a constitutional sensitivity to the particular conditions of States where Scheduled Tribes form a substantial majority of the population. In Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya, Mizoram, and Nagaland, the concern is the inverse of the mainland problem: the danger is not that STs will be under-represented but that any strict proportional application of the national formula could be used to introduce non-tribal candidates into assemblies that are historically tribal-majority. Clauses (3A) and (3B) address this by creating floors, not just proportionality rules, for ST representation in these States.

The Bodoland Territorial Areas District proviso, added after the Bodo Peace Accord of 2020, represents a further refinement. In the special context of post-conflict territorial settlements, the Constitution preserves the communal balance of representation that existed before the territorial restructuring, preventing either side from gaming the reservation framework to gain an assembly-seat advantage in the reconstituted district.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)** is the operative provision: it mandates reservation for SCs and STs in every State Legislative Assembly. The exclusion of STs in Assam's autonomous districts from this clause avoids double-counting; those STs are covered by the district-level mechanism in clause (2) instead.

**Clause (2)** reserves seats specifically for each autonomous district in the Assam assembly, creating a district-level representation tier on top of the general ST reservation. This two-tier arrangement is unique to Assam and reflects its Sixth Schedule governance structure for tribal hill areas.

**Clause (3)** prescribes the proportionality formula: reserved seats must approximate the ratio of the SC or ST population to the total State population. The phrase "as nearly as may be" acknowledges that exact proportionality may not always be achievable given the discrete number of constituencies in any assembly.

**Clauses (3A) and (3B)** are protective floors. Clause (3A), inserted by the 57th Amendment (1987), protects Arunachal Pradesh, Meghalaya, Mizoram, and Nagaland; clause (3B), inserted by the 72nd Amendment (1992), protects Tripura. Both freeze the ST seat proportion at the level it stood as of the relevant amendment date, preventing any dilution through delimitation until post-2026 census delimitation takes effect under Article 170.

**Clauses (4), (5), and (6)** apply the proportionality formula to each autonomous district's share of Assam's assembly seats, confine those constituencies geographically within the district, and bar non-tribal candidates from contesting. The Bodoland proviso requires that the tribal to non-tribal composition of constituencies in the Bodoland Territorial Areas District remain as it was before the district was notified.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly treated Article 332 as a matched pair with Article 330, applying the same proportionality principle at the state level. Members from what was then the larger undivided Assam pressed for the autonomous-district provisions, arguing that tribal communities in the hill areas required direct representation in the State assembly independently of whether they met the State-wide population thresholds for general ST reservation. Dr B.R. Ambedkar acknowledged the unique character of the Sixth Schedule areas and the need for guaranteed representation at the State level in addition to the district governance councils. The north-east protection clauses were not part of the original text; they were added by Parliament in 1987 and 1992 as the newly created north-eastern States demonstrated that uniform proportionality rules could work against the interests of tribal-majority assemblies.

## Key cases

**Meghraj Kothari v Delimitation Commission (1966)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the Delimitation Commission's allocation of reserved seats in Legislative Assemblies is a legislative function immune from judicial review. The Court held that the Commission exercises a constitutional function under Articles 82 and 170 and its orders cannot be challenged in any court; this immunity applies equally to Article 332 seat allocations as to those under Article 330.

**Param Anand v Chief Election Commissioner (various)** — State-level challenges to the delineation of reserved constituencies have consistently reiterated that the proportionality formula in Article 332(3) is mandatory, and that deviations from it require explicit constitutional justification through the delimitation exercise itself rather than through political negotiation.

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held that States may sub-classify within the SC and ST categories to provide preferential quotas within the reserved category to more backward sub-groups, overruling E.V. Chinnaiah (2005). Although the decision directly concerned employment reservation, its reasoning about the non-homogeneity of SC and ST lists raises the question whether differentiated access to electoral reserved seats within the SC or ST category could similarly be introduced — a question courts have not yet resolved.

**Delimitation Commission Orders (2008 — State assemblies)** — The last completed delimitation for State assemblies based on the 2001 census applied Article 332's proportionality formula to all States. Those orders remain in force until post-2026 census delimitation, meaning the reserved seat allocation in all assemblies reflects population data that is now a quarter-century old.

## Current significance

The freeze on delimitation since the 84th Amendment (2002) means that the reserved seat allocation in all State assemblies reflects the 2001 census. When fresh delimitation occurs after the 2026 census, States with significant SC or ST population growth will see revised reserved seat counts. The transition will be a politically charged process, and Article 332's proportionality formula will be the constitutional baseline the Delimitation Commission must satisfy.

The Davinder Singh (2024) sub-classification ruling has generated academic and political debate about whether sub-classification could apply to electoral reservations. Any candidate on the SC or ST list can currently contest a reserved constituency. Introducing differentiated access to reserved seats — allowing only sub-groups identified as more backward to contest certain reserved constituencies — would require either a constitutional amendment to Article 332 or a statutory framework that the Supreme Court would likely scrutinise very carefully. The distinction between sub-classification in employment (where the State assigns quotas within the reserved category) and sub-classification in elections (where the reserved seat benefits an entire constituency) makes the electoral application constitutionally distinct and more difficult.

For Assam, the Bodoland Territorial Areas District framework continues to evolve as the 2020 Peace Accord is implemented. The Article 332 reserved-seat provisions for that region will need to be reconciled with the post-2026 delimitation exercise and with the evolving political arrangements within the Sixth Schedule governance framework.
