## Why it matters

Article 150 is the constitutional provision that makes the CAG something more than an auditor of accounts as he finds them — it makes the CAG the architect of the accounting standards to which public money must conform. By requiring that account forms be prescribed on the CAG's advice, the Constitution ensures that the government cannot choose an accounting format that obscures information the CAG would need to conduct a meaningful audit. The provision closes the loop between the audit function (Article 149) and the report function (Article 151) by ensuring that what is audited, and how it is recorded, are both within the CAG's sphere of influence.

The 44th Amendment's substitution of "on the advice of" for "after consultation with" transformed the CAG's role in this area from advisory to determinative. Under the original formulation, the government was required to seek the CAG's views on accounting form but was free to reject them. Under the amended provision, the President must act on the CAG's advice, not merely receive it. This change was part of a broader set of post-Emergency constitutional corrections that sought to restore the independence of accountability institutions, and it represents the single most significant strengthening of the CAG's standard-setting authority since the Constitution came into force.

The practical importance of this power is greatest in debates about off-budget financing, contingent liabilities, and the use of Special Purpose Vehicles. When a government funds infrastructure through an off-balance-sheet entity, guarantees debt that does not appear in the consolidated accounts, or routes expenditure through autonomous bodies whose accounts are not consolidated with the government's own, the fiscal position of the Union or State that Parliament and the public see is incomplete. By prescribing account forms that require such items to be brought into the consolidated accounts, the CAG can — under Article 150 — expand dramatically what is visible about the true fiscal position of governments.

## Reading the provision

The provision as it now stands reads: "The accounts of the Union and of the States shall be kept in such form as the President may, on the advice of the Comptroller and Auditor-General of India, prescribe." The square brackets in the official text mark the text inserted by the 44th Amendment.

**"On the advice of":** This phrase, in the constitutional context, carries the same meaning as "on the advice of the Council of Ministers" in Article 74 — it is binding, not precatory. The President must act on the CAG's recommendation on matters of accounting form. If the CAG advises a transition to accrual-basis accounting, the President cannot reject that advice on policy grounds without acting contrary to the constitutional text. The executive must work with the CAG to resolve disagreements through deliberation, not by unilateral override.

**"Such form as the President may prescribe":** The President's role is to give formal legal sanction to the accounting standard advised by the CAG — not to choose between competing accounting frameworks. This separation of technical authority (the CAG) from formal legal prescription (the President) follows the pattern established in other provisions where a specialist constitutional officer advises a formal executive act.

**"Accounts of the Union and of the States":** The provision applies to both levels of the federal system simultaneously. The CAG's Article 150 power extends to prescribing accounting standards for State government accounts, not just Union accounts. This has significant implications for federal financial management: it means that accounting form in India is a constitutionally centralised function in the sense that a single constitutional officer — the CAG — sets the standards to which all governments at all levels must conform.

## The 44th Amendment — context and significance

The original Article 150 text ("after consultation with the Comptroller and Auditor-General") reflected a more modest view of the CAG's standard-setting role. The 44th Amendment Act, 1978, enacted by the Janata government in the aftermath of the Emergency, was a comprehensive effort to restore constitutional safeguards that had been weakened or bypassed during 1975–1977. The amendment changed the consultation formula to advice in Article 150, alongside other changes to Articles 352–360 (Emergency provisions), the fundamental rights articles, and the provisions relating to Prime Ministerial accountability. The Article 150 change was part of a conscious effort to ensure that independent constitutional institutions — the CAG, the Election Commission, and others — could not be marginalised by a determined executive through the simple mechanism of ignoring their views.

The change has had lasting practical significance. It means that any government seeking to introduce a new accounting system — whether a move to programme-based budgeting, gender-responsive budgeting as a formal accounting category, or accrual-basis accounting — must obtain the CAG's positive advice before the President can prescribe the new form. This gives the CAG a veto over accounting reforms that the executive might otherwise introduce unilaterally to suit its reporting preferences.

## Government Accounting Rules and the IPSAS transition

The Government Accounting Rules, 1990 (GAR), and the Civil Accounts Manual are the primary instruments through which Article 150's power is operationalised at the Union level. The Controller General of Accounts, under the Ministry of Finance, works in close coordination with the CAG's office in developing and revising these rules. At the State level, corresponding Accounts Codes and Treasury Codes are prescribed in consultation with State Accountants-General (who are officers of the CAG's department).

The most consequential ongoing exercise of Article 150 power is the transition from cash-basis to accrual-basis accounting. Cash-basis accounting records transactions when cash is received or paid; accrual-basis accounting records them when the underlying obligation arises. For governments with large contingent liabilities, unfunded pension obligations, and off-budget capital commitments, the difference between cash-basis and accrual-basis accounts can be enormous. The CAG has been a consistent advocate for accrual-basis accounting as the standard to be prescribed under Article 150, arguing that only accrual accounts give Parliament and the public an accurate picture of the government's true fiscal position.

International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS) — the global framework for government accounting — are accrual-based, and the CAG has worked with the International Organisation of Supreme Audit Institutions (INTOSAI) and the IPSAS Board to align India's government accounting standards with international practice. Article 150 is the constitutional provision that gives this alignment legal force at the domestic level: the President prescribes the form on the CAG's advice, and once prescribed, the form is constitutionally binding on all Union and State government accounts.

Several State governments — Odisha, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu among others — have piloted accrual accounting in selected departments partly in response to Article 150-based CAG recommendations. A full national transition remains work in progress, complicated by differences in State accounting infrastructure and the political sensitivity of presenting government liabilities on accrual terms.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor-General of India v. K.S. Jagannathan (1986) 2 SCC 679** — The Court's ruling on the width of the CAG's constitutional mandate under Articles 148–151 implicitly validated the accounting standards function under Article 150, treating the CAG's role in prescribing account forms as integral to the overall audit and reporting mandate rather than a separate administrative function.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v. Comptroller and Auditor-General of India (1989)** — The Court confirmed the CAG's authority over State accounting systems under Articles 149 and 150, holding that State governments cannot use the structure of their internal financial management to limit the CAG's access to information required for audit purposes — a ruling that reinforces the standard-setting function by preventing the creation of accounting systems designed to frustrate audit.

## Current significance

The Article 150 power is increasingly important as government finance becomes more complex. Three specific areas illustrate its contemporary significance.

**Off-budget financing:** Governments at both Union and State level have used off-budget mechanisms — Special Purpose Vehicles, extra-budgetary resources, and government-guaranteed debt — to fund expenditure without recording it in the main accounts. The CAG's ability under Article 150 to prescribe account forms that require consolidation of such items is one of the most powerful tools available for making true fiscal positions visible. Successive CAG reports have noted that the Union's off-budget borrowings, channelled through state-owned enterprises like FCI and NHAI, do not appear in the Union budget deficit figures, and have recommended accounting form changes that would bring them in.

**Public financial management reforms:** The Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act framework, the Finance Commission's recommendations on fiscal data transparency, and the Union government's programme-based budgeting initiatives all operate within the framework of accounting standards prescribed under Article 150. The CAG's advice on account forms shapes what information is produced for Parliamentary scrutiny and what comparisons can be made across years and governments.

**National digitisation of accounts:** The Public Financial Management System (PFMS) and the Treasury Single Account reforms being implemented across Union and State governments require the prescription of data standards that are in effect a form of account — the digital representation of financial transactions across the public sector. Article 150 is the constitutional authority under which the President can prescribe such standards on the CAG's advice, giving the CAG a role in shaping the digital infrastructure of public finance management in India.
