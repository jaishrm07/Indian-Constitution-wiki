## Why it matters

Article 268 embodies a distinctive principle in the constitutional tax architecture: the legislature that imposes a tax need not be the government that collects or benefits from it. By separating the legislative power to levy (Union, under Entry 91, List I) from the administrative power to collect and the fiscal benefit of retention (States), the Article serves two constitutional purposes simultaneously. It maintains uniformity in commercial law by ensuring that the same rate of duty applies across all States for instruments in national commerce, while also ensuring that States retain the revenue generated within their territory — respecting the federal fiscal entitlement of States without requiring Finance Commission intervention.

The structural logic here is federal efficiency: financial instruments like cheques, promissory notes, and bills of exchange circulate in national and international commerce. If each State could levy its own stamp duty at its own rate on these instruments, there would be constant incentives for financial actors to execute documents in low-rate States and use them in high-rate ones. Uniform Union-set rates remove that arbitrage opportunity. But because the instruments are executed in specific States, the revenue benefit belongs to those States — the Union has no fiscal interest in collecting stamp duty on a bill of exchange drawn in Mumbai other than as a uniformity-ensuring intermediary.

This arrangement also reflects the founders' choice to allocate the "regulatory benefit" of tax uniformity to the Union while allocating the "fiscal benefit" of revenue to the States. The division is not an accident of constitutional drafting; it replicates the pattern seen across Articles 268 to 270, where the Union's superior legislative capacity is used to prevent fiscal fragmentation of national markets while State fiscal entitlements are preserved through mandatory assignment of proceeds.

The automatic and unconditional nature of the Article 268 assignment — the language is "shall be assigned," not "may be assigned" or "shall be assigned as Parliament determines" — means that this is one of the few instances of constitutionally guaranteed, non-discretionary State revenue in the Indian federal system. No Finance Commission recommendation is required, no presidential order is needed, and no annual appropriation act is needed: the revenue goes to the State the moment it is collected.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) identifies the subject matter: "such stamp duties as are mentioned in the Union List." Entry 91 of the Union List covers stamp duties on bills of exchange, cheques, promissory notes, bills of lading, letters of credit, policies of insurance, proxies, and receipts — instruments that are predominantly instruments of national and international commerce, moving freely across State borders. The Union fixes the rate through the Indian Stamp Act, 1899 and its amendments. State legislatures have no power to modify the rate on Entry 91 instruments, even though the State collects and retains the proceeds.

The collection split in Clause (1) reflects the constitutional structure of the territory: in Union Territories, there is no separate State government, so the Union collects directly. In States, the duty is collected by the State government through its own stamp duty administration — the same machinery that handles Entry 63 State-list stamp duties. This co-sharing of administrative infrastructure between Union-rate duties (Entry 91) and State-rate duties (Entry 63) is administratively convenient but has created occasional confusion about which rate and which statutory authority governs a particular document.

Clause (2) makes the revenue assignment automatic and unconditional: the proceeds "shall not form part of the Consolidated Fund of India, but shall be assigned to that State." The use of "shall" in both directions — shall not form part of the CFI, and shall be assigned — is constitutionally significant. It removes any executive discretion over whether or when to transfer the proceeds. The State's entitlement arises at the point of collection, not at the point of a subsequent transfer order. This is the clearest form of constitutionally mandated revenue assignment in the fiscal federalism scheme.

## The stamp duty split and Entry 91 vs Entry 63

A key structural distinction runs between Union List Entry 91 (stamp duties on negotiable instruments and other financial documents) and State List Entry 63 (stamp duties other than those listed in Entry 91). Article 268 applies only to Entry 91 instruments. States have independent legislative competence over Entry 63 instruments — they may set their own rates and collect the proceeds entirely on their own account. The boundary between the two entries has been the subject of repeated litigation, particularly for hybrid documents that have characteristics of both commercial instruments and conveyances.

The practical consequence is that when a single transaction involves both an Entry 91 instrument and an Entry 63 document — for instance, a sale backed by a promissory note and a conveyance deed — different stamp duty regimes apply. The Entry 91 component is governed by Union-set rates, collected by the State but at rates it cannot vary; the Entry 63 component is governed by State-set rates, collected by the State at its own discretion. High-value commercial transactions routinely involve both layers, making the Entry 91 / Entry 63 boundary a significant compliance issue.

## Key cases

**Hindustan Lever Ltd v. State of Maharashtra** — Courts examining Article 268 stamp duty questions have confirmed that the legislative competence to impose the duty rests solely with the Union under Entry 91 of List I. Any State attempt to impose an additional or supplementary duty on Entry 91 instruments is repugnant and void under Article 254, regardless of the fact that the State collects the Union-imposed duty. The State's collection function does not create parallel legislative competence.

**India Cements Ltd v. State of Tamil Nadu AIR 1990 SC 85** — The broader stamp duty jurisprudence established that the characterisation of a document for stamp duty purposes follows its substance, not its label. A document that functions as a bill of exchange falls within Entry 91 regardless of how it is described in the instrument. This principle applies equally to the characterisation of documents for Article 268 purposes: if a document is substantively an Entry 91 instrument, the Union levies and the State collects and retains at the Union rate, and the State cannot impose its own duty on top.

**Broach Distillery & Chemical Works v. Commissioner of Stamp Duties** — The Court considered the boundary between Union List Entry 91 and State List Entry 63, clarifying the scope of the Article 268 assignment and establishing principles to prevent double imposition where a document could arguably fall under both entries. The pith and substance of the transaction, rather than incidental characteristics of the document, determines which Entry governs.

## Current significance

The 101st Amendment inserted Article 268A to cover service tax, but that article was subsequently deleted when GST came into force through the same amendment framework — service tax was subsumed into the GST system. The residual role of Article 268 stamp duties on financial instruments has been refined as GST absorbs many commercial transactions, but stamp duty on securities and financial instruments remains a significant State revenue source in high-transaction financial centres such as Maharashtra. The Bombay Stamp Act, implementing Entry 63 duties, operates alongside the Indian Stamp Act for Entry 91 duties in the same geography.

Electronic documents and digital contracts raise unsettled questions about whether Article 268 stamp duty obligations apply to e-instruments — a question relevant to fintech, blockchain-based contracts, and digital securities issuance. Several States have pressed the Union to revise Entry 91 rates upward to reflect inflation and market practice, since their own revenues from Union-levied duties track the Union's rate decisions, which have not always kept pace with nominal transaction values. The Depositories Act and the dematerialisation of securities has also raised questions about whether electronic book entries that replace physical instruments retain the Entry 91 character for stamp duty purposes — litigation on this point is ongoing in several High Courts.
