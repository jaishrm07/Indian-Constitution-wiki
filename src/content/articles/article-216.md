## Why it matters

Article 216's open-ended formula — "such other Judges as the President may from
time to time deem it necessary to appoint" — is both flexible and fragile. Its
flexibility allows court strength to grow as caseload demands. Its fragility
lies in the absence of any constitutional floor: there is no minimum number of
judges, no mandatory ratio of judges to population, and no time limit within
which vacancies must be filled. The result is that the executive and the
collegium together exercise enormous de facto power over the capacity of each
High Court, with no constitutional mechanism to compel timely action.

There are currently 25 High Courts in India. Several — including Allahabad,
Bombay, Calcutta, and Madras — predate the Constitution and trace their origins
to charters issued under the High Courts Act, 1861. Article 216 brought all of
them within a uniform constitutional structure and made the President, acting
on collegium recommendations, the authority for all composition decisions.

## Reading the provision

The article's current text — a single sentence plus asterisks marking excised
sub-clauses — is the product of successive constitutional amendments that
simplified an originally more elaborate scheme. The original Article 216
distinguished between permanent and additional judges and specified the
duration for which additional judges could be appointed. These distinctions were
progressively removed, reflecting the constitutional judgment that the judicial
hierarchy itself should manage court composition.

The President's power under Article 216 is exercised on the binding
recommendation of the collegium after the Second Judges Case (1993). The
collegium recommends both whom to appoint and — implicitly, through the volume
of recommendations it makes — how many judges a court should have. The
government's role is to accept, return for reconsideration, or delay acting
on recommendations. Delay is the most commonly used form of executive influence
over court composition.

The "sanctioned strength" concept does not appear in Article 216 but has
developed as an administrative framework. The government approves a sanctioned
strength — the maximum number of judges permitted — and the collegium recommends
names up to that ceiling. Vacancies arise when sanctioned posts are not filled.
The vacancy rate across all High Courts has persistently remained between 25
and 35 per cent over the past two decades.

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (Second Judges
Case, 1993)** — While most directly about Article 217, the Court's reasoning
about collegium primacy governs the appointment power under Article 216. The
number and identity of judges a court should have are matters on which the
collegium has primacy; the President cannot independently determine that fewer
judges are needed.

**All India Judges Association v Union of India (2002)** — The Supreme Court
directed the government to take concrete steps to fill judicial vacancies and
increase sanctioned strength, treating the failure to maintain adequate judicial
numbers as a constitutional problem connected to the right of access to justice.
The Court monitored compliance through periodic orders, making Article 216's
open-ended formula subject to a degree of judicial supervision.

**Union of India v Sankalchand Himatlal Sheth (1977)** — The Court held that
transfers and appointments alike must be made in a manner consistent with the
constitutional guarantee of an independent judiciary. The composition power in
Article 216 cannot be used to pack courts with judges sympathetic to the
government of the day.

## Current significance

The Allahabad High Court, with a sanctioned strength of over 160 judges and a
working strength that has at times fallen below 100, exemplifies the structural
problem created by Article 216's open-ended formula. Backlogs of over six
million cases at that single court reflect a vacancy crisis that is
constitutional in origin even if administrative in operation. The Law
Commission, parliamentary standing committees, and the Supreme Court itself
have periodically called for a statutory or constitutional floor on judicial
strength, but no amendment or legislation has been enacted.

The collegium's role in determining composition has been criticised for its
lack of transparency. The criteria for deciding how many judges a particular
court needs are not publicly stated. Recommendations are made without published
reasons. The phrase "deem it necessary" implies a reasoned judgment about
necessity, but neither the collegium nor the government publicly explains how
that judgment is made.

The parallel provision for the Supreme Court is Article 124(1), read alongside
the Supreme Court (Number of Judges) Act, 1956. Unlike Article 216, that
statutory framework fixes the Supreme Court's sanctioned strength at a precise
number. No comparable legislation fixes High Court strengths, leaving them
determined entirely by administrative consensus between the collegium and the
executive — a structural asymmetry between the apex court and the High Courts.
