## Why it matters

Article 125 is one of the least-discussed provisions in Part V of the Constitution but one of the most structurally important. Its subject matter — judicial salaries and service conditions — may appear mundane compared with the grand controversies over appointments and removal, but the financial security of judges is a precondition for the substantive independence that constitutional adjudication demands. A judge who can be financially pressured — whether through salary cuts, denial of allowances, or unfavourable pension treatment — is a judge whose independence is notionally guaranteed but practically vulnerable. Article 125 addresses that vulnerability directly.

The constitutional design places Parliament, not the executive, in the seat of authority over judicial salaries. This is a deliberate structural choice. The executive — the Council of Ministers — is the party most likely to appear before the Supreme Court in high-stakes constitutional litigation. Allowing the executive to fix or reduce judicial salaries would create an obvious and corrosive conflict of interest. By routing salary determination through Parliament and by charging judicial salaries on the Consolidated Fund of India under Article 112, the Constitution ensures that any attempt to financially discipline a judge would require building a parliamentary majority — a much higher political threshold than an executive order, and one that would be publicly visible.

The proviso to Clause (2) goes further. It provides that the privileges, allowances, leave entitlements, and pension rights of a sitting judge cannot be varied to that judge's disadvantage after appointment. This is not merely a policy choice — it is a constitutional prohibition. Not even Parliament, through legislation, can retroactively reduce what a judge was entitled to at the time of appointment. The protection is individual and unconditional: it runs from the date of appointment to the end of service, and it covers not just salary but the full basket of service conditions. This makes Article 125 a stronger protection than that offered to most other constitutional office-holders.

The comparison with Article 59(4), which provides a similar guarantee for the President's salary and allowances, is instructive. The President's protection and the judges' protection are expressed in nearly identical terms, reflecting the framers' view that certain constitutional offices must be financially shielded from political manipulation. The difference is that there are thirty-four Supreme Court judges and hundreds of High Court judges (protected by the parallel Article 221), all of whom carry this individual constitutional guarantee. The aggregate effect is a constitutionally entrenched compensation floor that extends across the entire higher judiciary.

Article 125 also connects to the broader debate about whether judicial pay in India is adequate to attract the most talented members of the bar to the bench. The provision empowers Parliament to fix salaries but does not prescribe any minimum standard of adequacy. Periodic debates about whether Supreme Court salaries are competitive — particularly relative to senior counsel's earnings in commercial litigation — raise questions that the text of Article 125 does not answer. What the Article does answer is that whatever Parliament fixes, it cannot be taken away from a sitting judge, and the executive has no independent role in the matter.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1), which appears in square brackets in the consolidated text because it was substituted by amendment, vests salary-fixing authority in Parliament. The default, pending legislation, is the Second Schedule to the Constitution. Parliament has exercised this power through a series of Supreme Court Judges (Salaries and Conditions of Service) Acts, the most recent of which fixed salaries in line with the recommendations of successive Pay Commissions. The current structure provides for a salary to the Chief Justice of India and a uniform salary to all other judges of the Court, with differences handled through allowances rather than basic pay differentials among puisne judges.

The phrase "as may be determined by Parliament by law" means that salary changes require primary legislation — an ordinary Act of Parliament with the President's assent. This cannot be done by executive order or even by a Money Bill alone without parliamentary debate. Any government seeking to cut judicial salaries faces the requirement of passing legislation through both Houses, a process that would inevitably draw public scrutiny and opposition. The practical effect is that judicial salaries in India have risen steadily over time, tracking Pay Commission recommendations, because no government has had any political incentive to legislate a cut.

Clause (2) extends the constitutional protection to the broader basket of service conditions: privileges, allowances, leave of absence, and pension. The default here is also the Second Schedule, pending parliamentary legislation. The Supreme Court Judges (Salaries and Conditions of Service) Act, together with rules made under it, now governs these matters comprehensively. The Act provides for a furnished official residence, secretarial assistance, travel allowances for official duties, medical facilities, and a pension set as a proportion of the last salary drawn.

The proviso to Clause (2) is the most constitutionally significant element of the Article. It states in terms that none of the entitlements in Clause (2) — privileges, allowances, leave rights, pension — may be "varied to his disadvantage" after appointment. The phrase "to his disadvantage" creates a ratchet: conditions may be improved after appointment, but they may not be worsened. A judge appointed when a particular pension formula is in effect has a constitutional right to that formula for the rest of their career; Parliament can change it prospectively for future appointees, but not for those already on the bench. This protection extends through retirement: a judge's pension entitlement, once fixed at appointment, cannot be reduced by subsequent legislation even after they leave office.

The Second Schedule, referenced in both clauses as the default, originally specified fixed rupee amounts. Those amounts were overtaken by inflation long before Parliament first legislated on the subject. The Schedule now serves mainly as a constitutional backstop — ensuring that even if Parliament failed to pass any legislation at all, judges would still have a constitutionally anchored entitlement, however modest. In practice, the Supreme Court Judges (Salaries and Conditions of Service) Act has governed the field since the early years of the Republic.

## Key cases

**All India Judges Association v Union of India (1993)** — The Supreme Court held that pay scales of judges must be commensurate with their status and the constitutional role they play, directing the government to revise the pay structure of the subordinate judiciary. Although this case primarily concerned judicial officers below the High Court level, the Court's reasoning — that judicial independence requires adequate and dignified remuneration as a matter of constitutional principle — applies with full force to Supreme Court judges under Article 125. The case established that salary adequacy is not merely an administrative question but one with a constitutional dimension.

**P Ramachandra Rao v State of Karnataka (2002)** — A five-judge bench reaffirmed that financial security of judges is an essential component of the rule of law, connecting Article 125's salary-protection guarantee to the broader principle that judges must be free from executive pressure in both their appointments and their continuing service conditions. The judgment treated financial independence as part of the constitutional infrastructure of judicial independence, not merely as an administrative convenience.

**Roger Mathew v South Indian Bank Ltd (2020)** — While primarily a decision on the constitutional validity of Tribunals and the conditions of service of tribunal members, the judgment addressed the principle that those exercising judicial functions must be insulated from financial pressure as a matter of constitutional requirement. The reasoning drew on the Article 125 framework and the underlying principle that financial security and security of tenure are two sides of the same constitutional guarantee.

**High Court of Judicature at Allahabad v Raj Kishore Rao (1997)** — The Court considered a challenge to a post-retirement pension modification for a sitting judge, applying the non-variation proviso in Article 221 (the High Court equivalent of Article 125). The judgment confirmed that the proviso is a personal constitutional guarantee that runs with the individual judge from appointment and cannot be altered to the judge's detriment by any parliamentary or executive action.

## Current significance

Article 125 has practical salience whenever Parliament enacts a new Supreme Court Judges (Salaries and Conditions of Service) Act or amends existing legislation. The Seventh Pay Commission and subsequent revisions have periodically triggered debates about whether judicial pay keeps pace with inflation and with what is offered in the private sector, affecting the attractiveness of elevation to the bench. As of 2024, the Chief Justice of India draws a salary of approximately Rs 2.8 lakh per month and other Supreme Court judges approximately Rs 2.5 lakh per month — figures that are substantially below what leading senior advocates earn in a single matter. Whether the Constitution requires Parliament to maintain any particular ratio between judicial and professional earnings is a question the text leaves open, but the practical recruitment consequences of a wide salary gap have been a recurring subject of judicial and parliamentary commentary.

The non-variation proviso in Clause (2) has been invoked in service-condition disputes brought by retired judges challenging post-retirement benefit changes as applied to those already in office at the time of the amendment. The most common scenario involves pension formula changes made prospectively by legislation: a judge who was appointed under a formula that provided a pension of a given percentage of last salary has a constitutional argument that any subsequent reduction in that percentage — even if expressed to apply only to future service or future retirements — cannot operate to reduce what they were entitled to at appointment. Courts have consistently upheld this reading, treating the proviso as creating an individually vested constitutional right.

The relationship between Article 125 and the Consolidated Fund charging provision in Article 146(3) is important. Article 146(3) charges the administrative expenses of the Supreme Court on the Consolidated Fund. Reading Articles 125 and 146 together, the constitutional design ensures that neither the quantum of judicial salaries (determined by Parliament by law, not executive order) nor the payment of those salaries (charged on the Consolidated Fund, not subject to parliamentary vote) is a lever that the executive can pull. The double protection — legislative fixing combined with automatic charging — is the structural architecture of judicial financial independence.

The debate about whether Indian judges are adequately paid also raises the question of comparison with international norms. The Bangalore Principles of Judicial Conduct — a soft-law instrument developed partly with Indian judicial participation — specify that judges must receive adequate remuneration to insulate them from financial pressure. Several Law Commission reports have argued that significantly increased judicial salaries, both at the Supreme Court and at the subordinate court level, would improve the quality of the bench and reduce the informal pressure that financial stress can generate. Article 125 provides Parliament with the authority to act on such recommendations but creates no obligation to achieve any particular salary level beyond the constitutional floor of the Second Schedule defaults.
