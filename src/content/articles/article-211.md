## What this Article does

Article 211 prohibits discussion in any State Legislature of the conduct of any Judge of the Supreme Court or of a High Court in the discharge of their duties. The provision is an absolute restriction — there is no qualification or exception based on public interest. It protects judicial independence by ensuring that judges cannot be subjected to legislative criticism for decisions made in the exercise of their judicial functions, thereby shielding the judiciary from political pressure transmitted through the legislature. The parallel Union provision is Article 121.

The article's scope is precise: it bars discussion of the conduct of a judge "in the discharge of his duties." This phrase limits the prohibition to judicial conduct — acts performed in the exercise of judicial functions. Administrative misconduct, personal conduct, or conduct in capacities other than the judicial office is not covered, though the boundary is sometimes contested. The restriction applies to all forms of discussion, including questions to the government, motions, resolutions, and debates: no vehicle for legislative debate may be used to canvass the judicial conduct of a sitting judge.

## The distinction between judges and judgments

A recurring interpretive question under Article 211 is whether the legislature may discuss a court's decision without discussing the judge's conduct. The provision bars discussion of the conduct of judges, not judgments as such. In principle, a legislature can debate the legal or policy implications of a court ruling without impugning the judge who delivered it. In practice, however, this line is difficult to maintain: criticism of a decision often slides into implied criticism of the judge who made it, particularly when the criticism is that the decision was motivated by bias, corruption, or improper influences.

Speakers and Chairmen of State Legislatures have developed rulings on this distinction, generally allowing discussion of judgments and their policy consequences while disallowing language that attributes improper motives to the judge personally. These Speaker's rulings have not uniformly resolved the tension, and the difficulty of maintaining a clean distinction between criticism of a decision and criticism of the judge's conduct in making it has meant that Article 211 continues to generate procedural controversy.

## Key cases

**Keshav Singh Reference (1965)** — The Supreme Court's presidential reference addressed the relationship between legislative privilege and judicial authority at length. The judgment made clear that provisions like Article 211 are not merely courtesies to the judiciary but are constitutional structural guarantees. The prohibition on discussion of judicial conduct in the discharge of duties means the legislature cannot use its floor as a forum for attacking judges who have decided cases in ways the majority dislikes.

**Sub-Committee on Judicial Accountability v Union of India (1991)** — The Supreme Court held that attempts to use parliamentary or legislative proceedings to pressure the judiciary, or to investigate judges through non-constitutional mechanisms, are inconsistent with the constitutional scheme of judicial independence. Article 211's prohibition on State legislative discussion of judicial conduct is one element of that broader scheme.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1998 (Third Judges Case)** — The Court's emphasis on the independence of the judiciary as a basic structural feature of the Constitution contextualises Article 211 as a provision that cannot be read down or circumvented even for ostensibly legitimate legislative purposes.

## Current significance

Article 211 remains relevant whenever State Legislatures seek to pass resolutions censuring High Court or Supreme Court decisions, or when ruling parties wish to use the floor of the House to apply pressure on the judiciary in the context of politically sensitive litigation. The prohibition extends to all judicial conduct in the discharge of duties, which means that even general criticism of a judge's approach to a class of cases — not merely specific rulings — falls within the restriction. Questions about the boundary between permissible discussion of judicial administration and impermissible criticism of judicial conduct continue to arise in practice.

In states where the High Court has been engaged in supervising government responses to public health emergencies, environmental regulation, or electoral disputes, the ruling party's temptation to use the Assembly floor to criticise the court has regularly come up against the Article 211 bar. Presiding officers have had to disallow questions and discussions on multiple occasions. The provision is therefore not merely a historical curiosity but an active constitutional constraint that requires frequent application in the day-to-day management of legislative business.

Article 211's counterpart at the Union level is Article 121. The two provisions are identically worded, and the Supreme Court's interpretation of Article 121 applies with equal force to Article 211. One difference in context is that Article 121 covers only the Supreme Court and High Courts, whereas Article 211 — being a State provision — covers both courts for the purpose of State Legislative proceedings. This means that a State Legislature is barred from discussing the conduct of a Supreme Court judge (a national court) just as much as it is barred from discussing a High Court judge whose decisions it may have a more immediate political interest in criticising.

The provision applies to all State Legislatures — both unicameral and bicameral — meaning that Legislative Councils are equally bound by the restriction. In bicameral states, questions about judicial conduct have occasionally been raised in the Council under the guise of general legislative or administrative inquiries, testing the Presiding Officers' willingness to apply the Article 211 bar in a House that is not directly accountable to a ministerial majority. The consistent application of the prohibition across both Houses is important for the coherence of the constitutional protection it provides to judicial independence at the State level.

The article reflects the broader constitutional architecture of Part VI, which mirrors the Union structure to ensure that state legislative institutions operate under a framework of comparable constitutional dignity and constraint to their parliamentary counterparts.
