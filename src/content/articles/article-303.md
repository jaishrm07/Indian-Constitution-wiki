  303. Restrictions on the legislative powers of the Union and of the States with regard to trade and commerce.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in article 302, neither Parliament nor the Legislature of a State shall have power to make any law giving, or authorising the giving of, any preference to one State over another, or making, or authorising the making of, any discrimination between one State and another, by virtue of any entry relating to trade and commerce in any of the Lists in the Seventh Schedule.

  (2) Nothing in clause (1) shall prevent Parliament from making any law giving, or authorising the giving of, any preference or making, or authorising the making of, any discrimination if it is declared by such law that it is necessary to do so for the purpose of dealing with a situation arising from scarcity of goods in any part of the territory of India.

## Why it matters

Article 303 is the equality-of-states guarantee embedded in Part XIII. While Articles 301 and 302
address the freedom of trade and Parliament's power to restrict it, Article 303 imposes a horizontal
constraint: neither Parliament nor any state legislature may use trade-and-commerce legislative
entries to favour one state over another or to discriminate between states. It ensures that the
common market created by Article 301 does not become an instrument of regional patronage or economic
rivalry between states.

The constitutional logic is federal neutrality. In a union of states, the national legislature might
be tempted — politically or fiscally — to route trade advantages to particular states. A state
legislature might frame its commercial regulations to disadvantage goods, traders, or enterprises
from other states. Article 303(1) forbids both forms of preference by operation of constitutional
law, not merely by political convention. The prohibition is categorical: the word "shall" means
there is no discretion, no balancing exercise, and no proportionality analysis available when
Article 303(1) is engaged. A law that gives a preference or creates a discrimination simply has no
constitutional place under a trade-and-commerce legislative entry.

The only constitutionally recognised exception is Article 303(2): Parliament may discriminate
between states if the discrimination is directed at a specific scarcity of goods in a particular
region and is declared as such in the law itself. This declaration requirement is substantive, not
merely formal — the law must genuinely address a supply crisis, not use scarcity as a pretext for
structural preference. Article 303(2) confers this exception only on Parliament, not on State
Legislatures. A state cannot justify inter-state discrimination in trade law on scarcity grounds;
only Parliament may do so, and only when the law contains an explicit declaration.

The reach of Article 303(1) is defined by its scope clause: "by virtue of any entry relating to
trade and commerce in any of the Lists in the Seventh Schedule." This means the prohibition applies
whenever a legislature purports to act under a trade-and-commerce entry (such as List I Entry 42,
inter-state trade and commerce; or List II entries on markets, money-lending, and trade in specific
goods). Laws that happen to affect trade but are enacted under other legislative entries — taxation,
public health, agriculture — are not directly within Article 303(1)'s prohibition, though they may
still be tested under Articles 301 and 304.

## Reading the provision

Article 303(1) contains two distinct prohibitions that operate in parallel. The first prohibition is
against "giving, or authorising the giving of, any preference to one State over another." Preference
means treating one state's trade, traders, or goods more favourably than those of another state. The
second prohibition is against "making, or authorising the making of, any discrimination between one
State and another." Discrimination is broader than preference: it covers any differential treatment,
whether or not the differentiating state benefits from it. A law that disadvantages goods from State
A relative to goods from State B discriminates between states even if it does not prefer any
particular state for any advantage.

The phrase "or authorising the giving of" and "or authorising the making of" extends the prohibition
to delegated legislative and executive action. A law that itself contains no preference but grants a
subordinate authority power to create preferences or discriminations is itself unconstitutional
under Article 303(1). This prevents Parliament or State Legislatures from achieving by delegation
what they cannot achieve directly. An executive order, a licensing scheme, or a procurement
guideline issued under a trade-and-commerce legislative entry falls within the prohibition if it
creates inter-state preferences or discriminations.

Article 303(2) creates the only permitted departure from the equality norm of Article 303(1). The
conditions are specific and cumulative. First, only Parliament may invoke the exception — State
Legislatures cannot. Second, the law must address a "situation arising from scarcity of goods in any
part of the territory of India." This is a factual condition: there must be an actual or imminent
shortage of specific goods. Third, the law must contain an explicit declaration that the preference
or discrimination is necessary to deal with that scarcity situation. The declaration is not a self-
validating formula; it is open to judicial review as to whether the stated scarcity conditions
actually exist and whether the discrimination is genuinely necessary to address them.

The interaction between Article 302 and Article 303 is textually precise. Article 303(1) opens with
"Notwithstanding anything in article 302," which means that Parliament's general power to impose
restrictions on trade freedom under Article 302 does not override the equality-of-states principle.
Even if Parliament can justify a restriction on trade as being in the public interest under Article
302, it cannot use that power to create inter-state preferences or discriminations unless it meets
the Article 303(2) scarcity exception.

## Key cases

**Tata Iron and Steel Co. Ltd. v. S.R. Sarkar (1960) SCR (3) 311** — The Supreme Court examined whether a central notification that effectively directed the movement and pricing of iron and steel in a manner beneficial to certain regional industries amounted to inter-state discrimination under Article 303. The decision clarified the distinction between a general regulation of trade (permissible) and a geographically targeted preference (prohibited under Article 303(1)). Importantly, the Court established that the prohibition applies to delegated executive action taken under a legislative instrument — not only to the primary legislation itself. An executive notification that creates a preference is as unconstitutional as a statutory preference.

**State of Madras v. N.K. Natarajan (1969) SCR (1) 258** — The Court addressed whether a state-level law that created preferential conditions for intra-state movement of goods relative to goods coming from another state constituted the kind of inter-state discrimination prohibited by Article 303. The decision reinforced that Article 303 is breached whenever trade law produces a differential outcome between state-of-origin goods and goods from other states, even if the law does not name any state explicitly. Facially neutral legislation that has a discriminatory effect on inter-state trade falls within the prohibition.

**Video Electronics Pvt. Ltd. v. State of Punjab (1990) Supp SCC 820** — The Court considered whether differential tax rates applied to electronic goods moving across state borders, justified ostensibly on quality control and consumer protection grounds, constituted discrimination between states under Article 303 read with Article 304. The decision confirmed that the category of the legislative entry under which a law is made determines whether Article 303 applies, and that courts will look to the substance and effect of a law rather than its formal description to determine whether it creates inter-state differentiation.

**Atiabari Tea Co. Ltd. v. State of Assam (1961) SCR (1) 809** — While primarily an Article 301 case, the Constitution Bench's analysis in Atiabari also addressed the relationship between Articles 301, 302, 303, and 304, confirming that the anti-discrimination principle of Article 303 is a freestanding constitutional command that cannot be overridden by Parliament's general restriction-making power under Article 302. The decision laid the ground for understanding Article 303 as part of the unified Part XIII framework rather than as an isolated prohibition.

**State of Gujarat v. Shantilal Mangaldas (1969) SCR (3) 341** — The Court considered whether State legislation operating under land and property entries (rather than trade-and-commerce entries) could be tested under Article 303. The decision confirmed the scope limitation in Article 303(1): the prohibition applies specifically to laws enacted "by virtue of any entry relating to trade and commerce." Laws passed under other Seventh Schedule entries, even if they incidentally affect trade, are not within Article 303(1)'s direct prohibition, though they may still be tested against Article 301 and Article 304's non-discrimination requirements.

## Current significance

Article 303's anti-discrimination command has a continuing role in two live contexts. First, in
state-level agricultural marketing regulation: several states maintain APMC (Agricultural Produce
Market Committee) frameworks that restrict the entry of produce from other states into local mandis,
or that impose differential fees on out-of-state produce. The 2020 Farm Laws, which were
subsequently repealed, aimed partly at removing exactly these Article 303-relevant barriers by
creating a nationwide market for agricultural trade. The repeal of those laws has left Article 303's
potential application to agricultural marketing fragmented and state-dependent.

Second, in procurement policy: where the central government directs procurement of goods
preferentially from particular states or regions — including through "Make in India" preferences,
government e-marketplace guidelines, and state-specific Public Distribution System procurement
mandates — Article 303 imposes a constitutional limit that is separate from, and potentially
stricter than, the general non-discrimination norms of Article 14. The constitutional question of
whether a "Buy Local" procurement policy constitutes a preference between states within the meaning
of Article 303(1) has not been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court.

With GST having removed many of the tax-discrimination vectors that Article 303 was historically
invoked against, the article's most practical remaining domain is non-tax regulatory discrimination
— licensing requirements, movement permits, differential quality standards, and procurement
preferences — which remains outside GST's scope. As central government initiatives increasingly seek
to create unified national markets for specific sectors (electricity, agriculture, healthcare,
education), the constitutional framework of Article 303 will continue to define the boundaries
within which differential treatment of states is permissible.
