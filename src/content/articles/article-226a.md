## Why it matters

Article 226A was part of the Forty-second Amendment's broader project of restructuring the relationship between Parliament, the executive, and the courts in ways that concentrated authority in the executive branch. By stripping High Courts of jurisdiction to examine whether Central laws violated the Constitution in writ proceedings, the provision effectively created a two-tier system in which the nearest court of constitutional review for most citizens — the High Court of their State — became unavailable for challenges to Union legislation.

The practical impact was severe. A citizen in Kerala whose rights were violated by a Central law could not get relief from the Kerala High Court under Article 226. They would have to approach the Supreme Court directly — a practical impossibility for most litigants given the cost, distance, and crowded Supreme Court docket. The restriction thus functioned less as a doctrine of constitutional adjudication and more as a barrier to access to justice, designed to reduce the number of Central laws that could be challenged and the number of challengers who could mount such challenges.

The provision illustrates how constitutional text can be weaponised against judicial independence. Article 226A did not openly say "the courts shall not protect rights against Central laws" — it used the procedural language of jurisdiction to achieve the same result, removing the mechanism through which most citizens would seek protection while formally leaving the underlying right intact.

## Reading the provision

The article is retained in the Constitution only as a square-bracket heading followed by the omission note. Renumbering surrounding articles would disturb cross-references in decades of judicial decisions and constitutional commentary, so the tombstone entry preserves the article number while signalling that no substantive provision exists. This technique — retaining the number while recording the omission — has been used for several other Emergency-era provisions as well.

The article's effective life was approximately 16 months — from the Forty-second Amendment's commencement to the Forty-third Amendment's commencement on 13 April 1978. It is therefore unlikely to have generated a settled body of doctrine, and no significant judgments under it are reported. Its constitutional importance is historical and structural rather than doctrinal.

## Key cases

There are no significant judgments applying Article 226A, given its brief effective life and the rapid political reversal that followed the Emergency. The relevant case law concerns the constitutional history and context rather than the article's application.

**Minerva Mills Ltd v. Union of India (1980)** — The Supreme Court's invalidation of parts of the Forty-second Amendment — including its attempts to immunise constitutional amendments from judicial review — is the most important judicial response to the Emergency-era amendments of which Article 226A was part. The Court held that the power to amend the Constitution cannot be used to damage or destroy its basic structure, including the High Courts' power of judicial review.

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)** — The Supreme Court's ruling in the election case preceding the Emergency, which struck down the retrospective validation of the Prime Minister's election by Parliament, was the immediate catalyst for the Emergency and the Forty-second Amendment. Understanding that constitutional confrontation illuminates why Article 226A was drafted — it was a direct response to judicial willingness to scrutinise Central legislation.

## Current significance

The history of Article 226A is a reference point in debates about judicial independence, the resilience of the High Courts' constitutional review power, and the dangers of constitutional amendments that concentrate power. Scholars cite the Forty-second Amendment's cluster of court-curbing provisions — including Article 226A, Article 228A, and changes to Article 368 — as cautionary examples when evaluating proposed constitutional changes that would restrict judicial review.

The Forty-third Amendment's clean reversal within two years, following a general election that removed the government responsible for the Emergency, reinforces the point that anti-judiciary amendments carry significant political costs. It also demonstrates that constitutional design can prove more resilient than it appears when threatened — the public and political reaction to the Emergency's judicial restrictions was strong enough to produce a comprehensive reversal within the normal electoral cycle.

The article serves today as a constitutional reminder that High Court review of Central legislation under Article 226 is not merely a judicial preference or a matter of statutory design — it is a constitutional function whose removal requires formal amendment, and whose removal carries the constitutional risk of being struck down as a violation of the basic structure.
