## Why it matters

Article 114 is the constitutional lock on public expenditure. The three-step sequence — financial statement (Article 112), demands for grants (Article 113), Appropriation Act (Article 114) — means that every rupee drawn from the Consolidated Fund has a parliamentary mandate behind it. The Appropriation Act is not a formality; it is the legal title under which central government expenditure is authorised. Any expenditure not covered by a valid Appropriation Act is constitutionally unauthorised, regardless of executive preferences.

The prohibition on amendments that change grant amounts or redirect funds (clause (2)) is the mirror image of the demands-for-grants stage — once Parliament has voted on amounts at the Article 113 stage, those amounts are fixed; the Appropriation Bill cannot be used as a vehicle for reopening them. This channelling of financial debate to the correct constitutional stage is a structural feature that keeps the budget process internally consistent.

## Reading the provision

Article 114 converts Parliament's voting decisions on demands for grants into a legally operative Appropriation Act. Once Lok Sabha has voted on demands for grants under Article 113, an Appropriation Bill must be introduced to authorise the actual withdrawal of funds — both for voted grants and for charged expenditure. Clause (2) prohibits any amendment to the Bill that would change grant amounts or redirect funds, and the presiding officer's ruling on admissibility is final. Clause (3) is the lock: no money may leave the Consolidated Fund except under appropriation made by a law passed in accordance with this article.

**The Appropriation Act as Money Bill:** An Appropriation Bill under Article 114 is a Money Bill under Article 110. Rajya Sabha's role is advisory only — it cannot block or modify the Appropriation Act. Any Rajya Sabha amendments are recommendations that Lok Sabha may accept or reject, and the Bill proceeds to Presidential assent if Rajya Sabha does not act within fourteen days.

**No rider legislation:** The strict constitutional purpose of an Appropriation Bill — to appropriate money out of the Consolidated Fund for the grants made and charged expenditure — means that policy provisions (legislative riders) inserted into Appropriation Bills face serious constitutional challenge. Clause (2)'s prohibition on amendments implies that the Bill's scope is limited to appropriation, and any attempt to enact new substantive law through an Appropriation Bill bypasses the normal legislative process under Article 107.

## Key cases

**In re: Under Article 143 — Delhi Laws Act (1951)** — The Supreme Court's early analysis of legislative competence touched on the principle that appropriation of public funds requires a law duly passed under Article 114, and that executive expenditure without appropriation is not merely irregular but constitutionally void.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1977)** — The Supreme Court's examination of inter-governmental fiscal relations confirmed that the Appropriation Act passed under Article 114 is a Money Bill for the purposes of the Parliament Acts procedure and that Rajya Sabha's role is advisory only — it cannot block or modify an Appropriation Act.

**Bhim Singh v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court addressed a challenge to the government's practice of including policy legislation within Appropriation Bills (a practice sometimes called "rider legislation"). The Court confirmed that clause (2) of Article 114 categorically prohibits amendments that vary or redirect grants, and by implication policy riders inserted without going through the Article 107 procedure raise questions of legislative competence.

## Current significance

The practice of passing omnibus Appropriation Bills — running to thousands of pages with hundreds of grants bundled together — means Parliament approves enormous expenditure with limited scrutiny of individual heads. The constitutional prohibition on amendments in clause (2) means opposition parties cannot use the Appropriation Bill debate to force targeted changes; their only remedy is a cut motion during the demands-for-grants stage under Article 113. In recent years, the Finance Ministry's use of "token cuts" accepted from opposition parties as a face-saving device (while keeping the substantive expenditure allocation unchanged) has drawn attention to the gap between the constitutional design of granular parliamentary financial control and the realities of modern budget procedure.
