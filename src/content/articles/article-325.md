## Why it matters

Before independence, separate communal electorates were a defining and divisive feature of colonial constitutional arrangements. The Government of India Acts of 1919 and 1935 created separate voter rolls and reserved seats through which Muslims, Sikhs, Anglo-Indians, and other recognised communities voted only for candidates from their own community. The Poona Pact of 1932 extended a version of this logic to the Depressed Classes, granting reserved constituencies and a separate primary election system. Dr B.R. Ambedkar, who had himself been a beneficiary of that arrangement and had negotiated it with Gandhi, came to the Constituent Assembly with a clear view: separate electorates had fuelled inter-communal competition, legitimised the premise that communities could only be represented by their own members, and had contributed to the polarisation preceding Partition. The single-roll guarantee of Article 325 was the constitutional burial of that system.

The practical stakes of Article 325 go beyond history. Any restriction on electoral roll inclusion that operates on the basis of religion, race, caste, or sex — whether explicit or operationally equivalent — violates this provision. This has concrete relevance for Aadhaar-electoral roll linkage debates, NRC-CAA interactions, and challenges to voter-roll deletions that fall disproportionately on minority communities. Courts and the Election Commission are required to administer the roll on the basis of citizenship alone, and any administrative practice that systematically disadvantages persons of a particular religion or caste in roll inclusion engages Article 325's prohibition.

The provision also interacts with the affirmative accommodation of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes through reserved constituencies. Article 330 and 332 reserve certain Lok Sabha and State Assembly seats for SC and ST candidates, but all voters — not just SC or ST voters — vote in those constituencies. Article 325 is what makes that design constitutional — there is still only one roll for the constituency, and no one is excluded or segregated on the basis of caste. Reserved constituencies are an overlay of candidate eligibility on a single integrated electorate, not a return to communal electorates in a different guise.

## Reading the provision

**"There shall be one general electoral roll for every territorial constituency":** The mandate is categorical — one roll, one constituency. There is no discretion to create parallel rolls for the same constituency. The word "general" contrasts with the "special" rolls of the colonial system. The constituency is defined territorially, not communally, and the roll follows the territory.

**"For election to either House of Parliament or to the House or either House of the Legislature of a State":** The guarantee extends to elections to all four categories of legislature — Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and State Legislative Councils. The breadth reflects the framers' intent that the single-roll principle must govern at every level of representative government, not just national elections.

**"No person shall be ineligible for inclusion in any such roll ... on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex or any of them":** "Ineligible for inclusion" covers the most direct application — no voter may be excluded from the roll because of their religious identity, caste, race, or sex. The word "only" is important: if a person is disqualified from the roll on a permissible ground under Article 326 (non-residence, unsoundness of mind, crime, or corrupt practice), the fact that they are also of a particular religion or caste does not make the disqualification unconstitutional — it is the religion or caste acting alone as a disqualifying criterion that is prohibited.

**"Or claim to be included in any special electoral roll":** This half of the prohibition is equally important. It bars not just state-imposed segregation but voluntary communal self-segregation in electoral rolls. Even if a community desired its own roll to preserve separate electoral identity, that claim cannot be accommodated. The Constitution chose integration over communal autonomy in the electoral sphere.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's rejection of separate electorates was not without dissent. Some members, including representatives of minority communities, argued that without separate electorates their communities would be permanently at the mercy of majority voters in mixed constituencies. Ambedkar's response — consistent with his Annihilation of Caste analysis — was that separate electorates deepened communal divisions rather than remedying them and that the real safeguard for minorities lay in constitutional rights, reserved seats within integrated constituencies, and the rule of law rather than in electoral segregation. The Assembly's decision was reinforced by the post-Partition political moment: the partition of the subcontinent had been partly rationalised by arguments about the impossibility of inter-communal coexistence, and separate electorates in independent India would have perpetuated exactly that logic.

Jawaharlal Nehru and other Congress leaders were also influenced by the experience of the 1946 elections, in which voting along communal lines in the separate-electorate system had produced the political basis for the Pakistan demand. The single-roll system was thus both a normative commitment to civic integration and a political rejection of the dynamics that had preceded Partition.

## Key cases

**Kuldip Nayar v Union of India (2006) 7 SCC 1** — A five-judge Constitution Bench, while deciding challenges to the open-ballot system for Rajya Sabha elections, affirmed that the entire Part XV framework — including Article 325 — reflects a unified democratic scheme in which the universality of the electoral roll is a constitutional guarantee and not merely a statutory arrangement. The Court treated Article 325's non-discrimination mandate as one of the foundational pillars of the constitutional design of elections that informs how Parliament may legislate under Articles 327 and 328.

**Anoop Baranwal v Union of India (2023) 6 SCC 161** — The five-judge Constitution Bench drew on Articles 324, 325, and 326 together to emphasise that the constitutional design of elections rests on equal, universal, and fair participation. The Court read Article 325's single-roll guarantee as one of the structural conditions for a legitimate election, concluding that the constitutional framework of elections demands institutional independence in its administration and that the appointment of Election Commissioners must be made through a process insulated from executive dominance.

**S Subramaniam Balaji v Government of Tamil Nadu (2013) 9 SCC 659** — The Supreme Court, in examining the constitutionality of political party promises of freebies as electoral offers, cited the Part XV framework including the Article 325 principle that electoral participation must be based on equal citizenship and not on communal or other group-based bargains that distort the integrity of the electoral process. The ruling reinforced that Part XV provisions set a constitutional baseline for how campaigns may be conducted.

**Lok Prahari v Union of India (2018) 4 SCC 699** — A Constitution Bench considered aspects of voter roll management and candidate disclosure requirements, reaffirming that the foundational commitment of Part XV — including the single integrated roll of Article 325 — must be read as creating affirmative obligations on Parliament and the Election Commission to maintain the integrity and inclusivity of the roll, not merely negative constraints against communal exclusion.

## Current significance

The most acute contemporary concern implicating Article 325 arises from the intersection of the National Register of Citizens process and the Citizenship Amendment Act, 2019. Critics argue that if CAA provides a religion-based fast track to citizenship for certain communities while NRC processes potentially exclude others, the operational effect could be to create a religion-based filter on voter eligibility — achieving through citizenship law what Article 325 prohibits directly in electoral law. The constitutional argument is that Article 325's prohibition on religion-based differentiation of voter rolls cannot be circumvented by using citizenship law as a prior filter to achieve the same communal disaggregation at the roll-compilation stage. This argument is live in the courts and has not been finally resolved.

The Aadhaar-electoral roll linkage programme raises related issues. If biometric-driven deletions from the voter roll fall disproportionately on particular religious, caste, or ethnic communities — whether through biometric failure rates, data mismatch patterns, or administrative oversight — the resulting roll could be effectively community-skewed in ways that violate Article 325's mandate of a single undifferentiated roll. The Supreme Court and Election Commission have both required that linkage programmes include procedural safeguards against arbitrary deletion, but whether those safeguards are adequate is a continuing debate.

Article 325 also remains relevant to ongoing debates about reservation in elections and the interaction between OBC reservation in local body elections and the single-roll principle. The Supreme Court's evolving jurisprudence on OBC reservation in panchayat and urban local body elections must remain consistent with Article 325's guarantee that participation in any electoral roll cannot be conditioned on caste identification.
