  Article 136 is the provision that transformed the Supreme Court from an apex civil and criminal court into something far more powerful — a constitutional authority that can, at its own discretion, reach into any case decided by any court or tribunal anywhere in India and call it up for examination. The words "may, in its discretion" are the most consequential phrase in the Indian appellate architecture. There is no prescribed condition, no mandatory ground, no required threshold of seriousness. This immense power has made the Supreme Court the most accessible apex court in the world — any litigant who can file a petition has, at least theoretically, access to the country's highest bench.   136. Special leave to appeal by the Supreme Court.—(1)

  Notwithstanding anything in this Chapter, the Supreme Court may, in its discretion, grant special leave to appeal from any judgment, decree, determination, sentence or order in any cause or matter passed or made by any court or tribunal in the territory of India.

  (2) Nothing in clause (1) shall apply to any judgment, determination, sentence or order passed or made by any court or tribunal constituted by or under any law relating to the Armed Forces.

## Why it matters

When the Constitution was being debated in the Constituent Assembly, the framers confronted a fundamental question about the Supreme Court's appellate jurisdiction: should it be defined by specific categories — civil matters, criminal matters, constitutional questions — leaving gaps for cases that fell between the categories, or should there be a residuary power broad enough to catch whatever those specific provisions missed? The drafters chose the latter course. Article 136 was conceived as the safety valve of the appellate system — the provision that would ensure the Supreme Court could always reach a case that deserved its attention, regardless of whether the case fit neatly into Articles 132, 133, or 134.

In practice, Article 136 has grown far beyond a safety valve. It has become the dominant gateway through which cases reach the Supreme Court. The special leave petition (SLP) is now the standard mechanism for seeking Supreme Court review of any adverse judgment — far more commonly used than appeals by right under Articles 132 to 134. This dominance of Article 136 over the more specific appellate provisions reflects both the provision's procedural convenience (no certificate required, no particular legal threshold to satisfy) and the Court's practice of granting leave with reasonable frequency in matters it considers important.

The stakes of Article 136 are diffuse but profound. Individually, a litigant who obtains leave and wins at the Supreme Court may recover property, avoid imprisonment, protect a reputation, or secure a business interest that would otherwise be lost. Collectively, the Court's exercise of Article 136 discretion shapes the entire legal landscape: it decides which legal questions receive authoritative resolution, which doctrines are developed, and which categories of dispute get the benefit of Supreme Court attention. The article is therefore both a procedural provision and a mechanism for the development of law at the national level.

## Reading the provision

**"Notwithstanding anything in this Chapter"**: Article 136 opens by declaring its independence from the preceding appellate provisions in Chapter IV of Part V. The specific appeal rights under Articles 132, 133, and 134 — which require particular grounds to be established — do not limit or exhaust the Supreme Court's power under Article 136. Even where an appeal does not meet the conditions of those specific articles, Article 136 remains available. And where an appeal would qualify under those articles, Article 136 can also be invoked as an alternative or supplementary route.

**"May, in its discretion"**: These four words carry the entire constitutional weight of the provision. The jurisdiction is permissive and discretionary — the Court may grant leave, but it is not compelled to do so even when all technical conditions are met. This distinguishes Article 136 sharply from the certificate-based appeal rights under Articles 132 to 134, where a right of appeal exists once the certificate is granted. Under Article 136, the Court at every stage — at the leave stage and even after leave is granted — retains discretion about how to proceed with the case. The Court can dismiss an SLP in limine without a hearing, can grant leave and then dismiss the appeal on the merits, or can grant leave and reverse or modify the judgment below.

**"Any judgment, decree, determination, sentence or order"**: The width of this formula is deliberate. It covers judgments (final decisions in civil proceedings), decrees (orders that finally determine the rights of the parties), determinations (findings by tribunals or administrative bodies), sentences (criminal outcomes), and orders (interlocutory and procedural decisions). Virtually every kind of adjudicatory output is within the scope of Article 136. Importantly, this includes interlocutory orders — orders made during the course of a proceeding rather than at its conclusion — though the Court is understandably more reluctant to grant leave from interlocutory orders as they do not finally determine the parties' rights.

**"Any court or tribunal"**: Article 136 is not limited to High Courts. It reaches subordinate courts, tribunals, regulatory bodies with adjudicatory functions, quasi-judicial authorities, and any other body that exercises a judicial or quasi-judicial function in the territory of India. This means the Supreme Court can potentially examine any judicial or quasi-judicial act anywhere in the country. Consumer forums, the National Green Tribunal, armed forces tribunal decisions on civil matters, company law tribunals, income tax appellate tribunals, election tribunals — all are potentially subject to Article 136 reach.

**The Armed Forces exclusion**: Clause (2) carves out one specific category: courts and tribunals constituted under laws relating to the Armed Forces. Courts martial, the Armed Forces Tribunal, and similar bodies are not subject to Article 136 jurisdiction. This exclusion reflects the traditional separation between civilian judicial review and military justice. Note that this exclusion covers Article 136 specifically; it does not prevent a person affected by a military tribunal's decision from seeking relief under Article 32 on fundamental rights grounds, though those too are practically constrained.

## The discretion — how it is exercised

The discretionary character of Article 136 requires the Court to develop its own principles for when to grant and when to refuse leave. Over seven decades of practice, several standards have emerged — none of them absolute, but all of them guiding.

The Court will ordinarily grant leave where the case raises a substantial question of law that is unsettled or where different High Courts have taken conflicting views. Doctrinal dissonance in the lower judiciary is a strong reason for the Supreme Court to intervene and settle the law under Article 136. The Court will also grant leave where there is a grave miscarriage of justice — where the lower court's decision, however technically correct in procedure, has produced an outcome so fundamentally wrong that allowing it to stand would undermine the basic requirements of justice. Cases involving serious violations of natural justice, manifest disregard for established legal principle, and perverse appreciation of evidence all fall in this category.

The Court will ordinarily decline leave where the judgment below is correct or at least defensible, where the case involves primarily factual dispute that has been carefully resolved by the courts below, and where the grant of leave would in effect turn the Supreme Court into a third court of fact. The Court has repeatedly stated that an SLP is not an invitation to a second look at the facts; it is reserved for legal questions of significance. But the Court has not always strictly observed this principle in practice.

## Article 136 and the other appellate articles — the relationship

Articles 132, 133, and 134 create structured rights of appeal for specific categories of cases when specified conditions are met. Article 136 is the residual provision. In theory, the structured rights of appeal should be used when they are available and Article 136 should be reserved for cases that fall outside them. In practice, because Article 136 is procedurally simpler and offers the petitioner the chance to make their case to the Court rather than to the High Court for a certificate, it has displaced the more structured routes in most day-to-day practice.

The consequences of this displacement are significant. A person who obtains a certificate under Article 133 has a right of appeal — the Supreme Court must hear the case on its merits. A person who files an SLP under Article 136 has no such right — the Court can and does dismiss SLPs in large numbers at the admission stage without any hearing on the merits. An SLP dismissal at the admission stage does not carry the constitutional authority of a decided case — it does not constitute a declaration of law under Article 141. An Article 133 appeal, once decided, does produce a binding legal declaration. This distinction has concrete consequences for litigants and for the development of precedent.

## The effect of SLP dismissal — the Kunhayammed question

One of the most practically important questions in Article 136 jurisprudence is what happens when an SLP is dismissed without granting leave — particularly when it is dismissed with only the words "dismissed" or "dismissed as not pressed" or "no merit." Does such a dismissal amount to an endorsement of the High Court's reasoning? Does it bind subsequent courts?

The Supreme Court in Kunhayammed v. State of Kerala (2000) settled this definitively. A dismissal of an SLP in limine — without issuing notice, without a hearing, without any expression of opinion on the merits — does not constitute a declaration of law under Article 141. It does not affirm the High Court's judgment on the merits. It does not operate as res judicata on the legal questions in the case. It means only that the Supreme Court chose, in the exercise of its discretion, not to examine the matter at that time and in that proceeding. Subsequent courts are not bound by such a dismissal as if it were a considered judgment. This ruling is foundational to understanding the precedential weight of SLP disposals.

## Key cases

**Pritam Singh v. The State (1950)** — One of the earliest authoritative statements of the principles governing Article 136 discretion. The Supreme Court held that the provision is to be exercised sparingly and in exceptional cases only; the Court will interfere only when there is a grave miscarriage of justice, a violation of principles of natural justice, or a serious departure from established procedure. These criteria — stated at the very beginning of the Constitution's operation — remain the baseline framework for SLP hearings, even as the Court's actual practice has sometimes been more liberal than the criteria suggest.

**Kunhayammed v. State of Kerala (2000)** — The definitive ruling on the precedential effect of in limine SLP dismissals. The Court held that a dismissal at the leave stage, without a reasoned opinion, does not operate as res judicata on the merits and does not bind subsequent courts as a declaration of law under Article 141. The SLP dismissal represents only the Court's decision not to exercise its discretion to grant leave — it says nothing about the correctness of the judgment below. This distinction is essential for understanding the limited legal significance of the enormous volume of SLP disposals the Court issues without reasoned judgments.

**N. Suriyakala v. A. Mohandoss (2007)** — The Court reiterated that Article 136 is not a regular appellate channel and that the mere existence of error in the judgment below — even a significant error of law — does not by itself warrant the exercise of Article 136 discretion. The provision is reserved for cases where justice demands the Court's intervention, and the Court should be guided by the nature of the legal question, the seriousness of the alleged injustice, and the systemic consequences of allowing the lower court's decision to stand uncorrected.

**Mathai @ Joby v. George (2016)** — A two-judge bench raised a formal concern about the Supreme Court's own practice under Article 136, observing that the Court had been exercising its SLP jurisdiction so liberally that it was effectively functioning as a third court of facts in many matters, particularly in civil and criminal appeals involving disputed factual findings. The judgment called for principled self-restraint — a return to the original understanding of Article 136 as an exceptional jurisdiction for extraordinary cases. The case opened a significant institutional debate about whether the Court can fulfil its constitutional role as a court of law if it continues to be overwhelmed by SLPs that are essentially fact-based disputes.

**Union Carbide Corporation v. Union of India (1991)** — The Bhopal gas disaster settlement case illustrated the extreme reach of Article 136 discretion. The Court, in reviewing a settlement negotiated by the Government of India with Union Carbide, used Article 136 to examine the fairness and constitutional adequacy of the settlement itself — going well beyond reviewing the formal legality of the High Court's order to examine whether the compensation arrangements were just. The case showed that Article 136 discretion, at its most expansive, can extend to reviewing the executive's conduct in litigation, not merely the judicial outcome below.

**Raj Deo Sharma v. The State of Bihar (1998)** — The Court addressed the practice of granting stays in criminal cases under Article 136 and imposed guidelines on when such stays should be granted. Because Article 136 can be used to stay the operation of criminal convictions and sentences — including detention orders — while an SLP is pending, the provision has practical significance for persons whose liberty is at stake pending the final resolution of their criminal appeal. The case illustrates how Article 136 procedural powers (interim relief) can be as consequential as the final appellate outcome.

**Krishnakumar v. State of Tamil Nadu (2016)** — The Court addressed the standards for granting SLPs in criminal matters, holding that the mere fact that the accused has been convicted and sentenced does not by itself warrant the grant of leave. The conviction must raise a genuine question of law or reveal a manifest error of justice. The case reinforced the principle that the criminal appellate jurisdiction under Article 136 is not a formula for delay in the execution of criminal judgments, and that courts must be vigilant against the SLP mechanism being used to prolong criminal proceedings without genuine legal justification.

## Current significance

The Article 136 SLP docket is the defining institutional challenge of the contemporary Supreme Court. As of recent years, the Court handles upwards of sixty thousand matters annually, the vast majority arriving as SLPs. The administrative demands of managing this docket — screening, categorising, listing, and disposing of SLP petitions — have fundamentally shaped the Court's structure, with bench composition, hearing times, and procedural practices all adapted to handle volume rather than to enable the deep, deliberative engagement that major constitutional questions require.

Multiple reform proposals have been advanced over the decades. The National Court of Appeal proposal — to create an intermediate appellate court that would handle the routine SLP work from various High Courts, leaving the Supreme Court free to focus on constitutional questions and questions of national importance — has been discussed by successive Law Commissions and in academic literature without producing legislation. The proposal faces political resistance because States resist a proposal that would route their appeals through an intermediate court rather than directly to the Supreme Court, and because the legal profession has developed practices and business models around direct Supreme Court access that create institutional inertia.

A more targeted reform proposal focuses specifically on Article 136 — either amending it to impose conditions on the grant of leave (borrowing from the "substantial question of law" framework of Article 133) or directing the Court to develop more rigorous internal criteria for admission. The Court itself has contributed to this discussion through judgments like Mathai v. George, where individual benches have called for greater restraint, but institutional change has not followed.

The digital age has added a new dimension to Article 136 jurisprudence. Petitions challenging digital surveillance, internet shutdowns, data protection orders, and platform regulation decisions have arrived as SLPs, requiring the Court to exercise its Article 136 discretion in matters where the legal frameworks are still developing and the implications of judicial decisions extend to millions of people simultaneously. The Court's ability to select the right cases for its attention — and to decline to intervene in cases that do not genuinely require its guidance — will determine whether Article 136 remains an instrument for the development of law or becomes a bottleneck that impedes both the Court and the legal system it was designed to oversee.
