  369. Temporary power to Parliament to make laws with respect to certain matters in the State List as if they were matters in the Concurrent List.—Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, Parliament shall, during a period of five years from the commencement of this Constitution, have power to make laws with respect to the following matters as if they were enumerated in the Concurrent List, namely:—

  (a) trade and commerce within a State in, and the production, supply and distribution of, cotton and woollen textiles, raw cotton (including ginned cotton and unginned cotton or kapas), cotton seed, paper (including newsprint), food-stuffs (including edible oilseeds and oil), cattle fodder (including oil-cakes and other concentrates), coal (including coke and derivatives of coal), iron, steel and mica;

  (b) offences against laws with respect to any of the matters mentioned in clause (a), jurisdiction and powers of all courts except the Supreme Court with respect to any of those matters, and fees in respect of any of those matters but not including fees taken in any court, but any law made by Parliament, which Parliament would not but for the provisions of this article have been competent to make, shall, to the extent of the incompetency, cease to have effect on the expiration of the said period, except as respects things done or omitted to be done before the expiration thereof. 1

## What this Article did

Article 369 was a transitional provision that temporarily expanded Parliament's legislative competence by treating specified State List subjects as if they were Concurrent List subjects for the first five years after the Constitution's commencement — that is, until 26 January 1955. The subjects covered were economically critical commodities and industries: cotton and woollen textiles, raw cotton (including ginned and unginned cotton), cotton seed, paper (including newsprint), foodstuffs (including edible oilseeds and oil), cattle fodder (including oil-cakes and concentrates), coal (including coke and coal derivatives), iron, steel, and mica. Parliament's power over intra-State trade and commerce in these commodities — ordinarily a State subject — was temporarily centralised to allow the national government to manage post-partition supply disruptions, industrial recovery, and the controlled economy of the early 1950s. Any law Parliament made under Article 369 that it would otherwise have lacked competence to make ceased to have effect at the end of the five-year period, except for acts done or omitted before expiry. The provision is now entirely spent: the five-year window closed on 26 January 1955.

## Reading the provision

The constitutional text of Article 369 operates through a simple but powerful device: for the specified five-year period, Parliament is to be treated as if the listed subjects appeared in List III (the Concurrent List) rather than List II (the State List). The phrase "as if they were enumerated in the Concurrent List" means that for this period Parliament could exercise concurrent legislative power over the listed subjects, and parliamentary legislation on those subjects would prevail over inconsistent State legislation under Article 254. The States' legislative power was not extinguished — States could still legislate on these subjects — but Parliament's legislation would override it in case of conflict.

Clause (b) extended the temporary concurrent power to ancillary matters: offences against laws on the listed subjects, the jurisdiction and powers of all courts except the Supreme Court in relation to those matters, and fees (excluding court fees). This companion power was essential to making clause (a) practically effective: the ability to legislate on price controls and distribution of coal was hollow without accompanying power to define offences and enforce them through a consistent jurisdictional framework.

The sunset mechanism was automatic and precise. Any law Parliament made under Article 369's expanded competence — that is, any law Parliament would not have been competent to make but for Article 369 — would "to the extent of the incompetency" cease to have effect upon the article's expiry, except for things already done or omitted before expiry. This means that the substantive provisions of such laws lapsed, but any prosecutions or actions already initiated under them could continue to finality. Parliament did not need to repeal these laws; they simply lost operative effect to the extent they depended on Article 369's temporary enlargement of competence.

The five-year period was a deliberate choice by the Constituent Assembly. It reflected the framers' expectation that the most acute phase of post-independence economic stabilisation would be complete by 1955 and that the permanent Concurrent List — which already included significant entry-level powers over trade and commerce — would be sufficient for Parliament's needs thereafter. The Essential Commodities Act 1955, enacted just as the Article 369 window was closing, placed Parliament's control over essential commodities on a permanent footing under the Concurrent List entries dealing with trade and commerce in foodstuffs and other items, ensuring continuity of regulatory control without Article 369.

## Historical context

At independence and in the early Republic, India's economy was still heavily regulated through wartime and post-war controls inherited from the colonial administration. The Defence of India Rules and Emergency Powers (Essential Goods) Ordinances that had governed commodity distribution during the Second World War had been extended under the Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act 1946, but this central legislation's constitutional basis under the new federal scheme was uncertain. The essential commodities on Article 369's list — textiles, food grains, edible oils, coal, and steel — were subject to price controls, rationing, and distribution schemes of national scope. The framers wanted to preserve Parliament's capacity to maintain these controls uniformly during the difficult economic transition, without permanently disturbing the federal structure by moving these subjects into the Concurrent List. Article 369 provided the necessary constitutional bridge while the permanent legislative architecture was designed and enacted.

The specific commodities on the list were chosen because they represented the critical supply-chain bottlenecks of early post-independence India. Cotton textiles were the largest organised industry; iron, steel, and coal were the foundation of planned industrialisation as envisaged in the early Five-Year Plans; foodstuffs and edible oils were essential to food security in a country that had experienced famine as recently as 1943; and mica was a major export commodity and industrial input. The article's commodity list reads as a catalogue of the Indian economy's most acute vulnerabilities in 1950.

## Key cases

**Harishankar Bagla v State of Madhya Pradesh AIR 1954 SC 465** — The Supreme Court upheld the validity of the Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act 1946 as continued by Parliament after the Constitution's commencement, on the basis that Parliament had competence over essential commodities during the transitional period. The Court's analysis of the overlap between Parliament's temporary Article 369 powers and its permanent Concurrent List powers over trade and commerce laid the groundwork for the Essential Commodities Act 1955.

**State of Bombay v FN Balsara AIR 1951 SC 318** — The Supreme Court considered the scope of parliamentary competence over regulated commodities in the early constitutional period. The case illustrates the kind of legislative overlaps between Union and State powers that the Article 369 window was designed to manage while permanent frameworks were established.

**Tika Ramji v State of UP AIR 1956 SC 676** — After the five-year window under Article 369 had closed, the Court addressed the validity of State legislation on sugarcane supply — a commodity not listed in Article 369 but analogous in regulatory complexity. The judgment drew on the framework established during the transitional period to assess the residual scope of State power over agricultural commodities, illustrating how the temporary Article 369 regime had shaped the constitutional understanding of the trade and commerce entries in the State and Concurrent Lists.

## The Essential Commodities Act 1955 as successor legislation

Parliament did not allow the Article 369 window to close in January 1955 without putting in place a permanent successor framework. The Essential Commodities Act 1955, enacted in the same year the Article 369 power expired, placed Parliament's control over essential commodities on a permanent basis under Entry 33 of the Concurrent List (production, supply and distribution of, and trade and commerce in, foodstuffs including edible oilseeds and oils) and Entry 34 (production, supply and distribution of, and trade and commerce in, cotton and woollen textiles). The Essential Commodities Act 1955 authorised the Central Government to declare any commodity as an "essential commodity" by notification and to regulate its production, storage, transport, distribution, and sale. This Act became one of the most important economic control statutes of the Indian Republic and was used extensively through the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s to manage food supplies, prevent hoarding, and control prices. It was significantly amended in 2020 to remove several categories of agricultural commodities from its ambit, as part of the 2020 farm law reforms — reforms that were subsequently repealed in 2021 following farmer protests, restoring the original position. The Essential Commodities Act thus represents the permanent institutionalisation of the central regulatory capacity that Article 369 had provided temporarily during the Constitution's earliest years.

## Companion transitional provisions in Part XXI

Article 369 was not the only transitional provision in Part XXI that expanded Parliament's competence during the early years. Article 370 (Jammu and Kashmir's special status, now substantially abrogated) was also part of the Part XXI transitional architecture, as were Articles 371 to 371J (special provisions for specific States). Article 372 preserved pre-constitutional laws. Article 374 provided for the Supreme Court's transitional jurisdiction. Articles 376–391 addressed the transitional position of civil servants, High Court judges, Governors, and members of public service commissions. Together, these provisions reflect a comprehensive transitional architecture: the Constitution was not a clean break establishing a brand-new system from zero, but a framework that had to absorb, manage, and phase out an entire pre-existing constitutional and administrative order while building the new one. Article 369 was the specifically economic element of this architecture — the mechanism for maintaining national economic controls during the initial stabilisation period. Its expiry in January 1955 was itself a constitutional milestone, marking the point at which the economic transition from colonial controlled economy to independent constitutional framework was considered sufficiently advanced that the permanent Seventh Schedule distribution could stand on its own.

## Current significance

Article 369 is a spent provision of historical rather than operational significance. Its relevance lies in what it reveals about the constitutional architecture of Indian federalism: the Seventh Schedule's distribution of legislative powers was understood from the outset as a framework that might need temporary central override during stabilisation, and the Constituent Assembly built such temporary provisions directly into the Constitution rather than relying on emergency powers to achieve the same result. Students of Indian federalism compare Article 369 with Articles 249 (Rajya Sabha resolution empowering Parliament to legislate on State List subjects in the national interest), 250 (Parliament's power over State subjects during a national emergency), and 252 (Parliament legislating for consenting States) to understand the full range of mechanisms by which Parliament can acquire or exercise power over State List subjects. Article 369 was distinctive in being a self-executing, time-limited grant requiring no triggering conditions — no emergency, no Rajya Sabha resolution, no State consent — beyond the simple passage of five years from the Constitution's commencement. This self-executing, self-terminating character made it a clean constitutional instrument, one that operated and then lapsed automatically, leaving the permanent federal allocation of the Seventh Schedule fully intact once the transitional period ended.
