## Why it matters

Article 165 is the State-level counterpart to Article 76, creating a parallel structure of constitutional law offices at each tier of India's federal system. The symmetry is deliberate — as the Union has its Attorney-General, each State has its Advocate-General, and the qualification, appointment, and tenure principles are structurally parallel. The Advocate-General is the principal legal officer and adviser of the State government, appearing in courts on the State's behalf, advising on legal matters of State governance, and — unlike most advocates — having the constitutional right under Article 177 to address the State Legislature.

The office matters for federal constitutional law for a specific reason: in major constitutional disputes before the Supreme Court — fiscal federalism cases, Article 131 suits between States, challenges to central legislation affecting State interests, and Governor's rule disputes — it is the Advocates-General who present the State's constitutional arguments at the highest level. The quality and independence of the Advocate-General's office therefore directly affects how States participate in constitutional development at the Supreme Court. When all States affected by a central law send their Advocates-General to a Constitution Bench, the federal dimension of the challenge is argued by officers with constitutional standing, not merely retained counsel.

The pleasure-doctrine tenure of the Advocate-General also raises important questions about institutional design. An officer who holds office at the government's pleasure and whose remuneration is set by the government faces structural incentives to align legal advice with the government's preferences. This is a general feature of the law-officer model, but it is particularly acute for the Advocate-General because the office advises the government on the constitutionality of its own actions and is the officer who must stand before courts and defend those actions. The tension between honest legal advice and political servility is built into the Article 165 framework.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Qualification:** The Advocate-General must be qualified to be appointed a High Court judge. Article 217(2) sets this standard: a citizen of India who has been a High Court judge for at least ten years, or an advocate of a High Court for at least ten years. This is a lower threshold than the Article 76 requirement for the Attorney-General (Supreme Court judge-equivalent), reflecting the different levels of the judicial hierarchy at which each officer primarily operates. However, the requirement of ten years' High Court advocacy experience ensures that the Advocate-General is a figure of professional standing and seniority, not a general political appointee.

**Clause (2) — Duties:** The Advocate-General's duties are defined in the same open-ended terms as the Attorney-General's in Article 76(2) — to give advice on legal matters referred by the Governor and to perform such other duties of a legal character as may be assigned. The reference to "functions conferred on him by or under this Constitution or any other law" expands the mandate to cover any role Parliament or the State Legislature may assign. Several States have enacted Law Officers Acts or State Government Pleaders Acts that specify detailed duties, privileges, and conditions of the Advocate-General's engagement, operating within the Article 165 framework.

**Clause (3) — Tenure and remuneration:** "During the pleasure of the Governor" means the Governor acts on the advice of the Council of Ministers on this matter. Since the appointment and removal of the Advocate-General is an executive act — not a matter on which the Governor exercises discretion — the Advocate-General's tenure effectively runs with the government that recommended the appointment. Remuneration is set by the Governor (on ministerial advice), not charged to the Consolidated Fund. This distinguishes the Advocate-General from independent constitutional officers like the CAG and the Chief Election Commissioner, whose financial independence is protected by the Consolidated Fund charge.

## Article 177 — the legislative right

Unlike the Attorney-General's parliamentary right under Article 88 (which extends to both Houses of Parliament and any joint sitting), the Advocate-General's legislative access is conferred by Article 177, which applies to State Legislatures. Article 177 provides that every Minister and the Advocate-General shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise take part in the proceedings of, the Legislative Assembly, and if a member of the Legislative Council, the Legislative Council also, and in any committee thereof — but without the right to vote. This is a significant constitutional right, distinguishing the Advocate-General from any privately retained counsel.

In practice, Advocates-General attend State Legislatures when bills are introduced on which they have advised, when legal questions arise during debate, or when the government needs its legal officer to clarify the government's legal position on a pending matter. The Advocate-General may also appear before legislative committees examining draft legislation. This legislative presence — to address the legislature, participate in committee work, and clarify legal positions — is a meaningful aspect of the office that goes beyond its purely judicial role.

## Contrast with the Attorney-General

The Advocate-General and Attorney-General are entirely separate offices — parallel constitutional creations with no hierarchical relationship. In Supreme Court proceedings involving both Union and State interests, the Attorney-General and the Advocates-General of affected States may appear on opposite sides, or may coordinate in defending constitutionally shared positions. Neither officer is subordinate to the other: the Advocate-General represents the State's legal interests independently, and the State's constitutional arguments can and do diverge from the Union's.

The differences between the two offices are also structural. The Attorney-General's right of audience under Article 76(3) expressly extends to "all courts in the territory of India" — a constitutionally explicit right. The Advocate-General's right of audience in courts is not expressly stated in Article 165; it flows from professional rules and State Law Officers Acts, with Supreme Court appearance rights depending on State-specific enrolment or convention. The Attorney-General has a constitutional right to address Parliament under Article 88; the Advocate-General's legislative right is limited by Article 177 to the State Legislature. These differences reflect the different constitutional footprints of Union and State law officers.

The qualification threshold also differs: Article 76 requires Supreme Court judge-equivalent qualification, while Article 165 requires High Court judge-equivalent qualification — a one-tier difference reflecting the levels of the judicial hierarchy at which each officer primarily operates. In practice, however, the most prominent Advocates-General of large States — Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, West Bengal — are senior advocates of the Supreme Court with qualifications that far exceed the Article 165 minimum.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — The Supreme Court held that the Governor acts on ministerial advice in all executive matters, including appointments. Since the Advocate-General's appointment is an executive act, tenure follows the pleasure of the elected ministry. The judgment confirmed that the Advocate-General is a law officer of the ministry in power, with tenure effectively tied to the government's continuation in office — the constitutional foundation for the established practice of Advocates-General resigning on a change of government.

**In re Kerala Education Bill (1958) SCR 995** — The Kerala Advocate-General participated in arguments before the Supreme Court on a Presidential reference under Article 143. The case illustrated the Advocate-General's right of audience before the Supreme Court in matters of State concern and the role of Advocates-General in major constitutional references affecting State interests.

**State of Haryana v. Haryana Sarpanch Association (2013)** — The Supreme Court observed that the State's litigation strategy, carried by the Advocate-General, must serve the public interest of the State and not merely the political interests of the ruling party. The judgment implicitly acknowledged the tension inherent in the pleasure-doctrine tenure — an officer who serves at governmental pleasure must yet discharge professional and constitutional obligations to the court.

**Union Carbide Corporation v. Union of India (1989) 3 SCC 38** — The Advocate-General of Madhya Pradesh's participation in the Bhopal settlement proceedings illustrated both the breadth of the Advocate-General's mandate under clause (2) and the limits imposed by the Court's oversight of settlements affecting public interest. The State's interest in the criminal proceedings — and the capacity of the State government to compromise them — were directly in issue, making the Advocate-General's role constitutionally central.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — In this foundational federalism case, Advocates-General of multiple States appeared to argue the constitutional limits on Presidential rule under Article 356. The coordinated appearance of Advocates-General from States whose governments had been dismissed illustrated the collective dimension of the Advocate-General's role in defending State constitutional interests before the Supreme Court in major federal disputes.

**State of Bihar v. Union of India (2022)** — The Bihar Advocate-General appeared in proceedings challenging the central government's reorganisation of the State cadre of IAS officers, illustrating how Advocates-General engage in contemporary federal disputes over administrative and fiscal powers. The case showed the Advocate-General operating in the frontier areas of cooperative federalism where constitutional text and political contest meet.

## Current significance

The Advocate-General's office has attracted increasing attention as States assert their rights in federal disputes over revenue sharing, legislative competence, and regulatory jurisdiction. Constitution Benches of the Supreme Court — in cases involving fiscal federalism, the Goods and Services Tax, State reservation policies, and Governor's powers — regularly feature Advocates-General of multiple States making sophisticated constitutional arguments. The quality of the Advocate-General's office directly affects the quality of federal constitutional argument at the Supreme Court, making the appointment decision one of genuine constitutional consequence.

Political transitions frequently trigger disputes about continuity of the Advocate-General after a change of government. The pleasure doctrine makes removal straightforward in principle, but the incoming government must make a fresh appointment, and until it does the outgoing Advocate-General continues in office. This interregnum has generated controversy in several States where the new government objects to the outgoing Advocate-General continuing to represent the State in pending matters.

The Law Commission of India and multiple State Bar Councils have recommended reforming the appointment process — introducing consultation with the State High Court, transparent criteria of seniority and competence, and fixed terms insulated from mid-term removal for political reasons. These proposals seek to preserve the Advocate-General's professional credibility while recognising that the role is inherently governmental. The tension between professional independence and political accountability — built into the pleasure-doctrine framework of Article 165(3) — remains one of the enduring institutional puzzles of the law-officer system, and its resolution will determine whether Advocates-General can give State governments the honest legal advice that the office's constitutional design contemplates.
