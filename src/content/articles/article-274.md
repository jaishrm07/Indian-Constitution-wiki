Prior recommendation of President required to Bills affecting taxation in which States are interested.—(1) No Bill or amendment which imposes or varies any tax or duty in which States are interested, or which varies the meaning of the expression "agricultural income" as defined for the purposes of the enactments relating to Indian income-tax, or which affects the principles on which under any of the foregoing provisions of this Chapter moneys are or may be distributable to States, or which imposes any such surcharge for the purposes of the Union as is mentioned in the foregoing provisions of this Chapter, shall be introduced or moved in either House of Parliament except on the recommendation of the President.

  (a) a tax or duty the whole or part of the net proceeds whereof are assigned to any State; or

## Why it matters

Article 274 is one of the Constitution's procedural safeguards for federalism — a device that operates not on the substance of what Parliament may legislate but on the process by which it can do so when States' fiscal interests are at stake. The Union holds superior legislative and executive capacity in tax matters: it levies most of the major taxes in the Indian system, it controls the legislative agenda in Parliament, and it sets budgetary priorities. Without Article 274, Parliament could introduce bills that vary tax-sharing formulas, shrink the divisible pool, redefine key terms like "agricultural income," or impose surcharges that bypass State entitlements — all in ordinary money Bills that States have no formal role in shaping, since the Rajya Sabha's role in money bills is limited.

Article 274's response to this structural asymmetry is to require that the President — acting on Cabinet advice — formally recommend any such legislation before it can be introduced or moved in either House. This is not a veto for States, and it does not give them a formal hearing in the process. What it does is require the executive to have consciously considered the fiscal impact on States and to have signalled approval before Parliament proceeds. A Bill enacted without the required recommendation is void for want of compliance with a constitutional condition precedent.

The provision also reflects the framers' understanding that the division of tax proceeds between the Union and States is not a matter for parliamentary discretion alone. Articles 268 to 273 create a carefully calibrated system in which each category of tax has a defined relationship with State revenues — assignment, sharing, devolution, or surcharge retention. Article 274 protects that system from being casually disrupted by ordinary legislation introduced without regard to its fiscal consequences for States.

The article's significance has grown with each major tax reform. When GST was designed and implemented through the 101st Constitutional Amendment and the attendant CGST, IGST, and UTGST Acts, every provision affecting State revenue shares had to pass through the Article 274 gate. This constitutional requirement was one reason the GST implementation required both constitutional amendment and extensive executive-level consultation — informal political process reinforcing the formal constitutional requirement.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) sets out four categories of Bills or amendments that require Presidential recommendation:

First, Bills that impose or vary any tax or duty "in which States are interested." Clause (2) gives this a precise technical meaning: taxes where net proceeds are wholly or partly assigned to States (direct assignment under Articles 268-269), and taxes whose net proceeds are the reference base for computing payments from the Consolidated Fund to States (devolution under Article 270, grants under Article 275). This is broad enough to cover income tax, corporation tax, customs, excise, and any GST-related legislation that affects the divisible pool or IGST apportionment.

Second, Bills that vary the meaning of "agricultural income" as defined for income-tax purposes. Agricultural income is outside Union income tax under Article 246 — it falls within State legislative competence (Entry 46, State List). The definition of agricultural income in the Income Tax Act draws the line between Union taxable income and State-taxable land revenue derivatives. Any attempt to narrow this definition expands the Union tax base at the expense of what States can tax; any widening reduces Union tax receipts and enlarges State fiscal space. Either direction requires Presidential recommendation, because changing this boundary is not merely a tax technicality but a structural reallocation of fiscal territory.

Third, Bills that affect the principles on which moneys are distributable to States under any of Articles 268 to 273. This covers Finance Commission implementation orders, amendments to devolution formulas prescribed by Parliament, and any change to the calculation methodology for net proceeds (which determines State shares). If Parliament wishes to change how the divisible pool is computed — for instance, by altering what "net proceeds" means, or by reclassifying a tax as a cess excluded from the pool — that falls within this category and requires Presidential recommendation.

Fourth, Bills that impose a surcharge for Union purposes of the kind mentioned in Articles 268 to 273 (specifically the surcharges under Article 271 that the Union retains entirely and that do not enter the divisible pool). Since surcharges shrink States' effective share of the tax base by generating revenue the Union need not distribute, Parliament cannot impose new surcharges without Presidential recommendation.

## Key cases

**State of Gujarat v. Ranjitsinh Brahmbhatta (1970)** — The Supreme Court held that the requirement of Presidential recommendation under Article 274 is a mandatory procedural condition and its absence renders the legislation void. The Court rejected the argument that the President's assent to the Act after its passage could retrospectively cure the defect of not having a recommendation before introduction. The constitutional requirement is pre-introduction, not post-enactment.

**State of Orissa v. Union of India** — The Court considered a challenge to Central excise amendments affecting the methodology for computing net proceeds under Article 270. The ruling confirmed that any Parliamentary legislation changing the computation methodology for net proceeds — and thereby altering State shares — requires Presidential recommendation under Article 274 before it can be introduced, not merely before it receives assent.

**Dakshina Bharat Jain Sabha v. State of Madras** — While involving the State List, the case illuminated how courts identify when a taxing provision crosses the threshold into territory "in which States are interested" under Article 274(2), focusing on whether the net proceeds of the levy serve as the reference point for State entitlements under any of Articles 268 to 275.

**Union of India v. Azadi Bachao Andolan (2003)** — In the context of tax treaty benefits and income characterisation, the Court observed that the line between Union-taxable income and agricultural income (exempt under Article 246 read with Entry 46 of the State List) is a constitutionally protected boundary. Parliamentary attempts to redefine income categories in ways that affect this boundary engage Article 274's protection for the agricultural income definition.

## Current significance

Article 274 has become more significant — and more frequently engaged — in the GST era, for three reasons. First, the GST legislative architecture is complex: CGST, IGST, UTGST Acts and their amendments directly affect taxes in which States are interested, meaning virtually every amendment to the GST Acts must navigate the Article 274 pre-condition. The annual Finance Acts that modify GST rates go through this process as a matter of course, but the requirement has added constitutional weight to what might otherwise be treated as routine fiscal legislation.

Second, the compensation cess under the GST (Compensation to States) Act 2017 was a tax imposed for a specific purpose — compensating States for revenue loss from GST transition — using net proceeds as the reference base for payment obligations. Any parliamentary modification to the compensation cess mechanism, including the decision in 2022 not to renew the guarantee period, engaged Article 274 questions. Several States argued in public legal commentary (though no definitive Supreme Court ruling resolved this) that extending or modifying the cess required Presidential recommendation precisely because the cess structure linked net proceeds to State entitlements.

Third, contemporary debates about bringing petroleum products and alcohol into GST, restructuring the IGST apportionment mechanism, or modifying the Finance Commission's divisible pool formula all implicate Article 274. Any such reform, requiring Parliamentary legislation, must pass through the Presidential recommendation gate. This gives Article 274 a continuing structural relevance as the constitutional mechanism ensuring that Parliament's fiscal legislation does not unilaterally reshape the Union-State financial relationship without at least formal executive acknowledgment of the impact on States. In a federal system where States have no role in the Lok Sabha's money bill process, Article 274 is one of the few express textual protections for State fiscal interests within the parliamentary process itself.
