  108. Joint sitting of both Houses in certain cases.—(1) If after a Bill has been passed by one House and transmitted to the other House—

  (a) the Bill is rejected by the other House; or

  (b) the Houses have finally disagreed as to the amendments to be made in the Bill; or

  (c) more than six months elapse from the date of the reception of the Bill by the other House without the Bill being passed by it, the President may, unless the Bill has elapsed by reason of a dissolution of the House of the People, notify to the Houses by message if they are sitting or by public notification if they are not sitting, his intention to summon them to meet in a joint sitting for the purpose of deliberating and voting on the Bill:

  Provided that nothing in this clause shall apply to a Money Bill.

  (2) In reckoning any such period of six months as is referred to in clause

  (1), no account shall be taken of any period during which the House referred to in sub-clause (c) of that clause is prorogued or adjourned for more than four consecutive days.

  (3) Where the President has under clause (1) notified his intention of summoning the Houses to meet in a joint sitting, neither House shall proceed further with the Bill, but the President may at any time after the date of his notification summon the Houses to meet in a joint sitting for the purpose specified in the notification and, if he does so, the Houses shall meet accordingly.

  (4) If at the joint sitting of the two Houses the Bill, with such amendments, if any, as are agreed to in joint sitting, is passed by a majority of the total number of members of both Houses present and voting, it shall be deemed for the purposes of this Constitution to have been passed by both Houses:

  Provided that at a joint sitting—

  (a) if the Bill, having been passed by one House, has not been passed by the other House with amendments and returned to the House in which it originated, no amendment shall be proposed to the Bill other than such amendments (if any) as are made necessary by the delay in the passage of the Bill;

  (b) if the Bill has been so passed and returned, only such amendments as aforesaid shall be proposed to the Bill and such other amendments as are relevant to the matters with respect to which the Houses have not agreed; and the decision of the person presiding as to the amendments which are admissible under this clause shall be final.

  (5) A joint sitting may be held under this article and a Bill passed thereat, notwithstanding that a dissolution of the House of the People has intervened since the President notified his intention to summon the Houses to meet therein.

## Why it matters

A bicameral Parliament is only meaningful if there is a mechanism to resolve genuine disagreement between the two Houses. Without Article 108, a determined Rajya Sabha majority could permanently block any Bill that Lok Sabha passed — even if the upper chamber's resistance bore no relationship to the popular mandate. The joint sitting is the constitutional answer to that problem: it gives deadlock a resolution path while still requiring a majority of both chambers' members voting together, rather than simply allowing the elected chamber to act unilaterally.

The provision also reflects the Constitution's calibrated view of Rajya Sabha's role. Rajya Sabha is designed to be a revising chamber — a forum in which states and experienced public figures can bring a deliberate second perspective to legislation. But the framers did not intend for it to be an equal House in all respects. By making the joint-sitting majority work in Lok Sabha's favour, the Constitution acknowledges that ultimately the House of the directly elected representatives must prevail when agreement cannot be reached. The joint-sitting mechanism is thus the constitutional expression of Lok Sabha's democratic primacy for ordinary legislation.

The political shadow of Article 108 is larger than its actual use. Because a government that commands a comfortable Lok Sabha majority can, in principle, always overcome Rajya Sabha opposition through a joint sitting, the threat of invoking it is itself a bargaining tool. Rajya Sabha's capacity to exact modifications to legislation — to insist on committee scrutiny, amendments, or delays — is real, but it operates in the knowledge that escalation is available to the government. This structural asymmetry shapes every legislative session in which the government lacks an upper-house majority.

## Reading the provision

**Three triggers — clauses (1)(a), (b), and (c):** A joint sitting may be convened when the receiving House (a) outright rejects the Bill; (b) passes the Bill with amendments the originating House will not accept; or (c) simply allows more than six months to pass without acting. The third trigger is the most practically significant. It prevents Rajya Sabha from pocket-vetoing a Bill by deferring it indefinitely. The six-month clock begins from the date of reception and pauses under clause (2) during any period when Rajya Sabha is prorogued or adjourned for more than four consecutive days — so the government cannot game the deadline by keeping Rajya Sabha continuously in recess.

**Money Bills excluded:** The proviso to clause (1) makes the joint-sitting mechanism entirely unavailable for Money Bills. Article 109 already gives Lok Sabha the decisive role for Money Bills — Rajya Sabha can make recommendations, but Lok Sabha is free to reject them and the Bill is deemed passed regardless. There is therefore neither need nor constitutional permission for a joint sitting on Money Bills.

**Notification and the freeze — clause (3):** Once the President notifies an intention to convene a joint sitting, both Houses are frozen — neither may proceed further with the Bill. The President may then convene the sitting at any time after the notification. This ensures that neither House can try to resolve the deadlock on its own terms after the joint-sitting path has been initiated.

**Voting at the joint sitting — clause (4):** The Bill is passed if a majority of the total number of members of both Houses present and voting agree. The majority is not a majority of each House separately but of the combined assembly. Since Lok Sabha can have up to 552 members (550 elected plus 2 nominated under Article 331) and Rajya Sabha up to 250 (238 elected plus 12 nominated under Article 80), the combined chamber has up to 802 members. In practice Lok Sabha's numerical dominance — roughly 2.2 times the size of Rajya Sabha — means that a government retaining its full Lok Sabha majority can pass a Bill at a joint sitting even if every Rajya Sabha member votes against.

**Amendments at the joint sitting:** The proviso to clause (4) strictly limits permissible amendments. If the Bill was not returned to the originating House with amendments, only amendments made necessary by delay may be proposed. If the Bill was returned with amendments, only those "delay" amendments and those directly related to the inter-House disagreement are permissible. The presiding officer's decision on admissibility is final, removing scope for extensive judicial review of individual amendment rulings.

**Presiding at the joint sitting:** The Speaker of Lok Sabha presides. This is not stated in Article 108 itself but flows from the Rules of Procedure for Joint Sittings made under Article 118. The Speaker's presiding role reinforces Lok Sabha's institutional primacy and means the procedural rulings at the joint sitting come from the elected chamber's presiding officer rather than the Chairman of Rajya Sabha.

**Survival after dissolution — clause (5):** Even if Lok Sabha is dissolved after the President has notified a joint sitting, the joint sitting may still be held and the Bill passed. This prevents the government from escaping the consequences of a joint-sitting notification by engineering its own dissolution — or, conversely, it means that an outgoing government cannot be deprived of the joint sitting it notified merely because an election intervenes.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The framers of the Constitution gave considerable thought to the relationship between the two Houses. The original Draft Constitution drew on the Government of India Act, 1935, which had its own deadlock-resolution provisions, and on comparative models including the Australian constitution's joint-sitting mechanism. Dr. B. R. Ambedkar and other members of the Drafting Committee emphasised that the joint-sitting route should be available only for genuine legislative disagreements and should not be so easy to invoke that it routinely overrode Rajya Sabha's deliberative function.

Members from larger States were particularly alert to the numerical implications. The joint sitting's simple majority rule means the outcome is determined by raw numbers, and States that are populous enough to elect large Lok Sabha delegations effectively dominate a joint sitting. Smaller States' representation in Rajya Sabha — which gives them disproportionate weight in the upper chamber — is diluted to near-insignificance at a joint sitting. This arithmetic was noted in Assembly debates and is one reason the joint-sitting mechanism is understood to be a last resort rather than a routine tool.

The six-month delay trigger was a compromise between those who wanted a shorter window (arguing that a Bill should not be held hostage for months) and those who wanted a longer one (arguing that Rajya Sabha needed adequate time for deliberate scrutiny). The current six months was considered a fair balance, with the exclusion of prorogation and long adjournments ensuring the clock reflects actual sitting time rather than calendar time.

## The only historical use — Dowry Prohibition Bill, 1961

The only joint sitting ever convened under Article 108 was in May 1961 to pass the Dowry Prohibition Bill. The Bill had been passed by Lok Sabha but Rajya Sabha refused to accept several Lok Sabha amendments. After notification by the President, the joint sitting was convened and presided over by the Speaker of Lok Sabha. The Bill was passed after brief debate, establishing that the constitutional machinery was workable.

The historic rarity of this event is itself constitutionally significant. In over seven decades of parliamentary democracy, only a single Bill has required a joint sitting — and even that sitting resolved a relatively contained amendment dispute rather than a fundamental inter-House conflict. The joint sitting has been discussed in several subsequent legislative standoffs — including during the debate on the Prevention of Terrorism Act and various financial Bills — but the government has always managed to find an alternative resolution, whether through negotiation, committee referral, or a fresh session.

A second occasion arose with the Banking Service Commission (Repeal) Bill during the Janata government of 1977–79. The President notified a joint sitting, but the government fell before the sitting could be convened and the Bill lapsed with the dissolution of Lok Sabha.

## Key cases

**Purushothaman Nambudiri v. State of Kerala (1962):** The Supreme Court, examining a State legislature's analogue to the joint-sitting mechanism, confirmed that constitutional passage requirements are mandatory conditions of valid legislation. The presiding officer or executive cannot unilaterally certify that inter-House disagreement has been resolved; the constitutional procedure must be followed in substance, not merely in form.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1964:** The Supreme Court's advisory opinion confirmed that the constitutional requirements for passage — including those that activate Article 108 — are mandatory. Courts may examine whether the conditions triggering a joint sitting were actually present, though the conduct of the sitting itself is protected from judicial review under Article 122.

**Kalpana Mehta v. Union of India (2018):** A Constitution Bench examined the limits of Article 122's protection for parliamentary proceedings and confirmed that courts can review whether the constitutional conditions for particular procedures, including Article 108 triggers, are satisfied — even if they cannot interrogate the internal conduct of proceedings once validly commenced. This distinction between pre-condition review and intra-proceedings review is important for understanding when courts will examine joint-sitting questions.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019):** A five-judge bench (later referred to seven judges) examined the boundaries of the Money Bill definition under Article 110, which directly affects the scope of Article 108. If a Bill is improperly certified as a Money Bill, the joint-sitting route is wrongly bypassed. The pending seven-judge bench reference will determine how strictly courts police the Money Bill certification that excludes legislation from Article 108's ambit.

## Current significance

Article 108 has been invoked only once in over seventy years, yet its shadow extends over every session in which the government lacks a Rajya Sabha majority. The threat of a joint sitting has been explicitly or implicitly deployed in negotiations over major legislation, inducing Rajya Sabha to accept modifications it might otherwise have resisted. Critics argue this undermines the deliberative and federal function that Rajya Sabha is meant to serve — because Lok Sabha's numerical dominance in a joint sitting means Rajya Sabha's considered objections can always be overridden if the government chooses to escalate. The question of whether the President's decision to call or not call a joint sitting is subject to judicial review, and whether a court can examine the bona fides of a government's decision to seek a joint sitting rather than negotiate, remains open.

The Money Bill route controversy under Articles 109 and 110 has made Article 108 less relevant than it might otherwise be. Governments facing an obstructive Rajya Sabha have increasingly preferred to classify legislation as Money Bills — bypassing Rajya Sabha's substantive participation altogether — rather than invoking the joint-sitting mechanism which still requires a majority of both Houses combined. The Aadhaar Act (2016), the Finance Acts that amended the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, and the Finance Act 2017 amendments that altered tribunal composition were all passed as Money Bills, avoiding both Article 108 and Rajya Sabha's independent legislative scrutiny. The pending judicial reference on the Money Bill definition will determine whether this legislative strategy has been constitutionally proper.

Constitutional reform proposals have periodically suggested restructuring the joint-sitting mechanism — for instance, requiring a two-thirds majority of the combined House at a joint sitting rather than a simple majority, or restricting its use to Bills that have undergone a joint Parliamentary committee examination. These proposals respond to the concern that the existing mechanism, with its automatic numerical advantage for Lok Sabha, provides too easy a path for any government with a comfortable lower-house majority to override the upper chamber. The counter-argument is that any government commanding a sustained majority in the directly elected chamber has democratic legitimacy sufficient to prevail in the event of a genuine deadlock. This normative debate is unresolved and will become sharper if future governments face extended periods of upper-house opposition that require them to choose between the joint-sitting and Money Bill routes.
