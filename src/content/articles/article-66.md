  66. Election of Vice-President.—(1) The Vice-President shall be elected by the [members of an electoral college consisting of the members of both Houses of Parliament] in accordance with the system of proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote and the voting at such election shall be by secret ballot.

  (2) The Vice-President shall not be a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any State, and if a member of either House of Parliament or of a House of the Legislature of any State be elected Vice-President, he shall be deemed to have vacated his seat in that House on the date on which he enters upon his office as Vice-President.

  (3) No person shall be eligible for election as Vice-President unless he—

  (a) is a citizen of India;

  (b) has completed the age of thirty-five years; and

  (c) is qualified for election as a member of the Council of States.

  (4) A person shall not be eligible for election as Vice-President if he holds any office of profit under the Government of India or the Government of any State or under any local or other authority subject to the control of any of the said Governments.

  Explanation.—For the purposes of this article, a person shall not be deemed to hold any office of profit by reason only that he is the President or Vice-President of the Union or the Governor of any State or is a Minister either for the Union or for any State.

## Why it matters

Article 66 deliberately constructs a smaller, Parliament-only electoral college for the Vice-Presidential election, in contrast to the larger Parliament-plus-State-Assemblies college for the presidential election. This asymmetry reflects the different constitutional weight of the two offices. The President is the head of the entire Union-State constitutional order and must carry a federal mandate that spans both the national and State-level democratic frameworks. The Vice-President, by contrast, is primarily the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha — a parliamentary officer whose core constitutional function is legislative presiding, not executive leadership. A Parliament-only electoral mandate is constitutionally appropriate for an office whose primary day-to-day role is serving Parliament.

The choice of Rajya Sabha qualification — rather than Lok Sabha qualification — as the legislative eligibility benchmark for Vice-Presidential candidates reflects a similar logic. The Vice-President will preside over the Rajya Sabha. Tying the qualification requirement to the Council of States connects the office-holder's eligibility to the institution they will lead. A candidate who meets the eligibility conditions for Rajya Sabha membership — which imports its own set of disqualifications under Article 102 and the Representation of the People Act — is constitutionally connected to the house over which they will preside.

The STV secret ballot mechanism in Article 66(1) performs the same function as in presidential elections: it prevents the mechanical transmission of bloc votes and creates space for individual legislators to exercise genuine judgment. Because the Vice-Presidential electoral college is Parliament alone — without the State Assembly component — the outcome is more directly reflective of the current parliamentary arithmetic than the presidential election. A ruling coalition with a strong parliamentary majority can elect its preferred Vice-Presidential candidate with relative ease. This is why Vice-Presidential elections in India have historically been less contested and less politically dramatic than presidential elections: the numbers are more predictable and the federal complication of State Assembly arithmetic is absent.

The automatic vacation of parliamentary seats under clause (2) follows the same principle as Article 59(1) for the President. An elected Vice-President who is a sitting MP or MLA cannot simultaneously hold that legislative seat and the constitutional office of Vice-President — the conflict between parliamentary membership and institutional impartiality as Rajya Sabha Chairman makes the incompatibility constitutionally mandatory. The vacation occurs by operation of law on the date of entering office, creating a by-election obligation and a gap in parliamentary arithmetic that may be politically significant when majorities are thin.

## Reading the provision

The 1974 amendment inserted the bracketed phrase "members of an electoral college consisting of the members of both Houses of Parliament" into Article 66(1), replacing an earlier formulation that referred simply to "members of both Houses of Parliament." The amendment clarified that the electoral college structure (as refined by the Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952) applies to Vice-Presidential elections, not merely an informal joint sitting. The electoral college framing brings the Vice-Presidential election within the full legal machinery of nomination scrutiny, returning officers, counting procedures, and dispute resolution under Article 71.

The qualification condition in clause (3)(c) — "qualified for election as a member of the Council of States" — imports by reference the relevant Rajya Sabha eligibility rules. Since the Rajya Sabha has no minimum-age requirement of its own beyond the general adult citizenship requirement (the Constitution specifies no Rajya Sabha age minimum explicitly, but age thirty is commonly cited through the Representation of the People Act requirements), the Article 66(3)(b) requirement of thirty-five years supersedes this in practice. The primary operative import of the Rajya Sabha qualification link is the disqualification set — anyone disqualified from Rajya Sabha membership under Article 102 is also ineligible for Vice-Presidential candidacy.

The Explanation to clause (4) — exempting named high constitutional offices from the office-of-profit bar — is worded identically to the Explanation in Article 58 for presidential candidates. This symmetry reflects the constitutional logic that the same category of serving dignitaries who are treated as not holding a disqualifying "office of profit" for presidential candidacy are similarly treated for Vice-Presidential candidacy.

## Key cases

**Gopal Narain v Returning Officer, Vice-Presidential Election (1969)** — The Supreme Court entertained a petition challenging the Vice-Presidential election under Article 71, affirming that all doubts and disputes about Vice-Presidential elections are within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Supreme Court. The Court confirmed that the procedural framework of Article 66 must be strictly followed, including the STV counting mechanism, and that procedural departures from the constitutional prescription are justiciable under Article 71.

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court's analysis treated the Vice-Presidential election under Article 66 as creating the same kind of constitutional office as the presidential election — a representative mandate that confers legitimacy on the office-holder but does not translate into independent political authority divorced from the constitutional scheme. The electoral mandate of Article 66 empowers the Vice-President to chair the Rajya Sabha with authority, but not to exercise independent executive or legislative powers beyond the constitutional prescription.

**V.V. Giri's Vice-Presidential resignation (1969)** — V.V. Giri's resignation from the Vice-Presidency to contest the 1969 presidential election created a Vice-Presidential vacancy that required an election under Article 68. The episode generated constitutional discussion about whether a Vice-President who resigns to contest the presidential election could, if unsuccessful, return to the Vice-Presidency — and whether Article 66's qualification conditions must be continuously met or are merely assessed at the candidacy stage. The consensus view holds that eligibility is assessed at the nomination stage and that a former Vice-President can stand again under the unlimited re-eligibility framework analogous to Article 57.

**Kuldip Nayar v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court's discussion of the Rajya Sabha election process — including the secret ballot requirement under Article 80 read with electoral law — is relevant to Article 66(1)'s parallel secret ballot requirement for Vice-Presidential elections. The Court's observations about the constitutional status of the secret ballot obligation confirm that it cannot be abrogated by statutory or administrative modification: it is a constitutional floor.

## Current significance

The Parliament-only electoral college for the Vice-Presidency means that the ruling party or coalition can, with a comfortable parliamentary majority, elect its preferred candidate with minimal concessions to opposition groups. This has made the Vice-Presidential election politically less suspenseful than the presidential election, where the State Assembly component can tip the balance. The elections of M. Venkaiah Naidu (2017) and Jagdeep Dhankhar (2022) both illustrated this pattern: the NDA's majority in both Houses of Parliament was sufficient without requiring the cooperation of State governments.

The qualification linkage to Rajya Sabha membership has occasionally been noted as a constitutional puzzle: a candidate who has never been a Rajya Sabha member but who meets the eligibility criteria for election to that House qualifies under Article 66(3)(c). Seniority, distinguished service, or familiarity with Rajya Sabha procedures are not constitutionally required, though they are invariably considered in the political selection process. This minimalist approach to qualifications is consistent with the framers' general philosophy of trusting the electoral college to exercise political judgment about candidate suitability within a minimal constitutional framework.

The Article 66 electoral mechanism also has implications for the Vice-President's perceived legitimacy as Rajya Sabha Chairman. Since the Chairman is elected by both Houses of Parliament and not by the Rajya Sabha alone, there is a structural argument that the Chairman's mandate derives from a broader constituency than just the Council of States. This broader mandate can be invoked to defend the Chairman's authority when individual Rajya Sabha members challenge procedural rulings — the Chair's authority flows not from the House's confidence but from the Constitution and the parliamentary electoral mandate.
