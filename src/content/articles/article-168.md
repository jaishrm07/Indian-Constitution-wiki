## Why it matters

Article 168 is the founding provision of every State's lawmaking machinery. Before any bill can be introduced, passed, or sent for gubernatorial assent, the constitutional architecture of the legislature must exist — and it is Article 168 that calls it into existence. The provision does three things simultaneously: it makes the Governor a constitutive member of the Legislature (not merely an external assenting authority), it differentiates between bicameral and unicameral states, and it names the chambers that subsequent articles then regulate in detail.

The inclusion of the Governor within the Legislature itself — rather than merely as an assenting authority outside it — has practical consequences that courts have repeatedly had to work through. When a Governor summons, prorogues, or withholds assent, the constitutional question is not simply whether the Governor acted lawfully as an executive but whether the Governor acted consistently with being a member of the legislative institution. This framing makes Articles 168–212 a coherent constitutional code for State legislatures in which the Governor plays a structural role at every stage.

The division between bicameral and unicameral states reflects a design choice inherited from pre-constitutional provincial arrangements and the Constituent Assembly's deliberations. Bicameralism was seen as appropriate for larger, more complex states where an upper house could provide a deliberative check on hasty legislation. Smaller states were left unicameral to avoid the paralysis and cost that a second chamber imposes on states with limited administrative capacity. The six bicameral states listed in clause (1)(a) are broadly the most populous, though the list has changed over time as states have used Article 169 to abolish or restore Councils.

The structural choice embedded in Article 168 also affects how constitutional emergencies under Article 356 operate at the State level. When a State Legislature is suspended, the legislative function passes to Parliament; when it is dissolved, different consequences follow for bicameral and unicameral states. The Council — being a permanent chamber that cannot be dissolved — continues to exist even after an Assembly is dissolved, creating a distinctive constitutional residuum in bicameral states that has been relevant in cases involving the Maharashtra and Arunachal Pradesh political crises.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — The Governor as a member of the Legislature**

Clause (1) specifies that the Legislature of every State "shall consist of the Governor" together with one or two Houses. The significance of this phrasing — placing the Governor within rather than outside the Legislature — is that it constitutionally integrates the assenting, summoning, and proroguing functions of the Governor into the legislative body itself. The Governor is not a separate check on the Legislature from outside; the Governor is one of its constituent elements. This matters when, for example, a Governor refuses to summon the Assembly or withholds assent without returning a bill — such actions are measured against the obligations of a constituent member of the Legislature, not merely those of a separate executive authority.

**Clause (1)(a) — Bicameral states**

The clause as it now reads lists Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh as having two Houses. This list has changed over time through the operation of Article 169. Madhya Pradesh once had a Council (abolished 1969); Tamil Nadu's Council was abolished in 1986 and Andhra Pradesh's was abolished in 2020 and restored in 2024. The bracketed notations in the official text reflect successive constitutional amendments that have updated the list as Councils were created or abolished by the process prescribed in Article 169.

**Clause (1)(b) and clause (2) — Nomenclature**

All states not in clause (1)(a) are unicameral. In bicameral states, the two chambers are the Legislative Council (upper house) and the Legislative Assembly (lower house). Unicameral states have only a Legislative Assembly. These names are constitutional designations, not merely conventional ones, and subsequent articles in Part VI differentiate rules that apply to each chamber by reference to these names.

## Key cases

**State of Himachal Pradesh v Umed Ram Sharma (1986)** — The Supreme Court noted that the Governor forms an integral part of the State Legislature under Article 168 and must therefore be involved in legislative processes in accordance with the constitutional scheme. The Court rejected a challenge that treated the Governor as external to the legislative body, confirming that the Governor's inclusion within the legislature is more than terminological.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 2002 (Gujarat Assembly dissolution)** — The Court considered whether the Governor, as a constituent of the Legislature under Article 168, could refuse to act on advice regarding legislative sessions. The judgment reinforced that the Governor's role within the Legislature is governed by the same principles of constitutional propriety that apply to executive action, and that gubernatorial action inconsistent with those principles is reviewable.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The five-judge Constitution Bench reviewed the Governor's actions in summoning a special session of the Arunachal Pradesh Legislature and held that the constitutional body constituted by Article 168 must be allowed to operate free from undue gubernatorial interference. The Court grounded its analysis in the principle that the Governor is a constituent member of the Legislature, not its external supervisor, and that this membership imports obligations of constitutional regularity.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Constitution Bench addressed the extraordinary events in Maharashtra involving rival claims to the Chief Ministership and the conduct of the State Legislature. The Court's analysis of the Governor's role in calling a floor test and the Speaker's conduct during anti-defection proceedings repeatedly returned to the foundational Article 168 architecture: the legislature is a constitutional institution of which the Governor is a part, and its proceedings must reflect that constitutional character.

## Current significance

The distinction between unicameral and bicameral legislatures in Article 168 is periodically renewed as a live political issue when states consider using Article 169 to create or abolish Councils. The Andhra Pradesh cycle — abolition in 2020 under one government, restoration in 2024 under another — illustrates that bicameralism is not a permanent structural commitment at the State level but a revisable constitutional choice within the Article 168 framework.

The Governor's integral membership of the Legislature under Article 168 has become particularly significant as Governors in several states have taken actions — prolonged delays in summoning sessions, directing the conduct of floor tests, withholding assent to bills for extended periods — that the Supreme Court has treated as raising questions of constitutional validity rather than mere political controversy. Each such case is decided against the background of Article 168's insistence that the Governor is a part of the Legislature, not its master.

Ongoing debates about cooperative federalism and Centre-State relations frequently involve the State Legislature as an institution. The constitutional design of Article 168 — which treats the State Legislature as a sovereign institution within its domain, complete with its own Governor as a constituent member — is the foundation on which arguments about State legislative autonomy rest, and it will continue to be cited as States assert their legislative independence against perceived central encroachment.
