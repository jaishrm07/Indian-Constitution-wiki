## What this Article did

Article 383 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that made provision for Governors of Provinces during the transitional period. It has been omitted.

The Constitution's Article 153 establishes a Governor for each State, appointed by the President. Before the first Governors were formally appointed under the new constitutional procedure, the persons who had been serving as Governors of Provinces under the Government of India Act, 1935 and the Indian Independence Act, 1947 framework continued in their roles. Article 383 gave constitutional sanction to this continuation, treating the existing Governors of Part A States as the Governors of the corresponding States under the new Constitution.

This was particularly important because the Governor plays a central constitutional role — summoning and proroguing the State Legislature, giving assent to Bills, acting as the constitutional head of the State executive, and exercising discretionary functions. Ensuring that each State had a legally valid Governor from the moment of commencement was constitutionally essential.

## Why it was omitted

Article 383 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, along with the cluster of transitional articles that had all served their purpose. By 1956, all Governors had been appointed under the normal constitutional procedure and the transitional arrangements were entirely spent.

## Historical context

At the time of commencement, the Governors of the former Provinces were persons who had been serving under the Dominion of India framework. Some were Indian politicians, others were senior civil servants. The first formally appointed constitutional Governors were in many cases the same individuals, given that continuity was desirable.

The role of Governor in India's Constitution was modelled on the constitutional head of a parliamentary government, with actual executive power vested in the Chief Minister and Council of Ministers. The transition from the more powerful provincial Governor of the Government of India Act, 1935 to the largely ceremonial constitutional Governor was managed partly through Article 383's continuity provision.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge to the validity of Governors during the transitional period is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 383 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in securing the constitutional continuity of State-level executive authority during the transition from the dominion to the republic framework in 1950.

The provision was part of a cluster — Articles 383, 384, 385, and 386 — that collectively validated State-level governance in both Part A (former Province) and Part B (former princely state) States. Without this cluster of provisions, the legal status of every State government action taken on 26 January 1950 and thereafter (until fresh elections and formal appointments under the new Constitution) could have been questioned. The framers of the Constitution clearly wished to avoid such uncertainty, and these provisions, though now omitted, achieved their purpose completely.

The Governors of Part A States in 1950 were a diverse group that included both senior politicians and civil servants. C Rajagopalachari (Madras), B G Kher (Bombay), K N Katju (Orissa), and others served as Governors of their respective States under the new constitutional framework validated by Article 383. Their constitutional status was essential for the functioning of the new constitutional order at the State level, and Article 383 ensured that status was unimpeachable from 26 January 1950.
