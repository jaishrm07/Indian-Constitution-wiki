## What this Article does

Article 332A is the State-level counterpart to Article 330A. Inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 — the Women's Reservation Act or Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam — it mandates one-third reservation of seats for women in the Legislative Assembly of every State. It was inserted simultaneously with Articles 330A and 334A as part of a single unified constitutional reform, ensuring that the women's reservation operates vertically across Parliament and all State Assemblies.

Clause (1) establishes the basic obligation: seats shall be reserved for women in every State Legislative Assembly. Clause (3) sets the quantum at approximately one-third of total directly elected seats, inclusive of the SC/ST sub-quota created by clause (2). Clause (2) carves out a nested reservation: of the seats already reserved for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes under Article 332(3), one-third shall be further reserved for women belonging to those communities. The layered structure mirrors the design of Article 330A.

## Reading the provision

The article operates as an exception to Article 170, which governs the composition of State Legislative Assemblies through direct election. The women's reservation under Article 332A will apply only to the Vidhan Sabha — the directly elected lower house — and not to the Vidhan Parishad (State Legislative Council) in bicameral States such as Maharashtra, Bihar, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh. Councils are partially elected by limited electorates (local bodies, graduates, teachers) and partially nominated by the Governor; the direct-election rationale for the reservation does not apply.

The SC/ST nested quota under clause (2) means that women from scheduled communities benefit from a double reservation layer. They first compete within SC/ST reserved constituencies (which exist under Article 332 proportional to the SC/ST population in each State), and the women's reservation then designates one-third of those SC/ST seats as specifically reserved for SC/ST women candidates.

## Federal dimension and Delhi

The 106th Amendment inserted a corresponding women's reservation for the Legislative Assembly of the National Capital Territory of Delhi directly into Article 239AA's framework. Delhi's Assembly is directly elected and follows a similar model to State Assemblies, so its inclusion alongside Article 332A ensures uniform application of the one-third reservation across the full set of directly elected sub-national legislatures.

The extension to all States simultaneously is a constitutionally significant choice: rather than leaving women's reservation at the State level to individual State legislatures to adopt, the 106th Amendment mandates it uniformly through the constitutional text. This contrasts with the position on OBC reservations, which are determined State-by-State.

## Deferred commencement

Like Article 330A, Article 332A does not come into operation immediately. Article 334A ties commencement to the publication of post-amendment census figures and a subsequent delimitation exercise. The 2021 census was not conducted on schedule. Both national and State women's reservations are therefore on hold pending census and delimitation, with practical commencement unlikely before approximately 2034.

## Completing the vertical architecture — local bodies comparison

Women's reservation in local self-government bodies was established earlier, by the Constitution (Seventy-third Amendment) Act, 1992 and the Constitution (Seventy-fourth Amendment) Act, 1992, which inserted Articles 243D and 243T respectively. Those provisions mandated not less than one-third reservation for women in Panchayats and urban local bodies, and many States have since extended this to 50 percent. Article 332A extends the same model to the State legislature tier, completing a vertical structure of constitutionally guaranteed women's representation from village panchayat to State Assembly to Parliament.

## Key cases

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — The Supreme Court's foundational ruling on reservations established the 50 percent ceiling on reservations in government employment, and distinguished political reservations from employment-based ones. Article 332A's electoral seat reservation draws on the constitutional distinction between the two: the 50 percent ceiling established in Indra Sawhney applies to Article 16 employment reservations, not to Article 332A's electoral-seat reservations, which are governed by a separate constitutional scheme.

**Visakha v State of Rajasthan (1997)** — While concerned with workplace harassment, the Supreme Court in this case read into Articles 14, 19, and 21 a substantive commitment to women's equal participation in public life. The 106th Amendment can be seen as the legislative fulfilment of that constitutional commitment at the representative-democracy level.

**State of Maharashtra v Indian Hotel and Restaurants Association (2013)** — The Supreme Court observed that positive discrimination for women through legislative action was constitutionally permissible even under general equality norms, reinforcing that Article 15(3)'s enabling clause for women-specific provisions supports reservation mechanisms at multiple institutional levels.

## Current significance

Article 332A, when operative, will transform the gender composition of every State Assembly in India. Currently, women's representation in State Assemblies ranges from under 5 percent in some States to around 14 percent in others, with no State approaching one-third through elections alone. The constitutional guarantee of a one-third floor represents a structural intervention of the same magnitude as the local body reservations of 1992, which successfully increased women's representation at the grassroots level within a few election cycles.

The unanswered questions — OBC sub-quota, precise rotation mechanism, interaction with Article 334's SC/ST sunset in 2030, and the deferred commencement — mean the full legal architecture of Article 332A remains incomplete. Parliament will need to enact implementing legislation specifying the rotation methodology before the first delimitation under the new regime.

State Delimitation Commissions and the central Delimitation Commission will face the complex task of designating which State constituencies are women-reserved, ensuring geographic distribution across the State, accounting for the nested SC/ST sub-quota, and building in the rotation mechanism so that reserved constituencies shift over successive elections. Litigation challenging delimitation decisions under Article 332A — particularly the selection of specific constituencies — is anticipated. The existing jurisprudence on delimitation under Articles 82 and 170, which treats delimitation orders as largely non-justiciable once passed, will govern the courts' approach to such challenges.
