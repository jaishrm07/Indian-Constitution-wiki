## What this Article originally did

In its original form, Article 314 protected the conditions of service of officers who held posts in the Indian Civil Service (ICS), the Indian Police Service (IPS), the Indian Audits and Accounts Service, and other services established by the Secretary of State for India before the Constitution's commencement. These officers had been recruited under the colonial framework and held contractual or statutory rights to particular pay scales, leave provisions, and pension entitlements negotiated with the Crown.

The article guaranteed that the new constitutional government of India — and State governments — could not vary the conditions of service of such officers to their disadvantage without their consent. It was a form of constitutional guarantee honouring the terms on which those officers had been recruited, ensuring that the transition from Dominion to Republic did not break the contractual relationship between government and its most senior servants.

## Why it was omitted

The Constitution (Twenty-eighth Amendment) Act, 1972 omitted Article 314 with effect from 29 August 1972. By that date, the great majority of officers who had been in ICS or comparable Secretary-of-State services at the Constitution's commencement in 1950 had either retired, died, or otherwise left government service. Those who remained had largely had their service conditions subsumed into the new framework of the Indian Administrative Service and other post-Independence all-India services. The continued presence of Article 314 in the Constitution had become unnecessary and potentially anomalous — a special constitutional guarantee for a dwindling group of officers whose contractual protections were by then a historical relic.

The 28th Amendment also omitted Article 312-A (inserted by the 28th Amendment itself as a replacement mechanism, then immediately spent) and made consequential changes to Part XIV to reflect the completion of the transitional period for ICS officers.

## Historical significance

Article 314 was one of a cluster of transitional provisions — alongside Articles 313, 373, and 376 — that managed the constitutional handover from colonial to democratic governance. Together they ensured that the constitutional revolution of 1950 did not destabilise the administrative apparatus by creating legal uncertainty about the rights of serving officers. The Constituent Assembly's willingness to give constitutional force to the service guarantees of ICS officers reflected both a pragmatic need for continuity of governance and a deliberate choice to honour inherited obligations rather than repudiate them.

V.P. Menon and Sardar Patel, who managed the administrative integration of the princely States, relied heavily on the continued service of ICS officers, and Article 314's protection was part of the package that made those officers willing to serve in the post-Independence administration.

## Key cases

**State of Assam v. Kuseswar Saikia (1960)** — While Article 314 was still in force, courts affirmed that the article's protection of existing officers was absolute and could not be circumvented by the executive re-characterising a service change as something other than a variation in conditions of service. The protection applied to any adverse change in pay, allowances, leave, or pension entitlements, not merely to formal alterations in the service rules.

**Union of India v. P.K. Roy (1968)** — The Supreme Court examined the scope of Article 314's protection for an ICS officer who argued that his absorption into the IAS on different terms constituted a constitutional violation. The Court held that absorption itself was permissible but that the new terms could not be less favourable than those guaranteed by Article 314 without the officer's consent.

## Current significance

Article 314 is omitted and has no operational effect. It is a constitutional fossil that serves as a historical marker of the transitional compact between the new Indian Republic and the officers of the colonial administration. Scholars of constitutional history and administrative law cite it as evidence of the Constitution's careful management of the colonial-to-democratic transition — a design choice that prioritised stable governance over symbolic break with the past. Its omission in 1972 marks the moment when that transitional generation of officers had effectively departed the stage.
