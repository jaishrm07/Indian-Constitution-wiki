  243ZL. Supersession and suspension of board and interim management.—(1) Notwithstanding anything contained in any law for the time being in force, no board shall be superseded or kept under suspension for a period exceeding six months:

  Provided that the board may be superseded or kept under suspension in a case—

  (i) of its persistent default; or (ii) of negligence in the performance of its duties; or (iii) the board has committed any act prejudicial to the interests of the co-operative society or its members; or (iv) there is stalemate in the constitution or functions of the board; or

  (v) the authority or body as provided by the Legislature of a State, by law, under clause (2) of article 243ZK, has failed to conduct elections in accordance with the provisions of the State Act:

  Provided further that the board of any such co-operative society shall not be superseded or kept under suspension where there is no Government shareholding or loan or financial assistance or any guarantee by the Government:

  Provided also that in case of a co-operative society carrying on the business of banking, the provisions of the Banking Regulation Act, 1949 shall also apply:

  Provided also that in case of a co-operative society, other than a multi-State co-operative society, carrying on the business of banking, the provisions of this clause shall have the effect as if for the words "six months", the words "one year" had been substituted.

  (2) In case of supersession of a board, the administrator appointed to manage the affairs of such co-operative society shall arrange for conduct of elections within the period specified in clause (1) and handover the management to the elected board.

  (3) The Legislature of a State may, by law, make provisions for the conditions of service of the administrator.

## Why it matters

Arbitrary supersession of co-operative society boards by State governments was one of the most politically entrenched governance abuses in the co-operative sector before the 97th Amendment. State governments used the supersession power under co-operative societies acts — theoretically available on grounds of mismanagement — to displace opposition-controlled or independently-minded co-operative boards and install politically aligned administrators who could manage the society's resources, credit allocation, and procurement decisions to partisan advantage. Some co-operatives remained under administrator-managed "interim management" for years or even decades without fresh elections.

Article 243ZL imposes a constitutional ceiling on this practice. The six-month maximum cap on supersession or suspension is the central constraint: an administrator cannot govern a co-operative society indefinitely. The administrator's primary mandate under clause (2) is not to manage the society as a going concern but to conduct elections and restore democratic management within the six-month window. This reorients the logic of supersession from an instrument of political control to a genuine emergency governance mechanism with a hard expiry.

## Grounds for supersession — the enumerated list

The permissible grounds for supersession are exhaustively enumerated in the provisos to clause (1). A board may only be superseded for persistent default, negligence, acts prejudicial to the society's interests, constitutional stalemate, or failure by the designated electoral authority to conduct elections. This closed list prevents States from inventing new grounds for supersession by executive order or by creative statutory interpretation. The enumeration creates a judicially reviewable checklist: a supersession order that does not fit within one of the specified grounds can be challenged as unconstitutional, and courts applying Part IXB have accepted this approach.

## The no-government-exposure immunity

The second proviso — prohibiting supersession of boards where there is no Government shareholding, loan, financial assistance, or guarantee — reflects an important policy judgment. Government's justification for intervening in the governance of a co-operative society is typically framed in terms of protecting public money. Where there is no public money involved, that justification evaporates: the society is genuinely a private, member-owned institution, and the State has no legitimate governance rationale for superseding its elected board. The immunity is therefore a structural protection for purely private co-operatives — housing societies, consumer co-operatives, small credit societies with no State participation — from the kind of arbitrary State intervention that Article 243ZL was designed to prevent.

## Banking co-operatives and the Banking Regulation Act

Co-operative banks occupy a distinctive position. The Banking Regulation Act, 1949 applies to co-operative banks (including urban co-operative banks and State co-operative banks), which means that the Reserve Bank of India's regulatory authority over banking institutions co-exists with the State government's co-operative societies authority. Article 243ZL acknowledges this by requiring that for banking co-operatives, the Banking Regulation Act's provisions also apply. The extended one-year period for non-multi-State banking co-operatives reflects the greater complexity and regulatory sensitivity of banking institutions, where a sudden management transition could affect depositors and the stability of the institution.

## Post-Rajendra N. Shah position

After the 2021 ruling, Article 243ZL remains fully operative for multi-State co-operative societies. For State co-operatives, States are not constitutionally compelled to observe the six-month cap or the enumerated grounds — they may legislate as they see fit for their own co-operative sectors. The constitutional framework nonetheless provides a normative model that courts have cited even in State co-operative contexts, and many States have enacted statutory supersession limitations broadly consistent with Part IXB standards. The provision remains an important reference point in litigation about the legality of specific supersession orders, even where the constitutional mandate does not directly bind the State co-operative in question.
