## Why it matters

The Speaker is the constitutional officer through whom the Lok Sabha exercises its most consequential
self-regulatory powers. Without a Speaker, the House cannot manage its proceedings, certify
legislation, or adjudicate disqualification petitions. Article 93 creates the constitutional
obligation to fill the Speakership and, unusually, also the Deputy Speakership — both "as soon as
may be." The repeated failure of recent Parliaments to elect a Deputy Speaker has tested whether
this constitutional obligation is enforceable, and has left the Article 95 succession chain exposed.
More fundamentally, the Speaker's dual role — as a partisan member elected on a party platform and
as the quasi-judicial adjudicator of anti-defection cases — is the central structural tension in
contemporary Indian parliamentary law.

The Speakership is constitutionally unusual in a specific way: the person who holds the office is
elected from among members who belong to political parties, almost always from the ruling party or
coalition, and yet is expected to function with the impartiality of a judge when presiding over the
House. The British convention — under which a Speaker resigns their party membership upon election
and their constituency goes uncontested — has never been adopted in India. Indian Speakers remain
party members, contest elections on party platforms, and are effectively nominated for the office
by the Prime Minister. The resulting structural tension between the partisan origins of the office
and its constitutional requirement of impartiality has been a persistent feature of commentary on
Indian parliamentary governance.

The Deputy Speakership has a specific constitutional importance beyond mere succession planning.
By convention, the Deputy Speaker has customarily been drawn from the principal opposition party,
serving as a constitutional acknowledgment that the House's presiding function must not be entirely
in the hands of the ruling majority. The abandonment of this convention since 2019 — when no Deputy
Speaker was elected for the entire duration of the 17th Lok Sabha — has been characterised by
constitutional scholars as a significant departure from parliamentary practice, though the courts
have been reluctant to treat the convention as enforceable.

The Article 93 obligation also has a practical dimension for parliamentary continuity. The Speaker
chairs joint sittings under Article 108, certifies Money Bills under Article 110, decides questions
of disqualification under the Tenth Schedule, and presides over the House's most consequential
procedural moments. The Deputy Speaker takes over when the Speaker is absent. The vacancy of either
office is therefore not merely a procedural inconvenience but a gap in the constitutional machinery
that sustains the Lok Sabha's institutional integrity.

## Reading the provision

The text is spare but constitutionally operative. Both the Speaker and Deputy Speaker must be chosen
from among the members of the House — neither office can be held by someone who is not a sitting
Lok Sabha member, in contrast to the Rajya Sabha where the Chairman (Vice-President) is not a
member. The obligation to fill both offices "as soon as may be" after the House is constituted, and
to fill any vacancy whenever it arises, reflects the constitutional necessity of continuous
presiding authority.

The phrase "as soon as may be" has been characterised as a constitutional obligation rather than a
mere aspiration, though the courts have not yet resolved whether it can be judicially enforced by
directing the House to hold an election within a defined period. The 17th Lok Sabha (2019-2024)
completed its full five-year term without ever electing a Deputy Speaker, leaving the constitutional
directive unmet for the longest continuous period in the Lok Sabha's history. A petition seeking
a writ of mandamus to direct the election of a Deputy Speaker was filed before the Supreme Court,
which heard preliminary arguments but did not ultimately issue directions — leaving the
constitutional status of the obligation unresolved.

The method of election is not specified in Article 93 itself — it is a simple majority of members
present and voting, governed by the Rules of Procedure of the Lok Sabha. There is no requirement
that the Deputy Speaker come from the opposition, though the convention to that effect operated for
most of independent India's history. The silence of Article 93 on the political composition of the
presiding offices reflects the framers' assumption that parliamentary norms, rather than
constitutional text, would govern how the offices were filled in practice.

The removal procedure for the Speaker is set out in Article 94, which requires 14 days' notice of
a resolution for removal and an effective majority of the total membership. This supermajority
requirement — combined with the notice period that allows the Speaker to rally support — makes
removal extremely difficult in practice. No Speaker of the Lok Sabha has ever been removed by
resolution. The high threshold reflects the constitutional need for stability in the presiding
office, but it has also meant that Speakers who exercise their powers in ways perceived as
partisan have faced no practical check other than political pressure.

## The Speaker's constitutional powers

The Speaker exercises powers that span the ordinary and the exceptional. Ordinary powers include
recognising members to speak, maintaining order, certifying documents, and ruling on points of
order. Exceptional powers include certifying a Bill as a Money Bill under Article 110 — a
certification that controls the Rajya Sabha's ability to amend or delay that Bill — and deciding
anti-defection petitions under the Tenth Schedule, which can result in a member losing their seat.

The Tenth Schedule anti-defection power is the most consequential and most contested power the
Speaker holds. When a member is alleged to have voluntarily given up membership of their political
party or voted against party directions without permission, any other member may file a petition
before the Speaker. The Speaker then adjudicates — effectively acting as a tribunal deciding
whether a sitting member shall retain their seat. This quasi-judicial function is exercised by a
person who is simultaneously a member of the political party that dominates the House, creating an
inherent conflict that the constitutional design does not resolve. The Supreme Court has responded
by developing a body of law on the Speaker's exercise of Tenth Schedule powers, requiring
procedural fairness, timeliness, and — following Nabam Rebia — imposing limitations when the
Speaker themselves faces a removal notice.

The Money Bill certification power under Article 110 is the Speaker's most significant legislative
power. A Bill certified as a Money Bill by the Speaker cannot be rejected by the Rajya Sabha — the
Rajya Sabha may only make recommendations, which the Lok Sabha may accept or reject. In recent
years, this power has been exercised to certify legislation that critics argue expands beyond the
traditional scope of financial legislation — including the Aadhaar Act 2016, the Finance Act 2017,
and others. The Supreme Court in Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd (2019) referred the
question of judicial review of Money Bill certification to a seven-judge Constitution Bench, a
reference that remains pending. Until it is resolved, the Speaker's certification remains subject
to challenge but not definitively reviewable.

## Key cases

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** — The Constitution Bench upheld the validity of the Tenth
Schedule while striking down paragraph 7, which had purported to exclude judicial review of the
Speaker's disqualification orders. The Court held that the Speaker, when adjudicating disqualification
petitions under the Tenth Schedule, acts as a tribunal exercising judicial functions and is subject
to the supervisory jurisdiction of the High Courts and the Supreme Court. This established the
foundational principle that the Speaker's Tenth Schedule decisions are not absolutely protected from
judicial scrutiny, while recognising a degree of deference to the Speaker's procedural rulings.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The five-judge Constitution Bench
held that a Speaker who is themselves the subject of a removal notice submitted by a requisite
number of members cannot adjudicate anti-defection petitions until the removal notice is disposed
of. The judgment fundamentally reshaped the understanding of the Speaker's office: it held that a
Speaker whose own position is under challenge cannot exercise quasi-judicial power over the members
who may be voting on that removal. The ruling also addressed the power to summon and prorogue
sessions, holding that these acts must be on ministerial advice. Nabam Rebia is now the leading case
on the limits of the Speaker's authority under Article 93 and the Tenth Schedule.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Constitution Bench held that the Lok Sabha's
power to expel members is subject to judicial review. The Court rejected the argument that Article
122, which bars courts from inquiring into parliamentary proceedings, gives the Speaker and the
House absolute immunity from judicial oversight. The distinction drawn in Raja Ram Pal is between
procedural irregularities (which Article 122 protects from judicial scrutiny) and constitutional
violations (which courts can address). The judgment significantly expanded the scope of judicial
oversight over decisions made by the Article 93 office and the House as a whole.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — The Supreme Court
held that anti-defection petitions must be decided within a reasonable time and that a presiding
officer's inaction is judicially reviewable. The Court's direction that the Speaker decide pending
petitions within four weeks applied equal urgency logic to the Lok Sabha Speaker. The judgment made
clear that the Speaker cannot indefinitely delay anti-defection decisions — a tactic that had been
used to shelter defecting members while their continuation in the House served the ruling party's
political interests.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd (2019)** — The Supreme Court referred to a seven-judge bench
the question of whether the Speaker's certification of a Bill as a Money Bill under Article 110 is
subject to judicial review, and if so, on what grounds. While not yet decided on merits, the
reference itself is significant: it establishes that the Speaker's Money Bill certification is not
beyond judicial scrutiny under Article 122, at least when the challenge goes to the substantive
validity of the certification rather than to the internal procedure by which it was made.

## Current significance

The Speakership has become a battleground for three structural issues. First, the anti-defection
adjudication power — widely criticised as inappropriate for a partisan presiding officer — has
generated repeated calls for transfer to an independent tribunal or an Election Commission function.
The Law Commission's 255th Report (2015) recommended that anti-defection cases be decided by the
Election Commission rather than the Speaker, a proposal that has not been implemented. Second, the
convention of allocating the Deputy Speakership to the principal opposition party has been abandoned
in recent Parliaments, with the ruling coalition retaining both offices, intensifying the perception
of partisan control over the House's presiding function.

The 17th Lok Sabha's failure to elect a Deputy Speaker set a constitutional precedent that is
formally problematic, even if not ultimately enforced by the courts. The absence of a Deputy Speaker
for a full five-year term means that Article 95's succession provision was effectively dormant for
the entire duration of that Parliament. When the Speaker was absent or unwell, the House presided
under pro-tem arrangements of Rule 8 of the Rules of Procedure rather than under the constitutionally
established office of Deputy Speaker. The constitutional anomaly was noted by multiple constitutional
scholars and in a Supreme Court petition, but the political consensus in favour of retaining both
presiding offices in ruling party hands proved stronger than the constitutional convention.

The Speaker's Money Bill certification power under Article 110 has been challenged as having been
used to bypass Rajya Sabha scrutiny by labelling legislation as Money Bills, with the Supreme Court
in Rojer Mathew referring the question to a larger bench — a reference still pending. Until the
seven-judge bench resolves the question, the Speaker's certification practice occupies a
constitutionally uncertain space: challenged but not definitively reviewed, exercised but with
acknowledged legitimacy questions. The resolution of that reference will determine whether Article
93's grant of the Money Bill certification power to the Speaker carries with it a constitutional
duty of good-faith exercise that courts can enforce.
