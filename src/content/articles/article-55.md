officialText: |-
  55. Manner of election of President.—(1) As far as practicable, there shall be uniformity in the scale of representation of the different States at the election of the President.

  (2) For the purpose of securing such uniformity among the States inter se as well as parity between the States as a whole and the Union, the number of votes which each elected member of Parliament and of the Legislative Assembly of each State is entitled to cast at such election shall be determined in the following manner:—

  (a) every elected member of the Legislative Assembly of a State shall have as many votes as there are multiples of one thousand in the quotient obtained by dividing the population of the State by the total number of the elected members of the Assembly;

  (b) if, after taking the said multiples of one thousand, the remainder is not less than five hundred, then the vote of each member referred to in sub-clause (a) shall be further increased by one;

  (c) each elected member of either House of Parliament shall have such number of votes as may be obtained by dividing the total number of votes assigned to the members of the Legislative Assemblies of the States under sub-clauses (a) and (b) by the total number of the elected members of both Houses of Parliament, fractions exceeding one-half being counted as one and other fractions being disregarded.

  (3) The election of the President shall be held in accordance with the system of proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote and the voting at such election shall be by secret ballot. [Explanation.—In this article, the expression "population" means the population as ascertained at the last preceding census of which the relevant figures have been published:

## Why it matters

Article 55 operationalises the electoral college created by Article 54 by addressing two separate problems: how to ensure that MLAs from different States carry roughly comparable democratic weight, and how to ensure that the State Assembly component of the electoral college is not swamped by the Parliamentary component or vice versa. The vote-weightage formula is the Constitution's mathematical answer to both questions. Without it, a large State with a small Assembly would have its MLAs casting enormously powerful votes, while a smaller State with a proportionally large Assembly would have its MLAs counting for very little. The formula corrects for this by calibrating each MLA's vote value to the ratio of population to assembly size.

The parity principle between States and Parliament — achieved by making the total Parliamentary vote weight equal to the total State Assembly vote weight — is the provision's federal masterstroke. It ensures that Parliament cannot dominate the presidential election merely by virtue of its members outnumbering State MLAs in absolute terms. The President's electoral mandate is genuinely bi-cameral in a federal sense: the Union and its constituent States jointly determine the outcome, with neither tier holding a structural majority.

The choice of the single transferable vote (STV) system by secret ballot is equally significant. STV allows voters to express a ranked preference rather than a binary choice. In a multi-candidate field, the votes of eliminated candidates are redistributed according to the next preferences marked on each ballot, and the process continues until a winner crosses the required quota. This prevents minority victories and encourages candidates who can build broad cross-party support rather than merely those with the largest single-bloc backing. The federal character of the electoral college is reinforced by the counting mechanism: a candidate who secures deep support from only one political stream may find that second-preference votes from other blocs prove decisive.

The census-freeze Explanation is perhaps the single most consequential technical detail in Article 55. By locking the "population" figure to the last published census — extended to the 1971 census until census figures after 2026 are published — the framers created a system that would be administratively stable but demographically stagnant. Every presidential election since 1971 has used population figures that are now more than five decades old. States that have grown fastest since 1971 (generally in northern and central India) are systematically underrepresented in MLA vote weight relative to their current populations, while States with historically larger assembly sizes (and lower population growth) carry comparatively higher per-MLA vote values.

## Reading the provision

The formula in Article 55(2) works in three steps. First, each MLA's vote value is computed by dividing the State's population (from the frozen census) by the number of elected Assembly seats, keeping only multiples of one thousand but rounding up if the remainder is five hundred or more. This gives a rough per-MLA population weight. Second, the total weight of all State MLA votes is aggregated across all States. Third, each MP's vote value is set by dividing that total aggregate MLA weight by the total number of elected MPs, again rounding fractions above a half up to one. The arithmetic ensures that the sum of all MPs' votes approximately equals the sum of all MLAs' votes — the parity principle.

The phrase "as far as practicable" in clause (1) acknowledges that perfect mathematical uniformity across all States may be unachievable. Some States have small populations and small assemblies; others have large populations spread across many constituencies. The formula approximates uniformity rather than mandating it. Courts have consistently treated this phrase as a legislative direction toward good-faith effort rather than a justiciable precision standard.

Clause (3)'s prescription of STV by secret ballot has constitutional implications beyond mere counting methodology. The secrecy requirement prevents party whips from verifying how individual legislators voted, making it constitutionally impossible for political parties to enforce discipline through post-election scrutiny of ballots. This design choice reflects the framers' preference for a presidential election in which individual legislators exercise genuine personal judgment, rather than mechanically transmitting bloc votes of their parliamentary parties.

## Key cases

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling confirmed that the elaborate electoral mechanism of Articles 54 and 55 creates a constitutional head whose authority ultimately flows from the Cabinet, not from an independent political mandate. The Court drew a sharp distinction between the President's formal election by the nation's representatives and the actual exercise of executive power, which must follow ministerial advice under Article 74. The judgment confirmed that neither the vote-value formula nor the STV counting mechanism vests in the President any independent policy-making authority beyond what Article 74 and Article 75 already confer on the Cabinet.

**In re Presidential Election, Special Reference No. 1 of 1974** — The Supreme Court clarified, in its advisory jurisdiction under Article 143, that vacancies in State Assemblies at the time of the presidential election do not invalidate the election. The mathematical formula of Article 55 is applied to sitting members at the time of the poll, not to the theoretical full complement. The Court confirmed that the Election Commission must use the current electoral roll, not a notional full membership, and that recalculating vote values each election on this basis is constitutionally mandatory.

**V.V. Giri v D.S. Dora (1970)** — The 1969 presidential election was the first decisive test of the Article 55 STV mechanism in a genuinely contested election. Varahagiri Venkata Giri's narrow first-preference lead over Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy fell short of the quota; only after preferences from eliminated candidates were transferred did Giri cross the winning threshold. The Supreme Court upheld the entire preference-transfer process, confirming that Article 55(3)'s STV system fully validates results determined by lower preferences rather than first-preference totals alone.

**Kuldip Nayar v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court's discussion of secret ballot obligations in the context of Rajya Sabha elections has direct relevance to Article 55(3). The Court observed that a constitutional secret-ballot requirement creates a floor that Parliament cannot abrogate by statute. The Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952, enacted under Article 71(3), must therefore maintain the secrecy guarantee of Article 55(3) — it can regulate the mechanics but cannot substitute open voting for the constitutionally mandated secret ballot.

**Election Commission of India v Dr Subramaniam Swamy** — Courts have confirmed that the Election Commission's role in administering the presidential election derives from Articles 324 and 71 read with the Presidential and Vice-Presidential Elections Act, 1952, which Parliament enacted under Article 55(3). The Commission's scheduling and procedural decisions are subject to constitutional constraint but enjoy a presumption of regularity. Its computation of vote values using the 1971 census figure is not a discretionary act — it is a constitutionally mandated application of the Explanation's frozen-census rule.

**Constituent Assembly Debates (November–December 1948)** — The vote-weighting formula was the subject of extensive technical debate in the Constituent Assembly. Dr Ambedkar defended the mathematical complexity as necessary to achieve the twin goals of inter-State uniformity and Parliament-State parity. An alternative proposal for a simple one-member-one-vote system was rejected on the ground that it would have given Parliament an overwhelming structural advantage over State Assemblies, destroying the federal parity at the heart of Article 55's design.

## Current significance

The 1971 census freeze has become one of the most contested aspects of the presidential election. Since 2026 is the year beyond which the next post-census figures are supposed to displace the 1971 benchmark, the upcoming census and delimitation exercise will directly affect the vote-value calculations. When fresh census figures are published and used, States with high population growth since 1971 — primarily in northern India — will see their MLAs' vote values rise substantially, while southern States with lower growth rates will see a relative decline. This arithmetic shift will alter the presidential electoral map for every subsequent election.

The STV mechanism draws attention each time a presidential election is closely contested. The 2017 election demonstrated that even with a clear Parliamentary majority, the STV counting process requires careful management of second and third preferences to guarantee victory, reinforcing the federal purpose embedded in Article 55's design. The secrecy of balloting continues to create uncertainty in tight elections, since party discipline cannot be enforced at the counting stage.

The 2022 presidential election, which saw NDA candidate Droupadi Murmu elected with a comfortable majority, nonetheless illustrated how the formula works across the federal spectrum — tribal legislators from various parties voted beyond party lines, demonstrating that the STV secret ballot design functions as the framers intended, producing outcomes that may diverge from strict party discipline.

The anti-defection dimension is another area where Article 55(3)'s secret ballot has live contemporary significance. Since the Tenth Schedule applies only to votes inside legislative chambers, and a presidential election ballot is cast outside those chambers in an electoral process governed by Article 55, the anti-defection law does not reach cross-voting in presidential elections. This constitutional insulation from party discipline means that presidential election campaigns must be political exercises in persuasion, not merely in counting votes — a feature that occasionally produces surprising results when candidates with broad moral or symbolic appeal attract support beyond their nominating coalition's numbers.
