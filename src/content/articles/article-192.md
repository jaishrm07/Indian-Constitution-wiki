## Why it matters

Article 192 is the State-level counterpart of Article 103, which provides the same mechanism for Parliament.

Together, they establish a constitutionally uniform approach to membership-disqualification adjudication: the formal decision rests with the executive head (President or Governor), but the substantive determination is delegated to the Election Commission, an independent constitutional body. The choice of the Election Commission rather than a court or a legislative committee reflects the framers' judgment that questions of disqualification — which directly affect the composition of legislatures — should be decided by the body charged with conducting elections, free from partisan pressure.

The Governor's constitutional role under Article 192 is therefore formally executive but substantively ministerial: he must obtain the Commission's opinion and is constitutionally required to follow it. He has no independent adjudicatory discretion — no power to reach a different conclusion than the Commission on the disqualification question, and no power to decline to make the reference at all once a disqualification question has validly arisen.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes the Governor as the formal decision-maker for disqualification questions arising under Article 191(1) — the grounds of office of profit, unsoundness of mind, insolvency, and non-citizenship, as well as statutory disqualifications under laws made by Parliament, including the Representation of the People Act.

The Governor's decision is stated to be "final," but courts have consistently interpreted this finality as excluding further executive review, not judicial review. The Supreme Court confirmed in Election Commission of India v Subramanian Swamy that judicial review under Articles 226 and 136 remains available to examine constitutional error, mala fides, or failure to follow the mandatory Article 192(2) process.

Clause (2) is the operative constraint: the Governor "shall obtain" the opinion of the Election Commission and "shall act according to such opinion." Both obligations are mandatory — the word "shall" is read as imperative in both instances, not permissive. There is no discretion: the Governor must seek the opinion and must follow it.

The Election Commission when providing its opinion under Article 192(2) functions as a quasi-judicial body. It gives the affected member notice, provides an opportunity to be heard, examines the factual record, and delivers a reasoned opinion. That opinion is the substantive determination of the disqualification question; the Governor's formal decision conforming to it is the constitutional mechanism through which that determination takes legal effect.

The article does not govern Tenth Schedule disqualifications (Article 191(2)), which are decided by the Speaker or Chairman. The clean structural separation between Article 192's Governor-plus-Election-Commission regime and the presiding officer's Tenth Schedule jurisdiction is a foundational feature of the State Legislature membership framework and must be maintained in proceedings and judicial review alike.

## Key cases

**Election Commission of India v Subramanian Swamy (1996)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the Governor's "final" decision under Article 192(1) does not mean unreviewable: it remains subject to judicial review on grounds of constitutional error or mala fides. The Court held that the Governor's obligation under clause (2) to obtain and follow the Election Commission's opinion is mandatory — a Governor acting contrary to the Commission's opinion acts without constitutional authority.

**B.R. Kapur v State of Tamil Nadu (2001)** — The Supreme Court examined the Governor's role under Article 192 following Jayalalithaa's disqualification as Chief Minister based on criminal conviction under the Representation of the People Act. The Court affirmed that once the Election Commission has expressed its opinion, the Governor has no independent adjudicatory discretion; the formal decision must conform to the Commission's determination.

**Jaya Bachchan v Union of India (2006)** — The Court's analysis of "office of profit" under the parallel parliamentary provision has been directly applied to Article 192 proceedings in State Legislature disqualification cases. The Court confirmed that factual ambiguities about whether a post constitutes an office of profit are resolved through the Election Commission's fact-finding, and the Commission's determination on those facts is authoritative.

**P.V. Narasimha Rao v State (1998)** — While primarily a parliamentary immunity case, the judgment's analysis of the constitutional status of members of the House as holders of a public trust applies equally to State legislators under Article 191-192, reinforcing that disqualification provisions must be interpreted to protect the integrity of legislative composition.

## The Election Commission's quasi-judicial process under Article 192

When a disqualification question is referred to it under Article 192(2), the Election Commission does not function as a regulatory or administrative body — it functions as a quasi-judicial tribunal. The affected member is given notice of the reference and an opportunity to file written submissions and be heard. The Commission examines the factual record, considers any legal submissions, and delivers a reasoned opinion. It is this reasoned opinion — not the Governor's order — that is the substantive legal determination of the disqualification question. Because the Election Commission's opinion is binding on the Governor, any challenge to the ultimate disqualification decision must in practice target the Commission's process and reasoning, not the Governor's order. Courts have accepted petitions for judicial review of Election Commission opinions under Article 192(2) on grounds of natural justice violation, manifest error of law, or perversity — treating the Commission's quasi-judicial function as fully subject to constitutional review notwithstanding the finality language in Article 192(1).

## Current significance

Delays in Article 192 proceedings have been a persistent structural problem. Unlike the Tenth Schedule, which has attracted judicial directives for expeditious decision-making, the Article 192 process has no mandatory time limit. A disqualification reference can remain pending with the Election Commission for years, during which the affected member continues to sit and vote — potentially in a hung Assembly where every vote is decisive. Governors have on occasion been alleged to have delayed even initiating the Election Commission reference, exploiting the absence of an enforceable timeline. Law Commission of India reports have recommended statutory time limits for Article 192 proceedings, modelled on the time-limit proposals for Tenth Schedule adjudication, but no legislative action has followed. The Supreme Court has noted this gap, and High Courts have in some cases directed the Governor to expeditiously make or act upon the Election Commission reference. The structural reliance on the Election Commission's binding opinion remains the article's principal constitutional safeguard against partisan disqualification decisions at the State level. The B.P. Singhal constraint on arbitrary gubernatorial conduct applies here too: a Governor who deliberately delays making a reference or acting on a Commission opinion for political reasons acts unconstitutionally, even if no precise time limit exists in the text of Article 192 itself.
