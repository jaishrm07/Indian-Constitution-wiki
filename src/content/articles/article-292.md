## Why it matters

Article 292 is the constitutional foundation of India's sovereign debt. Without it, there would be no clear constitutional basis for the Government of India to borrow on the markets, issue government securities, or give guarantees on behalf of public sector entities. The article places the borrowing power on a formal constitutional footing — anchoring it to the Consolidated Fund of India and making it subject to parliamentary control — while allowing considerable flexibility about how much Parliament chooses to borrow in any given period.

The practical significance is enormous: government securities issued under Article 292 form the bedrock of India's bond market, are the primary instrument for RBI's monetary operations, and underpin the insurance, pension fund, and banking sectors which are required to hold sovereign paper. Every rupee of government borrowing — whether for routine expenditure financing, capital investment, or emergency stimulus — draws its legal authority from this article.

## Reading the provision

The article vests the executive power of the Union in borrowing "upon the security of the Consolidated Fund of India." The security offered is not a specific asset but the general fiscal resources of the Union — future tax revenues, non-tax receipts, and the full revenue-generating capacity of the sovereign. This constitutional backing is what makes Indian government securities risk-free assets in the domestic regulatory framework.

The article allows Parliament to set limits on borrowing. Parliament has exercised this power through the Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act 2003 (FRBM Act), which originally targeted a fiscal deficit of 3 per cent of GDP and a revenue deficit of zero, with a path of annual reduction. The FRBM Act also extends to guarantees — the other power Article 292 grants — requiring the government to report contingent liabilities and work toward a ceiling on outstanding guarantees.

## The FRBM Act and its escape clauses

The FRBM Act's fiscal deficit targets represent Parliament's exercise of its Article 292 limiting power. The Act includes an escape clause allowing the government to exceed statutory deficit ceilings by up to 0.5 per cent of GDP when the N.K. Singh Committee's conditions are met — national security emergency, natural calamity, or structural reforms with macroeconomic impact. This clause was invoked heavily during the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-21), when the Union's fiscal deficit rose to approximately 9.2 per cent of GDP against a 3 per cent FRBM target, representing the largest peacetime deviation from statutory borrowing limits in India's constitutional history. The subsequent path of consolidation — returning to a 4.5 per cent deficit target by 2025-26 — represents the parliamentary response to the temporary suspension of the FRBM framework.

The guarantee limb of Article 292 creates a parallel set of contingent liabilities: the Union issues guarantees for borrowings by PSUs, NABARD, NHB, and other entities, for multilateral development bank loans, and for infrastructure project financing. These guarantees do not count as borrowing in the fiscal deficit calculation but represent claims on the Consolidated Fund if they are invoked. The total outstanding Union guarantee stock is substantially larger than the annual additions to explicit debt.

## Key cases

**Bhim Singh v. Union of India (2010) 5 SCC 538** — A public interest litigation challenging the Union's fiscal deficit levels and FRBM compliance raised the question of whether macro-fiscal management decisions under Article 292 are justiciable. The Court declined to direct specific fiscal targets, holding that the framing of fiscal policy is primarily a matter for Parliament and the executive, and that courts should not substitute their judgment for that of the constitutional bodies charged with managing public finance — illustrating the boundary between constitutional mandate and economic policy discretion.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India AIR 1978 SC 68** — While the case primarily addressed the scope of Article 131 and federal disputes, the Court's analysis of the Union's financial predominance in the constitutional scheme touched on the significance of the Union's unconstrained borrowing capacity under Article 292 as a structural feature of fiscal federalism — contrasting it with States' far more constrained borrowing powers under Article 293.

**In re Special Courts Bill (1979) 1 SCC 380** — Although a reference on legislative competence rather than borrowing per se, the Court's analysis of Parliament's power to limit and regulate the exercise of constitutional executive powers confirmed that Parliament's Article 292 limiting power is a genuine constraint — when Parliament enacts FRBM-style legislation, the executive's borrowing power is bounded by that statute and excess borrowing without parliamentary approval would be unconstitutional.

## Asymmetry with Article 293 — State borrowing constraints

Article 292 must be read alongside Article 293 (State borrowing). The Union's borrowing power under Article 292 is limited only by Parliament, which the executive dominates. State borrowing under Article 293 is subject to additional consent: where a State has outstanding Union loans, it cannot borrow without the Union's consent. This structural asymmetry — Union borrowing self-regulated, State borrowing requiring Union approval — has been a persistent source of federal tension. States have argued that the Union uses the consent condition in Article 293(3) as political leverage, conditioning consent on unrelated fiscal or policy compliance. The Finance Commission's recommendations on States' borrowing ceilings have been an important moderating mechanism, since the Commission's independent recommendations on fiscal deficit limits for States have been harder for the Union to override than a pure executive consent condition.

During the COVID-19 pandemic the Union temporarily relaxed the Article 293 consent conditions for States, allowing them to borrow up to 5 per cent of GSDP (from the normal 3 per cent) provided they met specified reform conditions in sectors such as power distribution, one-nation-one-ration card, and ease of doing business. This conditional relaxation — Union borrowing uncapped, State borrowing relaxed only on conditions — illustrated the structural asymmetry in practice, with some States accepting the reform conditions and others declining the extra borrowing room to avoid policy conditionality.

## Current significance

With India targeting a fiscal deficit glide path toward 4.5 per cent of GDP by 2025-26 and the 16th Finance Commission framing its devolution recommendations against this backdrop, Article 292 is at the centre of active fiscal federalism debates. The Union's post-COVID borrowing levels, the contingent liability created by the guarantee stock, and the treatment of off-budget financing vehicles — borrowings by FCI, NHAI, and other entities that are effectively Union liabilities but sit outside the FRBM computation — all raise questions about the substantive meaning of Parliament's Article 292 limiting power. Rating agencies and multilateral institutions have flagged these off-budget liabilities as potential risks, and there is growing pressure to bring them within the FRBM framework — which would require explicit parliamentary action under Article 292 to redefine the scope of the controlled borrowing ceiling.
