Appellate jurisdiction of Supreme Court in appeals from High Courts in regard to civil matters.—2[(1) An appeal shall lie to the Supreme Court from any judgment, decree or final order in a civil proceeding of a High Court in the territory of India [if the High Court certifies under article 134A—]

  (b) that in the opinion of the High Court the said question needs to be decided by the Supreme Court.]

## Why it matters

Litigation is, at its core, about finality — the assurance that disputes, once decided, are decided. But finality must be balanced against accuracy: a legal system that allows incorrect decisions to stand forever, especially on questions that affect many people beyond the immediate parties, fails both individual litigants and the coherent development of the law. Article 133 represents the constitutional resolution of this tension for civil matters. It creates a right of appeal to the Supreme Court, but only when the High Court itself certifies that something legally important and genuinely unsettled is at stake. The certification requirement is the Constitution's mechanism for ensuring that the Supreme Court's civil appellate jurisdiction is reserved for cases where the Court's voice is genuinely needed — not merely cases where one party is dissatisfied with the outcome.

The human stakes of a civil appeal to the Supreme Court can be very high. A property dispute that turns on an unsettled question of succession law may affect families for generations. A contract dispute that raises a question about the enforceability of certain commercial clauses may affect how an entire industry contracts. A family court ruling that depends on a disputed reading of personal law provisions has implications beyond the individual marriage. In all these cases, the question of whether a further appeal is available — and what conditions it must satisfy — directly shapes the rights and practical options of real people navigating the legal system.

Article 133 has also been shaped by institutional pressures that its framers could not fully have anticipated. The Supreme Court has become one of the most burdened apex courts in the world, with tens of thousands of cases filed each year. The vast majority of these cases arrive not through the certificate route of Articles 132-134 but through the special leave jurisdiction under Article 136. Understanding Article 133 therefore requires understanding it in relationship to Article 136 — the certificate route and the SLP route define together the avenues by which civil disputes ascend to the Supreme Court, and the practical eclipse of Article 133 by Article 136 is one of the defining features of Indian appellate practice.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the certificate requirement**: An appeal to the Supreme Court in a civil matter requires the High Court to certify under Article 134A (which was inserted by the Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act 1978 to provide a procedural mechanism for issuing certificates). The certification has two conjunctive elements: first, that the case involves a substantial question of law of general importance; and second, that the High Court is of the opinion that the question needs to be decided by the Supreme Court. Both conditions must be met — a question may be important but already settled by the Supreme Court, in which case the second condition is not met; or a question may be genuinely unsettled but of purely local significance, in which case the first condition may not be fully met.

The phrase "substantial question of law of general importance" is the operative filter. It has been extensively interpreted by the Supreme Court. The word "substantial" means that the question must be real and genuine — not academic, hypothetical, or already answered by binding precedent. The requirement of "general importance" means the question must have significance beyond the parties to the case — it must affect the legal rights of other persons in like situations, or the general administration of law, or the coherent development of a legal principle. A question that is fact-specific to the particular dispute before the court, no matter how complex or bitterly contested, is not a question of general importance in the requisite sense.

**Clause (2) — constitutional questions**: A party appealing under Clause (1) may also urge, as a ground of appeal, that a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of the Constitution has been wrongly decided by the High Court. This is a protective provision — it ensures that constitutional questions arising in civil proceedings are not denied appellate scrutiny merely because the case reached the Supreme Court through the civil, rather than constitutional, appeal route. Any civil case that also raises a constitutional interpretation issue can have that issue decided as part of the appeal.

**Clause (3) — single judge exclusion**: No appeal lies to the Supreme Court from the judgment or order of a single judge of a High Court in a civil matter, unless Parliament otherwise provides by law. This reflects a structural assumption that decisions of a single judge have not received the collegiate scrutiny that makes a further appeal to the apex court appropriate. The appropriate remedy against a single-judge decision is ordinarily an appeal to a Division Bench of the High Court. This clause limits the reach of Article 133 and ensures that the Supreme Court is not drawn into correcting the first-instance decisions of individual judges on questions that have not yet been fully argued before the High Court as a bench.

## The certificate jurisdiction in context

The original text of Article 133 (before the Thirtieth and Forty-Fourth Amendments) was more permissive. It allowed an appeal in civil matters where the High Court certified that (a) the case involved a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of the Constitution, or (b) the case involved some other substantial question of law, and the High Court was of opinion that the question was one that needed decision by the Supreme Court, or — before the Thirtieth Amendment removed it — (c) the valuation of the dispute exceeded a prescribed monetary threshold. The threshold-based right of appeal was abolished by the Constitution (Thirtieth Amendment) Act 1972, reflecting Parliament's recognition that access to the Supreme Court should be based on legal significance, not financial value.

The Forty-Fourth Amendment 1978 consolidated and simplified the remaining grounds. The current provision represents a deliberate narrowing — only genuine questions of law of general importance, as certified by the High Court, can found a right of appeal. A pure error of law on the facts of the particular case, a wrong appreciation of evidence, or a procedurally defective decision — none of these alone qualifies. The constitutional route is reserved for what the High Court itself certifies to be a question the country's apex court should answer.

## Article 133 and Article 136 — the practical relationship

The most significant feature of Article 133 in contemporary practice is that it has been largely overtaken by Article 136. Article 136 gives the Supreme Court discretion to grant special leave to appeal from any judgment of any court in India. Unlike Article 133, it requires no certificate and is not confined to civil matters or to questions of general importance — the Court may grant leave whenever it considers it appropriate to do so in the interests of justice. Because Article 136 is far more flexible — and because the Court has developed a practice of granting leave quite liberally in matters it considers meritorious — litigants who want to appeal a High Court civil judgment routinely file a special leave petition rather than seeking an Article 133 certificate.

This practical reality does not reduce Article 133 to irrelevance. There are structural differences between the two routes. An appeal under Article 133 is a matter of right once the certificate issues — the Supreme Court cannot refuse to entertain a certified appeal on the ground that it lacks merit, though it can decide the case against the appellant. An SLP under Article 136 is always at the Court's discretion, and the Court can refuse leave at the threshold without any opinion on the merits. The certificate route therefore offers a more secure avenue of appeal to a petitioner who can obtain a High Court certificate. In practice, however, the certificate is itself difficult to obtain — High Courts are reluctant to certify questions as nationally important when the matter is really a dispute between parties about their specific situation.

## Key cases

**Sir Chunilal V. Mehta and Sons Ltd. v. Century Spinning and Manufacturing Co. Ltd. (1962)** — The Supreme Court laid down the foundational test for "substantial question of law of general importance" under Article 133. The Court held that such a question must satisfy three requirements: it must be well arguable, meaning it is not plainly covered by a clear earlier decision; it must have general importance, meaning it affects people beyond the immediate parties; and it must be one whose decision by the Supreme Court is necessary to settle the law. A question that is merely debatable between lawyers, or one that seems important to the parties because they lose if it is answered a particular way, does not meet the threshold. This test has been consistently applied for over six decades and is the starting point for any Article 133 certificate application.

**Santosh Hazari v. Purushottam Tiwari (2001)** — The Supreme Court reiterated and refined the Chunilal Mehta test in the context of second appeals in civil matters, holding that a question already settled by the Supreme Court cannot be a "substantial question" in the required sense. The Court emphasised that the test is not subjective — it does not ask whether the litigant thinks the question is important — but objective, asking whether the legal community would recognise the question as raising a genuinely open issue. The judgment is the modern reference point for the distinction between a real legal controversy and a re-argument of settled law.

**Kondiba Dagadu Kadam v. Savitribai Sopan Gumal (1999)** — The Supreme Court clarified the distinction between a "question of law" and a "question of fact dressed up as a question of law" in the context of Second Appeals. The Court held that findings of fact, even complex factual findings, cannot be the basis for a substantial question of law merely because the parties disagree about how the evidence was assessed. The question must genuinely arise from the legal principles applicable to the case, not from the parties' dissatisfaction with factual conclusions. This case has been consistently applied by High Courts deciding whether to certify an Article 133 appeal or entertain a Second Appeal under Section 100 of the Code of Civil Procedure.

**Union of India v. Indusind Bank Ltd. (2016)** — The Supreme Court addressed an Article 133 certified appeal from the High Court of Bombay and emphasised that when a certificate is granted, the Supreme Court must deal with all aspects of the certified question fully, not merely answer the narrow technical point. The case illustrates how Article 133 appeals, once admitted, are heard as full appeals on the merits of the certified legal question, with the parties entitled to address all aspects of the issue — a broader hearing than a narrow SLP admission typically provides.

**General Manager, South Central Railway v. A.V.R. Siddhanti (1974)** — The Supreme Court considered what constitutes a "civil proceeding" for purposes of Article 133, confirming that the provision applies to all civil proceedings in High Courts — not just those arising from commercial disputes — including service matters, property disputes, and proceedings under special statutes that are civil in character. The definition of "civil proceeding" is broad, and the Court has declined to read it in a restricted technical sense.

**Hero Vinoth v. Seshammal (2006)** — The Supreme Court addressed the interplay between the certificate jurisdiction under Article 133 and the second appeal provisions under the Code of Civil Procedure. The Court clarified that the "substantial question of law" standard applicable under Section 100 CPC is substantially the same as the Article 133 standard, and that a party who has succeeded in a second appeal before the High Court on a legal question may seek an Article 133 certificate if the question is of general importance and remains unsettled at the Supreme Court level. The judgment illustrates how the Article 133 standard percolates into ordinary civil appellate procedure at the High Court level even when no certificate is ultimately sought.

## Current significance

Article 133 operates today primarily as the formal framework for understanding what civil appeal rights exist at the constitutional level, rather than as the day-to-day mechanism through which such appeals actually arrive at the Supreme Court. The SLP under Article 136 has become the dominant practical pathway, with Article 133 certificates filed only in a small fraction of cases — often where the petitioner wants the security of a right of appeal rather than the uncertainty of discretionary SLP admission.

Law Commission reports have periodically examined whether the Supreme Court's civil appellate jurisdiction should be more strictly regulated to reduce the Court's burden. The Setalvad Commission, the Malimath Committee, and various Law Commission reports have all noted that the liberal grant of SLPs in civil matters — matters that often involve nothing more than a disagreement about facts between private parties — has transformed the Supreme Court into a third appellate tier for civil disputes, which is inconsistent with its constitutional role as the apex court for questions of law of national importance. Article 133's certificate requirement was designed precisely to prevent this outcome; the fact that Article 136 SLPs have eclipsed it reflects an institutional evolution that the text of the Constitution did not anticipate.

The question of whether civil appellate jurisdiction should be transferred to an intermediate National Court of Appeal — freeing the Supreme Court to focus on constitutional questions and matters of national importance — has been discussed for decades without resolution. Any such reform would require either a constitutional amendment to Articles 133 and 136 or a legislative framework under Article 138 that restructures how civil appeals flow. Until that reform is made, Article 133 remains the constitutional statement of the terms on which civil matters are entitled as of right to reach the Supreme Court — even if, in practice, those terms are rarely the actual mechanism used.
