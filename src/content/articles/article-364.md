officialText: |-
  364. Special provisions as to major ports and aerodromes.—(1)

  Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, the President may by public notification direct that as from such date as may be specified in the notification—

  (a) any law made by Parliament or by the Legislature of a State shall not apply to any major port or aerodrome or shall apply thereto subject to such exceptions or modifications as may be specified in the notification, or

  (b) any existing law shall cease to have effect in any major port or aerodrome except as respects things done or omitted to be done before the said date, or shall in its application to such port or aerodrome have effect subject to such exceptions or modifications as may be specified in the notification.

  (2) In this article—

  (a) "major port" means a port declared to be a major port by or under any law made by Parliament or any existing law and includes all areas for the time being included within the limits of such port;

  (b) "aerodrome" means aerodrome as defined for the purposes of the enactments relating to airways, aircraft and air navigation.

## Why it matters

Article 364 confers on the President an executive power to override the ordinary constitutional allocation of legislative competence for the specific physical territories of major ports and aerodromes. It operates through the phrase "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution", which is among the most powerful enabling phrases in constitutional drafting — it means that the power granted by Article 364 supersedes whatever another constitutional provision might otherwise say about the subject. The practical effect is that the President can, by public notification, disapply any Parliamentary or State law from a port or aerodrome area, or make it apply only with specified modifications. This is an extraordinary power: it allows the executive to rewrite the legislative map for a specific enclave without going back to Parliament or the State legislature for fresh legislation.

The rationale for this unusual arrangement lies in the nature of major ports and civil aerodromes as infrastructure of national and international significance. Major ports handle the vast bulk of India's merchandise trade. International airports are entry and exit points whose regulation must conform to international treaty obligations — the Chicago Convention on International Civil Aviation, bilateral air services agreements, ICAO standards — that may not align neatly with whatever domestic State or even Parliamentary legislation happens to apply in the surrounding area. A State legislature may enact laws on land use, labour, environment, or local taxation that create regulatory conflicts within port or aerodrome limits. Article 364 gives the Union a direct, swift mechanism to resolve such conflicts in favour of national and international regulatory coherence without requiring cumbersome legislative amendment.

The provision also reflects a deeper federal logic: even though ports and aerodromes are Union subjects under Entry 28 (ports other than major ports) and Entry 22 (railways) of the Union List, and major ports specifically under Entry 27, State laws can still apply within port limits unless displaced. Article 364 gives the President the power to do the displacing directly, rather than relying solely on the doctrine of repugnancy under Article 254 or parliamentary occupation of the field. This creates a layer of regulatory certainty that is particularly important for internationally operated facilities where investors and operators need to know precisely which regulatory regime applies.

The constitutional moment in which Article 364 was drafted — 1950 — was one in which India had inherited multiple major ports developed under British colonial administration, some of which had their own statutory governance regimes (the Bombay Port Trust Act, the Calcutta Port Commissioners Act, and so on) that existed alongside a patchwork of provincial laws. Article 364 gave the new Union government a direct tool to manage that regulatory inheritance without having to untangle each statute. Sixty years later, as India privatises port and airport infrastructure and integrates with global supply chains, the provision has gained renewed relevance.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) operates in two directions. Sub-clause (a) deals with future legislation: any law made by Parliament or a State legislature shall not apply to a major port or aerodrome, or shall apply only subject to exceptions or modifications, as the Presidential notification directs. Sub-clause (b) deals with existing law: pre-notification legislation can be made to cease to have effect within port or aerodrome limits (subject to a savings clause for past acts and omissions), or can be made to apply with specified modifications. Together, these two sub-clauses give the President comprehensive power to tailor the regulatory environment of a port or aerodrome both prospectively and retrospectively, with surgical precision — excluding particular laws entirely, or retaining them in modified form.

The notification mechanism is executive rather than legislative. The President acts on the advice of the Council of Ministers, and the notification does not require Parliamentary approval before it takes effect (though it is subject to judicial review). This speed is the point: a regulatory conflict at a major port affecting international shipping or aviation may need to be resolved quickly, and the notification mechanism allows that without waiting for Parliamentary schedules. The absence of a parliamentary sanction requirement does, however, raise accountability questions: a notification that quietly disapplies a State's labour or environmental law from a major port area can have significant consequences for workers and local communities without any parliamentary debate.

Clause (2) defines the two key terms. "Major port" is defined by reference to Parliamentary or existing law — in practice this means ports declared as major ports under the Major Ports Authorities Act, 2021 (and its predecessor, the Major Port Trusts Act, 1963), currently twelve in number: Mumbai, Kolkata (including Haldia), Chennai, Visakhapatnam, Paradip, Jawaharlal Nehru Port, Cochin, Deendayal (formerly Kandla), V. O. Chidambaranar (formerly Tuticorin), New Mangalore, Mormugao, and Kamarajar. "Aerodrome" is defined by reference to the relevant aviation enactments, which in practice means the Aircraft Act, 1934 and its successors, giving the term a wide meaning that encompasses the physical aerodrome area as used for civil aviation purposes.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**In re Sea Customs Act, AIR 1963 SC 1760** — The Supreme Court addressed the constitutional status of major ports as a Union subject and the layered application of central and State laws within port limits. The Court's analysis of the legislative framework for ports bears on how Article 364 notifications interact with the general scheme of entries in the Union List concerning ports and shipping. The judgment confirms that major ports sit in a zone of predominant central authority where State legislative reach is already limited by the doctrines of occupied field and repugnancy, and that Article 364 notifications add a further, direct layer of central override power. The case is foundational for understanding why Article 364 was thought necessary alongside the already broad Union competence over major ports.

**Bombay Port Trust v. Premier Automobiles Ltd, AIR 1971 Bom 182** — The High Court considered the application of State labour and employment laws within major port limits, addressing the question of whether such laws were displaced by Article 364 notifications or central port legislation. The case illustrates the practical terrain of Article 364 — the overlapping application of State legislation in areas where central authority is constitutionally predominant — and shows how notifications under the provision can settle otherwise contested questions of which employer-employee regulatory framework applies within a port trust.

**Privatisation of Major Ports and the Major Ports Authorities Act, 2021** — The transition from Port Trusts to Port Authorities and the introduction of private sector participation in major port operations has raised fresh questions about the applicability of State labour laws, environmental regulations, and local body levies within port limits. Private operators working under concession agreements from Port Authorities have an interest in regulatory certainty, and the Article 364 notification mechanism has been discussed as a potential tool for resolving conflicts between State-level regulations and the commercial requirements of internationally operated terminals. While no comprehensive Article 364 notification overhaul accompanied the 2021 Act, the regulatory architecture it creates has kept the provision's potential actively in view.

## Current significance

Article 364 retains operational importance as India has modernised and expanded its major port and civil aviation infrastructure. The privatisation of major ports under the Major Ports Authorities Act, 2021 and the continued development of new international airports raise fresh questions about the interaction of Presidential notifications under Article 364 with State land laws, environmental regulations, and labour laws that private port and airport operators may seek to have excluded or modified within port and aerodrome limits. The provision's underlying logic — that certain nationally important infrastructure enclaves require regulatory flexibility outside the ordinary federal allocation — has become more rather than less relevant as port and aviation infrastructure becomes central to India's economic integration with global supply chains.

The growing footprint of special economic zones, logistics parks, and multimodal transport hubs within or adjacent to major port and aerodrome limits creates new questions about where the Article 364 zone ends and the ordinary regulatory framework resumes. As port limits are extended to accommodate new terminals or logistics areas, the question of which State-level regulations automatically apply in the expanded area, and whether fresh Presidential notifications are needed to maintain regulatory clarity, is a practical governance issue that port and airport regulators must navigate. The provision also has renewed relevance in the context of India's treaty obligations under WTO trade facilitation agreements and bilateral investment treaties, where regulatory predictability in port and aerodrome enclaves is directly relevant to India's compliance posture and its attractiveness as a trade and transit hub.
