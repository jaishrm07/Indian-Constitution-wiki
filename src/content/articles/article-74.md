## Why it matters

Without Article 74, Articles 52 and 53 — which declare the existence of a President and vest executive power in the President — could have created an independent executive head with genuinely discretionary governing authority. Article 74 converts that potential into a parliamentary presidency by mandating that the President act only on Cabinet advice. This single provision is the hinge on which India's entire system of parliamentary government turns. It means that democratic legitimacy in the Union executive flows from the elected Parliament's confidence in the Prime Minister and Cabinet — not from any personal authority of the President. Every ordinance, every appointment, every treaty ratification, and every proclamation of emergency that the Constitution formally attributes to "the President" is, in constitutional substance, a Cabinet act.

The 44th Amendment's addition of the reconsideration proviso in 1978 was a direct response to Emergency-era concerns that a President might feel constitutionally compelled to act on advice without any right to pause or question. The proviso gives the President a single remission right — the ability to return Cabinet advice with a request for reconsideration — but closes the loop immediately: once the Cabinet reconsiders and resubmits, the President must accept. The President thus becomes a constitutional mirror that can reflect once but must ultimately reflect the Cabinet's will. This design deliberately preserves a consultative check without creating a suspensive veto.

The non-inquiry rule in clause (2) reflects the confidentiality principle underlying Cabinet government. If the content of ministerial advice to the head of state were discoverable in litigation, Cabinet deliberation would be chilled and the confidential relationship between the President and the Prime Minister would be undermined. The rule is not a blank licence for the Cabinet — it shields what was said, not whether anything was said or whether the subject matter was one requiring advice at all.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the binding advice requirement.** The phrase "shall, in the exercise of his functions, act in accordance with such advice" is unambiguous and absolute. There is no qualifier suggesting the President may act independently in particular circumstances. The mandatory phrasing — "shall" not "may" or "should" — leaves no room for the President to substitute personal judgment for Cabinet advice once valid advice has been tendered. Any Presidential action taken contrary to Cabinet advice, or in the absence of advice on a matter that requires it, is therefore not merely politically irregular but constitutionally void.

**The 44th Amendment proviso — single reconsideration.** The proviso was inserted by the 44th Constitution (Amendment) Act, 1978, enacted after the Emergency. "Either generally or otherwise" means the President may ask the Cabinet to reconsider either the whole of its advice or a specific component. Critically, the proviso says the President shall act in accordance with advice "tendered after such reconsideration" — the reconsidered advice is just as binding as the original. The proviso cannot be used repeatedly on the same matter: there is no "second remission," and a second return of the same advice would itself be a constitutional violation.

**Clause (2) — non-justiciability of advice content.** The phrase "shall not be inquired into in any court" has two important limits that courts have identified. First, it shields only the content or substance of the advice tendered — "what advice was tendered." It does not shield questions about whether any advice was tendered, or whether the President's action fell within a category of functions for which Cabinet advice is constitutionally required. These threshold questions remain justiciable. Second, the rule does not protect the underlying facts or material placed before the Cabinet from judicial scrutiny. As the Supreme Court held in S.R. Bommai, while courts cannot examine the specific advice, they can examine the material that informed the Cabinet's decision to advise the President — a distinction that has been critical in Article 356 cases.

**Scope of the advice obligation.** The phrase "in the exercise of his functions" is broad but not unlimited. The President has a small set of constitutionally recognised discretionary functions — most importantly, the appointment of the Prime Minister when no party commands a clear Lok Sabha majority. In these situations, the President acts on personal assessment of parliamentary arithmetic rather than on Cabinet advice, and Article 74's binding mechanism does not apply until a new Cabinet has been formed and has tendered its first advice.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar explicitly defended the binding advice model in the Constituent Assembly (November 1949) by invoking the British Cabinet system. He acknowledged that the draft vested executive power in the President, but argued that this was a formal vest — the actual exercise of executive power was to flow through and from the Council of Ministers. Ambedkar drew on Walter Bagehot's description of the British constitutional monarchy to argue that the framers were creating a constitutional head, not a real executive — a President who signs what the Cabinet tells the Prime Minister to tell him to sign.

T.T. Krishnamachari raised the question of whether the President would be entirely powerless — essentially a rubber stamp. Ambedkar's response was that the President retains three functions that Bagehot described as the monarchy's real powers: the right to be consulted, the right to encourage, and the right to warn. Article 78's information duties on the Prime Minister supply the informational foundation for these three consultative functions. The binding advice rule and the information duties are complementary: the President must follow Cabinet advice, but the Cabinet must also keep the President informed.

The original draft of Article 74 did not include the reconsideration proviso — that was a post-Emergency addition. The Constituent Assembly debates therefore do not address the reconsideration mechanism directly. What the debates establish is the framers' clear intention to create a parliamentary presidency in the Westminster mould, with no ambiguity about whether the President might retain residual executive discretion.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — The landmark seven-judge bench decision definitively held that the President and Governors are constitutional heads who must act on ministerial advice in all matters except those expressly reserved by the Constitution for personal discretion. The Court confirmed that Article 74 makes Cabinet advice binding and that the Indian presidency is fully parliamentary — the President retains no residual executive discretion outside the constitutionally specified category of appointing a PM when no clear majority exists. The judgment is the foundational precedent on the President-Cabinet relationship and has been consistently applied in all subsequent cases.

**Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab (1955) 2 SCR 225** — The Supreme Court's early and influential formulation held that the executive power of the Union, though vested in the President under Article 53, is exercised through the Council of Ministers and the bureaucracy under their direction. The case arose from a challenge to the Punjab government's nationalisation of the textbook trade without legislative authority, but the Court's analysis of Articles 53 and 74 together established the principle that executive power is Cabinet power in substance, not Presidential power. This structural reading has underpinned the entire post-independence understanding of Union executive authority.

**Maru Ram v. Union of India (1981) 1 SCC 107** — The Constitution Bench examined the President's power of pardon under Article 72 and held that this too is exercised on Cabinet advice under Article 74. The Court rejected the argument that the pardon power is a personal Presidential prerogative. The judgment confirmed that there is essentially no function of the President under Part V that is exercised on personal judgment (other than the appointment of the PM in ambiguous parliamentary situations), and that every Presidential act including pardons and reprieves is a Cabinet act through the constitutional fiction of Presidential exercise.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The nine-judge bench examined Presidential Proclamations under Article 356 dissolving State governments. The Court held that while the President's satisfaction is in constitutional substance the Cabinet's satisfaction, the material that informed that satisfaction can be judicially reviewed to test its relevance, credibility, and good faith. Article 74(2) does not prevent courts from asking whether the material placed before the Cabinet could rationally justify the action recommended — only whether courts can examine the specific advice actually tendered. This reading prevents Article 74(2) from being used to insulate arbitrary Cabinet recommendations from judicial scrutiny.

**U.N.R. Rao v. Indira Gandhi (1971) 2 SCR 63** — The Supreme Court held that even after dissolution of the Lok Sabha, the Council of Ministers continues to aid and advise the President. Article 74's requirement that a Council exist at all times means that a caretaker Ministry remains constitutionally obligated to tender advice until a new government is formed. The President cannot govern alone during a dissolution period — the caretaker Cabinet's advice remains binding even though it has lost Lok Sabha confidence, subject only to the convention that the caretaker government will not take major policy decisions.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The five-judge bench reiterated, in the context of Article 163 (Governor-Cabinet relationship), that constitutional heads of state at both Union and State levels cannot act contrary to or without valid Council advice. Although decided primarily under Article 163, the judgment grounded its analysis in Article 74 by analogy and confirmed that the parliamentary design — an advice-bound head of state — is a structural feature of the Indian Constitution that cannot be circumvented by any head of state, whether President or Governor.

## Current significance

The most politically live questions under Article 74 cluster around the hung-parliament scenario — who may the President invite to form a government when no party commands a clear Lok Sabha majority, and on what basis? The President has genuine initial discretion in this situation: the discretion to assess parliamentary arithmetic and determine who is most likely to command confidence. But this is not a free-ranging discretion to prefer a government on non-parliamentary grounds. The emerging constitutional practice — invite the single-largest party or pre-election alliance, set a reasonable deadline for demonstrating majority support, and avoid acting on partisan calculations — reflects the constraints that Article 74's parliamentary logic imposes even on the President's residual discretion.

The reconsideration proviso has been used sparingly in practice. Presidents have returned specific Bills for reconsideration — most notably in the context of the GNCT Delhi Amendment in 2023, where questions arose about whether the President had indefinitely withheld assent — but the legal question of whether a President may return advice a second time or simply sit on reconsidered advice remains unresolved. The Constitution provides no deadline, and the courts have not been called upon to enforce the President's obligation to act after reconsideration in any concrete case. This lacuna becomes significant during periods of political distance between the President and the Cabinet.

President Droupadi Murmu's 2025 reference to the Supreme Court under Article 143 on the question of the Governor's obligations regarding State Bills creates a parallel constitutional debate about Presidential obligations under Articles 74 and 111. If the Court holds that Governors are time-bound in their response to State Bills, the same logic — derived from Article 74's binding advice structure — would likely apply to the President's response to Central Bills, potentially resolving one of the most significant gaps in the Union executive's constitutional framework.
