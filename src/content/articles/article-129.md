## Why it matters

Article 129 confers on the Supreme Court a constitutional contempt jurisdiction that is sui generis — it exists by virtue of court-of-record status and does not depend on, and cannot be abridged by, Parliament's statutory contempt scheme. This gives the Court an inherent self-protective power that has been exercised against political leaders, public intellectuals, and senior advocates, making it one of the most contested provisions in the Court's relationship with public discourse.

The practical significance of Article 129 is felt every time the Court enforces a direction against a reluctant State government, a public sector official, or a private party who has given an undertaking. Without the contempt jurisdiction, Supreme Court orders would be no more enforceable than any High Court order — the constitutional contempt power under Article 129 is what gives finality and coercive force to the Court's authority throughout India.

## Reading the provision

The text is deceptively short — a single sentence — but its legal effects are expansive because the phrase "court of record" imports the full body of common-law doctrine about what such courts can do.

The phrase "court of record" carries a common-law heritage. A court of record is one whose proceedings are permanently enrolled, whose acts cannot be questioned by a collateral tribunal, and which has inherent power to enforce its own authority. The constitutional phrase "all the powers of such a court including the power to punish for contempt of itself" makes explicit what was implied by common law: the Supreme Court may act against any conduct that interferes with the administration of justice or scandalises the Court's authority.

Two broad categories of contempt arise under Article 129. Civil contempt is wilful disobedience of an order of the Court — typically an injunction, undertaking, or direction — and its principal purpose is coercive compliance rather than punishment. Criminal contempt is broader and includes scandalising the Court, doing anything likely to prejudice a pending proceeding, or obstructing the administration of justice. The Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 applies statutory definitions and procedures, but the Court's constitutional jurisdiction under Article 129 is not constrained by the Act's provisions and can be exercised independently.

The constitutional jurisdiction under Article 129 is also distinguished from the general enforcement power under Article 144, which obliges all civil and judicial authorities to act in aid of the Supreme Court. Article 144 creates a duty of assistance; Article 129 creates a power to punish. Together they form a comprehensive enforcement architecture: Article 144 ensures voluntary compliance by constitutional authorities, while Article 129 provides the coercive sanction when compliance is refused.

The 2006 amendment to the Contempt of Courts Act introduced truth as a valid defence — a person may justify otherwise contemptuous statements by establishing that they are true and were made in the public interest. This reform followed years of criticism that criminal contempt for scandalising the Court was incompatible with Article 19(1)(a)'s guarantee of free speech. The "truth and public interest" defence does not apply where the contemptuous statement was not made in the public interest, giving the Court residual discretion over whether the defence succeeds in a given case.

## Key cases

**P N Duda v P Shiv Shankar (1988)** — The Court examined whether a sitting minister's public statements criticising the judiciary amounted to contempt. Holding that fair, bona fide criticism of the judiciary in the public interest does not constitute contempt, the bench cautioned that judges must not be placed on too high a pedestal. This remains the leading authority on the free speech boundary of the contempt power under Article 129. The judgment is significant not only for what it permits but for what it implies: the Court was acknowledging that the institutional legitimacy of the judiciary must ultimately rest on performance and public confidence, not on coercive suppression of criticism.

**Supreme Court Bar Association v Union of India (1998)** — A Constitution Bench confirmed the Court's undoubted power under Article 129 but drew a critical limit: the contempt jurisdiction cannot be used to suspend an advocate's licence, because that power is vested exclusively in the Bar Council under the Advocates Act. The ruling is the leading statement on how Article 129's constitutional jurisdiction interfaces with Parliamentary legislation — it supplements, but cannot supplant, the statutory order. This boundary has broad significance: it means that while Parliament cannot strip the Court of its Article 129 power, the Court cannot use that power to bypass Parliament's considered legislative choices about remedies and procedures.

**Arundhati Roy, In re (2002)** — The Court convicted the author and activist for scandalising the Court arising out of her affidavit in the Narmada dam displacement litigation. The case reaffirmed the constitutional contempt power as independent of the Contempt of Courts Act and generated intense debate about whether the "scandalising" category has any legitimate role in a constitutional democracy that guarantees free speech. The sentence — a symbolic one-day imprisonment and a nominal fine — did not resolve the underlying doctrinal tension. Scholars have pointed to the case as evidence that criminal contempt for scandalising operates primarily as a deterrent and symbolic assertion of authority rather than as a genuine remedy for institutional harm.

**Re Vijay Kurle (2020)** — The Court initiated suo motu contempt proceedings against a senior advocate and a retired judge for statements alleging bias in the assignment of cases to particular benches. The case illustrated the range of the contempt jurisdiction and the contested questions about whether criticism of judicial administration — as distinct from criticism of a particular judgment — falls within the Article 129 power. The case also raised questions about whether the Court's internal administrative decisions, such as roster assignment, can themselves be reviewed or criticised without constituting contempt — a boundary that remains unresolved.

## Contempt vs supervisory jurisdiction

Article 129's contempt jurisdiction is different from the Court's supervisory jurisdiction over lower courts and tribunals. Contempt is a self-protective power: it punishes interference with the Court's authority or disobedience of its orders. Supervisory jurisdiction under Article 136 and Article 139A is an appellate power: it corrects errors of law by subordinate courts. The two sometimes overlap — if a High Court persistently disobeys a Supreme Court direction, both Article 129 contempt and Article 136 appeal may be available — but their purposes, procedures, and grounds differ. Conflating them is a recurring source of confusion in litigation.

Article 129 also differs from the Court's enforcement jurisdiction under Article 32. Under Article 32, the Court enforces fundamental rights at the instance of a petitioner whose rights have been violated. Under Article 129, the Court acts to protect its own institutional authority and the integrity of its own proceedings. The two may coincide — disobeying an order made under Article 32 is both a fundamental rights violation and a contempt — but the constitutional roots and the procedural implications differ. Understanding this distinction helps in appreciating why contempt proceedings are initiated differently from writ petitions and why the remedies available in each proceeding are not interchangeable.

## Procedure in contempt matters

Contempt proceedings under Article 129 may be initiated by the Court suo motu or on a petition by any person who alleges that an order has been disobeyed or that the Court has been scandalised. The Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 sets out statutory procedures — notice, hearing, opportunity to purge contempt — but because Article 129's constitutional jurisdiction is independent, the Court is not confined to the Act's procedural framework. A person found in contempt may be sentenced to simple imprisonment of up to six months, a fine of up to Rs 2,000, or both. Courts regularly allow contemnors to "purge" civil contempt by belatedly complying with the order, and the purpose in civil contempt is compliance rather than punishment. Criminal contempt, particularly for scandalising, is treated more seriously because its target is the Court's institutional integrity rather than the rights of a private party.

The interaction between the Court's constitutional contempt power under Article 129 and the High Courts' similar power under Article 215 is also significant. High Courts are independently courts of record with their own contempt jurisdiction. Where contempt of a Supreme Court order is at issue, the Supreme Court retains exclusive jurisdiction; contempt of a High Court order falls primarily within the High Court's Article 215 power. Both powers flow from the same common-law court-of-record concept that Article 129 constitutionalises for the Supreme Court.

## The contempt power and accountability of judges

A recurring question is whether the contempt power can be used to silence criticism of the conduct of individual judges or the institution as a whole. The Court's position — drawn from P N Duda and reaffirmed in successive cases — is that fair and accurate reporting of court proceedings is not contempt, that bona fide criticism based on published judgments is not contempt, and that statements amounting to a sustained attempt to undermine public confidence in the court as an institution may constitute criminal contempt. This framework preserves space for journalistic and academic engagement with judicial decisions while retaining a residual category of actionable scandalising. Critics argue that even this residual category gives the Court too much power to police criticism of itself, and that it has occasionally been invoked against critics whose real offence was inconveniently accurate reportage rather than a genuine threat to judicial authority.

There is also a structural irony in the Court judging contempt of itself: it acts simultaneously as the complainant, the judge, and the tribunal. The Contempt of Courts Act provides some structural protection — notably, that the Court may request the Attorney-General to prosecute — but does not eliminate the institutional conflict of interest. Comparative experience suggests that routing such proceedings through an independent prosecutor or a different judicial body would better serve both justice and institutional legitimacy, but the constitutional text vests the power in the Court itself.

## Relationship with Article 19

The most principled constitutional challenge to the scandalising branch of criminal contempt is that it is an unreasonable restriction on the freedom of speech under Article 19(1)(a). The Contempt of Courts Act is not saved by any of the permitted restrictions in Article 19(2) — "contempt of court" appears as a ground for restriction in Article 19(2), but critics argue that this ground was intended to cover interference with pending proceedings rather than scandalising the institution in the abstract. The Law Commission's 1994 report recommended abolition of the scandalising category, noting that England had done so and that the cases in which it had been invoked in India did not demonstrate any genuine threat to the administration of justice. These recommendations have not been implemented, and the tension between Article 129 and Article 19(1)(a) remains unresolved by the Supreme Court itself.

## Current significance

Criminal contempt for scandalising the court remains on the books and is periodically invoked, but the 2006 truth defence has reduced its scope. The more frequent use of Article 129 is in civil contempt for non-compliance with Supreme Court orders, where the Court has initiated proceedings against Chief Secretaries of States, public sector management, and corporate officials. The relationship between Article 129 and Article 19(1)(a) — whether contempt law can be used to shield the institution from legitimate criticism — remains an open question that recurring high-profile proceedings keep in focus. Law Commission recommendations to decriminalise scandalising contempt, in line with the position in England and Australia, have not yet been acted upon by Parliament. The court of record status and the contempt jurisdiction remain foundational to the Supreme Court's authority, and the Article will continue to generate controversy wherever the line between permissible criticism and actionable contempt is contested.

The proliferation of social media has intensified the practical questions under Article 129. Statements that would previously have been confined to a pamphlet or a journal article can now reach millions of readers instantly, and the Court has had to consider whether the same contempt standards apply to online commentary as to traditional publication. No bright-line rule has yet emerged, and the intersection of Article 129, Article 19, and digital expression is a developing area of constitutional law.

More broadly, Article 129 sits at the intersection of two constitutional values that cannot be fully reconciled: the independence and authority of the judiciary, which the contempt power protects, and the freedom of expression and the public's legitimate interest in scrutinising those who exercise judicial power, which Article 19 protects. The balance the Court has struck — preserving a residual but narrowly applied criminal contempt jurisdiction alongside a robust space for criticism — will continue to be tested as both judicial authority and free expression claims become more prominent in the constitutional landscape.

The constitutional design ensures that the Court's authority to enforce its own orders is not dependent on the goodwill of the executive or the legislature — it flows directly from Article 129 as a constitutional grant. This makes the contempt jurisdiction ultimately a structural safeguard for judicial independence, not merely a tool for managing litigants. Its significance is therefore inseparable from the broader question of how the Constitution protects the institutional autonomy of the judiciary as a whole. Article 129, read together with Articles 50, 121, 211, and the independence provisions of Part V and Part VI, forms part of a constitutional architecture designed to ensure that the judiciary can perform its constitutional functions free from external pressure or internal intimidation.

Article 129 is thus a provision whose constitutional text is brief but whose practical and doctrinal reach is vast, touching every aspect of the Court's relationship with its own authority, with other institutions, and with the citizens whose rights it is charged with protecting.
