## What this Article did

Article 375 is a transitional continuity provision. Its purpose was to ensure that the entire machinery of government — courts, revenue authorities, executive officers, and ministerial staff — continued functioning without interruption when the Constitution of India came into force on 26 January 1950. Without such a provision, there could have been uncertainty about whether courts and officers operating under the Government of India Act, 1935 and the Indian Independence Act, 1947 retained their authority after those instruments were superseded by the new Constitution.

The article is universal in scope: it covers all courts (civil, criminal, and revenue), all authorities, and all officers (judicial, executive, and ministerial) throughout the territory of India. The phrase "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" is key — it means that while these institutions continued to function, they now did so within the constitutional framework, subject to fundamental rights, the new distribution of powers, and all other constitutional constraints.

## Historical context

When the Constitution came into force, India had a functioning judicial and administrative system that had been built up over more than a century under British rule. The colonial-era court hierarchy — from district and sessions courts up through High Courts — had deep roots, established procedures, and pending caseloads. Revenue courts, which adjudicated land disputes, were essential for agrarian administration in a country then overwhelmingly rural.

Disrupting these institutions on the day of constitutional commencement would have been administratively catastrophic. Article 375 prevented this by deeming all pre-existing courts and officers to carry on their work without needing to be formally re-established or reappointed under the new Constitution. This was part of a broader set of transitional provisions in Part XXI that ensured institutional continuity: Article 376 made similar provision for High Court judges, Article 377 for the Comptroller and Auditor-General, and Article 378 for Public Service Commissions.

The phrase "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" also meant that from 26 January 1950, these courts and officers became bound by fundamental rights. A person aggrieved by a court order or executive action from that date onwards could invoke constitutional remedies, even if the underlying proceeding had begun before commencement.

## Key cases

**Chiranjit Lal Chowdhuri v Union of India AIR 1951 SC 41** — Although not specifically about Article 375, early Supreme Court cases confirmed that the courts and authorities continuing under the transitional provisions were now subject to fundamental rights constraints, giving practical content to the "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" qualification in Article 375.

**State of Bombay v Purushottam Jog Naik AIR 1952 SC 317** — The Court considered the status of officers and courts that had continued from the pre-constitutional period, illustrating how Article 375 operated in practice to preserve institutional continuity while the Constitution simultaneously imposed new constraints on those institutions.

No case appears to have directly challenged the validity of Article 375 itself or argued that a specific court or authority lacked authority to function after commencement. The provision achieved its purpose of seamless continuity without significant litigation.

## Current significance

Article 375 is a spent provision in the narrow sense that the transition it managed occurred on 26 January 1950. However, it has continuing significance as a constitutional recognition that the institutions inherited from the colonial and dominion-era past were validated and continued under the new constitutional order. Every High Court in India that traces its lineage to a High Court established under the Indian High Courts Act, 1861 or later statutes derives its continued legitimacy, in part, from this provision. Article 375 thus forms part of the constitutional foundation for the continuity of judicial institutions in India.

## Why it matters

Article 375 reflects a fundamental choice made by the Constitution's framers about how to manage the relationship between the new constitutional order and the existing institutional fabric of government. The alternative approach — requiring all courts and officials to be freshly constituted under the new Constitution before they could act — would have created an unprecedented administrative vacuum. The chosen approach, validating continuity while imposing new constitutional constraints, meant that the system of courts and administration that citizens depended upon in their daily lives continued to function without disruption while simultaneously being transformed by the new constitutional framework they were now subject to.

The article is also significant for illustrating how the new Constitution treated the relationship between pre-constitutional institutions and the new fundamental rights framework. Rather than treating the Constitution as a radical break requiring institutional reconstruction from scratch, Article 375 validated the continuation of existing institutions while simultaneously subjecting them to the new constitutional order. This approach — continuity of institutions with transformation of the legal framework within which they operate — is characteristic of the transitional philosophy of Part XXI as a whole.
