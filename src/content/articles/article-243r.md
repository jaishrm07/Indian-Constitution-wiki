  243R. Composition of Municipalities.—(1) Save as provided in clause

  (2), all the seats in a Municipality shall be filled by persons chosen by direct election from the territorial constituencies in the Municipal area and for this purpose each Municipal area shall be divided into territorial constituencies to be known as wards.

  (2) The Legislature of a State may, by law, provide—

  (a) for the representation in a Municipality of—

  (i) persons having special knowledge or experience in Municipal administration; (ii) the members of the House of the People and the members of the Legislative Assembly of the State representing constituencies which comprise wholly or partly the Municipal area; (iii) the members of the Council of States and the members of the Legislative Council of the State registered as electors within the Municipal area; (iv) the Chairpersons of the Committees constituted under clause (5) of article 243S:

  Provided that the persons referred to in paragraph (i) shall not have the right to vote in the meetings of the Municipality;

  (b) the manner of election of the Chairperson of a Municipality.

## Why it matters

The Seventy-Fourth Amendment's central ambition was to make municipal government democratically legitimate rather than merely administratively functional. Article 243R gives effect to that ambition by making direct election from territorial wards the constitutional baseline for filling seats. Every municipal area must be divided into wards, and ward-level direct election is mandatory rather than a legislative choice. This distinguishes the post-1992 constitutional design from the pre-amendment era in which some States used appointment or indirect election for a significant portion of municipal seats. The provision in clause (2) allows supplementary representation — MPs, MLAs, nominees with municipal expertise, and ward committee chairpersons — but these categories are additions to a directly elected core, not substitutes for it. The explicit bar on voting rights for specially nominated members in clause (2)(a)(i) reflects a deliberate constitutional choice to preserve the democratic character of municipal decision-making even while drawing on administrative expertise.

## How courts use it

Courts have used Article 243R principally in two contexts. First, challenges to ward delimitation orders argue that ward boundaries have been drawn to dilute the representation of particular communities or to favour incumbents — these cases test whether the division of a municipal area into territorial constituencies meets the constitutional requirement of reasonably proportionate and geographically coherent wards. Second, challenges to State laws that expand the nominated or ex-officio component beyond what clause (2) permits, or that grant voting rights to members who fall within the expertise category, are tested against the mandatory language of the proviso to clause (2)(a)(i). Courts have been consistent that the voting bar for nominated expert members is an absolute constitutional rule that State legislatures cannot override.

## Key cases

**Mohd. Aslam v. Union of India (2003) — Ward delimitation constraints** — The Supreme Court held that the process of delimiting municipal wards must respect the constitutional mandate of direct election and cannot be used to perpetuate arrangements inconsistent with Article 243R. The ruling confirmed that ward delimitation is open to judicial review for compliance with Part IXA requirements, and that undue delay or gerrymandering of ward boundaries violates the constitutional scheme.

**State of Bihar v. Bal Mukund Sah (2000) — Nominated members and democratic character** — The Supreme Court examined State provisions for nominated members in local bodies and held that nominations cannot be used to subvert the directly elected majority or to give nominated members effective control over municipal proceedings. This principle directly informs the interpretation of Article 243R(2)(a)(i)'s voting bar.

**Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of Ahmedabad (2006) — Composition and electoral rolls** — The Supreme Court addressed questions about the validity of municipal elections where ward delimitation had not been updated to reflect population changes. The Court held that democratic representation under Article 243R requires periodic revision of ward boundaries so that the composition of the Municipality genuinely reflects the current population distribution of the municipal area.

## Current significance

The composition of municipalities has become contested territory as Indian cities absorb large migrant populations and as peri-urban wards carry far more residents than the wards within older municipal cores. Malapportionment across wards within the same municipal area sits in tension with Article 243R's requirement of direct election from territorial constituencies, and several petitions have argued that long delays in ward delimitation effectively dilute the votes of residents in high-growth wards. The Supreme Court has noted this structural problem without yet prescribing a mandatory delimitation cycle for urban local bodies, leaving a gap that reformers and litigants continue to press.
