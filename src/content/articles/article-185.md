  185. The Chairman or the Deputy Chairman not to preside while a resolution for his removal from office is under consideration.—(1) At any sitting of the Legislative Council, while any resolution for the removal of the Chairman from his office is under consideration, the Chairman, or while any resolution for the removal of the Deputy Chairman from his office is under consideration, the Deputy Chairman, shall not, though he is present, preside, and the provisions of clause (2) of article 184 shall apply in relation to every such sitting as they apply in relation to a sitting from which the Chairman or, as the case may be, the Deputy Chairman is absent.

  (2) The Chairman shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the Legislative Council while any resolution for his removal from office is under consideration in the Council and shall, notwithstanding anything in article 189, be entitled to vote only in the first instance on such resolution or on any other matter during such proceedings but not in the case of an equality of votes.

## What this Article does

Article 185 applies to the Chairman of the Legislative Council the same natural-justice safeguard that Article 181 applies to the Speaker of the Assembly: the presiding officer steps down from the chair whenever a removal resolution against them is under consideration, but retains the right to speak and to vote in the first instance. The chain of acting presiding officers under Article 184(2) takes over for the duration of that sitting. The article prevents a Chairman from using the powers of the chair to influence the very vote that might end their tenure.

The symmetry with Article 181 is deliberate. Both articles balance two competing values: the institutional authority of the presiding office must not be deployed for self-preservation, but the individual who holds the office retains their rights as a member of the House, including the right to address the House in their own defence and to participate in the vote on their fate.

## The right to speak and the limited right to vote

Clause (2) gives the Chairman under removal consideration a nuanced participation right. The Chairman may speak and otherwise take part in proceedings — this includes moving amendments, responding to arguments, and addressing the House on matters of procedure that arise. The voting right is limited to voting "in the first instance" — meaning that the Chairman cannot exercise the second or casting vote under Article 189 when the removal resolution is being voted on or when other matters arise during that debate. This restriction is calibrated precisely: the casting vote, which is available to the Chairman as a normal procedural tool, cannot be used to save the Chairman's own tenure in cases of an equality of votes.

The right to participate extends to "any other matter during such proceedings," meaning that if collateral issues arise — such as a quorum question or an amendment to the notice period — the Chairman can participate in those too, as a member rather than as presiding officer.

## Key cases

**Kihoto Hollohan v Zachillhu (1992)** — The Supreme Court's broad endorsement of the Speaker's/Chairman's quasi-judicial independence in anti-defection proceedings implicitly validates the Article 185 constraint as necessary to prevent that independence from being inverted: just as the Chairman must be free from executive interference when deciding disqualification petitions, so must the Council be free from the Chairman's presiding authority when considering the Chairman's own removal.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — The Court's extension of the Article 181 logic — that a presiding officer facing removal proceedings is constrained from taking consequential orders — has been read in state constitutional practice as equally applicable to the Chairman under Article 185, reinforcing that both Articles form a coherent constitutional restraint on the self-protective use of presiding powers.

**Sub-Committee on Judicial Accountability v Union of India (1991)** — The Court's observations about institutional conflict of interest in constitutional proceedings were applied in several state legislative disputes to confirm that the withdrawal requirement under Article 185 is absolute and cannot be waived by the Council or by the Chairman's agreement to remain impartial.

## Current significance

As with Article 181, the protection of Article 185 applies specifically to the sitting at which the removal resolution is under consideration. The window between the giving of notice (triggering the fourteen-day requirement under Article 183) and the actual sitting at which the resolution is debated remains a zone where the Chairman retains full powers, including anti-defection jurisdiction. This asymmetry has been a source of constitutional controversy in several states and has yet to receive comprehensive Supreme Court resolution.

In Maharashtra and Bihar, where political crises have tested the constitutional framework for presiding officers repeatedly, Article 185 has been invoked as a basis for challenging decisions taken by Chairmen who were simultaneously subject to removal notices from an opposition coalition. The question of whether a Chairman who is the subject of a pending removal notice retains jurisdiction over unrelated disqualification petitions during the period before the notice is debated has emerged as a significant unresolved constitutional issue.

The structural logic of Article 185 is ultimately about institutional trust. The Chairman is elected by the Council precisely because members trust them to preside impartially; that trust cannot hold if the Chairman is simultaneously defending their tenure from the same members whose votes they are trying to influence through the exercise of presiding powers. Article 185 enforces this logic at the precise moment when the Chairman's conflict of interest is most acute, while preserving the Chairman's dignity and participation rights as a member of the House.
