  Article 355 places two distinct obligations on the Central Government. First,
  it must protect every State from external attack and from serious internal
  violence that the State cannot contain on its own. Second, it must ensure

## Why it matters

The historical context of Article 355 is rooted in the framers' experience of Partition,
the integration of the princely states, and the early years of independence when communal
violence and separatist pressures were live threats to newly formed States. B R Ambedkar
and Jawaharlal Nehru supported a strong Union protective role precisely because the
experience of 1946–50 had demonstrated how quickly civil order could collapse and how
dependent State governments were on central support for their physical security.

Article 355 is a single sentence, but it performs a structural role that shapes the entire
emergency chapter of the Constitution. It declares a positive duty on the Union — not a
power — to protect States and to ensure that their governments function constitutionally.
This framing is deliberate: the framers located the Union's right to intervene in a State
not in a unilateral discretion but in an obligation. That distinction — duty rather than
discretion — is the foundation on which the Supreme Court built its SR Bommai doctrine.

The dual duty in Article 355 maps onto two distinct constitutional contexts. The first
duty — protection against external aggression and internal disturbance — corresponds to
the grounds for invoking a National Emergency under Article 352. Article 355 also supplies
the justification for deploying Union forces in a State without the State government's
consent when genuine armed violence has broken out — a constitutional peg for statutes
like the Armed Forces Special Powers Act, 1958.

The second duty — ensuring constitutional governance — is the predicate for Article 356.
A proclamation under Article 356 asserts that the Government of a State cannot be carried
on in accordance with the Constitution; Article 355 says the Union has a duty to ensure
it can be. The two provisions read together form a constitutional sequence: the duty
arises under 355, and the remedial power is exercised under 356.

Article 355's importance in federalism jurisprudence lies in what it prevents as much as
what it permits. By framing Union intervention as a duty to protect States — rather than
a power to override them — the provision embeds a protective purpose. The Bommai bench
was emphatic: the duty to ensure constitutional governance in a State is a duty owed to
that State, not a licence to circumvent it. Using Article 355 as a pretext to dismiss a
government that retains its majority is constitutionally inconsistent with the duty's
protective character.

The relationship between the Governor and Article 355 is an underappreciated dimension
of the provision. The Governor, as the constitutional head of the State Government, is
the Union's primary instrument for monitoring whether constitutional governance is
maintained. A Governor who reports adversely on a State government is representing to
the President that the Article 355 duty has been triggered. The Supreme Court in
Rameshwar Prasad examined whether that representation was accurate and in good faith —
and struck down a proclamation when it found the Governor had reported on the basis of
unconstitutional material.

The Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1988) dedicated an entire chapter to
Article 355 and concluded that the provision had been consistently misread as primarily
power-conferring when it is actually duty-imposing. The Commission recommended that the
Union should not use Article 355 to justify intervention without first exhausting other
constitutional options and without concrete evidence of genuine constitutional breakdown.
The Bommai bench drew heavily on the Sarkaria Commission's framework in constructing
the justiciability and floor-test doctrines that now govern Article 356 proclamations.

The AFSPA dimension of Article 355 continues to generate significant constitutional
controversy. The Armed Forces Special Powers Act, 1958 and its North-East variant allow
the Union Government to designate areas as "disturbed" and deploy Army units with powers
of detention and lethal force. The constitutional peg advanced for this Act is Article
355's duty to protect States against internal disturbance. Critics argue that AFSPA has
been continuously extended in some areas for decades without any genuine ongoing
assessment of whether the Article 355 duty remains engaged — turning a conditional duty
into a permanent administrative arrangement.

## Reading the provision

The provision's textual brevity is deceptive. A single sentence containing two duties
has generated over seven decades of contested interpretation — about what "internal
disturbance" means, about how the duty to ensure constitutional governance relates to
Article 356, and about what the Governor's constitutional obligations are under the
framework Article 355 establishes. The answers have shifted significantly as the Court's
federalism jurisprudence evolved from the deferential stance of the 1950s through the
pre-Bommai permissiveness of the 1970s–80s to the post-Bommai framework of limited
but genuine judicial scrutiny.

Article 355 is drafted as a single sentence containing two conjoined duties: to protect
States against external aggression and internal disturbance, and to ensure constitutional
governance. The conjunction is significant — both duties exist simultaneously and neither
subsumes the other. The protection duty is essentially military and police in character,
addressing physical threats to the State's existence; the governance-ensuring duty is
essentially constitutional and political, addressing failures of democratic governance.

The phrase "internal disturbance" has a complex constitutional history. In Article 352,
the original text allowed a National Emergency to be proclaimed on grounds of internal
disturbance — a term wide enough to cover political opposition, labour unrest, or any
disorder the Centre chose to characterise as threatening. The 1975 Emergency was
proclaimed in part on this ground. The 44th Amendment of 1978 replaced "internal
disturbance" with "armed rebellion" in Article 352, significantly raising the threshold
for a National Emergency. Crucially, this amendment did not change the text of Article
355, which still refers to "internal disturbance." There are therefore levels of internal
disturbance that engage the Union's protective duty without rising to the level that
would support a National Emergency proclamation.

The phrase "Government of every State is carried on in accordance with the provisions
of this Constitution" is the operative standard for Article 356. Bommai held that this
phrase does not mean perfect constitutional compliance at every level — that standard
would make every State perpetually vulnerable to a central takeover. Rather, it refers to
a systemic breakdown: a situation where the constitutional mechanisms for democratic
accountability — elections, legislature, executive accountability — have ceased to function.

The dual-duty structure also creates a constitutional hierarchy for Union responses to
State emergencies. Not every threat to a State justifies the full weight of Article 356
President's Rule. If the threat is to physical security — armed rebellion, communal
violence, cross-border infiltration — Article 355's first duty is engaged and the
appropriate Union response is military or police assistance, not the displacement of
the elected government. Article 355 authorises the first; Article 356 is the last resort
for the second.

## Key cases

**S R Bommai v Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench is
the foundational authority on the relationship between Articles 355 and 356. The Court
held that Article 355 imposes a genuine and justiciable duty on the Union, not merely a
rhetorical precondition for exercising power under Article 356. The satisfaction required
under Article 356 must correspond to an actual failure to discharge the Article 355 duty
to maintain constitutional governance. Proclamations issued without relevant material are
constitutionally invalid. The Court also held that the federal principle is a basic
structure element, meaning using Article 355 as a pretext to undermine State democracy
is unconstitutional.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — The application of Bommai to
Bihar's 2005 President's Rule proclamation. The Governor had reported that the largest
post-election coalition was being assembled through horse-trading and that allowing the
assembly to meet would produce an unconstitutional result. The Supreme Court held that
the Governor was not entitled to assess the constitutional legitimacy of a government's
majority before it had even faced the assembly. The dissolution of the assembly before
it ever met was held to be an inversion of the Article 355 duty — the duty is to protect
constitutional processes, not to pre-empt them.

**State of Rajasthan v Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — Decided before Bommai, in
the pre-justiciability era. The Janata government sought to dismiss nine Congress State
governments, and the Supreme Court by a six-to-one majority upheld a broad reading of
the Union's powers under Articles 355 and 356. The Court held that the President's
satisfaction was largely non-justiciable. The lone dissent by Justice Bhagwati, holding
that constitutional material must have a rational nexus to the conditions for invoking
Article 356, was eventually vindicated by Bommai.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The five-judge
bench held that the Governor of Arunachal Pradesh had acted unconstitutionally by
advancing the session of the Assembly to facilitate a no-confidence motion against the
Speaker. The Court held that when the Governor's own unconstitutional conduct created
the conditions cited as constitutional breakdown, the Article 355 duty of protection
cannot be discharged — indeed, the Governor violated the duty by engineering the crisis
rather than resolving it.

**Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v Union of India (1998) 2 SCC 109** — The
Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of AFSPA 1958, holding that the Act
was supported by the Article 355 duty to protect States against internal disturbance
and by the Union's executive power under Articles 73 and 246. The Court held that the
designation of an area as "disturbed" is subject to judicial review for being arbitrary
or mala fide, but that the Union has broad discretion in making such determinations.
The judgment is the foundational authority for the constitutional relationship between
Article 355 and AFSPA-type legislation.

**Extra Judicial Execution Victim Families Association (EEVFAM) v Union of India
(2016) 14 SCC 536** — The Supreme Court held that armed forces personnel acting under
AFSPA are not immune from prosecution for deaths that are not genuine encounter killings.
The Court located this requirement in the Article 21 guarantee of life and in the
principle that the Article 355 duty authorises the use of force against those actually
threatening the State, not against civilians. The judgment narrowed the impunity
associated with AFSPA operations while stopping short of declaring AFSPA unconstitutional.

**Punjab President's Rule cases (1987–92)** — During the Khalistan militancy, the Centre
invoked both dimensions of Article 355 to justify the longest continuous period of
President's Rule in any State. The legal challenges to the extensions of President's
Rule in Punjab, and the Election Commission's certifications that elections could not be
held, produced a body of constitutional law on the outer limits of emergency governance
under Articles 355 and 356 read together.

## Current significance

Article 355 has become a live site of constitutional controversy in the contemporary
period of Governor-Government conflicts in multiple States. When a Governor delays
action on Bills, refuses to call the Budget session, makes adverse reports to the
President, or takes steps designed to destabilise a duly elected government, the question
is whether the Governor is fulfilling or subverting the Article 355 duty of protection.
The Supreme Court has increasingly been asked to enforce the constitutional framework
that Article 355 underpins — not against rebellious State governments, but against
Governors whose conduct threatens to manufacture a constitutional breakdown.

The debate about the Armed Forces Special Powers Act remains constitutionally unresolved
under Article 355. Judicial challenges to the continuous extension of disturbed area
notifications in Manipur and other North-Eastern States have raised the question of
whether a perpetual "internal disturbance" designation is constitutionally consistent
with the Article 355 duty — which implies an emergency response to a genuine crisis,
not a permanent administrative arrangement. The 2016 EEVFAM ruling imposed
accountability for individual killings but did not address the structural question of
whether indefinitely extended AFSPA deployments are compatible with the constitutional
framework.

Post-Bommai, the most significant ongoing question about Article 355 is whether its
protective function creates any legally enforceable rights in State governments against
central interference. The Bommai framework allows States to challenge proclamations
that invoke Article 355 as a pretext; Nabam Rebia allows States to challenge Governor
conduct that creates rather than prevents constitutional crises. But there is no
established mechanism for a State to compel the Union to exercise its protective duty —
to require central intervention when a State is genuinely threatened by internal violence
that its own forces cannot contain. Article 355 creates an obligation but does not
specify remedies for its breach.

The Punchhi Commission on Centre-State Relations, which reported in 2010, recommended
significant reforms to the Article 355–356 framework: that the Centre should be required
to issue a warning to a State before proclaiming President's Rule, that the Governor's
report should be laid before Parliament, and that localised emergency provisions should
be available short of full President's Rule for specific regions of a State. None of
these recommendations has been implemented by constitutional amendment, leaving the
framework as articulated by Bommai and subsequent judicial decisions as the operative
constitutional law.
