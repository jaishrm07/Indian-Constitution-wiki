  181. The Speaker or the Deputy Speaker not to preside while a resolution for his removal from office is under consideration.—(1) At any sitting of the Legislative Assembly, while any resolution for the removal of the Speaker from his office is under consideration, the Speaker, or while any resolution for the removal of the Deputy Speaker from his office is under consideration, the Deputy Speaker, shall not, though he is present, preside, and the provisions of clause (2) of article 180 shall apply in relation to every such sitting as they apply in relation to a sitting from which the Speaker or, as the case may be, the Deputy Speaker, is absent.

  (2) The Speaker shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the Legislative Assembly while any resolution for his removal from office is under consideration in the Assembly and shall, notwithstanding anything in article 189, be entitled to vote only in the first instance on such resolution or on any other matter during such proceedings but not in the case of an equality of votes.

## What this Article does

Article 181 applies the natural-justice principle of nemo judex in causa sua — no one should be judge in their own cause — to the Assembly's internal proceedings for removing its presiding officers. A Speaker facing a removal resolution cannot chair the sitting at which that resolution is under consideration. The Deputy Speaker facing removal is similarly barred from presiding at the relevant sitting. An alternative presiding officer takes the chair under Article 180(2).

The article simultaneously protects the presiding officer's rights as a member: the Speaker retains the right to speak in self-defence and to participate in the proceedings, and can cast a vote in the first instance on the resolution and on any other matter arising during those proceedings. The single exception is the casting vote under Article 189(1) — the tie-breaking second vote available to the Speaker during ordinary proceedings — which is not available when the Speaker faces a removal resolution, to prevent the anomaly of the threatened Speaker casting a decisive vote to defeat the motion against them.

## The article is the State equivalent of Article 96

Article 181 mirrors Article 96, which imposes the same bar on the Lok Sabha Speaker and Deputy Speaker when facing removal motions in Parliament. The constitutional design is identical across the two levels: the presiding officer is disqualified from presiding but retains membership rights. The State-level provision has been more frequently litigated, because State Assemblies have experienced more frequent political crises in which removal notices have been deployed as tactical weapons in anti-defection battles.

## The scope of "under consideration"

The phrase "under consideration" is legally precise: Article 181's bar applies only at a sitting at which the removal resolution is actually being debated or voted upon. It does not apply merely because a notice of removal has been given, or because a motion has been admitted but not yet listed for debate. This narrow reading — confirmed by courts and Speaker's rulings — means that a Speaker who receives a removal notice continues to exercise all powers, including Tenth Schedule disqualification powers, until the resolution is actually taken up at a sitting.

## The right to speak and vote in the first instance

Clause (2) grants the Speaker the right to speak and to vote in the first instance during the removal sitting. "Vote in the first instance" means the Speaker votes as a member — once — on the resolution and on any other matter during those proceedings. This is to be distinguished from the casting vote under Article 189(1), which is an additional second vote cast only when the votes are equal. Article 181 explicitly preserves the first-instance vote while withholding the casting vote, ensuring that the Speaker cannot manufacture a tie in order to cast a decisive additional vote in their own favour.

## Interaction with the Tenth Schedule

The most significant development in Article 181 jurisprudence has been the extension of its logic to disqualification proceedings under the anti-defection law. The Nabam Rebia case established that once a notice for a Speaker's removal has been admitted, the Speaker's Tenth Schedule powers are themselves constrained. Allowing a Speaker facing a removal motion to proceed with disqualification orders against the very members who moved the notice would enable the Speaker to use a quasi-judicial power to neutralise a democratic mechanism for accountability.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — The Supreme Court's five-judge bench extended the Article 181 logic to disqualification proceedings: once a notice for the Speaker's removal has been admitted and the Speaker is under Article 181's constraints at the sitting where the removal resolution is under consideration, the Speaker cannot simultaneously use Tenth Schedule powers against the members who moved the notice. The Court held that allowing such parallel action would permit the threatened Speaker to weaponise a quasi-judicial power in their own political defence.

**Kerala Assembly Speaker removal proceedings (various)** — State legislative practice has confirmed that Article 181 applies specifically to the sitting at which the resolution is "under consideration," not to every sitting after notice is given but before the motion is listed. This creates a window during which a Speaker can still exercise full powers after receiving a removal notice, which has been used in practice to make disqualification orders before the removal motion is brought on.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v The Hon'ble Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — The Constitution Bench's emphasis on the Speaker's Tenth Schedule obligations as judicially enforceable obligations applied, by implication, to Article 181 situations: a Speaker cannot use the period between notice and the removal sitting to discharge disqualification petitions in a manner that defeats the removal mechanism. The Court's insistence on impartiality in Tenth Schedule proceedings reinforces the Article 181 principle that the Speaker must not be judge in their own cause.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Maharashtra crisis raised connected questions about the relationship between the Speaker's resignation and the Article 181 protections: a Speaker who resigns to avoid a removal proceeding extinguishes the Article 181 framework but may leave unresolved disqualification petitions and an Article 180 vacancy situation that requires careful constitutional management.

## Current significance

The article's protection is narrow but crucial: it applies only to the actual sitting where the removal resolution is under consideration, not to all sittings from the moment notice is received. This has been exploited in practice — Speakers who receive notice under Article 179(c) have continued to exercise all their powers in the intervening period, including making Tenth Schedule disqualification orders. The Nabam Rebia rule partially closes this gap for anti-defection matters, but broader questions about the Speaker's full range of powers after notice and before the actual debate day remain active in constitutional discourse, and further legislative or judicial clarification may be needed to fully align the Article 181 framework with the constitutional expectation of an impartial presiding officer.
