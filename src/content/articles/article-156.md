## Why it matters

The Governor's term structure in Article 156 creates a constitutional tension that has never been
fully resolved. The article gives the Governor a nominal five-year term but immediately subordinates
that term to the pleasure of the President. Since the President acts on Cabinet advice under Article
74, the "pleasure" is effectively the Union government's pleasure — meaning the Centre can remove
any Governor at any time without assigning reasons and without any legislative or judicial consent.
This design was deliberately chosen by the framers, who rejected proposals for a fixed term or a
removal procedure mirroring impeachment, wanting Governors who would remain responsive to the Centre
in the exercise of discretionary functions under Article 163. The consequence has been the recurring
use of the Governor's office as a political appointment that changes when central governments change
— producing friction in States governed by parties in opposition to the Centre.

The structural paradox at the heart of Article 156 is this: the Governor is expected to act as an
impartial constitutional head who gives advice on State affairs and exercises discretion in
accordance with constitutional principles — yet the Governor holds office at the pleasure of the
very Union executive whose political interests may conflict with those of the State. The pleasure
doctrine ensures that no Governor will remain in office beyond the Centre's tolerance, which creates
an institutional incentive toward political alignment that is structurally at odds with the
constitutional expectation of impartiality.

The Sarkaria Commission (1988) and the Punchhi Commission (2010) both recognised this paradox as
central to the problem of Centre-State relations and recommended moving away from the pleasure
doctrine toward a fixed, non-renewable term. Both commissions invoked democratic accountability
and the integrity of State governance as reasons for insulating the Governorship from the Centre's
political calculus. Neither recommendation has been enacted by Parliament.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the pleasure doctrine.** "The Governor shall hold office during the pleasure of the
President" is the governing constitutional rule. Tenure is at the Centre's will. The clause creates
no procedural requirement for removal — no notice, no hearing, no obligation to assign reasons, no
parliamentary role. The entire procedural content of the removal power has been supplied by the
Supreme Court in B.P. Singhal v. Union of India (2010), which read implicit constitutional
constraints into what would otherwise be a blank cheque.

**Clause (2) — voluntary resignation.** The Governor may resign by writing under hand addressed to
the President. In practice, Governors have been pressured to resign through informal signals from
the Union government rather than through formal removal proceedings — a pattern that B.P. Singhal
sought to address by requiring that even an informal removal must have a constitutional basis, not
merely a partisan one. A Governor who resigns under political pressure without genuine voluntariness
occupies a legally ambiguous position, but courts have been reluctant to look behind the formal
written resignation.

**Clause (3) — five-year term and holdover.** "Subject to the foregoing provisions" means that the
five-year term operates only insofar as the Governor has not been removed or resigned. The term
therefore functions in practice as an expectation or convention — not as a constitutional guarantee
of tenure. The proviso creates holdover tenure: a Governor whose five-year term has expired continues
in office until a successor is appointed. In practice this can extend for many months when the Centre
deliberately delays naming a replacement — for example, to keep a Governor aligned with the Centre's
interests in place during a period of State political instability, or to avoid legitimising a newly
elected State government by promptly completing the constitutional formality.

**Comparison with other constitutional functionaries.** The Governor's position under Article 156
differs sharply from that of other senior constitutional functionaries. Judges of the Supreme Court
and High Courts hold office "during good behaviour" and can only be removed by a presidential
address supported by a special majority of Parliament — a process that has almost never been
completed. The Chief Election Commissioner holds a protected tenure under Article 324 that has been
compared to that of a Supreme Court judge. The Comptroller and Auditor-General holds statutory
protections that in practice make mid-term removal politically costly. The Governor alone holds
office on the President's pleasure with no procedural safeguard on the face of the Constitution,
no parliamentary role in removal, and no institutional mechanism of the kind that protects judicial
independence. This contrast underlines why the Sarkaria and Punchhi Commissions viewed Article 156
reform as central to Centre-State harmony.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

In the Constituent Assembly debates, several members — including K.M. Munshi — argued for a fixed
term of five years with removal only through a process equivalent to presidential impeachment, on
the ground that a Governor who could be removed at will would inevitably become a partisan figure.
B.R. Ambedkar and the drafting committee resisted this, arguing that the Governor's position as a
constitutional head appointed by the Centre required that the Centre retain ultimate authority over
the tenure. The framers were also concerned that a fixed-term Governor who had lost the confidence
of both the Centre and the State government would become a constitutional embarrassment if removal
were made procedurally difficult.

The compromise — pleasure doctrine with a nominal five-year expectation — was inherited partly from
the Government of India Act 1935 model of provincial governors, who served at the Crown's pleasure.
The Constituent Assembly's failure to break cleanly from this colonial precedent has been criticised
by constitutional scholars as one of the major structural deficits of the constitutional design for
federalism.

## Key cases

**B.P. Singhal v. Union of India (2010) 6 SCC 331** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling directly
addressed whether Article 156(1) confers an absolute power of removal. The five-judge Constitution
Bench held it does not: while the President can remove a Governor without assigning reasons, the
power cannot be exercised arbitrarily, capriciously, or for political purposes such as installing a
new government's preferred nominee. The Court read into the pleasure doctrine an implied constitutional
constraint that the Governor must have acted in some manner warranting removal — loss of constitutional
confidence based on conduct. The Court declined to demand that reasons be given publicly — balancing
justiciability against executive confidentiality — and did not order reinstatement. B.P. Singhal
remains the only judicial curb on the pleasure doctrine in Article 156(1), but its practical effect
has been limited because the Court's refusal to require disclosure of reasons makes it extremely
difficult to establish in court that a removal was for impermissible political motives.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The nine-judge bench dealing with Article 356
elaborated on the constitutional position of Governors whose actions and recommendations trigger
Presidential Rule. The Court's insistence on judicial review of gubernatorial recommendations
implicitly required that Governors act in good faith rather than as political agents, reinforcing
that the pleasure doctrine does not permit turning the office into a partisan instrument for
manufacturing constitutional crises to facilitate central intervention.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — The Court recognised that Governors
are constitutional functionaries who cannot be assimilated to mere agents of the Union government,
even though they are Centre-appointed and hold at the Centre's pleasure. This structural observation
has been invoked in subsequent cases to argue that arbitrary removals undermine the constitutional
design of the Governorship and that the pleasure doctrine must be understood within the broader
constitutional scheme rather than as an unlimited licence to remove.

**Harshvardhan v. Union of India** — Multiple High Court petitions challenging gubernatorial removals
and alleged pressure to resign have been dismissed on the ground that the manner of removal under
Article 156(1) involves executive discretion of the President, and courts will not ordinarily
interfere unless a demonstrable constitutional violation — of the B.P. Singhal standard — is shown.
The challenge to removals alleged to be for political reasons has been held to be effectively
non-justiciable in the absence of disclosed reasons.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker (2016) 8 SCC 1** — Though primarily about the Governor's power
to summon the Assembly, the Court's analysis of the limits of gubernatorial discretion is relevant
to the tenure question: the Court held that the Governor's constitutional functions must be
exercised consistently with the aid-and-advice framework, and that the pleasure doctrine in Article
156(1) does not give the Centre licence to use the Governor as an instrument of partisan governance.

## Current significance

Governor removals and politically motivated tenures have been recurring controversies in Indian
federalism. Following changes of government at the Centre — in 2004, 2014, and since — waves of
gubernatorial departures have followed, as incoming administrations effectively replaced incumbents
with their own nominees. B.P. Singhal's nominal constraint has not in practice prevented this
pattern, because the Union government can pressure Governors to resign through informal means,
avoid formal removal proceedings entirely, and still achieve the political objective of installing
a sympathetic nominee. The Bihar (2005), Goa (2017), and Arunachal Pradesh (2016) controversies
all involved Governor actions that critics characterised as politically motivated, and the Supreme
Court's willingness to scrutinise such actions — as in Nabam Rebia and Bommai — reflects an ongoing
judicial effort to impose constitutional discipline on an office whose tenure structure offers
minimal formal protection.

The Punchhi Commission's recommendation for a fixed five-year term removable only through a
procedure analogous to presidential impeachment — including a role for the State Legislature or a
statutory inquiry — has gained renewed attention as federal tensions have escalated. The argument
for reform is straightforward: if the Governor is to act as a genuinely impartial constitutional
head, the appointment and tenure mechanism must insulate the office from the partisan calculations
of the Union executive. Article 156 as currently drafted provides no such insulation, and the
judicial constraint supplied by B.P. Singhal, while important in principle, has proven insufficient
in practice.
