officialText: |-
  263. Provisions with respect to an inter-State Council.—If at any time it appears to the President that the public interests would be served by the establishment of a Council charged with the duty of—

  (a) inquiring into and advising upon disputes which may have arisen between States;

  (b) investigating and discussing subjects in which some or all of the States, or the Union and one or more of the States, have a common interest; or

  (c) making recommendations upon any such subject and, in particular, recommendations for the better co-ordination of policy and action with respect to that subject, it shall be lawful for the President by order to establish such a Council, and to define the nature of the duties to be performed by it and its organisation and procedure.

## Why it matters

Article 263 creates the constitutional space for a structured, institutionalised forum of cooperative federalism — a body where the Union and all States can deliberate on shared concerns, coordinate policy, and receive advice on inter-State tensions without resorting to adversarial litigation before the Supreme Court. It addresses a fundamental problem in any federation: the legislative distribution of powers resolves who can make laws on what subject, but it does not in itself provide a mechanism for the Union and States to coordinate on the vast range of issues — internal security, economic policy, environmental regulation, infrastructure, public health — where their interests and actions are deeply interdependent. Article 263 provides that mechanism.

The provision also reflects the framers' recognition that Indian federalism could not operate on a purely adversarial basis. Courts can resolve disputes about legislative competence or constitutional obligations, but the ongoing business of government — managing shared rivers, coordinating disaster response, aligning social legislation, harmonising industrial standards — requires a different kind of forum. The Inter-State Council is the Constitution's answer: a politically legitimate, structured arena for the Union and States to negotiate, deliberate, and reach consensus before disputes reach the courts or the emergency provisions of Articles 356 and 365.

The Council's activation (or dormancy) is a barometer of the health of cooperative federalism at any given moment in Indian political history. When the Council meets regularly and its recommendations are implemented, the federal system is working cooperatively. When it is neglected — as it has been for long stretches — it signals that the Centre-State relationship has become predominantly financial (grants and conditionalities) or adversarial (political conflicts between the Centre and Opposition-governed States) rather than genuinely collaborative.

## Reading the provision

**The enabling structure — "if at any time it appears to the President":**

Article 263 is enabling, not mandatory. The President establishes the Council only if it "appears" to the President that the public interest would be served by establishment of a Council. There is no obligation to create the Council, no time limit, and no minimum frequency of meeting. Once created, the Presidential order defines the Council's duties, organisation, and procedure, giving the executive branch wide latitude to design the institution. This structural flexibility was deliberate — the framers did not want to constitutionalise the exact composition and procedural rules of the Council, preferring to allow those to be determined by executive order in light of the political realities of the time.

**Function (a) — dispute advisory:**

The Council may be charged with "inquiring into and advising upon disputes which may have arisen between States." This is advisory, not adjudicatory. The Council has no power to hand down binding determinations on inter-State disputes. Its role is to investigate and recommend, leaving ultimate resolution to negotiation, to the Supreme Court under Article 131, or to specialist tribunals (under Article 262 for water disputes). The advisory function is deliberately modest — the framers did not want the Council to become a quasi-judicial body that might compete with or duplicate the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction. The Council's value in dispute advisory lies in its ability to generate political consensus before legal lines harden.

**Function (b) — investigation and discussion:**

The Council may investigate and discuss subjects in which some or all States, or the Union and one or more States, have a common interest. This is the broadest of the three functions and is the one most actively deployed when the Council meets. Agenda items in Inter-State Council meetings have ranged across fiscal federalism and Finance Commission recommendations, internal security and deployment of paramilitary forces, social welfare legislation, environment policy, education standards, agricultural market regulation, and cooperative governance arrangements for national programmes.

**Function (c) — policy coordination recommendations:**

The Council may make recommendations for better coordination of policy and action on matters of common interest. These recommendations are not legally binding. Their political weight depends on the composition of the Council — chaired by the Prime Minister and attended by all Chief Ministers — and the degree of political consensus achieved. When the Council reaches consensus, the recommendations carry substantial political authority even without legal compulsion. The gap between recommendation and implementation has been the persistent weakness of the Council system.

## The Sarkaria Commission and the Council's establishment

The Inter-State Council was not established until 1990 — forty years after the Constitution came into force — because successive Central governments saw no political advantage in creating a structured forum where all Chief Ministers could collectively challenge or scrutinise Union policy. It took an independent commission to force the issue.

The Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1983–88), chaired by Justice R.S. Sarkaria, was established by the Rajiv Gandhi government and reported in 1988. The Commission's central recommendation on the Inter-State Council was unambiguous: it should be established without further delay, as a body with a professional secretariat, regular annual meetings, and a clear agenda process. The Commission found that the absence of such a forum had forced Centre-State disagreements into either bilateral executive negotiations (inherently asymmetric given the Union's fiscal dominance) or into the Supreme Court, where constitutional disputes hardened into adversarial positions that could have been resolved cooperatively.

Acting on the Sarkaria Commission's recommendation, the Inter-State Council was established by Presidential Order dated 28 May 1990 under the then V.P. Singh government. The Presidential Order defined the Council's composition: the Prime Minister as Chairman; all Chief Ministers of States; Chief Ministers of Union Territories with Legislatures (Delhi, Puducherry, Jammu and Kashmir); Administrators of Union Territories without Legislatures; and six Cabinet Ministers of the Union nominated by the Prime Minister. A Standing Committee processes matters before they reach the full Council. A small Secretariat provides technical support.

The Council met twelve times between 1990 and 2016. It considered the Sarkaria Commission recommendations themselves, internal security issues, Centre-State financial relations, and various policy coordination matters. Its work in the 1990s produced several recommendations on Centre-State relations that informed subsequent legislative and administrative reforms. After 2002 it lapsed into extended dormancy. A reconstituted Standing Committee was revived around 2022 to process pending recommendations from the Punchhi Commission, and Council meetings were revived in this period.

## The Punchhi Commission

The Punchhi Commission on Centre-State Relations (2007–10), chaired by Justice Madan Mohan Punchhi, was the second major systematic review of Centre-State relations after Sarkaria. Its recommendations on the Inter-State Council were more ambitious than Sarkaria's:

- Annual plenary meetings of the Council should be constitutionally mandated rather than discretionary.
- The Council should have a formal pre-legislative consultation role: Central Bills that substantially affect State subjects or State administration should be tabled before the Council before introduction in Parliament.
- The Council Secretariat should be professionalised and adequately staffed.
- A monitoring mechanism should be established to track the implementation of Council recommendations.
- The Council should be given a formal first-step role in Centre-State disputes before parties resort to Article 131 suits before the Supreme Court.

Most Punchhi Commission recommendations on the Inter-State Council have not been implemented, but they continue to be cited in academic and political debates about cooperative federalism.

## Key cases

**Kuldip Nayar v. Union of India (2006) 7 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court, while addressing election law questions about the qualification of voters for Rajya Sabha elections, affirmed the constitutional legitimacy of the Inter-State Council as a forum for cooperative federal deliberation. The Court noted that the mechanism of a Council under Article 263 reflects the Constitution's recognition that federalism in India cannot operate on a purely adversarial basis — structured consultation between the Union and States on matters of common interest is constitutionally envisaged and should be actively used.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — Although decided before the Council was established, this case's extensive treatment of the cooperative dimensions of Indian federalism provides the judicial backdrop for understanding Article 263. The Court observed that the Constitution's federal design envisages coordination mechanisms between the Centre and States that go beyond the adversarial framework of Articles 131 and 356 — Article 263 is the constitutional vessel for such coordination. The judgment is frequently cited in discussions about using the Inter-State Council to address Centre-State tensions before they escalate to Article 356 actions.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The Court's landmark nine-judge judgment on the scope and limits of President's Rule under Article 356 implicitly reinforced the constitutional case for active use of the Article 263 mechanism. Multiple judges observed that the political tensions between the Centre and Opposition-governed States that had driven excessive use of Article 356 could have been addressed through proper use of the Inter-State Council's advisory and coordination functions. The judgment's insistence on exhausting constitutional compliance mechanisms before resorting to President's Rule applies equally to the Inter-State Council's role in political dispute resolution.

**State of West Bengal v. Committee for Protection of Democratic Rights (2010) 3 SCC 571** — While not directly involving Article 263, the Court's discussion of federal democracy and cooperative governance noted that constitutional institutions established for inter-governmental coordination — including the Inter-State Council — should be used as mechanisms for managing the tension between Union and State authority. The judgment reinforced the principle that cooperative federalism is constitutionally preferred over adversarial federalism as a mode of governance.

## Article 263 and the National Development Council

The National Development Council (NDC), established in 1952 under an executive resolution, predated the Inter-State Council and served as the primary forum for Union-State consultation on planning and development. The NDC brought together the Prime Minister, Union Cabinet Ministers, and all Chief Ministers to consider the national plan, resource allocation, and development policy. The NDC was not a creature of Article 263 — it was established by executive order without constitutional grounding — but it effectively served the Article 263(b) and (c) functions for planning-related matters.

With the dissolution of the Planning Commission and its replacement by NITI Aayog in 2015, and the effective discontinuation of NDC meetings, the Inter-State Council has become more prominent as the only constitutionally grounded forum for Union-State consultation. NITI Aayog's Governing Council (chaired by the Prime Minister, attended by all Chief Ministers) has taken on some NDC functions, but it operates under an executive mandate rather than a constitutional one. Article 263 thus retains its constitutional significance as the provision for the only constitutionally authorised inter-governmental forum.

## Current significance

The Inter-State Council's record of infrequent meetings reflects a structural problem in Indian cooperative federalism: the institution depends entirely on the political willingness of the Union government to convene it, and the Union government often has no political incentive to create a forum where all Opposition Chief Ministers can collectively challenge Central policies. The Council met only twelve times between 1990 and 2016. Extended periods of dormancy — particularly from 2016 to 2022 — coincided with periods of significant Centre-State tension over fiscal transfers, Governors' conduct, the CAA-NRC controversy, farm laws, and the GST framework.

Reform proposals consistently return to the same cluster of ideas: mandatory annual meetings, a professional secretariat with independent research capacity, a formal pre-legislative consultation role, and a monitoring mechanism for implementation. More ambitious proposals — including those from the Punchhi Commission — would give the Council a formal first-step role in Centre-State disputes before litigation before the Supreme Court. None of these proposals has been legislated. Article 263 remains enabling and discretionary, which means the Inter-State Council is only as effective as the political will of the Prime Minister who chairs it.

The GST Council — created under Article 279A by the 101st Constitutional Amendment Act (2016) — is the most significant cooperative federalism institution in contemporary Indian governance. Unlike the Inter-State Council, the GST Council is constitutionally mandated (not discretionary), meets regularly, has a professional secretariat, and has binding decision-making procedures. The Supreme Court's ruling in Union of India v. Mohit Minerals (2022) — that GST Council recommendations are not binding but have persuasive weight — has put the constitutional design of the Council under renewed scrutiny. Comparative analysis of the GST Council's effectiveness against the Inter-State Council's dormancy provides the clearest illustration of the gap between the discretionary design of Article 263 and the demands of cooperative federalism in practice.
