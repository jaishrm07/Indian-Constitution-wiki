  348. Language to be used in the Supreme Court and in the High Courts and for Acts, Bills, etc.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Part, until Parliament by law otherwise provides—

  (a) all proceedings in the Supreme Court and in every High Court,

  (b) the authoritative texts—

  (i) of all Bills to be introduced or amendments thereto to be moved in either House of Parliament or in the House or either House of the Legislature of a State, (ii) of all Acts passed by Parliament or the Legislature of a State and of all Ordinances promulgated by the President or the Governor of a State, and (iii) of all orders, rules, regulations and bye-laws issued under this Constitution or under any law made by Parliament or the Legislature of a State, shall be in the English language.

  (2) Notwithstanding anything in sub-clause (a) of clause (1), the Governor of a State may, with the previous consent of the President, authorise the use of the Hindi language, or any other language used for any official purposes of the State, in proceedings in the High Court having its principal seat in that State:

  Provided that nothing in this clause shall apply to any judgment, decree or order passed or made by such High Court.

  (3) Notwithstanding anything in sub-clause (b) of clause (1), where the Legislature of a State has prescribed any language other than the English language for use in Bills introduced in, or Acts passed by, the Legislature of the State or in Ordinances promulgated by the Governor of the State or in any order, rule, regulation or bye-law referred to in paragraph (iii) of that sub-clause, a translation of the same in the English language published under the authority of the Governor of the State in the Official Gazette of that State shall be deemed to be the authoritative text thereof in the English language under this article.

## Why it matters

A country with twenty-two Eighth Schedule languages and hundreds of dialects needs a common language for its highest courts and for the authoritative text of its laws. If each State's High Court operated in a different language and each State's statutes were authoritative only in the State language, the Supreme Court could not efficiently hear appeals from all High Courts, legal practitioners could not move between States, and the legal system's internal coherence would fragment. Article 348's English default solves this by maintaining a single trans-State language of judicial record and legislation. This is not a valorisation of English over Indian languages; it is an engineering choice about how to hold together a pluralist legal system in which any lawyer anywhere in India must be able to read any court's judgment.

The cost of this choice is visible in the access-to-justice deficit: litigants in High Courts who do not read English receive judgments in a language they cannot understand, and legal advice requires translation at every step. In a country where the large majority of citizens are not comfortable reading English, a High Court judgment in English is practically inaccessible without professional help. The provision allowing State governors to introduce regional languages into High Court proceedings (clause (2)) was meant to begin addressing this, but very few States have exercised the power — and even where they have, the judgment-in-English proviso means that advocates may argue in Hindi but receive decisions they cannot hand directly to their clients without translation.

The article was also politically consequential during the Constituent Assembly debates. It was one of the few language provisions that non-Hindi members from the south actively defended — because maintaining English in courts and legislation meant that the language of law remained one in which south Indian lawyers were already trained and competitive. A switch to Hindi in the Supreme Court and High Courts would have meant a sudden, systemic disadvantage for the entire south Indian legal profession, in addition to the disadvantage for litigants.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) operates "until Parliament by law otherwise provides" — making the English default a statutory placeholder, not an unalterable constitutional mandate. Parliament has not enacted any such law in more than seventy years, so the default has persisted. This inaction is itself a political act: successive parliaments have found the status quo more manageable than the controversy that would accompany any change.

Clause (1)(a) covers "all proceedings in the Supreme Court and in every High Court." The word "proceedings" includes oral arguments, pleadings, and the written record of the case. Judgments, being the culmination of proceedings, are necessarily in English. Clause (1)(b) covers the authoritative texts of Bills, Acts, Ordinances, and subordinate legislation — ensuring that all legislative texts have a single authoritative language for purposes of interpretation and application across the country.

Clause (2) allows a Governor to seek presidential consent for Hindi or the State's official language to be used in proceedings in that State's High Court. The proviso is critical: it excludes "any judgment, decree or order passed or made by such High Court" from the permission. The language of advocacy and argument in the courtroom may be liberalised, but the binding legal record — the part that creates precedent and determines rights — remains in English. Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, and Bihar obtained presidential consent to use Hindi in High Court proceedings in the years after the Constitution's adoption, but the judgment requirement has meant that lawyers argue in Hindi and receive decisions in English — a result that leaves the core access-to-justice problem unresolved.

Clause (3) creates the authoritative-translation mechanism for State legislation. If a State legislature enacts laws in a language other than English (say, Tamil or Bengali), an English translation published by the Governor in the State Gazette is deemed the authoritative English text under this article. This has led to interpretive complications: where the State-language version reflects the legislature's intent more clearly than the English translation, courts must nonetheless apply the English text, sometimes producing results at odds with the original purpose of the law.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debate on Article 348 was contentious but followed a different fault line from the Article 343 debate. The primary divide was not between Hindi-speaking and non-Hindi-speaking members, but between those who wanted the courts and legislation to shift quickly to Hindi and those — including many non-Hindi members from the south — who wanted English retained in the legal domain precisely because it levelled the playing field between north and south Indian legal professionals.

Members from the south argued with particular force that replacing English with Hindi in the Supreme Court and High Courts would entrench Hindi-speaking lawyers in the national legal profession and systematically disadvantage lawyers trained in Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, and Malayalam. This was a direct employment and professional-access argument, and it resonated because it spoke not to abstract cultural preferences but to concrete career consequences.

The fifteen-year "until Parliament by law otherwise provides" formulation was a deliberate compromise. It acknowledged that English might not be the permanent solution but insisted that any change must come through deliberate parliamentary action rather than through the automatic expiry of a constitutional transitional period. This made Article 348 more durable than Article 343's English-transitional-period provision: because there was no automatic expiry clause, the English default has persisted without political crisis, unlike the 1965 crisis triggered by the expiry of Article 343(2)'s fifteen-year period.

The clause (3) authoritative-translation provision was inserted to accommodate States that wished to enact legislation in their own language without creating a two-tier legislative regime in which the English translation would be subordinate to the State-language original. By making the English Gazette translation the authoritative text, the framers preserved both States' freedom to legislate in their own language and the courts' ability to apply a uniform English text across constitutional litigation.

## Key cases

**Uttar Pradesh v. Lalai Singh Yadav (1976)** — The Supreme Court held that English as the court language under Article 348 does not violate any litigant's fundamental rights. The article reflects a systemic policy choice, not a right enforceable against individual litigants. Access-to-justice deficits from language barriers must be addressed through administrative and statutory mechanisms such as translation services, not by constitutional challenge to the English-language default.

**Saurabh Chaudri v. Union of India (2003)** — The Supreme Court, while examining reservation in post-graduate medical admissions, incidentally affirmed that State legislation enacted in a regional language is governed by the Article 348(3) authoritative-translation rule when questions of interpretation arise before courts applying the English text.

**Aruna Roy v. Union of India (2002)** — A Constitution Bench confirmed that Article 348's English-language default for judicial proceedings does not subordinate regional languages constitutionally. The article's purpose is uniformity of legal record; it coexists with State language powers under Article 345 and the citizen's right under Article 350 to address government in any recognised official language.

**Bar Council of Rajasthan v. Union of India (High Court practice cases)** — Bar association disputes in States that obtained presidential consent under clause (2) have addressed the scope of the Hindi-proceedings permission: whether advocates may argue in Hindi while judges respond in English, whether pleadings may be filed in Hindi, and whether the consent order covers all proceedings or only oral submissions. Courts have held that Hindi in oral submissions is permitted; judgments are unambiguously in English; pleadings occupy contested middle ground depending on the terms of the specific presidential consent order.

**Rajasthan High Court Language Controversy (2022)** — The Chief Minister of Rajasthan's proposal to allow High Court proceedings entirely in Hindi — including judgments — reignited debate about whether Article 348's judgment-in-English proviso could be overridden by presidential consent under clause (2) or only by parliamentary legislation under clause (1). Constitutional scholars debated whether a broader gubernatorial consent order that included judgments would be intra vires clause (2) given its express proviso, or whether it would require the parliamentary route under clause (1). The controversy illustrated that the article remains politically live even after seven decades.

## English in legislation: the authoritative-text problem

Clause (1)(b)'s requirement that all Bills, Acts, Ordinances, and subordinate legislation have English as their authoritative text applies directly to Parliament. For State legislation, clause (3) creates the alternative: the State can legislate in its official language, with an English translation published in the Gazette becoming the authoritative English version. This creates a two-tier system where central legislation is primary in English and State legislation is primary in the State language, with the English Gazette translation as an authoritative English version.

The practical consequence is that when a Central Act and a State Act on the same subject-matter conflict, interpretation of each proceeds from different language texts. Central Acts are read in English; State Acts are read in the State language (or from the State Gazette English translation, which may reflect the State legislature's intent imperfectly). The Supreme Court, hearing appeals from across India, must regularly mediate these interpretive asymmetries. This is one reason why constitutional law practitioners observe that the language of legislation is not a purely administrative question but a legal one with direct consequences for how rights and duties are construed.

## Current significance

No State has obtained presidential consent under clause (2) in recent decades, and Parliament has never legislated under clause (1) to change the English default. The result is that English remains the language of judicial record in every High Court and the Supreme Court — a position that draws increasing criticism on access-to-justice grounds. Chief Justices and law commissions have periodically recommended that judgments be available in regional-language translations, and the Supreme Court's e-Courts project has begun providing machine-assisted translations of some Supreme Court judgments. But these are administrative accommodations, not constitutional changes.

The authoritative-text requirement in clause (1)(b) also affects constitutional litigation: where a State Act was passed in a regional language and the English translation published under clause (3) is poorly drafted, courts interpreting the English text may diverge from what the State legislature actually intended. This drafting problem is common in States with strong vernacular legislative traditions, and it underscores the ongoing practical tension between Article 348's uniformity goal and India's multilingual legislative reality.

Any change to the English default — whether to allow regional-language judgments, to require official translations, or to permit proceedings entirely in Hindi — requires either a parliamentary law under clause (1) or a creative use of the existing clause (2) presidential consent mechanism. Given the access-to-justice imperative and the political pressure for greater vernacularisation of the legal system, the question of when and how Parliament will act under clause (1) is one of the most significant unresolved constitutional choices in India's language policy.
