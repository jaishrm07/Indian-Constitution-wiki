## Why it matters

Article 342A is the constitutional foundation of the OBC reservation framework. Before the 102nd Amendment (2018), OBC identification had no constitutional basis beyond the general language of Articles 15(4) and 16(4) — the power to make special provisions for "socially and educationally backward classes." The OBC list was maintained by Central and State governments as a matter of executive and statutory practice, governed principally by the Mandal Commission report of 1980 and the subsequent judgments in Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992). By inserting Article 342A, Parliament elevated OBC identification to the same constitutional plane as SC and ST identification under Articles 341 and 342, giving the Central OBC list a constitutional anchor while simultaneously triggering one of the most significant inter-governmental disputes over reservation powers in recent constitutional history.

The 102nd Amendment package was ambitious: it simultaneously inserted Article 342A (OBC list), Article 338B (National Commission for Backward Classes as a constitutional body), and Article 366(26C) (defining "socially and educationally backward classes" by reference to Article 342A). The intention was to give OBC identification the same constitutional permanence and parliamentary accountability that SC and ST identification had under Articles 341-342. The drafting, however, created a crisis: by closely paralleling the SC/ST model — in which only Parliament can alter the Presidential notification — the 102nd Amendment text was read by State governments and later by the Supreme Court as having stripped States of their long-established power to identify OBCs for State service reservations and State educational institution reservations.

The 105th Amendment (2021) was Parliament's remedial response. It preserved the Central List structure while expressly confirming State power to maintain separate OBC lists by law. The result is a constitutional architecture that is more complex than either the pre-2018 practice or the originally intended post-2018 structure: a dual-list system in which the Central List (for Central Government purposes) and State lists (for State purposes) coexist and may diverge. This dual-list structure has significant consequences for communities seeking OBC status, students applying for reservations across State lines, and policymakers managing the total quantum of reservations within the Indra Sawhney fifty per cent ceiling.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the Presidential Central List:** The President may, with respect to any State or Union territory (consulting the Governor for States), specify by public notification the socially and educationally backward classes that shall be in the Central List "for the purposes of the Central Government." The phrase "for the purposes of the Central Government" was inserted by the 105th Amendment and is critical: it confines the Presidential notification and its Central List to Central Government reservations — Central government services and Central educational institutions. It does not purport to determine OBC status for State purposes.

**Clause (2) — Parliament's exclusive amendment power for the Central List:** Once the President specifies a community in the Central List, only Parliament can alter the notification by including or excluding communities. No subsequent Presidential executive notification can vary the list. This mirrors the Article 341/342 structure and is designed to ensure that inclusions and exclusions from the Central List are politically visible and subject to democratic scrutiny rather than made by executive fiat. The Explanation (added with the 105th Amendment) defines "Central List" as the list prepared and maintained "by and for the Central Government" — reinforcing the confined scope.

**Clause (3) — State lists preserved:** Notwithstanding clauses (1) and (2), every State or Union territory may by law prepare and maintain, for its own purposes, a list of socially and educationally backward classes with entries different from the Central List. This clause was the 105th Amendment's principal substantive contribution. It operates as a carve-out from the Central List structure, confirming that State legislatures retain power to identify OBCs for State reservations. The requirement that State lists be maintained "by law" — that is, by State legislative enactment — represents a modest procedural safeguard: States cannot maintain OBC lists purely by executive order; the list requires a statutory basis at the State level.

**The terminology question — SEBC vs OBC:** Article 342A uses the phrase "socially and educationally backward classes" (SEBCs), which is the constitutional term also used in Articles 15(4) and 16(4). The everyday administrative and political term is "Other Backward Classes" (OBCs). The two terms are substantively overlapping but not identical: "SEBC" is the broader constitutional category, while "OBC" has a more specific administrative meaning rooted in the Mandal Commission framework of caste-based backwardness criteria. Article 366(26C), inserted by the 102nd Amendment, defines SEBCs as the classes specified in the Article 342A notification for the Central Government and as determined by State law for State purposes — a circular definition that has generated interpretive complexity in litigation.

## The 102nd Amendment — intent and effect

The Constitution (One Hundred and Second Amendment) Act, 2018, received Presidential assent on 11 August 2018. Its stated objective was to give constitutional status to OBC identification, modelled on the SC/ST framework. The National Commission for Backward Classes, previously a statutory body under the National Commission for Backward Classes Act, 1993, was elevated to a constitutional commission under the new Article 338B. The 102nd Amendment was enacted against a background in which several State governments had been expanding their OBC lists in ways that the Central government considered poorly scrutinised, and there was a political consensus that the OBC framework needed greater constitutional anchoring.

The crisis arose because the 102nd Amendment's language for Article 342A closely followed Articles 341 and 342 without explicitly preserving State power. States with large OBC constituencies — Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Bihar — read the new article as transferring OBC identification to the Centre exclusively. Tamil Nadu in particular was alarmed because its OBC list, which covers approximately sixty-nine per cent of the State's population, was significantly broader than the Central List. The Union government took the position that Article 342A had not stripped State power because States could still exercise power under Articles 15(4) and 16(4) independently of the Central List — a position the Supreme Court rejected in Jaishri Patil.

## The Jaishri Patil judgment and the 105th Amendment

**Maratha Reservation — Jaishri Patil v Chief Minister, Maharashtra (2021)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court decided two issues in this case. The first was the constitutional validity of Maharashtra's legislation providing sixteen per cent reservation for Marathas, above the fifty per cent ceiling established in Indra Sawhney. The Court struck down the Maratha reservation as violating the Indra Sawhney ceiling — there were no "extraordinary circumstances" justifying a breach of the fifty per cent limit. The second issue was whether the 102nd Amendment had stripped State governments of their power to identify OBCs. The Court, by a majority of three to two, held that the original Article 342A text did strip States of this power: after the 102nd Amendment, only the President could specify OBCs for both Central and State purposes, and the States' pre-existing OBC identification powers under Articles 15(4) and 16(4) were superseded. This holding was a direct trigger for the 105th Amendment.

Parliament enacted the Constitution (One Hundred and Fifth Amendment) Act, 2021, within weeks of the Jaishri Patil judgment. The 105th Amendment: (a) inserted the phrase "for the purposes of the Central Government" in clause (1); (b) inserted the Explanation defining "Central List"; and (c) inserted the new clause (3) expressly preserving State list power. The legislative intent was to neutralise the Supreme Court's majority holding by amending the constitutional text itself. The 105th Amendment was passed with broad cross-party support, reflecting the political consensus that the Jaishri Patil majority's reading had been contrary to the original legislative intent of the 102nd Amendment.

## Key cases

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — The foundational Mandal Commission case. A nine-judge Constitution Bench upheld 27 per cent OBC reservation under Article 16(4) but imposed the fifty per cent ceiling on total reservations (combining SC, ST, and OBC quotas). The Court also required the "creamy layer" exclusion — the more economically and socially advanced sections of backward classes must be excluded from OBC reservation benefits. Indra Sawhney established that the Backward Classes Commission's recommendations are relevant material for identifying SEBCs but are not binding on the government. The judgment's fifty per cent ceiling and creamy-layer doctrine directly govern the operation of Article 342A's Central List.

**Jaishri Patil v Chief Minister, Maharashtra (2021)** — The five-judge Bench struck down Maharashtra's Maratha reservation as exceeding the fifty per cent ceiling. More significantly for Article 342A, the majority held that the 102nd Amendment had vested exclusive OBC-identification power in the Centre, effectively stripping States of their power to maintain OBC lists. The dissenting opinion (Bhat J and Nageswara Rao J) held that the 102nd Amendment had not displaced State power under Articles 15(4) and 16(4). The majority reading was effectively overruled by the 105th Amendment's text.

**Pattali Makkal Katchi v Union of India (2021)** — Tamil Nadu's challenge to the 102nd Amendment reached the Supreme Court. The proceedings, decided alongside Jaishri Patil, produced the same constitutional ruling. Tamil Nadu had argued that its sixty-nine per cent OBC quota (implemented under a 1994 law protected by the Ninth Schedule) would be undermined if the Central government controlled State OBC identification. After the 105th Amendment, Tamil Nadu's State OBC list continues to operate independently under clause (3).

**Janhit Abhiyan v Union of India (2022)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench (three-to-two majority) upheld the Constitution (One Hundred and Third Amendment) Act, 2019, which inserted Articles 15(6) and 16(6) providing ten per cent reservation for Economically Weaker Sections (EWS) among the general category. The 103rd Amendment added a separate constitutional category that partially overlaps with the SEBC framework — EWS reservation is available to persons not already covered by SC, ST, or OBC reservations. While Article 342A was not at issue, the EWS reservation effectively pushes total reservations to sixty per cent in Central establishments (50% SC/ST/OBC + 10% EWS), straining the Indra Sawhney ceiling's application. The majority held that EWS reservation was consistent with the Basic Structure; the dissent (Ravindra Bhat J and Trivedi J) held it violated equality norms.

**Maharashtra v Vijay Ghogre (2023)** — Following the 105th Amendment, the Bombay High Court addressed whether Maharashtra's OBC legislation under the new clause (3) framework was valid. The court confirmed that State OBC lists maintained under State law pursuant to clause (3) operate independently of the Central List and that a community on the State list but not the Central List validly receives reservation in State government services and State educational institutions even absent Central recognition.

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024)** — While decided primarily for Scheduled Castes, the seven-judge Constitution Bench's ruling authorising sub-classification within reserved categories is likely to extend to OBCs under Article 342A. The Court held that States may sub-classify SC and ST lists to direct reservation benefits preferentially to the most backward sub-groups. OBC communities, which are even more diverse in their relative backwardness than SC or ST communities, are strong candidates for sub-classification legislation at both Central and State levels. The National Commission for Backward Classes has been asked to examine whether Davinder Singh principles apply to the Central OBC List.

## Interaction with the Indra Sawhney fifty per cent ceiling

The fifty per cent ceiling on reservations established by Indra Sawhney (1992) applies to the combined quota for SCs, STs, and OBCs. At the Central level, this means that the 27 per cent OBC quota (plus 15 per cent SC and 7.5 per cent ST) already reaches 49.5 per cent — essentially the full permissible limit. The 103rd Amendment's ten per cent EWS reservation technically breaches this ceiling but was upheld in Janhit Abhiyan as a permissible amendment because EWS reservation operates as a separate category carved out of the general (unreserved) seats rather than adding to the SC/ST/OBC quantum.

The Indra Sawhney ceiling creates a zero-sum dynamic in OBC politics: a newly included community in the Central List can only benefit if it comes within the existing twenty-seven per cent OBC quota, which means it competes with already-listed communities. Politically dominant communities seeking Central List inclusion therefore face intense resistance from communities already listed, who fear dilution of their quota share. This dynamic explains the long delays in processing Central List inclusion claims and the political pressure for sub-classification — breaking the OBC quota into sub-quotas for specific communities is one way to guarantee that newly included or historically marginalised communities actually receive reservation benefits rather than being crowded out by larger, more organised OBC communities.

## Current significance

The dual-list architecture created by the 105th Amendment has produced a complex inter-governmental landscape for OBC reservation. Major States — Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Bihar, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana — maintain OBC lists significantly different from the Central List. The same community may be OBC for State civil service and State university admission purposes (under the State list) but not for Central civil service and Central university admission purposes (if absent from the Central List). For communities that aspire to Central government employment or admission to Central universities, inclusion in the Central List — and Parliamentary legislation to secure that inclusion — is essential, and the process is slow and politically contested.

The National Commission for Backward Classes, now constituted as a constitutional body under Article 338B, performs an advisory function with respect to the Central List. The Commission examines inclusion and exclusion petitions, conducts surveys, and makes recommendations to the Central government and Parliament. However, its recommendations are advisory, not binding, and Parliament's exclusive amendment power under clause (2) means that even a positive NCBC recommendation cannot translate into Central List inclusion without parliamentary action. The backlog of pending inclusion petitions at the NCBC is significant, mirroring the ST list backlog under Article 342.

The interaction of Article 342A with the sub-classification question (post-Davinder Singh, 2024), the EWS reservation upheld in Janhit Abhiyan, and the creamy-layer exclusion requirements of Indra Sawhney makes OBC reservation one of the most layered and contested fields in Indian constitutional law. The 102nd Amendment's constitutional elevation of the OBC framework was intended to bring clarity; the subsequent judicial and legislative developments have instead produced a multi-tiered, frequently litigated regime in which the allocation of reservation benefits is determined by an interplay of Presidential notification, parliamentary legislation, State law, and Supreme Court doctrine that was not fully anticipated when Article 342A was inserted.
