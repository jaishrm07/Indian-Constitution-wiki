  233. Appointment of district judges.—(1) Appointments of persons to be, and the posting and promotion of, district judges in any State shall be made by the Governor of the State in consultation with the High Court exercising jurisdiction in relation to such State.

  (2) A person not already in the service of the Union or of the State shall only be eligible to be appointed a district judge if he has been for not less than seven years an advocate or a pleader and is recommended by the High Court for appointment.

## Why it matters

District judges are the principal fact-finders for the vast majority of Indian
litigation. More than forty million cases are pending in district and subordinate
courts. The district judge is the trial judge for civil suits, the sessions
judge for serious criminal cases, and the first court of appeal from the
magistracy and the subordinate civil judiciary. Their independence from state
government pressure is therefore a constitutional necessity, not a luxury.

Article 233 protects that independence by requiring the High Court's
participation in every appointment, posting, and promotion at this level — the
High Court being the constitutionally designated supervisor of the subordinate
judiciary under Article 235. If the Governor could appoint any district judge
without meaningful High Court input, the state government would have an
unmediated path to placing politically convenient individuals in the most
consequential positions in the trial judiciary. The consultation requirement
is the constitutional barrier to that outcome.

## Reading the provision

Article 233(1) covers three distinct decisions: appointment, posting, and
promotion of district judges. All three require the Governor to act in
consultation with the High Court. The article does not say the Governor must
follow the High Court's recommendation — it says consultation — but the Supreme
Court has held that consultation means effective consultation: the High Court's
views must be genuinely considered and given real weight, and departure from a
High Court recommendation requires cogent reasons.

Article 233(2) creates a separate eligibility requirement for persons not
already in State or Union service — the "direct recruit" route to district
judgeship. Such a person must have been an advocate or pleader for at least
seven years and must be recommended by the High Court. Clause (2) requires the
High Court's recommendation as a condition of eligibility, not just as an
element of consultation. A direct recruit who is not recommended by the High
Court cannot be appointed at all, regardless of the Governor's preference.

The article draws a constitutional line between "district judges" — governed by
Article 233 — and other persons in the judicial service below district judge
level, who are governed by Article 234. This two-tier framework reflects a
constitutional judgment that the most senior positions in the subordinate
judiciary require the highest level of High Court involvement, while positions
further down the hierarchy can be filled through a competitive examination-based
process under the High Court's control.

## Key cases

**Chandra Mohan v State of Uttar Pradesh (1967)** — The Supreme Court held that
"consultation" in Article 233 means effective consultation, not a formality.
The High Court's views must be genuinely sought and genuinely considered. The
Governor cannot simply inform the High Court of a decision already made. If the
Governor departs from the High Court's recommendation, there must be compelling
reasons. This remains the foundational statement on the meaning of consultation
in the context of subordinate judicial appointments.

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court reinforced that where
consultation with the High Court is constitutionally required for judicial
appointments under Article 233, the constitutional mandate of genuine
consultation must be observed regardless of the formal ministerial advice
mechanism. The Governor acts on ministerial advice for most functions, but the
quality of consultation required by Article 233 is not diminished by that
general rule.

**State of Haryana v Inder Prakash Anand (1976)** — The Court clarified the
scope of Article 233 in relation to members of the State judicial service who
are promoted to district judge level. The consultation requirement applies to
such promotions, and the High Court's role in the promotion process is
constitutionally protected against state government attempts to sideline it.

**All India Judges Association v Union of India (2002)** — The Supreme Court
used the Article 233 framework as a starting point for a comprehensive review of
subordinate judicial service conditions. The Court directed state governments
to fill district court vacancies expeditiously, treating chronic understaffing
as a constitutional failure that implicates the right of citizens to access an
effective judiciary.

## Current significance

Disputes between state governments and High Courts over specific district judge
appointments — including promotions passed over in favour of more junior
candidates — have led to litigation in several High Courts. Courts have been
willing to intervene when the consultation process was bypassed or when the
Governor departed from the High Court's recommendation without explanation,
treating such departures as constitutional violations rather than merely
irregular administrative decisions.

The seven-year advocacy requirement for direct recruits under Article 233(2)
has been interpreted strictly. Persons who have experience only in non-advocacy
legal roles — such as government law officers, academic lawyers, or tribunal
members — do not automatically satisfy the requirement unless they have also
practised as advocates for the required period. This has limited the direct
recruitment route in practice and has made promotion from the judicial service
the dominant pathway to district judgeship in most states.

The relationship between Article 233 and Articles 234 and 235 forms the complete
constitutional architecture for the subordinate judiciary. Article 234 governs
recruitment to judicial service positions below district judge, Article 235
vests control over subordinate judicial officers in the High Court, and Article
233 governs the apex tier of the subordinate judiciary. Together they create a
constitutionally insulated system that keeps the subordinate judiciary out of
direct executive control while retaining the Governor's formal appointment power
as the constitutional link between the state government and the judicial system.
