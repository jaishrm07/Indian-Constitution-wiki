plainEnglish: >-
  Article 224A, inserted in 1963, allows a High Court to call back retired judges — either from that court or any other High Court — to sit and decide cases when the court needs extra judicial strength beyond what it can staff through ordinary appointments. The process requires a reference from the Chief Justice of the High Court, the consent of the appointing authority, and — crucially — the individual consent of the retired judge, who cannot be compelled to serve. While sitting, the recalled judge enjoys all the jurisdiction and powers of a sitting judge but does not hold permanent office or acquire additional pension entitlement. With High Courts collectively carrying tens of millions of pending cases and permanent judicial vacancies running at 30 percent or more of sanctioned strength in some jurisdictions, the provision has attracted renewed attention as a constitutionally authorised emergency measure — but its practical impact has been limited by inadequate allowances, logistical barriers, and the reluctance of many High Court Chief Justices to invoke it.
officialText: |-
  [224A. Appointment of retired Judges at sittings of High Courts.—

  Notwithstanding anything in this Chapter, [the National Judicial Appointments Commission on a reference made to it by the Chief Justice of a High Court for any State, may with the previous consent of the President], request any person who has held the office of a Judge of that Court or of any other High Court to sit and act as a Judge of the High Court for that State, and every such person so requested shall, while so sitting and acting, be entitled to such allowances as the President may by order determine and have all the jurisdiction, powers and privileges of, but shall not otherwise be deemed to be, a Judge of that High Court:

  Provided that nothing in this article shall be deemed to require any such person as aforesaid to sit and act as a Judge of that High Court unless he consents so to do.]

## Why it matters

Article 224A was inserted by the Constitution (Fifteenth Amendment) Act, 1963, to address a simple but recurring problem — High Courts periodically face backlogs or vacancy crises that the ordinary appointment cycle is too slow to resolve. The article provides a constitutionally authorised mechanism to draw on the experience of retired judges who still have the capacity and willingness to hear cases without requiring the full nomination-and-appointment procedure that governs permanent appointments under Article 217.

The provision is conceptually distinct from Article 224, which addresses the appointment of additional judges — new appointees serving a fixed term — and from Article 217, which governs permanent appointments. Article 224A targets already-retired individuals. The critical constitutional feature is the double-consent structure: the appointing authority must agree to make the request, and the retired judge must individually consent to accept it. No retired judge can be pressed into service; the article is an invitation mechanism, not a conscription one.

The distinction "shall not otherwise be deemed to be a Judge of that High Court" is carefully chosen. A judge recalled under Article 224A exercises full judicial powers — their orders are as valid as those of a permanent judge, their participation in a bench counts for quorum, and their judgments bind the parties. But they do not thereby resume judicial office in the permanent or pensionable sense. They are not treated as re-appointed judges for service benefit calculations; they receive the allowance determined by the President's order, not a salary. This structure allows the article to function without triggering the full constitutional apparatus of appointment, security of tenure, and pension that governs regular judgeships.

## Reading the provision

The mechanism operates through a chain of institutional steps. The Chief Justice of the High Court must first make a reference — indicating that the court needs additional judicial strength and identifying a suitable retired judge. The appointing authority (the President, acting on the advice of the appropriate constitutional actors; or under the amended text, the NJAC) must consent to the request being made. Only then is the retired judge approached, and even at that stage their consent is required.

The proviso — "nothing in this article shall be deemed to require any such person as aforesaid to sit and act as a Judge of that High Court unless he consents so to do" — is not merely a formality. Retired judges who have resumed private practice or taken up arbitration engagements may decline. Those in poor health may decline. Those who object to the terms of the allowance order may decline. The article does not create any duty in the retired judge; it creates a power in the institution to invite, subject to the retired judge's acceptance.

The allowance payable is determined by the President by order. This executive determination of the financial terms has practical consequences — if the allowance is perceived as inadequate relative to the income a retired judge can earn from arbitration practice or legal consulting, fewer retired judges will accept the invitation. The allowance has historically been criticised as insufficiently competitive, contributing to the article's underuse.

The reference to the National Judicial Appointments Commission in the current text reflects the constitutional amendment made in 2014. As with Article 224, the NJAC's invalidation by the Supreme Court in 2015 means that the collegium process applies in practice to Article 224A references as well, even though the constitutional text nominally refers to the NJAC.

## Key cases

**Lok Prahari v. Union of India (2021)** — The Supreme Court in suo motu proceedings on judicial vacancies and delays delivered one of the most significant judicial statements on Article 224A. The Court noted that the article had been invoked extremely rarely relative to the scale of pendency across High Courts, and that there was no constitutional or practical bar to its wider use. The Court directed High Court Chief Justices to actively consider making references under Article 224A, particularly for benches dedicated to disposing of long-pending matters and cases of elderly or infirm litigants. The judgment effectively reactivated constitutional attention to an article that had languished in disuse for decades.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015) (Fourth Judges Case / NJAC Case)** — The Court's striking down of the NJAC and its reaffirmation of the collegium system has indirect consequences for Article 224A, since the article's text now nominally refers to the NJAC as the appointing authority. In practice, references for Article 224A appointments are processed through the collegium mechanism, consistent with the Court's holding that the NJAC amendment was constitutionally invalid.

**All India Judges Association v. Union of India (various monitoring proceedings)** — The Supreme Court's continuing monitoring jurisdiction over judicial vacancies and infrastructure has periodically brought Article 224A into focus as an under-utilised tool. The Court has noted that States with High Courts carrying the heaviest backlogs should be examining the article as a short-term intervention pending the completion of the permanent appointment process.

## The vacancy crisis and Article 224A's untapped potential

India's High Courts have faced persistent and severe vacancy crises throughout the post-Constitution period, but the problem reached acute dimensions in the 2010s. Sanctioned strength across all High Courts was approximately 1,080 judges in the mid-2020s, but actual working strength has routinely been 25 to 40 percent below that figure. Several High Courts have functioned for extended periods with fewer than half their sanctioned judges in position. The combined pendency of High Courts exceeded 60 lakh (6 million) cases by the early 2020s.

Against this backdrop, Article 224A's potential to provide immediate judicial capacity — without waiting for the collegium recommendation-and-appointment cycle that can take 12 to 24 months from recommendation to swearing-in — is substantial. A retired judge who retired only months earlier retains full familiarity with the court's practice and procedure, the applicable law in the court's jurisdiction, and the professional landscape of the bar. They can begin hearing cases almost immediately after a recall request is accepted.

The article's underuse in the face of this crisis reflects several converging factors. First, the allowance structure has been considered inadequate by many retired judges, particularly those who have established active arbitration or consulting practices. Second, some High Court Chief Justices have been reluctant to invoke the provision — partly for institutional reasons (a preference for pressing the government on permanent appointments rather than treating temporary measures as a substitute), and partly for practical ones (questions about how recalled judges fit into bench composition and seniority protocols). Third, the constitutional uncertainty introduced by the NJAC episode created procedural confusion about how references should be processed.

The Lok Prahari judgment addressed these inhibitions directly by affirming that the article is constitutionally sound, that the collegium process applies to it, and that Chief Justices should exercise their judgment on references without waiting for a comprehensive systemic solution to the vacancy problem. The Court's direction to the government to revise the allowance structure for recalled judges was a recognition that the financial deterrent had been a significant practical obstacle.

## Current significance

Post-Lok Prahari, some High Courts have moved to invoke Article 224A more actively, particularly for constituting special benches to clear old pending matters. Retired judges with expertise in specific areas — commercial law, constitutional law, land acquisition, service matters — have been recalled to sit on dedicated benches handling those categories of cases. This targeted use of the article makes constitutional sense: it matches the recalled judge's expertise to the court's most pressing backlogs, maximising the benefit of the temporary measure.

The article also has relevance in the context of judicial vacancies created by the elevation of High Court judges to the Supreme Court. When a senior judge is elevated, the High Court loses both a position and an experienced hand at the same time. The replacement through the permanent appointment process takes months. Article 224A provides a bridge — the elevated judge's predecessor, or a retired judge of equivalent seniority, can be recalled to sit on significant constitutional benches while the permanent appointment is processed.

The broader debate that Article 224A has catalysed concerns whether constitutional instruments should compensate for systemic failures in the permanent appointment process, or whether using them to paper over the vacancy crisis reduces the pressure for structural reform. Critics argue that generous use of Article 224A allows governments and the collegium to avoid confronting the root causes of the vacancy problem — slow appointment processes, inadequate sanctioned strength relative to caseload, and the absence of a timeline-bound appointment mechanism. Proponents argue that the article exists precisely for situations like the current one and that refusing to use it while litigants wait years for their cases to be heard is an unjustifiable formalism.

The question of whether Article 224A can be used to staff newly created High Court benches — rather than just supplement the existing bench at the principal seat — has also been raised. Circuit and bench sittings at locations away from the principal seat are particularly difficult to staff because judges are generally reluctant to travel. Retired judges resident near such locations could be recalled specifically for circuit sittings, improving access to justice without requiring the permanent relocation of sitting judges. This creative use of the provision has been endorsed in principle by the Law Commission but has not yet been systematically implemented.
