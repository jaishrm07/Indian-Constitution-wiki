## What this Article did (and why it was omitted)

Article 329A was inserted by the Constitution (Thirty-ninth Amendment) Act, 1975 — one of the most consequential and constitutionally objectionable amendments in Indian history. Its purpose was to insulate Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's election to Parliament from the judicial determination that had found it void. In June 1975, Justice Jagmohanlal Sinha of the Allahabad High Court set aside Indira Gandhi's 1971 election from Rae Bareli on the ground that her campaign had used government servants and resources in violation of the Representation of the People Act. The judgment, if sustained, would have disqualified her from Parliament and the Prime Ministership.

The 39th Amendment struck at this judgment in four ways. First, it inserted Article 329A, which retrospectively declared that the election of a person who holds or has held the office of Prime Minister or Speaker of the Lok Sabha "shall not be called in question" on the ground of any electoral malpractice if such election was declared valid by Parliament by law. Second, it purported to validate the Prime Minister's past election by Parliament's judgment standing in as the election tribunal. Third, it retrospectively amended certain provisions of the Representation of the People Act to exclude specific categories of conduct from the definition of corrupt practice. Fourth, it simultaneously stripped the Supreme Court of jurisdiction to hear the already-filed appeal from the Allahabad judgment.

The provision was a calculated deployment of the amending power to reverse a judicial outcome affecting the personal political survival of the sitting Prime Minister.

## The Supreme Court's response: Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)

The Supreme Court, sitting as a Constitution Bench of five judges, heard the appeal in August 1975 — just weeks after the Emergency had been declared under Article 352. The majority (four of five judges) struck down the core provisions of Article 329A as unconstitutional for violating the basic structure of the Constitution, even though the Emergency was in force and fundamental rights under Articles 14 and 19 were suspended.

Justice Y.V. Chandrachud, speaking for the majority on this issue, held that the provision destroyed the basic structure principle of free and fair elections — a principle which, as the Court had found in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973), Parliament cannot abrogate through the amending power under Article 368. The free and fair election requirement was held to be an indispensable feature of constitutional democracy that gave the democratic republic its essence.

The judgment stands as one of the most courageous exercises of judicial independence in Indian constitutional history. It was delivered at the height of the Emergency, when fundamental rights were suspended, press censorship was in force, and several political leaders had been detained. That the Supreme Court nonetheless invalidated a constitutional amendment designed to protect the Prime Minister is treated as a foundational moment in the doctrine that even Parliament's constituent power cannot touch the basic structure.

The Court, however, stayed its own judgment pending appeal — allowing Indira Gandhi to continue as Prime Minister while the Emergency remained in force. After the Emergency ended, she called and lost the 1977 elections.

## Formal omission and the 44th Amendment

After the Janata Party came to power in 1977, Parliament enacted the Constitution (Forty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, which systematically reversed the Emergency-era constitutional changes. Article 329A was formally omitted by section 36 of the 44th Amendment, effective 20 June 1979. With the omission, the ordinary electoral law framework — under which election petitions alleging malpractice are adjudicated by High Courts as election tribunals, with appeals to the Supreme Court — was restored.

## Long-term constitutional significance

Article 329A's brief existence and dramatic judicial invalidation shaped several permanent features of Indian constitutional doctrine:

First, it confirmed that the amending power under Article 368 cannot be used to reverse a specific judicial outcome in a way that destroys a basic structural feature of the Constitution. The principle was not merely that courts review amendments, but that retrospective legislative validation of a void election violates the separation of powers and electoral democracy as components of the basic structure.

Second, it established that the basic structure doctrine applies even when fundamental rights are suspended under Emergency, because some structural features — free elections, judicial review, separation of powers — are not "fundamental rights" in the technical sense but are conditions of constitutional government itself.

Third, it made the institutional design of independent election dispute resolution constitutionally protected: Parliament cannot substitute itself as the election tribunal for the purpose of validating a specific election, as that would eliminate the judicial check on electoral malpractice.

## How to find the judgment

The primary text of **Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)** is available through the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in using the citation **1975 Supp SCC 1**. The case is one of the most extensively commented-upon in Indian constitutional history and is cited in virtually every article or case dealing with the basic structure doctrine, free and fair elections, and the limits of the constituent power.
