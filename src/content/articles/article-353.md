  (a) notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, the executive power of the Union shall extend to the giving of directions to any State as to the manner in which the executive power thereof is to be exercised;

## Why it matters

Without Article 353, a National Emergency proclaimed under Article 352 would be a declaratory act with no immediate legislative or executive content. Article 353 is the provision that converts the proclamation into a working shift of governmental power. It does this through two distinct but related mechanisms.

The first is the expansion of Parliament's legislative competence. Under the normal scheme of the Seventh Schedule, the State List reserves subjects such as public order, police, agriculture, land, and local government exclusively for State Legislatures. Article 353(b) overrides this scheme and allows Parliament to make laws on any State List subject while the emergency is in operation. Laws so made do not displace the State Legislature — the State may still legislate — but Union law prevails over inconsistent State law under Article 254. This is the mechanism that allowed Parliament during the 1975 Emergency to enact sweeping legislation affecting subjects that would otherwise require state action.

The second mechanism is the expansion of Union executive power. Article 353(a) allows the Union executive to give binding directions to any State as to how the State must exercise its own executive power. The ordinary Article 256 power to give directions is limited to ensuring compliance with Union laws; the Article 353(a) power goes further and can direct States on matters within their exclusive executive domain. This converts the Union-State relationship during an emergency from a federation to something closer to a unitary system.

The two powers are related to but distinct from the general expansion in Article 250 (which allows Parliament to legislate on State List subjects on Rajya Sabha resolution even without an emergency) and Article 354 (which deals specifically with revenue distribution during an emergency). Article 353 is broader and automatic upon proclamation.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a) — executive directions.** The Union's power to give directions to States under Article 353(a) is not confined to particular subjects. It extends to the manner of exercise of the State's executive power as a whole. A Union direction issued under this clause during an emergency that a State refuses to follow would place the State in the same position as a State that fails to comply with Article 256 directions — potentially providing a basis for an Article 356 proclamation.

**Clause (b) — legislative power.** Parliament's legislative competence under Article 353(b) specifically includes the power to confer duties and impose functions on Union authorities with respect to State List matters. This enables Parliament to build a parallel or overriding Union administrative structure in areas normally reserved for States, which was used extensively during the 1975 Emergency.

**The proviso — partial emergencies.** Added in 1976, the proviso confines extended powers to the area covered by the proclamation. Where the emergency operates only in part of India, Article 353's extended powers apply to other States only insofar as the threat to national security emanates from activities connected to the emergency zone. This prevents the use of a localised emergency as a pretext for nationwide legislative and executive centralisation.

**Connection with Article 250.** Article 250 separately allows Parliament to legislate on State List subjects when the Rajya Sabha passes a resolution by two-thirds majority declaring it expedient in the national interest. Article 353 differs in that it is automatic, requires no separate resolution, and allows Union executive directions in addition to legislative power.

## Key cases

**ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla (1976)** — The combined effect of Articles 352, 353, 358, and 359 during the 1975 Emergency was examined by the Supreme Court. The majority's broad reading of the emergency framework as displacing normal constitutional constraints drew on the width of Parliament's Article 353 legislative powers and was later condemned as having been constitutionally wrong. The decision remains a cautionary demonstration of how Article 353 powers can be deployed to entrench an authoritarian administration.

**Minerva Mills v Union of India (1980)** — The Court's insistence that emergency powers must remain within constitutional limits, and that unlimited legislative power during an emergency is incompatible with the basic structure doctrine, directly constrains how Parliament exercises its Article 353(b) powers. The judgment confirmed that laws enacted by Parliament on State List subjects during an emergency must still respect the basic structure, including judicial review and the rule of law.

**Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v Union of India (1998)** — While addressing the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act rather than Article 353 directly, the Supreme Court held that Union executive action in States — including directions about public order and security — must trace its authority to a valid legal source and that the courts' supervisory jurisdiction is not ousted by executive declarations of emergency conditions. The ruling reinforces that Article 353(a) directions must be grounded in and proportionate to the emergency's legitimate security purpose.

## Current significance

Article 353 has not been formally invoked since the 1975 Emergency. However, its structural logic — the idea that internal security threats justify Union legislative and executive primacy over States — surfaces regularly in debates about disaster management legislation, counter-terrorism frameworks, and pandemic governance. The Disaster Management Act 2005, for example, creates Union authority over State action in disaster situations that echoes the Article 353 architecture without requiring a formal proclamation. Critics argue that this functional equivalent of Article 353 powers operates without the parliamentary oversight that the Constitution's emergency framework requires. The proviso's limitation of expanded powers to the area of the emergency has direct relevance to contemporary discussions about regional conflicts and targeted central intervention.
