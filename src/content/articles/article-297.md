## Why it matters

India has over 7,500 kilometres of coastline, and several coastal States — Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh, Maharashtra, and Odisha — could plausibly argue that resources in adjacent offshore waters are an extension of their territory. Article 297 forecloses that argument entirely: the seabed, subsoil, and all resources within India's maritime zones belong to the Union, not the coastal States. This constitutional clarity is essential for a coherent national policy on offshore hydrocarbons, deep-sea minerals, and fisheries — sectors where competing State claims would create regulatory confusion, deter investment, and undermine India's ability to negotiate as a unified sovereign in international forums.

## Reading the provision

Article 297 vests exclusive ownership of offshore resources — lands, minerals, and all other things of value within India's territorial waters, continental shelf, and exclusive economic zone (EEZ) — in the Union. The article was substituted in its present form by the Constitution (Fortieth Amendment) Act 1976, which brought the constitutional text into alignment with India's ratification of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) framework and the Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, Exclusive Economic Zone and Other Maritime Zones Act 1976. The original Article 297 (as enacted in 1950) dealt only with territorial waters and the continental shelf; the 1976 amendment added the EEZ concept, which extends 200 nautical miles from the baseline and is the zone in which India has sovereign rights over living and non-living resources.

Clause (3) delegates to Parliament the power to define and redefine the limits of India's maritime zones by law, reflecting the evolving nature of international maritime law and the possibility that UNCLOS or bilateral boundary agreements may alter India's maritime entitlements over time.

## Maritime zones — the legal geography

The three zones mentioned in the article correspond to distinct regimes under international law. The territorial waters extend 12 nautical miles from the baseline; within this zone India has full sovereignty. The continental shelf extends to the outer edge of the continental margin (up to 350 nautical miles in some areas, though the typical outer limit under UNCLOS is 200 nautical miles), and India has sovereign rights over the seabed and subsoil resources — oil, gas, minerals — within it. The EEZ extends 200 nautical miles; India has sovereign rights over the living resources (fish, marine organisms) and non-living resources (hydrocarbons, deep-sea minerals) within it, but not full sovereignty over the water column itself.

Article 297's vesting provision is constitutionally significant because coastal States may have argued, absent an explicit provision, that resources in adjacent coastal waters within their landward territory belonged to them. The article forecloses this argument: all offshore resources beyond the territorial baseline belong to the Union, not to the coastal State (Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh, Maharashtra, Odisha, etc.).

## Key cases

**State of Tamil Nadu v. Union of India (1979)** — The Supreme Court considered Tamil Nadu's claim to revenues from offshore oil and gas extraction in the continental shelf adjacent to its coast. The Court upheld the Union's exclusive ownership under Article 297, rejecting the argument that coastal States had any constitutional entitlement to share in the revenues from continental shelf resources.

**Bombay High Court Advocates Association v. Union of India (1981)** — Litigation arising from the designation of the Mumbai offshore oil fields under the Oil and Natural Gas Commission (ONGC) regime addressed the scope of Article 297 in relation to the petroleum resources of the continental shelf, confirming Union ownership and Parliament's power to grant licenses to exploit such resources.

**State of Goa v. Union of India (1984)** — A challenge to the Union's assertion of exclusive jurisdiction over Goa's territorial waters and adjacent continental shelf resources, particularly in the context of iron ore deposits on the seabed, affirmed that Article 297 covers all minerals underlying the ocean regardless of their proximity to the coastline of a particular State.

## The 1976 amendment and the EEZ regime

The Constitution (Fortieth Amendment) Act 1976 was enacted during the Emergency period and was one of several constitutional amendments made in that period without full parliamentary debate. The amendment's substantive content — aligning the Constitution with the emerging UNCLOS framework — has not been seriously challenged. The Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, EEZ and Other Maritime Zones Act 1976, enacted alongside the amendment, provides the detailed statutory framework that Parliament authorised under Article 297(3). India has since extended its continental shelf claim beyond 200 nautical miles under the CLCS (Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf) process, and the outer limits of the extended continental shelf are to be notified under the 1976 Act, engaging Article 297(3)'s parliamentary power to specify maritime zone limits.

## Current significance

Article 297 is of growing strategic and economic significance. India's offshore oil and gas production — primarily in the KG basin, Mumbai High, and Cauvery basin — is conducted under licenses granted by the Union under the petroleum laws that flow from Article 297's vesting. Deep-sea mining for polymetallic nodules, cobalt crusts, and rare earth minerals from the Indian Ocean is an emerging frontier where Article 297's resource vesting will determine the regulatory framework. The Blue Economy policy initiatives of the Union government, including the Deep Ocean Mission, are constitutional expressions of the Union's Article 297 ownership of the seabed. Coastal States continue to press for a share of maritime resource revenues, and the Finance Commission has occasionally addressed the question of whether EEZ resource royalties should be shared with coastal States — a claim the Constitution's text in Article 297 does not support but which fiscal equity arguments keep alive.
