## Why it matters

Free and fair elections require stability in the electoral process. If every delimitation order or every seat allocation could be challenged in court as a matter of course, elections could be perpetually delayed by injunctions, ward boundaries could be held in legal limbo, and the constitutional guarantee of five-yearly elections would become impossible to implement on schedule. Article 243ZG is the legislative answer to this problem for municipal elections: it places delimitation laws beyond ordinary judicial challenge and channels post-election grievances into a dedicated election petition mechanism. This mirrors the approach taken in Article 329 for Parliamentary and State Legislative elections, and creates a coherent architecture of electoral immunity for all three tiers of the democratic system.

The "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" language is significant. It means that Article 243ZG operates even against Article 226 (High Court writ jurisdiction) and Article 32 (Supreme Court's fundamental rights jurisdiction). A petitioner cannot avoid the election petition requirement by framing a municipal election dispute as a fundamental rights violation and going straight to the constitutional courts — at least not where the real dispute is about the validity of an election or a delimitation order.

## Clause (a) — The delimitation immunity

The immunity for delimitation laws under clause (a) applies to laws "made or purporting to be made under article 243ZA." The phrase "purporting to be made" is important: it extends the immunity even to delimitation orders that might not actually be validly made under Article 243ZA, provided they purport to be made under that provision. This prevents courts from scrutinising whether the formal conditions for making the delimitation order were satisfied. The immunity is not absolute in the sense of protecting fraud or malice — courts have read Article 329's equivalent immunity (and by extension, the Article 243ZG immunity) as not covering constitutional amendments challenged on basic structure grounds, and as not preventing courts from reviewing whether a body that purported to exercise delimitation powers was the kind of body to which the provision applied at all.

## Clause (b) — The election petition channel

Clause (b) creates an exclusive channel for challenging municipal election results: the election petition presented to the authority designated by State law. This means that a person who believes a municipal election was conducted improperly, that a candidate was disqualified, or that corrupt practices occurred must file an election petition in the prescribed manner rather than going to the High Court by writ. The State legislature has power to designate the authority that hears election petitions — typically a designated civil court or a special tribunal — and to prescribe the procedure for those petitions.

Where a State has not enacted legislation providing for an election petition mechanism, courts have faced a difficult question: does clause (b)'s exclusion operate even when no alternative forum exists? The general judicial approach has been that the exclusion operates once a State has provided a forum, but that the absence of a forum cannot be used to perpetually bar any challenge — courts retain jurisdiction to remedy the legislative gap by appropriate directions.

## Comparison with Article 329

Article 243ZG is the municipal-tier counterpart of Article 329, which provides equivalent immunities for Parliamentary and State Legislative elections. The two provisions use structurally identical formulations, and judicial interpretation of Article 329 substantially informs the reading of Article 243ZG. The Supreme Court's landmark rulings in election law under Article 329 — on the scope of election petition exclusivity, the permissibility of pre-election challenges, and the treatment of post-election administrative actions — are all relevant to the municipal election context.

One important distinction: Article 329's immunity has been interpreted as covering only matters that "call in question" an election, not pre-election matters such as voter list defects or candidate disqualification decisions. The same distinction applies to Article 243ZG. Courts have therefore entertained challenges to ward delimitation on grounds other than seeking to overturn a completed election — for example, mandamus applications seeking re-delimitation to ensure equal ward sizes or correct malapportionment — where the challenge is not to the result of a specific election but to the process leading up to it.

## Key cases

**N.P. Ponnuswami v. Returning Officer (1952) — The template for electoral exclusivity** — Though decided under Article 329 before the Seventy-Fourth Amendment, this foundational Supreme Court ruling established that electoral processes are intended to be self-contained and that constitutional courts should not interfere through writ jurisdiction with elections in progress. The principle has been consistently applied in municipal election contexts under Article 243ZG.

**Mohd. Aslam v. Union of India (2003) — Municipal election accountability** — The Supreme Court addressed the constitutional requirements for municipal elections and the non-derogable character of the electoral mandate. The Court confirmed that Article 243ZG's channelling function operates in conjunction with, not against, the State Election Commission's mandatory duty to conduct timely elections — the bar on court interference cannot be used by a State to indefinitely postpone elections.

**Kishan Singh Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of the City of Ahmedabad (2006) — Scope of election petition exclusivity** — The Supreme Court, in the context of local body elections, reiterated that the exclusive election petition channel of Article 243ZG(b) bars High Courts from entertaining writ petitions that are, in substance, challenges to election results, even when framed as fundamental rights violations. The Court held that electoral disputes have a dedicated forum and must be channelled there.

## Current significance

Municipal election litigation in India remains one of the heaviest streams of electoral case law. Ward delimitation in rapidly growing cities, OBC reservation notifications that delay elections, the constitutionality of mid-term extension of municipal bodies, and disputes about which body has jurisdiction to hear election petitions all generate significant litigation. Article 243ZG's bar reduces but does not eliminate this litigation: courts still receive large volumes of cases that test the boundaries of the immunity — pre-election challenges, candidate qualification disputes, campaign finance issues, and challenges to the election petition tribunal's constitution or jurisdiction. The provision's practical significance is in steering post-election challenges away from constitutional courts and into dedicated forums, which reduces the risk of election results being undone by injunctions from courts that have not heard the full election petition on its merits.
