officialText: |-
  132. Appellate jurisdiction of Supreme Court in appeals from High Courts in certain cases.—(1) An appeal shall lie to the Supreme Court from any judgment, decree or final order of a High Court in the territory of India, whether in a civil, criminal or other proceeding, [if the High Court certifies under article 134A] that the case involves a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution.

  (2)* * * * *

  (3) Where such a certificate is given, any party in the case may appeal to the Supreme Court on the ground that any such question as aforesaid has been wrongly decided .

  Explanation.—For the purposes of this article, the expression “final order” includes an order deciding an issue which, if decided in favour of the appellant, would be sufficient for the final disposal of the case.

## What this Article does

Article 132 creates an appellate pathway from any High Court to the
Supreme Court in cases that involve a substantial question of law as to
the interpretation of the Constitution. The pathway is available
regardless of the nature of the proceeding — civil, criminal, or
otherwise — which distinguishes Article 132 from the more limited
subject-matter appellate routes under Articles 133 and 134. It is the
constitutional-law appellate article: once a High Court certifies under
Article 134A that a substantial constitutional question is involved, any
party may appeal to the Supreme Court specifically on the ground that the
constitutional question was wrongly decided.

## Reading the provision

### The certificate requirement and Article 134A

Before the 44th Amendment restructured the appellate provisions, the
certificate was issued under the article itself. Article 134A now provides
the consolidated procedure for High Court certificates covering Articles
132, 133, and 134: the High Court may grant a certificate on its own
motion at the time of judgment or order, or on an application filed within
ninety days. The certificate filters appellate traffic to the Supreme
Court by requiring a judicial determination at the High Court level that
the constitutional question is genuinely substantial.

### What counts as a "substantial question as to interpretation of the Constitution"

The phrase has been interpreted purposively. A question is "substantial"
if it is not already settled by binding Supreme Court authority, is not
plainly covered by unambiguous constitutional text, and is of real — not
merely academic — importance to the determination of the case. Routine
applications of settled doctrine to facts do not qualify. The question
must be one of interpretation: whether a statute is unconstitutional, how
competing provisions of the Constitution are to be reconciled, or what the
scope of a fundamental right or federal power is. Factual disputes or
questions of statutory interpretation that do not require constitutional
analysis are excluded.

### How Articles 132, 133, and 134 differ

Article 132 is the broadest in scope: it covers civil, criminal, and all
other proceedings and its only jurisdictional trigger is the
constitutional question. Article 133 applies only to civil proceedings and
requires either that the value in dispute meets a threshold or that the
case involves a substantial question of general importance. Article 134
confers appellate jurisdiction in criminal proceedings in defined
circumstances including reversal of acquittal and imposition of capital
punishment. A case may engage multiple articles, but the constitutional
question pathway under Article 132 is available across all categories — a
point that matters when a criminal case also raises a constitutional
challenge.

### Clause (3) and party rights

Clause (3) specifies that once a certificate is granted, any party may
appeal to the Supreme Court on the ground that the constitutional question
was wrongly decided. The certificate does not simply lift the whole case
to the Supreme Court; it creates an independent appellate right for each
party on the certified constitutional question. The Supreme Court may also,
under Article 136, grant special leave even where the High Court has
refused a certificate, preserving ultimate control over its own docket.

## Key cases

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The thirteen-judge
bench that established the basic structure doctrine reached the Court
through the constitutional appellate channel, demonstrating that Article
132 is the appropriate vehicle for cases raising fundamental questions
about the limits of Parliament's constituent power. The judgment also
crystallised the rule under Article 145(3) that cases involving a
substantial question of constitutional law must be heard by at least five
judges, linking the appellate gateway to the required composition of the
deciding bench.

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India (1980)** — The challenge to clauses
of the 42nd Amendment that sought to exclude judicial review of
constitutional amendments reached the Supreme Court through Article 132's
constitutional appellate route. The Court struck down those clauses,
confirming that Article 132 jurisdiction extends to reviewing whether a
constitutional amendment itself violates the basic structure — i.e., the
article is not limited to reviewing ordinary legislation but can
scrutinise the Constitution's own modification.

**State of Bombay v. United Motors (India) Ltd. (1953)** — An early
Supreme Court decision that examined the scope of "substantial question of
law as to interpretation of the Constitution" and held that the question
must genuinely arise on the facts and be necessary for the decision, not
raised collaterally or incidentally. This interpretive baseline has guided
High Courts in deciding whether to grant certificates under Article 134A.

**Tirupati Balaji Developers Pvt. Ltd. v. State of Bihar (2004)** — The
Supreme Court reiterated that a "substantial question" under Article 132
must be a genuinely open question of constitutional interpretation,
distinguishing it from questions that, though framed in constitutional
terms, are really disputes about statutory meaning or factual application
of settled law. The ruling reinforces the gatekeeping function of the
certification requirement.

## Article 132 and the five-judge bench rule

Article 145(3) requires that cases involving a substantial question of law
as to the interpretation of the Constitution shall be heard by a bench of
not less than five judges. This rule directly tracks the language of
Article 132 and reflects the constitutional design that important
constitutional questions should receive collegiate scrutiny rather than
being settled by a small bench. In practice the Supreme Court sometimes
admits Article 132 appeals before a two-judge or three-judge bench for
procedural purposes, but the merits of a genuine substantial constitutional
question must be referred to a larger bench.

## Current significance

In practice, most constitutional cases reach the Supreme Court via Article
136 special leave petitions rather than through the Article 132 certificate
route, because special leave is broader and does not require prior High
Court certification. The certificate route nonetheless retains structural
importance: it reflects a deliberate design choice that some constitutional
questions ought to be identified and flagged by the High Court before
escalating, creating a two-stage filtering process. The "substantial
question" threshold under Article 132 also influences how the Court frames
its jurisdiction in large constitutional benches — a case admitted on a
constitutional question under Article 132 signals that the High Court
itself regarded the question as unresolved and significant, which bears on
the weight the Supreme Court gives to the High Court's reasoning below.
