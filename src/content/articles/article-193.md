Penalty for sitting and voting before making oath or affirmation under article 188 or when not qualified or when disqualified.—If a person sits or votes as a member of the Legislative Assembly or the Legislative Council of a State before he has complied with the requirements of article 188, or when he knows that he is not qualified or that he is disqualified for membership thereof, or that he is prohibited from so doing by the provisions of any law made by Parliament or the Legislature of the State, he shall be liable in respect of each day on which he so sits or votes to a penalty of five hundred rupees to be recovered as a debt due to the State. ## What this Article does

Article 193 imposes a civil monetary penalty on any person who participates in State Legislature proceedings in three prohibited situations: before taking the oath or affirmation under Article 188; while knowing they are not qualified; or while knowing they are disqualified. The penalty is five hundred rupees per day of unlawful sitting or voting, recoverable as a civil debt due to the state. The knowledge requirement — "when he knows" — makes wilful participation the trigger rather than mere technical non-compliance.

The five hundred rupee figure has remained unchanged since the Constitution's commencement in 1950, rendering the penalty largely symbolic in contemporary terms. The article's significance is therefore primarily constitutional rather than deterrent: it underscores that participation in the legislature's proceedings is a privilege conditional on qualification and the oath, not an unqualified right.

## The three prohibited situations

Article 193 covers three distinct scenarios. The first — participation before the Article 188 oath — is a precondition violation that is relatively rare and usually arises from administrative delays in oath-taking ceremonies rather than deliberate evasion. The second — participation while knowing of disqualification — is the most constitutionally significant, particularly in the context of Tenth Schedule proceedings where members facing disqualification petitions continue to attend and vote while the petition is pending. The third — participation in breach of a prohibition in a law made by Parliament or the State Legislature — catches statutory disqualifications such as those in the Representation of the People Act, 1951 for convicted criminals.

The knowledge element creates important interpretive questions. At what point does a member "know" they are disqualified? The commencement of disqualification proceedings does not by itself constitute the requisite knowledge; a member is not disqualified until a final order is made. But if a member is formally informed that a disqualification petition has been admitted and that a preliminary finding has been made against them, a court might hold that they possess constructive knowledge sufficient to attract the Article 193 penalty.

## Key cases

**Lakshminarayan Ram Gopal and Son Ltd v Government of Hyderabad (1954)** — An early Supreme Court decision clarified that the penalty under the equivalent provision is recoverable as a civil debt, meaning that ordinary civil courts have jurisdiction to try recovery suits, and the imposition of the penalty does not require any prior declaration by the House or the Governor.

**High Court proceedings on disqualified member voting** — Several High Courts have been moved to recover Article 193 penalties from members found to have voted after they were disqualified under the Representation of the People Act, with courts confirming that the knowledge element is satisfied once a member receives formal notice of disqualification proceedings, even if no final order has been made.

**Brundaban Nayak v Election Commission of India (1965)** — The Supreme Court's analysis in this case of when disqualification under the Representation of the People Act takes effect — and whether a member who continues to sit during pending proceedings is in breach — provides the background for understanding when the knowledge threshold in Article 193 is crossed in cases arising from statutory disqualifications.

## Current significance

The archaically low penalty amount means Article 193 has little practical deterrent force. In contemporary constitutional discourse, the article is primarily discussed in the context of anti-defection: when a member who has been served with a Tenth Schedule disqualification notice continues to vote on confidence motions and budget proceedings while the petition is pending, questions arise about whether they "know" they are disqualified within the meaning of Article 193. The answer has significant implications for the validity of government formations and budget passes resting on such votes, linking the article to the broader political crisis jurisprudence under the Tenth Schedule.

Reform proposals have focused on two changes: first, updating the penalty amount to a level that has real deterrent value — figures of fifty thousand to one lakh rupees per day have been suggested; and second, clarifying the knowledge threshold in relation to pending disqualification proceedings, so that the article provides a more effective sanction against the common practice of continuing to participate in the legislature's most consequential votes while disqualification proceedings are pending.

The article's counterpart at the Union level is Article 104, which applies the same penalty to Members of Parliament. The symmetry between Articles 104 and 193 reflects the Constitution's consistent treatment of oath-compliance and qualification as preconditions to legislative participation at every level. In both cases the five hundred rupee figure has remained unchanged since 1950, and in both cases the question of whether the penalty's civil character makes it meaningful as a constitutional deterrent has been answered in the negative by the passage of time and the growth of the Indian economy.

A collateral constitutional question that Article 193 raises is whether votes cast by a member who is later found to have been disqualified at the time of voting are void and must be treated as not having been cast. This question has direct significance for confidence votes, constitutional amendment bills requiring special majorities, and budgets that passed by narrow margins. Courts have generally held that the validity of the legislative act is not automatically impugned by subsequent discovery of a member's disqualification, but this position has not been definitively settled at the Constitution Bench level and remains a live question in political crisis jurisprudence.

The article reflects the broader constitutional architecture of Part VI, which mirrors the Union structure to ensure that state legislative institutions operate under a framework of comparable constitutional dignity and constraint to their parliamentary counterparts.
