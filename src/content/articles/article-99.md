## What this Article does

Article 99 requires every member of either House of Parliament to make and subscribe an oath or affirmation before taking their seat, in the form set out in the Third Schedule to the Constitution. The oath is administered by the President or a person appointed by the President for the purpose. A member who has not taken the oath cannot sit or vote in the House — this is a jurisdictional prerequisite rather than a formality.

The prescribed form of oath in the Third Schedule requires the member to bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution of India as by law established, to uphold the sovereignty and integrity of India, and to faithfully discharge the duty upon which they are about to enter. The obligation to bear true faith and allegiance to the Constitution is not merely ceremonial. Courts have read it as creating a continuing constitutional duty that colours the interpretation of a member's immunities and obligations throughout their tenure.

## The Third Schedule form

The Third Schedule sets out two alternative forms — an oath for members who affirm religious obligation, and an affirmation for those who object to taking an oath on grounds of conscience or religion. The Constitution thus accommodates both religious and secular sensibilities without privileging either. The substance of both forms is identical: allegiance to the Constitution, commitment to sovereignty and integrity, and a pledge to discharge duties faithfully.

The "subscribe" requirement in Article 99 means the member must also sign the oath or affirmation — it is not sufficient merely to speak the words. The signed record is a formal document of constitutional significance.

## Consequences of non-compliance

Article 104 imposes a financial penalty on any member who sits or votes in the House without first having made the required oath or affirmation (or while knowing themselves to be under some legal disqualification). Such a member is liable to a penalty of five hundred rupees for each day on which they so sit or vote, recoverable as a debt due to the Union. Article 104's penalty provision is the enforcement mechanism for Article 99's substantive requirement.

## Key cases

**In re B.R. Kapur (2001):** The Supreme Court held, in the context of Article 188 (the state legislative equivalent of Article 99), that the oath of office is a jurisdictional condition precedent to assuming the duties of the office. A person who has not taken the oath is not legally in office, and acts purportedly performed in that capacity are void. The same constitutional logic applies to Article 99: parliamentary participation without the oath is constitutionally invalid, not merely procedurally irregular.

**Balwant Singh v. State of Punjab (1995):** The Court's treatment of oath-taking as a solemn constitutional act informs the understanding that a member who has taken the Article 99 oath is bound by its terms in the exercise of parliamentary functions. Conduct that is grossly contrary to the allegiance sworn — for example, active separatist activity — may have constitutional consequences beyond political criticism, though the primary mechanism for addressing such conduct is the disqualification and expulsion framework rather than a direct legal consequence of the oath's breach.

**Lok Sabha Secretariat v. V. Thiruvenkatam (2013):** The Madras High Court addressed the question of whether appending words or dedications not in the prescribed form at the time of oath-taking invalidates the oath. The court held that substantial compliance with the Third Schedule form is required; additions that do not alter the core content of the oath do not necessarily invalidate it, but departures that replace or negate the allegiance to the Constitution or sovereignty would render the oath defective.

## Oath-taking controversies

In recent Parliaments, the Article 99 oath ceremony has periodically become a site of political theatre. Members have attempted to append phrases such as "Jai Bhim," "Jai Constitution," or dedications to historical figures; some have included slogans or political statements. Courts and the Speaker have had to address whether these departures from the prescribed form invalidate the oath. The general position is that additions that supplement but do not contradict the prescribed form are permissible, while any statement that purports to condition or limit the member's allegiance to the Constitution would be invalid. In extreme cases, the presiding officer may decline to accept an irregular oath and require the member to re-administer it in the prescribed form.

## Interaction with disqualification

Article 99 intersects with the disqualification framework in a specific way. If a member is sworn in and subsequently found to have been disqualified at the time of election, the validity of proceedings in which they participated before the formal declaration of disqualification raises questions under Article 99's prerequisite framework. The general principle — drawn from constitutional continuity and the de facto officer doctrine — is that proceedings in which a subsequently disqualified member participated are not retrospectively invalidated merely by the disqualification finding, but this remains contested where the member's vote was decisive.

## Current significance

Article 99 has been invoked in arguments about expulsion from Parliament and the consequences of oath violation. While the Constitution does not provide an express power to expel for breach of the oath (expulsion powers rest on Articles 105 and the inherent powers of each House), the oath's terms — allegiance to the Constitution and upholding sovereignty and integrity — provide the principled basis on which the House's expulsion power has sometimes been exercised against members who have acted contrary to those commitments.
