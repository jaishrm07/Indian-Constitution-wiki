## Why it matters

Article 43A was inserted by the 42nd Constitutional Amendment in 1976 as part of a sweeping set of constitutional changes made by the Indira Gandhi government during the Emergency period. Many of the 42nd Amendment's provisions — expanding Parliament's powers, curtailing judicial review — were repealed or diluted by the 44th Amendment in 1978. But Article 43A survived, reflecting a cross-party consensus that worker participation in management was a legitimate constitutional objective even if the broader Emergency-era constitutional architecture was rejected. Its survival through the democratic correction of the Emergency gives the directive a legitimacy that the broader 42nd Amendment package lacks.

Article 43A represents the Constitution's most direct engagement with the idea of industrial democracy. Where Articles 42 and 43 address what workers receive — safe conditions, a living wage, maternity protection — Article 43A addresses what workers do: it directs the State to give workers a structural voice in the governance of the enterprises where they work. This is a qualitatively different kind of labour right, concerned not with the terms of employment but with the distribution of power within the enterprise.

The distinction between Article 43A and the collective bargaining rights implied by Articles 19(1)(a) and 19(1)(c) is important. Collective bargaining allows workers to negotiate the terms of employment — wages, hours, conditions. Article 43A goes further: it requires the State to secure workers' participation in management itself — in the decisions about investment, retrenchment, reorganisation, and strategic direction that are conventionally reserved for owners and managers. The difference is between workers having a voice about the terms on which they submit to management and workers having a structural role in management as such. The latter is what Article 43A mandates, and it is precisely why the directive has remained so difficult to implement — it challenges the basic legal architecture of the modern corporation.

The provision reflects ideas about industrial democracy that were influential across post-war European constitutional traditions, particularly the model of co-determination established in West Germany, where worker representatives sit on company supervisory boards. For the framers of the 42nd Amendment, the directive responded to the critique that India's socialist commitments remained purely formal so long as workers had no institutionalised say in the decisions — about investment, retrenchment, pricing, and working practices — that most directly affected their lives. A worker who earns a living wage but has no voice in how the enterprise is run is still a subject of management rather than a participant in economic life.

Despite being on the books for nearly five decades, the directive remains one of the least legislated in Part IV. Parliament has not enacted comprehensive national legislation on worker participation in management at the level Article 43A contemplates. The Companies Act (2013) includes provisions for worker welfare committees and consultative mechanisms, but stops well short of requiring worker representation on boards or in key decision-making bodies. Employee Stock Ownership Plans (ESOPs), which have become widespread in the technology sector, give workers a financial stake in company performance but do not give them a governance voice — they are shareholders at law but not participants in management within the meaning of Article 43A. The result is a significant gap between the constitutional mandate and legislative reality.

## Reading the provision

The article uses deliberately expansive language: "undertakings, establishments or other organisations engaged in any industry." This covers not just the classic factory or mine but any organisation performing industrial activity — software companies, banks, transport undertakings, construction firms, and potentially platform enterprises that organise and direct labour even if they disclaim being employers. The breadth of this formulation is constitutionally significant: Article 43A is not limited to the organised manufacturing sector.

The direction to the State to take steps "by suitable legislation or in any other way" mirrors Article 43's approach and preserves legislative flexibility. The constitutional mandate is the outcome — worker participation in management — not the mechanism. This means courts assessing compliance with Article 43A must look at whether the State has taken genuine steps toward the constitutional goal, not merely whether it has passed legislation bearing the right label.

"Participation in the management" is distinct from "consultation about terms of employment." Management includes decisions about investment, technology, retrenchment, relocation, market strategy, and organisational structure — the kinds of decisions that most significantly affect whether a worker has a job, what that job consists of, and what the enterprise's future looks like. The constitutional directive is not satisfied by joint consultation committees that discuss welfare amenities but have no voice in management decisions. Courts have recognised this distinction while acknowledging that the precise content of the participation right depends on legislative implementation that Parliament has not yet provided.

In the context of company law, Article 43A has been used to argue that the shareholder-centric model enshrined in the Companies Act is constitutionally incomplete — that the enterprise must be understood as a community of interests (shareholders, workers, suppliers, community) rather than exclusively as property belonging to its shareholders. The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code and its treatment of workers' dues in the liquidation waterfall is a live area where Article 43A's constitutional vision of worker as stakeholder continues to be argued.

## Key cases

**National Textile Workers Union v. P. R. Ramakrishnan (1983)** — This is the foundational Article 43A judgment. A Constitution Bench held that workers in a company have a constitutional interest in its affairs and must be heard before the company is ordered to be wound up in liquidation proceedings. Justice Bhagwati's majority opinion held that the exclusive shareholder-centric model of company law must be read in light of Article 43A, and that the worker's stake in the enterprise — their livelihood, skills, and welfare — gives them a right of audience in proceedings that will extinguish the enterprise. The ruling is the clearest judicial statement that Article 43A alters the constitutional relationship between capital and labour in company affairs, and it has been cited in subsequent insolvency, restructuring, and closure litigation as establishing the worker's constitutional interest in the ongoing life of the enterprise.

**Workmen of Hindustan Lever v. Hindustan Lever (1984)** — The Supreme Court considered whether workers' unions could participate in conciliation and adjudication processes relating to corporate restructuring decisions that affected employment. It drew on Article 43A to confirm that the constitutional design supports legislative and adjudicative mechanisms that give workers a voice at the table when decisions affecting their employment are made, treating the directive as evidence that the Constitution does not regard the employer's managerial prerogative as constitutionally absolute.

**Hindustan Antibiotics Ltd. v. Their Workmen (1967)** — Though decided before Article 43A's insertion, this judgment by the Supreme Court recognised that workers have a legitimate interest in the governance and financial decisions of an enterprise beyond their immediate wages, and that industrial adjudication must take into account the broader constitutional vision of workers' welfare and voice. The case informed the constitutional framing that the 42nd Amendment later gave explicit form, and it is regularly cited alongside Article 43A as evidence of the Court's long-standing recognition that the enterprise is not the employer's property alone.

## Current significance

Article 43A has acquired renewed relevance in debates about corporate governance reform, the Labour Codes, and the regulation of platform and gig economy work. The four Labour Codes enacted between 2019 and 2020 consolidate existing industrial relations and social security law but do not establish meaningful structures for worker participation in management. Critics argue that this legislative choice is constitutionally contestable given the explicit directive in Article 43A that the State shall take steps to secure such participation. The failure to enact corresponding legislation more than four decades after the directive was inserted makes Article 43A one of the most conspicuous examples of constitutional aspiration unmatched by legislative will.

The article is also increasingly relevant to algorithmic management — the practice by which platform companies use automated systems to assign work, set pay rates, evaluate performance, and terminate workers, with no meaningful human accountability or worker input. The absence of any participatory mechanism in these systems sits in direct tension with the constitutional preference for workers having a voice in the management of the organisations where they work. Constitutional and statutory arguments drawing on Article 43A are beginning to feature in litigation about the regulatory framework for platform labour, alongside broader debates about board-level worker representation.

The ESOP debate is a live dimension of Article 43A's contemporary relevance. Proponents argue that ownership stakes through ESOPs partially satisfy the participation mandate; critics respond that owning shares does not give workers any voice in operational management or strategic decisions — that Article 43A contemplates governance participation, not merely financial participation. The Companies Act (2013) left this question unresolved and no legislation has since addressed it directly.

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code's treatment of workers' dues in insolvency proceedings — where workers are subordinate creditors below secured financial creditors — has been challenged partly on Article 43A grounds, with workers arguing that the constitutional vision of worker as stakeholder in the enterprise is incompatible with a liquidation regime that treats labour as merely one variety of debt. These arguments have so far not succeeded in the Supreme Court, but they continue to shape the constitutional debate about the limits of creditor-favourable insolvency regimes.

Looking comparatively, India has not followed the European co-determination path that Article 43A was modelled on. Germany's Mitbestimmungsgesetz (Co-determination Act) requires worker representatives on supervisory boards of large companies; similar requirements exist in Austria, Sweden, and other European systems. The Indian constitutional directive has remained largely unimplemented, reflecting the political economy of a liberalising economy in which organised labour's constitutional rights have been rhetorically acknowledged while their substantive implementation has been deferred. The constitutional architecture exists; the legislative will to activate it does not. Article 43A's unfulfilled potential is one of the most striking gaps between constitutional aspiration and legislative reality in modern Indian law.

The Joint Management Councils, Works Committees, and Joint Consultative Machinery that exist in some public sector undertakings and central government services represent partial steps toward Article 43A compliance, but they are predominantly consultative bodies without decision-making authority. Courts have noted these structures but have not required them to be expanded or converted into genuine co-determination mechanisms — treating the content and form of worker participation as a legislative judgment even while acknowledging the constitutional directive. This judicial deference is increasingly challenged by constitutional scholars who argue that the forty-year failure to implement Article 43A is itself a constitutional violation that courts have an obligation to address through structural directions.

The relationship between Article 43A and the freedom of trade and commerce guaranteed under Articles 19(1)(g) and 301 has also been debated. Employers have argued that mandatory worker participation in management would violate their constitutional right to carry on their business as they choose, while workers have argued that Article 43A's directive, read with Article 37 (which makes the directive principles fundamental to governance), requires Parliament to resolve any tension in favour of worker participation. Courts have not needed to resolve this tension directly because Parliament has not enacted comprehensive worker-participation legislation, but the constitutional question will become live if it does.

Article 43A also has implications for corporate social responsibility, employee share schemes, and the emerging discourse about stakeholder capitalism. If the Constitution's directive is that workers must participate in management — not merely receive wages — then corporate governance frameworks that treat shareholder value maximisation as the exclusive purpose of the enterprise are constitutionally incomplete. The Companies Act 2013's mandatory CSR provisions and its stakeholder-oriented formulation of directors' duties represent tentative moves in the direction Article 43A requires, but they do not yet constitute the structural worker-participation mandate that the article demands.

In the public sector, the National Textile Workers Union precedent has been applied in subsequent restructuring and privatisation cases to require that workers be heard before government decisions are taken to close, privatise, or restructure public enterprises. This has generated a body of procedural rights for workers in the public sector that partially fills the gap left by the absence of comprehensive worker-participation legislation. However, as privatisation has accelerated, the scope of this procedural protection has been progressively narrowed by the courts, which have treated the decision to privatise itself — as opposed to the process of doing so — as a matter for executive discretion not reviewable on Article 43A grounds. This tension between the constitutional aspiration of worker participation and the executive prerogative to restructure public enterprises is one of the live frontiers of Article 43A jurisprudence.

The relationship between Article 43A and the constitutional right to form trade unions under Article 19(1)(c) is also instructive. Trade union rights give workers a collective voice in negotiating terms of employment. Article 43A goes further, directing the State to give workers a role in the management of the enterprise itself. The two provisions are complementary: trade union rights are a necessary but not sufficient condition of the constitutional vision of worker participation that Article 43A sets out. A State that allows trade unions to exist but takes no steps to secure worker participation in management has satisfied Article 19(1)(c) but not Article 43A, illustrating the extent to which Article 43A represents a constitutional ambition that goes beyond the minimum requirements of freedom of association.

The unfulfilled promise of Article 43A raises broader questions about the enforceability of directive principles over time. Article 37 makes the directives "fundamental in the governance of the country" and places a duty on the State to apply them in making laws. If the State demonstrably fails to take any steps toward a directive principle's implementation over decades — as appears to be the case with Article 43A — the constitutional question arises whether courts can at some point treat the continued legislative failure as itself a constitutional violation. The answer developed in other directive principle contexts (Articles 21A, 39, 45) has been to wait for legislative implementation and then enforce it rigorously once provided, while continuing to urge Parliament to act. Article 43A may yet follow the same path if a future Parliament chooses to enact meaningful worker-participation legislation.

The constitutional potential of Article 43A has not been extinguished by legislative inaction; it remains available to be activated whenever political conditions permit. Article 43A's potential for constitutional transformation remains real. The growing scholarly, labour movement, and civil society literature on worker ownership, co-operative enterprise, and economic democracy draws on Article 43A as one of its constitutional anchors, arguing that the Indian Constitution's directive vision of the enterprise as a community of stakeholders is both legally authoritative and practically feasible.

Pilot programmes in worker-managed co-operatives, profit-sharing schemes, and sector-specific participation frameworks in public sector enterprises offer models for implementation that are consistent with the constitutional mandate. The Amul model of dairy co-operative enterprise — in which farmers participate in the governance and ownership of a multi-billion-rupee business — is often cited as demonstrating that Article 43A's vision is practically achievable in the Indian economic context, not merely a European import incompatible with Indian conditions.

The article's full potential remains, in the constitutional imaginary of Indian labour law, a horizon to be reached rather than an aspiration to be abandoned. The Constitution's authors placed it there as a direction of travel; the journey has barely begun, but the destination remains constitutionally prescribed.
