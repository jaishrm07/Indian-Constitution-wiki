## Why it matters

Article 31D is a constitutional tombstone — an omitted provision whose brief existence marks the outer boundary of parliamentary overreach during the Emergency. It was inserted by the Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976, as part of a package of amendments that collectively represented the most extensive single assault on constitutional limits in India's history. Its rapid repeal in 1977 was among the first acts of constitutional restoration by the Janata government and signals how Emergency-era constitutional engineering was understood even by its architects to be reversible rather than permanent.

## What Article 31D contained

In its short life, Article 31D would have shielded laws relating to "anti-national activities" and associations from challenge under any of the fundamental rights in Part III. This would have immunised legislation such as the Maintenance of Internal Security Act (MISA) — the primary instrument of Emergency-era preventive detention — from challenge under Articles 14, 19, 20, 21, and 22. The definition of "anti-national activities" was vague and broadly drafted, which critics argued would have given Parliament unlimited power to criminalise dissent and opposition political activity under the anti-national label.

## The Forty-Second Amendment context

The Forty-Second Amendment of 1976 was the Emergency-era Parliament's most ambitious constitutional rewriting effort. Alongside Article 31D and the expansion of Article 31C, it inserted the words "socialist" and "secular" into the Preamble, curtailed judicial review, made emergency powers easier to impose and harder to revoke, extended the term of the Lok Sabha to six years, and transferred subjects from the State List to the Concurrent List. The breadth of these changes reflected the Emergency government's attempt to constitutionalise its programme and insulate it from subsequent reversal — an attempt that largely failed when the Janata government used its own parliamentary majority to undo the changes.

## Omission by the Forty-Third Amendment

The Constitution (Forty-Third Amendment) Act, 1977, omitted Articles 31D and 32A. The Forty-Third Amendment was specifically targeted at reversing Emergency-era distortions to judicial power: it restored the Supreme Court's power to consider the constitutional validity of state laws in proceedings under Article 32 (which Article 32A had curtailed) and removed the anti-national-activities shield that Article 31D had created. The Forty-Fourth Amendment, enacted the same year, went further and restored the fundamental rights framework more broadly.

## Constitutional significance

Though brief and omitted, Article 31D's history carries several constitutional lessons. First, it demonstrates that even the fundamental rights provisions of Part III are amendable by Parliament — but such amendments are subject to the basic structure doctrine. Second, the provision's insertion and swift repeal illustrates the role of electoral accountability in constraining constitutional amendments: the Emergency-era government's electoral defeat enabled constitutional restoration. Third, Article 31D frames the later debates about the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act, the National Security Act, and other security legislation that critics argue serves functions similar to what Article 31D would have authorised — the difference being that these statutes must be tested against the unchanged fundamental rights framework.

## Current significance

No direct current litigation flows from Article 31D since the provision no longer exists. However, its history is regularly invoked in scholarship and advocacy about the constitutional limits of anti-terrorism and national security legislation, about the fundamental rights of persons accused of sedition, unlawful association, or terrorist activity, and about the circumstances under which democratic systems use constitutional machinery to entrench emergency powers against future correction. The Emergency itself, the Forty-Second Amendment's excess, and the swift Forty-Third and Forty-Fourth Amendment reversals together constitute the closest India has come to constitutional breakdown and constitutional renewal in a single sequence of events.
