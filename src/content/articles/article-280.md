  280. Finance Commission.—(1) The President shall, within two years from the commencement of this Constitution and thereafter at the expiration of every fifth year or at such earlier time as the President considers necessary, by order constitute a Finance Commission which shall consist of a Chairman and four other members to be appointed by the President.

  (2) Parliament may by law determine the qualifications which shall be requisite for appointment as members of the Commission and the manner in which they shall be selected.

  (3) It shall be the duty of the Commission to make recommendations to the President as to—

  (a) the distribution between the Union and the States of the net proceeds of taxes which are to be, or may be, divided between them under this Chapter and the allocation between the States of the respective shares of such proceeds;

  (b) the principles which should govern the grants-in-aid of the revenues of the States out of the Consolidated Fund of India; [(bb) the measures needed to augment the Consolidated Fund of a State to supplement the resources of the Panchayats in the State on the basis of the recommendations made by the Finance Commission of the State;] [(c) the measures needed to augment the Consolidated Fund of a State to supplement the resources of the Municipalities in the State on the basis of the recommendations made by the Finance Commission of the State;] [(d)] any other matter referred to the Commission by the President in the interests of sound finance.

  (4) The Commission shall determine their procedure and shall have such powers in the performance of their functions as Parliament may by law confer on them.

## Why it matters

Article 280 is the constitutional engine of fiscal federalism. India's Union government collects a disproportionate share of national tax revenue — income tax, corporation tax, Union excise duties, customs, and the central component of GST — while States bear the primary spending burden for health, education, agriculture, and public order. The Finance Commission (FC), convened every five years under Article 280, sits between these two realities and recommends how to redistribute Union tax revenues to the States. Without this constitutional mechanism, the Union could starve the States into fiscal dependence; with it, the redistribution formula is set by an independent expert body whose recommendations are politically difficult to refuse.

The 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendments deepened the Commission's mandate. Clauses (bb) and (c) of Article 280(3) — inserted in 1992 — extended the FC's remit to cover grants to augment State Consolidated Funds for Panchayats and Municipalities, creating a three-tier fiscal devolution chain: Union to States, and through the State Finance Commissions, from States to local bodies.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) sets the timeline: the first FC was to be constituted within two years of the Constitution coming into force; thereafter, one every five years or earlier if the President considers necessary. In practice every FC has been constituted ahead of its five-year anniversary, allowing the new award to take effect from 1 April of the year following the Constitution's quinquennial cycle. The Commission has a Chairman and four members appointed by the President; Parliament may by law prescribe their qualifications and selection process (clause 2).

Clause (3) is the operational core. The FC must make recommendations on: (a) the vertical division of the "divisible pool" of Union taxes between the Union and the States collectively, and the horizontal distribution among individual States; (b) the principles for grants-in-aid to States whose revenues are insufficient for meeting their expenditure; (bb) and (c) augmentation grants for local bodies; and (d) any other matter of sound finance referred by the President. The divisible pool is the key concept: it is the net proceeds of shareable central taxes after subtracting collection costs, and its vertical share for States has risen from 29.5 per cent under the 10th FC to 41 per cent under the 15th FC.

The FC's recommendations are advisory, not binding. The President acts on them through an order, and Parliament appropriates the funds. However, every FC since the first has had its core recommendations accepted, creating a constitutional convention of compliance that is close to binding in practice.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India AIR 1963 SC 1241** — The Supreme Court's landmark analysis of fiscal federalism in Part XII provided the constitutional backdrop against which the Finance Commission's role as a balancing mechanism is understood. The Court's treatment of the Union's financial supremacy — and the structural constraints on States' fiscal autonomy — explains why an independent FC is essential to the constitutional design.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India AIR 1978 SC 68** — The Court examined the relationship between Union executive directions and State fiscal autonomy, affirming that the Finance Commission's recommendations create a constitutional expectation of cooperative fiscal behaviour. The judgment is a reference point for the proposition that the Union cannot unilaterally alter the fiscal balance by withholding or redirecting FC-recommended devolution.

**Pradeep Chandra Parija v. Pramod Chandra Patnaik (2002) 1 SCC 1** — The Court held in the context of local body finance that constituting a State Finance Commission and augmenting local body resources is a constitutional obligation, not a policy option. This reinforced the downstream significance of Article 280(3)(bb) and (c) in ensuring that devolution reaches the third tier of government.

**Beed District Central Cooperative Bank Ltd. v. State of Maharashtra (2006)** — The Court touched on the Finance Commission framework when addressing grants and fiscal transfers, affirming that conditional grants by the Union to States must be within the constitutional framework of cooperative fiscal federalism and cannot override the States' constitutionally guaranteed share of divisible pool revenues.

## The 15th Finance Commission and its output

The 15th Finance Commission (2020–21 to 2025–26), chaired by N.K. Singh, recommended a total devolution of approximately Rs 41 lakh crore from the Centre to States over the five-year period — the largest devolution award in Indian fiscal history. It retained the 41 per cent vertical share of the divisible pool for States (the 1 per cent that the 14th FC had recommended for special category States was absorbed back into the shared pool following Jammu and Kashmir's reorganisation into Union Territories). Key innovations included performance-based grants tied to health outcomes, power sector reforms, and forest cover, and a dedicated local body grant of Rs 4.36 lakh crore to Panchayats and Municipalities.

The 15th FC also made recommendations on the Union government's own fiscal consolidation — a feature of later FCs that has gradually expanded the Commission's role beyond pure inter-governmental transfers into fiscal oversight of the Union as well.

## FC versus Planning Commission and NITI Aayog

For decades, the Planning Commission ran a parallel channel of Central transfers to States through "plan grants" and Centrally Sponsored Schemes that operated outside the FC framework. Critics argued this created a dual devolution system where the FC's constitutionally mandated transfers competed with the Planning Commission's discretionary allocations, weakening the FC's role. The Planning Commission's abolition in 2014 and replacement by NITI Aayog — which has no fiscal transfer functions — has in principle strengthened the FC's position as the dominant constitutional channel for Union-to-State fiscal flows, though Centrally Sponsored Schemes continue to operate as a large parallel transfer mechanism.

## Current significance

The 16th Finance Commission, constituted in 2023 under the chairmanship of Arvind Panagariya, is working on its report for the 2026–2031 award period. The central political controversy concerns the horizontal distribution formula: southern States with lower population growth rates argue that using 2011 census data rather than the 1971 population figures used by earlier FCs will penalise them for having succeeded in population control. This debate implicates the deepest tensions in Indian fiscal federalism — between equity (poorer and more populous northern States need more transfers) and incentive (more efficient, better-governed States should not lose revenue share). Article 280's quinquennial mechanism is the constitutional forum in which these tensions are renegotiated, making it one of the most consequential provisions in practical governance.
