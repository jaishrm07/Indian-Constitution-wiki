officialText: |-
  243Q. Constitution of Municipalities.—(1) There shall be constituted in every State,—

  (a) a Nagar Panchayat (by whatever name called) for a transitional area, that is to say, an area in transition from a rural area to an urban area;

  (b) a Municipal Council for a smaller urban area; and

  (c) a Municipal Corporation for a larger urban area, in accordance with the provisions of this Part:

  Provided that a Municipality under this clause may not be constituted in such urban area or part thereof as the Governor may, having regard to the size of the area and the municipal services being provided or proposed to be provided by an industrial establishment in that area and such other factors as he may deem fit, by public notification, specify to be an industrial township.

  (2) In this article, "a transitional area", "a smaller urban area" or "a larger urban area" means such area as the Governor may, having regard to the population of the area, the density of the population therein, the revenue generated for local administration, the percentage of employment in non-agricultural activities, the economic importance or such other factors as he may deem fit, specify by public notification for the purposes of this Part.

## Why it matters

The Seventy-Fourth Amendment of 1992 inserted Part IXA into the Constitution to give urban local government a constitutional foundation that it had previously lacked. Before the amendment, municipalities existed only by virtue of State statutes and could be superseded, merged, or dissolved at State discretion without any constitutional check. Article 243Q is the threshold provision of the entire Part IXA framework: it creates a mandatory, three-tier structure of municipal institutions calibrated to the degree of urbanisation of an area. Without this article, there would be no constitutional obligation to have any municipal institution at all. The "shall be constituted" language in clause (1) makes constitution of municipalities mandatory once an area qualifies under the classification criteria, in contrast to the permissive devolution language in Articles 243W and 243X. This mandatory character is what distinguishes Article 243Q from the rest of Part IXA: it is the one provision that is not permissive.

The three-tier structure — Nagar Panchayat for transitional areas, Municipal Council for smaller urban areas, Municipal Corporation for larger urban areas — reflects a constitutional recognition that urban governance needs vary with the scale and complexity of urbanisation. A large metropolitan area needs a full-scale Municipal Corporation with the institutional capacity, revenue base, and planning authority appropriate to a major city. A small town growing out of a village needs a lighter-weight body appropriate to its still-evolving character. A Nagar Panchayat is a transitional institution designed for areas in demographic and economic transition, providing constitutional governance where the State has determined that an area is no longer adequately governed by rural Panchayat institutions but is not yet fully urban. The classification criteria in clause (2) — population, population density, revenue generation, employment in non-agricultural activities, and economic importance — are the constitutional factors the Governor must weigh when specifying which areas fall into which category.

The downstream consequences of classification under Article 243Q are substantial. The category of municipality determines which composition rules under Article 243R apply, which reservation requirements under Article 243T are triggered, what financial framework applies under Articles 243X and 243Y, and which devolution obligations apply under Article 243W. Classification as a Municipal Corporation rather than a Municipal Council typically means a larger elected council, more mandatory ward committees, higher service delivery expectations, and a larger share of State Finance Commission awards. For peri-urban areas, delayed or erroneous classification — being kept as a Nagar Panchayat when population density and economic character warrant a full Municipal Council or Corporation — can mean years of governance deficit and reduced access to devolved functions and finances.

The industrial-township proviso to clause (1) is a constitutionally significant exclusion. It allows the Governor to exempt an urban area from the mandatory constitution of a municipality if an industrial establishment is providing or proposing to provide municipal services in that area. This proviso was designed for older industrial townships — like those of the Tatas and Birlas — where company townships predated the constitutional framework and provided comprehensive services to their employees. Its contemporary application to new private townships, Special Economic Zones, and industrial corridors is more contested. Critics argue that the proviso is being used to exclude economically significant urban areas from democratic municipal governance, creating a class of urban residents who receive private services but have no elected local government accountable to them.

## Reading the provision

The word "shall" in clause (1) — "There shall be constituted in every State" — is unambiguous mandatory language. It contrasts with the permissive "may" used in Articles 243W and 243X. This means that once an area satisfies the criteria for classification as a transitional area, smaller urban area, or larger urban area, the State government has a constitutional duty to constitute the appropriate municipal body. Courts have treated this as a justiciable obligation and have issued mandamus directing States to constitute or reconstitute municipalities where the classification criteria are clearly met and the State has failed to act.

Clause (1)(a) — "a Nagar Panchayat (by whatever name called) for a transitional area" — uses the phrase "by whatever name called" to accommodate the variety of State nomenclature. States use names like Town Area Committee, Notified Area Committee, Town Panchayat, or Small Town Municipality for what the Constitution calls a Nagar Panchayat. The constitutional characterisation as a body for areas "in transition from a rural area to an urban area" is functional and forward-looking: the Nagar Panchayat is a transitional institution, and areas classified as transitional should in principle be reclassified as full urban areas once the transition is complete.

Clause (1)(b) and (c) — "Municipal Council for a smaller urban area" and "Municipal Corporation for a larger urban area" — are the two fully urban categories. The Constitution does not define threshold population levels for these categories, leaving this entirely to the Governor's specification under clause (2). This has produced considerable inter-state variation: what counts as a "smaller" or "larger" urban area differs significantly across States, reflecting historical State municipal statutes and political considerations as much as objective demographic criteria. A city that would qualify as a Municipal Corporation in one State might be governed as a Municipal Council in another.

The proviso to clause (1) — the industrial-township exclusion — requires four elements to be satisfied before it can be invoked: (i) the area must be urban or part of an urban area; (ii) an industrial establishment must be providing or proposing to provide municipal services in the area; (iii) the Governor must be satisfied of this, taking into account the size of the area and "such other factors as he may deem fit"; and (iv) the exclusion must be notified by public notification. Courts have construed each element strictly. The industrial establishment must actually be providing or have a credible proposal to provide municipal services — not merely to operate an industry in the area. The Governor's satisfaction is subject to judicial review for non-application of mind, unreasonableness, or failure to consider relevant factors.

Clause (2) sets out the classification criteria that govern the Governor's specification of what constitutes each category of area. The listed factors are population, density, revenue generated for local administration, percentage of employment in non-agricultural activities, and economic importance. The phrase "or such other factors as he may deem fit" gives the Governor a residual discretion to consider additional relevant factors. However, the listed factors set the constitutional framework: a classification that ignores population and employment structure in favour of purely administrative or political convenience is susceptible to challenge as an arbitrary exercise of the specification power. The Governor's classification notifications are delegated legislation and are reviewable on standard administrative law grounds.

## Key cases

**Vikas Kishanrao Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021)** — While primarily an OBC reservation case, the Supreme Court's examination of Maharashtra's municipal election framework required close analysis of Article 243Q's classification and constitution requirements. The Court's reaffirmation that municipalities are constitutional institutions — not merely statutory bodies — whose constitution is mandated by Part IXA has direct implications for how Article 243Q is understood. Classification decisions and delays in constituting municipalities were examined in this context, and the Court signalled that governance gaps caused by failure to constitute or properly classify municipal bodies are constitutionally cognisable.

**Shankarsan Dash v. Union of India (1991) — Mandatory language in constitutional provisions** — Although decided before the Seventy-Fourth Amendment, this Supreme Court ruling on the word "shall" in constitutional text informs how Article 243Q(1) is read. The Court held that use of "shall" ordinarily imposes a mandatory obligation and that failure to comply could be the subject of constitutional challenge. High Courts have applied this principle when States delay constituting municipalities in transitionally urbanising areas, treating Article 243Q's "shall" as creating an enforceable constitutional duty.

**State of U.P. v. Pradhan Sangh Kshettra Samiti (1995)** — The Supreme Court held that the constitutional mandate to constitute local bodies and hold elections was not merely directory. Though focused on Panchayats, the ruling's reasoning about mandatory constitutionalism under the 73rd and 74th Amendments has been consistently applied to Article 243Q challenges before High Courts. The Court's logic — that the 74th Amendment's insertion into the Constitution was intended to create enforceable institutional obligations, not merely aspirational goals — undergirds the Article 243Q jurisprudence on mandatory constitution.

**Mohd. Aslam v. Union of India (2003) — Ward delimitation and municipal constitution** — The Supreme Court held that delimitation of wards and the process of constituting a municipality must proceed in compliance with the constitutional framework under Part IXA, and that delays by State governments in carrying out these processes were subject to mandamus. The ruling underscores that Article 243Q creates a justiciable obligation, not a discretionary power, for constituting the mandated municipal bodies. The case has been cited in subsequent litigation challenging unreasonably protracted delays in municipal reconstitution following census-based delimitation exercises.

**Rajiv Kumar Garg v. State of Haryana (Punjab and Haryana High Court)** — The Punjab and Haryana High Court examined challenges to the Governor's classification notifications under Article 243Q(2), testing whether the classification of specific areas as transitional rather than fully urban was consistent with the constitutional criteria. The Court held that classification under Article 243Q(2) is not an exercise of unguided discretion but must be based on the factors specified in the article, and that a classification that is inconsistent with objectively verifiable demographic and economic data can be challenged as arbitrary. The ruling has been an important reference point for classification challenges in growing peri-urban areas.

## Current significance

Rapid peri-urban growth across Indian metropolitan regions has created substantial pressure on the classification machinery in Article 243Q(2). Many areas that are demographically urban — with high population density, predominantly non-agricultural employment, and strong economic connections to nearby cities — remain administratively classified as rural or as transitional Nagar Panchayats for extended periods. This delay in classification deprives residents of the governance infrastructure they are constitutionally entitled to: elected local government, ward-level representation, devolution of urban functions, and access to urban financial frameworks. Courts have increasingly received petitions seeking directions to upgrade classification, and the Supreme Court has signalled that governance vacuums in peri-urban zones are constitutionally problematic. The urban fringe — a zone typically characterised by informality, rapid construction, inadequate services, and regulatory ambiguity — is often the zone where Article 243Q's classification obligation is most urgently needed and most poorly implemented.

The industrial-township proviso has attracted renewed attention as large industrial corridors, private integrated townships, and Special Economic Zones multiply. State governments have invoked the proviso to exclude economically significant areas from municipal governance, arguing that private developers or industrial estates provide adequate services. Critics argue that this defeats the 74th Amendment's purpose of universal urban self-government, because residents of such areas — often including migrant workers and lower-income communities who may not be employed by the industrial establishment itself — have no elected local government accountable to them. Judicial scrutiny of industrial-township notifications has intensified, with courts requiring States to demonstrate that private industrial services genuinely substitute for municipal governance rather than merely co-existing with a governance gap. The constitutional question of whether private service provision can permanently substitute for elected democratic government is one that Article 243Q's proviso raises but does not resolve.

The relationship between Article 243Q classification and the Census of India creates a recurring administrative challenge. The Census, conducted every ten years, provides the most authoritative data on population, density, and employment structure — the primary factors for Article 243Q(2) classification. But census data becomes available approximately two years after the census year, and States typically take additional years to revise municipal boundaries and classifications based on new census data. This means that areas that become urban in one census decade may not receive appropriate municipal governance until the following decade. The resulting governance gap — sometimes a decade long — has been identified by urban governance scholars and by successive Finance Commissions as a structural problem that Article 243Q's mandatory constitution language was intended to prevent but has not, in practice, prevented.
