## Why it matters

Article 32A was a targeted assault on the Supreme Court's role as the guardian of fundamental rights. By preventing the Court from examining the constitutional validity of state legislation in Article 32 proceedings, it would have created a significant gap in the enforcement of Part III: citizens could go to the Supreme Court claiming a fundamental right had been violated, but the Court could not consider whether the state law responsible for that violation was itself unconstitutional. Only the High Courts under Article 226, or the Supreme Court itself in separate appellate jurisdiction, would have retained that power — a fragmentation of remedial authority that would have substantially diluted the integrated fundamental rights enforcement system.

## What Article 32A contained

Inserted by the Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976, Article 32A provided that the Supreme Court shall not, in exercise of its jurisdiction under Article 32, consider the constitutional validity of any Central or State law. The restriction was directed specifically at the writ jurisdiction under Article 32 — the direct enforcement mechanism for fundamental rights — rather than at the appellate or advisory jurisdictions. The practical effect would have been that a person detained under a state law could petition the Supreme Court under Article 32, but the Court could not inquire whether the detaining law was constitutionally valid, only whether the executive had complied with its terms.

## Emergency-era judicial curtailment

Article 32A was one of several Emergency-era provisions that cumulatively attempted to insulate executive action from judicial scrutiny. Taken alongside the expansion of Article 31C (shielding directive-principle legislation from fundamental rights challenge), Article 31D (shielding anti-national activities laws), and amendments making emergency declarations and their duration less susceptible to judicial review, Article 32A formed part of a package whose cumulative effect was to move substantial governmental power beyond the reach of constitutional courts. The Supreme Court's 1976 decision in ADM Jabalpur v. Shivkant Shukla — permitting the suspension of Article 21 during the Emergency — illustrated the risks of this approach.

## Omission by the Forty-Third Amendment

The Constitution (Forty-Third Amendment) Act, 1977 specifically repealed Articles 31D and 32A as priority reversals of Emergency constitutional engineering. Parliament's action confirmed the cross-party consensus that removing Article 32 jurisdiction over state-law constitutionality was incompatible with the Constitution's design of integrated fundamental rights protection. The Forty-Fourth Amendment, enacted shortly after, went further in restoring the fundamental rights framework and adding protections against future Emergency-era overreach.

## Relationship to Article 32

Article 32 — described by Dr. Ambedkar as the "heart and soul of the Constitution" — guarantees the right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights and gives the Court power to issue writs including habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto, and certiorari. Article 32A would have severed the connection between this enforcement mechanism and the Court's power to invalidate unconstitutional state laws. Its omission restored the unity of fundamental rights enforcement: when a person claims that a state law violates her fundamental rights, the Supreme Court in Article 32 proceedings can adjudicate the validity of that law directly.

## Lessons for constitutional design

Article 32A's brief existence and rapid repeal illustrate several points. The fundamental rights provisions of Part III — including Article 32 itself — are amendable by Parliament under Article 368, but amendments that destroy the basic structure are invalid under the doctrine established in Kesavananda Bharati. It is arguable — though never directly decided since Article 32A was repealed before any such challenge was decided — that Article 32A would have been struck down as a violation of the basic structure if it had survived long enough to be challenged. The Court's power to enforce fundamental rights through Article 32 has been treated as itself a basic feature of the Constitution in subsequent cases.

## Current significance

No current litigation flows from Article 32A itself, but the provision's history informs contemporary debates about parliamentary attempts to restrict judicial review of security legislation, anti-terrorism laws, and special courts created by statute. Any statutory attempt to oust Supreme Court jurisdiction under Article 32 — as opposed to channelling review through specified procedures — faces the constitutional barrier that such ouster may violate the basic structure protection of Article 32 as a fundamental right and as a basic feature of the Constitution.
