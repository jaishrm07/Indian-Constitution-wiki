## What this Article does

Article 135 is a constitutional continuity provision that preserves all residual jurisdiction and powers that had vested in the pre-1950 Federal Court under laws existing at the commencement of the Constitution, to the extent they are not already covered by Articles 133 (civil appeals) or 134 (criminal appeals). The article operates as a gap-filler: any matter for which the Federal Court had jurisdiction under pre-constitution legislation but which does not fall within the specific appellate channels created by Articles 133 and 134 continues to be exercisable by the Supreme Court until Parliament legislates to the contrary.

The practical effect was to ensure that no jurisdictional void was created on 26 January 1950 when the Federal Court ceased to exist. Litigants who had pending proceedings or rights under Federal Court jurisdiction did not lose their appellate recourse simply because the new Constitution reorganised the court system.

## The Federal Court and its jurisdiction

The Federal Court of India was established under the Government of India Act, 1935, and began functioning in 1937. It exercised:

- **Original jurisdiction** in federal disputes between provinces and between provinces and the federation under Section 204 of the Government of India Act.
- **Appellate jurisdiction** in civil and criminal matters from provincial High Courts, under Sections 205 to 208.
- **Advisory jurisdiction** on questions of law referred by the Governor-General.

When the Constitution came into force, the Federal Court was abolished and the Supreme Court was constituted in its place. Articles 133 and 134 captured the bulk of the Federal Court's appellate jurisdiction in civil and criminal matters, but some areas of Federal Court jurisdiction — particularly those arising from specific provisions of the Government of India Act, 1935 still in force or from rules made under it — were not cleanly absorbed. Article 135 addressed this residual zone.

## The "existing law" qualifier

The article applies where "jurisdiction and powers in relation to that matter were exercisable by the Federal Court immediately before the commencement of this Constitution under any existing law." This has two requirements:

1. The jurisdiction must have existed in the Federal Court immediately before 26 January 1950.
2. The jurisdiction must have been conferred by "existing law" — defined under Article 366(10) as any law in force in India immediately before the commencement of the Constitution, including laws of legislatures that ceased to exist after the Constitution commenced.

The combination means that Article 135 captures jurisdiction grounded in pre-constitutional statutory instruments, not jurisdictions that were being claimed or expanded at the time of transition.

## The "until Parliament otherwise provides" clause

Article 135 is explicitly subject to Parliament's power to legislate. Parliament may, by law, transfer this residual Federal Court jurisdiction to other courts or tribunals, or extinguish it entirely. This has substantially occurred through post-1950 legislation reorganising appellate jurisdiction across various subject matters. The effect is that Article 135's practical scope has contracted over time as Parliament has occupied the field.

## Key cases

**State of Madras v. C.G. Menon (1954):** The Supreme Court addressed the scope of inherited Federal Court jurisdiction in the context of pre-constitution proceedings, confirming that Article 135 operates as a residual clause preserving jurisdictional continuity, and that it is not rendered redundant merely because most appellate matters are captured by Articles 133 and 134. The article fills the gaps that Articles 133 and 134 leave, not merely the gaps between them.

**Garikapati Veeraya v. N. Subbiah Choudhry (1957):** The Court examined the foundational principle that the right of appeal is governed by the law in force at the commencement of the suit or proceeding. Article 135 ensures that Federal Court-era rights of appeal — vested in litigants before 26 January 1950 — survived the constitutional transition and could be exercised before the Supreme Court as successor institution.

**The Premier Automobiles Ltd. v. Kamlekar Shantaram Wadke (1976):** The Court addressed the broader principle of jurisdictional succession from the Federal Court to the Supreme Court, affirming that the Supreme Court inherited not only the textual jurisdiction conferred by the Constitution but also the institutional standing and authority of the Federal Court as a matter of constitutional continuity.

## Relationship with Articles 372 and 373

Article 135 should be read alongside Articles 372 and 373, which provide for the general continuity of pre-constitution law and legal proceedings. Article 372 preserves all pre-constitution laws until repealed or modified. Article 373 preserves orders and proceedings of pre-constitution courts and tribunals. Article 135 applies specifically to the Federal Court's jurisdiction as an apex court, ensuring that the Supreme Court seamlessly inherited its predecessor's role.

## Current significance

Article 135 is of historical and transitional significance today. The pre-constitution legal landscape that gave the Federal Court its jurisdiction has been comprehensively superseded by parliamentary legislation and constitutional provisions. The article functions as a quiet constitutional floor, invoked only in rare cases involving pre-1950 proceedings or legislative instruments that still operate under the Government of India Act, 1935 framework. Its ongoing relevance is academic and archival — a reminder that the Supreme Court was designed as a seamless successor institution, not a court starting from a blank slate, and that constitutional transitions are managed through explicit continuity provisions of this kind.
