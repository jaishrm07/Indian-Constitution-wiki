  73. Extent of executive power of the Union.—(1) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, the executive power of the Union shall extend—

  (a) to the matters with respect to which Parliament has power to make laws; and

## Why it matters

Article 73 is the scope-defining provision for Union executive power. Article 53 vests executive power in the President and says it is to be exercised either directly or through subordinate officers. But Article 53 does not say how far that power extends — it says nothing about which subjects, which territories, or which activities fall within the Union's executive reach. That is the work of Article 73. Together, Articles 53 and 73 form the constitutional architecture of Union executive authority: the former vests the power; the latter defines its domain.

The "co-extensive" principle — that Union executive power extends as far as Parliament's legislative power — is the article's central organising idea. It means the Union does not need a specific statute authorising each executive act. If a subject appears in List I of the Seventh Schedule (the Union List), the Union executive can act on it even in the absence of Parliament-enacted legislation. This was definitively confirmed by the Supreme Court in Ram Jawaya Kapur (1955) and has shaped Indian executive governance ever since. It means, practically, that the Union government can issue executive orders, notifications, and policy directives on Union List subjects without waiting for Parliament to pass enabling legislation — a doctrine that has enormous implications for the pace of Union-level governance and for the boundaries between legislative and executive action.

Sub-clause (b) introduces an entirely separate source of executive authority: treaty rights and agreements. This is a sovereign power that the Union exercises on behalf of India in international relations. Unlike sub-clause (a), the treaty-execution power is not constrained by the concurrent list proviso. The Union can exercise rights and authority derived from treaties even on subjects that would otherwise fall within State legislative competence. This has significant federal implications: international trade agreements, for instance, may require the Union to take executive action on subjects like agriculture or retail trade that are otherwise State subjects, and the Union's treaty-execution power under Article 73(1)(b) provides the constitutional basis for this reach into areas of State jurisdiction.

The federalism dimension of Article 73 is where the article has its most contested contemporary significance. In a federal constitution, defining the limits of central executive power is as important as defining its scope. The proviso to clause (1)(a) is the Constitution's answer: on concurrent-list subjects (Seventh Schedule, List III), Union executive power does not automatically displace State executive power. The Union can act in a State on concurrent-list subjects only if Parliament has expressly authorised it or the Constitution itself provides for it. This is a structural check on Union executive dominance in the concurrent sphere, designed to preserve the space for State executive action that would otherwise be in danger of being swept away by a broadly interpreted co-extensive doctrine.

## Reading the provision

The opening phrase "Subject to the provisions of this Constitution" makes Article 73's grant of executive power subordinate to all other constitutional provisions. This means that Article 73 cannot be read to override fundamental rights under Part III, the specific grants of power in emergency provisions under Articles 352-360, the restrictions on Union action in State subjects under Article 162, or any other constitutional limitation. The executive power of the Union is broad but it operates within the Constitution's own internal discipline.

Sub-clause (a) ties executive power to Parliament's "power to make laws" — not to laws Parliament has actually made. This is the precise basis for the co-extensive doctrine. The relevant test is not whether legislation exists on the subject but whether Parliament has legislative competence over the subject. If Parliament could make a law on the subject (even if it has not), the Union can exercise executive power on it. This interpretation has been consistently affirmed by the Supreme Court and is now settled constitutional doctrine.

The proviso to clause (1)(a) imposes a territorial and subject-matter qualification. On concurrent-list subjects — over which both Parliament and State legislatures have power to make laws — the Union's executive power "shall not... extend in any State" unless the Constitution expressly provides or Parliament expressly authorises. This means the default position on concurrent-list subjects is that Union executive power stops at the State border, and State executive power continues to operate. Parliament can always break through this default by specifically authorising Union executive action in States on concurrent subjects, but absent such authorisation the concurrent domain is a space of shared executive jurisdiction where State executive action is presumptively valid.

Clause (2) is a transitional device that was essential in 1950 when the Constitution came into force and the new allocation of powers between Union and States was just beginning to take effect. It preserved the executive machinery that existed before the Constitution — State-level departments and officers who had been exercising functions that were now assigned to the Union List — to continue operating until Parliament made alternative provision. Without this clause, the entire apparatus of governance in Union-List subjects would have had to be rebuilt from scratch on the Constitution's commencement date, a practical impossibility. The clause has diminishing relevance today because Parliament has since made provision for nearly all Union List matters, but it remains in force as a residual continuity guarantee.

## Key cases

**Rai Sahib Ram Jawaya Kapur v State of Punjab (1955)** — The landmark ruling that gave doctrinal content to Article 73's "co-extensive" principle. The case arose from the Punjab Government's scheme to nationalise the textbook trade, which affected publishers in contractual relations with the State. The Supreme Court, per Chief Justice Mukherjea, held that the executive power of the Union is not limited to areas where Parliament has already legislated: it extends to all subjects within Parliament's legislative competence, and the Union executive can act on those subjects through executive orders and policies without specific legislative authorisation. The problem before the Court — whether the State could use executive power to implement a scheme in a subject touching on Union competence — was resolved by clarifying that Union executive power is co-extensive with Union legislative competence, not with Union legislative action. This ruling is the foundational authority on Article 73 and continues to be cited in every major case on Union executive power.

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The seven-judge Constitution Bench's comprehensive analysis of Part V drew on Article 73 to explain that the Union's executive power, though constitutionally vested in the President under Article 53, is in practice exercised through the Council of Ministers. The Court held that Article 73 defines the domain of executive power while Articles 53 and 74 define who exercises it and through what mechanism. The President's formal constitutional authority under Article 53 and the scope of that authority under Article 73 together form the constitutional framework within which Cabinet government operates on a day-to-day basis. This integration of scope (Article 73) with exercise (Articles 53-74) is the bedrock of India's constitutional executive architecture.

**Union of India v Hindustan Development Corporation (1993)** — The Supreme Court examined the extent of Union executive power in the context of the government's power to enter commercial contracts and transactions. The Court affirmed that the Union's executive power under Article 73 extends to actions that are "reasonably related" to the subjects within Parliament's legislative competence, including entering into commercial agreements, creating government corporations, and participating in market transactions. The ruling confirmed that Article 73's domain is not limited to purely regulatory or administrative acts but encompasses the full range of governmental activities — commercial, financial, and developmental — that a modern state must engage in to discharge its constitutional functions.

**Government of NCT of Delhi v Union of India (2018 and 2023)** — The Constitution Bench's extensive jurisprudence on the governance of the National Capital Territory tested the boundaries of Article 73 in a federal context. Delhi is not a full State: its legislature lacks power over certain subjects even within List II, and the Lieutenant Governor occupies an executive role that the Union controls. The Court's 2018 ruling established that the elected government of Delhi — not the Lieutenant Governor — has executive authority over subjects within the Delhi Legislative Assembly's competence, while the Union retains executive authority through the L-G on matters reserved to Parliament. The 2023 five-judge bench further clarified the Union-State executive divide in Delhi, making Article 73's concurrent-list proviso central to the analysis. These rulings have made Article 73's federalism dimension one of the most actively litigated constitutional questions in the current period.

**State of Rajasthan v Union of India (1977)** — In the challenge to the Central Government's dissolution of State Assemblies, the Supreme Court examined Article 73 alongside Article 356 to understand the scope of Union executive authority in relation to State governments. The Court confirmed that Union executive power under Article 73 does not encompass the power to direct State governments in matters within State legislative competence — that would require a specific constitutional provision such as Article 356 or Article 365. This case established an important limit: Article 73 defines the Union's affirmative executive domain; it does not by itself authorise Union interference with the States' own executive domain.

## Current significance

Article 73's co-extensive doctrine has acquired fresh significance in the context of policy-making by executive order. The Union government's use of executive notifications, cabinet decisions, and administrative orders to implement major policy changes in Union List areas — including telecommunications, banking, insurance, and national security — without prior parliamentary legislation has been justified by reference to Article 73's co-extensive principle. Critics argue that when the executive action has a significant financial or regulatory impact on citizens, the absence of prior legislative sanction raises due process and rule of law concerns even if Article 73 technically authorises the action. The co-extensive doctrine thus sits at the intersection of the separation of powers debate and the rule of law debate in contemporary Indian public law.

The treaty-execution power in sub-clause (b) has become one of the most contested aspects of Union executive authority in the context of international trade agreements. When the Union negotiates and implements trade agreements that affect State-level industries, agricultural produce, or retail trade — all subjects with a State legislative dimension — the Union invokes the treaty-execution power as its constitutional authority. State governments have contested this, arguing that treaty execution cannot override the federal distribution of legislative powers established by the Seventh Schedule. The question of whether Article 73(1)(b)'s treaty-execution power is subject to the same concurrent-list proviso that limits clause (1)(a) has not been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court and remains an open and contested constitutional question.

The Article 73 co-extensive doctrine also has significant implications for cooperative federalism. Where the Union executive acts on concurrent-list subjects using Article 73 authority — even without specific parliamentary legislation — it can effectively pre-empt State executive action in the same sphere. The concurrent-list proviso is supposed to prevent this, but its enforcement depends on States actively challenging Union executive overreach. In practice, many Union executive actions in the concurrent sphere — including in education, environmental regulation, and public health — proceed without formal legal challenge, establishing administrative precedents that gradually expand Union executive dominance in areas formally shared between the Union and the States.
