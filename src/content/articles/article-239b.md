officialText: |-
  [239B. Power of administrator to promulgate Ordinances during recess of Legislature.—(1) If at any time, except when the Legislature of [the Union territory of [Puducherry]] is in session, the administrator thereof is satisfied that circumstances exist which render it necessary for him to take immediate action, he may promulgate such Ordinances as the circumstances appear to him to require:

  Provided that no such Ordinance shall be promulgated by the administrator except after obtaining instructions from the President in that behalf:

  Provided further that whenever the said Legislature is dissolved, or its functioning remains suspended on account of any action taken under any such law as is referred to in clause (1) of article 239A, the administrator shall not promulgate any Ordinance during the period of such dissolution or suspension.

  (2) An Ordinance promulgated under this article in pursuance of instructions from the President shall be deemed to be an Act of the Legislature of the Union territory which has been duly enacted after complying with the provisions in that behalf contained in any such law as is referred to in clause (1) of article 239A, but every such Ordinance—

  (a) shall be laid before the Legislature of the Union territory and shall cease to operate at the expiration of six weeks from the reassembly of the Legislature or if, before the expiration of that period, a resolution disapproving it is passed by the Legislature, upon the passing of the resolution; and

  (b) may be withdrawn at any time by the administrator after obtaining instructions from the President in that behalf.

  (3) If and so far as an Ordinance under this article makes any provision which would not be valid if enacted in an Act of the Legislature of the Union territory made after complying with the provisions in that behalf contained in any such law as is referred to in clause (1) of article 239A, it shall be void.]

## Why it matters

Article 239B creates the ordinance-making power for UTs that have elected legislatures under Parliament's law — primarily Puducherry, with the framework applied to Delhi by cross-reference in Article 239AA(8). The provision mirrors, in the UT context, the Governor's ordinance power under Article 213 for States and the President's ordinance power under Article 123. Two features mark it as structurally different from the State and Union ordinance powers. First, the administrator cannot act on personal satisfaction alone: the proviso in clause (1) requires that presidential instructions be obtained before promulgation. The ordinance is therefore a joint act — the administrator is the form, the President (acting through the Union executive) is the substantive authority. Second, no ordinance may be promulgated when the legislature is dissolved or suspended under an Article 239A emergency provision, closing the gap that might otherwise allow the administrator to legislate by fiat precisely when normal democratic constraints are absent.

The provision matters because it defines the outer limit of the administrator's role as a legislative actor. In the ordinary Article 239/239A scheme the administrator exercises executive authority delegated by the President; Article 239B is the exceptional grant of quasi-legislative authority for genuine emergencies between sessions, tightly controlled by requiring presidential instructions.

## How courts use it

Courts use Article 239B to determine whether a particular ordinance is intra vires both as to subject matter — it must not go beyond the legislative competence the assembly possesses under the relevant Parliament law — and as to procedure, meaning prior presidential instructions must have been obtained. Clause (3)'s void-for-excess provision is read as a direct limitation: the administrator's ordinance power does not enlarge the assembly's legislative competence. Courts also read the provision alongside the general principles developed under Articles 123 and 213 on the misuse of ordinance powers, including the question of whether repeated re-promulgation to circumvent legislative rejection is constitutionally permissible.

## Key cases

**D.C. Wadhwa v. State of Bihar (1987)** — While dealing with Article 213 (State Governor's ordinance power), the Supreme Court held that the power to issue ordinances is a conditional exception to the normal legislative process and cannot be used as a device to bypass the legislature. The principle that ordinance power is an emergency measure rather than a parallel legislative channel applies equally to Article 239B.

**A.K. Roy v. Union of India (1982)** — The Court addressed the constitutional limits on the President's ordinance power under Article 123, holding that the requirement of presidential satisfaction is justiciable and that courts can examine whether the preconditions for ordinance promulgation have been met. The same framework applies to the President-instruction requirement under Article 239B.

**Krishna Kumar Singh v. State of Bihar (2017)** — The seven-judge Constitution Bench comprehensively reviewed ordinance power under Articles 123 and 213, confirming that repeated re-promulgation without legislative approval is unconstitutional and that the legislative body must be given a genuine opportunity to approve or reject ordinances. This principle governs the Article 239B framework for UT legislatures.

## Current significance

The Article 239B framework for Puducherry has generated periodic disputes between the administrator and the elected government, particularly when the administrator relies on presidential instructions as a basis for legislative action that the local government opposes. The provision's application to Delhi through Article 239AA(8) has become more significant following the 2023 GNCTD Amendment Act's curtailment of the elected government's authority over services, since the interplay between the assembly's restricted competence and the ordinance power creates new questions about the scope of emergency legislation available to the administrator in Delhi.

The requirement of prior presidential instructions as a precondition for any UT ordinance ensures that every ordinance in this framework involves direct central executive involvement — a design feature that both controls the administrator's discretion and keeps the Union government institutionally responsible for UT legislative emergencies. That design continues to be tested whenever there is political divergence between the centrally appointed administrator and the elected UT government.
