## Why it matters

Article 43 is the Constitution's most detailed statement of what a worker's life ought to look like. It goes well beyond fixing a minimum wage floor: it demands that working conditions ensure a decent standard of life and — in a formulation that would be unusual in any constitution — full enjoyment of leisure and social and cultural opportunities. This expansive vision of labour welfare reflects the framers' conviction that work is not merely an economic transaction but a dimension of a person's full humanity, and that the State must actively work to ensure that those who labour are not reduced to bare survival.

The article covers all workers — agricultural, industrial, or otherwise. This universality was deliberate. In 1950, the vast majority of India's workforce was agricultural or engaged in the informal economy, entirely outside the reach of the small body of factory and industrial legislation that existed. Article 43 insists that the constitutional commitment to a living wage and decent conditions is not limited to the organised sector; it extends to every person who works, wherever they work. The additional directive to promote cottage industries in rural areas reflects the Gandhian strand in the Constitution's economic vision — support for decentralised, community-based production as an alternative to industrial concentration.

A living wage, as distinguished from a minimum wage, is a wage sufficient not merely to prevent starvation but to sustain a decent human life: adequate food, clothing, shelter, education for children, and participation in community and cultural life. The Supreme Court has used this constitutional distinction — between subsistence wages and living wages — as a framework for evaluating whether minimum-wage legislation and wage-fixation by industrial tribunals are consistent with the constitutional mandate. Article 43A, added by the 42nd Amendment, builds on this foundation by directing the State to secure workers' participation in the management of their enterprises — extending the constitutional concern from what workers receive to the voice they have in how their workplaces are governed.

The constitutional commitment to "full enjoyment of leisure and social and cultural opportunities" deserves particular attention. No comparable constitutional provision in most democratic systems goes this far. It reflects the framers' understanding that extreme poverty and overwork are not merely economic deprivations but cultural and social ones — that a person who works sixteen-hour days and cannot afford any leisure, cultural participation, or social engagement is not living a fully human life even if they are technically above a starvation wage. This formulation makes Article 43 the constitutional home for arguments about rest periods, limits on working hours, paid annual leave, and the right of workers to participate in community and cultural life on terms compatible with their human dignity.

## Reading the provision

The provision addresses four related but distinct matters. First, a living wage — the core of the article. Second, conditions of work ensuring a decent standard of life — targeting the physical and contractual environment of work: hours, safety, rest, and dignity. Third, full enjoyment of leisure and social and cultural opportunities — a uniquely ambitious constitutional formulation, recognising that workers are not merely units of production but social and cultural beings whose participation in community life must be protected from the depredations of overwork and poverty. Fourth, the promotion of cottage industries on an individual or co-operative basis in rural areas — a Gandhian prescription for decentralised production that the Constitution chose to give constitutional expression.

The article leaves the method of implementation open: the State may act "by suitable legislation or economic organisation or in any other way." This flexibility acknowledges that securing a living wage and decent conditions for all workers — across agriculture, industry, domestic work, and the informal economy — requires a range of instruments: statutory minimum wages, collective bargaining frameworks, wage boards, cooperative organisation, and State economic intervention. Courts have used this breadth to resist the argument that the directive applies only to formal, industrial employment.

The distinction between a minimum wage and a living wage is not merely semantic. Industrial tribunals in India apply a three-tier classification to wages: the minimum wage (necessary for bare subsistence), the fair wage (somewhere between minimum and living), and the living wage (sufficient for a full human life consistent with Article 43). The constitutional directive points toward the living wage as the aspiration, meaning that industrial adjudication and legislative wage-fixing must be measured against an objective that exceeds the minimum floor even if they cannot always immediately reach the living-wage target.

## Key cases

**Unichoy v. State of Kerala (1961)** — The Supreme Court held that the concept of a living wage in Article 43 sets a constitutional aspiration that minimum wage legislation must be oriented toward, and that wages fixed by government notification ought to take into account the worker's need for a decent standard of life — including food, clothing, shelter, education, and medical care — not merely bare subsistence. The Court established the doctrinal hierarchy between minimum wage, fair wage, and living wage that continues to inform wage jurisprudence, making clear that the constitutional target is the living wage, even where current minimum-wage notifications fall short of it. The case is the first definitive judicial articulation of the minimum/fair/living wage trichotomy derived from Article 43.

**Standard Vacuum Refining Co. v. Their Workmen (1961)** — The Supreme Court, in a wage dispute before the industrial tribunal, drew on Article 43 to confirm that wage-fixation must be guided by the living wage standard. The Court held that where a profitable enterprise can afford to pay closer to a living wage, the tribunal should award accordingly, treating the constitutional mandate as a factor that tilts the balance in wage disputes toward the worker's standard-of-life entitlements rather than merely toward market-clearing wages. The decision reinforced that the employer's capacity to pay is a relevant constitutional consideration alongside the worker's need for a living wage.

**People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (1982)** — The Asiad Workers case arose from construction workers engaged on Delhi venues for the Asian Games being paid below the statutory minimum wage. The Supreme Court held that this constituted forced labour within Article 23's prohibition, reasoning that Article 43's living-wage directive established the constitutional benchmark and that any arrangement by which workers are paid less than the minimum wage — particularly in State-sanctioned projects — is incompatible with the constitutional design. The judgment is among the most important in connecting the living-wage directive directly to an enforceable fundamental right. Justice Bhagwati held that the constitutional commitment to a living wage makes below-minimum-wage payment not merely a statutory breach but a fundamental rights violation. This connection between Article 43's living-wage aspiration and Article 23's prohibition on forced labour is one of the most creative bridges in Indian labour constitutional law.

**Workmen v. Reptakos Brett & Co. Ltd. (1992)** — The Supreme Court laid down the criteria for fixing minimum wages and confirmed that the relevant constituents — the worker's food, clothing, housing, fuel and lighting, children's education, medical expenses, and a small provision for old-age and social obligations — must all be factored into the minimum wage calculation. The Court grounded this requirement in Article 43, holding that minimum wage fixation divorced from these life-sustaining requirements would be constitutionally inadequate as a progressive step toward the living wage that the directive mandates. These criteria, known as the Reptakos Brett criteria, are still used in minimum wage notifications and industrial award calculations.

## Current significance

Article 43 is the constitutional anchor for ongoing debates about the Code on Wages (2019), which consolidates minimum wage, payment of wages, bonus, and equal remuneration laws but has been criticised for weakening enforcement mechanisms and potentially reducing real wage floors for some categories of workers. The distinction between a national floor wage and a living wage — the former a policy instrument, the latter a constitutional aspiration — continues to shape public interest litigation and parliamentary advocacy about the adequacy of India's wage framework.

The article is increasingly relevant to gig economy workers, agricultural labourers, domestic workers, and informal construction workers, all of whom fall outside or at the margins of formal wage protection. Platform workers delivering food, driving cabs, and performing other digitally intermediated tasks lack access to overtime protections, rest periods, and the social and cultural conditions that Article 43 envisages. Constitutional litigation invoking Article 43, alongside Article 21 and Article 41, argues that the State's failure to extend meaningful wage and conditions protection to these workers is inconsistent with the constitutional commitment to a decent standard of life for all who work.

The Article 43 mandate for promotion of cottage industries retains practical relevance in debates about Khadi and Village Industries Commission programmes, the handloom weaver support schemes, and the government's policy of promoting self-employment through schemes such as MUDRA loans. The constitutional status of this directive has been used to argue that State fiscal support for artisanal and cottage production is not merely a welfare measure but a constitutional obligation — one that the State cannot abandon in favour of large-scale industrial policy without engaging with the constitutional preference for decentralised, community-based production in rural areas.

Courts have also used Article 43 in the context of scheme workers — anganwadi workers, ASHA workers, and mid-day meal workers — who perform essential State functions but are classified as "honorary volunteers" rather than employees, thereby denying them minimum wage protections. Litigation relying on Article 43 alongside Articles 14 and 21 has sought to challenge this classification as constitutionally impermissible, arguing that the constitutional standard of a living wage for all workers does not admit of an exception for workers performing State welfare services on the pretext that they are volunteers rather than employees.

The relationship between Article 43 and Article 43A is one of constitutional complementarity. Article 43 addresses what workers receive from the labour relationship — wages, conditions, social participation. Article 43A addresses what voice workers have in the enterprise. Together they represent the Constitution's full vision of the worker as a stakeholder in production: entitled not merely to a living wage but to a seat at the table where decisions about the enterprise, including decisions about wages and conditions, are made. This complementarity is increasingly invoked by constitutional litigants and scholars who argue that the two directives, read together, require a comprehensive industrial democracy framework that India's legislative record has conspicuously failed to build.

The Code on Wages 2019 — one of four Labour Codes enacted by the Modi government to consolidate and rationalise labour legislation — introduced a national floor wage and simplified the minimum wage structure but has been criticised for increasing employer discretion in wage-setting and reducing the coverage of workers entitled to minimum wage protection. Article 43's constitutional standard remains the benchmark against which these legislative changes are evaluated: the constitutional aspiration is a living wage for all workers, and simplification of the wage framework is constitutionally permissible only if it maintains, rather than erodes, the progressive journey toward that aspiration.

Internationally, Article 43's living wage aspiration anticipates by decades the campaign for a "living wage" that has become central to progressive labour politics in the United States, United Kingdom, and other countries. The Indian Constitution's explicit constitutional recognition that the target of wage policy is not subsistence but a fully human life places India's constitutional design in the vanguard of democratic welfare-state constitutionalism, even as the gap between constitutional aspiration and legislative and judicial reality remains wide.

The Supreme Court's monitoring of minimum wage compliance through the PUDR framework — holding that State-funded projects must pay workers at least the statutory minimum wage or face contempt of court — represents one of the most direct judicial enforcements of a directive principle in Indian constitutional history. Article 43's living wage standard is the constitutional anchor for this monitoring, even when the immediate legal trigger is Article 23's prohibition on forced labour. The connection that the Asiad Workers case drew between below-minimum-wage payment and forced labour has made Article 43's directive a source of enforceable fundamental rights obligations in a way that no other directive principle — with the possible exception of Articles 41 and 45 — has achieved.

The interaction between Article 43 and Articles 19(1)(g) and 19(6) is important in the context of minimum wage challenges. Employers have occasionally challenged minimum wage notifications as unreasonable restrictions on their freedom to carry on business. Courts have consistently upheld minimum wage legislation, treating Article 43's living wage directive as providing a compelling constitutional reason for the restriction that falls squarely within Article 19(6)'s "interest of the general public" ground. This means that Article 43 functions not only as a directive to legislate minimum wages but as the constitutional justification for the restriction on employer freedom that such legislation necessarily entails.
