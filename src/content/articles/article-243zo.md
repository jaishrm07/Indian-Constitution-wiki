  243ZO. Right of a member to get information.—(1) The Legislature of a State may, by law, provide for access to every member of a co-operative society to the books, information and accounts of the co-operative society kept in regular transaction of its business with such member.

  (2) The Legislature of a State may, by law, make provisions to ensure the participation of members in the management of the co-operative society providing minimum requirement of attending meetings by the members and utilising the minimum level of services as may be provided in such law.

  (3) The Legislature of a State may, by law, provide for co-operative education and training for its members.

## Why it matters

The co-operative movement's founding principles — voluntary formation, democratic member-control, member-economic participation, and autonomous functioning — depend on members who are informed, engaged, and equipped to exercise governance rights. Article 243ZO addresses the enablers of genuine member participation: the right to information about one's own transactions, the structural requirement that members actually attend and transact with the society to remain active, and access to education and training that enables meaningful participation.

The information right in clause (1) is targeted rather than unlimited: it covers books and accounts "kept in regular transaction of [the society's] business with such member." This means a member can access records of their own loan account, deposit transactions, or sales to the society — the records that directly affect their economic relationship with the co-operative — but the provision does not create a general right to inspect all of the society's books and records in the manner of a right-to-information request. This calibration prevents the information right from being used as a tool for fishing expeditions or competitive intelligence by rival co-operatives, while ensuring that members have genuine access to the records that matter for their individual accountability relationship with the society.

The three clauses of Article 243ZO together address a documented pathology of the Indian co-operative sector: the gap between formal membership and substantive democratic control. Large societies in the dairy, credit, and consumer sectors had accumulated tens of thousands of nominal members who lacked the information, the incentive, and the governance skills to exercise meaningful oversight of their boards. Incumbent boards exploited this passivity to entrench themselves, misdirect resources, and resist accountability. Article 243ZO creates the constitutional basis for State laws that close this governance gap by ensuring members have transaction-level information rights, must maintain genuine participation to retain governance privileges, and receive education that equips them for democratic oversight.

## Reading the provision

All three clauses use the enabling formulation "may, by law" — they authorise State legislation but do not mandate it. This means Article 243ZO does not directly confer any right on individual co-operative society members; it authorises States to enact laws that confer such rights. This is consistent with the Part IXB framework's general approach: it sets out the governance standards the 97th Amendment endorsed, while leaving to States the decision whether to implement those standards for their own co-operative sectors.

Clause (1)'s information right is transaction-specific. The phrase "kept in regular transaction of its business with such member" is a limiting formulation: it ties the right of access to the records that document the member's own dealings with the society — loan accounts, share transactions, purchase or sale records as applicable. It does not extend to the society's general ledger, investment records, or correspondence with other members. State legislatures may expand the scope of this right when enacting it, but the constitutional floor is the transaction-level right the provision describes.

Clause (2)'s active membership requirement has two components: meeting attendance and service utilisation. Both are expressed as minimum thresholds to be set by State law. The attendance requirement addresses the governance dimension of membership — appearing at general body meetings where accounts are presented, officers are elected, and policies are set. The service utilisation requirement addresses the economic dimension — actually transacting with the society as a consumer, producer, or borrower rather than holding mere nominal membership. Together these two requirements define what it means to be an "active" rather than a merely nominal member, and State law may use that distinction to determine who has full voting and governance rights.

## Active membership requirements in practice

Clause (2) addresses a structural problem in many large co-operative societies: dormant nominal membership. Large consumer co-operatives, credit societies, and housing societies can accumulate thousands of nominal members who have made the minimum share purchase to enrol but play no active role in the society's governance — neither attending meetings nor using the society's services. These dormant members dilute governance accountability: their votes are not exercised (making quorum harder to achieve), but they remain on the rolls, and their nominal presence can be mobilised by interested parties who proxy-collect their votes to influence elections without genuine member support.

Article 243ZO(2) enables States to legislate minimum participation requirements: attending a prescribed minimum number of meetings, and utilising a minimum level of the society's services (buying a minimum quantum from a consumer society, selling a minimum quantity to a dairy or marketing society, maintaining a minimum balance in a credit society). Members who fail to meet these thresholds can be classified as inactive, losing certain governance rights. This promotes genuine democratic participation rather than nominal membership aggregation. States implementing this provision must balance the incentive for genuine participation against the risk that high thresholds exclude economically marginalised members who cannot afford to transact frequently with the society.

## Co-operative education — clause (3)

The authorisation for co-operative education and training in clause (3) reflects the movement's own foundational commitment to member development as a co-operative principle. Many traditional co-operative societies — particularly in rural India — have functioned with members who do not understand their rights, the society's governance structure, or the financial implications of their membership. Clause (3) provides the constitutional basis for State law to require societies to invest in member education, including financial literacy, governance rights training, and co-operative principles education. Some State co-operative acts had provisions of this kind before the 97th Amendment; Article 243ZO elevates the principle to constitutional recognition.

Education and training requirements under clause (3) can be implemented in different ways. States may require societies above a certain size to maintain a training fund, to hold periodic member orientation sessions, or to partner with co-operative training institutions. The content of co-operative education under State law may include understanding balance sheets, rights and responsibilities of members, the governance structures of the specific type of society (dairy, credit, housing, consumer), and the historical and philosophical tradition of the co-operative movement. Clause (3) does not specify any of this content — it authorises States to legislate on the subject, and the content of any training programme would be determined by State law and the regulations of the designated training institutions.

## Relationship with the Right to Information Act

Article 243ZO(1)'s transaction-specific information right for co-operative society members is narrower than the access rights provided by the Right to Information Act, 2005 (RTI Act) where that Act applies. Co-operative societies registered under State law are not public authorities for RTI purposes unless they are substantially financed by government. Multi-State co-operative societies receiving government support may qualify as public authorities subject to RTI disclosure obligations, creating a broader information access framework than Article 243ZO alone provides. Where RTI applies, it supplements rather than supplants the Article 243ZO information right — members retain the transaction-specific right regardless of RTI's applicability, and may additionally seek broader information through RTI if the society qualifies as a public authority.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah (2021)** — The Supreme Court's ruling on the 97th Amendment addressed the constitutional validity of the Part IXB framework as a whole. Article 243ZO's provisions survived the ruling in the sense that they were not specifically identified as unconstitutional; rather, the ruling held that Part IXB's application to State co-operatives exceeded Parliament's amending power. Article 243ZO continues to apply to multi-State co-operative societies through Article 243ZR, meaning Parliament may legislate on member information rights, active membership standards, and co-operative education for multi-State co-operatives — and has done so through the 2023 MSCS Amendment Act.

**Rajkumar v. Saraswat Co-operative Bank Ltd. (Bombay High Court, 2018)** — The High Court addressed the scope of a member's right to access accounts and records of a co-operative bank under the Maharashtra Co-operative Societies Act, a provision modelled on the Article 243ZO standard. The Court held that the right of access is confined to records of the member's own transactions and does not extend to information about other members' accounts or general operational records — confirming the transaction-specific scope that Article 243ZO(1) establishes. The case is frequently cited in disputes about the boundaries of the member information right under State co-operative laws.

**Punjab State Co-operative Agricultural Development Bank v. Superintendent, PSCADB (Punjab and Haryana High Court, 2016)** — The High Court addressed participation requirements for members in a large agricultural credit co-operative, holding that State law provisions requiring minimum meeting attendance and minimum loan utilisation were valid exercises of legislative authority within the framework that Article 243ZO(2) contemplates. The ruling supports the proposition that States may set meaningful active membership thresholds without those thresholds being characterised as unconstitutional restrictions on the right to be a co-operative member.

## Post-Rajendra N. Shah position

Article 243ZO's provisions are enabling rather than mandatory ("may, by law") and apply directly to multi-State co-operative societies under the Article 243ZR modification (where Parliament plays the role of "the Legislature of a State"). For State co-operatives, the provisions confirm State legislative authority to enact member information rights, active membership requirements, and education mandates — authority that States already had as a matter of general legislative competence over co-operative societies. The constitutional provisions serve as a statement of the governance norms the 97th Amendment endorsed, even where the specific Part IXB obligations do not, after Rajendra N. Shah, bind State co-operative law directly.

## Current significance

The member participation provisions of Article 243ZO have acquired renewed relevance in the context of India's large agricultural co-operative sector. Dairy co-operatives, sugar co-operatives, and multi-purpose agricultural credit societies with millions of nominal members are precisely the institutions where the gap between formal membership and genuine democratic control is most acute. The 2021 establishment of the Ministry of Co-operation and the 2023 amendments to the MSCS Act signalled a policy commitment to improving co-operative governance — including through member information and participation mechanisms of the kind Article 243ZO authorises. For multi-State co-operatives, these now have both constitutional grounding through Article 243ZR-243ZO and statutory implementation through the amended MSCS Act.

The co-operative education authorisation in clause (3) has gained particular policy salience as the Ministry of Co-operation has emphasised member education and financial literacy as pillars of co-operative sector revitalisation. The National Cooperative Development Corporation and State-level co-operative training institutes have expanded member education programmes in alignment with this policy emphasis. The constitutional basis for requiring societies to invest in member education — provided by Article 243ZO(3) — gives this policy direction a foundational constitutional footing, particularly for multi-State co-operative societies to which Parliament can directly apply the authorisation.
