## What this Article does

Article 161 confers on the Governor the power to grant pardons, reprieves, respites, or remissions of punishment, and to suspend, remit, or commute sentences, for offences against laws that fall within the State's executive domain. This power mirrors the President's pardoning power under Article 72 but is confined to offences under laws to which the State's executive power extends. Unlike Article 72, Article 161 does not extend to death sentences imposed under Central legislation, a distinction that has been extensively litigated.

## The five forms of clemency

The article distinguishes five distinct forms of executive clemency:

- **Pardon**: complete forgiveness of an offence, effectively wiping out both conviction and sentence.
- **Reprieve**: temporary suspension of a sentence, typically pending further proceedings or a mercy petition.
- **Respite**: substitution of a lighter sentence for a heavier one on special grounds such as the physical condition of the convict.
- **Remission**: reduction in the amount of a sentence without changing its character — a ten-year term reduced to six years, for example.
- **Commutation**: substitution of a less severe form of punishment — converting a death sentence to life imprisonment, or a rigorous sentence to a simple one.

## Scope: State laws versus Central laws

The critical limiting phrase in Article 161 is "any offence against any law relating to a matter to which the executive power of the State extends." This ties the pardoning power directly to Article 162, which defines the extent of the State's executive power. The Governor can grant clemency for offences under State laws (State List subjects) and, in concurrent-list subjects, where the State's executive power has not been excluded by Parliament's express conferral of authority on the Union.

A recurring controversy has been whether the Governor has power under Article 161 in death-sentence cases where the conviction is under Central law. The Supreme Court has consistently held that a death sentence confirmed by courts on a charge under Central legislation falls within the President's clemency power under Article 72 rather than the Governor's Article 161 power, even when the sentence was imposed by a State High Court. However, where a death sentence has been imposed for an offence under a State law, the Governor's Article 161 power applies.

## Ministerial advice and the Governor's role

The pardoning power under Article 161 is in principle exercisable on the aid and advice of the State Council of Ministers. The Governor is not expected to apply independent judgment about the merits of the clemency petition — that judgment belongs to the elected State government, which considers the case and sends a recommendation. Where the State government recommends clemency, the Governor is generally expected to act on that advice. The power cannot be exercised arbitrarily, and courts have held that the material considered must be placed before the Governor so that any grant of clemency is an informed act.

## Key cases

**Epuru Sudhakar v Government of Andhra Pradesh (2006)** — The Supreme Court held that the pardoning power under Article 161, like the President's power under Article 72, is not absolute and is subject to limited judicial review. The Court ruled that an order of remission can be struck down if passed without application of mind, on extraneous or political considerations, or in violation of the rights of the victim. This was the first significant ruling treating gubernatorial clemency under Article 161 as reviewable rather than an act of sovereign grace beyond the reach of courts.

**Satpal v State of Haryana (2000)** — The Supreme Court set aside an order of remission by the Governor of Haryana, holding that the Governor had not been supplied with the relevant materials by the State government, and that the power under Article 161 must be exercised in an informed and non-arbitrary manner. The decision confirmed that the constitutional grant of clemency power carries an implicit duty to consider relevant material.

**State of Punjab v Joginder Singh (1990)** — The Court clarified that when a sentence of death has been confirmed and a petition for remission is pending before the Governor under Article 161, inordinate delay in deciding the petition can itself become a ground for commutation, as prolonged uncertainty constitutes additional punishment beyond what the court imposed.

**Kehar Singh v Union of India (1989)** — Although this case concerned Article 72 and the President's power, the Court's holding that the pardoning power is a constitutional function separate from the judicial power, and that the executive may independently consider the matter beyond what the courts have weighed, is equally applicable to Article 161.

## Current significance

The pardoning power under Article 161 has attracted public and judicial attention in cases involving convicted politicians, life-term prisoners, and death-row convicts serving sentences for State-law offences. Pre-election remissions affecting large categories of prisoners have been challenged as politically motivated acts violating the Epuru Sudhakar standard of non-arbitrariness. The Supreme Court's insistence that the power is not a political favour but a quasi-judicial act subject to judicial review has made it a live constitutional issue, particularly in states where remissions have been challenged as rewarding political loyalty or being extended in a discriminatory manner to particular communities ahead of elections.
