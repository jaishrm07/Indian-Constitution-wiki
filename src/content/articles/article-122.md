## Why it matters

Parliamentary sovereignty requires that the legislature be the master of its own proceedings. If courts could override the Speaker's rulings or invalidate Bills because a procedural rule was not followed, the judiciary could effectively control the legislature's conduct of business. Article 122 prevents that. But the immunity it creates is not absolute: it covers the internal management of parliamentary procedure, not the constitutional validity of what Parliament does. The line between these two things — between how Parliament acts (immune) and what Parliament enacts (reviewable) — is one of the most actively litigated questions in Indian constitutional law.

Article 122 operates alongside Article 105 (privileges of members) to create the constitutional framework for parliamentary immunity. Article 105 protects what members say and how they vote from external legal proceedings. Article 122 protects the internal management of parliamentary proceedings from judicial invalidation. Together they ensure that neither the presiding officer's exercise of procedural authority nor the substantive content of parliamentary debate can be dragged into courts by litigants seeking to challenge parliamentary outcomes on procedural grounds.

The immunity is a structural feature of the separation of powers, not a personal privilege for politicians. Its purpose is to protect the legislature as an institution, ensuring that the elected representatives of the people are free to conduct their business without judicial interference in how they manage their own affairs — subject always to the Constitution's own requirements, which cannot be waived by Parliament's claim to procedural autonomy.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — procedural irregularity not reviewable:** Courts cannot invalidate parliamentary proceedings merely because Parliament did not follow its own rules of procedure. If a Bill was passed without the prescribed number of readings, or a division of votes was not properly called, or a member spoke for longer than the rules permitted, none of these procedural breaches gives a court the power to declare the resulting legislation invalid.

The word "proceedings" is key. It covers the internal conduct of parliamentary business: debate, voting, committee referrals, the Speaker's rulings in the chair, and the management of time and agenda. It does not cover the constitutional conditions that Parliament must satisfy — such as:
- the requirement that a Bill be passed by both Houses under Article 107;
- the requirement that a constitutional amendment satisfy the special majority under Article 368;
- the requirement that a Money Bill bear the Speaker's certificate under Article 110(4).

Those conditions are constitutional requirements, not procedural rules, and courts can examine whether they have been met.

**Clause (2) — personal immunity for presiding officers:** The Speaker, Deputy Speaker, Chairman of Rajya Sabha, Deputy Chairman, and other members exercising constitutional powers over procedure and order cannot be taken to court for their exercise of those powers. A member expelled from Parliament cannot sue the Speaker in a civil court for wrongful expulsion, nor can a member who is ruled out of order bring a contempt action against the presiding officer for the ruling.

The immunity is personal and functional. It attaches to constitutional powers over procedure and order. The Speaker acting in a quasi-judicial capacity — for instance, deciding a disqualification petition under the Tenth Schedule — is not exercising a "procedural" power in the Article 122 sense: disqualification decisions affect membership rights and are constitutionally mandated to follow principles of natural justice. The immunity therefore does not apply.

**What is a "proceeding" for Article 122 purposes:**

Protected as proceedings:
- Debates and speeches in the chamber
- Voting and division results
- Committee proceedings and reports
- The Speaker's rulings on points of order
- Procedural decisions on admission of Bills

Not protected as mere procedural irregularity:
- Failure to satisfy constitutional passage requirements (Article 107)
- Failure to obtain Presidential recommendation for financial Bills (Article 117)
- Failure to follow the special majority for constitutional amendments (Article 368)
- Unreasonable delay in deciding disqualification petitions (Tenth Schedule)

**The Article 105–Article 122 distinction:** Article 105 provides substantive privilege immunity — members cannot be proceeded against in any court for anything said or voted in Parliament. Article 122 provides procedural immunity — courts cannot invalidate proceedings on procedural-irregularity grounds. These are overlapping but distinct shields. A member who says something in Parliament is protected by Article 105 from defamation proceedings; the proceedings in which the member spoke are protected by Article 122 from invalidation on procedural grounds.

## Key cases

**In re: Powers, Privileges and Immunities of State Legislatures, Special Reference No. 1 of 1964:** The Supreme Court's advisory opinion on the analogous State provision (Article 212) established the foundational distinction: courts cannot inquire into proceedings for procedural irregularity, but "proceedings" has constitutional limits. A legislative act that violates a specific constitutional mandate — rather than merely departing from a procedural rule — may still attract judicial scrutiny. This framework was extended to Article 122 in subsequent cases.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Hon'ble Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007):** A nine-judge Constitution Bench — the most significant Article 122 authority — held that the expulsion of members of Parliament for alleged involvement in accepting bribes to ask questions (the cash-for-queries scandal) was judicially reviewable. The Court held that Article 122 protects Parliament's power to regulate its own proceedings but does not immunise Parliament's punitive power over members from constitutional scrutiny. Expulsion affects constitutional rights and goes beyond mere procedural regulation. Courts can review whether Parliament's punitive action stays within constitutional bounds, even if they cannot review internal procedural decisions.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992):** The Constitution Bench held that the Speaker's decisions under the Tenth Schedule (anti-defection law) are amenable to judicial review for constitutional violation, even though the Speaker is the constitutionally designated tribunal. The Tenth Schedule expressly requires the Speaker to apply constitutional criteria and to decide according to quasi-judicial standards. Article 122 does not shield constitutionally mandated quasi-judicial decisions from review.

**Kalpana Mehta v. Union of India (2018):** A Constitution Bench confirmed that parliamentary committees' reports and the documents before them may be placed before courts in appropriate cases, and that Article 122 does not create an evidentiary exclusion for parliamentary material in all contexts — only a bar on challenging the proceedings as such.

## Current significance

The Speaker's disqualification jurisdiction has become the central live issue under Article 122. In multiple State legislatures, Speakers have deliberately delayed deciding anti-defection petitions — sometimes for years — to allow governments to remain in power despite having lost a legislative majority. Petitioners have argued that such delay is not a "proceeding" entitled to Article 122 immunity but a constitutional dereliction. The Supreme Court in Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020) held that the Speaker must decide disqualification petitions within a reasonable time and that unreasonable delay itself constitutes a violation of the Tenth Schedule that courts may address. The Court took the further step of directing the Speaker to decide within a fixed period — an order that would have been unthinkable under an absolute reading of Article 122. The emerging doctrine draws a firm line: Article 122 protects Parliament's management of its own legislative process but does not immunise the Speaker's adjudicatory and quasi-judicial functions, or Parliament's punitive powers over members, from constitutional review.

The money bill certification controversy has raised a new dimension of Article 122's limits. Petitioners in the Aadhaar case and Rojer Mathew argued that the Speaker's certification of Bills as Money Bills is reviewable by courts despite Article 122, because it involves not an internal procedural ruling but a constitutional classification that determines which procedure applies. The Supreme Court's Constitution Benches in those cases accepted that the certification question could be examined — a significant narrowing of Article 122's previously understood scope — though the Court ultimately relied on other grounds to decide the merits. The question of exactly how much of the Speaker's certification function is shielded by Article 122 and how much is exposed to constitutional review remains unsettled.

Article 122 also sits in tension with the right to information and parliamentary transparency. Parliamentarians and civil society groups have argued that parliamentary committees' proceedings, internal communications, and unpublished reports are protected from disclosure under Article 122's proceedings immunity. Courts have grappled with the distinction between the immunity against challenge (Article 122's core purpose) and the invocation of Article 122 to resist transparency — a use that the framers did not contemplate and that the Supreme Court has been cautious about endorsing without qualification.
