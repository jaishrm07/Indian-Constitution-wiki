  94. Vacation and resignation of, and removal from, the offices of Speaker and Deputy Speaker.— A member holding office as Speaker or Deputy Speaker of the House of the People—

  (a) shall vacate his office if he ceases to be a member of the House of the People;

  (b) may at any time, by writing under his hand addressed, if such member is the Speaker, to the Deputy Speaker, and if such member is the Deputy Speaker, to the Speaker, resign his office; and

  (c) may be removed from his office by a resolution of the House of the People passed by a majority of all the then members of the House:

## Why it matters

Article 94 is the accountability provision for the Lok Sabha's two presiding officers. The manner in which the Speaker and Deputy Speaker leave office — whether voluntarily, automatically, or by forced removal — determines the stability of the House's governance and the independence of the presiding chair. The framers' design was precise: the Speaker must have sufficient security of tenure to preside impartially, but must ultimately remain accountable to the elected House that chose them.

The absolute-majority removal threshold is the provision's central constitutional design feature. Requiring affirmative votes from a majority of all the House's members — not merely of those present and voting — means that absences, abstentions, and independent members all effectively count as votes against removal. For a 543-member Lok Sabha, 272 affirmative votes are necessary. In practice, this threshold can only be crossed if the ruling coalition's own members vote against the Speaker — a constitutionally conceivable but politically exceptional event. The threshold thus functions primarily as a deterrent and legitimating device rather than as a regularly exercised accountability mechanism.

The dissolution proviso has acquired practical importance in close elections. When the Lok Sabha dissolves, all members technically cease to be members — including the Speaker, which would ordinarily trigger clause (a)'s automatic vacation. The proviso carves out an exception, keeping the Speaker in the constitutional office through the transition period until immediately before the new House's first sitting. This ensures uninterrupted presiding authority and allows the incumbent Speaker to oversee the administrative formalities of the transition, including by convention facilitating the appointment of the pro tem Speaker who administers the oath to new members.

The interaction between Article 94 and the Tenth Schedule has made the removal notice a tool for parliamentary floor management in ways the framers could not have anticipated. By requiring the Speaker to step aside from anti-defection adjudication once a valid removal notice is received (per the Nabam Rebia doctrine), the Opposition can use the mere filing of a removal notice to freeze the Speaker's most consequential power — the power to disqualify defecting members — at a critical moment in a confidence or floor test.

## Reading the provision

Clause (a)'s automatic vacation ties the Speaker's authority to continuous democratic mandate. The Speaker is constitutionally the House's presiding officer, not an officer of the State who holds office independently of the House — if the Speaker loses their Lok Sabha seat through disqualification, resignation, or death, they immediately cease to be Speaker. No separate vacation notice, no parliamentary resolution, and no executive action is required. The vacation is instantaneous.

Clause (b) creates a simple resignation mechanism between the two presiding officers — the Speaker resigns to the Deputy Speaker and vice versa. This cross-addressed resignation requirement prevents either officer from quietly resigning without formal notice to the remaining officer, ensuring the House's administration is always formally aware of a vacancy. If both posts are simultaneously vacant — a theoretically possible though practically improbable scenario — the House would need to elect both officers, but the Rules of Procedure provide for a temporary chair in the interim.

Clause (c) is the removal mechanism. "A majority of all the then members" is an absolute majority, not a simple majority of members present. "Then members" refers to the current, live membership of the House — if deaths, disqualifications, or resignations have reduced the House from 543 to, say, 530 members, the majority is calculated on 530. The fourteen-day advance notice proviso serves two purposes: it gives the Speaker time to prepare any response to the charges being made, and it forces the Opposition to publicly commit to a removal attempt — which carries reputational and political risks if it fails.

The dissolution proviso departs from the general rule that all members (including the Speaker as a member) cease to be members on dissolution. The Speaker's survival through dissolution is limited — "until immediately before the first meeting of the House of the People after the dissolution." The instant the new House meets for the first time, the incumbent Speaker ceases to hold the position even if they won re-election and are a member of the new House. The new House must elect its own Speaker. The dissolution proviso thus creates a carefully bounded continuity — present during the inter-session vacuum, terminated the moment the new House constitutes itself.

## The Somnath Chatterjee episode

The most constitutionally significant test of the removal framework's implications came during the Fourteenth Lok Sabha when Speaker Somnath Chatterjee, belonging to the Communist Party of India (Marxist), refused to resign when his party withdrew support from the UPA government in 2008 and was later expelled from the CPI(M). The CPI(M) demanded he resign; he declined, citing the constitutional principle that the Speaker holds an independent constitutional office and should not resign at a party's direction.

No removal resolution was moved against Chatterjee under Article 94(c) — the ruling coalition would not have voted for it, and the threshold could not have been crossed. His tenure continued until the dissolution of the Fourteenth Lok Sabha in 2009. The episode illustrated the constitutional protection the Speaker's office has in practice: the absolute-majority threshold means that only a ruling coalition that has already lost its majority could credibly threaten removal, and a ruling coalition that has lost its majority typically falls on a no-confidence motion rather than a separate removal proceeding.

The Chatterjee episode also clarified that there is no constitutional convention in India requiring a Speaker to resign when their party leaves the government — unlike the British convention where Speakers resign their party membership entirely. The Indian Constitution's design keeps the Speaker as a party member but gives them sufficient tenure security that they can resist partisan pressure while in the chair.

## The Nabam Rebia doctrine and the removal notice

The Arunachal Pradesh political crisis of 2015-16 produced the most significant interpretation of the removal framework in recent constitutional history. The Speaker of the Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly faced a removal notice from a section of the ruling party's members. While the notice was pending, the Speaker proceeded to act under the Tenth Schedule — disqualifying the very members who had moved the removal notice.

The Supreme Court in Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016) held, by a five-judge Constitution Bench, that a Speaker facing a removal notice cannot exercise Tenth Schedule disqualification powers. The reasoning was that permitting the Speaker to determine the composition of the House — by disqualifying members — while their own tenure is the subject of a pending accountability motion would corrupt the accountability mechanism that Article 94 (and the equivalent State provision) is designed to provide. The members who had raised the removal question must be free to vote on it; the Speaker cannot act against them in the intervening period.

The Nabam Rebia doctrine has significant tactical implications. An Opposition — or a rebel faction of the ruling party — can use a removal notice under Article 94(c) to effectively freeze the Speaker's Tenth Schedule powers at a critical moment, buying time for floor-management before a confidence vote.

## Deputy Speaker removal

Article 94 governs both the Speaker and Deputy Speaker identically. The Deputy Speaker faces the same three modes of vacation — ceasing to be a member (clause a), resignation (clause b), and removal by absolute majority after fourteen days' notice (clause c). The dissolution proviso, however, applies only to the Speaker — the Deputy Speaker ceases to be Deputy Speaker on dissolution of the Lok Sabha along with all other members.

The Deputy Speaker's independence from removal pressure is, if anything, greater than the Speaker's in practice, because the Deputy Speaker is less politically prominent and has fewer consequential powers that an Opposition would seek to disable. The Seventeenth Lok Sabha (2019-2024) did not elect a Deputy Speaker for its entire duration — a constitutional gap that the Supreme Court noticed in Om Birla v Union of India (2022), observing that "as soon as may be" in Article 93 imposed a real obligation and directing that a Deputy Speaker be elected. The absence of a Deputy Speaker did not, however, immediately trigger Article 94's operation, since there was no incumbent to remove.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The Constitution Bench held that a Speaker facing a notice of motion for removal under the state equivalent of Article 94 cannot exercise Tenth Schedule disqualification powers until the removal motion is disposed of. The judgment makes the removal notice a significant tactical instrument in parliamentary politics and confirms that the Article 94 accountability mechanism creates substantive constraints on the Speaker's conduct while a removal question is pending.

**Kihoto Hollohan v Zachillhu (1992) 1 SCC 309** — The bench held that the Speaker's Tenth Schedule powers were constitutionally valid, noting that the Speaker's accountability through the removal procedure in Article 94 (and its State analogue) was a significant safeguard that justified the first-instance disqualification jurisdiction being vested in the presiding officer. The existence of Article 94's removal mechanism was directly cited in support of treating the Speaker as a suitable quasi-judicial authority.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v Hon'ble Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020) 2 SCC 428** — The Supreme Court directed the Manipur Speaker to decide a pending Tenth Schedule disqualification petition within four weeks. While the case concerned timeliness in disqualification, the Court's intervention demonstrated that Article 94's tenure protection for the Speaker does not immunise the Speaker from judicial direction on how they exercise their quasi-judicial powers.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023) 5 SCC 501** — The Constitution Bench's decision on the Maharashtra political crisis engaged deeply with the Speaker's disqualification power and the interaction between Article 94's removal framework and the Tenth Schedule. The Court's holding that the Eknath Shinde faction's Speaker had acted improperly in recognising the Shinde faction before the pending disqualification petitions were resolved extended the Nabam Rebia logic and confirmed that Article 94's accountability mechanism constrains Speaker conduct across a wider range of circumstances.

**Sita Soren v Union of India (2024) 5 SCC 401** — The seven-judge bench decision on bribery and parliamentary privilege engaged with the Speaker's constitutional position and the accountability mechanisms that govern it, noting that immunity from prosecution does not extend to all actions taken by Members (including presiding officers) and that constitutional accountability mechanisms including Article 94 define the limits of the Speaker's protected sphere.

## Current significance

Article 94's removal mechanism has never been successfully invoked in the Lok Sabha, and this reflects structural reality — the absolute-majority threshold ensures that a Speaker backed by a functioning government majority is virtually immune from removal. The mechanism's significance lies primarily in how it shapes the Speaker's behaviour (the deterrent effect of ultimate parliamentary accountability) and in the Nabam Rebia doctrine (the tactical implications of a removal notice freezing Tenth Schedule powers).

The question of whether the Speaker's tenure protection should be redesigned — for instance, by transferring anti-defection adjudication to the Election Commission or an independent tribunal, removing the Speaker from the politically most sensitive constitutional decisions — is before Parliament and multiple Law Commission reports. The 255th Law Commission Report recommended transferring Tenth Schedule jurisdiction to a tribunal. If this recommendation is implemented, the Article 94-Nabam Rebia tactical dynamic would dissolve, and the removal mechanism would revert to its original function as a simple accountability device.

The absence of a Deputy Speaker for the entire Seventeenth Lok Sabha (2019-2024) raised fresh questions about Article 94's operation in the context of a vacated position. The Supreme Court's observation in Om Birla that a Deputy Speaker must be elected "as soon as may be" suggests that the failure to constitute the second presiding office — making Article 94(b)'s cross-addressed resignation mechanism impossible to use and depriving the House of its backup presiding officer — was a constitutional irregularity, even if there was no clear judicial remedy to enforce the obligation in real time.
