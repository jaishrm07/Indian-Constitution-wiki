  (a) the principles which should govern—

  (i) the distribution between the State and the Municipalities of the net proceeds of the taxes, duties, tolls and fees leviable by the State, which may be divided between them under this Part and the allocation between the Municipalities at all levels of their respective shares of such proceeds; (ii) the determination of the taxes, duties, tolls and fees which may be assigned to, or appropriated by, the Municipalities; (iii) the grants-in-aid to the Municipalities from the Consolidated Fund of the State;

  (b) the measures needed to improve the financial position of the Municipalities;

  (c) any other matter referred to the Finance Commission by the Governor in the interests of sound finance of the Municipalities.

  (2) The Governor shall cause every recommendation made by the Commission under this article together with an explanatory memorandum as to the action taken thereon to be laid before the Legislature of the State.

## Why it matters

Article 243Y is the constitutional cornerstone of municipal finance reform in India. It creates a periodic, institutionalised mechanism for reviewing whether Municipalities have adequate financial resources to perform their functions, and for making binding recommendations to the state government about how resources should be allocated. Without this mechanism, the fiscal relationship between the state and its municipalities would be entirely at the discretion of the state government, which has consistently shown a preference for maintaining central control over urban finances rather than devolving meaningful fiscal autonomy.

The design choice to use the same Finance Commission for both Panchayats (under Article 243I) and Municipalities (under Article 243Y) has both advantages and drawbacks. The advantage is efficiency and an integrated view of local government finance across both rural and urban tiers. The drawback is that Panchayat finance — which involves large numbers of small bodies with limited capacity — presents different challenges from municipal finance — which involves fewer bodies, larger tax bases, and more complex capital investment requirements. In practice, State Finance Commissions have tended to focus more intensively on the Panchayat side, with municipal finance recommendations often less detailed and less rigorously implemented.

The mandatory laying of Finance Commission reports before the State legislature (clause 2), along with the explanatory memorandum on action taken, creates a constitutional accountability mechanism: the legislature is informed of both the Commission's recommendations and whether the executive has followed them. This transparency requirement is frequently more important in practice than the recommendations themselves, because it creates a public record of the gap between constitutional recommendations and actual devolution.

## How courts use it

Courts have used Article 243Y primarily to establish that the Finance Commission is a constitutional institution whose periodic constitution is mandatory, not optional. The Governor's obligation under Article 243I (incorporated into the Article 243Y framework) to constitute the Finance Commission within one year of the 73rd and 74th Amendments' commencement, and then at five-year intervals, has been treated as a constitutional duty that can be enforced by High Courts through writ jurisdiction.

Courts have also addressed the legal status of Finance Commission recommendations. The orthodox position is that Finance Commission recommendations are advisory rather than binding on the state government. However, courts have held that the explanatory memorandum requirement gives the legislature and the public a basis to scrutinise non-compliance with Finance Commission recommendations, and that a systematic pattern of ignoring Finance Commission recommendations without explanation could in principle be challenged as a violation of the constitutional scheme of fiscal devolution.

## Key cases

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of the overall scheme of Parts IX and IXA — including the fiscal and financial architecture — directly engages with the Finance Commission mechanism. The Court emphasised that the 73rd and 74th Amendments' objectives of meaningful local self-government require adequate financing, and that the Finance Commission is the constitutional mechanism to ensure this. The judgment treated the entire local finance framework, including Article 243Y, as an integrated constitutional design rather than a set of isolated provisions.

**State of West Bengal v. Committee for Protection of Democratic Rights (2010)** — The Supreme Court's discussion of the constitutional status of the Panchayati Raj and municipality frameworks confirmed that the financial institutions created by Part IX and Part IXA, including the Finance Commission, have constitutional standing and cannot be reduced to mere administrative conveniences.

**Thirteenth Finance Commission Report (2010-15)** — While a central (rather than State) Finance Commission report, this document extensively documented the inadequacy of State Finance Commission awards to municipalities and the failure of many states to implement their own SFC recommendations. The Thirteenth FC's analysis of the gap between Article 243Y's constitutional design and practical implementation became an important reference point for municipal finance litigation.

## Current significance

The chronic underfunding of Indian municipalities — documented in successive central Finance Commission reports, state Finance Commission reports, and academic studies — represents the most significant practical failure of the Part IXA constitutional framework. Indian municipalities collectively raise own-source revenues of approximately one percent of GDP, compared to three to four percent in comparable middle-income countries. They are structurally dependent on conditional grants from state and central governments, which fund specific schemes rather than core municipal functions like roads, water supply, and solid waste management.

State Finance Commissions under Article 243Y have been constituted and have made recommendations across multiple cycles, but compliance with those recommendations has been consistently poor. Several states have not tabled Finance Commission reports within the required time, and the explanatory memoranda on action taken — when produced — reveal that large proportions of Finance Commission recommendations have not been implemented. The Fifteenth Central Finance Commission (2021-26) attempted to address this by directly tying central grants to local bodies to conditions of performance and transparency, but the structural problem of inadequate and unpredictable municipal finance remains a defining feature of urban governance in India.
