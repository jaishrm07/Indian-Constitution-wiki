## Why it matters

Article 256 is the constitutional mechanism by which parliamentary legislation is made effective at the State level without requiring the Union to build a parallel field administration for every Union List subject. Indian federalism operates on the assumption that States will administer most laws — including Central laws — through their own bureaucracies and police forces. Article 256 supplies the constitutional guarantee that backs this assumption: States are under a positive constitutional duty to ensure compliance, and the Union has a corresponding direction power to enforce that duty when a State falls short. Without this provision, Parliament's legislative supremacy on Union List and Concurrent List subjects would be legally impeccable but administratively hollow; a State could nullify a Central law simply by deciding not to implement it.

The article sits at the centre of a three-tier federal compliance architecture. The first tier is Article 256 itself — the duty to comply and the direction power to enforce it. The second tier is Article 365, which makes non-compliance with Union directions under the Constitution a ground for Presidential satisfaction that constitutional governance in the State has broken down. The third tier is Article 356, under which a Presidential proclamation imposing President's Rule may be issued on the basis of that satisfaction. Courts and commissions have insisted that the tiers are meant to be traversed sequentially: directions should be issued and given reasonable time to produce compliance before the emergency provisions are invoked. This sequential requirement protects State autonomy by ensuring that the extreme remedy of President's Rule is not deployed without first exhausting the ordinary constitutional mechanism.

The provision also reflects a foundational choice the Constituent Assembly made about the character of Indian federalism. The framers studied the Canadian model (which gives the Dominion order-in-council powers over provincial legislation) and the Australian model (which relies more heavily on financial incentives). They chose a middle path: India's federation would operate through a combination of legislative supremacy of Parliament, administrative reliance on State machinery, and a constitutional obligation backed by a direction power and an emergency consequence. Article 256 is the expression of that choice at the executive-compliance level.

## Reading the provision

**The State's duty — "ensure compliance":** The opening clause imposes an affirmative obligation on every State. The executive power of every State "shall be so exercised as to ensure compliance" with (a) laws made by Parliament, and (b) existing laws which apply in that State. "Existing laws" under the transitional provisions of the Constitution include pre-Constitutional legislation that continued to operate after 1950 — colonial-era central statutes, orders-in-council, and regulations that were not repealed by the new Parliament. The State's duty extends to both categories: not just fresh parliamentary enactments but the entire body of Central and pre-Constitutional law in force.

The word "ensure" is significant. It is not merely an obligation to attempt compliance or to not actively obstruct compliance; it is an obligation to ensure it — to take affirmative administrative action. A State that passively fails to enforce a Central law on drugs, food safety, or environmental standards violates Article 256 even if it has issued no order contravening that law. Administrative inaction is a constitutional failure, not a neutral default.

**The Union's direction power — "as may appear necessary":** The second limb of the article gives the Government of India the power to issue binding directions to any State "as may appear to the Government of India to be necessary" for the purpose of ensuring compliance. This is a broadly worded grant of executive power. The standard is subjective on its face — what appears necessary to the Government of India — and courts have held that judicial review of the necessity judgment is limited, requiring the challenger to show that the direction is clearly unrelated to the compliance purpose or was issued in manifest bad faith.

Directions under Article 256 are qualitatively different from ordinary administrative communications. They are constitutional commands rooted in Part XI. A State that receives and defies an Article 256 direction is not merely disagreeing with Central policy; it is breaching a constitutional obligation, with the legal consequence under Article 365 flowing automatically.

**What Article 256 does not authorise:** The direction power is tied to the specific purpose of ensuring compliance with parliamentary law. It cannot be used as a general supervisory authority over State administration of State-list subjects. The Union cannot issue an Article 256 direction instructing a State to manage its police or its education system in a particular way unless those matters are governed by a specific parliamentary enactment with which the State is failing to comply. The directions power is instrumental — directed at making Union laws effective — not a plenary power of Central superintendence.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly discussed the executive compliance framework in the context of the broader debate about federalism and Centre-State relations in November and December 1948. B.R. Ambedkar's draft drew on the Government of India Act 1935 and on the recommendations of the Constitutional Adviser B.N. Rau. The principal concern was how to make the legislative authority of the Union effective without creating a centralised bureaucracy that would replicate the entire State administrative machinery at the Central level.

K.M. Munshi and T.T. Krishnamachari both emphasised that the direction power under what became Article 256 should be used sparingly and only as a genuine compliance instrument, not as a vehicle for political interference in State governance. The link to the emergency provisions — what became Articles 365 and 356 — was explicitly designed as a last resort, not a hair-trigger. The framers envisaged a cooperative executive relationship as the norm, with the direction power and emergency consequences reserved for genuine constitutional impasse.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — In this landmark nine-judge Constitution Bench decision, five States challenged the constitutional validity of using Article 356 against them. While the primary issue was the scope of Presidential satisfaction under Article 356, the Court's judgment extensively addressed the relationship between Articles 256, 257, 365, and 356. Justice Beg (for the majority) confirmed that Article 256 directions are a constitutionally mandated intermediate step and that persistent failure to comply with such directions is a formal ground for Article 365 satisfaction. The judgment established that the compliance architecture of Part XI is meant to be a graduated progression, not bypassed. This remains the leading authority on the sequencing of the federal compliance mechanism.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1978) 3 SCC 1** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench addressed the Union's power to issue directions under Articles 256 and 257 and examined the boundary between legitimate compliance directions and illegitimate usurpation of State executive authority. The Court held that Article 256 directions must bear a rational and direct connection to enforcing a specific parliamentary law — they cannot be disguised instruments for controlling State policy on State List matters. The purposive limitation on the directions power remains the most important judicial gloss on the provision's scope.

**SR Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — Although principally concerned with Article 356, the nine-judge Constitution Bench in Bommai affirmed the procedural role of the compliance architecture. Nine separate judgments collectively endorsed the proposition that the Union must exhaust the mechanisms of Articles 256 and 257 — issuing directions and awaiting compliance — before certifying constitutional failure of the State government under Article 365 and resorting to President's Rule. The judgment made judicial review of President's Rule proclamations available on the ground of mala fides or non-application of mind, reinforcing the constitutional significance of the pre-356 compliance steps.

**Jayantilal Amratlal Shodhan v. F.N. Rana AIR 1964 SC 648** — The Court observed that Article 256 operates at the level of executive power and does not override the distinct constitutional scheme for legislative power. A State's duty to comply with parliamentary laws presupposes that those laws are intra vires. Article 256 does not cure a constitutionally defective law or compel a State to administer a law that is void; if the State disputes the validity of a parliamentary law, that challenge must be resolved through the appropriate judicial channels.

**Bhagat Ram v. State of Himachal Pradesh (1983) 2 SCC 442** — The Court, while examining administrative service matters, addressed the extent to which directions under Article 256 could specify the manner of administering a Central law by State officials. The judgment clarified that Article 256 directions may extend to procedural and operational aspects of enforcement — not merely the fact of compliance — so long as the direction remains tethered to the objective of making a specific parliamentary law effective.

## Current significance

Article 256 has gained renewed salience in standoffs between the Union and Opposition-governed States over the implementation of centrally-sponsored schemes and national security directives. Constitutional questions have been debated about whether Union directions can compel States to deploy police for Central infrastructure projects, to implement the National Register of Citizens, to follow Central directives on school curriculum under the National Education Policy, or to give effect to agricultural market reforms. The Tamil Nadu Governor's assent controversy (2023–25), in which the Governor of Tamil Nadu withheld assent from several State Bills, raised the related question of whether a Governor's conduct is connected to the State's overall Article 256 compliance obligations — a question the Supreme Court addressed in State of Tamil Nadu v. Governor of Tamil Nadu (2025).

The Sarkaria Commission (1988) recommended that Article 256 directions should be issued in writing, with specific identification of the parliamentary law whose compliance is at stake, and that the State should be given reasonable time to respond before Article 365 consequences are triggered. The Punchhi Commission (2010) echoed these recommendations and further proposed codifying the compliance-direction procedure so that it cannot be used as a political pressure tool dressed up as constitutional enforcement. These recommendations have not yet been legislated but inform current debates about Centre-State relations.

Academic debate continues over whether Article 256 directions can extend to fiscal matters — compelling States to disburse Centrally-sponsored scheme funds on a specified timeline or in a specified manner, or to match-fund Central programmes. The Union has generally preferred to use financial conditionality under Article 282 (discretionary grants) rather than Article 256 directions for fiscal compliance, partly because the direction-and-defiance route carries the politically charged consequence of triggering the emergency architecture. The GST Council era has added a new dimension: States that believe GST Council decisions infringe their constitutional fiscal autonomy have raised the question of whether the Union could use Article 256 to direct compliance with Council resolutions — a constitutional question that no court has yet authoritatively resolved.
