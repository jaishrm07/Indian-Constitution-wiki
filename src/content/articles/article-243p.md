  243P. Definitions.—In this Part, unless the context otherwise requires,—

  (a) "Committee" means a Committee constituted under article 243S;

  (b) "district" means a district in a State;

  (c) "Metropolitan area" means an area having a population of ten lakhs or more, comprised in one or more districts and consisting of two or more Municipalities or Panchayats or other contiguous areas, specified by the Governor by public notification to be a Metropolitan area for the purposes of this Part;

  (d) "Municipal area" means the territorial area of a Municipality as is notified by the Governor;

  (e) "Municipality" means an institution of self-government constituted under article 243Q;

  (f) "Panchayat" means a Panchayat constituted under article 243B;

  (g) "population" means the population as ascertained at the last preceding census of which the relevant figures have been published.

## Why it matters

Article 243P does for Part IXA what Article 243 does for Part IX: it establishes the definitional vocabulary on which all the operative provisions of the municipal governance framework depend. The 74th Amendment (1992), which inserted Part IXA, gave constitutional status to urban local bodies for the first time in India's constitutional history. Before 1992, municipalities and municipal corporations were purely statutory creations of state law, with no guaranteed structure, composition, or powers. Article 243P's definitions mark the transition from that statutory to a constitutional framework for urban governance.

Several definitions deserve particular attention. "Metropolitan area" is defined with a population threshold of ten lakh (one million) — significantly higher than the three-lakh threshold for Wards Committees under Article 243S — and requires Governor notification. This definition matters because Metropolitan areas attract the special planning authority requirements of Article 243ZE, which is the constitutional basis for bodies like the Delhi Development Authority or the Bengaluru Metropolitan Land Transport Authority, though the implementation of that framework has been highly uneven. "Municipal area" as Governor-notified means that the geographic boundaries of municipal jurisdiction are an executive decision, not a self-defining feature of urban settlement — creating recurring controversies when rapidly urbanising areas are or are not brought within a Municipal area.

The definition of "Municipality" as simply an institution constituted under Article 243Q — regardless of its local name — serves the same anti-circumvention function as Part IX's definition of "Panchayat." States cannot escape Part IXA obligations by calling an urban local body something other than a "municipality." All such bodies, once constituted for urban areas under the relevant state law, are Municipalities for constitutional purposes.

## How courts use it

Courts use Article 243P's definitions when resolving disputes about whether a particular body is a Municipality subject to Part IXA's requirements — for instance, on election timing, reservation of seats, or the obligation to constitute Wards Committees. The Metropolitan area definition has been invoked in litigation about the applicability of Article 243ZE's planning authority requirements to specific cities, and courts have held that the Governor's notification is a necessary condition for Metropolitan area status, meaning the constitutional obligations attached to Metropolitan areas do not arise merely from a city crossing the population threshold.

The population definition's dependence on published census figures has the same effect in the Part IXA context as in Part IX: stale census data freezes the metropolitan area thresholds and ward delimitation calculations at historically outmoded figures, potentially allowing rapidly growing cities to avoid the stronger governance requirements that would apply once they cross the relevant thresholds.

## Key cases

**S.R. Chaudhuri v. State of Punjab (2001)** — In the broader context of the constitutionality of practices in legislative institutions, the Supreme Court considered the meaning and scope of constitutional definitions. The reasoning about how definition clauses function to control the scope of constitutional provisions generally is applicable to Article 243P's role in delimiting Part IXA's reach.

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004)** — While the case principally addressed Panchayat elections under Part IX, the Supreme Court's discussion of the relationship between constitutional definitions and state-level definitions in local governance legislation is relevant to interpreting Article 243P. The Court held that state law definitions that deviate from constitutional definitions must be scrutinised for constitutional conformity.

**Municipal Corporation of Delhi v. Gurnam Kaur (1989)** — In the pre-74th Amendment context, the Supreme Court addressed the status of municipal corporations as statutory bodies. The contrast between that statutory status and the constitutional status conferred by Part IXA and its definitions illustrates the significance of Article 243P's definitional framework in establishing municipalities as constitutional institutions.

## Current significance

The Article 243P definition of "Metropolitan area" has become increasingly important as India's urban agglomerations have grown. Cities like Hyderabad, Pune, Chennai, Ahmedabad, and Kolkata — each with populations well above ten lakh in their core municipal areas and far above that when surrounding urban settlements are included — fall within the Metropolitan area definition, triggering the constitutional requirements for Metropolitan Planning Committees under Article 243ZE. However, most states have not properly constituted these Committees, creating a systematic gap between the constitutional framework and urban governance reality. The definitions in Article 243P are the first step in understanding and litigating that gap.
