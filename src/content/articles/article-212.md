## Why it matters

Article 212 protects the separation of powers between the legislative and judicial branches at the State level. Democratic legislatures need to be able to conduct their proceedings without constant threat of judicial intervention at the suit of any aggrieved party — whether a member denied the right to speak, a government whose bill was passed by what it claims was an irregular procedure, or a private citizen who objects to how a particular session was conducted. If every procedural choice made by the Speaker or the House were subject to immediate judicial scrutiny, the legislative process would become hostage to litigation and the courts would in effect become supervisors of Parliament's internal workings.

The provision reflects a constitutional choice to assign procedural sovereignty to the Legislature. The House, through its Speaker and its Standing Orders, governs its own procedure. Courts defer to that governance on procedural questions. This deference is not unlimited — the bar in clause (1) applies only to irregularities of procedure, not to constitutional transgressions — but within its domain it is absolute.

The importance of Article 212 has grown substantially in the political context of State legislatures, where Speakers, Deputy Speakers, and the conduct of sessions have become flashpoints in political crises. In every such crisis — Arunachal Pradesh in 2016, Uttarakhand in 2016, Karnataka in 2018–2019, Maharashtra in 2022 — the question of how far courts could go in reviewing the Speaker's conduct, the Governor's summoning of sessions, and the conduct of business in the House arose directly. The answers courts have given have progressively narrowed the practical scope of Article 212's bar by consistently characterising the relevant challenges as going to constitutional validity rather than mere procedural irregularity.

Article 212 is also the constitutional underpinning of legislative privilege at the procedural level — the principle that what happens inside the Legislature is the Legislature's own business. This connects it to the privilege provisions in Article 194, which protect speeches, votes, and publications. Together, Articles 194 and 212 create a zone of legislative autonomy within which the Legislature governs itself, accountable to voters rather than courts for the quality of its internal processes.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — The bar on procedural challenges**

The bar in clause (1) applies to challenges on the ground of "any alleged irregularity of procedure." The crucial word is "procedure." If the challenge is that the Legislature violated a procedural rule — failed to give adequate notice of an amendment, skipped a reading, conducted a vote without proper quorum as a matter of internal standing orders — Article 212(1) bars the court from inquiring into it. The Legislature's internal procedural rules are its own concern.

The bar does not extend to challenges on the ground that the Legislature violated the Constitution itself. If a constitutional amendment was passed without the requisite special majority under Article 368, or if the Speaker denied a member's right to speak in circumstances amounting to a violation of Article 14 or Article 21, or if business was conducted in a session that was invalidly convened contrary to constitutional requirements — these are constitutional challenges, not mere procedural complaints, and Article 212(1) does not bar judicial review of them.

The characterisation exercise — determining whether a particular challenge is "procedural" (Article 212 bars it) or "constitutional" (Article 212 does not bar it) — is the central difficulty in applying clause (1). Courts have become increasingly willing to characterise challenges as constitutional in nature, particularly when they involve the Speaker's exercise of anti-defection jurisdiction, the conduct of floor tests, or the validity of special sessions called without Cabinet advice. This expansive reading of "constitutional challenge" has effectively shrunk the category of "procedural irregularity" that clause (1) protects.

**Clause (2) — Personal immunity of presiding officers and members**

Clause (2) is narrower and more specific than clause (1). It applies only to officers and members who have been vested with powers by or under the Constitution for regulating procedure, conducting business, or maintaining order in the Legislature. In practice, this means primarily the Speaker and Deputy Speaker, and to a lesser extent committee chairs and other designated presiding members.

The clause protects their exercise of these specific powers from court jurisdiction — a Speaker ruling a question out of order, a Deputy Speaker maintaining order during a debate, a committee chair admitting or rejecting evidence. These functions are constitutionally assigned to these officers and are not subject to court supervision even if a party believes they were exercised incorrectly.

However, clause (2) does not give these officers blanket immunity. Their exercise of powers not vested by the Constitution — administrative decisions, communications with outside parties, actions taken in their personal rather than official capacity — is not covered. Courts have also distinguished between the exercise of constitutional procedural powers (protected) and actions that purport to be procedural exercises but are in substance constitutional violations (not protected).

## Key cases

**Keshav Singh Reference (1965)** — This is the foundational case on the relationship between legislative privilege and judicial authority at the State level. The Uttar Pradesh Legislative Assembly committed Keshav Singh (a non-member who had published a pamphlet critical of a member) for contempt. The Allahabad High Court issued notice in a habeas corpus petition on his behalf. The Assembly then passed a resolution holding the High Court judges who had issued the notice in contempt. The President referred the matter to the Supreme Court under Article 143. The Full Court — all the judges of the Supreme Court sitting together — held that Article 212 bars courts from inquiring into procedural irregularities in legislative proceedings but does not exclude judicial review where the legislature has acted in violation of fundamental rights or outside its jurisdiction entirely. The case drew the outer boundary of Article 212's protection and remains the primary authority on the relationship between legislative privilege and the courts' constitutional jurisdiction under Article 226.

**Raja Ram Pal v Hon'ble Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench comprehensively examined the scope of Articles 105 and 122 — the Union Parliament equivalents of Articles 194 and 212 — holding that courts cannot examine alleged procedural irregularities under clause (1), but that expulsion of members and other actions that transgress constitutional provisions are reviewable. The Court affirmed the distinction between procedural irregularity (unreviewable) and constitutional transgression (reviewable) as the central interpretive line for both Article 122 and Article 212. Since the provisions are identically worded, the Raja Ram Pal analysis applies directly to Article 212 State Legislature cases.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — The five-judge Constitution Bench reviewed the Speaker's procedural actions during the Arunachal Pradesh political crisis, including the Speaker's attempt to schedule a session at the direction of the Governor (whose advance of the session date was itself held to be unconstitutional). The Court held that Article 212 does not immunise the Speaker from judicial review when their actions implement unconstitutional executive directions or independently violate constitutional provisions. The case is the clearest post-Keshav Singh authority on how Article 212(1) interacts with constitutional challenges to legislative sessions.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Constitution Bench addressed the conduct of the Maharashtra Legislature during the June 2022 political crisis, in which rival factions of the Shiv Sena each claimed to be the legitimate government supported by the Assembly's majority. The Court held that Article 212 does not bar judicial review of the Speaker's conduct in relation to anti-defection proceedings, floor tests, and the composition of the Assembly, because these are questions of constitutional validity — whether the constitutional requirements for defection and disqualification under the Tenth Schedule were observed — not merely procedural irregularities in the conduct of House business. The case further cemented the principle that the Article 212 bar is narrower than its text might suggest when fundamental constitutional questions are at stake.

**Manipur Political Crisis (2020–2022)** — Judicial proceedings arising from the Manipur Assembly Speaker's delay in deciding disqualification petitions under the Tenth Schedule produced orders from the Supreme Court setting timelines for decision. These orders were grounded in the constitutional obligation to exercise the disqualification jurisdiction, not merely a preference for prompt procedure, and were therefore held to be consistent with Article 212 — reinforcing that the article does not give presiding officers unlimited time to exercise constitutional powers.

## Current significance

Article 212 is invoked in every case where a State government seeks to use the legislature's internal proceedings as a shield against judicial oversight. The provision has been tested in the context of anti-defection proceedings, Speaker's rulings on disqualification, the passage of bills under disputed procedures, and contempt actions by Houses against individuals. The line between unreviewable procedural irregularity and reviewable constitutional violation remains contested and continues to generate litigation.

The frequency of judicial interventions in State legislative affairs since 2016 — in Arunachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Manipur, and other states — has effectively narrowed Article 212's practical scope. Courts have been willing to intervene where the challenge is characterised as going to constitutional validity, and have shown considerable skill in characterising challenges that might once have been seen as procedural as raising constitutional questions instead. Article 212 now functions primarily as a bar to technical procedural grievances rather than to substantive constitutional claims.

Some scholars argue that this evolution has tilted too far toward judicial supervision of legislative proceedings, undermining the separation of powers rationale that Article 212 was designed to protect. The concern is that aggressive judicial review of Speaker's rulings and legislative sessions, while justified in cases of genuine constitutional violation, risks drawing courts into political disputes that are more appropriately resolved through the electoral process and democratic accountability. The Supreme Court has acknowledged this concern in several judgments, emphasising that judicial intervention in legislative affairs should be exceptional and confined to clear constitutional breaches.

Article 212's counterpart for the Union Parliament is Article 122. The two provisions are identically worded and the Supreme Court's interpretations of Article 122 are directly applicable to Article 212. The symmetry ensures that State Legislatures enjoy the same constitutional protection from procedural judicial review as Parliament, reflecting the Constitution's treatment of both levels of legislature as equally sovereign within their respective domains. The principal practical difference is that political crises in State Legislatures have been more frequent and more acute than at the parliamentary level, making Article 212 litigation correspondingly more active and its constitutional interpretation more dynamically developed in State High Courts and the Supreme Court.
