## What this Article does

Article 37 is the operating instruction for the whole of Part IV. It declares, in one breath, that the Directive Principles of State Policy are not directly enforceable by any court, yet simultaneously insists they are "fundamental in the governance of the country" and impose a positive duty on the State to apply them when making laws. This dual character — non-justiciable yet legally significant — is the central tension that the Supreme Court has navigated across more than seven decades of constitutional adjudication.

The non-justiciability clause reflects a deliberate choice by the framers. The Constituent Assembly was aware that many directive principles required resources that post-independence India could not guarantee and that the executive and legislature needed room to make policy choices about sequencing and priority. Yet the framers did not want these principles reduced to mere political rhetoric. The formula they chose — "fundamental in governance" combined with a duty to apply in lawmaking — was intended to give the principles real but non-coercive legal weight. They would guide, constrain, and inform, without replacing rights enforcement with socio-economic mandates.

## Non-justiciability and its limits

Article 37's non-justiciability clause establishes the outer boundary: courts will not issue writs compelling the State to implement a directive principle as if it were a fundamental right. No court will direct Parliament to enact a uniform civil code under Article 44, or order the executive to eliminate inequality under Article 38. At this level, the clause is absolute and courts have consistently respected it.

But non-justiciability is not the same as legal irrelevance. Courts have found three distinct pathways through which directive principles exercise real legal force despite Article 37's bar on direct enforcement.

The first pathway is interpretive. Courts routinely use directive principles to determine the content of fundamental rights under Part III — in particular, the right to life under Article 21. When a court holds that Article 21 includes the right to health, education, livelihood, a clean environment, or dignified working conditions, it is reading the relevant directive principle — Articles 47, 45, 39, 48A, or 43 — into the fundamental right. The directive is not being enforced directly; instead, it gives substantive content to the right that is being enforced. This pathway has been so extensively used that large areas of directive-principle content have become indirectly justiciable through Article 21.

The second pathway is protective. Article 37's duty clause supports legislation that implements a directive principle even when that legislation would otherwise be vulnerable to challenge under Articles 14 or 19. Courts apply a more deferential standard of review to laws with a genuine directive-principle objective, treating the duty to apply directives in lawmaking as a legitimate constitutional purpose that can justify regulatory burdens on property or economic rights. This protective function is what underlies the constitutional jurisprudence on land reform, rent control, and industrial regulation.

The third pathway is supervisory. In public interest litigation, courts use directive principles as the benchmark against which they assess whether the State has met its governance duty. Where legislation or executive policy falls demonstrably short of the directive's minimum standard — for example, chronic failure to provide basic legal aid or to eliminate bonded labour — courts issue directions that are nominally targeted at specific statutory failures but are informed by the Article 37 duty that the State must keep these principles in view when acting.

## The Part III vs Part IV balance

The most contested constitutional question generated by Article 37 is the relationship between Directive Principles and Fundamental Rights when they point in different directions. Can a law that abridges a fundamental right be saved because it implements a directive principle? Can Parliament amend the Constitution to give directive principles primacy over fundamental rights?

These questions reached their climax in a series of landmark cases in the 1970s and 1980s. The Forty-Second Amendment of 1976 — enacted during the Emergency — inserted Article 31C in a form that gave all directive principles primacy over Articles 14, 19, and 31, effectively allowing any law claiming directive-principle purpose to override fundamental rights without judicial scrutiny. This represented one vision of what Article 37's duty clause might imply: that the obligation to implement directives is weighty enough to override rights protection when the two conflict.

The Supreme Court rejected this vision comprehensively. The constitutional balance between Parts III and IV is not a hierarchy in which one can override the other but a mutual dependence in which each enriches the meaning of the other. Article 37's duty clause requires effort and application of the principles; it does not license the destruction of fundamental rights.

## Key cases

**State of Bihar v. Kameshwar Singh (1952)** — One of the earliest cases to address the legal weight of directive principles despite Article 37's non-justiciability. The Court held that directive principles can be used to identify whether a law serves a legitimate constitutional purpose, and that the legislature's duty under Article 37 is a relevant consideration in evaluating a law's validity. This ruling established from the start that "fundamental in governance" meant more than a moral aspiration.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The Constitution Bench held that the basic structure of the Constitution cannot be amended by Parliament. In the course of this judgment, the Court addressed the relationship between Part III and Part IV, holding that fundamental rights and directive principles are complementary and together constitute the core of the Constitution's vision for a just social order. Neither Part can be made absolutely subordinate to the other through constitutional amendment without damaging the basic structure.

**State of Kerala v. N.M. Thomas (1976)** — The Court held that Article 16(4) — which permits reservations in government employment for backward classes — is not an exception to Article 16(1) but an instance of it, and that reading directive principles together with fundamental rights supports rather than undermines equality doctrine. The judgment is an important early example of the integrative approach to Parts III and IV, using the directive on social and economic equality to enrich the content of the equality guarantee.

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India (1980)** — A Constitution Bench struck down the extended form of Article 31C inserted by the Forty-Second Amendment. The Court held that Articles 14 and 19 cannot be wholly abrogated to implement directive principles because the balance between fundamental rights and directive principles is itself a feature of the Constitution's basic structure. Article 37's duty formulation was read as requiring legislative effort, not as licensing the elimination of rights review. Parliament may implement directives through legislation, but must do so in ways that remain consistent with the fundamental rights framework.

**Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985)** — The Court held that the right to livelihood is part of the right to life under Article 21, using Articles 39(a) and 41 (directive principles on livelihood and the right to work) as interpretive resources. The practical outcome was that pavement dwellers could not be evicted without reasonable notice and resettlement options — a socio-economic right created by reading a directive principle into a fundamental right.

**Unni Krishnan J.P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993)** — The Court read Articles 41 and 45 (directive principles on education) together with Article 21 to hold that the right to free education up to the age of fourteen was a fundamental right enforceable under Article 32. This was the clearest pre-86th-Amendment example of using Article 37's duty as the doctrinal foundation for converting a directive into a justiciable claim through Article 21, and it directly preceded the enactment of Article 21A.

## Current significance

Article 37's non-justiciability clause continues to set the outer boundary of what courts will do directly, but the practical gap between directive and right has narrowed considerably through Article 21 jurisprudence. Courts now routinely use directive principles as interpretive tools to determine the content of fundamental rights, and they read legislative omissions against the standard set by Article 37's duty clause when evaluating public interest litigation.

The balance between rights and directives remains the central tension in Indian constitutional law. Property rights, reservations, affirmative action, environmental regulation, and labour rights all require courts to navigate the space between Part III and Part IV. Every time a court expands the content of Article 21, it is implicitly using Article 37 to justify drawing directive-principle substance into the fundamental rights sphere. Article 37 is therefore not merely the operating clause for a non-justiciable Part; it is the constitutional hinge on which the transformation of Indian social-rights jurisprudence has turned.
