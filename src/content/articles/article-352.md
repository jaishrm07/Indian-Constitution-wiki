plainEnglish: Article 352 allows the President to declare a National Emergency whenever the security of India is threatened by war, external aggression, or armed rebellion. Before issuing such a proclamation the President must receive a written resolution from the full Cabinet. Once issued, the proclamation must be approved by both Houses of Parliament within one month by a special majority — a two-thirds majority of members present and voting plus an absolute majority of the total membership. The emergency lasts for six months and can be renewed in further six-month blocks. The Lok Sabha can force a revocation at any time if one-tenth of its members give written notice and a majority votes to disapprove the proclamation. After the 44th Amendment of 1978, internal disturbance is no longer sufficient ground; only "armed rebellion" qualifies.

Proclamation of Emergency.—(1) If the President is satisfied that a grave emergency exists whereby the security of India or of any part of the territory thereof is threatened, whether by war or external aggression or [armed rebellion], he may, by Proclamation, make a declaration to that effect [in respect of the whole of India or of such part of the territory thereof as may be specified in the Proclamation.] [Explanation.—A Proclamation of Emergency declaring that the security of India or any part of the territory thereof is threatened by war or by external aggression or by armed rebellion may be made before the actual occurrence of war or of any such aggression or rebellion, if the President is satisfied that there is imminent danger thereof.] [(2) A Proclamation issued under clause (1) may be varied or revoked by a subsequent Proclamation.

## Why it matters

Article 352 is the Constitution's highest-order emergency lever. When a National Emergency is proclaimed, the federal distribution of legislative power is overridden — Parliament may legislate on State List subjects under Article 353, the President may modify revenue-sharing under Article 354, and Articles 358 and 359 affect the enforceability of fundamental rights. The provision's history is inseparable from the 1975 Emergency, which Prime Minister Indira Gandhi justified initially under the "internal disturbance" limb. That experience led Parliament to enact the 44th Amendment in 1978, substituting "armed rebellion" for "internal disturbance" and introducing the procedural bulwarks now embedded in the article.

The three grounds for proclamation — war, external aggression, and armed rebellion — can each support a proclamation even before the event actually occurs if the President is satisfied that imminent danger exists. Importantly, Article 352(9) permits different proclamations on different grounds to be in operation simultaneously.

## Reading the provision

**Grounds for proclamation.** Only war, external aggression, or armed rebellion satisfy the threshold. The original text allowed proclamation on grounds of "internal disturbance," a phrase wide enough to cover political opposition. The 44th Amendment's replacement of that phrase with "armed rebellion" was a deliberate narrowing to prevent misuse for domestic political advantage.

**Cabinet resolution in writing.** Article 352(3) requires the decision of the Union Cabinet — meaning the full Council of Ministers of Cabinet rank, not merely the Prime Minister — to be communicated to the President in writing before any proclamation is issued. This requirement was added by the 44th Amendment after revelations that the 1975 proclamation was issued on the verbal advice of the Prime Minister alone.

**Parliamentary approval.** Under Article 352(4) and (6), the proclamation must be approved by both Houses within one month by a special double majority: a majority of the total membership of each House and at least two-thirds of those present and voting. If the Lok Sabha is dissolved, the Rajya Sabha may approve the proclamation, but the Lok Sabha must ratify it within thirty days of reconstitution. This requirement ensures that even a government with a comfortable simple majority cannot proclaim an emergency if it cannot command two-thirds support.

**Duration and renewal.** Once approved, the emergency lasts for six months. Continuation requires further resolutions by the same special majority every six months. There is no outer time limit written into the provision, but the parliamentary requirement at each renewal acts as a periodic check.

**Revocation by Lok Sabha.** Under clauses (7) and (8), the Lok Sabha may revoke a proclamation by a simple majority resolution. One-tenth of Lok Sabha members may give written notice demanding a special sitting within fourteen days for this purpose. This anti-majoritarianism safeguard means the government cannot rely on its Rajya Sabha numbers to keep an emergency alive against the will of the directly elected lower house.

**Judicial review.** Article 352 itself does not mention courts. The 42nd Amendment of 1976 had purported to make emergency proclamations non-justiciable, but the Supreme Court in Minerva Mills struck that down, confirming that courts retain the power to examine whether the constitutional conditions for a valid proclamation were met.

## Key cases

**ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla (1976)** — Decided at the height of the 1975 Emergency, the Supreme Court majority held that the presidential order under Article 359 suspending enforcement of Article 21 barred all habeas corpus petitions. The ruling tacitly validated the use of Article 352 to immunise mass detentions from judicial scrutiny. Justice H R Khanna's solitary dissent — arguing that life and liberty exist independently of the Constitution and courts cannot be wholly excluded from examining the legality of detention — was vindicated by the 44th Amendment and by subsequent Supreme Court jurisprudence. The decision remains the most condemned ruling in Indian constitutional history.

**Minerva Mills v Union of India (1980)** — The Court struck down sections 55 and 4 of the 42nd Amendment, which had sought to make the satisfaction of the President under Article 352 non-justiciable and to expand Parliament's amending power without limit. The Court held that unlimited power to amend the Constitution during an emergency is inconsistent with the basic structure doctrine and that judicial review of whether the conditions for an emergency proclamation are satisfied cannot be foreclosed by amendment. The judgment's limited-review position — courts can examine satisfaction but not substitute their own assessment — remains the governing standard.

**S R Bommai v Union of India (1994)** — Although primarily an Article 356 case, the nine-judge bench's discussion of the scope of presidential satisfaction and judicial review of emergency-type proclamations is directly applicable to Article 352 assessments of satisfaction. The Court confirmed that satisfaction must be based on relevant material and cannot rest on extraneous or irrelevant considerations, and that courts retain jurisdiction to enquire whether the constitutional threshold has been crossed.

**Justice K S Puttaswamy v Union of India (2017)** — The nine-judge constitutional bench's privacy judgment, while not an emergency case, reinforced the position that Articles 20 and 21 are non-derogable and that human dignity is a foundational constitutional value. The ruling entrenches the post-44th Amendment position that even a validly proclaimed emergency cannot licence the complete removal of judicial protection for life and personal liberty.

## Current significance

India has not proclaimed a National Emergency since the third one ended in 1977, and the 44th Amendment is generally regarded as a successful structural reform. Nevertheless, the provision remains constitutionally significant in several respects. First, the threshold of "armed rebellion" has not been judicially defined with precision, leaving room for executive interpretation in situations of severe internal violence that fall short of conventional armed conflict. Second, the special majority requirement has not been tested in a modern context where coalition politics might produce a government unable to muster two-thirds parliamentary support. Third, the Rajya Sabha's role as a continuing check when the Lok Sabha is dissolved raises unresolved questions about the Senate's capacity to function as an accountability mechanism under political pressure. Fourth, the relationship between Article 352 and the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act — which can impose near-emergency conditions in designated disturbed areas without any proclamation — is a persistent subject of constitutional critique.
