## Why it matters

Article 215 performs two distinct but related functions. First, it guarantees
that High Court judgments have permanent evidentiary status and binding
authority over all subordinate courts within the court's territorial
jurisdiction. Second — and more practically significant — it anchors the High
Court's contempt power directly in the Constitution rather than in legislation,
placing it beyond the reach of ordinary parliamentary amendment. A legislature
that wishes to restrict a High Court's contempt power must first amend the
Constitution, a far higher bar than modifying an ordinary statute.

Without the power to punish those who defy or obstruct its orders, a High Court
would be a tribunal whose judgments are requests rather than commands. The
framers understood this and embedded the power in Article 215 rather than
leaving it to the Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, which governs procedure but
cannot override the constitutional grant.

## Reading the provision

"Court of record" is a term inherited from English common law. A court of
record has three defining attributes: its proceedings are formally enrolled and
permanently preserved as evidence of judicial acts; its judgments are conclusive
as to the matters decided and cannot be re-examined in any collateral proceeding
in an inferior court; and it has inherent power to fine or imprison for contempt.
All three attributes apply to every Indian High Court by virtue of Article 215.

The phrase "all the powers of such a court including the power to punish for
contempt of itself" uses "including" deliberately. The Supreme Court has read
this as a non-exhaustive list: the contempt power is an illustration of
court-of-record powers, not their exhaustive definition. This construction
keeps the High Court's inherent jurisdiction expansive, covering situations
the framers may not have specifically anticipated.

The distinction between Article 215 and the position of subordinate courts is
constitutionally significant. A civil or criminal court below the High Court
level is not a court of record in this constitutional sense. Its proceedings
can be corrected, reversed, or re-examined by the High Court through appellate,
revisional, and superintendence jurisdictions under Articles 226 and 227. The
High Court's own record, by contrast, is authoritative and final within its
jurisdiction, subject only to correction by the Supreme Court.

## Key cases

**Sukhdev Singh Sodhi v Chief Justice and Judges of the PEPSU High Court (1954)**
— The Supreme Court confirmed that the power to punish for contempt is an
integral part of court-of-record status under Article 215 and cannot be
curtailed by the legislature. Because the Constitution itself confers the power,
no ordinary statute can reduce or eliminate it. This principle was established
early and has not been disturbed.

**Delhi Judicial Service Association v State of Gujarat (1991)** — The Court
clarified the relationship between Article 215's contempt power and Articles 129
and 142, holding that the High Court's contempt jurisdiction under Article 215
is plenary with respect to courts and persons within its territorial
jurisdiction. The case arose from the unlawful detention of judicial officers.

**Pallav Sheth v Custodian (2001)** — The Supreme Court examined the
interaction between Article 215's contempt power and the time-limit provisions
of the Contempt of Courts Act, 1971. The Act's procedural limitations apply to
the statutory exercise of contempt power but the constitutional power under
Article 215 is not extinguished by a procedural lapse.

**Re: Arundhati Roy (2002)** — The Court examined criminal contempt under
Article 129, and the reasoning was applied by High Courts to their own Article
215 jurisdiction, clarifying that the court-of-record contempt power encompasses
conduct that scandalises the court or tends to obstruct justice even outside the
courtroom, while affirming that fair criticism of judicial decisions is not
contempt.

## Current significance

The contempt power in Article 215 has drawn close public scrutiny as High Courts
have initiated suo motu contempt proceedings against state functionaries for
non-compliance with orders on public health, environment, and rights matters.
The interplay between Article 215's constitutional contempt power and the
Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 — which limits the grounds for contempt and the
maximum sentence — continues to generate litigation about whether Parliament
can modify what Article 215 directly grants. The prevailing view is that
Parliament can regulate the exercise of contempt jurisdiction but cannot
eliminate the constitutional core.

The emergence of specialised tribunals with ouster clauses purporting to exclude
High Court review has sharpened the importance of Article 215. Courts have
consistently held that Article 215's court-of-record status — and the
accompanying Article 226 writ jurisdiction — cannot be removed by ordinary
legislation. The High Court remains a constitutional check on all inferior
judicial and quasi-judicial bodies regardless of what a particular statute says.

The corresponding provision for the Supreme Court is Article 129. Together,
Articles 129 and 215 establish the hierarchical structure of courts of record
in India: the Supreme Court as the apex court of record with national
jurisdiction, and twenty-five High Courts as courts of record with territorial
jurisdictions covering the entire country, creating a hierarchy of binding
precedent that is constitutionally rather than legislatively anchored.
