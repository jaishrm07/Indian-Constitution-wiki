## Why it matters

Article 330 is the constitutional foundation for political representation of two of India's most historically marginalised communities at the national level. Before the Constitution, Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes had limited access to legislative power. The framers, led by Dr B.R. Ambedkar, insisted that parity in social and economic status could not be achieved without parity in political voice. Reserving seats in the Lok Sabha ensured that SC and ST communities would have guaranteed representatives in the national Parliament from the first day of constitutional democracy — representatives who are themselves members of those communities and who are directly accountable to the voters of reserved constituencies.

The mechanism Article 330 adopts is proportional reservation within an integrated electorate. Reserved constituencies are those where only SC or ST candidates may contest, but all voters in that constituency — regardless of caste or tribe — vote for the winner. This design has a dual effect. First, SC and ST elected members owe their political survival to a broad, mixed electorate, which reduces the risk of their isolation as single-community representatives with no cross-community political base. Second, SC and ST voters in every State are guaranteed that their demographic weight translates into a floor of national representation — the formula makes reserved seat counts mathematically tethered to population share rather than politically negotiable. The contrast with the old Poona Pact separate electorate system — which Ambedkar himself had signed under pressure — is deliberate. Article 330's reserved constituency design delivers representation without electoral segregation.

The article's significance also lies in what it does not guarantee. Article 330 does not guarantee SC or ST members specific cabinet positions, committee chairmanships, or levels of government power — it guarantees their presence in the legislature. And that presence matters — reserved-seat MPs have historically shaped debates on land rights, minimum wages, welfare schemes, and anti-atrocity enforcement in ways that would otherwise have lacked consistent legislative champions. The proportionality formula has also acted as a structural check on majoritarian delimitation exercises that might otherwise reduce SC or ST representation below their demographic weight by manipulating constituency geography.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — three categories of reservation:** The provision reserves seats for three distinct groups. Sub-clause (a) reserves seats for Scheduled Castes — the communities whose names appear in the presidential orders made under Article 341. Sub-clause (b) reserves seats for Scheduled Tribes other than those in Assam's autonomous Sixth Schedule districts — broadly, ST populations across the country except for the specifically treated north-eastern category. Sub-clause (c) reserves seats specifically for Scheduled Tribes in Assam's autonomous districts under the Sixth Schedule. The separate treatment of Assam's autonomous districts reflects the constitutional recognition of Sixth Schedule self-governance and the distinct political geography of tribal autonomy in the north-east, where tribal populations are geographically concentrated and constitute a majority in their district territories.

**Clause (2) — the proportionality formula:** The number of reserved seats in any State or Union territory must bear, "as nearly as may be," the same proportion to that unit's total Lok Sabha seats as the SC or ST population of the relevant area bears to the total population of the State or Union territory. "As nearly as may be" acknowledges that exact mathematical proportionality may not be achievable when dealing with whole-number seat counts — some rounding is constitutionally permissible. The formula is self-executing once the relevant population figures are available: it requires a calculation, not a discretionary political decision about how many reserved seats to allocate.

**Clause (3) — Assam autonomous district floor guarantee:** Notwithstanding clause (2), the reserved seats for Scheduled Tribes in Assam's autonomous Sixth Schedule districts must reflect at minimum the proportion that the ST population of those districts bears to Assam's total population. This is a floor guarantee — the proportion cannot go below that minimum, but could be higher if the general proportionality formula would yield a larger number for the autonomous district ST population. The guarantee ensures that the Sixth Schedule tribal populations, whose distinct constitutional status is separately recognised, cannot receive proportionally less representation than their demographic weight warrants.

**The Explanation — frozen census reference:** The definition of "population" for this article and Article 332 is pegged to the last census of which figures have been published. The proviso — added by constitutional amendment and most recently updated by the 104th Amendment — freezes the census reference at the 2001 census until figures from the first census taken after 2026 are published. This freeze was a political compromise to protect southern and smaller States, whose population growth has been slower, from an immediate loss of Lok Sabha seats and reserved seat allocations under a fresh delimitation before they were politically ready for that rebalancing. The consequence is that SC and ST seat allocations in every State continue to be calculated on over two-decade-old population data, a growing distortion of the proportionality principle.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's decision to provide reserved constituencies rather than separate electorates for Scheduled Castes was one of the most consequential choices of the drafting process. Ambedkar had himself negotiated the Poona Pact in 1932 under conditions of duress — Gandhi's fast-unto-death had forced him to accept reserved seats within a joint electorate in place of the separate communal electorates that the British had initially granted. Coming to the Constituent Assembly, Ambedkar was both the principal advocate for SC political protection and the architect of a solution that would integrate rather than segregate SC voters and candidates.

The debate within the Assembly focused on whether the reservation should be time-limited or permanent. Ambedkar and others argued that reservation was compensatory — a transitional measure to allow historically excluded communities to establish political presence until they could compete on equal terms. The original sunset provision in what became Article 334 set the term at ten years. Most members understood that ten years was optimistic but believed that explicitly time-limiting the provision was constitutionally necessary to prevent it from becoming an indefinitely entrenched group right inconsistent with the article's one-person-one-vote foundation. The repeated extensions have demonstrated that the ten-year assumption was profoundly incorrect, and constitutional commentary increasingly questions whether the sunset clause retains any constitutional meaning.

## Key cases

**Meghraj Kothari v Delimitation Commission (1966) 3 SCR 683** — The Supreme Court upheld the authority of the Delimitation Commission to allocate reserved constituencies and confirmed that courts cannot interfere with delimitation orders under the Delimitation Act. The foundational principle established — that delimitation and the selection of which constituencies are reserved is a non-justiciable legislative function protected by Article 329(a) — has governed every subsequent challenge to the arrangement of SC and ST reserved seats in the Lok Sabha. Parties cannot litigate the Commission's choice of which particular constituencies to designate as reserved.

**Indira Sawhney v Union of India (1992) 3 SCC 217** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench ruling primarily concerned OBC reservations in employment, but its broader reasoning about the constitutional basis for reservation — that it is a compensatory rather than punitive tool — shapes how Article 330 is read. The Court's observation that SC and ST reservations under Articles 330 and 332 occupy a more firmly grounded constitutional position than OBC reservations has been cited to protect the proportional SC and ST seat formula from being subjected to the 50 percent ceiling that applies to employment reservations under Article 16.

**K Krishna Murthy v Union of India (2010) 7 SCC 202** — Although primarily dealing with reservation in local body elections under Article 243D, the Supreme Court's analysis of the SC and ST reservation framework illuminates the proportionality principle in Article 330(2). The Court held that any reservation scheme must remain tethered to population data and cannot be arbitrary, and that the proportionality formula is a constitutional floor rather than an aspirational guideline. The reasoning applies by extension to the Article 330 framework and its census-linked proportionality requirement.

**Hukum Chand Shyam Lal v Union of India AIR 1976 SC 789** — The Court addressed a challenge to the redelimitation of reserved constituencies, holding that once a constituency has been reserved by a valid Delimitation Order, the order is protected from judicial challenge by Article 329(a), and that the merits of the Commission's choice of which constituency to reserve cannot be scrutinised. The case illustrates the interaction between Article 330's reservation mandate and Article 329's bar against judicial interference with delimitation.

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024) 6 SCC 1** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held, overruling E.V. Chinnaiah (2004), that States may sub-classify within the Scheduled Caste category to concentrate the benefit of reservation on more backward sub-groups. While the ruling directly concerns employment reservation, its implications for legislative seat reservation under Article 330 remain a matter of ongoing debate. If SC sub-groups can be differentiated for employment purposes, can reserved Lok Sabha constituencies be earmarked for particular SC sub-groups within a State? The Court left this question open, but it will likely reach the Court as the implications of Davinder Singh extend across domains.

**Election Commission of India v St Mary's School (2008) 13 SCC 643** — The Court reiterated that the reservation of seats and the delimitation of reserved constituencies are matters exclusively within the constitutional and statutory framework administered by the Election Commission and the Delimitation Commission. Private parties cannot seek to expand or contract reserved seat allocations through litigation — the only avenue for such changes is through the parliamentary and constitutional amendment process.

## Current significance

The Explanation's frozen census reference — 2001 data — has become increasingly significant as India approaches the next census and linked delimitation. Southern States with lower population growth rates have consistently expressed concern that a fresh delimitation based on current population figures will shift Lok Sabha seats northward, reducing their representation. The freeze protects those States from immediate seat losses but means that SC and ST seat allocations in every State continue to be calculated on over two-decade-old population data. When the post-2026 census triggers a fresh delimitation, the number of reserved SC and ST seats in each State will be recalculated using updated population shares, with potentially substantial reallocation consequences for States where SC and ST populations have grown disproportionately relative to the overall population.

The 104th Amendment (2020) brought a notable structural change alongside extending the reservation deadline to 2030 — it allowed the Anglo-Indian nominated seat provision (Article 331) to lapse while retaining SC and ST reservations. This differential treatment confirms that SC and ST seat reservation under Article 330 is constitutionally distinct from other forms of special parliamentary representation and is considered more firmly grounded. The amendment also set the stage for the Women's Reservation Act (106th Amendment, 2023), which inserts new Article 330A reserving one-third of Lok Sabha seats for women, including one-third of reserved SC and ST seats. Like the existing Article 330 framework, women's reservation is also tied to the post-2026 census delimitation trigger — meaning the two largest pending changes to Lok Sabha composition are constitutionally linked to the same census and delimitation event.

The political consequences of the coming delimitation — fresh SC and ST seat calculations, potential rebalancing of seats between northern and southern States, and the activation of women's reservation within the reserved category framework — make the post-2026 census and delimitation one of the most consequential constitutional events in the history of Indian representative democracy. Article 330's proportionality formula, and the political bargaining over which States gain and which lose under a fresh calculation, will be at the centre of that moment.
