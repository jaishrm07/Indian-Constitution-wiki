## Why it matters

When India became independent, vast tracts of agricultural land were controlled by zamindars and other intermediaries under colonial-era tenancy arrangements. The new government's agrarian reform agenda — abolishing zamindari, distributing land to tillers, and capping individual land holdings — faced immediate constitutional challenge: courts struck down state zamindari abolition laws as violating the right to property under Articles 19(1)(f) and 31, and the right to equality under Article 14.

Parliament responded with the First Constitutional Amendment, 1951, inserting Article 31A to create a categorical immunity for estate-acquisition laws. By declaring that such laws "shall not be deemed to be void" on grounds of inconsistency with Articles 14 or 19, the provision removed the primary constitutional basis for landlord challenges and allowed land redistribution to proceed. Article 31B and the Ninth Schedule were added simultaneously to shelter specific listed laws from challenge altogether.

Article 31A thus marks the beginning of a long constitutional tension between property rights and redistributive social policy — a tension that animated Indian constitutional law for decades, ultimately leading to the deletion of the right to property as a fundamental right by the Forty-Fourth Amendment, 1978.

## Reading the provision

The saving clause operates in two layers. First, clause (1) lists five protected categories of legislation: acquisition of estates or rights in them; temporary takeover of property management; amalgamation of corporations; extinguishment of managing-agency rights; and cancellation of mineral or oil exploration licences. Any law falling within one of these categories cannot be challenged under Articles 14 or 19, notwithstanding Article 13's general requirement that laws inconsistent with fundamental rights are void.

Second, two provisos limit the immunity. The first requires that a state law must have received Presidential assent before Article 31A protection attaches — a safeguard ensuring central scrutiny of the most significant agrarian reforms. The second proviso, added by the Fourth Amendment, 1955, protects personal-cultivation land up to the applicable ceiling limit: the State cannot acquire such land without paying compensation at not less than market value, carving out a floor of protection for small cultivators even within the saving clause regime.

The broad definition of "estate" in clause (2) — encompassing jagirs, inams, ryotwari settlements, and any agricultural land — was deliberately drafted to capture every form of intermediary tenure existing across India's diverse regional land systems.

## Key cases

**Shankari Prasad v Union of India (1951)** — The Supreme Court upheld the First Constitutional Amendment and the insertion of Article 31A, rejecting the argument that the amendment itself abridged fundamental rights. The Court held that Parliament's constituent power under Article 368 was not subject to the fundamental rights provisions, a position later qualified by Kesavananda Bharati. The decision cleared the path for zamindari abolition to proceed.

**State of Bihar v Kameshwar Singh (1952)** — The Patna High Court had struck down the Bihar Land Reforms Act, 1950, as violating the right to property. The Supreme Court, after Article 31A came into force, treated that Act as protected, illustrating the immediate practical effect of the saving clause in validating state land reform statutes that would otherwise have been unconstitutional.

**Waman Rao v Union of India (1981)** — A Constitution Bench drew a critical temporal line: laws inserted into the Ninth Schedule on or before 24 April 1973 (the date of Kesavananda Bharati) enjoy absolute immunity; those inserted after that date are open to challenge if they damage the basic structure. This ruling confirmed that Article 31A and the original Ninth Schedule entries remain fully protected but placed a ceiling on the Parliament's ability to extend similar immunity to future legislation.

**I.R. Coelho v State of Tamil Nadu (2007)** — A nine-judge bench reaffirmed and sharpened the Waman Rao distinction. All Ninth Schedule laws inserted after April 1973 are subject to judicial review on basic structure and fundamental rights grounds to the extent they abrogate rights forming part of the basic structure. Importantly, the Court held that the right to equality under Article 14 and the core of Article 19 freedoms are themselves basic structure elements, meaning that even Article 31A-protected laws cannot destroy those core guarantees entirely.

## Current significance

Article 31A remains an active provision. State governments continue to invoke it to defend land-reform and estate-management statutes challenged in high courts, particularly in disputes arising from urban land acquisition characterised as "estate" acquisition. The second proviso on personal-cultivation land is especially live in contemporary litigation, where landowners challenge below-market acquisition of agricultural land by arguing that their holdings fall within the ceiling-limit protection.

Beyond land law, the five protected categories in Article 31A(1) have been interpreted expansively to cover corporate amalgamations and the cancellation of natural-resource licences — categories that arise in modern regulatory disputes well outside the original agrarian context. The interaction between Article 31A protection and the compensation jurisprudence developed under the Land Acquisition Act, 2013 continues to generate litigation, as states seek to use the Article 31A saving to sidestep the more generous compensation formula in the 2013 Act.
