## What this Article did

Article 359A was inserted by the Constitution (Fifty-ninth Amendment) Act, 1988 to address the acute security situation in Punjab arising from the Khalistan militancy. By the late 1980s, the State was under prolonged President's Rule (Article 356) and was experiencing significant armed violence. The article was part of a package of measures, alongside the 59th Amendment's amendments to Articles 352 and 356, intended to give the Union government expanded constitutional tools to manage the crisis.

In its operational effect, Article 359A authorised the President to make orders applicable specifically to Punjab that had the effect of suspending the right of any person detained in connection with any action taken in that State to move any court for the enforcement of any rights conferred by Part III of the Constitution (other than Articles 20 and 21, which remain non-suspendable even under Article 359 after the 44th Amendment). It also permitted Article 352 to be proclaimed in respect of Punjab alone, without a nationwide Emergency — a departure from the ordinary rule that Article 352 applies to India or a specified part thereof, previously interpreted as requiring national application.

The provision was extremely controversial. It allowed mass detention in Punjab with severely curtailed judicial oversight, at a time when allegations of enforced disappearances, custodial deaths, and extra-judicial killings in the State were already widespread. Critics argued that the constitutional amendment legitimised practices that violated international human rights norms and the core of India's constitutional commitment to personal liberty.

## The Punjab militancy context

The period from 1984 to the early 1990s saw sustained armed insurgency in Punjab. The 1984 Operation Blue Star (the Indian Army's operation in the Golden Temple complex in Amritsar), the assassination of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi in October 1984, and the subsequent Delhi riots, followed by years of militant attacks and state reprisals, created a security and human rights crisis without precedent in post-independence India. The State was under President's Rule for extended periods, and ordinary criminal law enforcement faced structural challenges.

The 59th Amendment and Article 359A were the legislative response to this environment. Parliament inserted the provisions in 1988, during the tenure of Prime Minister Rajiv Gandhi, reflecting the Union government's view that it needed expanded emergency powers specifically tailored to the Punjab situation.

## Omission by the 63rd Amendment (1989)

The Constitution (Sixty-third Amendment) Act, 1989 omitted Article 359A and reversed the Punjab-specific amendments made to Articles 352 and 356 by the 59th Amendment. The 63rd Amendment was enacted after the general elections of 1989 brought the National Front government of V.P. Singh to power. By this point, the peak of the militancy had subsided, and the incoming government's civil liberties orientation made the retention of these exceptional provisions politically untenable.

Section 3 of the 63rd Amendment omitted Article 359A, effective 6 January 1990. The provision had thus been operative for less than two years. The broader Punjab security situation continued to be managed through ordinary Article 356 President's Rule, the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act, and the National Security Act, rather than through additional constitutional emergency provisions.

## Constitutional significance

Article 359A raises deep questions about the appropriate constitutional response to internal armed conflict that falls short of the conditions specified in Article 352 for a national Emergency. The provision illustrated the tension between two imperatives: the state's need for effective security tools in an active insurgency, and the individual's constitutional right not to be detained without meaningful judicial oversight even in extraordinary circumstances.

Its insertion demonstrated that Parliament was willing to use the amending power to suspend constitutional rights protections for a specific State when faced with a sustained security threat — a practice that drew criticism as both undermining the constitutional guarantee of fundamental rights and failing to provide the transparency and oversight that a formal Article 352 Emergency would have required (including the requirement for continued parliamentary approval).

The 63rd Amendment's reversal, driven by political change rather than constitutional principle, left unresolved the question of whether India's constitution as designed adequately provides for regional armed conflicts that are serious but not national in scope. This question recurs in debates about AFSPA, special security legislation in conflict-affected States, and the appropriate constitutional architecture for managing prolonged sub-national security crises.

## Lessons for today

The experience of Articles 359A and the 59th/63rd Amendments is studied as a cautionary example of state constitutional powers in armed conflict. The provisions that were inserted and reversed within two years left behind a record of constitutional law suspensions during which serious human rights violations are documented, and a democratic accountability gap because the Punjab-specific emergency did not attract the same parliamentary scrutiny as a formal national Emergency under Article 352.
