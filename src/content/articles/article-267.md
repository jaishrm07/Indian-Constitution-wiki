## Why it matters

Article 267 solves a recurring problem in parliamentary democracy: genuine emergencies cannot always wait for the legislature to convene, deliberate, and pass an Appropriation Act. A flood, a pandemic, a sudden security threat, or an urgent international obligation may require the government to spend money immediately, before the full parliamentary approval process can complete. Article 267 provides a constitutionally sanctioned answer — a standing emergency fund that the executive can draw on instantly, subject to the firm obligation to return the money once legislative approval is obtained.

The mechanism is designed to preserve parliamentary supremacy while enabling executive flexibility in genuine emergencies. It does not give the executive permanent discretionary spending power. The Contingency Fund is an imprest — a revolving float that must be replenished after every use. The President or Governor may draw from it only for "unforeseen expenditure," and only pending — not instead of — parliamentary or legislative authorisation under the Supplementary Demands process. Legislative supremacy over public spending is not suspended; it is temporarily deferred with a mandatory recoupment obligation that ensures the legislature retains ultimate control.

The Fund is distinct from the Consolidated Fund (Article 266): it is not a general revenue pool and it is not available for planned or programmatic expenditure. It is a limited liquidity buffer for genuine emergencies, and its small corpus relative to total government expenditure is a constitutional signal about its intended scope. Using the Contingency Fund for routine expenditure that could have gone through the normal Budget and Supplementary Demands process would be a misuse of the constitutional emergency mechanism — and one that CAG reports have periodically flagged at both Union and State levels.

The Contingency Fund of India was established by the Contingency Fund of India Act 1950. Its corpus has been enhanced several times: it stood at Rs 500 crore for much of the post-liberalisation period, was increased to Rs 30,000 crore in 2021 as Parliament recognised the scale of COVID-19-related emergency spending. Critics argue even this enlarged corpus may be inadequate given climate-related disasters and public health emergencies that now require rapid large-scale government response. The debate about the Fund's appropriate size tracks a deeper constitutional question about the boundary between genuine emergency spending and executive avoidance of parliamentary oversight.

## Reading the provision

Article 267(1) establishes the Contingency Fund of India by authorising Parliament to create it by law. Three design elements in the text define its constitutional character. First, the Fund is "in the nature of an imprest" — a float, not a permanent endowment. The sum is advanced for immediate use and then replenished when the legislature approves the expenditure and the Appropriation Act provides for the original cost. This imprest structure is the constitutional guarantee against the Contingency Fund becoming a parallel channel for bypassing parliamentary appropriation. Second, the Fund is placed at the disposal of the President — it is an executive instrument in the sense that the President may make advances from it without prior parliamentary approval, but it is established and its corpus determined by Parliament through statute. Third, advances from it are only for "unforeseen expenditure" and only "pending authorisation" — the temporal and purposive limits are built into the constitutional text.

Article 267(2) mirrors the Union structure at the State level. Each State Legislature may establish its own Contingency Fund by law, placed at the Governor's disposal, for unforeseen State expenditure pending Supplementary Demand authorisation under Articles 205 or 206. The symmetry reflects the federal design: every unit of government that operates through a Consolidated Fund and an appropriation system needs a corresponding emergency-access mechanism.

The recoupment obligation — the constitutional requirement to seek Supplementary Demands under Article 115 (Vote on Account), Article 116, Article 205, or Article 206 to authorise the expenditure and reimburse the Contingency Fund — is not stated explicitly in Article 267 itself but is inherent in the imprest design and in the reference to those provisions. An advance that is never recouped effectively converts the Contingency Fund into a permanent unappropriated spending channel, violating the constitutional architecture. The advance mechanism is temporary by design; if it were permanent, it would be unconstitutional as an appropriation without legislative authority under Article 266(3).

## Key cases

**Bhim Singh v. Union of India** — Although centred on Article 266, the Court's reasoning confirmed that executive-held funds such as the Contingency Fund remain constitutionally accountable. The executive's power to make advances does not bypass legislative supremacy over public finance, because recoupment is mandatory and constitutionally required. The President's authority under Article 267 is derived from the statute establishing the Fund, not from any inherent executive spending power.

**CAG Reports on Contingency Fund utilisation** — Multiple Comptroller and Auditor-General reports have flagged instances where advances from State Contingency Funds were not promptly recouped through Supplementary Demands, and where the "unforeseen" requirement was stretched to cover expenditure that could have been anticipated in the original Budget. While audit reports are not judicial decisions, they constitute the primary accountability mechanism under Article 267 and define the constitutional accountability norms that courts apply when the Fund's use is litigated.

**State of Assam v. Union of India (emergency flood relief proceedings)** — State High Courts hearing public interest petitions about flood relief spending have examined whether State governments were lawfully drawing on the Contingency Fund for disaster relief or bypassing the Consolidated Fund appropriation process. Courts reaffirmed that even genuine disaster relief must follow the Article 267 imprest mechanism — advance from Contingency Fund followed by Supplementary Demand — rather than treating the Fund as a permanent disaster-relief reserve that bypasses legislative oversight entirely.

**Union of India v. Association of Unified Telecom Service Providers** — In tax and levy disputes where the government sought to retain or redirect certain receipts outside the Appropriation Act framework, courts have applied the Article 267 principle by analogy: executive financial transactions that are not backed by legislative appropriation cannot be validated by reference to executive-held funds. The Contingency Fund's reach is limited to genuine advance-pending-recoupment scenarios.

## Current significance

During the COVID-19 pandemic (2020-21), the Union government drew on the Contingency Fund of India on multiple occasions to fund emergency health procurement and relief expenditure before Supplementary Demands could be placed before Parliament. Parliament subsequently enhanced the Fund's corpus to Rs 30,000 crore to accommodate the scale of emergency deployment. This episode reopened the debate about whether the Fund's ceiling should be inflation-indexed, linked to a percentage of total Budget estimates, or otherwise made proportionate to the scale of emergencies that modern governance must absorb.

At the State level, several States similarly drew on their Contingency Funds for COVID relief and flood relief operations, and a number were criticised by State CAGs for delays in recoupment extending beyond one or two Budget cycles. Delayed recoupment is not a technical accounting issue: it undermines the constitutional transparency requirement that makes the Contingency Fund legitimate. An advance that is left unrecouped effectively converts emergency spending into off-Budget expenditure invisible to the Appropriation Act and the accountability architecture of Article 266.

More broadly, the Contingency Fund architecture under Article 267 is being re-examined in light of climate change and disaster-risk financing. Extreme weather events — cyclones, floods, heatwaves, crop failures — are increasing in frequency, making the category of "unforeseen expenditure" more routine than the framers anticipated. This creates a structural tension: if climate disasters are frequent and predictable in aggregate, should they be handled through the Contingency Fund's emergency imprest, or through a dedicated statutory fund with its own legislative basis? Proposals for a separate constitutionally backed disaster fund, or for significantly enlarging the Contingency Fund's corpus with automatic indexation, are on the policy agenda. Any such change, however, must preserve the imprest and recoupment logic that makes Article 267 constitutionally accountable — or it will need a constitutional amendment rather than a simple statutory revision.
