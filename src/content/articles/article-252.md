  252. Power of Parliament to legislate for two or more States by consent and adoption of such legislation by any other State.—(1) If it appears to the Legislatures of two or more States to be desirable that any of the matters with respect to which Parliament has no power to make laws for the States except as provided in articles 249 and 250 should be regulated in such States by Parliament by law, and if resolutions to that effect are passed by all the Houses of the Legislatures of those States, it shall be lawful for Parliament to pass an act for regulating that matter accordingly, and any Act so passed shall apply to such States and to any other State by which it is adopted afterwards by resolution passed in that behalf by the House or, where there are two Houses, by each of the Houses of the Legislature of that State.

  (2) Any Act so passed by Parliament may be amended or repealed by an Act of Parliament passed or adopted in like manner but shall not, as respects any State to which it applies, be amended or repealed by an Act of the Legislature of that State.

## Why it matters

Article 252 is the cooperative federalism clause for State List subjects — the provision that enables voluntary legal harmonisation across States on matters that the Constitution has deliberately reserved for State-level regulation. The ordinary rule is that Parliament cannot legislate on State List subjects except in the narrow exceptional circumstances of Articles 249 and 250. Article 252 creates a third route: if two or more State Legislatures consent by resolution, Parliament can legislate on the subject for those States alone, producing a uniform Central law in the consenting States while leaving non-consenting States free to regulate the matter independently. This design makes Article 252 an instrument of selective federalism — it permits legislative uniformity without compulsion, letting individual States opt into a common framework when they judge it advantageous.

The significance of Article 252 extends beyond the specific laws it has produced. It embodies a principle that is increasingly invoked in contemporary debates about cooperative federalism: that Central legislative authority over State subjects is not inherently coercive but can be consensual, limited, and reversible at the national level even while being permanent at the individual State level. States that consent surrender some degree of their legislative freedom on the subject — they can no longer amend or repeal the Central law — but they do so in exchange for the benefits of uniformity, clarity, and the credibility that Parliamentary legislation brings.

Article 252 has produced legislation on subjects as varied as transplantation of human organs, prize competitions, and urban land ceilings, demonstrating its practical utility across health, commerce, and urban governance — all areas the framers considered primarily State concerns. Each of these laws reflects a moment where key States judged that a patchwork of independent State laws was less beneficial than a shared Central framework, and Article 252 was the constitutional mechanism that made that choice actionable. Understanding Article 252 is therefore essential for anyone tracing how uniform national regulatory regimes emerge on State List subjects without constitutional amendment.

The irrevocability of consent under Article 252(2) is perhaps the provision's most striking feature from a federal theory standpoint. A State that consents under Article 252(1) permanently transfers its power to amend or repeal the resulting Act to Parliament. This is not a negotiated arrangement that can be unwound — it is a constitutional consequence that attaches the moment the State Legislature passes its resolution. States entering an Article 252 arrangement are therefore giving up more than the typical intergovernmental agreement would imply. Legal and political advisers to State governments must appreciate this irreversibility before recommending the resolution route.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the consent mechanism:**

"If it appears to the Legislatures of two or more States to be desirable that any of the matters with respect to which Parliament has no power to make laws for the States except as provided in articles 249 and 250 should be regulated in such States by Parliament by law, and if resolutions to that effect are passed by all the Houses of the Legislatures of those States, it shall be lawful for Parliament to pass an act for regulating that matter accordingly."

Several structural elements must be noted:

*Two or more States is the minimum.* Article 252 cannot be invoked for a single State. The minimum threshold of two States reflects the provision's purpose — to enable multi-State harmonisation, not to give any single State a mechanism to invite Central legislation for itself alone (States already have the option of requesting Parliamentary legislation through political channels, but Article 252 gives that request constitutional force only when multiple States act together).

*Resolutions must pass through all Houses.* States with bicameral legislatures must secure passage of the resolution through both Houses. A Lower House resolution alone is insufficient. This requirement ensures that the consent is a considered legislative act, not merely an executive preference.

*The subject matter must be a State List matter.* Article 252 applies specifically to matters on which "Parliament has no power to make laws for the States except as provided in articles 249 and 250" — in other words, matters in List II (the State List) of the Seventh Schedule. Concurrent List subjects already permit Parliamentary legislation and do not require Article 252 consent.

*Adoption by other States.* Any State that did not participate in the initial consent can subsequently adopt the Act by passing a resolution in its own Legislature. Adoption by subsequent resolution gives the Act the same force in the adopting State as it has in the original consenting States. The adopting State is also bound by Article 252(2)'s bar on unilateral amendment.

**Clause (2) — lock-in of Parliamentary control:**

"Any Act so passed by Parliament may be amended or repealed by an Act of Parliament passed or adopted in like manner but shall not, as respects any State to which it applies, be amended or repealed by an Act of the Legislature of that State."

This is the irrevocability clause. Once a State is bound — whether as an original consenting State or as an adopting State — it cannot unilaterally amend or repeal the Central Act through State legislation. Only Parliament can amend or repeal, and it can do so only through the same consent mechanism — by passing an Act adopted in like manner (meaning a new round of State consents for the amending Act). This creates a constitutional lock that prevents individual States from defecting from the common framework after they have joined it.

The phrase "in like manner" for Parliamentary amendment is important. It means that the amending Act itself must be made in the same manner as the original — following the consent-resolution procedure. Parliament cannot unilaterally amend a Section 252 Act by ordinary legislation without the consent of the originally consenting States, unless the amending Act is framed as a fresh exercise of Parliamentary legislative power on a different legal basis (for example, the Concurrent List or Union List, if the subject also appears there).

## Key cases

**Ukha Kolhe v. State of Maharashtra (1963) 3 SCR 830** — One of the earliest Supreme Court decisions to examine the character of legislation passed under Article 252. The Court confirmed that an Act passed under Article 252 is a full Parliamentary Act, not delegated or secondary legislation. It has the same constitutional status and legal force as any Act of Parliament and is to be construed and challenged on the same grounds. The fact that its origin lies in State consent does not alter its fundamental character as a Parliamentary enactment. Courts must apply the standard tests of vires and repugnancy to such Acts as they would to any Central law.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. Union of India (1976) — Prize Competitions Act** — The Supreme Court examined the Prize Competitions Act 1955, enacted under Article 252 with the consent of several States. The Court confirmed that a State that has consented to or adopted an Article 252 Act cannot subsequently enact inconsistent State legislation on the same subject, even though the subject is formally within the State List for non-consenting States. Article 252(2) operates as a constitutional bar, not merely a political understanding. A State Legislature that attempts to override an Article 252 Act through fresh legislation is acting without competence — its law is void to the extent of the inconsistency.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1977) 4 SCC 608** — The Supreme Court examined the contours of Parliamentary legislative power and affirmed the basic constitutional principle that Article 252 is a vehicle for cooperative, consent-based federalism. The provision does not affect the federal distribution of power for States that have not consented; such States remain fully competent to regulate the subject independently. The Court noted that Article 252 represents the Constitution's recognition that uniform national regulation is sometimes practically necessary on State subjects, but that the mechanism for achieving it must respect State autonomy by requiring consent rather than imposing Central will.

**Transplantation of Human Organs Act cases (1994 onwards)** — The Transplantation of Human Organs Act 1994 was enacted partly under Article 252, with Goa, Himachal Pradesh, and Maharashtra passing initial consent resolutions. Subsequent States adopted the Act. Several High Court challenges raised questions about whether the Act validly covered modifications to the original consent framework as the Act was amended over time. These cases have produced judicial guidance on the meaning of "adopted in like manner" for amending Acts and the consequences for States that joined after the original Act was passed.

## Article 252 versus constitutional amendment

The most instructive comparison for understanding Article 252's limits is the GST experience. The Goods and Services Tax regime required the 101st Constitutional Amendment (inserting Articles 246A, 269A, and 279A) rather than an Article 252 consent arrangement. The political choice to seek constitutional amendment rather than use Article 252 reflects several critical differences between the two routes.

A constitutional amendment under Article 368 binds all States, whether or not they consent. Article 252, by contrast, applies only to States that have passed consent resolutions — non-consenting States remain outside the framework entirely. For a tax reform requiring universal application to function, partial coverage is structurally fatal. Article 252 could not have produced a workable GST because it could not bind every State and the entire GST chain depends on every taxable transaction being uniformly covered.

A constitutional amendment also creates a permanent, entrenched framework that cannot be undone by Parliamentary repeal alone — it requires the Article 368 process including State ratification in some cases. An Article 252 Act, by contrast, can be repealed by Parliament (through the same consent-based process), making it less suitable for fiscal frameworks that need long-term investment certainty. The GST Council's embedded architecture under Article 279A could not exist under an Article 252 Act — it required constitutional status.

Article 252 remains the appropriate mechanism for subjects where a coalition of willing States can benefit from uniform law, where universal participation is not essential, and where the subject matter does not need permanent constitutional entrenchment. Labour law reform, land acquisition standards, and cross-State environmental regulation are fields where Article 252 discussions have occurred or are ongoing. The provision is best understood as an intermediate tool — more binding than intergovernmental agreements, less coercive than either Parliamentary emergency powers or constitutional amendment.

## Current significance

Article 252 has produced important legislation across several decades: the Merchant Shipping Act for inland waterways, the Prize Competitions Act, the Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act 1976 (subsequently repealed by many States, which demonstrates that Article 252 is not the ideal mechanism for subjects requiring frequent State-level calibration), and the Transplantation of Human Organs Act 1994. The mechanism is actively discussed in connection with labour law reform (where consolidation into four Labour Codes has created tension between the Concurrent List basis for some labour laws and the need for State-level flexibility), agricultural marketing reform (before and after the withdrawn Farm Acts), and inter-State water distribution.

A recurring challenge with Article 252 is the difficulty of securing simultaneous resolutions from multiple State Legislatures when political parties differ across States. The mechanism works smoothly when the Central government enjoys cooperative relations with States or when a clear common interest is visible. It becomes politically fraught when States with different governing parties disagree about whether uniformity is desirable. The political economy of Article 252 therefore rewards the Central government for building State coalitions before seeking consent — the provision's formal requirements are a ceiling, not a floor, on what inter-governmental coordination is necessary.

The provision also reveals something fundamental about the Indian constitutional design's view of federalism: that States are political actors whose consent matters when their reserved legislative domain is being colonised, even voluntarily. Unlike Article 249 (which bypasses State consent through a Rajya Sabha supermajority) or Article 250 (which operates during emergencies), Article 252 treats States as genuine partners whose individual legislative houses must affirmatively choose to invite Parliamentary regulation. This consent requirement, however imperfect in practice, is a meaningful constitutional protection for State autonomy.
