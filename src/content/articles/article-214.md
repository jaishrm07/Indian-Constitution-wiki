## What this Article does

Article 214 declares that there shall be a High Court for each State, making the High Court a constitutionally mandated institution rather than a creature of ordinary legislation. The present text is a spare and powerful one — "There shall be a High Court for each State" — but it carries enormous constitutional weight. It transforms the High Court from an institution that Parliament could theoretically abolish or restructure at will into one whose existence is guaranteed by the Constitution itself.

The one-State-one-High-Court principle is the baseline established by Article 214, with Article 231 carving out a separate parliamentary authority to create a common High Court for two or more States. Several joint High Courts exist under this provision, notably for Punjab and Haryana (at Chandigarh) and historically for Assam, Nagaland, Meghalaya, Manipur, Tripura, and Mizoram (before several got their own High Courts). When a common High Court exists under Article 231, Article 214's guarantee continues to be satisfied for each participating State through the shared institution.

## High Courts as constitutional courts

High Courts are not merely appellate courts for ordinary civil and criminal matters. They are constitutional courts of the States, exercising a range of distinct constitutional jurisdictions:

**Original writ jurisdiction under Article 226** — High Courts can issue writs including habeas corpus, mandamus, certiorari, prohibition, and quo warranto to enforce fundamental rights and other legal rights against any person or authority within their territorial jurisdiction. This jurisdiction is wider than the Supreme Court's Article 32 jurisdiction because it extends to legal rights beyond fundamental rights.

**Superintendence under Article 227** — High Courts exercise superintendence over all courts and tribunals in the State, a power that cannot be ousted by Parliament even when it establishes specialised tribunals.

**Judicial review** — High Courts review the constitutional validity of legislation under Articles 13, 32 (read with 226), and 226, striking down laws that violate fundamental rights or exceed legislative competence.

**Appellate jurisdiction** — High Courts hear appeals from subordinate courts in civil and criminal matters under Articles 225 and various procedural codes.

**Reference jurisdiction** — Questions of constitutional interpretation can be referred from subordinate courts to the High Court, and from the High Court to the Supreme Court.

The guarantee of a High Court in each State is thus a guarantee of access to these constitutional jurisdictions for citizens of each State, not merely the provision of an appellate tier.

## Independence of High Courts

The independence of High Courts is protected by several constitutional provisions that flow from or interact with Article 214:

**Appointment** — High Court judges are appointed by the President under Article 217 after consultation with the Chief Justice of India, the Governor, and the Chief Justice of the High Court. The collegium process developed by the Supreme Court ensures the judiciary has a predominant role.

**Tenure** — Judges hold office until age 62 (compared to 65 for Supreme Court judges), and can only be removed by the same cumbersome impeachment process applicable to Supreme Court judges under Article 124(4).

**Remuneration** — Charged to the Consolidated Fund of the State, the salaries and allowances of High Court judges are not subject to the vote of the State legislature, protecting them from financial pressure.

**Transfer** — The President can transfer judges between High Courts under Article 222, exercising this power on the recommendation of the Chief Justice of India. Transfer has been used as both a means of managing judicial resources and — controversially — as a form of pressure on inconvenient judges.

## Relationship with the Supreme Court

High Courts occupy a unique position in the constitutional hierarchy. They are subordinate to the Supreme Court in the sense that Article 141 declarations of law bind them, and appeals lie from High Court decisions to the Supreme Court under Articles 132–136. But High Courts are not merely subordinate courts in the administrative sense: they are constitutional courts in their own right, with jurisdiction and powers derived directly from the Constitution.

The Supreme Court cannot ordinarily interfere with High Court proceedings through writ jurisdiction; the proper route is appeal. High Courts can take positions on constitutional questions that differ from Supreme Court dicta (as opposed to the ratio decidendi of binding decisions), triggering the process by which conflicting High Court views are resolved at the Supreme Court level. This dynamic of constitutional dialogue between High Courts and the Supreme Court is a healthy feature of India's multi-tier constitutional judiciary.

## Jurisdiction over Union Territories

Article 230 allows Parliament to extend a High Court's jurisdiction to Union Territories. Several Union Territories are within the jurisdiction of adjacent State High Courts: Delhi is within the Delhi High Court (created as a separate High Court in 1966), Puducherry within the Madras High Court, Lakshadweep within the Kerala High Court. Article 214's one-State-one-court guarantee does not extend to Union Territories, but those territories are not left without High Court access.

## Key cases

**L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997) 3 SCC 261** — The Constitution Bench held that High Courts are a part of the basic structure of the Constitution and that their power of judicial review under Articles 226 and 227 cannot be excluded by Parliament. Legislation that creates tribunals must preserve an appellate layer before the High Court to be constitutionally valid. This judgment confirmed that the Article 214 guarantee of a High Court is not merely institutional but also functional: the High Court must retain its essential judicial review jurisdiction.

**State of West Bengal v. Committee for Protection of Democratic Rights (2010) 3 SCC 571** — The Supreme Court held that a High Court under Part VI can, in appropriate cases involving fundamental rights violations, direct a Central investigation agency like the CBI to investigate crimes within the State, even against the State government's wishes. The Court affirmed that High Courts hold a coordinate constitutional status with the Supreme Court for the protection of fundamental rights within their territorial jurisdiction.

**Union of India v. Madras Bar Association (2010) 11 SCC 1** — The Court reinforced that Parliament cannot destroy the essential judicial character of High Courts even while creating specialised tribunals, and that the Article 214 guarantee is part of the Constitution's basic structure. Tribunals can supplement but cannot supplant the High Courts.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2020) 6 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court, addressing the National Company Law Appellate Tribunal and other financial tribunals, revisited the question of exclusion of High Court jurisdiction in light of L. Chandra Kumar, confirming that Article 214 High Courts must remain the constitutional superior courts for their States and that any tribunal architecture that effectively eliminates High Court oversight is unconstitutional.

**S.P. Gupta v. Union of India (1981) Supp SCC 87 (First Judges Case)** — The Supreme Court addressed the constitutional importance of High Court independence and the process for appointment and transfer of judges, engaging directly with the institutional protections that give substance to Article 214's guarantee of a High Court in every State. The case initiated three decades of judicial-appointments jurisprudence culminating in the collegium system.

## Current significance

Article 214 continues to anchor the most important ongoing debates in Indian constitutional law. The proliferation of Central and State tribunals — the National Green Tribunal, NCLT, DRT, SAT, and dozens of sector-specific bodies — keeps alive the question of whether these institutions are displacing the High Courts that Article 214 guarantees. Following L. Chandra Kumar and Rojer Mathew, courts and legislators must continually assess whether tribunal architectures preserve adequate High Court oversight.

Proposals to merge smaller High Courts — such as the Manipur, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Sikkim High Courts — to address acute vacancy shortages and infrastructure limitations have been recommended by parliamentary committees but have not been adopted, partly because of the constitutional and political complexity of modifying the Article 214 baseline for those States.

The question of the scope of High Court jurisdiction over the National Capital Territory of Delhi — where the Union government, not the elected State government, exercises significant administrative powers — has engaged Article 214 at its intersection with the Delhi Governance provisions of Article 239AA. The Delhi High Court's relationship to both the State and Union governmental structures makes it a constitutional institution at the cutting edge of federalism debates.
