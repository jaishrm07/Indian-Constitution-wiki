## Why it matters

Article 241 addresses a specific constitutional lacuna: the constitutional chapters on High Courts
(Articles 214–231 in Chapter V of Part VI) are framed around States, not Union territories. A
State must have a High Court under Article 214; a UT has no such constitutional entitlement. Article
241 solves this by giving Parliament a menu of choices — constitute a dedicated UT High Court by
law, declare an existing court within the UT to be a High Court, or extend a neighbouring State
High Court's jurisdiction to cover the UT. The flexibility is deliberate: Parliament can match
the judicial architecture to the territory's size, population, and administrative capacity without
being constitutionally required to create a standalone High Court for every small territory.

In practice Parliament has used all three approaches. The High Court of Delhi was constituted by
Parliament under this power through the Delhi High Court Act 1966 and is now also linked to the
NCT's special constitutional status under Article 239AA. For smaller UTs, Parliament has preferred
to extend mainland High Court jurisdiction under clause (4): the Calcutta High Court has jurisdiction
over the Andaman and Nicobar Islands; the Bombay High Court has jurisdiction over Goa (now a State),
Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu; and the Kerala High Court has jurisdiction over
Lakshadweep. This approach avoids the cost of establishing standalone courts in geographically
remote territories, but it creates access-to-justice challenges for residents who must travel
hundreds or thousands of kilometres to reach the High Court bench.

The significance of Article 241 extends beyond access issues. The provision ensures that every
inch of Indian territory is within the supervisory jurisdiction of a High Court — and therefore
that the constitutionality of executive action, the validity of presidential regulations under
Article 240, and the protection of fundamental rights are all judicially supervised within the
UT governance framework. The absence of a local legislature in most UTs makes High Court oversight
especially important as a check on the presidential administration model in Article 239.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Parliament's three options.** Parliament may (a) constitute a High Court for a UT
by law — creating an entirely new institution; (b) declare any existing court in the UT to be a
High Court "for all or any of the purposes of this Constitution" — upgrading an existing subordinate
court to constitutional status; or (c) use clause (4) to extend a State High Court's jurisdiction.
The phrase "for all or any of the purposes" in option (b) is significant — it permits Parliament
to create a partial High Court status, giving an existing court High Court powers for some
constitutional purposes (such as Article 226 writs) but not others (such as supervisory jurisdiction
over subordinate courts under Article 227).

**Clause (2) — application of Chapter V standards.** All the provisions of Chapter V of Part VI
(Articles 214–231) apply to UT High Courts as they apply to State High Courts under Article 214,
subject to modifications Parliament may make. This means UT High Court judges are appointed through
the same collegium process under Articles 217 and 222, hold the same security of tenure, receive
the same constitutional protections, and exercise the same original and appellate jurisdiction as
their State counterparts, unless Parliament has expressly modified a provision. Parliament's power
to make "modifications or exceptions" is a significant flexibility — it allows the UT High Court
framework to be adjusted to accommodate the different governance architecture of a territory that
lacks a State government and State Legislature as such.

**Clause (3) — continuity of pre-1956 jurisdiction.** This clause preserves the jurisdiction of
any High Court that was exercising jurisdiction over a UT territory immediately before the
Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956 came into force. The Seventh Amendment reorganised States
on a linguistic basis and reclassified many territories; clause (3) ensures that judicial coverage
was not accidentally lost during that reorganisation by providing for continuity of existing
High Court jurisdiction over UT areas.

**Clause (4) — extension and exclusion of State High Court jurisdiction.** Parliament may extend
or exclude the jurisdiction of any State High Court to or from a Union territory or part of it.
This is the mechanism through which Parliament has assigned High Court coverage for the island
UTs. The provision preserves Parliament's future flexibility — it can reassign UT coverage from one
High Court to another, or create a new UT High Court and retract the extended State High Court
jurisdiction, without being constitutionally constrained by any prior arrangement.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The question of judicial administration for Union territories was addressed in the Constituent
Assembly in the context of the broader reorganisation of India's judicial map. The Seventh Amendment
(1956) was the first major post-independence reorganisation, and it prompted the insertion of Article
241's predecessor provisions to deal with the judicial arrangements for the newly classified
territories. The decision to give Parliament full flexibility over UT judicial architecture — rather
than constitutionalising a specific arrangement — reflected the framers' awareness that the UT
category itself was fluid and that territories might be reorganised, upgraded to States, or merged
in ways that would require corresponding adjustments to the judicial map.

## Key cases

**Delhi Judicial Service Association v. State of Gujarat (1991) 4 SCC 406** — The Supreme Court's
discussion of High Court supervisory powers and the structure of the judicial system under the
Constitution informs the interpretation of what it means for a UT court to be declared a High
Court "for all or any of the purposes of this Constitution" — the qualifying phrase in Article
241(1)(b). The Court emphasised the unitary superintendence of the Supreme Court over all courts
in India, which applies equally to UT High Courts as to State High Courts.

**Union of India v. Madras Bar Association (2021) 7 SCC 369** — The Court's analysis of Parliament's
power to modify the constitutional framework for High Courts through legislation provides context
for understanding the "modifications or exceptions" Parliament may make under Article 241(2). The
judgment addresses how far Parliament can go in structuring the institutional arrangements of
constitutional courts without undermining judicial independence — a question directly relevant to
the scope of Parliament's power to adapt Chapter V provisions for UT High Courts.

**L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997) 3 SCC 261** — The seven-judge bench's ruling that
the power of judicial review under Articles 226 and 227 is a basic feature of the Constitution and
cannot be abrogated or curtailed even by Parliament affects how Article 241's High Courts must be
constituted. Parliament's power to make "modifications or exceptions" to Chapter V provisions for
UT High Courts cannot extend to denying UT High Courts their supervisory jurisdiction or their
power to issue writs for enforcement of fundamental rights.

**NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018) 8 SCC 501 and (2023) Services judgment** — The Delhi
High Court, constituted under Article 241 and linked to the NCT under Article 239AA, has been the
institutional forum for the major constitutional litigation over Delhi's governance architecture.
The Court's own constitutional foundation under Article 241 is relevant to understanding the basis
of its jurisdiction to adjudicate disputes between the elected Delhi government and the Union
executive operating through the Lieutenant Governor.

**Vashisht Narayan Singh v. State of Bihar** — Questions about the jurisdictional competence of
High Courts over UT matters have arisen in the context of determining whether a High Court
exercising extended jurisdiction under Article 241 can issue writs that run throughout the UT and
whether administrative orders of the UT administration are subject to the same supervisory
jurisdiction as State executive action under Article 226. Courts have generally held that the
extension of jurisdiction under clause (4) carries with it the full supervisory and writ jurisdiction
of the High Court over the UT's administration.

## Current significance

The Delhi High Court, constituted under Article 241 and now also linked to the NCT's special
constitutional status under Article 239AA, handles the largest volume of constitutional and public
law litigation touching on UT governance in India. Its jurisdiction over disputes between the elected
Delhi government, the Lieutenant Governor, and the Union — including challenges to the GNCTD
Amendment Act 2023 — makes the Court's own constitutional foundation under this article
institutionally important. The Delhi High Court is perhaps the only High Court whose constitutional
basis, institutional powers, and practical role are simultaneously governed by three distinct
frameworks — Article 241, the Delhi High Court Act 1966, and the Chapter V provisions as adapted
for the NCT context.

For smaller UTs, the extension of mainland High Court jurisdiction under clause (4) has practical
limitations: parties must travel to a distant High Court for constitutional relief, raising
access-to-justice concerns. The Andaman and Nicobar Islands are 1,200 kilometres from Kolkata;
Lakshadweep is several hundred kilometres off the Kerala coast. The arrangement has been periodically
questioned, and calls for permanent circuit benches or dedicated benches at the UT level reflect
the ongoing tension between judicial economy and the constitutional guarantee of effective High
Court access. The Supreme Court's periodic directions establishing circuit sittings of mainland
High Courts in UT territories represent a partial accommodation of this concern within the existing
Article 241 framework.

The adequacy of the current judicial architecture for Ladakh — the most recently created major UT,
with a High Court arrangement inherited from J&K — is also under active discussion. The J&K and
Ladakh High Court continues to serve both Jammu and Kashmir and Ladakh following the 2019
reorganisation, but the long-term judicial arrangement for each territory, particularly as and when
J&K's statehood is restored, will require Parliament to exercise its Article 241 powers to establish
an appropriate judicial structure.
