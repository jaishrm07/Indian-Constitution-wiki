## Why it matters

Article 64 creates a unique institutional fusion: the same person is simultaneously the nation's constitutional presidential successor and the presiding officer of the upper house of Parliament. No other major constitutional democracy uses this exact design, although the United States Vice-President presiding over the Senate is its closest analogue. In India the combination is especially consequential because the Rajya Sabha's role in the legislative process is substantial — it can block ordinary legislation and its Chairman has significant procedural authority over the conduct of parliamentary business.

The ex officio nature of the chairmanship is what makes Article 64 constitutionally distinct from the parallel provision for the Speaker of the Lok Sabha under Article 93. The Speaker is elected by the Lok Sabha from among its own members. The Rajya Sabha Chairman is not elected by the Rajya Sabha at all — the chairmanship flows automatically from holding the Vice-Presidency, which is determined by a joint Parliament electoral college (under Article 66). This means the Rajya Sabha Chairman's authority is constitutionally grounded in the Vice-Presidential office rather than in the confidence of the upper house itself. Critics of this design argue that it creates a structural alignment between the Chair and the executive that the Rajya Sabha cannot independently correct except by initiating the difficult Vice-Presidential removal procedure under Article 67.

The prohibition on holding any other office of profit mirrors the condition imposed on the President by Article 59(2) and reinforces the principle that constitutional heads — both executive and legislative — must owe exclusive institutional loyalty to their constitutional office. A Vice-President who simultaneously served on a paid board, regulatory commission, or advisory body would have conflicts of loyalty that would compromise the impartiality required of a Rajya Sabha presiding officer.

The proviso's clean separation of the chairmanship role from the presidential function during any Article 65 substitution reflects an important constitutional design principle: the separation of legislative and executive authority is maintained even when the same person temporarily holds both constitutional roles in sequence. When the Vice-President steps up to act as President, the Rajya Sabha does not go without a presiding officer — the Deputy Chairman under Article 91 takes over. The Vice-President forfeits the Chairman's salary during this period (receiving presidential emoluments instead), preventing any double compensation for simultaneous constitutional functions.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "ex officio Chairman of the Council of States" has precise constitutional meaning. "Ex officio" means by virtue of office — the Vice-President does not need to be elected, confirmed, or approved by the Rajya Sabha in any separate process. The chairmanship attaches automatically and immediately on the Vice-President entering office under Article 69's oath. This automatic vesting distinguishes the Vice-President's chairmanship from that of the Deputy Chairman under Article 91, who is elected by the Rajya Sabha from among its members.

The "shall not hold any other office of profit" condition creates a constitutional prohibition that is self-executing: a Vice-President who accepts a remunerative position in violation of this condition would be acting unconstitutionally. Unlike the pre-election office-of-profit bar under Article 66(4) — which must be satisfied before a person can stand for election — the Article 64 prohibition is a continuing condition throughout the tenure, analogous to Article 59(2) for the President.

The proviso uses both "acts as President" and "discharges the functions of the President" — tracking the two situations in Article 65: clause (1) covers true vacancies (death, resignation, removal) where the Vice-President fully "acts as President," while clause (2) covers temporary incapacities where the Vice-President merely "discharges the functions." Both trigger the suspension of the chairmanship duties and the chairmanship salary. The distinction matters constitutionally: in the first case the Vice-Presidential office itself is still filled (the Vice-President continues to hold that office while acting up), whereas in the second case the Vice-President never ceases to be Vice-President — they just temporarily exercise presidential functions.

## Key cases

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court's survey of Part V noted that the Vice-President's dual role as potential presidential successor and Rajya Sabha Chairman gives the office a unique constitutional character. The ex officio chairmanship is a constitutional appointment, not a parliamentary election, distinguishing the Vice-President's authority to preside from that of a Speaker elected by the House. The Court confirmed that both roles must be exercised within the constitutional framework — the Chairman cannot act arbitrarily in procedural matters any more than the President can act arbitrarily in executive matters.

**Subhash C. Agarwal v Union of India (RTI matters, 2019)** — The Supreme Court's Constitution Bench in the Chief Justice-as-public-authority case and the broader RTI and constitutional office litigation confirmed that the Vice-President, acting as Rajya Sabha Chairman under Article 64, is a public authority subject to the Right to Information Act. The Court's reasoning established that the constitutional basis for the chairmanship in Article 64 does not insulate the office from transparency obligations under ordinary legislation enacted in furtherance of constitutional rights.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Constitution Bench's analysis of the anti-defection schedule and the powers of a Speaker or Chairman under the Tenth Schedule has implications for the Vice-President's chairmanship under Article 64. The Court's reasoning about the limits of presiding officer discretion — that procedural decisions must be fair, non-partisan, and amenable to review for constitutional excess — applies equally to the Rajya Sabha Chairman's exercise of powers under Article 64.

**Rajya Sabha — Vice-Presidential removal debate (2023)** — Opposition parties tabled notices of removal of Vice-President Jagdeep Dhankhar under Article 67(b), citing alleged partisan conduct of Rajya Sabha proceedings under Article 64. While the removal attempt did not succeed (it lacked the required majority), it generated extensive constitutional debate about the relationship between the chairmanship function and parliamentary independence. The episode illustrated that Article 64's institutional design — combining electoral mandate and presiding authority — creates structural tensions that cannot be resolved purely by convention.

## Current significance

The Vice-President's role as Rajya Sabha Chairman has become one of the most politically contested aspects of Indian constitutional governance in periods of divided Parliament. The Chairman's powers include: deciding whether a Bill is a Money Bill (which affects whether the Rajya Sabha can amend it); presiding over Question Hour; ruling on points of order; suspending unruly members; and authenticating Bills passed by the House. Each of these powers has generated controversy in recent parliamentary sessions.

The ex officio nature of the chairmanship — derived from Article 64 rather than from the Rajya Sabha's own election — has fuelled criticism that the design structurally favours the ruling majority, since the Vice-President is elected by Parliament under Article 66 and a ruling majority in Parliament controls that election. The counter-argument is that the same structural advantage applies to the Lok Sabha Speaker, who is elected by the Lok Sabha's majority under Article 93, and that constitutional convention and institutional pride rather than formal independence safeguards are the real checks on presiding-officer partisanship in parliamentary systems.

The proviso's salary-separation mechanism has practical implications for compensation law. When a Vice-President performs presidential functions under Article 65 for any sustained period — as happened in 1969 and in 2022 when the outgoing President's term ended before the new President was sworn in — the transition between chairmanship emoluments and presidential emoluments must be managed through the Second Schedule and the relevant emoluments legislation, creating a brief but constitutionally significant compensation adjustment.
