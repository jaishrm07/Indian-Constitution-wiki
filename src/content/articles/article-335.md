  335. Claims of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes to services and posts.—The claims of the members of the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes shall be taken into consideration, consistently with the maintenance of efficiency of administration, in the making of appointments to services and posts in connection with the affairs of the Union or of a State:

## Why it matters

Article 335 is the constitutional provision that has generated the most sustained and politically charged litigation in the Indian reservation framework, specifically on the question of promotional reservation. While the debate over initial appointment quotas was largely settled by Indra Sawhney (1992), the question of whether reservation can extend to promotions — and if so, under what conditions — has occupied the Supreme Court in three major Constitution Bench decisions over twenty years. The stakes are high: promotional reservation determines who rises to the senior levels of the Indian Administrative Service, the central police organisations, and the Union and State secretariats — the posts that shape policy and exercise administrative power.

The efficiency qualifier in the main text is the core constitutional tension. The framers understood that a State must be able to govern effectively and that reservation, like all affirmative action measures, carries the risk of reducing the average qualification level in a service if applied without regard to competence. But they also understood that the pre-existing services were virtually closed to SC and ST communities regardless of qualification, reflecting not merit-based selection but caste-based exclusion. Article 335 reflects an attempt to hold both truths simultaneously: SC and ST claims must be considered, but they must be considered alongside the State's need for an efficient administration.

The 82nd Amendment, which inserted the proviso, shifted the constitutional balance by clarifying that the efficiency qualifier cannot be used to categorically exclude promotional reservation or mark relaxations from the constitutional toolkit. That proviso did not eliminate the efficiency requirement; it clarified that the requirement cannot be deployed as an automatic bar. The resulting framework — efficiency as a balancing condition rather than a categorical limit — is the terrain on which the Supreme Court has been working ever since.

## Reading the provision

**The main clause** imposes two simultaneous obligations. The State must take SC and ST members' claims into consideration — meaning it cannot simply ignore those claims when making appointments. And it must do so consistently with maintaining efficiency — meaning it cannot implement reservation in ways that are demonstrated to undermine the overall efficiency of the service in question. The phrase "take into consideration" is weaker than a mandatory directive; the State is not required to grant reservation in every case, but it must consciously engage with the claims of SC and ST members in its appointments decisions.

**The 82nd Amendment proviso** clarifies three specific things that the efficiency qualifier does not prohibit: relaxation of qualifying marks in examinations; lowering of evaluation standards for SC and ST candidates; and reservation in promotions to any class or classes of services. The proviso was specifically aimed at the judicial use of Article 335's efficiency language to strike down promotional reservation schemes in the immediate post-Indra Sawhney period, when some High Courts had held that promotions — especially to senior and specialised posts — required pure merit and could not be subject to quotas.

The proviso does not, however, grant an unconditional right to promotional reservation. What it does is remove Article 335 as an automatic constitutional bar. The question of whether promotional reservation is permissible in a given service or cadre remains subject to the conditions developed by the Supreme Court in M. Nagaraj, Jarnail Singh, and B.K. Pavitra — requiring the State to demonstrate inadequate representation and consistent with the efficiency of the service at the aggregate level.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debates on what became Article 335 focused on the apparent tension between the commitment to SC and ST inclusion and the demands of a newly independent State that required competent civil services to govern. Dr Ambedkar addressed this tension directly, arguing that the efficiency argument had historically been used to justify the near-total exclusion of SC and ST communities from the civil services and that a genuinely efficient civil service could not be one that was drawn exclusively from the upper castes. He contended that representation of the governed communities in the governing apparatus was itself a component of administrative efficiency in a democratic State.

Other members, including several from the Indian Civil Service background, pressed for stronger language requiring that reservation not impair the quality of administration. The compromise — "consistently with the maintenance of efficiency" as a qualifying condition rather than an overriding bar — emerged from these debates and has defined the interpretive battles over the article ever since. The 1947 framing left to courts the question of how to balance these competing considerations, a question that has proved enduringly contested.

## Key cases

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — The nine-judge bench, while primarily deciding OBC reservations under Article 16(4), addressed Article 335's role in the constitutional framework. The Court held that reservation in initial appointments was constitutionally valid and that the efficiency requirement in Article 335 was not an independent ground to limit SC and ST reservation in entry-level posts. The majority also addressed promotional reservation, with a split on whether Article 16(4A) extending reservation to promotions could be sustained.

**M. Nagaraj v Union of India (2006)** — A Constitution Bench upheld the constitutional validity of Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B), which allow reservation in promotions with consequential seniority, subject to the State demonstrating three conditions: quantifiable data on backwardness of the class, inadequate representation in the grade or service, and compliance with the overall efficiency of administration as required by Article 335. The Court treated Article 335 not as a bar to promotional reservation but as a constitutional safeguard requiring a balancing exercise, with the State bearing the burden of demonstrating that the reservation scheme is consistent with overall efficiency.

**Jarnail Singh v Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018)** — A five-judge bench modified M. Nagaraj to the extent that States need not collect quantifiable data demonstrating the individual backwardness of SC and ST employees seeking promotion — since their backwardness is constitutionally presumed by their inclusion in the Presidential Schedules. The Court reaffirmed, however, that the efficiency condition in Article 335 requires a conscious assessment at the aggregate service level: the State must show that promotional reservation has been designed so as not to impair the overall efficiency of the service or cadre. Jarnail Singh also left open the question of whether the creamy-layer principle should apply to SC and ST communities in the promotional context, a question taken up in Davinder Singh (2024).

**B.K. Pavitra v Union of India (No. 2) (2019)** — The Supreme Court upheld the Karnataka Reservation in Promotion Act, finding that the State had conducted an adequate exercise under the Jarnail Singh framework. The State's data demonstrated inadequate representation of SCs and STs at the relevant promotion levels, and the Court found that the reservation scheme was designed consistently with maintaining overall administrative efficiency. The judgment confirmed that the reduced Jarnail Singh burden — no need for individual backwardness data — was workable in practice and that States could successfully discharge it through cadre-level analysis.

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench upheld sub-classification within SC and ST lists for employment reservation, overruling E.V. Chinnaiah (2005). While the primary holding concerned the power of States to sub-classify, Justice B.R. Gavai's influential concurrence argued that the creamy-layer principle should be extended to SCs and STs in the promotional context — a view that if adopted as binding ratio would require a fundamental restructuring of the promotional reservation framework under Article 335 and Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B).

**Union of India v Virpal Singh Chauhan (1995)** — A case addressing the seniority consequences of promotional reservation, the Court held that consequential seniority — where a person promoted under reservation is treated as senior to a general-category person who would have been promoted earlier on merit — was not permissible without specific constitutional authority. This ruling led directly to the constitutional amendments inserting Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) to provide that authority.

## Current significance

The post-Jarnail Singh landscape has stabilised promotional reservation law at the point where States must demonstrate inadequate representation and aggregate efficiency — a burden that is practically achievable as shown by B.K. Pavitra — but are not required to show individual backwardness of each SC or ST employee in the promotional pool. Several States have conducted the required exercises and enacted or re-enacted promotional reservation laws. The resulting cadre-specific data on representation levels is increasingly used not just in litigation but in administrative planning for workforce diversity targets.

The most significant pending question is the creamy-layer issue flagged in Davinder Singh (2024). Justice Gavai's concurrence argued that once sub-classification within SC lists is permissible — on the basis that the SC category is not a homogeneous group — it follows logically that the most socially advanced within that category (the equivalent of the OBC creamy layer) should not continue to benefit from reservation in promotions. If a future Constitution Bench adopts this reasoning as binding, it would require Parliament to amend Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) or for the executive to redefine the eligible class for promotional reservation within SC and ST lists. This would be the most significant restructuring of the SC and ST reservation framework since the Mandal case.

Article 335 also has relevance to the growing discussion about lateral entry into the Indian Administrative Service. If senior positions are filled by lateral entrants from the private sector without any reservation requirement, the proportion of SC and ST members in the effective senior administration would decline even if the formal reserved quota in direct recruitment and promotions is maintained. The question of whether the Article 335 efficiency-plus-inclusion framework applies to lateral entry schemes has not yet been conclusively addressed by the courts.
