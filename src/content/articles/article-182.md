## What this Article does

Article 182 is the bicameral counterpart of Article 178: it requires the Legislative Council of every state that has one to elect two of its members as Chairman and Deputy Chairman respectively. The Chairman presides over the Council, maintains order, certifies whether bills conform to Council procedure, and in general performs for the upper house the same institutional role that the Speaker performs for the Assembly. Vacancies in either office must be filled by fresh election from within the Council.

Only six states currently have a Legislative Council: Andhra Pradesh, Bihar, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh. The Article is therefore of direct operational relevance only in these bicameral states, though its constitutional significance extends to any state that may in the future create a Council under Article 169.

## The office in the constitutional scheme

The Chairman of the Legislative Council occupies a position of considerable constitutional authority. Like the Speaker of the Assembly, the Chairman is elected by the members of the House and is expected to act impartially once elected. The Chairman presides over debates, maintains order, decides points of order, certifies bills and money bills, and exercises a casting vote when there is a tie. Under the Tenth Schedule, the Chairman also exercises quasi-judicial powers in deciding disqualification petitions against members of the Council, making the office a site of both procedural and adjudicatory authority. The Deputy Chairman performs all these functions in the Chairman's absence and, in states where the Council is active, the Deputy Chairman often presides over significant legislative business.

The requirement that the Council choose its presiding officers "as soon as may be" imposes a constitutional obligation of prompt action after each general election or vacancy, reflecting the framers' intent that presiding authority must not remain unfilled for extended periods. However, the Constitution does not prescribe a specific time limit, and states have sometimes allowed vacancies to linger when the political arithmetic of the Council makes consensus difficult.

## Key cases

**Kihoto Hollohan v Zachillhu (1992)** — Though directly about the Tenth Schedule and the Speaker's role, the Constitution Bench's analysis confirmed that the Chairman of the Legislative Council holds an equivalent quasi-judicial position under the Schedule for Council members, meaning the Article 182 office carries the same anti-defection adjudicatory responsibilities and corresponding judicial review scrutiny as the Speakership.

**Maharashtra Legislative Council Chairman proceedings (2022)** — During the Maharashtra political crisis, the Chairman's exercise of procedural powers in the Council was scrutinised alongside the Speaker's decisions in the Assembly. Courts affirmed that the Chairman of a Legislative Council exercises powers under Articles 182–185 in parallel to, but independently from, the Speaker's powers under Articles 178–181, and is subject to the same Nabam Rebia constraints when a removal motion is pending.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — While this case arose in the Assembly context, the Supreme Court's holding that a presiding officer cannot take consequential decisions when facing a removal notice has been applied by analogy to the Chairman of the Council, creating a significant structural constraint on the Article 182 office.

## Current significance

The office of Chairman of the Legislative Council has grown in constitutional prominence as bicameral states have become arenas for political crises involving floor-crossing and competing claims to majority. In Bihar, Karnataka, and Maharashtra, Tenth Schedule proceedings against Council members have tested the boundaries of the Chairman's quasi-judicial independence. The structural problem identified in the Assembly context — that the elected presiding officer may be partial in disqualification decisions affecting the ruling coalition — applies with equal force to the Chairman of the Council, prompting ongoing calls for reform to the adjudicatory mechanism across both Houses.

Proposals to create an independent tribunal for anti-defection adjudication, currently under consideration by parliamentary and academic bodies, would directly affect the Article 182 office by removing the Chairman's Tenth Schedule jurisdiction. Whether such reform requires a constitutional amendment has itself been a matter of debate, since the Tenth Schedule expressly vests the function in the presiding officer.

## Comparison with the Union provision

Article 182 mirrors Article 93 for the Rajya Sabha in structure and function. Just as the Rajya Sabha elects a Deputy Chairman from among its members (the Vice-President serving as ex officio Chairman), the Legislative Council elects both its Chairman and Deputy Chairman from among its own members, making the Council entirely self-governing in respect of its presiding officers. This contrasts with the Rajya Sabha's dual-officer model and makes the Chairman of the Legislative Council more analogous to a Speaker than to the Rajya Sabha's Deputy Chairman. The constitutional decision to give the Council complete authority over its presiding officers reflects the framers' intention to preserve the institutional independence of both Houses of a bicameral state legislature.

The article reflects the broader constitutional architecture of Part VI, which deliberately mirrors the Union structure of Part V to ensure that state legislative institutions operate under a framework of comparable constitutional dignity and constraint.
