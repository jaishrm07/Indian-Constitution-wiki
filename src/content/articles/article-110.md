  110. Definition of "Money Bills".—(1) For the purposes of this Chapter, a Bill shall be deemed to be a Money Bill if it contains only provisions dealing with all or any of the following matters, namely:—

  (a) the imposition, abolition, remission, alteration or regulation of any tax;

  (b) the regulation of the borrowing of money or the giving of any guarantee by the Government of India, or the amendment of the law with respect to any financial obligations undertaken or to be undertaken by the Government of India;

  (c) the custody of the Consolidated Fund or the Contingency Fund of India, the payment of moneys into or the withdrawal of moneys from any such Fund;

  (d) the appropriation of moneys out of the Consolidated Fund of India;

  (e) the declaring of any expenditure to be expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund of India or the increasing of the amount of any such expenditure;

  (f) the receipt of money on account of the Consolidated Fund of India or the public account of India or the custody or issue of such money or the audit of the accounts of the Union or of a State; or

  (g) any matter incidental to any of the matters specified in subclauses (a) to (f).

  (2) A Bill shall not be deemed to be a Money Bill by reason only that it provides for the imposition of fines or other pecuniary penalties, or for the demand or payment of fees for licences or fees for services rendered, or by reason that it provides for the imposition, abolition, remission, alteration or regulation of any tax by any local authority or body for local purposes.

  (3) If any question arises whether a Bill is a Money Bill or not, the decision of the Speaker of the House of the People thereon shall be final.

  (4) There shall be endorsed on every Money Bill when it is transmitted to the Council of States under article 109, and when it is presented to the President for assent under article 111, the certificate of the Speaker of the House of the People signed by him that it is a Money Bill.

## Why it matters

Article 110 is the gatekeeper provision for the entire Money Bill procedure. The definition it contains determines when Rajya Sabha loses its co-equal legislative status and becomes a mere advisory chamber. Because the Article 109 procedure strips Rajya Sabha of its veto, the line Article 110 draws between Money Bills and other financial legislation is constitutionally consequential: a Bill that should go through Rajya Sabha as a co-legislator can, if misclassified, bypass it entirely. The accuracy of that classification — and whether anyone can review it — has become the central controversy in Indian parliamentary law.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the exhaustive list:** A Bill is a Money Bill if it contains "only provisions dealing with" one or more of seven categories:
- (a) imposition, abolition, remission, alteration or regulation of any tax;
- (b) regulation of government borrowing or amendment of financial obligation law;
- (c) custody or management of the Consolidated Fund or Contingency Fund;
- (d) appropriation of money from the Consolidated Fund;
- (e) declaring expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund or increasing such expenditure;
- (f) receipts, custody, issue, or audit of Consolidated Fund and public account moneys; and
- (g) any matter incidental to the above.

The word "only" is critical. A Bill that contains a provision falling outside these seven categories is disqualified from being a Money Bill — no matter how many provisions it also has that do fall within them. Clause (1)(g) — the "incidental matters" gateway — has been the site of aggressive government interpretation, with the argument that any provision with a financial dimension can be characterised as incidental to one of the preceding clauses.

**Clause (2) — what does NOT count:** Fines and penalties, licence fees and service fees, and taxes levied by local authorities for local purposes do not make a Bill a Money Bill. This prevents every regulatory statute (which routinely imposes fines) from being classified as a Money Bill merely because it has a financial element.

**Clause (3) — Speaker's certificate as final:** The Lok Sabha Speaker's decision on whether a Bill is a Money Bill is expressed to be "final." This is the most contested clause. Governments have relied on finality to argue that once the Speaker certifies a Bill, courts cannot look behind the certificate. Critics and a significant body of Supreme Court jurisprudence have distinguished between the Speaker's decision being conclusive as a matter of internal parliamentary procedure (protected by Article 122) and the constitutional validity of the certification being beyond review (not so easily insulated).

**Clause (4) — physical endorsement:** The Speaker's certificate must be physically endorsed on the Bill as it is transmitted to Rajya Sabha under Article 109 and when presented to the President for assent under Article 111. This is a constitutional formality whose absence could potentially affect the validity of the Act.

## Key cases

**K. S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2019) — Aadhaar Act:** A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Speaker's certificate classifying the Aadhaar Act as a Money Bill by a 4–1 majority. The majority held that Aadhaar's core function — routing welfare benefits from the Consolidated Fund to enrolled beneficiaries — brought it within clauses (1)(a) and (d). Justice D. Y. Chandrachud in dissent held that sections enabling private entities to use the Aadhaar authentication infrastructure were entirely outside the Article 110 definition, that the "only provisions dealing with" requirement was not satisfied, and that the majority's reading of clause (1)(g) was so expansive as to render the rest of the definition meaningless.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019):** A five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously declined to uphold the Finance Act 2017's money bill classification in respect of its tribunal amendments. The Court held that the Finance Act had used the money bill route to amend multiple statutes — the Prevention of Money-laundering Act, the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, the Tribunals Reforms Act, and others — without the relevant provisions having any genuine nexus to Article 110's categories. The Court referred the question of the reviewability of the Speaker's certificate, and the outer limits of clause (1)(g), to a seven-judge Constitution Bench. That reference remains pending.

**S. Ramachandra Rao v. Union of India (2012):** The Supreme Court distinguished between the procedural finality of the Speaker's certificate — which courts will not review as a mere procedural matter under Article 122 — and the substantive constitutional question of whether the conditions for a Money Bill are actually satisfied. Courts, the bench indicated, retain jurisdiction to examine the latter, especially where classification affects legislative competence.

## Current significance

The pending Rojer Mathew seven-judge bench reference is the most important unresolved question in Indian parliamentary procedure. At stake is whether the money bill route — which bypasses Rajya Sabha's co-equal legislative role — can continue to be used to pass wide-ranging policy legislation by inserting it into Finance Bills on the theory that any connection to taxation or appropriation invokes clause (1)(g). If the larger bench upholds broad reviewability of the Speaker's certificate and narrows clause (1)(g), it will require future governments to route non-fiscal legislation through Rajya Sabha rather than bypassing it. If the bench confirms the certificate's unreviewability, the Article 110 definition becomes, in effect, whatever the Speaker of the day chooses to say it is.
