## Why it matters

Article 31C addresses the structural tension between Part III (Fundamental Rights) and Part IV (Directive Principles) by shielding certain welfare legislation from fundamental rights challenge. Its history — insertion, expansion, judicial curtailment, and stabilisation — tracks the most important constitutional controversies of the post-Independence era. The final shape of Article 31C, as determined by the basic structure doctrine, reflects the Court's conclusion that neither Part III nor Part IV can entirely subordinate the other: the Constitution requires a balance between them, and that balance is itself a basic feature.

## Origins and the Twenty-Fifth Amendment

Article 31C was inserted by the Constitution (Twenty-Fifth Amendment) Act, 1971, as part of the Indira Gandhi government's programme of economic nationalisation and redistribution. In its original form it shielded laws giving effect to Articles 39(b) (equitable distribution of material resources of the community) and 39(c) (prevention of concentration of wealth and means of production) from challenge under Articles 14 and 19. It also contained a clause barring courts from examining whether the law actually gave effect to these principles — a judicial-review exclusion that was immediately controversial.

## The Forty-Second Amendment's expansion

The Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976 — enacted during the Emergency — expanded Article 31C dramatically. The amendment changed the scope of protection from laws giving effect to Articles 39(b) and 39(c) to laws giving effect to any principle in Part IV. This would have immunised any law that Parliament declared to be implementing any directive principle from challenge under any fundamental right, effectively enabling Parliament to override Part III under cover of directive principle compliance.

## Key cases

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The Constitution Bench upheld the original Article 31C as inserted by the Twenty-Fifth Amendment but struck down the clause that barred courts from questioning whether a law genuinely gives effect to the stated directive principle. The Court held that if Parliament could declare any law as giving effect to a directive principle and foreclose judicial scrutiny of that declaration, it would create an unlimited mechanism to evade fundamental rights, destroying the basic structure.

**Minerva Mills Ltd v. Union of India (1980)** — A five-judge bench struck down the Forty-Second Amendment's expansion of Article 31C. The Court held that extending the shield to cover all directive principles against all fundamental rights would upset the essential balance between Parts III and IV that is itself a basic feature of the Constitution. Absolute primacy of directive principles over fundamental rights would be just as inconsistent with the basic structure as complete subordination of directive principles. The ruling restored Article 31C to its pre-Forty-Second-Amendment scope, protecting only laws giving effect to Articles 39(b) and 39(c).

**Sanjeev Coke Manufacturing Co v. Bharat Coking Coal Ltd (1983)** — The Supreme Court applied Article 31C to uphold the Coal Mines (Nationalisation) Amendment Act, holding that the legislation genuinely gave effect to Article 39(c)'s directive against concentration of means of production. The ruling confirmed that courts examine whether a sufficient nexus exists between the law and Articles 39(b) or 39(c) — the declaration in the law is not conclusive.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. L. Abu Kavur Bai (1984)** — The Court reiterated that even under Article 31C, courts examine the direct and proximate connection between the law and the directive principle invoked. A mere declaration does not suffice; the substance of the law must implement the stated principle.

## How courts apply Article 31C today

In its current form Article 31C protection operates as follows: a law giving effect to Articles 39(b) or 39(c) cannot be struck down on the ground that it is inconsistent with Article 14 or Article 19. However, the law must genuinely pursue those directive principles — a nexus test that courts apply. The law can still be challenged on other grounds: legislative competence, violation of other fundamental rights such as Article 21, or violation of the basic structure. State laws require presidential assent to attract Article 31C protection.

## Current significance

Article 31C is central to the debate about the relationship between economic redistribution and individual rights. The restriction to Articles 39(b) and (c) means that laws directed at resource reallocation and prevention of wealth concentration can claim the shield, while broader welfare legislation must independently satisfy Articles 14 and 19. Questions about Article 31C's scope have re-emerged in the context of natural resources as community property, state laws on urban land ceilings, and agricultural land redistribution. The Supreme Court's 2024 reference to a constitution bench on the meaning of "material resources of the community" in Article 39(b) — and whether private property can count as such a resource for redistributive purposes — directly implicates the scope of Article 31C protection.
