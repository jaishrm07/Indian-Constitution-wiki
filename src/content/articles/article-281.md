## What this Article does

Article 281 mandates parliamentary transparency for Finance Commission recommendations. Once the Finance Commission submits its report to the President under Article 280, the President must cause the report — together with an explanatory memorandum setting out the action taken or proposed on each recommendation — to be laid before both Houses of Parliament. This is a constitutional obligation: it cannot be deferred or refused.

The article does not make Finance Commission recommendations binding on the Union government. Constitutionally, the recommendations remain advisory. However, the explanatory memorandum requirement is significant: the President (acting on Cabinet advice) must publicly explain what the government has accepted, rejected, or modified, and why. This creates political accountability for departures from Finance Commission awards, even if there is no legal mechanism to compel implementation.

The legal status of Finance Commission recommendations has been a persistent doctrinal question. Courts have consistently held that they are advisory — no writ lies to compel the Union to implement a recommendation — but the combination of constitutional mandation (Article 280) and public accountability (Article 281) gives Finance Commission awards a quasi-constitutional status in practice. No government has ever rejected a core devolution recommendation; departures, where they occur, are at the margin of grants-in-aid or conditional transfer recommendations.

The explanatory memorandum required by Article 281 is a distinctive constitutional device. Most parliamentary accountability mechanisms require the laying of reports before Parliament without any accompanying explanation. Article 281's requirement that the memorandum address "action taken thereon" means the government must engage substantively with every recommendation — acceptance, partial acceptance, rejection, or deferral — rather than simply tabling the Finance Commission report and leaving Parliament to draw its own conclusions. This transparency obligation has become a model for how the Constitution creates accountability around the recommendations of other constitutional bodies, including the National Human Rights Commission and Election Commission reports that are laid before Parliament.

## How courts use it

Courts have not treated Article 281 as creating justiciable rights for States to compel the Union to implement Finance Commission recommendations. The Supreme Court's consistent position is that the laying requirement in Article 281 is a parliamentary accountability obligation directed at the executive branch, not a mechanism that creates enforceable claims by third parties. Once the report and memorandum are laid before Parliament, the constitutional obligation under Article 281 is discharged.

However, courts have invoked Article 281's transparency architecture to assess whether the Union's fiscal behaviour is consistent with the broader constitutional scheme of cooperative federalism. In Centre-State fiscal disputes, the explanatory memorandum laid under Article 281 has been used as documentary evidence of what the Union committed to implement, and departures from those commitments have been subjected to scrutiny — not as enforceable legal obligations but as evidence bearing on the good faith of Union fiscal behaviour.

## Key cases

**Sarkaria Commission Report (1988)** — While the Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations was not a judicial body, its report extensively analysed the gap between Finance Commission recommendations and actual fiscal transfers, recommending that the distinction between the Finance Commission (constitutional body) and the Planning Commission (extra-constitutional body) be maintained and that the Finance Commission's awards be given greater statutory finality. The report shaped subsequent academic and legal discussion on whether Article 281's transparency requirement creates any enforceable obligation.

**State of Kerala v. Union of India (Grants Dispute)** — The Supreme Court, declining to direct the Union to implement specific Finance Commission grants, confirmed that Article 281 creates an obligation of parliamentary transparency, not judicial enforceability. The Court held that once the recommendations are laid before Parliament with an explanatory memorandum, the constitutional obligation is discharged; implementation is a matter of political accountability rather than legal compulsion.

**Tamil Nadu v. Union of India (NITI Aayog and Finance Commission)** — States challenged the Union's use of the NITI Aayog to re-negotiate or supplement Finance Commission awards through conditions attached to centrally sponsored schemes. The Court's observations (while not disposing of the challenge on merits) distinguished between the Finance Commission's constitutionally mandated role under Articles 280-281 and the executive's discretionary grants power under Article 282, suggesting that the two cannot be used interchangeably to undercut Finance Commission awards.

## Current significance

The Fifteenth Finance Commission's recommendations (2021-26), covering the GST transition period and COVID-19 fiscal pressures, generated significant political controversy. Several States argued that the Union's failure to implement grants recommendations in full, and its use of cess collections (which reduce the divisible pool) without fully explaining the impact in the Article 281 memorandum, amounted to a constitutional violation. The GST compensation payment disputes (2020-22), where the Union delayed payment of the constitutionally agreed compensation to States, also invoked Article 281 in arguments about accountability for Finance Commission-related commitments. The Sixteenth Finance Commission (constituted in 2023 for the 2026-31 award period) is expected to make recommendations on the divisible pool impact of cesses and the post-GST fiscal federalism framework, renewing the relevance of Article 281's transparency and accountability architecture.

The Article 281 memorandum has also become an important document in parliamentary debates about fiscal federalism: it is tabled alongside the Finance Commission report and provides the only official government-authored explanation of why particular recommendations were accepted, modified, or not implemented. Scholars of comparative federalism have noted that India's constitutional requirement for this accompanying memorandum is more demanding than equivalent provisions in many other federal constitutions, where executive non-implementation of fiscal commission recommendations is legally unreviewable and need not be publicly explained.

Read together, Articles 280 and 281 create a constitutional cycle of fiscal accountability: periodic constitution of the Finance Commission (Article 280), submission of recommendations to the President, and public tabling with an explanatory memorandum (Article 281). The effectiveness of this cycle as a check on fiscal centralisation depends on the seriousness with which the Union engages with the explanatory memorandum obligation — a question that parliamentary committees on Finance and Public Accounts regularly examine.
