  378. Provisions as to Public Service Commissions.—(1) The members of the Public Service Commission for the Dominion of India holding office immediately before the commencement of this Constitution shall, unless they have elected otherwise, become on such commencement the members of the Public Service Commission for the Union and shall, notwithstanding anything in clauses (1) and (2) of article 316 but subject to the proviso to clause (2) of that article, continue to hold office until the expiration of their term of office as determined under the rules which were applicable immediately before such commencement to such members.

  (2) The Members of a Public Service Commission of a Province or of a Public Service Commission serving the needs of a group of Provinces holding office immediately before the commencement of this Constitution shall, unless they have elected otherwise, become on such commencement the members of the Public Service Commission for the corresponding State or the members of the Joint State Public Service Commission serving the needs of the corresponding States, as the case may be, and shall, notwithstanding anything in clauses (1) and (2) of article 316 but subject to the proviso to clause (2) of that article, continue to hold office until the expiration of their term of office as determined under the rules which were applicable immediately before such commencement to such members.

## What this Article did

Article 378 is the transitional provision for members of Public Service Commissions. It addressed the continuation in office of members of two types of pre-constitutional Public Service Commissions when the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950.

Clause (1) dealt with the Public Service Commission for the Dominion of India. Members holding office immediately before commencement automatically became members of the Union Public Service Commission (UPSC), the corresponding constitutional body established under Articles 315 to 323. They continued in office until the expiration of their terms as determined by the pre-constitutional rules, notwithstanding the new eligibility and tenure rules in Article 316.

Clause (2) dealt with members of provincial-level or group-of-provinces Public Service Commissions. They became members of the corresponding State Public Service Commission or Joint State Public Service Commission and similarly continued until the end of their original terms.

In both cases, the article preserved the existing terms of appointment (including salary and tenure) rather than requiring members to be reappointed under the new constitutional procedures specified in Article 316. This protected incumbents' legitimate expectations while avoiding an administrative hiatus in the staffing bodies that were essential for government recruitment.

## Historical context

Public Service Commissions were among the most important institutions of the Government of India Act, 1935 framework. They advised on recruitment and personnel matters for the civil services. The Federal Public Service Commission (for the Dominion) and the provincial-level bodies had developed independent, professional cultures.

The Constitution, by establishing the UPSC and State PSCs under Part XIV (Articles 315-323), created constitutionally protected independent bodies for public recruitment, with tenure security, guaranteed salaries, and removal only by a formal presidential order after an inquiry. Article 378 ensured that the transition to these new constitutionally protected bodies was seamless — the same persons continued, but now with enhanced constitutional protection.

The article also reflects a key concern of the Constituent Assembly: that the civil services, which were essential for the functioning of the new state, should not be disrupted during the transition. Continuity of the recommending and recruiting bodies was part of that broader continuity strategy.

## Key cases

No major constitutional case specifically litigated the operation of Article 378. The transition of PSC members was primarily an administrative matter resolved by presidential orders and the operation of existing service rules. The provision achieved its purpose without generating significant judicial controversy.

Questions about the tenure and removal of PSC members more generally were addressed in later cases under Articles 316 and 317, but Article 378's transitional role had long since been exhausted by the time those cases arose.

## Current significance

Article 378 is spent. All members whose tenure it governed have long since retired. The provision's significance is historical: it illustrates how the Constitution managed the transition of key recruitment and advisory institutions while protecting the rights of incumbent members. The UPSC and State PSCs, which continue to play a central role in public service recruitment in India, trace their institutional continuity in part to the seamless transition effected by Article 378. The provision is part of a cluster of transitional articles (375 to 378A) that collectively ensured that government institutions continued operating without break across the divide between the dominion era and the republic.
