## Why it matters

A modern government is also a commercial actor — it buys goods and services through procurement, builds and leases infrastructure, sells public assets, and carries on trading operations in strategic sectors. Article 298 gives this commercial activity a constitutional foundation, establishing that governments do not need a specific law authorising each individual trade or contract. This matters because without such a provision, every government purchase, every tender, every public sector enterprise would be challenged as an excess of executive power in the absence of express legislative authority. The provisos ensure that this broad commercial power does not override the legislative division of powers — if a government acts commercially in the other government's legislative sphere, that other government's legislation still governs the activity.

## Reading the provision

Article 298, substituted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956, confers on both the Union and every State government the executive power to engage in trade or business, to acquire, hold, and dispose of property, and to enter into contracts — all for any purpose. This is a broad enabling provision: it means the government can establish trading enterprises, run commercial undertakings, buy and sell goods and services, and conclude agreements with private parties without needing specific legislative authority for each transaction. The two provisos introduce a cross-legislative condition: where a State carries on business or enters contracts in an area where Parliament may make laws (Union or Concurrent List), Parliament's legislation can override or regulate the State's executive action, and vice versa where the Union acts in a sphere within State legislative competence.

## Relationship with Article 73

Article 298 extends executive power in the commercial sphere beyond the scope normally defined by the legislative Lists in Article 246. Article 73 defines the Union's general executive power as coextensive with Parliament's legislative power — meaning the Centre can ordinarily act executively only in areas where Parliament can legislate. Article 298's effect is to supplement Article 73 by giving both governments a separate, broader commercial power that is not confined by the legislative Lists: a State can, under Article 298, carry on any trade or business regardless of which List it falls under, subject only to the overriding power of Parliament if the activity touches a Union or Concurrent subject.

The provisos are the limiting mechanism. They prevent Article 298 from becoming a device by which governments override legislative boundaries entirely: if the Union engages in a business that is within State legislative competence, State legislation in that area can still regulate the Union's commercial activity. The net effect is that Article 298 expands executive commercial latitude but preserves legislative supremacy to set the rules for commercial activity within each government's own sphere.

## Government-as-trader and the constitutional framework

Article 298 is the constitutional basis for the entire structure of public sector enterprises, government trading corporations, and State commercial undertakings. The Food Corporation of India, Coal India, ONGC, State electricity boards, State road transport corporations, and hundreds of other government enterprises derive their operational authority ultimately from Article 298 (in conjunction with specific parliamentary or State legislation that creates the statutory body). Where such enterprises are incorporated as companies under the Companies Act, they operate under dual authority — the statutory company law framework and the underlying constitutional power in Article 298 that authorises the government to hold shares and engage in commercial activity.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — The Supreme Court, while addressing emergency powers, discussed the scope of the Union's executive commercial powers under Article 298 and Article 73, noting that the government's power to engage in economic activities does not require specific legislative authorisation for each act.

**Praga Tools Corporation v. C.A. Imanual (1969)** — The Court examined the constitutional basis for government-owned companies engaging in commercial activity, affirming that Article 298 provides the constitutional underpinning for the Union's participation in industry and commerce through companies in which it holds equity.

**State Trading Corporation of India v. Commercial Tax Officer (1963)** — A Constitution Bench considered whether a government trading corporation fell within the definition of the "State" for the purpose of Part III rights. The Court's reasoning about the nature of the STC as a vehicle for the Union's Article 298 trading power shed light on the relationship between government commercial activity and constitutional rights obligations.

**New Delhi Municipal Council v. State of Punjab (1997)** — The Court's analysis of the scope of the Union's executive powers in the Delhi context touched on the interaction between Article 298's commercial power and the territorial jurisdiction of State and municipal legislation, with implications for which government's law governs government-as-trader activities in a Union territory.

## Current significance

Article 298 underpins the entire architecture of government commercial activity and public procurement in India. As the Union and State governments pursue privatisation, asset monetisation, and public-private partnerships, Article 298 provides the constitutional authority for governments to enter into complex commercial arrangements — leasing infrastructure, establishing joint ventures, selling government assets, and contracting for the provision of public services. The article's interaction with Article 299 is particularly important: while Article 298 authorises the governments to make contracts, Article 299 specifies the constitutional form those contracts must take (expressed in the President's or Governor's name), with non-compliance potentially voiding the contract. The growth of government e-procurement platforms and standard bidding documents has focused attention on whether Article 299's formal requirements are satisfied by digital contracting processes — a question with significant practical implications for public procurement law.
