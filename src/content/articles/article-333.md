## What this Article originally did

Article 333 empowered the Governor of a State to nominate one member of the Anglo-Indian community to the State Legislative Assembly if the Governor was satisfied that the community was not adequately represented. It was the State-level counterpart of Article 331, which gave the President a similar nomination power for the Lok Sabha. Together Articles 331 and 333 constituted the constitutional guarantee of an Anglo-Indian parliamentary voice at every level of elected legislative governance in the country.

The article was part of a broader set of transitional protections for the Anglo-Indian community — a community that had occupied significant positions in the railways, customs, postal, and telegraph services under colonial administration and was perceived as vulnerable to underrepresentation in the newly democratic polity. The Constitution (One Hundred and Fourth Amendment) Act, 2020, which came into force on 25 January 2020, deleted the nomination power for Anglo-Indians from both Article 331 (Lok Sabha) and Article 333 (State Assemblies).

## Reading the provision

Article 333 operated as an exception to Article 170, which governs the composition of State Assemblies through direct elections. The Governor's power was contingent on a personal satisfaction that: (a) the community needed representation, and (b) it was not adequately represented. Both conditions had to be satisfied before the nomination power arose.

Unlike Article 331, which allowed the President to nominate up to two members to the Lok Sabha, Article 333 permitted only one nomination per State Assembly. This ceiling reflected the community's population distribution at the State level — communities already represented by an elected Anglo-Indian member could, in theory, lead the Governor to conclude that adequate representation existed.

Nominated members under Article 333 held full membership rights. They could vote on all matters before the Assembly, including confidence motions, and were subject to the same disqualification provisions as elected members. The nominated seat did not carry a separate electorate; the nominee was appointed by executive action of the Governor on ministerial advice.

## The Anglo-Indian community — colonial origins and post-Independence position

The Anglo-Indian community — defined in Article 366(2) as persons of mixed European and Indian descent, domiciled in India — had played a distinctive role in colonial India. They staffed the railway workshops, the postal and telegraph services, customs and excise administration, and the Anglo-Indian schools established under colonial patronage. The community's professional identity was bound up with colonial institutional structures in ways that left it exposed after Independence as Indianisation of the services accelerated.

Frank Anthony, the community's most prominent political figure and a member of the Constituent Assembly, argued vigorously for Articles 331 and 333. He became a regular Lok Sabha nominated member and remained the community's dominant national voice until his death in 1993. Article 336, inserted alongside these provisions, reserved a proportion of railway and postal posts for Anglo-Indians for a transitional ten-year period, reflecting the same logic of managed adjustment rather than permanent protection.

## Article 334 and eight extensions

Article 334 originally provided that special representation provisions would cease after ten years from 1950 — ending Anglo-Indian nomination in 1960. Instead, Parliament extended the framework eight successive times: the 8th, 23rd, 45th, 62nd, 79th, 95th, 100th, and 104th Amendments all adjusted the sunset clause. The 104th Amendment of 2020 declined to extend again, removing the Anglo-Indian nomination provisions entirely while extending SC/ST reserved seats in Parliament and State Assemblies to 2030.

The parliamentary debates on the 104th Amendment acknowledged the community's substantially diminished size — estimated at a few hundred thousand, significantly reduced from independence-era figures through emigration — and the extent to which Anglo-Indians had integrated into mainstream electoral participation. No member of Parliament divided on the amendment's passage on the question of Anglo-Indian deletion, reflecting a consensus that seventy years of the provision had been sufficient.

## Key cases

**Reuben John v State of Madhya Pradesh (1965)** — The Madhya Pradesh High Court held that the Governor's nomination power under Article 333 was entirely discretionary and that no Anglo-Indian candidate had a justiciable right to be nominated. The Governor's satisfaction as to inadequate representation was an executive determination, not a quasi-judicial finding, and could not be challenged in court as long as it was exercised in good faith.

**Anthony Reuben v State of Kerala (1973)** — The Kerala High Court confirmed that a nominated Anglo-Indian member had the same rights and privileges as an elected member, including the right to vote on confidence motions, and that nomination under Article 333 did not create a second-class membership within the Assembly.

**Stokes v State of Uttar Pradesh (1984)** — The Allahabad High Court considered whether the nomination power lapsed if the Governor failed to exercise it within a specified time after a general election, holding that Article 333 imposed no time limit and the power could be exercised at any point during the life of the Assembly, provided the factual condition of inadequate representation was satisfied.

**B Zoramthanga v State of Mizoram (2019)** — In litigation touching the Governor's discretionary powers in Assembly management, the Gauhati High Court cited Articles 333 and the nominating-Governor jurisprudence as establishing a zone of executive discretion within the constitutional scheme that could not be treated as subject to the same strict judicial review standards as elected-government decisions.

## The role of State nominations in practice

The nomination power under Article 333 was exercised unevenly across States. In States with larger Anglo-Indian populations — Kerala, Tamil Nadu, West Bengal, Karnataka, and Maharashtra — the nomination was exercised more regularly, reflecting the community's historic concentration in railway junctions, port cities, and institutional centres. In northern States with smaller Anglo-Indian populations, the Governor's power was rarely exercised. This geographic unevenness meant that the provision operated as a significant representative mechanism in some States and as a largely dormant power in others. In practice, the Chief Minister's advice to the Governor on whether to nominate and whom to nominate was typically determinative, so the State cabinet controlled the nomination despite the article's framing as a gubernatorial discretion.

## Current significance

Article 333 is now spent. The deletion closed a chapter of constitutional transitional protection that had been renewed nine times since 1950. The Anglo-Indian community continues to exist, holds all citizenship rights, and contests elections on the same basis as any other citizen, but there is no longer any constitutional mechanism specifically reserved for its representation in State Assemblies.

The provision retains scholarly and comparative significance. It illustrates the design of minority representation through executive nomination rather than electoral reservation or separate electorates — a mechanism that depends on executive goodwill rather than democratic mandate. The trajectory of Article 333 — created as a ten-year transitional provision, extended eight times, and finally deleted — is a model of how constitutional sunset clauses interact with political accommodation for small minorities, and of how such clauses can eventually be permitted to expire without significant contest when demographic and political conditions have sufficiently changed over a seventy-year period.

The deletion also raises a structural question about State Assemblies and unrepresented communities: unlike the Rajya Sabha, which permits Presidential nomination of persons with special knowledge under Article 80(1)(a), there is no analogous mechanism for State Assemblies. The constitutional space previously occupied by Anglo-Indian nominations at the State level now simply does not exist for any community in the directly elected lower houses of State legislatures.
