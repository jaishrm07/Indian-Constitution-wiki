  163. Council of Ministers to aid and advise Governor.—(1) There shall be a Council of Ministers with the Chief Minister at the head to aid and advise the Governor in the exercise of his functions, except in so far as he is by or under this Constitution required to exercise his functions or any of them in his discretion.

  (2) If any question arises whether any matter is or is not a matter as respects which the Governor is by or under this Constitution required to act in his discretion, the decision of the Governor in his discretion shall be final, and the validity of anything done by the Governor shall not be called in question on the ground that he ought or ought not to have acted in his discretion.

## Why it matters

India's constitutional design for the States borrowed the Westminster parliamentary model at the provincial level. In that model, an elected legislature, a Chief Minister commanding majority support, and a Cabinet collectively responsible to the legislature constitute the real government. The Governor, appointed by the President, is the constitutional head — formally empowered by several articles, but expected to exercise most of those formal powers on the advice of the elected executive. Article 163 is the provision that encodes this expectation: the Council of Ministers aids and advises the Governor, and the Governor ordinarily follows that advice.

The constitutional stakes are high because the provision contains an exception that has been the source of persistent controversy. When the Constitution requires the Governor to act in discretion, ministerial advice is not binding. The framers identified a narrow set of circumstances — principally situations where no clear majority exists and a government must be formed or tested, where the legislature must be dissolved, or where reserved legislation is involved — where the Governor must exercise independent judgment. But the boundary between legitimate discretion and illegitimate partisan interference has been contested at every stage of Indian constitutional history.

Article 163 matters acutely because its abuse produces a direct democratic injury. A Governor who converts discretionary powers into tools of partisan obstruction effectively holds an elected government hostage — refusing to summon sessions, stalling legislation, sending adverse reports to the Centre, or giving advantages to opposition parties during confidence votes. The constitutional guarantee of representative government at the State level depends on Article 163 being read to confine rather than expand gubernatorial discretion.

The provision also contains the two most controversial procedural clauses in the Governor's constitutional regime. Clause (2)'s finality clause appears to make the Governor's assessment of what falls within discretion unreviewable. Clause (3) bars courts from inquiring into ministerial advice. These two provisions, read together, created a near-total immunity for gubernatorial action for the first decades after the Constitution — an immunity that the Supreme Court has progressively unwound by distinguishing justiciability of the discretion claim from justiciability of the act itself.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the general rule and the exception**: The default is ministerial advice. Governors exercise their functions on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. The exception — "except in so far as he is by or under this Constitution required to exercise his functions... in his discretion" — does not itself specify what those discretionary functions are. That specification is scattered across other articles: appointing a Chief Minister when no majority is clear (Article 164), recommending President's Rule to the Centre (Article 356), reserving legislation for Presidential assent (Articles 200–201), and a handful of other provisions. The discretion is therefore not a general residue but a set of specific identified exceptions.

**Clause (2) — the finality clause**: This clause provides that if a question arises about whether a matter falls within the Governor's discretion, the Governor's determination is final. Courts cannot invalidate an act done by the Governor on the ground that the Governor should or should not have acted in discretion. The purpose was to prevent litigants from challenging every gubernatorial act by claiming the wrong standard was applied. But the Supreme Court has interpreted this clause narrowly: it protects the Governor's characterisation of the nature of the power, but does not immunise the exercise of that power from review for constitutional validity. A Governor who claims to exercise discretion but actually acts in bad faith, or who manufactures a constitutional crisis to justify invoking discretion, cannot shelter behind clause (2) to prevent the act itself from being examined.

**Clause (3) — the cabinet secrecy bar**: Courts cannot inquire into whether ministerial advice was tendered or what that advice was. This mirrors the cabinet secrecy principle in Westminster systems — collective ministerial responsibility depends partly on the privacy of Cabinet deliberations. The clause prevents a Governor from later claiming that no advice was tendered (which would have implied discretion was available) or using the content of advice to justify independent action. It also prevents litigants from compelling Governors or Ministers to disclose the content of inter-executive deliberations. The bar is procedural and evidentiary, not substantive — it cannot be used to validate unconstitutional gubernatorial conduct.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debate over the Governor's role was extensive and sometimes bitter. Several members argued that the Governor should be directly elected and given independent authority as the State's chief executive. Dr. Ambedkar and Jawaharlal Nehru resisted this, arguing that direct election of the Governor would create a competing democratic mandate at the State level and destabilise the parliamentary government of the State. The decision to have an appointed Governor acting on ministerial advice replicated the colonial Governor-General structure but with democratic accountability at the Cabinet level.

The discretion clause was included at the insistence of framers who were concerned about States that might experience genuine constitutional breakdown — where no stable majority existed or where the elected government itself was violating the Constitution. The intention was that the discretion would be narrow and genuinely exceptional. Dr. Ambedkar specifically assured the Assembly that the Governor would not be a political agent of the Centre but a constitutional functionary. The subsequent history has complicated that assurance substantially.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held that the President and the Governor are constitutional heads who must act on the aid and advice of their respective Councils of Ministers in nearly all matters. The Court read Article 163 as confirming the Westminster parliamentary convention — the Governor's independent discretion is narrow and confined to specific constitutional provisions. The Court held that Governors who act against ministerial advice in non-discretionary matters violate the Constitution. This judgment established the foundational principle that pervades all subsequent Article 163 jurisprudence.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — A five-judge bench held that the Governor's power to summon, prorogue, and dissolve the Assembly is exercisable only on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers headed by a Chief Minister who commands majority support, unless one of the narrow constitutionally-specified discretions applies. The Court struck down the Governor's unilateral decision to advance the Assembly session date — an act that precipitated a constitutional crisis — and held that the consequent President's Rule proclamation was unconstitutional. Critically, the Court held that Article 163(2)'s finality clause does not immunise patently unconstitutional gubernatorial conduct from review. The Governor cannot claim that any exercise of power is "discretionary" merely by labelling it so.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court invalidated the imposition of President's Rule in Bihar after the 2005 election, holding that the Governor's report that no post-election government could be formed was itself based on constitutionally impermissible conduct. The Governor had acted on intelligence reports about potential horse-trading rather than directing a floor test. The judgment reinforced that a Governor cannot subvert the democratic mandate by refusing to give an elected majority a fair opportunity to demonstrate support on the floor of the House.

**Madhya Pradesh Special Police Establishment v State of Madhya Pradesh (2004)** — The Court held that where the Council of Ministers has a personal interest in a prosecution sanction decision, the Governor may legitimately act in discretion and refuse to follow advice that is tainted by conflict of interest. This case defines the outer limit of the ministerial advice rule: genuine constitutional discretion exists and can be exercised, but only where the specific constitutional conditions for discretion — including an identifiable constitutional interest the Governor is protecting — are present. It cannot be extrapolated into a general reserve power.

**State of Punjab v Principal Secretary to the Governor (2023)** — The Supreme Court held that the Governor of Punjab had unconstitutionally withheld consent to the calling of a special session of the State Assembly and had exceeded constitutional limits by unilaterally fixing the date of the budget session. The Court ruled that the calling of a session is exercisable on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers, and that the Governor's attempts to delay or direct the timing of sessions without Cabinet advice were unconstitutional. The judgment further narrowed the scope of permissible gubernatorial discretion in relation to legislative sessions.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The five-judge bench held that the Governor of Maharashtra had acted improperly in directing Uddhav Thackeray to prove his majority on the floor of the House at a time when there was no credible material before the Governor that the government had lost majority. A floor test can only be directed when the Governor has objective material — not simply a demand by opposition politicians — to conclude that the existing government has lost majority. Acting on a defection-driven letter from rebel MLAs does not itself constitute such material.

## Current significance

Article 163 is the constitutional provision at the centre of the most active cluster of federal disputes in contemporary India. The years since 2020 have seen an unprecedented series of Supreme Court interventions in Governor-State disputes — in Arunachal Pradesh, Manipur, Maharashtra, Bihar, Punjab, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and Telangana. The pattern in each State has been variations on a theme: an elected government complaining that the Governor is acting as a partisan actor of the Centre rather than as a constitutional functionary.

The specific flashpoints in Article 163 litigation have included Governors refusing to convene sessions on Cabinet advice, fixing session dates unilaterally, declining to give floor tests, sending adverse reports to the Centre about governments that have not lost majority, withholding assent to Bills passed by State Assemblies, and removing items from the Governor's address without Cabinet approval. In each of these cases, the question before the Court has been whether the Governor was acting within the discretion the Constitution allows or was converting a ceremonial constitutional role into a political instrument.

The Court's post-Nabam Rebia and post-Subhash Desai jurisprudence has consistently narrowed the Governor's discretion zone while stopping short of making gubernatorial conduct fully justiciable in every dimension. The tension between clause (2)'s finality principle and the basic structure requirement that democratic government of the States be preserved has not been fully resolved. What is settled is that Article 163 does not permit a Governor to act as an agent of the Central Government in displacing a State government that commands majority support in the Assembly.

The Sarkaria Commission (1988) and the Punchhi Commission (2010) both recommended that the Governor's discretionary powers be strictly circumscribed by legislation or constitutional amendment and that the relationship between the Governor and the elected State government be governed by clear constitutional norms. Neither set of recommendations has been legislated, leaving Article 163 to be developed entirely through judicial interpretation — a process that continues to generate litigation with each change of government at the Centre or in States governed by different parties.
