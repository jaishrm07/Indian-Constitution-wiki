## What this Article does

Article 394 is the commencement provision of the Constitution. It serves two functions: first, it specifies which articles came into force immediately upon the Constitution's adoption (before the formal date of 26 January 1950); and second, it establishes 26 January 1950 as the official commencement date of the Constitution.

The articles listed as coming into force "at once" — Articles 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 60, 324, 366, 367, 379, 380, 388, 391, 392, and 393 — were those dealing with citizenship, the oath of the President, the Election Commission, definitions, and the transitional provisional Parliament and presidential arrangements. These needed to be operative from the moment of the Constituent Assembly's adoption of the Constitution (26 November 1949) to allow the transitional arrangements to be put in place before the full Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950.

All remaining provisions came into force on 26 January 1950.

## Why it matters

The two-stage commencement structure in Article 394 — immediate commencement of certain articles, full commencement on 26 January 1950 — was not merely a technical drafting device. It reflected the need to manage a complex constitutional transition in which different elements of the new framework needed to be in place at different times. The citizenship articles needed to be operative from adoption so that the Republic's citizens could be identified before commencement. The transitional Parliament and executive provisions needed to be operative so that those institutions could be constituted before 26 January. The Election Commission provision (Article 324) needed to be operative to allow preparations for the first elections to begin. Article 394's two-stage structure made all of this possible.

## Historical context

The Constituent Assembly adopted the Constitution of India on 26 November 1949. But the formal commencement date was set as 26 January 1950, chosen to coincide with the anniversary of the Purna Swaraj (Complete Independence) declaration made by the Indian National Congress on 26 January 1930. This date gave the Republic of India's commencement a resonance with the history of the independence movement.

The gap between 26 November 1949 (adoption) and 26 January 1950 (commencement) was used to put in place the transitional arrangements provided for in Part XXI. The articles that came into force immediately at adoption — particularly those about citizenship (Articles 5-9) and the transitional Parliament and executive (Articles 379, 380, 388, 391, 392) — needed to operate during this two-month period so that the Republic was fully constituted from day one.

Article 394 is thus the provision that establishes the foundational constitutional fact: the Constitution of India came into force at the commencement of 26 January 1950. All references in the Constitution to "the commencement of this Constitution" refer to that date.

## Key cases

The commencement date of 26 January 1950 is referenced in countless constitutional cases. Questions about whether particular constitutional provisions, rights, or obligations applied to events before or after commencement have frequently required courts to address the significance of this date.

**State of Bihar v Maharajadhiraja Sir Kameshwar Singh AIR 1952 SC 252** — The Supreme Court considered whether fundamental rights under the new Constitution protected pre-commencement property rights, requiring analysis of when and how the Constitution came into force under Article 394.

## Current significance

Article 394 is an operative constitutional provision in the sense that the commencement date it establishes — 26 January 1950 — continues to be the reference point for every provision of the Constitution that uses the phrase "the commencement of this Constitution." Republic Day, celebrated on 26 January each year, marks the date established by Article 394 as the day the Constitution came into force and India became a Republic.

The distinction Article 394 draws between provisions that came into force immediately upon adoption (26 November 1949) and those that came into force on 26 January 1950 is also of historical legal significance. During the intervening two months, the articles that had come into force immediately — particularly the citizenship articles (Articles 5-9) — were operative, and persons who claimed citizenship rights on 26 January 1950 had to establish their status under those already-operative provisions. This created a technical body of law about pre-commencement constitutional rights that courts had occasion to address in early citizenship and property cases.
