  18. Abolition of titles.—(1) No title, not being a military or academic distinction, shall be conferred by the State.

  (2) No citizen of India shall accept any title from any foreign State.

  (3) No person who is not a citizen of India shall, while he holds any office of profit or trust under the State, accept without the consent of the President any title from any foreign State.

  (4) No person holding any office of profit or trust under the State shall, without the consent of the President, accept any present, emolument, or office of any kind from or under any foreign State.

## Why it matters

Article 18 reflects the founders' republican commitment to equality of status. The abolition of titles was a deliberate repudiation of the colonial and princely systems that used honours, titles, and foreign emoluments to create a stratified social order whose loyalties could be purchased or directed by external powers. By barring the state from conferring titles, the Constitution sought to eliminate a mechanism of social distinction that sat uneasily with the equality guarantees in Articles 14 to 17.

## Reading the provision

Article 18 operates in four clauses. Clause (1) bars the state from conferring any title except military and academic distinctions — ranks in the armed forces and university degrees fall outside the ban. Clause (2) bars all Indian citizens from accepting any foreign title. Clauses (3) and (4) address persons holding public office: non-citizens in public roles cannot accept foreign titles without presidential consent, and no office-holder may accept a foreign present, emolument, or office without the same consent. The ban covers all forms of foreign-state-derived benefit that could create divided loyalty or dependence.

## The Bharat Ratna debate

The principal constitutional controversy around Article 18 concerns civilian honours such as Bharat Ratna, Padma Vibhushan, Padma Bhushan, and Padma Shri. These awards are not expressly mentioned in Article 18 and were introduced by government order rather than legislation. Critics argued that they effectively constitute titles conferred by the state in violation of clause (1). The government's position, consistently maintained, is that these are decorations — recognitions of merit — rather than titles conveying hereditary or social status.

## Key cases

**Balaji Raghavan v. Union of India (1996)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench considered a direct challenge to the Bharat Ratna and Padma awards. The Court upheld the awards but by a split reasoning: a majority held that they are not "titles" within Article 18(1) because they confer no hereditary right, no special legal status, and no economic privilege. The Court drew the distinction between a title that elevates a person above others in formal social standing and a mere recognition or commendation. However, the Court also imposed a restriction: the awards cannot be used as suffixes or prefixes to the recipient's name, because if used in that fashion they would begin to function as titles and fall foul of Article 18.

**Re: Election Symbols Case — T.N. Seshan v. Union of India (1995)** — While not directly about Article 18, the Court's broader observations about formal equality and republican values informed the constitutional framework within which civilian honours operate. Subsequent decisions have cited Article 18 as a marker of the Constitution's commitment to de-feudalisation.

## Scope of exceptions

Military distinctions such as Param Vir Chakra, Vir Chakra, and Maha Vir Chakra and academic designations such as university degrees and professional licences are explicitly excluded from Article 18(1). The rationale is that military distinctions recognise service performed in defence of the state and do not create a hereditary social hierarchy, while academic designations convey verified professional competence rather than birth-derived privilege. The boundary between a permissible decoration and a prohibited title remains contested at the margins.

## How courts use it

Article 18 is invoked relatively rarely in litigation compared to other Part III guarantees, but it functions as a constitutional backdrop against which statutory schemes granting differential access based on former princely status or colonial honours are assessed. Courts have used the republican value embedded in Article 18 to reinforce anti-discrimination readings of Articles 14 and 15 when assessing statutes that perpetuate distinctions derived from colonial-era designations.

## Current significance

The Balaji Raghavan restrictions on how awardees may display their honours are routinely ignored in practice, with Bharat Ratna recipients commonly identified by that designation in official contexts. No enforcement mechanism exists for clause (1) violations since the prohibition is addressed to the state rather than creating a private cause of action. The foreign emoluments clauses in Article 18(3) and (4) have acquired renewed relevance in debates about conflicts of interest for public officials who accept international prizes, fellowships, or positions of advisory authority from foreign entities.
