  [371C. Special provision with respect to the State of Manipur.—(1)

  Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, the President may, by order made with respect to the State of Manipur, provide for the constitution and functions of a committee of the Legislative Assembly of the State consisting of members of that Assembly elected from the Hill Areas of that State, for the modifications to be made in the rules of business of the Government and in the rules of procedure of the Legislative Assembly of the State and for any special responsibility of the Governor in order to secure the proper functioning of such committee.

  (2) The Governor shall annually, or whenever so required by the President, make a report to the President regarding the administration of the Hill Areas in the State of Manipur and the executive power of the Union shall extend to the giving of directions to the State as to the administration of the said areas.

  Explanation.—In this article, the expression "Hill Areas" means such areas as the President may, by order, declare to be Hill areas.]

## Why it matters

Manipur's constitutional history is defined by a sharp geographical and demographic divide between its hilly periphery and the Imphal valley plains. The hill areas, covering roughly ninety percent of the State's land area but inhabited by a minority of its population, are home predominantly to Naga and Kuki-Zo tribal communities. The valley, though smaller in area, contains the majority of the State's population and has historically dominated State politics. When Manipur became a full State in 1972 under the Twenty-seventh Amendment, hill communities needed constitutional assurance that their interests would not simply be submerged by the numerical majority of the valley. Article 371C was that assurance.

The article does more than create a legislative committee. Clause (2) imposes a mandatory annual reporting obligation on the Governor directly to the President, and extends Union executive power to issue directions on Hill Area administration to the State. This makes Article 371C the only provision in the 371-series that creates a standing federal supervisory authority over a defined sub-State region that operates routinely rather than only in an emergency. The usual federal principle is that the Union does not supervise State administration except through specific constitutional mechanisms — primarily Article 356 — but in Manipur's Hill Areas, Union oversight is constitutionally built into the State's normal functioning.

The significance of Article 371C extends beyond Manipur to the general question of how federal constitutions manage intra-state diversity. The hill-valley divide predates the Constitution. Under British colonial administration, the Manipur hill tracts were governed separately under a Regulation regime giving hill communities a degree of administrative autonomy. The Manipur State Merger Agreement of 1949 brought the territory into the Indian Union, but the administrative distinction between valley and hills persisted. Article 371C formalised that distinction within the constitutional order of a full State. Whether a constitutional committee and an annual report can substitute for genuine political equity between communities in a deeply divided State is a question Manipur's experience has answered only partially and painfully.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the enabling power:** The President may make an order for Manipur providing for three things: the constitution and functions of a committee of the Legislative Assembly consisting of members elected from the Hill Areas; modifications to the rules of business of the Government and the rules of procedure of the Legislative Assembly to facilitate the committee; and a special responsibility of the Governor to secure the committee's proper functioning. All three elements are held together — without the procedural modifications the committee cannot function, and without the Governor's special responsibility there is no constitutional enforcement mechanism.

The "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" opening gives the President's order authority to override ordinary constitutional provisions on Governor-Cabinet relations and Assembly procedure. This is essential because the special responsibility involves the Governor acting to secure the committee's functioning, which may in some circumstances require independent judgment distinct from purely following Cabinet advice under Article 163.

The phrase "special responsibility of the Governor" is deliberately open-textured. The Constitution uses the term in the 371-series provisions but does not define it or specify remedies for its breach. Unlike a constitutional duty backed by an enforceable legal remedy, the Governor's "special responsibility" is more in the nature of a constitutional expectation than a directly justiciable obligation. Effective enforcement runs through the Governor's reporting obligation to the President under clause (2) and through political accountability.

**Clause (2) — the reporting and supervisory mechanism:** The Governor must report annually to the President — or whenever the President requires — on the administration of the Hill Areas. The Union's executive power extends to giving directions to the State on Hill Area administration. This is constitutionally exceptional — a standing directive power over State governance in a defined geographical area, exercisable without declaring any emergency. It reflects the founding judgment that the hill-valley divide in Manipur was a problem requiring not just a State-level solution but ongoing Union engagement.

The Explanation defines "Hill Areas" as whatever areas the President declares to be Hill Areas by order. Currently the Hill Areas comprise the ten hill districts of Manipur. The flexibility of presidential definition allows the Hill Areas designation to be updated if administrative boundaries change.

The Manipur Legislative Assembly (Hill Areas Committee) Order, 1972 operationalised clause (1). It specified the composition of the committee (all MLAs elected from Hill Areas constituencies), the Bills and budget matters that must be mandatorily referred to it, and the procedure for the committee's work.

## The Twenty-seventh Amendment context

Manipur did not participate in the original Constituent Assembly as an independent State — it was a princely State that merged with India in 1949 and became a Union Territory in 1956. When it became a full State in 1972, the constitutional arrangements for hill communities had to be designed retrospectively. Parliamentary debates on the Twenty-seventh Amendment record sustained engagement by Manipur hill-area representatives who insisted that a committee mechanism backed by Governor oversight was essential to prevent the valley political majority from legislating on hill land, forests, and customary governance without adequate consultation. The Union government accepted this demand, giving Article 371C a more robust structure than the analogous Article 371B for Assam — in particular the addition of the mandatory annual reporting obligation and the Union's directive power in clause (2).

The Manipur Hill Areas Act, which governs land alienation and regulation in the hill districts, operates within the constitutional framework Article 371C creates. The Act's land protection provisions prevent transfer of tribal land to non-tribals, reflecting the same protective logic that animates the customary law shields in Articles 371A and 371G. The Hill Areas Committee is specifically empowered to scrutinise Bills affecting hill land before they reach the full Assembly.

## Key cases

**The 1972 Presidential Orders and the constitution of the Hill Areas Committee** — Article 371C became operative through the Manipur Legislative Assembly (Hill Areas Committee) Order, 1972 and related Orders specifying what constitutes Hill Areas. These Orders defined the hill districts as Hill Areas and established the Committee as a standing committee to which specified Bills and budget estimates relating to hill areas must be referred. The constitution of this Committee has been subject to political friction over the years, particularly regarding which Bills require mandatory referral and whether the Committee's recommendations are binding on the full Assembly.

**Legislative disputes over mandatory referral (recurring, 2000s–2020s)** — The Hill Areas Committee's most persistent constitutional controversy concerns Bills passed without adequate referral to the Committee or where the Committee's recommendations are overridden by the full Assembly. Hill area legislators and community organisations have repeatedly argued that the Governor's special responsibility requires active intervention when the committee's functioning is being compromised by the valley majority. These disputes have not produced definitive Supreme Court jurisprudence clarifying the Governor's duties, leaving the constitutional protection dependent on political negotiation rather than legal enforcement.

**Manipur Hill Areas Autonomous District Councils Act litigation (Gauhati High Court, 2010–2015)** — The Manipur Hill Areas Autonomous District Councils Act, 2000 sought to create district councils in the hill areas, raising questions about the relationship between this statutory framework and the Article 371C committee mechanism. The Gauhati High Court confirmed that Article 371C's committee does not exhaust the constitutional space for hill-area autonomy arrangements and that the State may create additional institutional layers within the framework the article provides.

**Supreme Court suo motu proceedings on the 2023 Manipur violence (2023–2024)** — Following the outbreak of ethnic violence between Meitei and Kuki-Zo communities in May 2023, the Supreme Court took suo motu cognisance of the situation under Article 32. The proceedings addressed the State government's response to the violence, investigation of specific incidents, and the humanitarian situation. The Court's jurisdiction was exercised primarily under Article 32 and Article 142 rather than through direct construction of Article 371C. The Governor's reporting obligation under clause (2), which should inform Union intervention in Hill Area governance, became part of the public and parliamentary debate about whether adequate constitutional safeguards had been in place and adequately exercised.

**Naga People's Movement v Union of India (Supreme Court, 1998)** — While primarily about the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act in Manipur and Nagaland, this ruling addressed the constitutional framework within which Union law-enforcement powers operate in North-East India. The Court's discussion of the relationship between Union security powers and constitutional protections for hill communities under provisions including Article 371C provided relevant context for assessing the limits of those protections.

## Current significance

The 2023 Manipur violence has fundamentally altered the political and legal conversation around Article 371C. What had been an obscure provision in Part XXI became the subject of parliamentary debate, Supreme Court proceedings, and public interest litigation. The violence — involving the displacement of tens of thousands of people, destruction of hundreds of villages, and sustained armed conflict between communities — exposed the limits of the constitutional architecture. The Hill Areas Committee existed; the Governor had a special responsibility; the annual reporting obligation was in place. Yet none of these mechanisms prevented or contained the violence when deep communal conflict erupted.

Three specific reforms have been discussed in the context of the 2023 crisis. First, whether the Hill Areas Committee's recommendations should be made binding rather than advisory on the full Assembly for Bills specifically affecting hill land and governance. Second, whether the Governor's special responsibility should be given more specific procedural content through amendment to the Presidential Order. Third, whether a separate territorial arrangement — an autonomous council or even a separate State or Union Territory — would better serve the needs of hill communities than the intra-legislative committee model. None of these reforms has been enacted, and Article 371C remains in its 1971 form.

For the Indian constitution more broadly, Manipur's experience with Article 371C is a reminder that constitutional provisions protecting minority communities within a State are only as effective as the political will to implement them. The Governor's reporting obligation, the Presidential Order framework, the Hill Areas Committee — all of these exist on paper. Whether they function in practice depends on decisions that are fundamentally political in character. Article 371C is thus a case study in the limits of constitutional design as a solution to deep social and political conflict.

*Article 371C was inserted by the Constitution (Twenty-seventh Amendment) Act, 1971, effective 15 February 1972, when Manipur attained full Statehood.*
