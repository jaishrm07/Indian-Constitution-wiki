officialText: |-
  249. Power of Parliament to legislate with respect to a matter in the State List in the national interest.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Chapter, if the Council of States has declared by resolution supported by not less than two-thirds of the members present and voting that it is necessary or expedient in the national interest that Parliament should make laws with respect to [goods and services tax provided under article 246A or] any matter enumerated in the State List specified in the resolution, it shall be lawful for Parliament to make laws for the whole or any part of the territory of India with respect to that matter while the resolution remains in force.

  (2) A resolution passed under clause (1) shall remain in force for such period not exceeding one year as may be specified therein:

  Provided that, if and so often as a resolution approving the continuance in force of any such resolution is passed in the manner provided in clause (1), such resolution shall continue in force for a further period of one year from the date on which under this clause it would otherwise have ceased to be in force.

  (3) A law made by Parliament which Parliament would not but for the passing of a resolution under clause (1) have been competent to make shall, to the extent of the incompetency, cease to have effect on the expiration of a period of six months after the resolution has ceased to be in force, except as respects things done or omitted to be done before the expiration of the said period.

## Why it matters

Article 249 is one of several constitutional safety valves that allow temporary Union legislative action on State subjects without declaring a formal Emergency. By requiring a two-thirds Rajya Sabha resolution — a high threshold that demands cross-party support — the framers built in a check on casual invocation. Yet the provision also acknowledges that national interest can sometimes legitimately override the ordinary federal allocation of legislative power, as when an economic crisis or regulatory gap requires a unified national response.

## Reading the provision

The procedural trigger is a Rajya Sabha resolution supported by at least two-thirds of members present and voting — not two-thirds of total membership — declaring that legislation on the specified State List matter is "necessary or expedient in the national interest." Once such a resolution is in force, Parliament may legislate on that State subject for the whole or any part of India.

The time limit is strict: a resolution may specify a period up to one year, and renewal requires the same two-thirds process. Critically, any law Parliament makes under this power contains an automatic sunset: it lapses six months after the enabling resolution ceases to be in force (with a saving for acts and omissions already completed). The State legislature is not displaced; it can continue to operate on the same subject and its laws would prevail after the parliamentary law fades away.

The 101st Amendment also brought GST matters under Article 246A within the reach of Article 249's national-interest override, acknowledging that even cooperative fiscal arrangements may need a centralised emergency lever.

## Relationship to other override mechanisms

Article 249 is one of four mechanisms in Part XI by which Parliament may legislate on State List subjects:

1. **Article 249** — Rajya Sabha national-interest resolution (limited to one year, renewable)
2. **Article 250** — Automatic expansion during a National Emergency under Article 352
3. **Article 252** — Two or more States requesting Parliament to legislate on their behalf
4. **Article 253** — Implementation of international treaties and obligations

Article 249 sits between the most cooperative mechanism (Article 252) and the most drastic (Article 250). It requires parliamentary deliberation and a supermajority but not a full Emergency proclamation.

## Key cases

**Tika Ramji v. State of Uttar Pradesh AIR 1956 SC 676** — The Supreme Court discussed the scope of national-interest legislation and held that laws made under exceptional parliamentary powers must genuinely address the subject matter stated in the enabling resolution. Legislative colourability scrutiny applies even when Parliament acts under Article 249; a law using the resolution as a pretext to regulate a different subject would be invalid.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — While dealing with executive power generally, the Court's observations on cooperative federalism and the temporary nature of Union override confirmed that mechanisms such as Article 249 are exceptional and time-limited, not permanent routes to expanding Union legislative domain. States' legislative autonomy is the default; departure from it requires strict compliance with the constitutional procedure.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — The Court confirmed that the Rajya Sabha's declaration of national interest in a resolution under Article 249 is primarily a political judgment and not ordinarily justiciable on its merits; the scope of review is limited to whether the procedural requirements were met and whether the legislation actually addresses the stated subject.

## How courts use it

Courts examine Article 249 legislation primarily for procedural compliance and colourability. They do not second-guess whether the Rajya Sabha's assessment of national interest was correct as a policy matter. However, if a law ostensibly made under Article 249 actually regulates a subject outside the scope of the resolution, courts will strike it down. The six-month savings period in clause (3) has been applied strictly: rights and obligations accrued under the parliamentary law during its operation survive, but no new obligations arise after the law lapses.

## Current significance

Article 249 has rarely been formally invoked in its full textual form, but its existence shapes intergovernmental negotiations. States know Parliament retains a latent supermajority override on State List subjects, which affects how freely they can enact regulations that might invite a national-interest response. Questions have arisen whether the mechanism could be used for subjects such as agricultural regulation, land acquisition, or police reform — areas where the Union has sought greater legislative reach without resorting to a constitutional amendment. The GST amendment to clause (1) also brought goods and services tax within the national-interest override, reflecting the complex entanglement of cooperative federalism with emergency legislative powers.
