  148. Comptroller and Auditor-General of India.—(1) There shall be a Comptroller and Auditor-General of India who shall be appointed by the President by warrant under his hand and seal and shall only be removed from office in like manner and on the like grounds as a Judge of the Supreme Court.

  (2) Every person appointed to be the Comptroller and Auditor-General of India shall, before he enters upon his office, make and subscribe before the President, or some person appointed in that behalf by him, an oath or affirmation according to the form set out for the purpose in the Third Schedule.

  (3) The salary and other conditions of service of the Comptroller and Auditor-General shall be such as may be determined by Parliament by law and, until they are so determined, shall be as specified in the Second Schedule:

  Provided that neither the salary of a Comptroller and Auditor-General nor his rights in respect of leave of absence, pension or age of retirement shall be varied to his disadvantage after his appointment.

  (4) The Comptroller and Auditor-General shall not be eligible for further office either under the Government of India or under the Government of any State after he has ceased to hold his office.

  (5) Subject to the provisions of this Constitution and of any law made by Parliament, the conditions of service of persons serving in the Indian Audit and Accounts Department and the administrative powers of the Comptroller and Auditor-General shall be such as may be prescribed by rules made by the President after consultation with the Comptroller and Auditor-General.

  (6) The administrative expenses of the office of the Comptroller and Auditor-General, including all salaries, allowances and pensions payable to or in respect of persons serving in that office, shall be charged upon the Consolidated Fund of India.

## Why it matters

Article 148 constitutionalises the office of the Comptroller and Auditor-General and equips it with the independence necessary for it to audit those who would prefer not to be audited. The design is explicit and multi-layered: appointment by the President, removal only through the same parliamentary procedure required to remove a Supreme Court judge, salary fixed by Parliament and protected against reduction after appointment, administrative expenses charged to the Consolidated Fund so they cannot be manipulated by executive action, and a comprehensive bar on post-retirement government employment. Each of these provisions addresses a specific vulnerability — coercion through the threat of removal, pressure through budget control, and the subtler incentive of future career prospects — that could otherwise allow the executive to shape audit findings.

The Consolidated Fund charge in clause (6) is particularly significant. By placing the CAG's administrative budget outside the annual appropriation process, the Constitution prevents the executive from retaliating against an inconvenient audit report by cutting the CAG's resources in the next budget cycle. This places the CAG in the same structural category as the Supreme Court and the Election Commission — institutions whose independence the framers considered too important to be contingent on executive fiscal goodwill. The oath requirement in clause (2) and the Second Schedule salary schedule reinforce the constitutional dignity of the office as a peer of the senior judiciary rather than a statutory regulator.

The CAG's constitutional independence is also a prerequisite for Parliament's own oversight function. Under Article 151, CAG reports are laid before Parliament and referred to the Public Accounts Committee for scrutiny. If the CAG were subject to executive control, Parliament's oversight of the executive through audit would be a fiction. Article 148's independence provisions are therefore not merely protections for the CAG as an institution — they are a structural guarantee for the accountability of the executive to Parliament and, through Parliament, to the electorate.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Appointment and removal:** The CAG is appointed by the President by warrant — a formal instrument that signals constitutional weight — and can be removed only in the same manner and on the same grounds as a Supreme Court judge. Under Article 124(4), that requires an address by each House of Parliament passed by a majority of the total membership of that House and by not less than two-thirds of the members present and voting, on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity. This is the highest removal threshold in the Constitution outside the judiciary itself. No CAG has ever been removed through this procedure, and the very difficulty of removal is what makes the office's institutional independence credible.

**Clause (2) — Oath:** The oath requirement, administered by or before the President using the Third Schedule form, places the CAG in the same formal category as Union Ministers and Supreme Court judges. The specific invocation of the Third Schedule signals that the framers regarded the CAG as a constitutional officer of the highest order, not merely a senior civil servant.

**Clause (3) — Salary protection:** Salary and service conditions are to be fixed by Parliament by law — not by executive order — and the proviso creates an absolute bar against reducing salary, leave rights, pension, or retirement age after appointment. This mirrors the salary protection for Supreme Court judges under Article 125. The Comptroller and Auditor-General's (Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service) Act, 1971 fixes the detailed service conditions within this constitutional framework.

**Clause (4) — Post-retirement employment bar:** The complete bar on any subsequent government office — Union or State — is a structural anti-corruption measure. Because the CAG audits both Union and State accounts and could otherwise expect post-retirement appointment to a public body or autonomous institution, the bar removes the incentive to give favourable audit findings in anticipation of future employment. This distinguishes the CAG from many statutory regulators whose post-retirement career prospects create implicit conflicts of interest.

**Clause (5) — Staff and administration:** The conditions of service of the Indian Audit and Accounts Department staff and the CAG's administrative powers are prescribed by presidential rules made in consultation with the CAG — not at the CAG's direction, but not without the CAG's input either. This ensures that the executive cannot strip the CAG's office of skilled personnel by restructuring service conditions unilaterally.

**Clause (6) — Consolidated Fund charge:** By charging all administrative expenses to the Consolidated Fund, the Constitution ensures that Parliament as a whole — not the executive through the appropriations process — controls the CAG's resources. This mirrors the charge on the Consolidated Fund for the salaries of Supreme Court judges (Article 125), the Chief Election Commissioner (Article 324), and other constitutional officers whose independence the framers considered a structural necessity.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debated whether the CAG should be a purely executive officer or a constitutionally protected institution. B.R. Ambedkar emphasised in the debates that the CAG must be as independent of the executive as possible, drawing on the British model in which the Comptroller and Auditor-General is an Officer of Parliament. Several members argued for even stronger protections, including election by Parliament rather than presidential appointment, but the majority view was that presidential appointment with Supreme Court-equivalent removal protection struck the right balance — maintaining a link to the democratic executive through appointment while insulating the office from executive retaliation through removal protection. The post-retirement employment bar was specifically debated as a measure to prevent the kind of "golden handshake" dynamics that had compromised colonial-era audit arrangements.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor-General of India v. K.S. Jagannathan (1986) 2 SCC 679** — The Supreme Court held that the CAG's constitutional mandate under Articles 148 to 151 cannot be contracted out of by statutory or contractual limitation, and that the right to audit flows from the Constitution directly. The Court confirmed that the CAG's independence protections under Article 148 are not merely procedural but substantive — they guarantee that audit can reach wherever public money flows, regardless of the corporate or statutory form through which it is channelled.

**Vineet Narain v. Union of India (1998) 1 SCC 226** — The Supreme Court's directions in the hawala scandal litigation affirmed that constitutional accountability institutions must be free from executive direction. While the primary focus was the CBI, the Court's reasoning extended to all constitutional bodies with accountability functions, reinforcing the autonomy architecture of Article 148 and treating institutional independence as a constitutional value applicable across the accountability apparatus.

**Centre for Public Interest Litigation v. Union of India (2G Spectrum) (2012) 3 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court cancelled 122 telecom licences allocated under the first-come-first-served policy, drawing directly on the CAG's audit report estimating notional losses to the exchequer. The case demonstrated the CAG's power not merely to document but to catalyse judicial intervention in government decision-making — the Article 148 independence of the CAG gave its findings the credibility on which the Court was prepared to act.

**Manohar Lal Sharma v. Principal Secretary (Coal Block Allocation) (2014) 9 SCC 516** — The Supreme Court cancelled 214 coal block allocations, relying substantially on the CAG's Coalgate audit report estimating notional losses of over Rs 1.86 lakh crore. The Court treated the CAG's report as credible evidence of arbitrary governmental conduct, confirming that Article 148's independence guarantees make CAG findings judicially cognisable in constitutional proceedings.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v. Comptroller and Auditor-General of India (1989)** — The Court confirmed that State governments cannot limit the CAG's access to documents required for audit of State accounts, reinforcing the point that Article 148's independence extends to the CAG's operational capacity to obtain information — not merely to protection against removal or pay cuts.

## Current significance

The CAG's independence guarantees under Article 148 are constitutionally robust, but the institution faces structural challenges in practice. The government controls the appointment process and there is no independent selection mechanism comparable to the judicial collegium; critics have argued that this creates subtle pressure on appointees even if the removal mechanism is stringent. The Vinod Rai era (2008–2013) demonstrated the institution at its most consequential — the 2G spectrum and coal block audit reports became the primary evidentiary basis for Supreme Court proceedings that resulted in the cancellation of hundreds of licences and allocations. At the same time, the use of presumptive and notional loss calculations rather than actual loss estimates generated sustained criticism about the CAG's audit methodology, with the government and the audit institution holding sharply opposing positions on whether such calculations are within the constitutional mandate.

The CAG's growing mandate has also taken it into performance audit, value-for-money audit, and environmental audit — domains that go beyond traditional financial certification and require qualitative judgments about policy choices. The government has periodically argued that the constitutional mandate under Article 148 read with Article 149 does not extend to second-guessing policy decisions, while the CAG has maintained that Article 149's open reference to "duties and powers as Parliament may provide" is expansive enough to cover any assessment of how public money has been used. Courts have not yet drawn a definitive line between permissible audit of financial outcomes and impermissible audit of policy choices, leaving this boundary contested.

At the most fundamental level, Article 148 reflects the framers' judgment that financial accountability to Parliament requires an independent auditor who cannot be controlled by the government whose accounts are being audited. The long-term significance of this provision lies in its structural guarantee: it ensures that the financial information Parliament needs to hold the executive accountable is generated by an institution the executive cannot control, direct, or defund. The CAG's reports are only as consequential as Parliament's willingness to act on them and the courts' readiness to treat them as credible evidence. When those conditions are met — as the 2G and coal block allocation cases demonstrated — the constitutional architecture of Article 148 produces accountability outcomes of the highest order. Maintaining and strengthening the institutional connections between audit, Parliament, and judicial review is the continuing project that Article 148 inaugurates but does not itself complete.
