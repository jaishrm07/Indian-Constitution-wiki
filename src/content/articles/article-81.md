  (a) not more than [five hundred and thirty members] chosen by direct election from territorial constituencies in the States, and

  (b) not more than [twenty members] to represent the Union territories, chosen in such manner as Parliament may by law provide.

## Why it matters

Article 81 is the constitutional source of the Lok Sabha's democratic legitimacy.
Direct election from population-proportional constituencies is the design principle — a House of the People must genuinely represent the people.
But the article also embeds a major political compromise — a population freeze that prevents States from being penalised for demographic success.
This tension between the principle of proportional representation and the protection of States that controlled their populations defines the most consequential electoral debate India faces as the post-2026 delimitation approaches.

The Lok Sabha is the primary democratic chamber of Parliament.
It is the House from which the government draws its mandate under Article 75(3), and it holds the decisive voice over money and appropriations under Articles 109 and 110.
The entire edifice of democratic accountability in India's parliamentary system rests on the Lok Sabha being constituted as Article 81 specifies — by direct election, from constituencies that are as equal as practicable, so that each citizen's vote carries roughly the same weight in determining who governs the country.

The population-proportionality requirement in Article 81(2) embodies a fundamental principle of electoral democracy — one person, one vote, one value.
Constituencies that diverge widely in population make some votes mathematically more powerful than others.
A constituency of 500,000 voters elects one member; if a neighbouring constituency has 2,000,000 voters and also elects one member, the first constituency's voters have four times the electoral power of the second's.
The article's mandate that constituencies be as equal as practicable is designed to prevent this kind of built-in inequality.

The article's history is also the history of India's demographic and political transformation.
In 1950, when the Constitution came into force, India's population was approximately 361 million.
By 1971 — the census that determines current inter-State seat allocations — it had grown to 548 million.
As of 2011, the last published census, it was 1.21 billion.
The enormous variation in population growth rates across States over those decades is the source of the representation controversy that Article 81's freeze was designed to manage, and that post-2026 delimitation must resolve.

The 104th Constitutional Amendment of 2020 is a significant recent change to the article's original structure.
That Amendment removed the provision for two Anglo-Indian nominated members to the Lok Sabha under the original Article 331.
The nominated-membership category for Anglo-Indians had been a transitional measure — intended to ensure representation for a small community unlikely to win general-election constituencies — but was extended repeatedly from its original sunset of ten years.
Its removal in 2020 reduces the maximum Lok Sabha membership while also closing a mechanism that had been designed to protect a minority community's representation in the national legislature.

## Reading the provision

The article sets a ceiling rather than a fixed number for Lok Sabha seats — "not more than" 530 from the States and 20 from Union Territories.
The ceiling has been reached at 530; the current House has 543 members (530 State seats plus 13 Union Territory seats).
Clause (2) requires both inter-State proportionality (States' seat allocations should reflect their population share) and intra-State proportionality (constituencies within each State should be roughly equal in population).
Both requirements are qualified by the phrase "so far as practicable," acknowledging that administrative boundaries, geographical constraints, and minimum-representation requirements for small States will inevitably produce imperfect proportionality.

The proviso for small States — those with populations not exceeding six million — exempts them from the strict proportionality requirement, ensuring they retain a minimum presence in the House regardless of how small their population is relative to the average constituency size.
This proviso protects small northeastern States, island territories, and smaller hill States from losing their voice in the House altogether as national average constituency sizes grow.

Clause (1)(b)'s provision for Union Territory representation — up to 20 members, chosen in a manner prescribed by Parliament — reflects the different constitutional status of Union Territories.
They are not States, their residents are not represented in the Rajya Sabha through the Article 80(4) mechanism, and their governance is more directly controlled by the Union under Article 239.
Parliament has enacted law to create single-member or multi-member Union Territory constituencies by direct election, broadly following the State model.

The definitional clause for "population" in clause (3) is where the political history of India's representation debate is most visible.
The freeze — currently requiring population to be read as the 1971 census for inter-State seat allocations and the 2001 census for intra-State constituency boundaries — was introduced by the 42nd Amendment in 1976 and extended multiple times.
It will expire when post-2026 census figures are published, at which point the inter-State seat allocation must be recalculated against current data.
The result will be the first revision of the inter-State seat allocation since 1977, and it is expected to produce the largest redistribution of parliamentary representation in India's history.

The proviso structure of clause (3) is technically intricate.
The 1971 census data governs how many seats each State gets.
The 2001 census data governs how those seats are distributed within each State — that is, how constituency boundaries are drawn.
When the freeze lifts, both layers of the calculation must be updated, using the census data then available.
The Delimitation Commission that conducts the post-2026 exercise will be working with population data at least five decades newer than the data that has governed Lok Sabha composition throughout the current political era.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debated the Lok Sabha's composition against the backdrop of universal adult suffrage — a decision that was itself contested.
India in 1947 had very high levels of illiteracy, and some members questioned whether universal suffrage was appropriate or whether property, literacy, or tax-payment requirements should be imposed.
The framers rejected all such restrictions decisively, with Dr. Ambedkar arguing that democracy meant government by the people without qualification, and that literacy tests and property requirements were colonial devices for excluding the poor from governance.

The population-proportionality requirement was included without significant controversy — it was the natural corollary of representing the people as individuals.
The more contested questions were about reserved seats for scheduled castes and scheduled tribes, the representation of minorities, and the treatment of small States.
The final structure — proportionality with the "so far as practicable" qualification, reserved seats within State allocations rather than separate electorates, and the small-State proviso — reflected a series of compromises between the principle of equal representation and the practical requirements of governing a continent-sized, deeply diverse democracy.

The decision to fix a ceiling for Lok Sabha seats rather than requiring a fixed ratio of representatives to population was a deliberate choice.
The framers recognised that India's rapid population growth would, if a fixed ratio were used, produce an unwieldy House.
A ceiling of "not more than" 500 (later 530) was set to allow for reasonable parliamentary functioning.
The current size of 543 members is close to the maximum, and debates about whether the post-2026 delimitation should expand the House — by raising the ceiling through constitutional amendment — are part of the broader conversation about how to manage the representation problem without stripping seats from any State.

## Key cases

**Mohd. Shakeel Wahid Ahmed v. Union of India (1983)** — Petitioners challenged constituency delimitation on the grounds that population disparities violated the equal-representation guarantee in Article 81(2).
The Supreme Court held that delimitation orders are immune from judicial review under Article 329(a), and that the Delimitation Commission's methodology is not open to challenge in courts so long as it falls within the statutory framework.
The equality standard in Article 81 is a standard of practicability, not mathematical precision.

**Meghraj Kothari v. Delimitation Commission (1967)** — The Supreme Court clarified the finality of delimitation orders and reinforced that the constitutional equality standard allows for the practical latitude the phrase "so far as practicable" introduces.
Courts do not substitute their judgment for the Delimitation Commission's on how that latitude should be used.
The case established the foundational principle that delimitation decisions — though they directly affect democratic representation — are not justiciable in ordinary courts, a principle grounded in the interplay between Article 81 and Article 329.

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)** — The Supreme Court's recognition of free and fair elections as a basic structure of the Constitution directly protects the principle that Article 81 embodies — that the Lok Sabha must be constituted through a genuine popular election in which each vote carries roughly equal weight.
The basic structure doctrine means Parliament cannot amend Article 81 in ways that would fundamentally undermine the equal-representation principle that makes the House of the People genuinely representative of the people.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (2002)** — The Supreme Court required candidates in parliamentary elections — including Lok Sabha elections — to disclose criminal antecedents, assets, and educational qualifications.
The judgment relied on the voter's right to information as a component of the constitutional right to free speech and as a precondition for the kind of informed electoral choice that makes direct election from territorial constituencies meaningful.
The case reinforces the principle that Article 81's direct-election model requires transparency in the candidates who present themselves for election.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** — The Supreme Court upheld the Tenth Schedule's anti-defection provisions while preserving judicial review of procedural compliance.
The case bears on Article 81 because the Lok Sabha's composition after a general election can be affected by defections and the Speaker's disqualification decisions — the Tenth Schedule's application to Lok Sabha members means that the House's actual working composition may differ from what the election results would suggest if disqualification proceedings are active.

**Waman Rao v. Union of India (1981)** — The Supreme Court clarified the relationship between the basic structure doctrine and constitutional amendments affecting electoral rights and representation.
The judgment reinforces that Article 81's core principle — genuine, population-proportional popular representation — is protected against legislative manipulation of the very constitutional mechanism that gives Parliament its democratic mandate.

## Current significance

Article 81 sits at the intersection of representation, federalism, and demography in a way that is immediately politically live.
When the post-2026 delimitation occurs, populous States — Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan — are projected to gain a large number of additional Lok Sabha seats.
States that achieved earlier fertility transitions — Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka — may see their relative weight in the House decline significantly.
Projections based on 2011 census data suggest that the four largest northern States could collectively account for well over 200 seats in a reconstituted Lok Sabha if the ceiling is expanded proportionally, compared to fewer than 100 for the four largest southern States.

This shift has reopened debates about whether population alone should be the constitutional criterion for parliamentary representation.
Some scholars argue additional factors — fiscal contribution, gender parity in voting participation, or explicit federal protections — should be constitutionally built in.
Others argue the article's existing structure, with its "so far as practicable" qualification, provides enough flexibility.
As of early 2026, no constitutional amendment on the question had been tabled, though the issue is prominent in the political discourse of southern State governments and their national political representatives.

One proposed response to the north-south imbalance is to expand the total size of the Lok Sabha — by amending the ceiling in Article 81(1) from 530 to a larger number — in ways that give additional seats to all States, with high-growth States receiving proportionally more but low-growth States receiving some increase too.
This approach would require a constitutional amendment but would avoid any State being a net loser of seats in absolute terms.
The political feasibility of such an amendment depends on whether the necessary two-thirds majority in both Houses can be assembled — which is itself a function of the current composition of Parliament, a constitutional circularity that makes the problem difficult to resolve through the ordinary mechanisms the Constitution provides.

The removal of the Anglo-Indian nominated-membership provision by the 104th Amendment raises a broader question.
If the Constitution could include nominated representation for a small community in the Lok Sabha when the framers judged that community unlikely to win general-election seats, what does that precedent say about other communities or regions that find themselves systematically under-represented in a population-proportional House?
The 104th Amendment's elimination of that provision, rather than its extension, signals a constitutional preference for pure direct election without demographic reservations beyond the scheduled-caste and scheduled-tribe reservation system.
That signal will be tested when the post-2026 delimitation forces a definitive choice about Article 81's underlying principles.
