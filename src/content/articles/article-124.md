  (2) Every Judge of the Supreme Court shall be appointed by the President by warrant under his hand and seal [on the recommendation of the National Judicial Appointments Commission referred to in article 124A] and shall hold office until he attains the age of sixty-five years: [* * * * *]

  (a) a Judge may, by writing under his hand addressed to the President, resign his office;

  (b) a Judge may be removed from his office in the manner provided in clause (4). [(2A) The age of a Judge of the Supreme Court shall be determined by such authority and in such manner as Parliament may by law provide.]

  (3) A person shall not be qualified for appointment as a Judge of the Supreme Court unless he is a citizen of India and—

  (a) has been for at least five years a Judge of a High Court or of two or more such Courts in succession; or

  (b) has been for at least ten years an advocate of a High Court or of two or more such Courts in succession; or

  (c) is, in the opinion of the President, a distinguished jurist.

  Explanation I.—In this clause "High Court'' means a High Court which exercises, or which at any time before the commencement of this Constitution exercised, jurisdiction in any part of the territory of India.

  Explanation II.—In computing for the purpose of this clause the period during which a person has been an advocate, any period during which a person has held judicial office not inferior to that of a district judge after he became an advocate shall be included.

  (4) A Judge of the Supreme Court shall not be removed from his office except by an order of the President passed after an address by each House of Parliament supported by a majority of the total membership of that House and by a majority of not less than two-thirds of the members of that House present and voting has been presented to the President in the same session for such removal on the ground of proved misbehaviour or incapacity.

  (5) Parliament may by law regulate the procedure for the presentation of an address and for the investigation and proof of the misbehaviour or incapacity of a Judge under clause (4).

  (6) Every person appointed to be a Judge of the Supreme Court shall, before he enters upon his office, make and subscribe before the President, or some person appointed in that behalf by him, an oath or affirmation according to the form set out for the purpose in the Third Schedule.

  (7) No person who has held office as a Judge of the Supreme Court shall plead or act in any court or before any authority within the territory of India.

## Why it matters

Article 124 is the constitutional foundation for the Supreme Court of India. It defines who may be a judge, how judges are appointed and removed, and what safeguards insulate the Court from executive pressure. The political and legal contests over the appointment process — spanning four decades and four major court decisions — have generated some of the most consequential institutional conflicts in Indian constitutional history and continue to define the relationship between the executive and the judiciary.

The appointment of judges to the Supreme Court is among the most consequential acts of governance in a constitutional democracy. Judges appointed to the Court routinely decide questions of life and liberty, the validity of legislation passed by Parliament, the scope of fundamental rights, and the boundaries of executive power. The process by which such judges are selected therefore directly determines the Court's character and its relationship to the political branches. Article 124 is the text from which every dispute about that process flows.

The framers of the Constitution were acutely conscious of what had happened in other countries when judicial appointments were made purely by the executive. They observed the way in which the American Senate's confirmation process had become politicised, and they were aware of colonial India's experience with a judiciary that was perceived as an instrument of executive will. The resulting text reflects a deliberate choice to require presidential appointment — keeping the head of state formally in the picture — while framing the role of the Chief Justice as an input that, the framers expected, would carry decisive weight in practice.

What no one anticipated was that the question of how much weight the Chief Justice's view should carry would itself become the subject of a four-decade constitutional struggle. The trajectory from the First Judges Case in 1981, through the Second Judges Case in 1993, the Third Judges Case in 1998, and finally the NJAC Case in 2015 is the story of the Supreme Court progressively asserting control over its own composition — a development with no close parallel anywhere in the common law world.

The independence of the judiciary is now settled as an unamendable element of the Constitution's basic structure. That conclusion was reached precisely through controversies over Article 124. Understanding the text of this Article is therefore inseparable from understanding why India's constitutional structure looks the way it does today: a system where a collegium of judges exercises effective control over judicial appointments, where the written text diverges from operative constitutional practice, and where any legislative attempt at reform must navigate the NJAC judgment's holding that certain features of judicial independence are beyond Parliament's amending power.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) creates the Supreme Court as a constitutional entity and sets a default ceiling of seven puisne judges. The phrase "until Parliament by law prescribes a larger number" has been invoked repeatedly. The Supreme Court (Number of Judges) Act, as periodically amended, now provides for a sanctioned strength of thirty-four judges including the Chief Justice. Whether the Court actually sits at or near this strength at any given time depends on the pace of collegium recommendations and executive action on those recommendations — a point of friction that became particularly acute during the 2021–2023 period when the government was accused of deliberately delaying action on pending collegium recommendations.

Clause (2) is the most contested provision in the Article. It requires appointment by the President "by warrant under his hand and seal." In the constitutional structure, presidential action of this kind ordinarily means action on the advice of the Cabinet under Article 74. The question that the Judges Cases resolved — progressively in favour of the judiciary — was whether the word "consultation" in the original clause (before the Ninety-ninth Amendment) required only seeking the Chief Justice's view or required effective compliance with it. The answer given in the Second Judges Case, and confirmed in the Third, was that consultation meant concurrence in all but name: the executive could return a recommendation once, but if the collegium reiterated it, the appointment was to proceed. The text of Clause (2) as currently printed refers to the National Judicial Appointments Commission inserted by the Ninety-ninth Amendment, but that amendment was struck down in 2015. The operative appointment mechanism is therefore the collegium system developed through case law — a striking instance where the constitutional text and constitutional practice diverge entirely.

Clause (3) sets three alternative qualification routes: five years as a High Court judge, ten years as a High Court advocate, or being, in the President's opinion, a distinguished jurist. The third category — distinguished jurist — has never been used in the history of the Court, despite periodic suggestions that eminent academics or retired law officers might qualify. The reasons are structural: the collegium is composed of sitting judges who have navigated the traditional route through the bar and the High Courts, and the collegium process naturally produces candidates drawn from the same pathway.

Clause (4) prescribes removal only by a parliamentary address supported by a majority of total membership and at least two-thirds of members present and voting in each House — a double-lock deliberately designed to make removal rare. The threshold is higher than that required for constitutional amendment under Article 368 in ordinary cases. No judge of the Supreme Court has ever been removed under this procedure. The only impeachment motion to come close was that against Justice V Ramaswami in 1993, which failed because the Indian National Congress abstained, preventing the requisite majority from being assembled.

Clause (5) empowers Parliament to regulate the procedure for impeachment proceedings. The Judges (Inquiry) Act 1968 provides for a three-member committee — including a Supreme Court judge, a Chief Justice of a High Court, and a distinguished jurist — to investigate allegations before an address is moved. The practical effect is to create a preliminary filter that prevents frivolous impeachment attempts while still leaving the ultimate decision with Parliament.

Clause (6) requires every judge to take an oath or affirmation before the President before entering office, in the form set out in the Third Schedule. The oath binds the judge to uphold the Constitution and to perform duties without fear or favour, affection or ill-will.

Clause (7) imposes a permanent post-retirement bar on pleading or acting before any court or authority in India. The purpose is to eliminate any incentive for a sitting judge to decide cases in ways that might generate goodwill among potential future clients or employers. The bar is absolute in scope — it covers all courts and all authorities, not merely courts of the judge's previous jurisdiction. This distinguishes the Supreme Court from High Courts, where a similar bar exists under Article 220 but is confined to courts within the judge's former jurisdiction.

## The collegium system

The collegium is not mentioned in the Constitution but is entirely a creation of judicial interpretation. Its evolution across three landmark decisions is essential to understanding how Article 124 works in practice.

The First Judges Case in 1981 gave the executive primacy: the Chief Justice's recommendation was entitled to weight but was not binding. This ruling provided a constitutional imprimatur for what had already been practiced — the Indira Gandhi government had in 1973 superseded three senior judges and appointed Justice A N Ray as Chief Justice, triggering a crisis about executive interference in judicial appointments. The First Judges Case resolved the legal question in the government's favour, holding that "consultation" did not mean "concurrence."

The Second Judges Case in 1993 reversed this. A nine-judge bench held that "consultation" in Article 124(2) meant effective concurrence — the Chief Justice's view, formed in consultation with the two most senior puisne judges, was entitled to primacy. The judgment held that the executive's power to appoint was a formal constitutional step but that the substantive selection power lay with the Chief Justice. The ruling was driven partly by the memory of the supersession crisis and partly by the Court's sense that leaving appointment power with the executive created an unacceptable risk of subordinating judicial independence to political considerations.

The Third Judges Case in 1998 was decided on a Presidential Reference under Article 143, after the government raised questions about the correct composition and procedures of the collegium established by the Second Judges Case. The nine-judge bench expanded the collegium to five members — the Chief Justice and the four most senior puisne judges — and elaborated detailed norms including rules on reiteration of recommendations and on the handling of cases where collegium members had conflicts of interest. The reiteration rule — that if the collegium reiterated a recommendation, the executive was bound to act on it — was the most significant operational outcome.

The NJAC Case in 2015 brought matters to a head. Following years of criticism of the collegium for its opacity, delay, and alleged nepotism, Parliament passed the Constitution (Ninety-ninth Amendment) Act creating the National Judicial Appointments Commission. The NJAC's composition — the Chief Justice and two senior puisne judges alongside the Law Minister and two eminent persons chosen by a panel including the Prime Minister, the Leader of the Opposition, and the Chief Justice — was designed to introduce broader accountability into the process while retaining a judicial majority. A Constitution Bench struck it down four-to-one. The majority held that judicial independence is an unamendable element of the basic structure, that the Law Minister's presence on the Commission as a permanent member was incompatible with that independence, and that the eminent persons provision created an insufficiently constrained veto mechanism. Justice Chelameshwar, the sole dissenter, argued that the collegium's transparency record was poor and that the NJAC was a constitutional response to a genuine institutional problem.

## Key cases

**S P Gupta v Union of India (1981) — First Judges Case** — A seven-judge bench held that the executive had primacy in appointments, interpreting "consultation" with the Chief Justice as obligatory in form but not determinative in effect. The case provided a legal framework for the political supersession of senior judges that had been practiced since 1973. The ruling shaped a decade in which the executive asserted effective control over the composition of the higher judiciary.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (1993) — Second Judges Case** — A nine-judge bench reversed the First Judges Case, gave the Chief Justice effective primacy, and created the collegium as an institutional body with decisive authority over recommendations. The Court held that the independence of the judiciary was a basic structure element and that an appointment process that gave the executive final say was incompatible with that independence. The judgment did not articulate detailed procedures for collegium functioning, a gap that created subsequent uncertainty.

**In re Special Reference No 1 of 1998 — Third Judges Case** — On a Presidential Reference under Article 143, a nine-judge bench expanded the collegium to five members and issued detailed guidelines on its procedures. The reiteration rule — that a reiterated collegium recommendation is binding on the executive — was the most operationally significant holding. The judgment also addressed the obligation of the collegium to give reasons and to consult other judges of the Court when making recommendations about those judges' home High Courts.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (2015) — NJAC Case** — A Constitution Bench of five judges struck down the Constitution (Ninety-ninth Amendment) Act and the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act by a majority of four to one. The majority held that judicial independence is unamendable, that the NJAC's design compromised it, and that the collegium system — however imperfect — was constitutionally preferable to any mechanism that gave the executive a formal seat in the appointments process. The lone dissent by Justice J Chelameswar argued that the collegium had a poor transparency record and that the NJAC was a legitimate and arguably superior constitutional innovation.

**K Veeraswami v Union of India (1991)** — The Court considered whether a sitting judge could be prosecuted for a criminal offence without prior sanction, reading Article 124(4)'s removal procedure in light of the need to protect judicial independence. The ruling that criminal prosecution requires prior sanction from the Chief Justice (or the President in the Chief Justice's case) was a significant extension of the protections surrounding judicial tenure.

**Justice K S Puttaswamy v Union of India (2017)** — While primarily a privacy judgment, the case reaffirmed in several concurrences that judicial independence — including the independence secured by Article 124's appointment and tenure protections — is a foundational element of the constitutional order that cannot be compromised by ordinary legislation.

## Current significance

The collegium system remains constitutionally operative but has faced sustained criticism from the executive, the bar, and academic commentators for opacity, delay, and self-referential decision-making. The Supreme Court and the Union government engaged in a prolonged public dispute from 2021 onward about whether the government can indefinitely defer acting on collegium recommendations and whether collegium resolutions must be published with reasons. The Court has begun publishing resolutions, and some reasons are now given, but the system lacks statutory backing or a formal transparency regime. Any legislative attempt to reform appointments must overcome the NJAC judgment's holding that judicial independence is unamendable — a constitutional constraint with no clear parallel in other democracies.

The question of what happens when the executive returns a recommendation — as has occurred with multiple collegium nominees between 2022 and 2025 — remains unresolved in procedural terms. The Third Judges Case said the executive must act on a reiterated recommendation, but it did not specify a timeline or a mechanism for enforcement. When the government has held recommendations for months or years without formally accepting or rejecting them, the Court has lacked a clean constitutional instrument to compel action. Contempt proceedings against the executive remain a theoretical possibility but one fraught with institutional peril.

The "distinguished jurist" route in Clause (3)(c) has never been used, and periodic proposals to appoint eminent academics or retired civil servants to the Court have not materialised. The collegium's composition — entirely drawn from sitting judges who rose through the conventional bar and bench pathway — means that the Court's intellectual culture is shaped by a relatively homogenous professional background. Critics argue this limits the range of perspectives brought to constitutional adjudication; defenders argue that professional judges bring the institutional experience and accumulated judicial culture that constitutional courts require.

The permanent post-retirement bar in Clause (7) has generated ongoing debate about whether it deters talented advocates from accepting elevation. A senior advocate who accepts appointment to the Supreme Court surrenders a potentially lucrative practice in perpetuity. There have been repeated suggestions that this bar be revisited, particularly as the profession's commercial sector has grown and the opportunity cost of judicial appointment has risen. The bar has remained unchanged, however, reflecting a continued constitutional commitment to ensuring that judicial decisions are made without any eye on post-retirement opportunity.
