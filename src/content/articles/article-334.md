## Why it matters

Article 334 encodes a fundamental assumption of the framers: that caste-based political reservation was a transitional remedy for a historically created disadvantage, not a permanent feature of the constitutional order. By building in a sunset clause, the Constituent Assembly signalled that Parliament should be required to return periodically to the question of whether the conditions justifying reservation — the inability of SC and ST communities to secure adequate political representation through the ordinary competitive electoral process — still existed. The periodic extension requirement is thus a form of constitutional accountability: a government that extends the period implicitly represents to Parliament that those conditions persist.

In practice, Parliament has extended the sunset without a serious parliamentary re-examination of whether the underlying conditions have changed. Each extension has been passed relatively quickly, often as a constitutional amendment Bill with substantial cross-party support, reflecting the political consensus that SC and ST communities remain disadvantaged and that withdrawal of reserved seats would produce precipitous declines in their legislative representation. The doctrinal question — whether the democratic legitimacy of SC and ST elected representatives under reservation is the same as that of open-seat representatives — has been largely kept out of political discourse, but it resurfaces in academic and judicial contexts.

The 104th Amendment's differential treatment of the two clauses — extending SC and ST reservation to 2030 but allowing Anglo-Indian nomination to lapse in 2020 — is politically significant. Parliament made a deliberate distinction between a community that had become numerically and socially integrated into the mainstream and communities that continue to face structural disadvantage. This differential treatment reinforces the framers' original conception of reservation as a remedy for specific, identifiable disadvantage rather than a generalised corrective device.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a)** covers the reservation of seats for SCs and STs in the Lok Sabha and in State Legislative Assemblies under Articles 330 and 332. The 104th Amendment extended this period to eighty years from the Constitution's commencement — meaning the reserved seats under Articles 330 and 332 are constitutionally valid until 26 January 2030.

**Clause (b)** covered the Anglo-Indian nominated representation under Articles 331 and 333, which allowed the President (for the Lok Sabha) and the Governor (for State assemblies) to nominate up to two Anglo-Indian members if that community was not adequately represented through elections. This provision expired on 25 January 2020, seventy years after commencement, when Parliament chose not to extend it alongside the SC and ST reservation extension in the 104th Amendment.

**The proviso** protects any Parliament or State assembly that is in existence when the sunset arrives: the reserved seats in that House remain in force until the House is dissolved. This prevents a mid-term constitutional crisis where reserved seats could suddenly become open seats partway through a legislative term.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Dr B.R. Ambedkar's defence of the sunset clause is one of the most cited passages in Indian constitutional history. Ambedkar argued that making reservation permanent would be inconsistent with the aspiration to move toward a society based on equal citizenship, and that fixing a terminal date would compel Parliament to make a conscious, visible, and periodically renewable political choice about whether the conditions justifying reservation continued to exist. He accepted that the initial period of ten years was somewhat arbitrary but insisted that the obligation to revisit — rather than to assume indefinite continuation — was the constitutionally responsible approach.

Other members, including K.M. Munshi, expressed concern that even time-limited reservation would entrench communal thinking in the legislative process. The Constituent Assembly ultimately accepted Ambedkar's view that the social reality of SC and ST disadvantage required the transitional protection even at that risk. T.T. Krishnamachari raised procedural questions about what would happen when the period expired: would the Delimitation Commission automatically redraw all constituencies as general seats? The proviso was inserted to answer that concern by ensuring existing Houses could complete their terms.

## History of amendments to Article 334

The ten-year period set in 1950 would have expired in 1960. Parliament has extended it eight times: the 8th Amendment Act (1960) to twenty years; the 23rd Amendment (1969) to thirty years; the 45th Amendment (1980) to forty years; the 62nd Amendment (1989) to fifty years; the 79th Amendment (1999) to sixty years; the 95th Amendment (2009) to seventy years for both SC and ST reservation and Anglo-Indian nomination; and finally the 104th Amendment (2019) to eighty years for SC and ST reservation and seventy years for Anglo-Indian nomination. The consistent pattern of extension — eight times over seventy years — has been read by courts as evidence of Parliament's settled determination that SC and ST representation remains insufficiently robust to survive without constitutional protection.

## Key cases

**Waman Rao v Union of India (1981)** — A Constitution Bench considered whether constitutional amendments extending the reservation period are immune from challenge under the basic structure doctrine. The Court held that amendments extending protective provisions for historically marginalised groups are consistent with, rather than destructive of, the basic structure of the Constitution. This reasoning has been applied to each successive extension amendment.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v Pradip Tandon (1975)** — The Court examined the theoretical basis for time-limited reservation, observing that the constitutional commitment to ameliorating SC and ST disadvantage must be assessed against actual social conditions rather than assumed away, and that parliamentary extensions of Article 334 represent legislative acknowledgment that those conditions persist.

**Municipal Council, Ratlam v Vardhichand (1980)** — The Court's affirmation of the constitutional commitment to ameliorating social disadvantage provides the normative backdrop against which periodic extensions of Article 334 are evaluated, linking the sunset-extension dynamic to the broader transformative constitutionalism framework.

**Lok Sabha Debates on the 104th Amendment (2019)** — The parliamentary debates on the 104th Amendment are constitutionally significant as evidence of legislative intent behind the most recent extension. Members across parties cited persistent socio-economic backwardness, under-representation in State bureaucracies, and electoral data showing that SC and ST candidates continue to win predominantly from reserved rather than open constituencies — demonstrating dependence on reservation that the framers' model of periodic review was designed to assess.

## Current significance

The 2030 sunset intersects with three other constitutional processes simultaneously. First, the post-2026 census will trigger the first delimitation since 2002, redrawing all Lok Sabha and State assembly constituencies; the Delimitation Commission will apply Articles 330 and 332 proportionality formulas to produce a new reserved-seat allocation that will operate within whatever remains of the reservation period. Second, whether Parliament will extend Article 334 before 2030 — and whether it will do so with a more thorough review of reservation's social impact than in previous extensions — is a major pending legislative question that may be politically contentious given the simultaneity with delimitation. Third, the Davinder Singh (2024) ruling on sub-classification raises new structural questions about whether the post-extension reserved seat framework should incorporate differentiation within SC and ST categories rather than treating each category as a monolithic block.

The lapse of Anglo-Indian nominated seats in 2020 has been largely uncontested in the courts and in political discourse, suggesting that Parliament's assessment that the community has achieved sufficient integration is broadly accepted. That precedent — a sunset that was actually allowed to operate for the first time — may shape political arguments about the long-term trajectory of SC and ST electoral reservation, even if the timescale for any eventual review is far longer.
