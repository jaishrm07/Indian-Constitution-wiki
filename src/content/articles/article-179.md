  179. Vacation and resignation of, and removal from, the offices of Speaker and Deputy Speaker.—A member holding office as Speaker or Deputy Speaker of an Assembly—

  (a) shall vacate his office if he ceases to be a member of the Assembly;

  (b) may at any time by writing under his hand addressed, if such member is the Speaker, to the Deputy Speaker, and if such member is the Deputy Speaker, to the Speaker, resign his office; and

## Why it matters

Article 179 is the mechanism that determines the Speaker's security of tenure and accountability to the House. The presiding officer of a State Legislative Assembly holds the most consequential internal position in State parliamentary life — maintaining order, certifying legislation, and adjudicating anti-defection petitions under the Tenth Schedule that can end elected members' careers. To perform these functions with even a semblance of impartiality, the Speaker needs sufficient protection against arbitrary removal, but the Speaker also needs to remain ultimately answerable to the democratic institution that chose them. Article 179's design attempts to balance these competing needs through a three-mode exit framework, an absolute-majority removal threshold, and a dissolution proviso that extends the Speaker's continuity through the interregnum period.

In practice, the most significant feature of Article 179 is the intersection of clause (c)'s removal mechanism with the Tenth Schedule. A Speaker who is simultaneously the ruling majority's nominee and the sole adjudicator of whether members of that majority's opponents have defected creates a structural tension the Constitution did not fully resolve. The Nabam Rebia doctrine — which grew directly from events in the Arunachal Pradesh Assembly in 2015-16 where the removal and disqualification procedures were weaponised against each other — gave the removal notice a tactical dimension the framers could not have foreseen. Filing a removal notice freezes the Speaker's most politically sensitive power. This has made Article 179 one of the most practically important provisions in State legislative constitutional law.

The absolute majority threshold for removal — meaning votes in favour must exceed half of all then members of the Assembly, not merely half of those present — is deliberately high. For an Assembly of 182 members, 92 affirmative votes are required, regardless of how many members participate in the division. This threshold can only be crossed if a substantial number of the ruling party's own members vote against their Speaker — constitutionally conceivable but politically exceptional. The provision thus functions primarily as a deterrent and legitimacy device: the Speaker knows that removal is possible if their conduct is sufficiently egregious to unite a cross-party majority, but also knows that routine partisan displeasure will not result in removal.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (a) — Automatic vacation.** A Speaker or Deputy Speaker who ceases to be a member of the Assembly vacates the presiding office automatically. No separate notification, no Assembly resolution, and no executive order is required — the vacation is instantaneous upon losing membership. This ties the presiding office to democratic mandate: the Speaker governs the Assembly only as long as they remain a member accountable to an electorate. The clause covers disqualification, resignation from membership, death, and any other reason for ceasing to be a member.

**Clause (b) — Voluntary resignation.** The Speaker resigns by writing under hand addressed to the Deputy Speaker; the Deputy Speaker resigns by writing to the Speaker. This cross-addressed structure ensures formal notice to the remaining presiding officer, preventing a quiet or informal exit. The resignation takes effect upon receipt and acceptance — a question of some practical importance when the Speaker tenders a resignation but then seeks to retract it before it is processed. The Subhash Desai litigation in 2022-23 involved precisely this question: whether a Speaker's resignation was effective once tendered, or could be withdrawn before being acted upon by the Assembly.

**Clause (c) — Removal by absolute majority.** The removal mechanism requires: (i) written notice of at least fourteen days of the intention to move a resolution; and (ii) passage of the resolution by a majority of all the then members of the Assembly. "All the then members" is an absolute majority of the Assembly's current total — not of those present and voting, and not of the full maximum strength of the Assembly. If vacancies have reduced the Assembly's membership from 182 to 175 members, the majority is calculated on 175. The fourteen-day notice period gives the Speaker time to respond to the charges being made against them and forces the mover to publicly commit — with reputational consequences if the removal fails.

**The dissolution proviso.** When the Assembly is dissolved, all members — including the Speaker as a member — technically cease to be members, which would normally trigger clause (a)'s automatic vacation. The proviso carves out an exception: the Speaker does not vacate the office upon dissolution but continues until immediately before the first meeting of the new Assembly. This creates a bounded continuity — present during the period between dissolution and the new House's first sitting, terminated the instant the new House meets. The proviso applies only to the Speaker, not the Deputy Speaker, who vacates upon dissolution along with other members and whose office in the new Assembly must be filled under Article 178 after the new Speaker is chosen.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Article 179 was adopted as the State-level counterpart of the corresponding provision for the Lok Sabha with relatively little independent debate. The framers' principal concerns about the presiding office were addressed in the central provisions. When the State legislative chapter was drawn up, the mirroring approach was adopted: the substantive design choices about removal thresholds, dissolution continuity, and resignation procedures had already been made at the Union level, and the State provisions followed the same pattern.

One aspect that attracted some discussion was the fourteen-day notice requirement. The framers recognised that a removal proceeding against the Speaker would inevitably be a highly political event, and they wanted both to ensure that the Speaker had adequate time to respond and to prevent the removal proceedings from being launched as a last-minute tactical move without prior notice to the House. The absolute majority threshold was equally deliberate: a simple majority removal process would have made the Speaker's tenure as precarious as any ordinary motion, undermining the institutional independence the framers wanted the presiding office to have.

## The Arunachal Pradesh crisis of 2015-16

Article 179 and its relationship with the Tenth Schedule came to its most severe test in the Arunachal Pradesh Assembly crisis of 2015-16. A notice of motion for removal of the Speaker under the equivalent of clause (c) was submitted by a group of ruling party members who were also facing disqualification petitions before the Speaker. While the removal notice was pending, the Speaker proceeded to advance the date of the Assembly's budget session by executive order and also acted under the Tenth Schedule to disqualify the members who had moved the removal notice.

The Supreme Court in Nabam Rebia held that the Speaker could not exercise Tenth Schedule disqualification powers while a removal notice against the Speaker was pending. The reasoning was structural: Article 179(c)'s removal mechanism creates a constitutional accountability relationship between the Speaker and the members of the Assembly. If the Speaker can disqualify the members who moved the removal notice — thereby changing the Assembly's composition and potentially the arithmetic of the removal vote — the accountability mechanism is corrupted. The Speaker cannot be the judge in proceedings that will determine the composition of the jury on their own removal.

The Arunachal Pradesh crisis also had a significant gubernatorial dimension: the Governor had advanced the Assembly session date on advice that was disputed, and the Court ultimately restored the earlier session date. The interaction between Article 179's removal mechanism, the Tenth Schedule's disqualification procedure, the Governor's role under Articles 174 and 175, and the Council of Ministers' authority under Article 163 made this one of the most legally complex constitutional crises in the States since 1993.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The five-judge Constitution Bench held that a Speaker against whom a notice for removal under Article 179(c) has been submitted cannot exercise the power to disqualify members under the Tenth Schedule until the removal motion is disposed of. The Court's reasoning rested on the constitutional scheme of accountability — the Speaker cannot use quasi-judicial power over the House's composition to affect the membership-composition of the removal vote. The judgment is the leading authority on the interaction between Article 179 and the Tenth Schedule, and applies to all State Legislative Assemblies by virtue of the constitutional parallel between Article 179 and Article 94.

**Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023) 5 SCC 501** — The Supreme Court's extended engagement with the Maharashtra political crisis traversed the operation of Article 179(b) on the resignation mechanism. The Court examined whether the Speaker's written resignation was effective immediately upon its execution or required acceptance or formal processing. The judgment clarified that the Speaker's tenure does not end merely because a resignation letter has been written — the formal constitutional sequence matters, and actions taken by a person who still lawfully holds the Speaker's office pending resignation cannot be retrospectively voided simply because the resignation was intended.

**Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2019) 17 SCC 1** — While primarily decided on the Tenth Schedule's scope, the Supreme Court engaged with Article 179's dissolution proviso in the context of an Assembly that had a pending election. The Court confirmed that the Speaker's continuation until just before the new Assembly's first meeting means the Speaker retains the power to make disqualification orders during the interregnum, though the political consequences of such orders before an election are constitutionally complex.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020) 2 SCC 428** — The Constitution Bench held that the Speaker's obligation to decide disqualification petitions within a reasonable time is judicially enforceable. This obligation attaches to whoever holds the Article 179 office — the Speaker, or the person performing the Speaker's duties under Article 180. The judgment confirmed that the Speaker's tenure protection under Article 179 does not insulate the office from judicial direction on how constitutional functions are discharged.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992) 1 SCC 309** — The Constitution Bench upheld the validity of the Tenth Schedule and the Speaker's role as first-instance adjudicator of disqualification petitions, noting that the removal mechanism in Article 179 (and its Union counterpart Article 94) was a structural safeguard that made the Speaker a suitable constitutional adjudicator. The existence of the removal procedure was cited in support of treating the Speaker as institutionally accountable rather than entrenched.

## Current significance

Article 179 sits at the centre of the most recurring constitutional drama in State legislative politics. The combination of the Nabam Rebia doctrine, the tactical possibilities of a removal notice, and the structural conflict in the Speaker's dual role as ruling-majority's nominee and quasi-judicial anti-defection adjudicator has made the provision a permanent feature of political crisis management in States.

The dissolution proviso has acquired new relevance as floor-test disputes and dissolution controversies have multiplied. When a government loses its majority and the Governor is considering whether to recommend dissolution, the Speaker's continuation through dissolution — and the question of whether the Speaker can take consequential Tenth Schedule decisions in the period between dissolution and the new House's first meeting — has created litigation in multiple States. Courts have generally confirmed that the Speaker retains the power to act during the dissolution interregnum, though with the same Article 179-derived constraints.

Reform proposals aimed at reducing the strategic manipulation of the Article 179 removal framework have included transferring Tenth Schedule jurisdiction to the Election Commission or an independent tribunal. This would eliminate the Nabam Rebia dynamic by removing the Speaker's disqualification powers — and thereby the tactical value of a removal notice — but would require a constitutional amendment. Until such reform is adopted, Article 179 and its interaction with the Tenth Schedule will continue to generate litigation at every moment of political instability in the States.
