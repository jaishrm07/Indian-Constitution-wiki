## Why it matters

The Governor's oath is the Constitution's most direct statement of what the office is for. Before any power is exercised — before a single Bill is signed, a session summoned, or ministerial advice accepted or refused — the incoming Governor stands before the Chief Justice of the High Court and makes a public, subscribed commitment that is recorded in writing. The act is simultaneously ceremonial and legally consequential: the commitment to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution and the law" is the standard against which Governor conduct is measured in every subsequent dispute about whether the Governor has acted within constitutional bounds.

The choice of the Chief Justice as the administering authority is the provision's most constitutionally revealing feature. The President takes the oath under Article 60 before the Chief Justice of India; the Chief Minister takes the oath administered by the Governor. The Governor alone — among the major constitutional authorities — takes the oath before the State's highest judicial officer. This places the Governor's entry into office within the judicial sphere rather than the executive chain of command, signalling that accountability runs to the Constitution and the people of the State rather than to the Union government that appointed the Governor. Critics who argue that Governors have acted as agents of the Centre during political crises at the State level consistently invoke this design choice as evidence that such conduct is constitutionally improper.

The oath's final commitment — to devote oneself to the "service and well-being of the people of the State" rather than to the Union or to the party in power at the Centre — is equally important. It places the Governor in a relationship of constitutional obligation with the inhabitants of the State governed, and has been used by constitutional scholars to argue that a Governor who consistently thwarts the policy agenda of an elected state government has violated the constitutional purpose of the office, even if specific actions are technically within the Governor's formal powers.

## Reading the provision

**The persons bound.** The obligation falls on "every Governor and every person discharging the functions of the Governor." The scope is deliberately broader than the Governor formally appointed under Article 155. It encompasses a person designated under Article 160 to discharge gubernatorial functions in an unspecified contingency, and theoretically the Chief Justice of the High Court if the President has made no other arrangement under Article 160 and an urgent gubernatorial function must be performed. Each such person must take the oath before performing any gubernatorial act — the constitutional obligation is triggered by the functional role, not the formal designation.

**Administering authority — the Chief Justice.** The oath is administered by the Chief Justice of the High Court exercising jurisdiction in relation to the State. If the Chief Justice is absent, the senior-most available judge of that Court steps in. The Constitution does not permit the oath to be administered by any executive authority — the Union Home Minister, the President's Secretary, or the outgoing Governor — so even in practical contingencies, the judicial character of the administering authority is preserved. This stands in deliberate contrast to the Governor's own role in administering oaths to the Chief Minister and Council of Ministers under Article 164.

**The three-part commitment.** The prescribed text contains three distinct obligations. First, faithful execution of the office — the Governor will discharge the constitutional functions diligently, acting on ministerial advice where required and exercising discretion where constitutionally permitted, but not abdicating the office by prolonged inaction. Second, preservation, protection, and defence of the Constitution and the law — the same formulation as the Presidential oath under Article 60, treating the Governor as a constitutional guardian at the State level rather than a mere executive functionary. Third, devotion to the service and well-being of the people of the State — an obligation that runs to the people directly, not mediated through any political party or Union government instruction.

**Oath or affirmation.** The text permits either a religious oath ("swear in the name of God") or a secular affirmation ("solemnly affirm"), reflecting the constitutional scheme of personal liberty in matters of religion and belief. The Governor subscribes the oath or affirmation in writing — "make and subscribe" — creating a documentary record.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The oath provision attracted relatively little debate in the Constituent Assembly, which treated it as a natural companion to the Governor's appointment and conditions of office rather than a major point of constitutional design. The framers' substantive debates about the Governor focused on whether the office should exist at all given the colonial associations of the Governor-General's administration. When Article 159 was considered, the Assembly's attention was already largely settled on the structure of gubernatorial functions rather than on the mechanics of entry into office.

However, the choice of the Chief Justice rather than the President as administering authority was a deliberate constitutional statement. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar's explanations of the Governor's role as a constitutional guardian rather than a Union agent are relevant to understanding why the oath is administered by the State's highest judicial officer. The framers regarded the High Court Chief Justice as the natural custodian of constitutional standards at the State level, and the Administrator of Oaths Rules of 1971 subsequently confirmed that the Chief Justice administers the Governor's oath as a constitutional function of the court, not as a personal favour to the executive.

## Key cases

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court, invalidating the Bihar Assembly dissolution, emphasised that a Governor who has taken the oath under Article 159 is personally and constitutionally bound to act in accordance with it. The majority held that a Governor who makes a report to the President recommending dissolution on factually incorrect or politically motivated grounds commits a constitutional wrong, not merely a political error, because the article 159 commitment to "preserve, protect and defend the Constitution" is an active obligation requiring good faith in the exercise of every gubernatorial function.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The nine-judge bench's foundational analysis of gubernatorial obligations under President's Rule proceedings treated the oath as the normative baseline. A Governor who reports to the President that constitutional government cannot be carried on in accordance with the Constitution is making a representation that engages the Article 159 commitment — if the report is demonstrably false or politically motivated, it violates the oath obligation, even if no separate penal consequence flows from the violation. The judgment created a framework for subjecting gubernatorial conduct to constitutional scrutiny measured against the oath standard.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The Constitution Bench's analysis of gubernatorial action in the Arunachal Pradesh political crisis treated the oath text as an indicator of the Governor's primary obligation to the Constitution. The Court held that the Governor's action in advancing the date of a budget session without ministerial advice was inconsistent with the constitutional undertaking to defend the Constitution, confirming that Article 159 operates as an active constraint on gubernatorial conduct rather than a one-time threshold formality.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — The Constitution Bench's foundational ruling that the Governor acts on ministerial advice in non-discretionary matters rests on the same constitutional foundation as the Article 159 oath. The Court's reasoning that the Governor is not a free agent but a constitutional authority bound by the Constitution's scheme of responsible government is consistent with the oath's structure: a Governor who pledged to "faithfully execute the office" cannot claim a general freedom to act contrary to the constitutional framework of cabinet government that the oath implies.

**B.P. Singhal v. Union of India (2010) 6 SCC 331** — The Supreme Court, reviewing the removal of a Governor before the expiry of the five-year term, noted that the oath taken under Article 159 creates a constitutional relationship between the Governor and the State that is not severable by executive displeasure alone. The judgment confirmed that Governors cannot be removed capriciously, and the oath obligation to the people of the State was part of the constitutional relationship that afforded the Governor a degree of institutional protection against pure executive discretion.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. Governor of Tamil Nadu (2023)** — The Supreme Court's intervention in the Tamil Nadu Bills withholding controversy cited the Governor's constitutional obligations — rooted in the Article 159 oath — as the framework for examining whether prolonged refusal to grant assent to Bills passed by an elected legislature was constitutionally permissible. The Court's direction to the Governor to act on the pending Bills within a fixed period effectively held that the oath obligation of faithful execution required active discharge of constitutional functions, not passive deferral.

## Current significance

Article 159 has moved from ceremonial text to active constitutional vocabulary in the recurring confrontations between Governors and elected state governments since 2017. In Kerala, Tamil Nadu, West Bengal, Telangana, and Punjab, Governors have withheld assent to Bills, delayed summoning sessions, delivered addresses that departed from the ministry-approved text, and made public statements that aligned with Central government positions. Opposition parties and constitutional scholars in each case have invoked the Article 159 oath — specifically the duties of faithful execution and service to the people of the State — as the standard against which this conduct should be measured.

The Supreme Court's increasing willingness to scrutinise gubernatorial action under Articles 163 and 200 has brought the oath obligation implicitly into judicial review. When the Court in the Tamil Nadu Bills case and the Punjab Governor's session-summoning dispute examined whether the Governor had acted constitutionally, the underlying normative framework was the obligation assumed under Article 159: a Governor who pledged to serve the people of the State cannot indefinitely obstruct the legislative program of those people's elected representatives without constitutional justification. The oath text, with its explicit reference to "the people of the State" rather than to the Union or the appointing authority, has become a resource in this constitutional argument.

Whether the oath creates independently enforceable legal obligations beyond the general framework of constitutional review is still debated. No Governor has been successfully held in contempt for oath violation, and the oath's enforcement is mediated through the normal tools of constitutional review — mandamus for inaction, judicial review of specific acts — rather than through any special oath-breach proceeding. But as an interpretive standard shaping what "constitutional exercise of function" means, the Article 159 commitment remains one of the most practically relevant provisions in the Governor's Part VI framework.
