## Why it matters

Part III of the Constitution guarantees fundamental rights as national, uniform entitlements — they protect every person within the territory of India regardless of which state they are in. This national character would be undermined if state legislatures could independently set the rules for enforcing, restricting, or punishing violations of these rights. A state that prescribes weak penalties for the practice of untouchability would effectively create a zone where Article 17's prohibition has reduced constitutional force. A state that sets different conditions for restricting armed forces' rights under Article 33 would fragment the operational framework for national security forces that are constitutionally federal in character.

Article 35 addresses this structural danger by drawing a hard line of exclusive parliamentary competence at the close of Part III. Its placement at the end of the fundamental rights chapter is deliberate — it is the constitutional coda that ensures the enforcement architecture matches the rights' national character. The matters reserved exclusively for Parliament include residence requirements for state public employment (Article 16(3)), regulation of the Supreme Court's writ jurisdiction (Article 32(3)), modification of armed and security forces' rights (Article 33), and indemnification for martial law acts (Article 34). All of these cross state lines — writ jurisdiction, armed forces deployment, and national security require uniform regulation. State legislation in these areas would produce not just inconsistency but constitutional incoherence.

The provision also imposes the Constitution's most direct legislative duty on Parliament. Clause (a)(ii) does not merely empower Parliament to prescribe punishments for constitutional offences — it commands Parliament to do so "as soon as may be." This language creates an enforceable obligation on the legislature, distinct from the aspirational language of directive principles. The most consequential target of this duty is untouchability under Article 17, which the Constitution declares an offence but for which it prescribes no punishment. Parliament could not simply declare untouchability an offence and leave the matter there — Article 35 compels it to legislate criminal sanctions. The delay before Parliament did so in 1955 was itself a constitutional shortcoming.

## Reading the provision

Article 35 works through three interlocking rules that operate across its two clauses.

**The exclusion rule** — Clause (a)(i) removes from state legislatures any power to legislate on matters reserved by Articles 16(3), 32(3), 33, and 34. These are not matters on which states have concurrent power with Parliament (as in the Concurrent List under the Seventh Schedule). They are exclusively parliamentary — a state law purporting to restrict armed forces personnel's right to approach courts, or to set residence qualifications for state employment, would be void for want of legislative competence, regardless of whether it conflicted with any specific central law. The exclusion is absolute.

Article 16(3) permits Parliament to prescribe residence qualifications for appointments to state government posts in a particular State or Union Territory — allowing Parliament to permit states to give preference to locals for certain jobs. If states could legislate this directly, the result would be uncontrolled "sons-of-the-soil" legislation. By restricting this power to Parliament, Article 35 ensures that regional employment preferences remain subject to national oversight.

Article 32(3) permits Parliament to confer on other courts the power to issue directions or writs for enforcing fundamental rights — potentially extending writ jurisdiction beyond the Supreme Court. If states could legislate on this, the fundamental structure of fundamental rights enforcement could be altered state by state. Parliament alone may authorise High Courts to exercise the equivalent of Article 32 jurisdiction.

Article 33 permits Parliament to modify the application of Part III rights to armed and security forces — restricting their rights to move courts, form associations, or demand full procedural protections in the interests of discipline and national security. If states could modify these rights for state police forces independently, the result would be a patchwork of different rights regimes for different forces. Parliament alone sets these modifications, ensuring that armed forces personnel across India operate under consistent rights constraints.

Article 34 permits Parliament to indemnify persons for acts done under martial law. As discussed in relation to Article 34 itself, this is exclusively parliamentary — no state could grant such indemnification.

**The punishment rule** — Clause (a)(ii) requires Parliament — and only Parliament — to prescribe punishments for acts declared to be offences under Part III. The constitutional text identifies two primary offences: the practice of untouchability under Article 17 and traffic in human beings or forced labour under Article 23. The Constitution declares these offences but sets no penalties, relying on Parliament to supply them.

Parliament's obligation is not permissive but mandatory — "Parliament shall, as soon as may be after the commencement of this Constitution, make laws for prescribing punishment." The phrase "as soon as may be" has been interpreted to mean promptly, in constitutional time, not indefinitely deferred. The Untouchability (Offences) Act 1955 — enacted five years after the Constitution came into force — discharged this obligation for Article 17, albeit belatedly. States cannot create their own penal regimes for practising untouchability; they can only enforce the Parliament-enacted framework.

The Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976 and the Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act 1956 perform the equivalent function for Article 23's prohibition of traffic in human beings and forced labour, providing the criminal framework that Article 35 requires Parliament to legislate.

**The continuity rule** — Clause (b) keeps pre-Constitution laws on the reserved matters in force until Parliament replaces them. This prevented a legislative vacuum on 26 January 1950. Colonial-era laws governing security force service conditions, residency qualifications for government employment, and martial law procedures continued in force under Article 35(b) even after the Constitution took effect, giving Parliament the time to address each area systematically. The Explanation ties "law in force" to the definition in Article 372, which covers not just statutes but also ordinances, orders, bye-laws, rules, and regulations — ensuring comprehensive continuity across the full range of sub-legislative instruments.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debate on what became Article 35 was technically focused but constitutionally consequential. The drafting committee understood that placing punishments for fundamental rights offences in the directive principles — aspirational, non-justiciable — would have weakened the rights themselves. By putting the punishment obligation in Part III itself, and making Parliament responsible, the framers created a harder duty than if they had merely expected Parliament to legislate as a policy matter.

Dr Ambedkar drew particular attention to the untouchability provision. The practice of untouchability was a fundamental rights violation (Article 17), not merely a social wrong — and the Constitution's declaration of it as an offence required a criminal sanction to have real effect. The "as soon as may be" language was included over objections that it gave Parliament too little flexibility, with the drafting committee's position being that some urgency was appropriate given the gravity of the constitutional offence.

## Key cases

**Bidi Supply Co v Union of India AIR 1956 SC 479** — An early Supreme Court decision on Article 35(b). The Court held that pre-Constitution laws on matters within the reserved categories continue in force until Parliament amends or repeals them, and must be read harmoniously with the fundamental rights provisions they implement. This established the interpretive baseline for Article 35(b)'s transitional function and confirmed that the continuity rule does not preserve laws that are themselves inconsistent with fundamental rights.

**State of Gujarat v Vora Fiddali Badruddin Mithibarwala AIR 1964 SC 1043** — The Supreme Court held that Article 35(a)(ii) prevents states from creating separate punishment regimes for acts falling within the scope of Part III's constitutional offences. The Court confirmed that enforcement standards for untouchability and trafficking must be set nationally by Parliament, not fragmented by state-by-state variations in criminal sanctions. The ruling is the leading authority on the exclusive character of Parliament's punishment-prescribing power under Article 35.

**Kartar Singh v State of Punjab (1994) 3 SCC 569** — The Constitution Bench addressed the exclusive parliamentary domain over Part III offences and security-force powers in the context of anti-terrorism legislation. The Court confirmed that Parliament's power under Article 35, read with Article 33, to legislate on the rights of armed and security forces is exclusive and that state legislation on the same matters would be unconstitutional. It also observed that courts will scrutinise Parliament's exercise of its Article 35 power to ensure that any modification of Part III rights genuinely serves the protective purposes authorised by Articles 33 and 34.

**Subramanian Swamy v Director, CBI (2014) 8 SCC 682** — The Court addressed Article 35 in the context of Parliament's exclusive power to prescribe punishments for constitutional offences, confirming that the regime of punishments for atrocities against scheduled castes under the SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act flows directly from Parliament's Article 35 mandate and cannot be modified by state legislatures. Any state attempt to dilute these punishments or create parallel regimes would be void.

**Surya Narayan Choudhary v Union of India (1982)** — In a challenge to the residency qualification framework under Article 16(3), the Court confirmed that the power to prescribe such qualifications belongs exclusively to Parliament under Article 35(a)(i), and that state-level administrative instructions purporting to create residency-based employment preferences beyond what Parliament had authorised were constitutionally impermissible.

## Current significance

Article 35 has practical relevance wherever Parliament has been slow to fulfil its exclusive mandate. The Protection of Civil Rights Act 1955 and the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act 1989 are its most significant products. Litigation under both statutes continuously raises questions about enforcement mechanisms, the relationship between central and state law enforcement, and whether state-level schemes supplement or trespass upon the exclusively parliamentary punishment regime. The 2018 Supreme Court judgment in Dr Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v State of Maharashtra, which attempted to modify the procedure for arrests under the Atrocities Act, and the subsequent parliamentary legislative reversal through the 2018 Amendment to restore the original Act, illustrate how politically charged Parliament's Article 35 mandate remains.

The article also frames ongoing debates about service conditions for state police and paramilitary forces. States frequently seek to modify the rights of their armed police units in ways that touch on the right to form associations (Article 19(1)(c)) or the right to move courts (Article 32 as read through Article 35). The boundary between Parliament's exclusive domain under Articles 33 and 35 and the states' residual power over their own police forces is contested in litigation and legislative design. As Parliament enacts laws governing central paramilitary forces, and states simultaneously seek to regulate their own armed police, the exclusive legislative space that Article 35 protects remains a live constitutional frontier.

The "as soon as may be" duty on Parliament — which was considered non-justiciable for decades — has begun to receive judicial attention in the context of other constitutional duties requiring expeditious legislative implementation. Courts have cited this language in Article 35(a)(ii) as evidence that the Constitution imposes enforceable time-bound obligations on Parliament in the fundamental rights domain, a development with potential implications for other areas where Parliament has deferred action on rights-related legislation.
