  When a case is already pending in a subordinate court and involves a substantial question of constitutional interpretation that must be resolved for the case to be decided, the High Court shall withdraw the case to itself. officialText: |-
  228. Transfer of certain cases to High Court.—If the High Court is satisfied that a case pending in a court subordinate to it involves a substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution the determination of which is necessary for the disposal of the case, [it shall withdraw the case and *** may—]

  (a) either dispose of the case itself, or

  (b) determine the said question of law and return the case to the court from which the case has been so withdrawn together with a copy of its judgment on such question, and the said court shall on receipt thereof proceed to dispose of the case in conformity with such judgment.

## Why it matters

Article 228 is the mechanism by which constitutional questions arising in the course of ordinary subordinate court litigation are elevated to the institution with authority to decide them definitively. Without it, a trial court adjudicating a land dispute, a service matter, or a criminal prosecution under a particular law could be left to apply or ignore constitutional arguments without any institutional mechanism compelling the High Court's intervention. The mandatory withdrawal obligation — the article says "shall withdraw," not "may withdraw" — reflects the Constitution's judgment that trial courts are not the appropriate final arbiters of constitutional interpretation.

The two-track outcome structure is equally important. The High Court can retain and decide the entire case when the constitutional issue is so fundamental that returning it would serve no purpose. Alternatively, it can answer only the constitutional question and return the case for completion — preserving the subordinate court's fact-finding function and the procedural record already built there, while centralising the constitutional determination. This flexibility makes Article 228 practical rather than merely theoretical: courts can use it without disrupting years of subordinate court proceedings.

## Reading the provision

Two cumulative conditions must be satisfied before the mandatory withdrawal obligation arises. First, there must be a "substantial question of law as to the interpretation of this Constitution." A question is substantial if it is genuinely open — not already settled by Supreme Court or High Court precedent — and not merely incidentally invoked. If the constitutional question is already authoritatively answered, the subordinate court should simply apply the precedent without withdrawal.

Second, the determination of that question must be "necessary for the disposal of the case." This necessity requirement is a further filter: a substantial constitutional question that the case can be decided without reaching does not trigger Article 228. The constitutional argument must be load-bearing for the outcome, not a background argument raised speculatively.

Both conditions must be satisfied by the High Court before the withdrawal obligation activates. The withdrawal can occur on the High Court's own motion — when the court becomes aware of a constitutional question being decided inconsistently across subordinate courts — or on an application by a party.

Article 228 complements but is distinct from Article 226 and Article 227. Under Article 226, a party approaches the High Court directly to challenge a law or order. Under Article 228, the High Court intervenes in an already-pending subordinate court case. Under Article 227, the High Court exercises general superintendence. Article 228 is the most targeted of the three: specific conditions, mandatory withdrawal, and a defined range of outcomes.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Rajendra Kumar Jain (1981)** — The Rajasthan High Court addressed the threshold for invoking Article 228, holding that the constitutional question must be both substantial and necessary for disposal. The mere incidental presence of a constitutional argument does not suffice; the question must be one without whose determination the case cannot be decided.

**Raj Narain Singh v. Patna Administration Committee (1954)** — An early ruling on the predecessor provision established that Article 228 is not a general jurisdiction-conferring provision but a specific withdrawal mechanism. The High Court must be satisfied on the particular facts of the pending case, not merely convinced in the abstract that a constitutional question exists in the field of law relevant to the dispute.

**Naresh Shridhar Mirajkar v. State of Maharashtra (1966)** — The Supreme Court acknowledged that the High Court acts as the constitutional court of first resort for cases arising within its territorial jurisdiction and that the Article 228 withdrawal power reinforces this institutional role by preventing fragmentation of constitutional interpretation across subordinate courts.

**Tarkeshwar Sio Thakur Jiu v. M/s Jagannath Jew Jiu (1974)** — The Supreme Court clarified that where the High Court answers the constitutional question and returns the case, the subordinate court is bound by that judgment and has no residual authority to re-open the constitutional issue.

## Current significance

Article 228 has practical importance in States where trial courts regularly encounter constitutional challenges to land acquisition laws, reservation policies, and State service rules. Questions about the interpretation of fundamental rights provisions arise frequently in lower court proceedings, and Article 228 ensures that those questions are resolved by an institution with authority to bind co-ordinate courts within its jurisdiction.

Parties use Article 228 strategically as well as defensively. A defendant facing conviction under a law of disputed constitutional validity may move the High Court under Article 228 to have the constitutional question resolved before the trial concludes. A landlord or tenant challenging a rent-control law's constitutional validity mid-trial can seek withdrawal. The High Court on its own motion may act when it becomes aware that a constitutional question is being decided inconsistently across subordinate courts — for example, where different magistrate courts in the same State are applying different readings of a statute's constitutional validity.

The provision thus functions as both a party remedy and an institutional quality-control mechanism for constitutional interpretation at the trial level. Its design — mandatory withdrawal once conditions are met, flexible outcome post-withdrawal, preservation of the subordinate court's fact-finding where possible — makes it an effective and frequently invoked tool for maintaining the coherence of constitutional doctrine at the level where most ordinary Indians first encounter the legal system.
