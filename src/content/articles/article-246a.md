  [246A. Special provision with respect to goods and services tax.—(1)

  Notwithstanding anything contained in articles 246 and 254, Parliament, and, subject to clause (2), the Legislature of every State, have power to make laws with respect to goods and services tax imposed by the Union or by such State.

  (2) Parliament has exclusive power to make laws with respect to goods and services tax where the supply of goods, or of services, or both takes place in the course of inter-State trade or commerce.

## Why it matters

Article 246A is the constitutional heart of India's Goods and Services Tax. Before 2016, the Union List and State List divided taxing power so firmly that a unified national consumption tax was impossible: Parliament could not tax services sold by intra-State traders, and States could not tax inter-State sales or services at all. A constitutional amendment was the only route to creating a single, destination-based consumption tax that would operate across the entire territory of India. Article 246A — inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act 2016 — provides that authorisation. It is one of the most significant fiscal re-engineering exercises in the Constitution's history, converting what had been an exclusive and divided tax structure into a concurrent one while carving out inter-State supply for Parliament alone.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) contains the core innovation. The opening "notwithstanding anything contained in articles 246 and 254" is a deliberate override of both the Seventh Schedule's list-based allocation of legislative competence and the repugnancy rule in Article 254 that normally resolves conflicts between Union and State legislation. This means that for GST, neither Parliament's exclusive domain over Union List subjects nor the State legislature's exclusive domain over State List subjects blocks concurrent operation. Both Parliament and every State legislature have simultaneous power to enact GST law on their respective supply of goods and services.

Clause (2) then carves an exclusive parliamentary domain from that concurrent field: where the supply of goods or services takes place in the course of inter-State trade or commerce, only Parliament may legislate. This is implemented through the Integrated Goods and Services Tax (IGST) Act 2017, under which Parliament taxes inter-State supply and the revenue is then apportioned between the origin and destination states through the GST Council mechanism.

The Explanation ties the commencement of Article 246A to a recommendation of the GST Council under Article 279A(5), ensuring that the technical readiness of the Council preceded the constitutional provisions taking effect. This gave the cooperative federal body — itself a constitutional novelty — a role in determining when the new legislative architecture would activate.

## GST Council and cooperative federalism

Article 246A cannot be understood in isolation from Article 279A, which creates the GST Council: a body comprising the Union Finance Minister as chair, the Union Minister of State for Finance, and the Finance Ministers of all States. The Council makes recommendations on tax rates, exemptions, threshold limits, model GST laws, and dispute resolution. While Council decisions are technically recommendatory, the unanimity-seeking voting formula — which gives the Centre a weighted vote of one-third and States a collective two-thirds — creates a practical requirement for Centre-State consensus before any GST rate change takes effect. This is a structural departure from the classical model of parliamentary sovereignty, replacing unilateral Central legislation on fiscal matters with a negotiated inter-governmental process.

The political economy of this design reflects a bargain: States surrendered their exclusive tax bases (entry tax, octroi, State VAT, entertainment tax) and Parliament surrendered its exclusive power over Central excise and service tax in exchange for a share of a larger, more efficient common tax base. The bargain was underpinned by a constitutional guarantee of revenue compensation for five years, which the Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act 2016 arranged through Article 18 of the IGST Act rather than a freestanding constitutional provision.

## Key cases

**Mohit Minerals Pvt Ltd v. Union of India (2022) 10 SCC 700** — The Supreme Court held that the GST Council's recommendations are recommendatory and not binding on States, confirming that the legislative sovereignty of State legislatures under Article 246A is not displaced by Council decisions. The judgment rejected the argument that once the Council recommends a rate or policy, Parliament and State legislatures are constitutionally obliged to enact it, affirming that the federal architecture created by Article 246A preserves State legislative autonomy even within the cooperative framework.

**Union of India v. Filco Trade Centre Pvt Ltd (2022)** — The Supreme Court examined the transitional provisions under the GST regime, holding that the shift from the VAT and Central excise regimes to the GST regime engaged Article 246A's allocation of power and that neither Parliament nor the States could, even through the GST Council, exercise legislative power in excess of what Article 246A and Article 269A authorise.

**Apex Laboratories Pvt Ltd v. Deputy Commissioner of Income Tax (2022)** — While primarily a direct tax case, the Supreme Court's observations on the relationship between Parliament's fiscal legislation and State fiscal powers acknowledged that Article 246A had fundamentally altered the concurrent nature of consumption-tax legislation and that this had implications for how the "enumerated list" in the Seventh Schedule was to be read after the 101st Amendment.

## Current significance

Article 246A's practical stakes are enormous: GST is India's largest single source of tax revenue, and the constitutional allocation it establishes determines fiscal autonomy for every State. Ongoing tensions include the question of whether petroleum products should be brought into GST (which requires a Council recommendation to trigger Article 246A coverage), the treatment of compensation cess after the expiry of the compensation period, and disputes about whether the IGST apportionment mechanism adequately compensates origin States for the shift to a destination-based regime. The article also raises unresolved questions about the basic structure doctrine: whether the cooperative federalism model it creates, by making State fiscal legislation practically dependent on Council consensus, is consistent with the federal features that Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973) 4 SCC 225 held to be unamendable components of the Constitution's basic structure.
