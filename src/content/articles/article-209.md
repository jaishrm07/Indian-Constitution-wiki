Regulation by law of procedure in the Legislature of the State in relation to financial business.—The Legislature of a State may, for the purpose of the timely completion of financial business, regulate by law the procedure of, and the conduct of business in, the House or Houses of the Legislature of the State in relation to any financial matter or to any Bill for the appropriation of moneys out of the Consolidated Fund of the State, and, if and so far as any provision of any law so made is inconsistent with any rule made by the House or either House of the Legislature of the State under clause (1) of article 208 or with any rule or standing order having effect in relation to the Legislature of the State under clause (2) of that article, such provision shall prevail.

## What this Article does

Article 209 allows the State Legislature to enact a law regulating procedure and the conduct of business in relation to financial matters and Appropriation Bills, specifically to ensure the timely completion of financial business. Where any such law conflicts with the rules made by the legislature under Article 208(1) or with standing orders that have effect under Article 208(2), the law made under Article 209 prevails. This provision creates a constitutional override mechanism enabling the State to ensure that budget passage is not blocked by procedural impasse. It is the State counterpart of Article 119.

The rationale is essentially practical: the State's financial business — the passage of the annual budget, supplementary demands, and the Appropriation Bill — must be completed before the start of the new financial year, or the government cannot spend money lawfully. If procedural rules allow the House to obstruct this process indefinitely, governance is paralysed. Article 209 allows the legislature to enact a law that can override such obstructive rules, ensuring that financial business reaches its constitutional conclusion.

## The hierarchy of norms under Article 209

Article 209 creates a clear hierarchy when its domain is activated. At the apex is the Constitution itself, which sets the outer limits within which all three layers must operate. Below that is a law made under Article 209, which prevails over inconsistent rules. Below that are the procedural rules made under Article 208(1) and the standing orders with continuing effect under Article 208(2). This hierarchy is unusual in that it allows a law enacted by the same body whose internal rules it overrides: the State Legislature can effectively override its own procedural rules by enacting legislation, but only in the specific domain of financial business and only for the purpose of timely completion.

The phrase "for the purpose of the timely completion of financial business" is a constitutional purpose test. A law enacted under Article 209 must demonstrably serve this purpose; it cannot be used to override procedural rules for other reasons. Courts examining the validity of an Article 209 law would therefore ask whether the law is genuinely connected to ensuring timely financial business, not merely whether it formally amends a procedural rule.

## Key cases

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1964 (Keshav Singh)** — The Supreme Court's treatment of the relationship between legislative rules and statutory law in the context of legislative procedure is directly relevant to Article 209's hierarchy of norms: a law made under Article 209 overrides conflicting rules, but both operate within the outer limits set by the Constitution itself.

**State of Punjab v Satya Pal (1969)** — The Punjab and Haryana High Court considered the scope of State legislation governing legislative procedure in the context of financial business, and the ruling informs the understanding of how Article 209 law — which expressly prevails over inconsistent legislative rules — operates in practice.

**In re: Article 31C of the Constitution (Kesavananda Bharati aftermath)** — Observations in subsequent judgments about the supremacy of constitutional provisions over legislative rules, and the principle that legislative procedural laws are themselves subject to constitutional review, frame the interpretive context for Article 209, which must itself remain within the constitutional bounds set by Articles 202 to 207.

## Current significance

Article 209 provides the constitutional basis for State laws that impose time limits on budget debates, prescribe the order of financial business, and provide for the automatic passing of Appropriation Bills if the legislature fails to complete consideration within a specified period. Such laws are a practical response to the risk of governance paralysis if financial business is not completed before the start of the financial year. The provision also has federal significance: the State Legislature's power to regulate its own financial procedure through law is an expression of legislative autonomy within its domain.

Questions about whether Article 209 laws can be used to curtail opposition debate rights in relation to budget estimates — a politically sensitive matter — have arisen in states where governments have enacted laws prescribing very short time limits for debate. Courts have generally deferred to the legislature's judgment about what constitutes "timely completion," but the scope for abuse of the Article 209 power to curtail minority participation in financial scrutiny has not been fully examined. Comparative analysis with Article 119 at the Union level, where similar laws apply to Parliament's financial business, suggests that the constitutional framework is broadly functional but would benefit from clearer procedural safeguards for minority voice.

Article 209 has also been invoked in the context of bicameral states, where disagreements between the Legislative Assembly and the Legislative Council on budget-related bills can threaten timely passage. In states where the Council has obstructed or delayed financial legislation, Article 209 laws that modify procedure to ensure passage have been constitutionally defended on the ground that the timely completion purpose is especially compelling when the entire budget cycle is at risk. The interaction between Article 209 and the inter-House relationship provisions in Articles 197 to 199 — particularly the special procedure for money bills — has generated specific procedural questions that each bicameral state has had to resolve through its rules and legislation.

The framers chose to make the override mechanism available by statute rather than requiring a constitutional amendment each time a State wished to adjust its financial procedure, reflecting a pragmatic view that financial governance is too important to be left to the rigidities of constitutional text alone. The State's law-making power under Article 209 is therefore a constitutionally sanctioned form of legislative self-regulation, available only in the specific domain of financial business and exercisable only for the purpose of timely completion — a narrow but constitutionally important grant of authority that acknowledges the practical realities of parliamentary financial management.
