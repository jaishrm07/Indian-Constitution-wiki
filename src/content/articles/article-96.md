  Article 96 constitutionalises the rule against bias in the Lok Sabha's most consequential internal proceeding — when a motion to remove the Speaker or Deputy Speaker is before the House, the affected officer must step aside and may not preside. officialText: |-
  96. The Speaker or the Deputy Speaker not to preside while a resolution for his removal from office is under consideration.—(1) At any sitting of the House of the People, while any resolution for the removal of the Speaker from his office is under consideration, the Speaker, or while any resolution for the removal of the Deputy Speaker from his office is under consideration, the Deputy Speaker, shall not, though he is present, preside, and the provisions of clause (2) of article 95 shall apply in relation to every such sitting as they apply in relation to a sitting from which the Speaker, or, as the case may be, the Deputy Speaker, is absent.

  (2) The Speaker shall have the right to speak in, and otherwise to take part in the proceedings of, the House of the People while any resolution for his removal from office is under consideration in the House and shall, notwithstanding anything in article 100, be entitled to vote only in the first instance on such resolution or on any other matter during such proceedings but not in the case of an equality of votes.

## Why it matters

Article 96 embeds the natural-justice principle nemo judex in causa sua — no person shall be a judge in their own cause — into the specific constitutional context of Speaker and Deputy Speaker removal. The provision is more than procedural courtesy; it is a constitutional mandate that prevents a presiding officer from using control over parliamentary procedure to defeat the accountability mechanism to which they are personally subject. The Speaker's ordinary powers over who speaks, how long they speak, whether amendments are admitted, and whether proceedings are adjourned are so extensive that an unrecused Speaker could effectively prevent removal proceedings from being conducted at all. Article 96 removes that possibility by mandating recusal as an automatic constitutional consequence of a removal resolution being under consideration.

The article has significance beyond its immediate operational purpose. It is one of the foundations on which the Supreme Court in Nabam Rebia built the doctrine that a presiding officer facing removal cannot exercise any discretionary power that could affect the composition of the House and thereby the outcome of the removal vote. This extension has made Article 96 one of the most practically consequential constitutional provisions in contemporary parliamentary politics, because it enables tactical use of removal notices to suspend the Speaker's Tenth Schedule disqualification powers at critical moments.

The provision also reveals the Constitution's nuanced approach to the Speaker's dual role. The Speaker is simultaneously a constitutional presiding officer — expected to be impartial — and an elected member of the Lok Sabha who retains a political identity. Article 96 acknowledges both dimensions: the Speaker must step aside as presiding officer (because the impartiality of the chair cannot be maintained during personal removal proceedings), but retains a vote as a member (because the Speaker does not forfeit membership rights simply by facing removal).

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) applies the recusal rule to both the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker. When a removal resolution is under consideration against either officer, they "shall not, though he is present, preside." The obligation is mandatory and self-executing — it does not depend on anyone's request, the presiding officer's voluntary decision, or a ruling of the House. The affected officer must step aside immediately when the removal resolution comes under consideration, and the Article 95(2) ordinary-absence substitution mechanism fills the presiding gap automatically.

Article 95(2)'s substitution mechanism — which clause (1) activates — directs that a member determined by the rules or by the House shall act as Speaker. In practice, this means a member of the panel of chairpersons, nominated by the Speaker in advance under the Rules of Procedure, takes the chair. The constitutional design ensures there is always a substitute available even before a removal proceeding is anticipated, so that the House does not need to improvise when the Speaker is required to step aside.

Clause (2) specifies the Speaker's residual rights during removal proceedings with precision. The Speaker retains the right to speak and otherwise participate — the same right that every member has, and indeed broader in practice since the Speaker's contribution to the debate on their own removal carries natural weight. But the voting right is calibrated differently from the ordinary position under Article 100: the Speaker gets a first-instance vote on the removal resolution and on any other matter during the proceedings, but not a casting vote in the case of a tie.

The difference from the ordinary Article 100 rule is deliberate and significant. Under Article 100, the Speaker has no first-instance vote — the Speaker votes only to resolve a tie. Under Article 96, this is reversed: the Speaker gets the ordinary member's first-instance vote but loses the casting vote in a tie. This preserves the Speaker's status as a voter on the question of their own removal — denying the vote entirely would effectively disenfranchise an elected member — while preventing the Speaker from casting a double vote that could determine the outcome of the removal vote in their favour.

## The conflict-of-interest principle

The rule that a presiding officer must not preside over proceedings directed against themselves is an application of the natural-justice maxim nemo judex in causa sua. The Constitution codifies it explicitly rather than leaving it to convention, ensuring that there is no ambiguity when a removal notice is admitted. The companion Article 94 sets out the conditions for removal — a fourteen-day notice and a resolution passed by a majority of all then members of the House — making the removal procedure a serious and infrequent event, which is precisely why the Article 96 constraint matters when it is triggered.

The fourteen-day notice requirement in Article 94 has an important interaction with Article 96. The constraint on the Speaker begins when the removal resolution is "under consideration" — not when the notice is given. This means that during the fourteen-day notice period, before the formal resolution is actually put before the House for consideration, Article 96 does not technically apply in its literal terms. However, the Nabam Rebia doctrine fills this gap by holding that the constraint on discretionary powers arises earlier, from the moment the notice is admitted.

## The Somnath Chatterjee controversy

The most prominent recent episode involving Article 96's underlying principles at the national level was the position of Speaker Somnath Chatterjee during the 2008 confidence motion crisis. The Left parties, whose support had sustained the UPA government, withdrew support over the nuclear deal, triggering a confidence vote. Somnath Chatterjee, elected as Speaker from the Left-supported CPI(M), refused to resign his Speakership when directed by his party to do so. The CPI(M) ultimately expelled him from the party.

The episode raised the question of whether a Speaker who has been expelled from their party, or whose party no longer supports the government, can continue to preside over a confidence vote that directly determines the government's survival. While Article 96 does not address this scenario — it deals only with the Speaker's own removal, not with political complications arising from party loyalty — the episode illustrated the inherent tension between the Speaker's obligation of impartiality and the partisan identity that brought them to the chair. Somnath Chatterjee's position that the Speakership required him to transcend party discipline during his tenure in office directly engaged the constitutional theory that Article 96 embodies — that the presiding officer's role is institutional, not partisan.

## Relationship with anti-defection jurisdiction

Article 96 has acquired renewed political importance because the Speaker's most sensitive power — deciding disqualification petitions under the Tenth Schedule — creates incentives for opposition members and ruling-party dissidents to move removal notices as a tactical measure. By triggering Article 96 (as extended by Nabam Rebia), they can freeze the Speaker's ability to adjudicate pending disqualification petitions. This dynamic has played out in several State assembly contexts and, by constitutional analogy, applies to the Lok Sabha as well.

The Supreme Court in Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary (2023) continued to grapple with the consequences of Speaker disqualification jurisdiction being suspended while anti-defection petitions remain pending. The Court's extended engagement confirmed that the Nabam Rebia principle remains operative and that a Speaker whose removal is pending cannot adjudicate Tenth Schedule petitions even if the disqualification petitions were filed before the removal notice. This creates a sequencing problem: a government facing defection can file disqualification petitions against defectors, who then respond by filing a removal notice against the Speaker, thereby suspending the disqualification proceedings.

The constitutional resolution of this tactical game has been to place increasing pressure on the Supreme Court to step in and either decide disqualification petitions directly or appoint a substitute adjudicator. The Court has resisted both options as departures from the constitutional design of presiding-officer adjudication, but the repeated inability of the constitutional mechanism to function when it is most needed has generated sustained calls for structural reform.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Supreme Court's five-judge bench extended the principle of Article 96 (and its Rajya Sabha counterpart Article 92) to hold that a presiding officer against whom a removal notice has been admitted cannot exercise discretionary, quasi-judicial powers — such as Tenth Schedule disqualification decisions — that could affect the composition of the House and thereby the outcome of the removal vote. The Court treated Articles 92 and 96 as embodying a broader natural-justice principle rather than rules limited to the physical act of presiding. This remains the leading case on the reach of Article 96's underlying logic.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** — The constitutional bench observed that the Speaker's removal procedure in Article 94, read together with Article 96, was one of the structural checks that the framers placed on the otherwise unreviewable powers vested in the Speaker under the Tenth Schedule. The existence of Article 96 was part of the Court's reasoning for why the Speaker's role in anti-defection adjudication did not violate fundamental rights — the Speaker is not a permanently entrenched power-holder immune from removal.

**Speaker, Lok Sabha v. K.S. Rajan (1994)** — A Division Bench affirmed that the procedural requirements in Article 96 are mandatory. Any sitting conducted in violation of the requirement that the affected Speaker not preside would be constitutionally infirm, exposing any disqualification order passed on such a day to challenge on procedural grounds.

**Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of the Maharashtra political crisis and the suspension of the Speaker's disqualification jurisdiction applied and elaborated on Nabam Rebia, working through the practical consequences of Article 96's underlying principle in conditions of contested parliamentary arithmetic. The case confirmed that the constraints derived from Article 96 apply in real time during political crises, not only in settled conditions.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — The Supreme Court issued a direction to the Speaker to decide a pending disqualification petition, engaging the question of how long Article 96-derived delays in adjudication can persist before the Court must intervene to protect the constitutional rights of the petitioner. The case moved the jurisprudence toward time-bound adjudication requirements.

## Current significance

Article 96 is at the heart of the most contested area of parliamentary procedure in contemporary India. The Tenth Schedule, enacted in 1985, gave speakers unprecedented quasi-judicial authority, and the combination of Articles 94, 96, and the Nabam Rebia doctrine has made removal-notice tactics a standard instrument of political management during floor-crossing crises. Reform proposals — including transfer of anti-defection adjudication to an independent tribunal — are partly motivated by the desire to eliminate the Article 96 and Nabam Rebia dynamic that has allowed Speaker disqualification proceedings to be paralysed by tactical removal notices.

The constitutional design tension exposed by Article 96 is between two legitimate principles. The first is that the Speaker must be constitutionally accountable and cannot use the tools of the office to defeat the accountability mechanism. The second is that the Tenth Schedule's anti-defection provisions must be capable of functioning as a deterrent against floor-crossing, which requires timely adjudication. When these two principles are in conflict — as they regularly are during political crises — Article 96 in its current interpreted form privileges the accountability principle over the anti-defection enforcement principle.

Whether this balance is constitutionally correct is a question that the Supreme Court has not definitively resolved. The Court's increasing willingness to supervise the timeliness of disqualification proceedings suggests a judicial view that the anti-defection enforcement interest deserves protection even when Article 96-derived constraints apply, but the mechanism for protecting it — judicial oversight of a nominally non-justiciable presiding-officer function — is itself constitutionally contested. The reform conversation generated by this tension is one of the most important ongoing constitutional debates in India's parliamentary law.
