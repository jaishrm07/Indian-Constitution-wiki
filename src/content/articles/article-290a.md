## Why it matters

Article 290A is one of the most constitutionally unusual provisions in Part XII — a Finance article that directly names specific religious endowment bodies and directs permanent public expenditure toward them. It was inserted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956 as part of the States Reorganisation exercise, resolving the practical problem of how to fund temples that had historically been supported from the revenues of the princely state of Travancore-Cochin once those territories were divided between Kerala and the Madras State (later Tamil Nadu). By making the payments a "charged" constitutional obligation, the framers gave the Devaswom Funds a degree of financial security that no annual appropriation bill could easily disturb.

The provision also sits at the intersection of the Constitution's secular character and its recognition of existing religious endowment arrangements — making it a flashpoint in contemporary debates about differential State treatment of Hindu versus non-Hindu religious institutions.

## Reading the provision

The sums are precise and fixed: Rs 46,50,000 annually from Kerala to the Travancore Devaswom Fund, and Rs 13,50,000 annually from Tamil Nadu to its Devaswom Fund for temples in the transferred territories. Being "charged on" the respective Consolidated Funds means these amounts are drawn automatically — they do not pass through the annual appropriations process and the State legislature cannot vote to reduce or withhold them. The article does not establish the Devaswom Funds themselves; it presupposes their existence under State legislation and directs financial support to them.

The territorial reference point is the transfer of 1 November 1956 — the date the States Reorganisation Act took effect — and the funds apply only to temples in the territories that were transferred on that date from Travancore-Cochin to the successor States. The Cochin Devaswom Board, covering temples in the former Cochin State portion of Kerala, is funded separately under State legislation without a constitutional charge.

## Key cases

**Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (Sabarimala, 2018)** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling on women's entry to the Sabarimala temple arose directly from the Travancore Devaswom Board's administration of the shrine. The Board's status as a State-regulated and State-funded entity — whose financial base includes the Article 290A constitutional payment — was central to the Court's analysis of whether it could assert religious autonomy rights under Article 26 against a constitutional challenge brought under Article 25.

**Shirur Mutt v. Commissioner of Religious Endowments (1954)** — The foundational ruling on State regulation of Hindu religious institutions drew the line between permissible regulatory oversight of temple management and impermissible interference with religious doctrine and practice. The framework established here governs the constitutionality of the Devaswom Board model that Article 290A sustains financially.

**TMA Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The eleven-judge Bench's analysis of minority educational institutions and the constitutional principles governing State involvement in religious and community institutions provides the broader doctrinal backdrop against which the differential treatment of Hindu temples through Article 290A is evaluated, particularly in relation to arguments under Articles 14, 25, and 26.

## Historical background — the Travancore-Cochin settlement

The Travancore Devaswom Board was established under the Travancore-Cochin Hindu Religious Institutions Act 1950 and inherited the administration of thousands of temples that had historically been under the sovereign patronage of the Travancore royal family. When the States Reorganisation Act 1956 divided Travancore-Cochin between the new State of Kerala and the Madras State, the temples in the transferred Madras-bound territories lost their original funding link. Article 290A was the constitutional answer — a permanent charged obligation on each successor State replicating the financial relationship between the former princely state and its temple administration bodies.

The Travancore Devaswom Board now administers over 1,200 temples in Kerala, including Sabarimala, Attukal Bhagavathy, and Ettumanoor Mahadeva — among the most visited pilgrimage sites in India. The Board employs thousands of staff and its governance is heavily regulated by the State government, with board members appointed by the Kerala government and accounts audited by the Comptroller and Auditor General.

## Current significance

Article 290A attracts sustained attention in the debate about differential State involvement in Hindu versus non-Hindu religious institutions. Critics argue that the State management of Hindu temples under Devaswom Boards — combined with the Article 290A payment obligation — contrasts with the relative autonomy afforded to Muslim Waqf properties and Christian church properties, raising questions under Articles 14 and 25-28. Defenders respond that the Devaswom Boards are public institutions managing previously princely-state-administered temples, and that public funding of public religious administration does not amount to preferential State sponsorship of Hinduism.

The frozen monetary amounts in the article — unchanged since 1956 — are now worth a small fraction of their original real value; they are largely symbolic rather than a meaningful contribution to the Boards' finances, which rely mainly on temple receipts and State grants under ordinary legislation. Petitions challenging the constitutionality of State management of Hindu temples, which indirectly implicate the Article 290A framework, have been filed in several High Courts and the Supreme Court, and the broader debate intersects with ongoing legislative changes to the Waqf regime that have prompted renewed comparative scrutiny of how the Constitution treats different religious communities' properties.
