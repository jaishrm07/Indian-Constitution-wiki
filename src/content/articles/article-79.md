## Why it matters

Article 79 is the constitutional birth certificate of Parliament.
It does more than name an institution — it determines who belongs to it.
By including the President within Parliament's composition, the framers ensured that the head of state is constitutionally integrated into the legislative process rather than standing apart from it as a purely executive actor.
Every downstream provision about how Parliament functions — passing Bills, amending the Constitution, disciplining members, exercising privilege — derives its meaning from what Article 79 says Parliament is.
The provision is the foundation on which the entire parliamentary architecture of Part V rests.

The inclusion of the President in Parliament is not a technicality.
It produces concrete legal results.
Under Article 111, a Bill passed by both Houses does not become law until the President gives assent.
That assent is an act of Parliament, not a supervening executive approval.
Under Article 368, an amendment to the Constitution requires a special majority "of Parliament" — the President's assent to the amending Bill is therefore part of the constituent act itself, not merely its ratification by an outside authority.
The Supreme Court has drawn on this structure to reason about what Parliament can and cannot do under its amending power, and about whether the President's failure to act on a Bill within a reasonable time constitutes a constitutional dereliction.

The bicameral element of Article 79 is equally foundational.
The framers chose a two-chamber Parliament because a single chamber representing the national electorate directly would give no structural weight to the States as States.
The second chamber — the Council of States — exists to express federalism within the legislative process.
Parliament's bicameral design means that most legislation must secure the agreement of two differently constituted bodies before it can receive Presidential assent.
This is a built-in deliberative mechanism, a check on legislative haste, and a guarantee that the interests of federating units receive formal consideration in the Union's lawmaking.

The choice between a parliamentary and a presidential system was among the most consequential decisions the Constituent Assembly made.
In a presidential system, the executive and the legislature are separately elected and separately constituted — the President would not be part of Parliament.
The framers rejected this design, drawing lessons from the Westminster tradition and from the perceived risks of a presidential system's rigidity.
Under the chosen model, executive accountability runs through Parliament, and the President's role within Parliament — however nominal in practice — symbolises the inseparability of governance and legislation.

The definition of Parliament in Article 79 also determines the scope of parliamentary privilege.
Privilege — the immunity of Parliament and its members from external legal proceedings in respect of legislative acts — attaches to "Parliament" as constituted by this article.
Courts have had to grapple repeatedly with where the boundaries of that privilege run, which requires them to first identify with precision what Parliament is.
Article 79's definition is therefore not merely structural history; it is an active tool of constitutional interpretation.

## Reading the provision

The text is deceptively spare — Parliament consists of the President and two Houses.
The inclusion of the President is the operative constitutional choice.
It means Presidential assent under Article 111 is the completion of a parliamentary act, not the signature of a reviewing executive.
It means constitutional amendments under Article 368, which require a special majority "of Parliament," must include Presidential assent to be valid.
And it means the question of what happens when a President sits on a Bill indefinitely — the so-called pocket veto — is a question about whether a component of Parliament is fulfilling its constitutional duty.

The phrase "for the Union" identifies Parliament as a body of the Union of India rather than of any component State.
State legislatures are constituted separately under Part VI.
Parliament's jurisdiction is national — it legislates on Union List subjects as of right, on Concurrent List subjects in competition with State legislatures, and on State List subjects only in exceptional circumstances under Articles 249, 250, 252, 253, and 356.
The constitutional geography of legislative power begins with this phrase.

The two Houses are named — the Council of States and the House of the People — rather than being left to be named by ordinary law.
This locks the bicameral structure into the Constitution at the deepest level.
Parliament cannot by ordinary legislation abolish either House or merge them.
Any move toward a unicameral Parliament would require a constitutional amendment and would face the question of whether bicameralism is a feature of the Constitution's basic structure.

The two Houses reflect the framers' choice of a bicameral legislature with differentiated mandates.
The Rajya Sabha represents the federating units; the Lok Sabha represents the people directly.
Their distinct compositions (Articles 80 and 81), terms (Article 83), and powers (Articles 109–110) give the bicameral structure its substance, but Article 79 establishes its existence.
The powers of the two Houses are not symmetrical — the Lok Sabha has financial preeminence under Article 109, and it is the Lok Sabha that decides the fate of the government under Article 75(3) — but both Houses are constituted by Article 79 as co-equal components of Parliament for all other purposes.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The inclusion of the President in Parliament was not controversial in the Constituent Assembly.
What was debated was the deeper question of whether India should adopt a parliamentary or presidential executive at all.
Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, as chairman of the Drafting Committee, explained the choice in favour of parliamentary government on grounds of accountability — in a parliamentary system, the executive is daily accountable to the legislature and can be removed by it, whereas a presidential executive is fixed in office regardless of its legislative support.
The framers saw the Westminster model of responsible government as better suited to India's diversity and its need for flexible, coalition-capable governance.

Jawaharlal Nehru argued in the Assembly that India's mass democracy required an executive that retained the confidence of elected representatives and could be held answerable through parliamentary debate and votes of no-confidence.
The presidential model — exemplified by the United States — was seen as appropriate for a society with a longer tradition of constitutional stability but potentially too rigid for a new democracy navigating profound social and economic transformation.
This context explains why Article 79 places the President inside Parliament rather than constituting the President as an independent elected executive standing apart from it.

The naming of the two Houses in Article 79 was itself a deliberate choice.
"Council of States" and "House of the People" were chosen to reflect the functional identities of the chambers — one representing the States as constituent units of the Union, the other representing the citizenry as individuals.
K.M. Munshi and others in the Assembly emphasised that the names should embody the constitutional theory of each House rather than simply borrowing British nomenclature (Lords and Commons) or American (Senate and House).
The constitutional self-consciousness of those choices is visible in Article 79's plain text.

## Key cases

**Keshavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The Court's analysis of Parliament's constituent power under Article 368 necessarily engaged with what "Parliament" means as a constitutional body.
The holding that Parliament cannot destroy the Constitution's basic structure implicitly constrains the institution Article 79 creates — Parliament cannot use its own definition to amend itself out of constitutional accountability.

**In re: Special Reference No. 1 of 1964 (Keshav Singh case)** — The Supreme Court addressed the relationship between parliamentary privilege and judicial review, drawing on the constitutional status of Parliament as constituted by Article 79 to calibrate the limits of privilege against fundamental rights.
The Court held that parliamentary privilege, though broad, does not extend so far as to displace the fundamental rights of persons outside Parliament — a result that depended on Parliament's nature as a constitutionally defined institution rather than a sovereign body in the British sense.

**Indira Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975)** — The Supreme Court examined free and fair elections as a basic feature of the Constitution, necessarily engaging with Parliament as the representative institution Article 79 constitutes and whose electoral foundation gives it legitimacy.
The judgment's recognition of free and fair elections as basic structure depends on Parliament's character as a democratically constituted body, which Article 79 establishes.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Court examined the expulsion of Lok Sabha members for alleged corruption in the cash-for-questions scandal.
The analysis drew on Article 79 to define Parliament's character as an institution and the scope of its self-regulatory powers within the constitutional framework.
The Court held that expulsion — while within parliamentary privilege — is reviewable for compliance with natural justice, an outcome made possible by Parliament's constitutional rather than sovereign status.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker (2016)** — The Supreme Court's examination of the Speaker's powers during a constitutional crisis in Arunachal Pradesh, though directly concerning a State legislature, engaged extensively with the constitutional theory of Parliament established under Article 79 and applied by analogy to State legislatures under Article 168.
The judgment's insistence on procedural regularity within legislatures drew on the principle that Parliament is a creature of and subject to the Constitution.

**State of Punjab v. Sat Pal Dang (1969)** — An early case addressing the immunity of parliamentary proceedings from external legal challenge.
The Court's reasoning rested on Article 79's constitution of Parliament as a defined constitutional body with specific privileges, distinguishing it from an ordinary institution that enjoys no special protection in legal proceedings.

## Current significance

Article 79's inclusion of the President in Parliament's composition has immediate practical relevance.
Critics of Presidents who have indefinitely withheld assent from State Bills argue that the corresponding Presidential obligation at the Union level is equally binding — the President must participate in completing the legislative process because the President is constitutionally part of Parliament.
The Supreme Court's 2025 ruling in the Tamil Nadu Governor case, holding that Governors cannot sit indefinitely on State Bills, has sharpened attention to the equivalent question at the Union level.
The President's role is defined not by Article 200 (Governors and State Bills) but by Article 111, which must be read in light of Article 79's composition of Parliament.

The One Nation One Election debate also turns partly on Article 79.
Synchronising election cycles would affect the constitutional relationship between the House's defined term under Article 83 and the President's power of dissolution under Article 85 — all provisions whose scope derives from what Parliament is and who constitutes it under Article 79.
The proposal to conduct simultaneous elections would require amending the Constitution's provisions about the term of Parliament, and the structure of Parliament as defined in Article 79 is the starting point for any such analysis.

The basic structure doctrine — which holds that certain features of the Constitution cannot be amended even by Parliament using its Article 368 power — gives Article 79 an important second life.
Since Parliament cannot destroy its own democratic character or its bicameral composition (if these are basic features), Article 79 represents not just a structural starting point but a constitutionally entrenched one.
The framing of Parliament in Article 79 is thus protected against abolition or fundamental alteration even by a Parliament commanding the largest possible legislative majority.

The question of whether India should move toward greater use of joint sittings — Article 108 permits joint sittings to resolve disagreements between the two Houses — or toward a more openly unequal bicameralism in which the Lok Sabha's supremacy is formalised rather than situational, keeps Article 79 doctrinally active.
Its simple sentence about what Parliament consists of continues to anchor debates about the relationship between the Union's three branches and about the nature of democratic accountability in India's constitutional order.

Article 79 also has a less-examined dimension — what it says about the President's relationship to Parliament when both are exercising constituent power.
Under Article 368, Parliament proposes and the President assents.
But the President is, under Article 79, a component of that same Parliament.
This means the President's assent to a constitutional amendment is both the act of the head of the executive under Article 53 and the completion of a parliamentary act under Article 79 — a constitutional duality that the courts have not had occasion to fully disaggregate.
If the basic structure doctrine ever required a court to examine whether an amendment was properly "passed by Parliament" in a formalistic sense, Article 79's definition of Parliament would be the starting point.

Parliament's electoral character — which Article 79 implicitly presupposes by constituting a body that includes elected Houses — has itself been treated as a basic structural feature.
If Parliament is to be a democratic institution in the sense that Article 79 envisions, its composition must result from free and genuine elections.
This connects Article 79 directly to the basic structure cases and to the Election Commission's constitutional mandate to conduct elections that are free, fair, and frequent.

The article's apparent simplicity — a single declaratory sentence — conceals the extent to which the entire parliamentary framework of Part V is structurally dependent on it.
Articles 80 through 122, which deal with the composition, powers, procedures, privileges, and dissolution of Parliament, all derive their operational meaning from the institution that Article 79 creates.
No provision in Part V can be read without reference to this foundation.
It is the constitutional sentence that makes all the others about Parliament coherent.
The framers could have said more — they could have elaborated on the President's role in Parliament, specified the terms of the two Houses here, or enumerated the powers of the legislature in a single omnibus clause.
They chose not to, and the spare architecture of Article 79 — creating the institution and naming its components without further elaboration — is itself a constitutionally significant choice, leaving the substance of those relationships to be worked out through the detailed provisions that follow.
