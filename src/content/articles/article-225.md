plainEnglish: >-
  Article 225 preserves the jurisdiction that High Courts in existence before 26 January 1950 had under the Government of India Act 1935 and their founding Letters Patent, subject to the Constitution itself and to legislation made by a competent legislature. This continuity provision ensured that the courts did not lose their inherited jurisdictions — original civil, appellate, revisional, criminal, admiralty, matrimonial, testamentary, and insolvency — on the mere commencement of the Constitution. The proviso removed restrictions that had previously blocked High Courts from exercising original jurisdiction in revenue matters, expanding access to the courts at the moment of constitutional commencement. Parliament and State Legislatures may modify the inherited jurisdiction, but they cannot override the writ jurisdiction under Article 226 or the supervisory jurisdiction under Article 227, which the Constitution itself directly confers. Courts examining legislation that purports to curtail High Court jurisdiction must ask two questions — whether the jurisdiction was part of the Article 225 inheritance, and whether the enacting legislature has adequate constitutional competence to modify it. The provision is the jurisdictional starting point for all High Courts that predate the Constitution, and it anchors a two-step analytical framework for challenges to legislation that channels disputes away from those courts.

  225. Jurisdiction of existing High Courts.—Subject to the provisions of this Constitution and to the provisions of any law of the appropriate Legislature made by virtue of powers conferred on that Legislature by this Constitution, the jurisdiction of, and the law administered in, any existing High Court, and the respective powers of the Judges thereof in relation to the administration of justice in the Court, including any power to make rules of Court and to regulate the sittings of the Court and of members thereof sitting alone or in Division Courts, shall be the same as immediately before the commencement of this Constitution:

## Why it matters

Article 225 serves the vital transitional function of ensuring that established courts did not suffer a jurisdictional vacuum when the Constitution came into force. Without it, the question of where a High Court's authority came from — pre-constitutional instruments that might be superseded, or a new constitutional grant that Parliament would need to enact — could have created dangerous uncertainty about the status of pending cases, ongoing proceedings, and court-made rules governing procedure and sittings. The preservation is immediate and automatic: no further legislative act was needed to continue the inherited jurisdictions.

The provision has permanent constitutional significance beyond its transitional role. It establishes that the inherited jurisdiction is modifiable only by legislation made under constitutional competence, and it anchors a two-step judicial analysis for any challenge to legislation purporting to curtail or exclude High Court jurisdiction. First, was the jurisdiction part of the Article 225 inheritance? Second, does the legislature enacting the exclusion have constitutional competence to do so, and has the exclusion been effected in a constitutionally permissible manner? This two-step framework disciplines the analysis across a very wide range of legislative interventions — from the creation of specialised tribunals to State legislation reorganising revenue adjudication.

The proviso was a significant substantive change, not merely a housekeeping provision. Before the Constitution, High Courts' original jurisdiction was blocked in relation to revenue matters — a legacy of the colonial system that treated land revenue and its collection as executive rather than judicial terrain. The proviso swept that restriction away at the moment the Constitution commenced. Citizens could immediately approach their High Courts in original jurisdiction over revenue acts that previously fell outside the courts' reach. This expansion of access to justice was deliberate and emblematic of the Constitution's commitment to establishing a justiciable state in place of the executive-dominated revenue system of British India.

## Reading the provision

**The opening phrase — "existing High Court"**: Article 225 applies only to courts that were in existence immediately before 26 January 1950. This includes the Chartered High Courts (Bombay, Calcutta, Madras, Allahabad, and others established by Letters Patent), and the Chief Courts and Judicial Commissioners' Courts that became High Courts under the Government of India Act 1935. High Courts established after the Constitution's commencement derive their jurisdiction from the constitutional provisions (primarily Article 214) and from legislation enacted under those provisions — they have no "inherited" jurisdiction to preserve.

**"Jurisdiction of, and the law administered in, any existing High Court"**: Article 225 preserves two distinct things — the jurisdiction itself (the categories of matters over which the court may adjudicate) and the law administered (the substantive and procedural legal rules applied in the exercise of that jurisdiction). This dual preservation means that both the court's competence and the applicable legal framework carry over without interruption. A High Court's capacity to apply Letters Patent provisions, its original jurisdiction rules, its appellate rules, and its Rules of Court under inherited rule-making power — all survived the Constitution's commencement.

**"The respective powers of the Judges thereof in relation to the administration of justice"**: This phrase encompasses individual judges' powers — powers vested in a single judge sitting alone, powers exercisable only by a Division Court, jurisdiction specific to the Chief Justice, and powers relating to the constitution and management of benches. The preservation ensures that these internal distribution-of-powers questions — which judge can hear what, and how full benches are constituted — continue to be governed by the inherited instruments unless modified by competent legislation.

**"Including any power to make rules of Court and to regulate the sittings of the Court"**: Article 225 expressly includes the inherited rule-making power — High Courts' power to govern their own procedure, formats, filing requirements, and sitting arrangements. This preserved rule-making power is an element of judicial independence: courts retain authority over their own procedure without requiring fresh legislative delegation. The High Courts' Original Side Rules, Appellate Side Rules, Letters Patent Rules, and Rules of Court in criminal jurisdiction all draw their continuing validity from Article 225.

**"Subject to the provisions of this Constitution and to the provisions of any law of the appropriate Legislature"**: The preservation is not absolute. The Constitution itself takes precedence — so Article 226's writ jurisdiction, Article 227's superintendence power, and all other constitutional provisions override the inherited jurisdiction to the extent of any conflict. Equally, competent legislation may modify the inherited jurisdiction. The State Legislature may modify jurisdiction in matters within State competence; Parliament may modify it in matters within Union competence. Neither legislature may, however, modify jurisdiction in a manner that goes beyond its constitutional competence or that violates constitutional guarantees — including the basic structure protection of High Court judicial review.

**The revenue proviso**: The restriction removed by the proviso was the prohibition on exercising original jurisdiction over revenue matters — a restriction that traced back to Crown grants and Letters Patent provisions excluding revenue jurisdiction. The proviso's language is immediate and automatic: the restriction "shall no longer apply." From 26 January 1950 onwards, High Courts with original jurisdiction could exercise that jurisdiction in revenue matters and over acts ordered or done in the collection of revenue.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The debate in the Constituent Assembly on what became Article 225 was pragmatic and focused on continuity. Members were concerned to avoid any gap in judicial authority at the moment of constitutional commencement, and the preservation approach was broadly accepted. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar explained that the article was designed to preserve, not override, the existing jurisdictional structure — the Constitution was building upon an existing judicial architecture rather than establishing a new one from scratch.

The revenue proviso attracted specific debate. Several members noted that the exclusion of revenue jurisdiction from the original civil jurisdiction of Chartered High Courts had been a persistent source of injustice — citizens suffering from arbitrary revenue collection practices had been denied access to the High Court's original jurisdiction. The removal of this restriction at the moment of constitutional commencement was supported as a direct expression of the Constitution's commitment to access to justice.

Jawahar Lal Nehru's government had proposed that the inherited jurisdictions be subject to explicit parliamentary ratification, but this approach was rejected as unnecessarily complicated and potentially disruptive. The Assembly preferred the automatic preservation approach — carrying forward all inherited jurisdiction and relying on legislation to make targeted modifications where needed.

## Key cases

**Kumaon Motor Owners Union Ltd v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1966)** — The Supreme Court held that where a pre-Constitution Letters Patent granted original jurisdiction to a High Court, that jurisdiction is preserved by Article 225 and cannot be impliedly removed by general Central legislation. Parliament must use express language to exclude jurisdiction that Article 225 has saved. This principle — that implied exclusion is insufficient to override the Article 225 preservation — is foundational to the ongoing vitality of High Courts' inherited original jurisdiction.

**Mohammad Nooh v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1957)** — The Court confirmed that Article 225 saves the appellate jurisdiction exercised by pre-Constitution High Courts under the Code of Civil Procedure, and that a State Legislature acting under Part VI cannot, without express language, curtail jurisdiction that Article 225 has preserved. The judgment underscored that Article 225 imposes a real constraint on State legislative power: modification requires positive, express legislative intention.

**Radhey Shyam v. Chhabi Nath (2015)** — The Supreme Court discussed the relationship between Article 225 and Article 226, holding that the original civil jurisdiction of High Courts preserved under Article 225 coexists with, and is not replaced by, the writ jurisdiction under Article 226. Each species of jurisdiction has its own scope and purpose. A party with a civil claim in a High Court with Letters Patent original jurisdiction may choose between the original civil jurisdiction and a writ petition — the two are not mutually exclusive, and the availability of writ jurisdiction does not diminish the inherited original civil jurisdiction.

**L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997)** — While primarily decided on Article 226 grounds, L. Chandra Kumar's basic structure reasoning applies equally to Article 225: the inherited original jurisdiction and appellate jurisdiction of High Courts cannot be entirely excluded by Parliament through the creation of exclusive tribunals. The constitutional minimum of High Court oversight over statutory bodies is a feature of both Article 225 (inheritance) and Article 226 (direct constitutional grant).

**Naresh Shridhar Mirajkar v. State of Maharashtra (1966)** — The Supreme Court examined the scope of High Court jurisdiction including the court's authority under its inherited powers to regulate its own proceedings and to exercise contempt jurisdiction. The case illustrated how Article 225's preservation of judicial powers extends to the court's authority to manage its own processes — including coercive powers over parties in proceedings.

**Union of India v. Bhana Mal (2005)** — The Court addressed the relationship between Article 225 jurisdiction and the jurisdiction of specialised tribunals, reaffirming that the creation of a tribunal with exclusive first-instance jurisdiction does not extinguish the High Court's preserved supervisory and revisional jurisdiction where the legislature has not expressly excluded it.

## Current significance

Article 225 has ongoing relevance wherever States attempt to channel litigation away from High Courts into specialised tribunals or revenue boards. Courts examining such legislation must ask whether the jurisdiction sought to be excluded was part of the High Court's Article 225 inheritance, triggering questions about whether the legislature has adequate constitutional authority to remove it and whether such removal is consistent with the basic structure.

The interaction between Article 225 and the proliferation of specialised tribunals has generated a substantial body of litigation. State administrative tribunals, consumer forums, the National Green Tribunal, Debt Recovery Tribunals, and similar bodies often have jurisdiction overlapping with what the High Courts inherited under Article 225. Courts have consistently held that while Parliament may create such bodies and confer exclusive first-instance jurisdiction on them, the supervisory jurisdiction of the High Court under Articles 226 and 227 cannot be excluded — preserving a constitutional minimum of High Court oversight regardless of how extensively Article 225 jurisdiction is otherwise redistributed.

The Letters Patent appellate jurisdiction — preserved under Article 225 — remains practically significant in the Chartered High Courts (Bombay, Calcutta, and Madras). Letters Patent Appeals from single-judge decisions to Division Benches continue to be filed, creating an additional tier of High Court appellate review not available in courts without Letters Patent inheritance. The availability or non-availability of this tier has consequences for the finality of High Court single-judge orders, and reform proposals to regularise the appellate structure across all High Courts necessarily engage Article 225.

The provision is also relevant in Union Territories whose High Courts trace their jurisdiction through Letters Patent and similar pre-Constitution instruments. Any challenge to the scope of such jurisdiction necessarily engages the Article 225 preservation clause as the starting point for identifying what was saved and what competent legislation has since modified. High Courts' admiralty, testamentary, and matrimonial jurisdiction under old Letters Patent provisions — areas with sparse modern legislative coverage — also depend substantially on the Article 225 inheritance, making the provision relevant to contemporary litigation in those domains.
