## Why it matters

Article 328 represents the federal dimension of India's election law scheme. The framers of the Constitution faced a structural choice: should election law for State Legislatures be entirely a matter for Parliament (as under Article 327) or should State Legislatures have some constitutional role in designing the elections that constitute their own Houses? The answer they chose was a layered one — Parliament has primary power over elections to both Parliament and State Legislatures under Article 327, but States retain a residual power under Article 328 for matters Parliament has not yet covered. This design acknowledges that State Legislatures have a legitimate interest in the rules governing their own constitution, while ensuring that the national uniformity of the core electoral framework is maintained through parliamentary legislation.

The practical significance of Article 328 is limited by Parliament's comprehensive exercise of its Article 327 power. The Representation of the People Act 1950 and the Representation of the People Act 1951 together cover the preparation of electoral rolls, the delimitation of constituencies, the nomination and qualification of candidates, the conduct of polling, corrupt practices, election petitions, and the disqualification of members. This near-total occupation of the field leaves States with a narrow residual space — primarily procedural matters for State Legislative Councils and incidental administrative arrangements not governed by parliamentary legislation.

Despite its narrow scope, Article 328 has constitutional significance as an index of the federal character of India's electoral architecture. In federalism debates about the "One Nation One Election" proposal, Article 328 becomes a central reference point — compelling States to hold elections at Parliament-mandated intervals and freezing the composition of State Legislatures for extended periods would effectively annihilate the State legislative role that Article 328 preserves, raising questions about whether such a reform requires a constitutional amendment or could be achieved through ordinary Article 327 legislation alone.

## Reading the provision

**"Subject to the provisions of this Constitution":** Like Article 327, the State Legislature's power is bounded by the entire Constitution — including fundamental rights, Part XV's specific provisions, and the basic structure. State election legislation cannot discriminate on grounds of religion, race, caste, or sex (Article 325), cannot restrict the franchise beyond Article 326's enumerated grounds, and must defer to the Election Commission's superintendence under Article 324.

**"In so far as provision in that behalf is not made by Parliament":** This is the operative pre-emption clause. The State Legislature's power is conditional — it exists only in the spaces Parliament has not occupied. This differs from the general Seventh Schedule scheme under Article 254, where Parliament's legislation on a Concurrent List subject prevails over inconsistent State legislation. Under Article 328, the State power exists in the residual gap rather than in competition with parliamentary legislation. If Parliament enacts a provision on an electoral matter, that matter is no longer available for State legislation at all — not even concurrent legislation.

**"The Legislature of a State may from time to time by law make provision with respect to all matters relating to, or in connection with, the elections to the House or either House of the Legislature of the State":** The scope of the State power, where it exists, is as broad as Parliament's power under Article 327 — "all matters relating to, or in connection with," the elections to the State Legislature. The State Legislature can address preparatory, conduct, and post-election matters for its own Houses to the extent Parliament has not done so. The phrase "House or either House" acknowledges that States may have both a Legislative Assembly and a Legislative Council.

**"Including the preparation of electoral rolls":** The specific mention of electoral rolls in Article 328 is significant because it identifies an area where State legislative power is explicitly recognised. In practice, however, Parliament's RPA 1950 has comprehensively occupied the field of electoral roll preparation, leaving little for States to regulate separately. The mention is a reminder that before Parliament legislated comprehensively, States did have meaningful electoral roll power — and would have it again if Parliament were to repeal or narrow its legislation.

**"And all other matters necessary for securing the due constitution of such House or Houses":** The same residual catch-all as Article 327's — any matter functionally necessary to achieve a properly constituted State Legislature falls within the State's residual power, provided Parliament has not legislated on it.

## The parliamentary pre-emption structure

The relationship between Articles 327 and 328 mirrors the general framework of Articles 246 and 254 on legislative competence but is specific to elections. Once Parliament legislates on an electoral matter for State Legislature elections, that matter is occupied and the State's Article 328 power in that area is superseded. The pre-emption is field-by-field rather than global — Parliament's legislation on corrupt practices does not displace State power to legislate on administrative arrangements for counting venues, for example, if Parliament has not covered that specific matter.

In practice, Parliament's occupation through the Representation of the People Acts is so comprehensive — covering electoral rolls, constituency delimitation, qualification and disqualification of voters and candidates, polling procedure, counting, corrupt practices, election petitions, and member disqualification — that there is very little left for State Legislatures to legislate on under Article 328. The narrow residual space that survives consists primarily of procedural arrangements for State Legislative Councils, certain state-specific administrative provisions, and matters so local and incidental that Parliament has not found it necessary to legislate on them.

## Article 328 and State Legislative Councils

One area where Article 328 retains practical significance is the constitution and operation of State Legislative Councils. Parliament has not legislated comprehensively on the procedures for constituting all categories of Vidhan Parishad members — graduates' constituencies, teachers' constituencies, local authority representatives, members elected by the Legislative Assembly itself, and Governor's nominees all have distinct constitutional bases and procedural requirements. Some States have enacted rules under Article 328 to address gaps in the parliamentary framework for Vidhan Parishad elections, making these councils the clearest surviving instance of the residual Article 328 power being actively exercised.

The creation, abolition, and procedural design of Legislative Councils involves Article 169 (for creation and abolition by Parliament on State resolution) and Article 171 (for composition). Article 328 provides the residual legislative power for filling procedural gaps in their constitution, and States that have active Legislative Councils retain this residual space even as the Representation of the People Acts have progressively narrowed it.

## Key cases

**N P Ponnuswami v Returning Officer, Namakkal Constituency AIR 1952 SC 64** — The Supreme Court held that election laws enacted under both Articles 327 and 328 form part of the same integrated constitutional scheme for elections. The Court emphasised that courts should be slow to interfere with elections by way of prerogative writs given Article 329's bar, which applies to elections conducted under laws made under both provisions. The judgment treated State election law under Article 328 as constitutionally coordinate with parliamentary election law under Article 327, differing only in legislative competence — both are subject to the same constitutional framework and the same institutional oversight by the Election Commission.

**Election Commission of India v Ashok Kumar (2000) 8 SCC 216** — The Court drew a clear line between State legislative competence to make election law (preserved by Article 328) and State governmental power to administer elections (which remains constitutionally vested in the Election Commission under Article 324 regardless of which legislature enacted the governing statute). A State may exercise Article 328 to legislate procedural rules, but no State can use Article 328 legislation to displace the Election Commission from its constitutional superintendence role or to vest election administration in a State executive body. The case confirmed that Article 328's residual legislative power does not imply any parallel administrative authority.

**Kuldip Nayar v Union of India (2006) 7 SCC 1** — The five-judge bench confirmed that the Part XV framework operates as a unified constitutional scheme and that State Legislatures exercising Article 328 powers are subject to the same constitutional constraints as Parliament exercising Article 327 powers — including the overarching requirements of the Model Code of Conduct as implemented by the Election Commission under Article 324. State election laws enacted under Article 328 cannot achieve what Parliament could not achieve under Article 327.

**Meghraj Kothari v Delimitation Commission (1966) 3 SCR 683** — In a case concerning the allocation of reserved constituencies, the Court confirmed that delimitation orders made under parliamentary legislation under Article 327 are protected from judicial challenge by Article 329(a) and that State legislative power under Article 328 cannot be used to override or supplement delimitation orders made by a Commission acting under Parliament's authority. The case illustrated the hierarchy — parliamentary legislation under Article 327 displaces State power under Article 328 for any specific electoral matter it covers.

## Current significance

Article 328 matters most in debates about constitutional federalism and the allocation of election-related powers between the Centre and the States. The "One Nation One Election" proposal — which would require all State Assembly elections to be synchronised with the Lok Sabha cycle — raises the sharpest Article 328 questions. If Parliament were to enact legislation under Article 327 prescribing fixed common election dates and requiring State Assemblies to extend or abbreviate their terms to align with the national schedule, this would effectively eliminate the residual State legislative role that Article 328 preserves for the constitution of State Houses. Critics argue that such legislation would require constitutional amendments to Part VI (State government) and the provisions governing State Assembly terms, not merely parliamentary legislation under Article 327.

The interaction between Article 328 and the delimitation freeze — constituency numbers frozen at 1971 levels until the first delimitation after the 2026 census — means that any fresh delimitation under parliamentary authority will comprehensively reset the constituency geography for State Assemblies as well, further narrowing the space within which Article 328 would have practical relevance. After a fresh delimitation, the State's only remaining Article 328 power would relate to matters genuinely not covered by Parliament's comprehensive post-delimitation legislation.
