## Why it matters

Article 277 solved a concrete administrative crisis that the framers foresaw clearly. When the Constitution came into force, it reorganised legislative competence over taxation subjects through the Seventh Schedule. Parliament acquired exclusive jurisdiction over the Union List, States acquired exclusive jurisdiction over the State List, and both could legislate on the Concurrent List. But the pre-existing fiscal architecture of British India and the princely states did not map cleanly onto this new allocation. States had been levying taxes on subjects that the Constitution now placed in the Union List. Municipalities had been charging cesses on commodities that Parliament would now regulate. If Article 277 did not exist, every such levy would have been void from the moment the Constitution came into force — leaving States and local bodies without revenue that they depended on, and potentially creating overnight fiscal chaos across hundreds of jurisdictions.

The savings clause allowed the existing fiscal machinery to keep running while the new constitutional order settled in. Parliament could at any time legislate on those subjects and its legislation would supersede the State or local levy — the protection was not a permanent entrenchment of State taxation on Union List subjects. But until Parliament acted, the pre-existing levies continued as if constitutionally authorised. This phased transition design — where existing law continues until specifically displaced by new law — is a recurring structural feature of Indian constitutional drafting, used again in Article 372 (which continued pre-Constitution laws generally) and later replicated in the GST transition provisions.

Article 277 also reveals a deeper principle about the relationship between the Constitution and the pre-existing legal order. The framers did not treat the Constitution's enactment as a complete legislative break — a Day One in which all prior law was void unless re-enacted. Instead, they engineered a continuity mechanism: prior law that was valid under the prior order continues until the new constitutional order displaces it, even if the competence basis for that prior law has shifted. This approach preserved the legitimacy and operability of the pre-existing fiscal system while asserting parliamentary supremacy over its future.

## Reading the provision

**"Any taxes, duties, cesses or fees":** The provision covers all four categories of fiscal levy — taxes (mandatory contributions for general revenue), duties (levies on specific activities or goods), cesses (dedicated surcharges), and fees (charges for specific services). The breadth of this language was deliberate: the framers wanted to protect the entire revenue stream of States and municipalities, not just specific categories of levy. The inclusion of fees is notable — Article 277 extends to user charges and licence fees that might otherwise have been challenged as regulatory impositions on Union List subjects.

**"Immediately before the commencement of this Constitution, were being lawfully levied":** Three conditions are embedded in this phrase. "Immediately before" means on and immediately preceding 26 January 1950 — not simply that the levy existed at some prior point. A tax that was on the books but not being actively collected on that date does not qualify. "Lawfully levied" means the levy had legal authority under the then-applicable provincial or princely state law — a tax collected without statutory backing or under an ultra vires order gets no Article 277 protection. "Were being ... levied" uses the continuous present, confirming that actual, ongoing collection is required, not merely a latent power to levy.

**"By the Government of any State or by any municipality or other local authority or body":** The protection extends not just to State governments but to the full range of sub-State fiscal entities — municipalities, district boards, panchayats, port trusts, and other local authorities. This was important because local body levies were, if anything, more varied and more closely tied to specific Union List subjects (shipping, railways, customs at local wharves) than State government levies. The reference to "other local authority or body" is broad enough to cover both statutory and non-statutory local entities.

**"For the purposes of the State, municipality, district or other local area":** The savings protection is tied to the purpose for which the levy was originally imposed. If a municipality was levying a cess on goods for the purpose of funding local roads, the Article 277 protection preserves that cess for that purpose. The levy cannot be redirected to a different purpose — it continues to be applied to the same purposes it was serving before the Constitution's commencement. This ensures that the savings clause does not become a vehicle for States or municipalities to repurpose pre-Constitutional levies after the fact.

**"Notwithstanding that those taxes, duties, cesses or fees are mentioned in the Union List":** The operative saving. Even if the subject of the levy appears in the Union List — which, as a matter of constitutional allocation, gives Parliament exclusive power to tax it — the pre-Constitutional levy is preserved. The Union List mention does not retroactively strike down what was lawfully imposed before. However, the protection is explicitly conditional on Parliament not having acted: "until provision to the contrary is made by Parliament by law."

**"Until provision to the contrary is made by Parliament by law":** Parliament's power to displace a saved levy is plenary and immediate. Once Parliament enacts legislation on the relevant Union List subject that either expressly or by necessary implication displaces the State or local levy, the Article 277 protection expires. Parliament does not need to enact a specific repeal of the State or local levy — a comprehensive Union enactment on the subject that covers the same ground will suffice. The burden is on the party asserting the Article 277 protection to show that Parliament has not made "provision to the contrary."

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debates on what became Article 277 (Draft Article 264) were brief but focused on a practical concern expressed by several members from provincial governments: revenue continuity for State administrations. Several delegates noted that provincial governments were collecting taxes on items that the new constitutional scheme placed in the Union List, and that an abrupt cutoff would disrupt government finances at the very moment when the new republican administration was getting established.

The drafting committee modelled Article 277 partly on transitional provisions in the Government of India Act 1935, which had similarly saved existing provincial taxes when it transferred subjects between the federal and provincial lists. B.R. Ambedkar emphasised that Article 277 was a practical necessity, not a permanent constitutional adjustment — it did not confer new competence on States or municipalities to levy taxes on Union List subjects, it merely saved what already existed. The distinction between a savings clause (preserving existing powers) and a grants clause (creating new powers) was important to the framers in maintaining the structural integrity of the Seventh Schedule allocation.

Some members expressed concern that the "until provision to the contrary is made by Parliament" language placed too much in Parliament's hands — States might be left indefinitely with fiscal uncertainty about whether a given levy was still protected. The response was that Parliament's legislative activity on Union List subjects would naturally and promptly displace the saved levies, and that Article 265 (no tax without authority of law) would serve as a constitutional backstop if a levy continued to be collected after Parliament had occupied the field.

## Key cases

**Hingir-Rampur Coal Co. Ltd. v. State of Orissa (1961):** The Supreme Court held that Article 277's savings protection is strictly limited to levies that were actually being collected on 26 January 1950. A levy that had been imposed by statute but whose collection had lapsed before that date, or a cess that was first imposed after commencement, cannot claim the Article 277 shield. The Court applied the "immediately before" condition rigorously, confirming that the savings clause is a transitional device, not a retrospective validation mechanism.

**Boddu Paidanna and Sons v. State of Madras (1952):** An early Supreme Court case exploring the interaction between Article 277 and the Union's exclusive legislative competence under the Seventh Schedule. The Court established that Parliament's power to displace a saved levy is plenary — it does not need to expressly repeal the pre-Constitutional State enactment but can displace it by comprehensive Union legislation that covers the same subject. Once displaced, the Article 277 protection cannot revive even if Union legislation is subsequently amended.

**State of Madhya Pradesh v. Mahalaxmi Fabric Mills Ltd. (1995):** The Court examined whether a pre-Constitutional State entry tax on inter-State goods could claim Article 277 protection after Parliament enacted the Central Sales Tax Act. The Court confirmed that the CST Act's comprehensive regulation of inter-State trade taxes constituted "provision to the contrary" under Article 277, displacing the State levy. The ruling established that Parliament's displacing legislation need not be a specific response to a particular Article 277 levy — general Union legislation on the subject suffices.

**State of Orissa v. M.A. Tulloch and Co. (1964):** The Supreme Court considered whether a mineral-related cess levied by the Orissa government before 1950 continued to be valid under Article 277 after Parliament's Mines and Minerals (Regulation and Development) Act occupied the field. The Court held that Union legislation that comprehensively regulates a Union List subject displaces State and local levies previously saved by Article 277, and that the question of displacement is determined by examining the true nature and extent of the Union enactment, not merely its express repeal provisions.

**R.C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970):** While primarily about property rights and compensation, the case engaged with the savings-clause mechanism under Part XII in the context of the transition from pre-Constitutional banking legislation to the post-1950 Union regulatory regime, providing doctrinal guidance on how to determine when Union legislation has "made provision to the contrary" sufficient to displace a pre-Constitutional levy or arrangement.

## Current significance

Article 277's direct operational significance today is minimal — Parliament systematically occupied Union List tax subjects within the first two decades after 1950, and the specific pre-Constitutional levies that Article 277 was designed to protect have long since been displaced. However, the article retains interpretive relevance in two contexts.

First, litigation about the heritage levies of older municipal bodies occasionally invokes Article 277 when a municipal tax is challenged on legislative competence grounds. Some port trust charges, municipal octroi-type levies in older cities, and local development cesses in princely state successor regions trace their legal lineage back to pre-Constitutional instruments. Where a challenge is based on the argument that the levy's subject falls in the Union List, Article 277 may provide a defence — but only if the party can establish that the levy was in fact being collected immediately before 26 January 1950 and that Parliament has not since made provision to the contrary.

Second, and more importantly for contemporary constitutional law, Article 277 is a design precedent that illuminates how India's Constitution handles major fiscal transitions. The One Hundred and First Amendment Act (2016) and the subsequent GST legislation performed a structurally similar function. The CGST Act's transitional provisions — preserving the legal effect of existing assessments, credits, refunds, and pending matters under the pre-GST regime — are the modern equivalent of Article 277's savings logic. Transitional provisions that protect pre-reform legal relationships while the new regime takes over are a persistent design feature of Indian constitutional and fiscal architecture, and Article 277 is their constitutional ancestor.

Read alongside Article 265 (no tax without authority of law), Article 246 and the Seventh Schedule (distribution of legislative competence), and Article 372 (general savings of pre-Constitution laws), Article 277 reveals the constitutional design principle that the establishment of a new constitutional order does not void the pre-existing fiscal apparatus overnight. Rather, the pre-existing order continues within its old scope, under its old legal authority, until the new order makes specific provision. This phased displacement model — rather than an immediate vesting model — has proved to be a durable and pragmatic approach to constitutional transitions with significant fiscal dimensions.
