  320. Functions of Public Service Commissions.—(1) It shall be the duty of the Union and the State Public Service Commissions to conduct examinations for appointments to the services of the Union and the services of the State respectively.

  (2) It shall also be the duty of the Union Public Service Commission, if requested by any two or more States so to do, to assist those States in framing and operating schemes of joint recruitment for any services for which candidates possessing special qualifications are required.

  (3) The Union Public Service Commission or the State Public Service Commission, as the case may be, shall be consulted—

  (a) on all matters relating to methods of recruitment to civil services and for civil posts;

  (b) on the principles to be followed in making appointments to civil services and posts and in making promotions and transfers from one service to another and on the suitability of candidates for such appointments, promotions or transfers;

  (c) on all disciplinary matters affecting a person serving under the Government of India or the Government of a State in a civil capacity, including memorials or petitions relating to such matters;

  (d) on any claim by or in respect of a person who is serving or has served under the Government of India or the Government of a State or under the Crown in India or under the Government of an Indian State, in a civil capacity, that any costs incurred by him in defending legal proceedings instituted against him in respect of acts done or purporting to be done in the execution of his duty should be paid out of the Consolidated Fund of India, or, as the case may be, out of the Consolidated Fund of the State;

  (e) on any claim for the award of a pension in respect of injuries sustained by a person while serving under the Government of India or the Government of a State or under the Crown in India or under the Government of an Indian State, in a civil capacity, and any question as to the amount of any such award, and it shall be the duty of a Public Service Commission to advise on any matter so referred to them and on any other matter which the President, or, as the case may be, the Governor of the State, may refer to them:

  Provided that the President as respects the all-India services and also as respects other services and posts in connection with the affairs of the Union, and the Governor , as respects other services and posts in connection with the affairs of a State, may make regulations specifying the matters in which either generally, or in any particular class of case or in any particular circumstances, it shall not be necessary for a Public Service Commission to be consulted.

  (4) Nothing in clause (3) shall require a Public Service Commission to be consulted as respects the manner in which any provision referred to in clause

  (4) of article 16 may be made or as respects the manner in which effect may be given to the provisions of article 335.

  (5) All regulations made under the proviso to clause (3) by the President or the Governor of a State shall be laid for not less than fourteen days before each House of Parliament or the House or each House of the Legislature of the State, as the case may be, as soon as possible after they are made, and shall be subject to such modifications, whether by way of repeal or amendment, as both Houses of Parliament or the House or both Houses of the Legislature of the State may make during the session in which they are so laid.

## Why it matters

Establishing a Public Service Commission in Article 315 is meaningless unless the Commission actually has something meaningful to do and is consulted before important personnel decisions are made. Article 320 gives the commission its mandate. The two core functions — examining and consulting — are complementary: examination produces a ranked list of candidates, while consultation on promotions, transfers, and disciplinary matters extends the commission's merit-protecting role across the entire arc of a civil servant's career. Without Article 320's consultation requirements, governments could fill posts by examination through the UPSC but then manage the civil service thereafter through purely political promotions and disciplinary decisions that bypass the commission entirely.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) is the examination clause: the UPSC and SPSCs shall conduct examinations for appointments to Union and State services respectively. This duty is mandatory — the commission must conduct examinations, not merely offer to do so.

Clause (2) gives the UPSC an additional cross-State function: on request from two or more States, it can assist in framing and operating joint recruitment schemes for specialised services. This has been used for technical and scientific recruitments.

Clause (3) is the consultation clause and generates the most litigation. The UPSC or SPSC "shall be consulted" on: (a) methods of recruitment to civil services and posts; (b) principles for appointments, promotions, and transfers, and the suitability of candidates; (c) all disciplinary matters including memorials and petitions; (d) claims for costs in defending legal proceedings by civil servants; and (e) claims for pensions for injuries sustained in service. The breadth of the consultation mandate is extensive.

The proviso to clause (3) permits the President or Governor to make regulations exempting specified categories of posts or circumstances from the consultation requirement. This exemption power has been liberally used over the decades — large categories of Group C and Group D posts, and many technical recruitment boards, are now outside mandatory UPSC/SPSC consultation. These regulations must be laid before Parliament or the State Legislature and are subject to legislative modification.

Clause (4) carves out two permanent exemptions from clause (3) consultation: provisions for reservations under Article 16(4) and the carrying out of Article 335 (adequate representation of SCs and STs in services). This ensures the UPSC cannot obstruct reservation policy by refusing to be consulted about its implementation.

The nature of UPSC advice is advisory, not binding. Government may depart from UPSC recommendations, but courts have held that genuine consultation must take place and a departure without recorded reasons may be procedurally vulnerable.

## Key cases

**State of Uttar Pradesh v Manbodhan Lal Srivastava (1957)** — A Constitution Bench drew a crucial distinction between consultation as a constitutional duty on the executive and consultation as a condition precedent for the validity of the appointment. Non-consultation does not automatically void an appointment, but it makes the government's action constitutionally irregular and subject to challenge.

**A K Kraipak v Union of India (1969)** — The Supreme Court held that the UPSC's selection process must comply with natural justice; where a member of the selection committee is also a candidate, the process is vitiated by bias and the selection is void. Article 320 imposes not just a procedural duty to consult but a substantive duty to ensure that the consultation is fair.

**Union of India v K P Joseph (1973)** — The Court examined the UPSC's role in disciplinary matters under clause (3)(c) and confirmed that while the Government is not bound to follow the Commission's advice, it must genuinely consult it. A decision in disciplinary proceedings taken without real consultation with the UPSC, or one that departs from UPSC advice without any explanation, is procedurally defective.

**Durgacharan Naik v State of Orissa (1961)** — The Court held that the exemption regulations made by the President or Governor under the proviso to clause (3) must be within the scope of the permissible exemption and that an exemption so broad as to effectively nullify the consultation requirement would be constitutionally questionable.

## Current significance

Article 320 consultation remains heavily litigated in service tribunals and High Courts. The most common disputes involve promotions where UPSC/SPSC consultation was claimed to be inadequate, disciplinary proceedings where the UPSC's recommendation against dismissal was overridden, and the validity of examination rules changed after the examination process began. The growing list of posts exempt from UPSC consultation under the proviso regulations is periodically challenged on the ground that the exemptions effectively deprive the Article 315 mandate of substance. The exclusion of reservation decisions from the consultation requirement in clause (4) has become more important as courts address the interplay between merit-based UPSC selection and the constitutional obligation to ensure adequate representation of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in public services.
