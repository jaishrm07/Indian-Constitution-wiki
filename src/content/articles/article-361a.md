plainEnglish: >-
  Article 361A gives the press and broadcast media a constitutional shield
  against defamation and other legal claims when they report on what happens
  in Parliament or a State Legislature. A newspaper or broadcaster that
  accurately covers a parliamentary debate — including allegations made by
  members under parliamentary privilege — cannot be sued simply because
  someone mentioned in the proceedings is unhappy with the report. Two
  conditions must be met: the report must be substantially true (not a
  distorted or misleading selection of what was said), and the publication

  from the protection. The provision was inserted by the 44th Amendment
  in 1978, as part of a post-Emergency constitutional restoration that
  recognised the importance of press freedom in parliamentary democracy.
officialText: |-
  [361A. Protection of publication of proceedings of Parliament and State Legislatures.—(1) No person shall be liable to any proceedings, civil or criminal, in any court in respect of the publication in a newspaper of a substantially true report of any proceedings of either House of Parliament or the Legislative Assembly, or, as the case may be, either House of the Legislature, of a State, unless the publication is proved to have been made with malice:

  Provided that nothing in this clause shall apply to the publication of any report of the proceedings of a secret sitting of either House of Parliament or the Legislative Assembly, or, as the case may be, either House of the Legislature, of a State.

  (2) Clause (1) shall apply in relation to reports or matters broadcast by means of wireless telegraphy as part of any programme or service provided by means of a broadcasting station as it applies in relation to reports or matters published in a newspaper.

  Explanation.—In this article, "newspaper" includes a news agency report containing material for publication in a newspaper.]

## Why it matters

The constitutional design of democratic accountability requires both a legislature that
can speak freely and a press that can report that speech accurately. These two
requirements are not self-executing: they need constitutional infrastructure. Articles
105 and 194 provide absolute privilege for legislative speech; Article 361A provides
the corresponding protection for press reporting of that speech. Without both limbs
of this structure, either the legislature is effectively silenced or the press is
effectively muzzled. Article 361A closes the second gap.

Democratic accountability depends on citizens knowing what is said and decided in their
legislature. Without that knowledge, parliamentary debate is little more than a
performance before an absent audience. Before Article 361A was inserted in 1978, the
press faced a structural vulnerability: a member could allege on the floor of the House
that a named private citizen had committed fraud, corruption, or any offence, and the
newspaper that accurately reported the allegation could face a defamation action from
the aggrieved person. The legal risk fell on the reporter, not the speaker — creating
a chilling effect that discouraged media coverage of the most contentious legislative
proceedings.

Article 361A resolves this asymmetry by extending a qualified immunity to the downstream
reporting of parliamentary proceedings. The immunity is not absolute — it requires
substantial truth and absence of malice — but within those limits it ensures that the
press can report parliamentary allegations, debates, and motions without facing legal
consequences for doing so. This reflects a constitutional judgment that the democratic
value of an informed citizenry outweighs the individual interest in not having
parliamentary allegations widely disseminated.

The provision was inserted by the Constitution (Forty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, the
Janata government's response to the constitutional damage done during the 1975–77
Emergency. That Emergency involved systematic press censorship — newspapers were blacked
out, correspondents were arrested, and legislative proceedings were manipulated to
preclude informed reporting. The 44th Amendment reversed a number of Emergency-era
changes and constitutionalised press freedoms that had previously rested on ordinary
law. Article 361A was one element of this constitutional restoration.

The scope of parliamentary proceedings covered by Article 361A is broad — it covers
reports of proceedings of either House of Parliament or the Legislative Assembly, or
either House of the Legislature, of a State. This means the protection extends to State
Legislature proceedings as much as to parliamentary ones, recognising that the
democratic argument for transparency applies at both levels. The live-streaming of
parliamentary proceedings via Lok Sabha TV, Rajya Sabha TV, and online platforms is
constitutionally sheltered by Article 361A's clause (2) extension to broadcasts.

The provision also reflects the constitutional relationship between freedom of speech
under Article 19(1)(a) and the special status of legislative proceedings. Article 19(2)
permits reasonable restrictions on free speech; Article 19(1)(a) would not by itself
provide an absolute shield for parliamentary reporting. Article 361A provides a
specific and stronger protection, occupying constitutional space that Article 19 alone
could not fill because the "reasonable restrictions" framework would allow Parliament
to impose liability for accurate parliamentary reporting if it chose to do so.

## Reading the provision

The 44th Amendment's insertion of Article 361A followed extensive parliamentary debate
about the appropriate scope of press protection for parliamentary reporting. Several
members of Parliament who had been imprisoned or harassed during the Emergency argued
that constitutional protection was essential rather than merely desirable. The
alternative — relying on ordinary legislation to protect parliamentary reporting — was
shown by the Emergency to be inadequate, since ordinary legislation can be repealed or
overridden by the same Parliament whose proceedings it protects. Only a constitutional
provision could give the press a protection that Parliament itself could not easily remove.

Clause (1) establishes the core protection. "No person" — extending beyond newspapers
to include anyone who publishes — "shall be liable to any proceedings, civil or
criminal" — covering defamation, criminal contempt, breach of privilege claims, and any
other form of legal liability — in respect of the publication "in a newspaper" of a
"substantially true report" of "any proceedings" of Parliament or a State Legislature,
"unless the publication is proved to have been made with malice." The scope is broad
and the burden of proving malice rests on the claimant.

The qualifier "substantially true" does the most interpretive work in the article.
Verbatim accuracy is not required, but the report must fairly represent what was said
or done. A selective excerpt that gives the impression a member made an allegation she
actually refuted in the same speech would not be substantially true — the omission
would distort the meaning of the proceedings. Equally, accurate quotation placed in
a misleading context would undermine the substantial truth requirement. Courts have
read this condition purposively, asking whether an ordinary reader would come away with
a substantially accurate understanding of what occurred.

The "malice" condition is the second gateway. In this context malice means an improper
purpose: using the publication of parliamentary proceedings as a vehicle to injure a
specific person, rather than to inform the public of what was debated and decided. A
journalist who selects and edits a parliamentary report specifically to harm a named
individual, knowing it does so unfairly, acts with malice even if every quoted sentence
is technically accurate. The burden of proving this malice is on the person seeking
to overcome the constitutional protection, making it a high bar to cross.

Clause (2) extends the protection to broadcasts. It was drafted with All India Radio
in mind but the reference to "wireless telegraphy" and "broadcasting station" has been
construed to include television and, by analogy, modern digital broadcast media. The
identical substantive conditions — substantial truth and absence of malice — apply
to broadcasts. Clause (2) does not expressly address online news portals or social
media livestreams of parliamentary proceedings, and courts have had to consider whether
these fall within the extended protection by analogy.

An important and unresolved question under Article 361A is the extent to which edited
clips, summary reports, and social media posts of parliamentary proceedings attract
the constitutional protection. The "substantially true report" condition is
straightforward for a comprehensive newspaper account of a debate. It is less clear for
a thirty-second video clip of a single statement, or for a social media post that quotes
a single sentence from a multi-hour debate. Courts have not definitively addressed
whether such partial reports satisfy the substantial truth condition, and the question
is becoming increasingly practically significant.

## Relationship with parliamentary privilege

Articles 105(1) and 105(2) give members of Parliament absolute immunity from court
proceedings for anything said or done in Parliament in the exercise of their membership.
This immunity is complete and does not require truth or good faith — a member may say
anything in Parliament, however defamatory or false, without legal consequence. The
comparable provision for State Legislatures is Article 194.

Article 361A is the press analogue of this privilege, but it is structurally different
in two respects. First, it is qualified rather than absolute — the substantial truth
and absence of malice conditions limit it. Second, its beneficiary is not the author
of the statement (who is already protected by Articles 105 and 194) but the reporter.
The two provisions work in sequence: the member speaks under absolute privilege; the
journalist reports under Article 361A's qualified privilege; and the public receives
information about parliamentary proceedings under the constitutional protection that
both together provide.

Without this sequential structure, the absolute privilege of members would be
constitutionally self-defeating. If reports of parliamentary allegations were legally
actionable, the chilling effect on the press would be so severe that parliamentary
privilege would operate in a constitutional vacuum — insulating the speaker while
preventing the speech from reaching anyone.

## Secret sittings

The proviso in clause (1) withholds the protection entirely for reports of secret
sittings. Both Houses of Parliament have procedural rules permitting sittings in camera
when sensitive matters — national security, intelligence operations, diplomatic
negotiations — are under discussion. The constitutional protection for reporting does
not extend to these sessions because the House itself has determined that the public
interest in confidentiality outweighs the public interest in transparency for those
proceedings. A journalist who obtains and publishes what was said in a secret sitting
receives no Article 361A protection even if the report is accurate and made without
malice.

## Key cases

**Searchlight case — M S M Sharma v Sri Krishna Sinha (1959) SCR 806** — Decided before
Article 361A was inserted, this case considered whether the Bihar Legislature's resolution
directing the editor of a newspaper to appear and apologise for publishing expunged
remarks was valid. The Court upheld the power of the legislature to take such action.
The case illustrates the pre-1978 constitutional gap that Article 361A was subsequently
designed to address — the press had no constitutional immunity and was vulnerable to
legislative action for reporting parliamentary proceedings.

**Keshav Singh v Speaker, UP Legislative Assembly (1965)** — A seven-judge bench
examined the powers of a State Legislature over a person who circulated a pamphlet
about proceedings of the House. Though the case turned on contempt rather than
defamation, it engaged the broader relationship between press freedom and parliamentary
privilege in the pre-Article 361A constitutional framework. The judgment revealed the
precarious position of the press in relation to legislative proceedings before the
44th Amendment provided constitutional protection.

**Tej Kiran Jain v N Sanjiva Reddy (1970) 2 SCC 272** — The Supreme Court held that a
defamation action arising out of a speech in Parliament was barred by Article 105(2).
The Court confirmed that the parliamentary privilege was absolute for members, but the
case also illustrated the asymmetry — the newspaper reporting the speech had no
equivalent constitutional protection, a gap eventually closed by Article 361A.

**P V Narasimha Rao v State (CBI/SPE) (1998) 4 SCC 626** — While primarily concerned
with the scope of parliamentary speech and vote immunity under Article 105(2) in the
JMM bribery case, the Supreme Court's analysis of the policy rationale for parliamentary
privilege is directly relevant to understanding why Article 361A protection for press
reporting of those proceedings is constitutionally necessary. The democratic rationale
for privilege is only fully effective if the proceedings are reportable.

**Subramanian Swamy v Union of India (2016) 7 SCC 221** — The constitutional bench
upheld criminal defamation under Sections 499 and 500 IPC as a reasonable restriction
on free speech under Article 19(2). The judgment explicitly noted that constitutional
provisions including Article 361A provide specific immunities for protected categories
of speech, and that the existence of these targeted immunities is consistent with the
broader framework of qualified speech rights under Article 19. The ruling reinforced
the doctrinal significance of Article 361A as a specific constitutional protection
standing alongside the general Article 19 framework.

## Current significance

Article 361A's significance has expanded as the forms of parliamentary reporting have
multiplied. When the provision was inserted in 1978, "newspaper" and "broadcast" were
the principal channels through which legislative proceedings reached the public. Today,
live-streaming of parliamentary proceedings, video clipping on social media, podcast
summaries, and news aggregation sites all raise the question of whether Article 361A's
protection applies. Courts have generally construed the provision's scope broadly,
consistent with its democratic rationale, but the specific inclusion of online portals
in the protection remains an area of evolving law.

The provision's practical importance also arises in the context of contempt and privilege
proceedings initiated by State Legislatures against journalists. When a legislature
attempts to take punitive action against a journalist for accurately reporting its
proceedings, Article 361A provides a constitutional defence. The key contested questions
are whether the report was substantially true and whether any malice was proved —
questions that courts will examine independently, without deferring entirely to the
legislature's own assessment.

The relationship between Article 361A and the constitutional guarantee under Article
19(1)(a) was clarified in Subramanian Swamy: Article 361A is a specific constitutional
provision that operates alongside the general free speech framework. A journalist facing
liability for parliamentary reporting can invoke Article 361A directly as a constitutional
bar to proceedings; she does not need to show that the restriction on her speech was
unreasonable under Article 19(2). This makes Article 361A an important free-standing
constitutional protection rather than a dependent sub-rule of Article 19.
