officialText: |-
  123. Power of President to promulgate Ordinances during recess of Parliament.—(1) If at any time, except when both Houses of Parliament are in session, the President is satisfied that circumstances exist which render it necessary for him to take immediate action, he may promulgate such Ordinances as the circumstances appear to him to require.

  (2) An Ordinance promulgated under this article shall have the same force and effect as an Act of Parliament, but every such Ordinance—

  (a) shall be laid before both Houses of Parliament and shall cease to operate at the expiration of six weeks from the reassembly of Parliament, or, if before the expiration of that period resolutions disapproving it are passed by both Houses, upon the passing of the second of those resolutions; and

  (b) may be withdrawn at any time by the President.

  Explanation.—Where the Houses of Parliament are summoned to reassemble on different dates, the period of six weeks shall be reckoned from the later of those dates for the purposes of this clause.

  (3) If and so far as an Ordinance under this article makes any provision which Parliament would not under this Constitution be competent to enact, it shall be void.

## Why it matters

The Ordinance power under Article 123 is a constitutional concession to the reality that urgent legislative action may sometimes be needed when Parliament is not in session. It gives the executive temporary legislative capacity without requiring Parliament to be continuously in session. But it also creates a structural tension: a power that allows the executive to legislate without Parliament, even temporarily, is a power that can be abused to bypass the legislature on politically sensitive measures, particularly when the government has difficulty managing its parliamentary majority. The history of Article 123 is largely the history of the courts calibrating the limits of that power.

The Ordinance power is one of a cluster of presidential emergency powers under the Constitution — distinct from, but conceptually adjacent to, the proclamation powers under Articles 352, 356, and 360. Unlike those provisions, Article 123 requires no formal emergency; it requires only that Parliament is not in session and that the President is satisfied that circumstances require immediate action. This low threshold makes it the most frequently used executive extraordinary power in Indian constitutional practice.

The framers borrowed the Ordinance power from the Government of India Act, 1935, which gave the Governor-General similar authority. The provision was retained in the Constitution as a practical necessity but was intended to be used sparingly, with Parliament as the primary source of legislative authority. The subsequent history of the provision — characterised by increasing routine use and the re-promulgation controversy — represents a significant departure from the framers' intention, and one that the Supreme Court has only partially corrected through constitutional adjudication.

## Reading the provision

**The triggering condition:** The President may issue an Ordinance only when Parliament is not in session — at least one House must be in recess. The President must be "satisfied that circumstances exist which render it necessary for him to take immediate action." The satisfaction is the President's own, but the President acts on Cabinet advice under Article 74. In practice, Ordinances are Cabinet decisions executed through the President.

**The legislative equivalence:** An Ordinance has the same force and effect as an Act of Parliament. It can amend or repeal existing legislation, create new offences, alter rights, and override State laws on concurrent-list subjects — exactly as a parliamentary statute can. The only structural limitation is clause (3): an Ordinance cannot do what Parliament itself could not do. An Ordinance that transgresses constitutional limits on legislative power — including fundamental rights — is void.

**The six-week sunset:** Every Ordinance must be laid before both Houses when Parliament reassembles. It ceases to operate six weeks after reassembly unless it is earlier:
- disapproved by resolutions of both Houses (ceases from passing of the second resolution); or
- withdrawn by the President.

If Parliament simply does nothing within six weeks, the Ordinance lapses automatically. If the Houses reassemble on different dates, the six-week period runs from the later date.

**The re-promulgation problem:** The six-week lapse mechanism is designed to ensure that every Ordinance receives parliamentary scrutiny. Governments discovered that they could avoid this by re-promulgating — issuing a new, identical Ordinance just before the existing one lapsed, thereby perpetually extending the legislative measure without ever putting it before Parliament. The Bihar government re-promulgated some Ordinances dozens of times over many years. This practice was declared unconstitutional in D. C. Wadhwa, and the unconstitutionality was confirmed and deepened by the seven-judge bench in Krishna Kumar Singh.

**Article 123 and emergency powers:** The Ordinance power is distinct from the emergency provisions under Articles 352–360. It does not require a national emergency or any special circumstances beyond presidential satisfaction that immediate action is needed. It is an ordinary executive tool for the inter-session period, not an emergency power — though it may be used during emergencies as well.

**Scope of Ordinance-making power:** An Ordinance may be promulgated on any subject within Parliament's legislative competence — Union List, Concurrent List, and (in some circumstances) subjects on the State List when Parliament has acquired authority under Articles 249, 250, or 253. An Ordinance on a State List subject not covered by any such expanded jurisdiction would be void under clause (3).

## Key cases

**R. C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970):** The Supreme Court held that the Bank Nationalisation Ordinance was subject to judicial review as an executive act affecting fundamental rights, specifically the right to property under Article 19(1)(f). The Court rejected the argument that the Ordinance's legislative force made it immune from challenge, establishing that Ordinances are reviewable for violations of fundamental rights just as Acts of Parliament are.

**D. C. Wadhwa v. State of Bihar (1987):** A landmark three-judge bench decision declaring that the systematic re-promulgation of Ordinances without placing them before the legislature is a constitutional fraud and a subversion of parliamentary democracy. The Court held that the Ordinance power under Article 213 (the State-level equivalent) must be exercised in good faith as an emergency measure, not as a routine substitute for legislation. The State of Bihar had re-promulgated some Ordinances continuously for years. The Court declared the practice unconstitutional.

**A. K. Roy v. Union of India (1982):** The Supreme Court, upholding the National Security (Second Amendment) Ordinance against a habeas corpus challenge, confirmed that Presidential satisfaction for an Ordinance is subject to limited judicial review. Courts can examine whether satisfaction was arrived at in bad faith, mala fide, or in circumstances making it constitutionally impermissible — but cannot substitute their own judgment for the President's on the question of urgency.

**Krishna Kumar Singh v. State of Bihar (2017):** A seven-judge Constitution Bench — the most significant Article 123 authority — comprehensively overhauled the law on Ordinances. The bench held:
- (i) Presidential and Gubernatorial satisfaction for promulgating an Ordinance is subject to judicial review;
- (ii) an Ordinance must not be used as a tool to bypass legislative scrutiny;
- (iii) re-promulgation is unconstitutional as affirmed in D. C. Wadhwa — the Bihar government's successive re-promulgations of the Bihar Intermediate Education Council Ordinance were declared void;
- (iv) a lapsed Ordinance cannot by itself generate permanent rights, vested interests, or completed transactions that estop courts from granting relief — unless those rights were independently acquired through valid transactions during the Ordinance's currency.

## Current significance

Ordinance-making has accelerated at both Union and State levels in recent decades, with the Union government promulgating dozens of Ordinances between parliamentary sessions. Critics regularly argue that Ordinances are issued not because Parliament cannot be assembled but because the government anticipates parliamentary resistance. The Krishna Kumar Singh ruling established constitutional limits on re-promulgation, but enforcement remains difficult: by the time a court rules on an Ordinance's constitutional validity, the Ordinance has typically been replaced by an Act of Parliament, mooting the specific challenge. The deeper constitutional question — whether the President, on Cabinet advice, may decline to promulgate an Ordinance on the ground that the claimed urgency is pretextual — remains unresolved. The parallel question at the State level is whether a Governor may withhold Ordinance promulgation recommended by the Council of Ministers, a question that mirrors the controversy over gubernatorial discretion in assent under Article 200.

Several significant Ordinances have been issued on matters of major constitutional and economic consequence — including the Land Acquisition (Amendment) Ordinances of 2014–2015, which were re-promulgated after Parliament failed to pass the corresponding Bill, and the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (Amendment) Ordinance of 2017. In each case, the Ordinance created immediate legal obligations that affected private rights, raising questions about how the six-week lapse rule interacts with obligations already incurred. Krishna Kumar Singh's holding that lapsed Ordinances cannot create permanent rights is easier to state than to apply: courts have struggled to determine which obligations incurred under an Ordinance survive its lapse and which are extinguished.

The Constitution places the Ordinance power in the President's hands, exercised on Cabinet advice. This makes Ordinances ultimately accountable to Parliament rather than being immune from parliamentary control — because Parliament can pass a disapproving resolution and because the six-week lapse mechanism forces the government to obtain parliamentary approval for any Ordinance it wants to maintain. The systemic problem is not that the mechanism lacks accountability in theory but that in practice the government can control the parliamentary timetable, ensuring that Ordinances are tabled but never voted on within the six-week window, simply lapsing without ever being put to a parliamentary test of approval or disapproval.
