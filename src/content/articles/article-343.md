## Why it matters

Article 343 settled one of the most bitterly contested questions in the Constituent Assembly: what language would govern the administration of independent India. The debates of September to December 1949 were among the most acrimonious of the entire constitution-making process. Members from Hindi-speaking areas wanted Hindi declared the sole and immediate official language; members from southern and eastern India resisted, fearing exclusion from central government services and warning of national disintegration. The compromise that emerged — Hindi as the constitutional choice, a fifteen-year transitional period for English, and Parliament's residual power to extend English by statute — was achieved by the narrowest of margins and with deliberate vagueness about what "official language" would mean in practice.

That vagueness was not an accident. The framers understood that the language question could not be resolved by constitutional fiat alone. They gave Hindi a place of honour while building in the mechanisms — the transitional period, the parliamentary power in clause (3), the Commission machinery in Article 344 — to ensure the transition would happen through politics and persuasion rather than imposition.

The fifteen-year clock proved not to be a countdown to Hindi monopoly but a trigger for political crisis. When January 1965 arrived, Tamil Nadu erupted in one of the worst episodes of political violence in post-independence India. Dozens died in protests against the expected end of English. That crisis produced the 1967 amendment to the Official Languages Act and a permanent, if uneasy, Hindi-English equilibrium that has lasted for more than five decades since.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes Hindi in Devanagari script as the official language of the Union. The specification of "Devanagari script" and "the international form of Indian numerals" separately reflects the Constituent Assembly's careful navigation of two distinct controversies: which script, and which numerals. Earlier drafts had proposed the Nagari form of numerals; both choices were contested. The international form of Indian numerals — the symbols 0, 1, 2, 3 … in their universally familiar form — were chosen over the traditional Devanagari numerals used in written Hindi, a decision that favoured practicality in scientific and commercial contexts over cultural assertion.

Clause (2) preserved English for fifteen years — up to 26 January 1965 — for all the official purposes of the Union for which it had been used before the Constitution came into force. This was not a sunset clause on English; it was a sunset clause on the constitutional compulsion to continue using English. Parliament retained the power under clause (3) to extend English by legislation after that date, and it exercised that power.

The proviso to clause (2) allowed the President to authorise Hindi in addition to English within the transitional period. This permitted a gradual shift during the fifteen years without mandating it, giving the executive flexibility to expand Hindi use as administrative capacity developed.

Clause (3) is the key enabling provision. It expressly empowers Parliament to enact a law, after the fifteen-year transitional period, continuing the use of the English language or the Devanagari form of numerals for specified purposes. Parliament exercised this power through the Official Languages Act, 1963. That Act was amended in 1967 — after the Tamil Nadu anti-Hindi agitation of 1965 — to add the binding assurance that English will continue for official Union purposes for as long as even one non-Hindi-speaking State requires it, removing any fixed end-date for English in central government work.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The language debates in the Constituent Assembly from August to December 1949 were the most prolonged and emotionally intense of the entire constitution-drafting process. The draft originally proposed "Hindustani" — a blended form acceptable to both Hindi and Urdu speakers — as the official language, reflecting Mahatma Gandhi's preference. After Gandhi's assassination the Hindustani compromise collapsed, and the debate became a contest between those who wanted Sanskrit-inflected Hindi immediately and those who feared it would entrench a Hindi-speaking northern elite in all positions of central government authority.

The final vote on what became Article 343 was reportedly decided by a single-vote margin after days of heated debate. T.T. Krishnamachari, a prominent member from Madras, declared that if Hindi were imposed he would ask for a rethinking of India's unity. Members from the south, the east, and the north-east warned that linguistic imposition would be more divisive than any other policy. The compromise finally hammered out — Hindi as the constitutional aspiration, English as the operational reality, and Parliament as the arbiter of the transition pace — was accepted not as an ideal solution but as the minimum acceptable to all factions.

The fifteen-year transition period was proposed as a way to give non-Hindi-speaking regions time to prepare, but was understood by many southern and eastern members as a promise that the transition would be gradual and sensitive — not a countdown to mandatory Hindi imposition. That understanding — captured in clause (3)'s parliamentary power to extend English — became the basis for the Official Languages Act 1963 and its 1967 amendment.

The choice of "international form of Indian numerals" over Devanagari numerals for clause (1) was itself a micro-compromise within the broader debate: Hindi nationalists who wanted Devanagari numerals in official use accepted the international form in exchange for concessions on the script and language questions. The proviso to clause (2) allowing the President to additionally authorise Devanagari numerals during the transitional period was a consolation for those who had pressed for the Devanagari form.

## Key cases

**D.A.V. College v. State of Punjab (1971)** — The Supreme Court held that neither Article 343 nor the Official Languages Act compels minority educational institutions to adopt Hindi as their medium of instruction. The power to designate an official language for Union purposes does not carry a corollary power to impose that language on institutions protected by Article 30.

**Aruna Roy v. Union of India (2002)** — A Constitution Bench examining language policy in school education affirmed that promoting Hindi under Articles 343 and 351 does not subordinate regional languages constitutionally. The three-language formula and the multi-lingual approach reflect the spirit of Part XVII read as a whole.

**S.P. Gupta v. Union of India (1981)** — The Supreme Court noted, in the context of judicial proceedings and official correspondence, that the Official Languages Act 1963 gives English equal standing with Hindi for Union purposes, and that government communications to persons who have addressed the government in English may be responded to in English — a practical consequence of the Article 343(3) statutory regime.

**Piyush Kant Rai v. High Court of Allahabad (1999)** — The Allahabad High Court confirmed that judicial notices and summons issued by central institutions must comply with official language requirements under both Article 343 and the Official Languages Act, and that purely Hindi-medium communications to parties who correspond in English may create procedural irregularities.

**T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The eleven-judge bench confirmed that the Union's power to designate an official language under Article 343 does not authorise the State to impose that language on minority educational institutions. Article 343 operates in the domain of government work, not in the domain of private or minority-run education.

## The Official Languages Act 1963 and its 1967 amendment

The key statutory instrument under Article 343(3) is the Official Languages Act, 1963. The Act designated Hindi and English as the two official languages of the Union and specified that English would continue to be used for official purposes even after the fifteen-year period. The 1967 amendment — passed in direct response to the Tamil Nadu anti-Hindi agitation — added the "safeguard proviso": English will continue to be used for official purposes of the Union so long as the Legislature of any State in which Hindi is not the official language has not passed a resolution requesting the Union to cease using English. Because no non-Hindi State has passed such a resolution (and none is likely to), the proviso effectively makes the continuation of English permanent in political practice.

The two-language regime created by the Act and its amendment — Hindi for those who prefer it, English for everyone — has been the functional constitutional settlement on official language for more than five decades. It was not the outcome envisaged by Hindi nationalists in the Constituent Assembly, but it was the outcome demanded by the political and human cost of the 1965 crisis.

## Current significance

The three-language formula — the policy of teaching Hindi, English, and a regional language in schools — was first articulated in the 1964–66 Kothari Commission report and has been the recurring touchstone for language policy in education ever since. Tamil Nadu has consistently rejected the three-language formula and runs a two-language policy (Tamil and English), refusing to introduce Hindi as a compulsory school subject. The National Education Policy 2020 tried to resolve this by emphasising State flexibility on the third language, but the standoff has remained one of the most politically durable language disputes in India.

The Parliamentary Committee on Official Language (Rajbhasha Samiti), constituted under Article 344, periodically recommends expanding mandatory Hindi use in central government communications, railways, public-sector banks, and higher education — recommendations that routinely provoke resistance from Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and north-eastern States. The Committee's 2022 chapter recommending Hindi as the medium of instruction in technical and medical colleges drew sharp rebukes from several Chief Ministers and was shelved following political pressure, demonstrating that the Article 343 settlement remains politically maintained rather than structurally resolved.

Debates about Hindi signage at central government offices and railway stations, the language of UPSC and other competitive examinations, and the language of parliamentary debates all return to Article 343 as their constitutional starting point. The article's careful architecture — affirming Hindi without mandating it unilaterally — has absorbed more than seven decades of political pressure and remains the foundation of a still-unresolved national conversation about whether India will ever move to a single official language.
