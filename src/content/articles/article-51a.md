## Why it matters

Article 51A, inserted by the Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act 1976, introduced a chapter of fundamental duties into the Constitution for the first time. No other chapter of duties existed in the original text: the Constitution had been drafted with fundamental rights and directive principles as its two normative frameworks, and the inclusion of duties as a distinct Part IVA was a significant structural addition. The Swaran Singh Committee, which recommended the fundamental duties, drew on socialist constitutional models — particularly the Soviet Constitution — that paired individual rights with citizen obligations. But the framers of the 42nd Amendment were careful not to make the duties directly enforceable: unlike rights, duties cannot be taken to court for direct enforcement, and Article 51A cannot be used to override or restrict Fundamental Rights.

Originally ten in number, an eleventh duty — clause (k) — regarding parental provision for a child's education was added by the 86th Amendment in 2002, paired with the insertion of Article 21A. This pairing reflects the constitutional structure of correlative rights and responsibilities: the State bears the primary obligation to provide schooling under Article 21A; parents and guardians bear a corresponding constitutional duty under 51A(k) to ensure their child has access to it.

The duties are non-justiciable in the direct sense: no citizen can be hauled into court and penalised simply for breaching a duty listed in Article 51A without legislation imposing a specific obligation. But courts have developed the duties into powerful interpretive tools — using them to construe statutes, evaluate legislative restrictions on rights, impose obligations on institutions performing public functions, and signal the constitutional community's normative expectations of all citizens.

## Reading the provision

The eleven duties cluster into four groups. The first cluster — clauses (a) to (d) — concerns loyalty to the constitutional order and national community: respecting the Constitution and national symbols, cherishing the values of the national movement, upholding national integrity, and defending the country. These duties are civic and nationalistic in character. The second cluster — clauses (e) and (f) — addresses social harmony and cultural stewardship: promoting brotherhood, renouncing practices derogatory to women's dignity, and preserving India's composite cultural heritage. The third cluster — clauses (g) and (h) — addresses the natural world and the life of the mind: protecting the environment and developing scientific temper, humanism, and the spirit of inquiry. Clauses (i) and (j) address civic responsibility and personal and collective excellence. Clause (k) stands apart as the direct correlate of a fundamental right.

Courts have used Article 51A in three main ways. First, as a statutory interpretation tool: when a statute can be read in multiple ways, the reading consistent with constitutional duties is preferred. Second, as justification for legislative restrictions on rights: a law that implements a constitutional duty is treated as serving a compelling constitutional purpose, which is relevant in assessing whether restrictions on Article 19 rights are reasonable. Third, as a source of obligations for institutions: courts have invoked Article 51A to impose environmental, educational, and public-welfare obligations on educational institutions, corporations, and government bodies, treating compliance with constitutional duties as a condition of the institutional privileges those bodies enjoy.

Critically, Article 51A cannot be read in a way that narrows fundamental rights or licenses coercion in the name of duty. The Supreme Court has been firm that the non-justiciable character of the duties is not merely formal — it reflects a constitutional choice to express collective values through exhortation and interpretation rather than coercion. Attempts to use Article 51A to justify censorship, compelled expression, or enforced patriotism have been rejected as inconsistent with the rights-protective design of the Constitution.

## Key cases

**AIIMS Students Union v. AIIMS (2001)** — The Supreme Court held that fundamental duties under Article 51A are constitutionally relevant to the interpretation of statutes and to the assessment of State action. The Court deployed Article 51A(j) — the duty to strive for excellence — alongside Articles 14 and 21 to assess the constitutional validity of merit-based admission procedures in medical institutions. Justice Ruma Pal's opinion treated the duty as evidence that the constitutional scheme values meritocratic excellence as a civic virtue with constitutional recognition, and that admission policies promoting excellence are consistent with the constitutional design. The judgment is the clearest judicial statement that fundamental duties function as interpretive aids capable of influencing the outcome of rights-based challenges.

**Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala (1986)** — The Court, while upholding Jehovah's Witness students' right not to sing the national anthem, examined the scope of the duty under Article 51A(a) to respect the National Anthem and held that standing respectfully without singing constitutes adequate compliance. The judgment is a foundational statement that Article 51A duties must be interpreted harmoniously with fundamental rights: the duty to respect the national anthem does not authorise the State to compel active participation against a citizen's sincere religious conviction under Article 25. Rights and duties coexist; duties do not override rights.

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Environmental cases, 1987 onwards)** — In a long series of environmental public interest litigation cases, the Court cited Article 51A(g) — the duty to protect and improve the natural environment — alongside Article 48A's directive to the State to develop the constitutional foundation for environmental protection obligations. The duty has been used to extend obligations to private actors — corporations, industries, citizens — who would not otherwise be constitutional duty-bearers, treating the constitutional duty as evidence that environmental protection is a community obligation, not merely a State regulatory function.

**Ranganatha Misra Commission Report and subsequent decisions** — The National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution (2002) and the Ranganatha Misra Commission (2007) both recommended making fundamental duties justiciable, at least in part. Courts have since cited the duties in decisions on national flag use, cultural heritage protection, and the obligation of educational institutions to teach civic values — treating the duties as establishing constitutional standards that Parliament may, and in some instances should, translate into enforceable norms.

## Current significance

There is ongoing academic and political debate about whether fundamental duties should be made directly justiciable, either through umbrella legislation or by connecting them to specific sanctions. Parliament has already enacted legislation in several duty-related areas: environmental protection statutes, laws criminalising practices derogatory to women's dignity, and provisions protecting public property — all traceable to corresponding duties under Article 51A.

Article 51A(h)'s duty to develop the scientific temper is invoked in debates about religious practices, pseudoscience in public policy, and curriculum design, particularly where courts are asked whether State-supported activities — teaching astrology in universities, funding non-scientific medical systems — are consistent with the constitutional commitment to humanism and inquiry.

Article 51A(e)'s duty to renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women has been cited in gender equality litigation as a constitutional reinforcement of statutory prohibitions on dowry, female foeticide, and religious exclusions of women. Combined with the equality guarantees in Articles 14 and 15, and the right to life under Article 21, the duty adds a constitutional obligations dimension to cases where the courts are asked to develop standards of behaviour consistent with the constitutional vision of gender equality.

Clause (k)'s duty on parents and guardians correlates directly with the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009, raising the question of whether Article 51A(k) creates obligations on parents that the State can legislate to uphold — a question relevant to debates about home schooling, minority educational institutions, and the conditions under which parental choice in education can lawfully be constrained in the child's interests.
