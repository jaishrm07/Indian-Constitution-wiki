## Why it matters

India came to independence as a country with hundreds of languages and a deep question about how its federal units — States organised partly on linguistic lines — would communicate with each other and with the Union. Article 346 provides the common medium for that communication. It ties inter-State and State-Union correspondence to whatever language is authorised for Union official purposes at any given time — a dynamic reference rather than a fixed constitutional choice — meaning that Parliament retains the power to change the official language regime through statute, and that change automatically updates Article 346's default rule.

The provision is a critical pillar of linguistic federalism precisely because it distinguishes between internal State communication (subject to State choice under Article 345) and external governmental communication (subject to the Union's authorised language). Without this distinction, a State government could insist on using only its own official language in correspondence with the Union or other States, creating administrative gridlock in a country where State languages span five major families and dozens of distinct scripts. Article 346's default rule eliminates that possibility: every State, regardless of its own official language, must communicate with the Union and with other States in the Union-authorised language.

The stakes of this provision are heightened by its interaction with the politics of Hindi. When the fifteen-year transitional period under Article 343(2) ended in 1965 and the Hindi lobby sought to make Hindi the sole official language, the resulting anti-Hindi agitations in Tamil Nadu forced the enactment of the 1967 amendment to the Official Languages Act, which continued English as a co-authorised language. Because Article 346 ties inter-governmental communication to the Union's authorised languages, that amendment directly preserved English as a constitutionally compliant medium for all inter-governmental correspondence with non-Hindi States. Article 346, read against the Official Languages Act, is the constitutional anchor for the continuing use of English in federal administrative correspondence.

## Reading the provision

The main rule is deceptively simple but has a significant moving part. The official language for inter-State and State-Union communication is "the language for the time being authorised for use in the Union for official purposes." This is not a self-contained constitutional answer — it requires one to look at the Official Languages Act 1963 and any rules made under it. Under the Official Languages Act as it stands, both Hindi and English are authorised for Union official purposes. This means a State may communicate with the Union in either Hindi or English, and the Union must be capable of receiving and responding in both.

The practical operation is governed by the Official Languages Rules, 1976, made under the Official Languages Act. These Rules divide the country into three "regions" for the purposes of inter-governmental correspondence: Region A (Hindi-speaking States and Union territories), Region B (States with substantial Hindi use, primarily Manipur, Punjab, and Gujarat), and Region C (States where English is the predominant inter-governmental medium — southern States, West Bengal, and north-eastern States). Communications between Region A offices and offices in Regions B and C are to be in both Hindi and English, while communications within Region A can be in Hindi alone. This tripartite regime operationalises Article 346 for day-to-day correspondence.

The proviso does not require any formal agreement procedure — it says that if States agree, Hindi may be used. In practice, inter-State correspondence among Hindi-speaking States has proceeded in Hindi without any formal bilateral agreement, because all parties find it practical. The proviso's real constitutional function is protective: it ensures that non-Hindi States that have never agreed to use Hindi can invoke the absence of any agreement to insist on the Union-authorised language (effectively English) for communications with other States. The proviso accommodates the sociolinguistic reality that Hindi-speaking States find natural coordination through Hindi while protecting non-Hindi States from any implication that Hindi has a constitutional claim on their external official correspondence.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's discussion of what became Article 346 was brief relative to the debate on Article 343, largely because members recognised that inter-governmental communication needed a workable common medium and the dynamic reference to the Union-authorised language provided it without pre-empting the resolution of the primary language controversy. Non-Hindi members accepted the provision because it tied inter-governmental communication to whatever the evolving statutory framework would authorise — and they intended to use the Official Languages Act to ensure English remained in that framework.

The proviso allowing Hindi-speaking States to use Hindi between themselves by agreement was introduced as an accommodation for the Hindi belt rather than as a constitutional mandate. Members from southern States accepted the proviso on the understanding that it was genuinely voluntary — no State would be required to agree, and no State's refusal to agree could be characterised as a constitutional violation. This understanding has held: no non-Hindi State has ever been compelled to accept Hindi-medium correspondence through the Article 346 proviso.

## Key cases

**The Anti-Hindi Agitations and the Official Languages Act, 1963** — The most consequential event in Article 346's constitutional history is the political crisis of 1965. When the constitutional fifteen-year transitional period expired and the government indicated that Hindi would become the sole official language, Tamil Nadu erupted. The agitation compelled the Congress government to continue English as a co-official language through the 1967 amendment to the Official Languages Act. Because Article 346 links inter-governmental communication language to the Union's authorised language, the Act's continuation of English directly preserved English as a constitutionally compliant medium for inter-State and State-Union correspondence. The political settlement reached in 1965 finds its constitutional expression in the continuing operation of Article 346 read with the Official Languages Act.

**Inter-State Water Disputes Tribunal proceedings** — In proceedings before the Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal and other inter-State water disputes tribunals, the question of whether State governments' submissions must conform to Article 346 arose in administrative contexts. The consistent practice — affirmed in correspondence guidelines — is that English satisfies the Article 346 requirement in all cases, and States submitting documents in their own language must provide English translations. This practical resolution avoids the impasse that would arise if Karnataka submitted pleadings in Kannada to Tamil Nadu or the Union without an English version.

**Official Languages Act Section 3 litigation and central office circulars** — Disputes about whether central government offices operating in non-Hindi States must issue circulars to State governments in English have been resolved by reference to both Article 346 and the Official Languages Rules. Courts and tribunals have consistently held that issuing a communication solely in Hindi to a State government in Region C violates the constitutional framework and the Rules, keeping Article 346 alive as an enforceable constitutional norm rather than merely a policy aspiration.

**Tamil Nadu v. Union of India (language-medium circular disputes)** — Tamil Nadu has periodically challenged Union circulars that require State-level compliance only in Hindi, arguing that such circulars breach both Article 346 and the Official Languages Rules by effectively requiring communication in a language not constitutionally authorised as the sole official language for inter-governmental correspondence. Courts have generally sustained Tamil Nadu's position that English-medium compliance satisfies the constitutional obligation.

## The asymmetry between Hindi-speaking and non-Hindi States

Article 346 creates a practical asymmetry in the burden of multilingual compliance that has been a source of ongoing political friction. Hindi-speaking States can conduct all official correspondence — both internally under Article 345 and externally under Article 346's proviso — in a single language: Hindi. Non-Hindi States, by contrast, must maintain capacity to communicate in both their own official language (for internal purposes) and in English or Hindi (for external communications under Article 346). This asymmetry means that non-Hindi State officials must be functionally bilingual in English even when conducting central government correspondence, while Hindi State officials face no comparable requirement. The asymmetry has been used by Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and other non-Hindi States to argue that the current arrangement systematically burdens non-Hindi communities in ways that the clause (3) safeguard in Article 344 and the spirit of constitutional federalism should prevent.

## Current significance

Article 346's practical importance is felt most acutely when central ministries issue circulars, notifications, and official communications only in Hindi to State governments and State-level offices in non-Hindi States. The controversy recurs with regularity: a central ministry issues a policy circular solely in Hindi, a non-Hindi State government protests that it cannot effectively act on communications it cannot fully process, and the episode triggers both a political response and, occasionally, legal proceedings. The Official Languages Rules require bilingual communications to non-Hindi States, but compliance is uneven.

The emergence of digital government has added new dimensions to the Article 346 question. When central government portals require inputs only in Hindi (or in a transliteration system based on Devanagari script), States whose official language uses a different script are excluded from seamless participation in centralised systems. These practical barriers to multilingual administrative integration are the twenty-first-century face of the problem Article 346 was designed to address in 1950, and they are increasingly the subject of parliamentary questions, High Court petitions, and digital inclusion advocacy.

The article's proviso continues to shape regional political dynamics. The Hindi-speaking States broadly share a linguistic common ground that makes coordination easier. Southern States — Tamil Nadu in particular — have consistently positioned the rejection of mandatory Hindi as a matter of constitutional right, and Article 346 provides the constitutional vocabulary for that position: Hindi's use in inter-State communication with non-Hindi States is permissive (requiring agreement) rather than mandatory, and this distinction is the constitutional foundation for periodic southern reassertion that "One Nation One Language" has no support in the Constitution's text.
