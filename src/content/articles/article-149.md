## Why it matters

Article 149 is the functional engine of the CAG's audit mandate. Where Article 148 creates the office and protects its independence, Article 149 defines what the CAG actually does — and, crucially, assigns to Parliament rather than the executive the power to set those limits. This choice has two important consequences. First, it means the executive cannot narrow the CAG's audit mandate by executive order or administrative instruction; only Parliament can modify the scope of CAG audit. Second, the open-ended reference to "any other authority or body" means that Parliament can extend the mandate beyond traditional departmental accounts to cover government companies, grant-aided bodies, autonomous institutions, and — if Parliament chooses — entirely new categories of public-interest entities as they emerge.

The transitional clause — preserving the pre-Constitution Auditor-General's powers until Parliament legislated — ensured continuity of audit across the Independence transition. Parliament exercised its Article 149 mandate through the Comptroller and Auditor-General's (Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service) Act, 1971, which remains the primary statute through which the constitutional provision operates. The 1971 Act is not a limit on what Parliament can authorise; it is the current expression of Parliament's Article 149 discretion, subject to expansion or modification by any subsequent legislation.

The practical significance of Article 149 has been demonstrated most vividly in the period since the 1990s, when the CAG moved beyond traditional compliance audit into performance audit, propriety audit, and value-for-money assessments. Each of these audit modes raises the question of whether the CAG's constitutional mandate extends to making qualitative judgments about government policy choices — a question that remains contested but that courts have generally declined to resolve definitively, leaving Article 149's expansive text to do its work.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 149 is notably open — it speaks of duties and powers "in relation to the accounts of the Union and of the States and of any other authority or body as may be prescribed by or under any law made by Parliament." Three elements deserve attention.

**Parliamentary prescription:** By making Parliament the legislature that defines the CAG's mandate, the framers placed the audit function in the same structural relationship to Parliament that the armed forces bear to the civil power — the external controller sets the scope, ensuring that the executive cannot shrink or expand the CAG's mandate for self-interested reasons. This is a strong structural choice that distinguishes India's constitutional audit from systems in which the auditor-general's powers are defined by executive regulation.

**The "any other authority or body" formula:** This open-ended phrase was deliberately drafted to allow Parliament to bring new categories of public-finance entity within the CAG's mandate without constitutional amendment. As the public sector expanded after Independence — from departmental accounts to public sector undertakings, statutory corporations, urban local bodies, and autonomous institutions funded by government grants — Parliament could extend the 1971 Act's reach without needing to alter the Constitution. The phrase has been the focus of the most significant disputes about the CAG's audit scope, including the BCCI controversy and the question of CAG audit over private telecom operators holding spectrum licences.

**The transitional default:** Until Parliament legislated, the CAG had the powers of the Auditor-General of India under the colonial-era Government Audit Act. This transitional default prevented a gap in public financial accountability during the period between the Constitution's commencement and the enactment of the 1971 Act — a period of over two decades in which audit continued under the inherited framework.

## The CAG Act 1971 and the scope of audit

The Comptroller and Auditor-General's (Duties, Powers and Conditions of Service) Act, 1971 gives detailed content to Article 149. Under the Act, the CAG audits:

- All receipts and expenditures from the Consolidated Fund of India and each State's Consolidated Fund, including all grants and loans made from these funds
- All transactions relating to Contingency Funds and Public Accounts of the Union and States
- The accounts of stores and stock held by government departments
- Government companies under Section 619 of the Companies Act (as a statutory auditor or by conducting supplementary audit of accounts already audited by chartered accountant firms)
- Any body that has received loans or grants from the Consolidated Funds exceeding a threshold specified in the Act (the "substantially financed" test, currently set at 75 per cent of total expenditure or Rs 25 lakh, whichever is less)
- Local bodies to the extent that Parliament or a State legislature directs

Performance audit — systematic examination of the economy, efficiency, and effectiveness of government programmes — is not expressly mandated by the 1971 Act but has been institutionalised by the CAG on the basis that Article 149's reference to "duties as Parliament may prescribe" includes all forms of audit that Parliament has not excluded, and that the pre-Constitution Auditor-General's powers included an element of propriety audit.

## The Vinod Rai era — 2G and Coalgate

The period 2008 to 2013, when Vinod Rai served as CAG, is widely regarded as the most consequential in the institution's post-Independence history. Under his tenure the CAG produced landmark performance and propriety audit reports that exposed systemic failures of public resource management at scale.

The 2G Spectrum Allocation Audit Report (2010) estimated that the allocation of spectrum licences through a first-come-first-served policy at 2001 prices, rather than through competitive auction at 2008 market values, resulted in a notional loss to the exchequer of Rs 1.76 lakh crore. The methodology — comparing actual revenue received with the estimated revenue that competitive auction would have yielded — was controversial, and the government contested both the figures and the CAG's authority to make such policy-based comparisons. The report was tabled in Parliament under Article 151 and became the primary factual basis for the PIL in which the Supreme Court cancelled 122 licences in 2012.

The Coal Block Allocation Audit Report (2012) — the Coalgate report — estimated notional losses of Rs 1.86 lakh crore from the allocation of coal blocks to private and public entities without competitive bidding between 1993 and 2010. The CAG found that the allocation process was opaque, non-transparent, and did not follow a screening committee's recommendations. The report led to CBI investigations, parliamentary committee inquiries, and ultimately the Supreme Court's cancellation of 214 coal block allocations in 2014.

Both reports demonstrated that the Article 149 mandate, when actively exercised by a determined CAG, can extend far beyond the verification of account accuracy into policy-level accountability findings that drive judicial and parliamentary intervention in governance.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor-General of India v. K.S. Jagannathan (1986) 2 SCC 679** — The Supreme Court held that the CAG's powers under the 1971 Act, read with Article 149, extend to the audit of government companies and that neither the Companies Act structure nor the memoranda of association of such companies can curtail the constitutional audit mandate. The judgment affirmed that the "any other authority or body" formula in Article 149 is given real content by Parliament's legislation and cannot be contracted out of by corporate structure.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v. Comptroller and Auditor-General of India (1989)** — The Court confirmed that the CAG's jurisdiction over State accounts under Article 149 is not subordinate to the financial administration structures of State governments, and that States cannot limit the CAG's access to documents required for audit purposes. This ruling closed the avenue of State-level obstruction of Union-level constitutional audit.

**Centre for Public Interest Litigation v. Union of India (2G Spectrum) (2012) 3 SCC 1** — The CAG's 2G spectrum audit report, tabled under Article 151, was the primary factual basis for the Supreme Court's cancellation of 122 licences. The Court's reliance on the CAG's findings gave Article 149's audit mandate a judicially cognisable character that no statutory audit could match, confirming the constitutional weight of the CAG's performance audit function.

**Manohar Lal Sharma v. Principal Secretary (Coal Block Allocation) (2014) 9 SCC 516** — The Supreme Court cancelled 214 coal block allocations substantially on the basis of the CAG's Coalgate report. The Court treated the CAG's performance audit as credible evidence of arbitrary governmental conduct, the second major case in two years in which the Article 149 audit mandate produced direct constitutional consequences.

## Scope disputes — the BCCI and private entities question

The most vivid illustration of Article 149's outer limits arose from the question of whether the CAG could audit the Board of Control for Cricket in India. As a private body that controls a quasi-monopoly over professional cricket under a government-recognised status, the BCCI occupied a grey zone — not substantially financed by government, yet exercising a public governance function over a national sport. The Law Commission and parliamentary committees recommended extending the CAG's mandate to the BCCI through legislation, but no statute was enacted. The controversy highlighted that Article 149 can be expanded by Parliament to cover entirely new categories of public-interest entities, but that absent such legislation the constitutional provision alone does not reach private bodies, however much they resemble public ones in functional terms.

A related dispute concerns private telecom operators holding spectrum licences granted by the government. Courts and parliamentary committees have read Article 149's "any other authority or body" formula broadly, but the line between auditing a private company that holds public licences and conducting a commercially invasive inspection remained contested in the post-2G litigation.

## Current significance

Article 149's scope has been continuously tested as the boundaries of public finance shift. The 2020s have added new terrain — digital public infrastructure, government-backed fintech platforms, and sovereign green bonds. Whether the "any other authority or body" formulation can constitutionally reach such entities — and whether Parliament will legislate to bring them within the CAG's mandate — is among the unresolved questions at the frontier of India's public accountability framework.

The debate over performance audit methodology — the use of notional and presumptive loss figures — is a continuing challenge to the Article 149 mandate. Governments have argued that such methodology converts the CAG from a financial auditor into a policy reviewer, a role that the constitutional provision does not sanction. The CAG has maintained that measuring the efficiency of resource use is integral to any meaningful audit of public accounts. Courts have declined to resolve this boundary definitively, leaving the shape of the Article 149 mandate in the most consequential cases dependent on the CAG's own institutional judgment about where compliance audit ends and performance audit begins.

Parliamentary committees — particularly the Public Accounts Committee and the Estimates Committee — continue to press for an extended Article 149 mandate to cover off-budget financing vehicles, Public Private Partnerships, and major government-sponsored autonomous institutions. Each such extension requires amendment of the 1971 Act rather than the Constitution, giving Parliament the ability to expand the audit perimeter as public finance architecture evolves without the high threshold of constitutional amendment.
