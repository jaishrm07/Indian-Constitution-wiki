## What this Article did

Article 379 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that addressed the provisional Parliament and its Speaker and Deputy Speaker. It has been omitted.

When the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950, India did not immediately have a new Parliament elected under the new constitutional framework. General elections had not yet been held under the adult suffrage provisions of the Constitution. The Constituent Assembly — which had been drafting the Constitution — was reconstituted as the provisional Parliament of India under Article 379, allowing India to have a functioning central legislature while preparations for the first general elections were made.

Article 379 provided for the continuation of the existing Constituent Assembly members as members of the provisional Parliament and made provisions for its Speaker and Deputy Speaker. The provisional Parliament functioned from 26 January 1950 until the First Lok Sabha was constituted after the first general elections held in 1951-52. It enacted important legislation during this period, including the Preventive Detention Act, 1950 and the People's Representation Act, 1950.

## Why it matters

The constitutional recognition of a provisional Parliament was essential for India's democratic legitimacy during the transition period. Without Article 379, there would have been a serious question about the constitutional basis for Parliament's exercise of legislative power between January 1950 and April 1952. The laws passed by the provisional Parliament — including major legislation on elections, preventive detention, and citizenship — derive their constitutional validity in part from the fact that Article 379 gave the provisional Parliament a firm constitutional foundation as India's legislature until the First Lok Sabha could be constituted.

## Why it was omitted

Article 379 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, along with a cluster of other articles (380 to 392) that had also served their transitional purpose. By 1956, the provisional Parliament had long since been superseded by the elected First Lok Sabha. There was no reason to retain provisions about a body that no longer existed and could never again be invoked. The Seventh Amendment removed these spent provisions as part of a major constitutional consolidation that also reorganised the States on linguistic lines.

## Historical context

The provisional Parliament of India was a constitutionally significant institution. It bridged the gap between the Constituent Assembly, which had drafted the Constitution, and the first elected Parliament under adult franchise. The decision to convert the Constituent Assembly into a provisional Parliament was deliberate and enabled the government to maintain legislative continuity while preparing for the first general elections — one of the largest democratic exercises in world history at the time, covering a population of over 350 million with near-universal adult franchise.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge to the validity of legislation passed by the provisional Parliament is recorded. The transition from provisional to elected Parliament was managed smoothly. The First Lok Sabha, elected in 1952, inherited the legislative legacy of the provisional Parliament and continued the constitutional framework Article 379 had helped maintain.

## Current significance

Article 379 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in establishing the constitutional basis for the provisional Parliament that governed India during the critical period of 1950-1952 — a period that included the enactment of foundational legislation, the organisation of the first general elections, and the consolidation of the new Republic's administrative machinery.

The provisional Parliament enacted dozens of important statutes, including the Representation of the People Act, 1950 (establishing electoral rolls and the framework for elections), the Preventive Detention Act, 1950, and the Adaptation of Laws Order, 1950. Its Speaker, G V Mavalankar, went on to serve as Speaker of the First Lok Sabha. The institutional continuity between the provisional Parliament and the elected Parliament was thus both constitutional and personal, reflecting the broader strategy of managed transition that characterises Part XXI of the Constitution.
