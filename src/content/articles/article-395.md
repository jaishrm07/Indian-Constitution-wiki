## What this Article does

Article 395 is the repeal provision — the final operative article of the Constitution of India. With a single sentence, it swept away the two foundational instruments of British rule in India and replaced them with the new Constitution.

The article repeals:

1. The Indian Independence Act, 1947 — the Act of the British Parliament that divided British India into the two independent dominions of India and Pakistan.
2. The Government of India Act, 1935 — the principal constitutional instrument under which British India had been governed, and which had provided the framework for the Dominion of India from 1947 to 1950.
3. All enactments amending or supplementing the Government of India Act, 1935.

One important exception is carved out: the Abolition of Privy Council Jurisdiction Act, 1949 is expressly not repealed. That Act had abolished the jurisdiction of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council (the British colonial court of final appeal) over India. Retaining that Act alongside the new Constitution preserved the termination of Privy Council jurisdiction and avoided any argument that the repeal of the 1947 Act might revive it.

## What was repealed and why it mattered

### The Indian Independence Act, 1947

The Indian Independence Act was enacted by the United Kingdom Parliament on 18 July 1947. It created the two independent dominions of India and Pakistan, divided Bengal and Punjab between them, ended the paramountcy of the British Crown over the Indian princely states, and provided for the lapse of British suzerainty. It was, in a sense, the birth certificate of independent India as a dominion under the British Crown.

But the 1947 Act was a British statute, and its continued existence as the foundational document of India's constitutional status was symbolically incompatible with a fully sovereign republic. By repealing it, Article 395 removed the last formal linkage between India's constitutional status and an Act of the British Parliament. From 26 January 1950, India's constitutional foundation was entirely domestic — the Constitution of India, enacted by the Constituent Assembly as the expression of the sovereign will of the people of India.

### The Government of India Act, 1935

The Government of India Act, 1935 was the most comprehensive constitutional instrument of British India. Its 321 sections and 10 Schedules governed the federal structure, the distribution of legislative powers, the High Courts, the Federal Court, the civil service, the Governor-General's extraordinary powers, provincial governance, and a host of other matters. The Constitution of India's framers drew extensively on the 1935 Act in drafting the new Constitution — borrowing its federal structure, the three-list division of legislative subjects, the powers of the Governor, the service provisions, and much else.

Despite this inheritance, the 1935 Act was fundamentally incompatible with a democratic, sovereign republic. It had been designed for a colonial constitutional framework in which the British Crown retained ultimate control and in which the Governor-General and Governors wielded extensive discretionary powers. Fundamental rights, separation of powers in the republican sense, and parliamentary sovereignty over the executive were either absent or severely constrained.

The repeal of the 1935 Act and all its supplementary enactments replaced this colonial framework with the new Constitution, which vested sovereignty in the people of India and created a framework of fundamental rights, democratic governance, and constitutional supremacy.

### The Abolition of Privy Council Jurisdiction Act, 1949

The express carve-out for this Act was legally significant. Before 1949, appeals from Indian courts lay to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in London — the final court of appeal for the British Empire. The Abolition of Privy Council Jurisdiction Act, 1949, abolished this appellate jurisdiction and vested final appellate authority in the Federal Court of India (which became the Supreme Court of India under the new Constitution). If Article 395 had repealed the 1947 Act without this exception, there might have been an argument that the jurisdictional changes effected by the 1949 Act were somehow affected. The express exception eliminated this risk, ensuring that the Supreme Court remained unambiguously the final court of appeal with no vestigial Privy Council jurisdiction.

## Historical significance

Article 395 represents the constitutional moment of complete independence. The Indian Independence Act, 1947 had created India as a dominion — a member of the British Commonwealth with the British Crown as head of state. The Constitution of India made India a Republic — a sovereign, democratic, independent nation in which all constitutional authority derived from the people of India, not from any British enactment.

The repeal effected by Article 395 was not merely symbolic. It removed from the legal order two instruments that could otherwise have been invoked as potential sources of constitutional authority alongside or above the new Constitution. By sweeping them away, Article 395 ensured that the Constitution of India was unambiguously supreme — the sole source of constitutional authority in the new Republic.

Granville Austin, the historian of India's Constitution, described the Constitution as having two primary goals: building a democratic nation and achieving social revolution. Article 395 secured the first condition for both by clearing the field of competing constitutional instruments and making the new Constitution the sole and supreme law.

## Key cases

**In re Berubari Union and Exchange of Enclaves AIR 1960 SC 845** — The Supreme Court considered the effect of Article 395's repeal of the Indian Independence Act, 1947 in the context of a boundary dispute with Pakistan. The Court held that although the 1947 Act had been repealed, the legal effects of transactions completed under it (including the partition boundary) remained valid. This was an important clarification that repeal by Article 395 did not undo completed historical facts but only removed the 1947 Act as a continuing source of constitutional authority.

**Synthetics and Chemicals Ltd v State of UP (1990) 1 SCC 109** — The Supreme Court addressed legislative competence questions that turned on the distribution of powers originally mapped out under the Government of India Act, 1935 and carried forward into the Seventh Schedule of the new Constitution. Cases like this illustrate how the 1935 Act's legacy persisted through the constitutional framework even after its formal repeal, because the Constitution's framers consciously incorporated its federal structure into the new document.

**State of WB v Union of India AIR 1963 SC 1241** — The Supreme Court examined the nature of Indian federalism partly in light of the pre-constitutional framework. The Court noted that the Government of India Act, 1935's federal provisions had been substantially reproduced in the new Constitution, but under a fundamentally different — republican and sovereign — constitutional order. The repeal of the 1935 Act by Article 395 marked the decisive break from the colonial constitutional model.

## Current significance

Article 395 is spent in the sense that the repeal it effected was instantaneous and final on 26 January 1950. No further operation of the article is required. But its constitutional significance endures: it is the provision that completed the transition from colonial dominion to sovereign republic. The entire constitutional order of modern India rests on this act of repeal — on the fact that from 26 January 1950, there was no British statute standing behind or above the Constitution of India. The article is the last word of the Constitution and, in a sense, the first word of India's constitutional independence.
