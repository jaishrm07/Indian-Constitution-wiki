## What this Article does

Article 10 provides that every person who is or is deemed to be a citizen of India under Part II shall continue to be a citizen, subject to any law that Parliament may make. It is a continuity guarantee: citizenship once vested at commencement is not automatically lost. The qualifying clause "subject to the provisions of any law that may be made by Parliament" is the constitutional gateway for the Citizenship Act, 1955 to regulate termination, renunciation, and deprivation of citizenship.

## The continuity guarantee

The guarantee in Article 10 serves two related purposes. First, it prevents citizenship from being stripped away without a valid law — administrative action alone is insufficient. Second, it confirms that citizenship is a continuing status, not one that has to be renewed or reaffirmed periodically. A person who was a citizen on 26 January 1950 remained a citizen unless and until Parliament's law provided for termination and that law was applied to them.

Importantly, Article 10 does not independently create citizenship — it only continues what Articles 5 to 8 have already granted. A person who did not qualify under any of those articles cannot derive citizenship from Article 10 alone. The provision is a gateway, not an independent source.

## Parliament's power to terminate

The phrase "subject to the provisions of any law that may be made by Parliament" makes Article 10 self-limiting. Parliament may by law provide for the termination or deprivation of citizenship on specified grounds. The Citizenship Act, 1955 exercises this power in several ways:
- **Renunciation** (section 8): a citizen may voluntarily renounce Indian citizenship by declaration registered with a prescribed authority.
- **Termination** (section 9): citizenship terminates automatically on voluntary acquisition of citizenship of another country, carrying forward the principle of Article 9 into the statutory framework.
- **Deprivation** (section 10): the Central Government may deprive a citizen by registration or naturalisation of citizenship on grounds including fraud in obtaining citizenship, disloyalty, assistance to an enemy during war, or sentence of imprisonment of two or more years within five years of registration.

The article does not specify that termination or deprivation must follow any particular procedural form, but courts have implied requirements of natural justice, and the Supreme Court has held that citizenship-stripping orders must comply with procedural fairness standards.

## Key cases

**Shanno Devi v. Mangal Sain Wason (1961) AIR 1961 SC 58** — The Supreme Court confirmed that Article 10 does not independently create citizenship but preserves the citizenship status that Articles 5 to 8 have conferred, and that Parliament's power under the article's saving clause extends to enacting conditions for loss of citizenship prospectively.

**Md. Sadiq Ali v. Union of India (1966) AIR 1967 SC 381** — The Court held that Article 10 read with Article 11 gives Parliament broad authority to prescribe the grounds on which a citizen may be deprived of citizenship, and that such deprivation does not offend Article 10's continuity guarantee so long as the law is validly enacted.

**Louis De Raedt v. Union of India (1991) 3 SCC 554** — The Court confirmed that Article 10's guarantee applies only to persons who fall within the Part II provisions; foreigners cannot derive any right from it, and the right to reside in India is distinct from the right to citizenship itself.

## NRC and the continuity question

The National Register of Citizens exercise in Assam requires individuals to prove that they or their ancestors were citizens of India at the relevant reference date (24 March 1971 under the Assam Accord). Article 10's continuity principle is relevant here: a person who was a citizen on 26 January 1950 (by virtue of Articles 5 or 6) remained a citizen unless a law terminated their citizenship. If no such termination occurred, citizenship continues through subsequent generations subject to the ordinary rules of the Citizenship Act.

## Current significance

Article 10 is cited in challenges to citizenship-stripping orders under section 10 of the Citizenship Act, where petitioners argue that the continuity guarantee requires any deprivation to meet strict procedural and substantive standards. It is also relevant to debates about the scope of NRC operations — critics argue that any citizenship-denial process must satisfy Article 10's implicit procedural requirements and cannot be based on mere administrative presumption.
