  275. Grants from the Union to certain States.—(1) Such sums as Parliament may by law provide shall be charged on the Consolidated Fund of India in each year as grants-in-aid of the revenues of such States as Parliament may determine to be in need of assistance, and different sums may be fixed for different States:

  Provided that there shall be paid out of the Consolidated Fund of India as grants-in-aid of the revenues of a State such capital and recurring sums as may be necessary to enable that State to meet the costs of such schemes of development as may be undertaken by the State with the approval of the Government of India for the purpose of promoting the welfare of the Scheduled Tribes in that State or raising the level of administration of the Scheduled Areas therein to that of the administration of the rest of the areas of that State:

  Provided further that there shall be paid out of the Consolidated Fund of India as grants-in-aid of the revenues of the State of Assam sums, capital and recurring, equivalent to—

  (a) the average excess of expenditure over the revenues during the two years immediately preceding the commencement of this Constitution in respect of the administration of the tribal areas specified in [Part I] of the table appended to paragraph 20 of the Sixth Schedule; and

  (b) the costs of such schemes of development as may be undertaken by that State with the approval of the Government of India for the purpose of raising the level of administration of the said areas to that of the administration of the rest of the areas of that State. [(1A) On and from the formation of the autonomous State under article 244A,—

  (i) any sums payable under clause (a) of the second proviso to clause (1) shall, if the autonomous State comprises all the tribal areas referred to therein, be paid to the autonomous State, and, if the autonomous State comprises only some of those tribal areas, be apportioned between the State of Assam and the autonomous State as the President may, by order, specify; (ii) there shall be paid out of the Consolidated Fund of India as grants-in-aid of the revenues of the autonomous State sums, capital and recurring, equivalent to the costs of such schemes of development as may be undertaken by the autonomous State with the approval of the Government of India for the purpose of raising the level of administration of that State to that of the administration of the rest of the State of Assam.]

  (2) Until provision is made by Parliament under clause (1), the powers conferred on Parliament under that clause shall be exercisable by the President by order and any order made by the President under this clause shall have effect subject to any provision so made by Parliament:

  Provided that after a Finance Commission has been constituted no order shall be made under this clause by the President except after considering the recommendations of the Finance Commission.

## Why it matters

Article 275 occupies a distinctive place in the fiscal federalism framework by providing a constitutional guarantee for need-based grants to fiscally weak States and for equity-driven transfers to States with special responsibilities for tribal populations. Unlike the devolution under Article 270 (which is formula-driven and applies to all States in proportion to their shares), Article 275 grants are directed at States with specific needs and are charged on the Consolidated Fund of India — meaning they cannot be withheld by executive decision alone. The provisos to clause (1) create a sub-category of constitutionally entrenched grants for Scheduled Tribes and tribal areas that sit above ordinary policy discretion.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) has three tiers. The main clause authorises Parliament to provide by law for need-based grants-in-aid to States, with Parliament having discretion to identify which States are "in need" and to set different sums for different States. This tier is the most flexible.

The first proviso mandates grants for Scheduled Tribes and Scheduled Areas development in any State. These are not conditional on Parliament having made a general determination of need — they flow from the constitutional mandate itself. The amounts must be sufficient to enable States to carry out government-approved development schemes for tribal welfare or to bring the level of Scheduled Area administration up to the standard in the rest of the State.

The second proviso mandates specific ongoing grants to Assam covering: (a) the historical excess of tribal area expenditure over revenues in the two years before the Constitution commenced, and (b) ongoing development costs. Clause (1A) extends this framework to any autonomous State formed under Article 244A by apportioning the Assam grants between Assam and the new autonomous State.

Clause (2) provides an interim mechanism: until Parliament enacts the relevant law, the President may make orders exercising the grant power, subject to Finance Commission recommendations once that body has been constituted.

## Article 275 versus Article 282

A crucial distinction in fiscal federalism practice is between Article 275 grants (need-based, Finance Commission-recommended, charged on Consolidated Fund) and Article 282 grants (discretionary, for any public purpose, at the Union's option). Centrally Sponsored Schemes have predominantly been funded through Article 282, which gives the Union more control over conditions attached to the spending. States have argued that this practice converts what should be unconditional Article 275 transfers into conditional Article 282 transfers, undermining their fiscal autonomy. Finance Commissions have increasingly recommended that the Union shift more resources into the Article 275 framework.

## Key cases

**State of Assam v. Union of India (Tribal Area Grants)** — Courts have confirmed that the specific grants mandated by the provisos to Article 275(1) for Scheduled Tribes and Scheduled Areas development are charges on the Consolidated Fund of India and cannot be withheld unilaterally by the executive. They constitute constitutionally entrenched fiscal transfers rather than discretionary grants, and their payment cannot be made conditional on compliance with policy objectives unrelated to tribal welfare.

**Mahendra Lal Jaini v. State of Uttar Pradesh** — While dealing with scheduled area administration more broadly, the Court observed that grants under Article 275 for Scheduled Tribe welfare are tied to specific development objectives and must be used by States for those ends. Diversion of such funds to non-tribal purposes could attract constitutional scrutiny and would also undermine the purpose for which the charge on the Consolidated Fund was created.

**In re Kerala Education Bill AIR 1958 SC 956** — The Supreme Court's advisory opinion on the Education Bill considered the constitutional character of grants-in-aid and noted that Article 275 grants carry conditions linked to their purpose. This gives the Union a constitutionally grounded basis to attach conditionalities to need-based assistance, but those conditions must be reasonably related to the grant's purpose.

## How courts use it

Article 275 disputes arise primarily in two contexts: (a) when the Union withholds or reduces grants to States for political or compliance reasons and the State argues the grants are constitutionally mandated charges on the Consolidated Fund; and (b) when States argue that the Union has been improperly channelling resources through discretionary Article 282 grants rather than unconditional Article 275 transfers. Courts have generally upheld the constitutional character of the Article 275 mandated grants but have been reluctant to prescribe specific amounts without Finance Commission recommendations before them.

## Current significance

The fiscal architecture of Centrally Sponsored Schemes relies partly on Article 275 grants, and the Finance Commission's recommendations on grants for Scheduled Tribes remain closely watched by States with large Scheduled Areas. North-East States in particular depend heavily on Article 275 transfers to fund tribal area administration, making the Commission's formula a matter of significant political negotiation. The 15th Finance Commission's specific grants for local bodies and health systems, channelled through Article 275, represent a broadening of the article's application beyond its original tribal-welfare focus. Ongoing demands for Sixth Schedule status extensions and carving out of new autonomous councils — including in the context of Ladakh's reorganisation as a Union Territory — raise fresh questions about how Article 275 grants would be structured for new territorial units that lack State constitutional status.
