## Why it matters

Article 366 is the Constitution's interpretive foundation. Every time a court, a lawyer, or a citizen tries to understand what a constitutional provision means, the first question is whether the key terms in that provision carry a meaning fixed by Article 366. If a defined term appears, its Article 366 meaning governs — displacing colloquial usage, dictionary definitions, or the meaning the term might bear in ordinary statutory context. This makes Article 366 the doctrinal starting point for a vast range of constitutional questions, from the scope of reservation policies to the structure of fiscal federalism.

The definitions in Article 366 are not merely technical housekeeping. Several of them reflect substantive constitutional choices about who belongs to constitutionally protected categories, how those categories are constituted and modified over time, and what the relationship is between constitutional text and executive notification. The decision to define "Scheduled Castes" and "Scheduled Tribes" by cross-reference to presidential orders under Articles 341 and 342 — rather than by a fixed list in the Constitution itself — was a deliberate choice to make these categories ambulatory and updatable without requiring constitutional amendment. That choice has produced decades of litigation and political controversy about the boundaries of the categories and the process for revising them.

Article 366 has been amended more than any other single provision in the Constitution. Each amendment adds, deletes, or substitutes a definition, reflecting a substantive constitutional change elsewhere in the document. The addition of the GST-related definitions in 2016, the reservation-related definitions in 2018 and 2021, and the periodic revision of definitions relating to the States and their classification illustrate that Article 366 is a living index of the Constitution's evolving substantive commitments, not a static glossary.

The preamble to the definitions list — "unless the context otherwise requires" — gives courts a limited escape valve when a mechanical application of a definition would produce absurd, inconsistent, or clearly unintended results. This context-sensitivity is exercised sparingly: courts treat Article 366 definitions as presumptively governing and require a strong contextual signal before departing from them. The combination of presumptive force and contextual flexibility makes Article 366 an instrument of interpretive discipline rather than interpretive rigidity.

## Purpose of a constitutional definitions article

Article 366 performs a specialised constitutional function: it provides authoritative meanings for technical expressions used throughout the Constitution, avoiding the need to define each term at the point of use. A Constitution that defines key terms in the operative provisions where they first appear risks inconsistency — a term defined narrowly in one article might be construed more broadly in another — and creates difficulty when the same term appears in dozens of different provisions. Centralising definitions in a single article ensures uniform application and reduces interpretive fragmentation.

The article has been amended multiple times to accommodate new constitutional concepts, reflect structural changes in governance, and delete obsolete terms. Deleted and substituted clauses are noted with asterisks in official editions. Clause (4A) (relating to certain financial instruments) and clause (21) (relating to a specific territorial arrangement) have been deleted. The entry for "Ruler" was amended by the Twenty-sixth Amendment that also omitted Article 362. These amendments trace the evolution of the Constitution's substantive concerns over seventy-five years.

The relationship between Article 366 and Article 367 is constitutionally important. Article 366 provides the Constitution's specific glossary of defined terms. Article 367 imports the General Clauses Act 1897 as a residual interpretive toolkit for everything not addressed by Article 366's specific definitions. Together, the two articles create a layered interpretive infrastructure: Article 366 governs where specific definitions exist; the General Clauses Act governs where they do not; and the constitutional context can override both. This layering means that constitutional interpretation proceeds from the most specific to the most general, with the context of the particular provision as the final adjudicator.

## Key definitions and their constitutional significance

**"Scheduled Castes" (clause 24)** — Defined by cross-reference to Article 341, which empowers the President to specify castes, races, or tribes for the purposes of the Constitution by order, subject to parliamentary modification. This makes the definition ambulatory: the communities actually included in "Scheduled Castes" are determined by current presidential notifications and their parliamentary amendments, not by a fixed constitutional list. The cross-reference design was chosen because the framers recognised that the boundaries of Scheduled Caste identity were socially and politically contested, and that a fixed list would be too inflexible to reflect changes in social conditions or the results of political negotiation over inclusion and exclusion. The resulting litigation — over whether a particular community is or should be included, whether sub-classification within SCs is constitutionally permissible, and whether conversion to a non-Hindu religion removes a person from an SC list — has been among the most contested in Indian constitutional jurisprudence.

**"Scheduled Tribes" (clause 25)** — Defined by cross-reference to Article 342, in the same ambulatory structure as Scheduled Castes. Presidential orders under Article 342 specify tribal communities for constitutional purposes. The definition carries the same flexibility and the same litigation risk as clause 24. The Scheduled Tribes list has additional constitutional significance because of the Fifth and Sixth Schedules' special governance provisions for tribal areas, and because many land rights and forest rights protections are triggered by ST status.

**"Socially and educationally backward classes" (clause 26C)** — Inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and Fifth Amendment) Act, 2021. The 102nd Amendment (2018) had inserted Article 342A, giving the President power to specify socially and educationally backward classes (SEBCs or OBCs) for constitutional purposes — parallel to the SC/ST notification mechanism. The Supreme Court in Janhit Abhiyan v Union of India (2022) considered the 103rd Amendment's EWS reservation and the 102nd Amendment's OBC notification mechanism in detail, examining whether the Article 342A structure, read with the new clause 26C definition, validly altered the pre-existing scheme under which States had primary authority to identify backward classes. The 105th Amendment clarified that States retain their own backward class determination powers; the Article 342A and clause 26C mechanisms operate for Central purposes only. The definition in clause 26C is thus the constitutional anchor for a continuing adjustment of federal authority over reservation categories.

**"State" (clause 26B)** — Inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act, 2016, in connection with the GST reform. The definition provides that for the purposes of Articles 246A, 268, 269, 269A, and 279A, "State" includes a Union territory with legislature. This was necessary because the GST framework allocates legislative and fiscal powers between "States" and the Centre — if "State" retained its ordinary constitutional meaning (excluding Union territories), Union territories with legislatures (Delhi, Puducherry, and later Jammu and Kashmir) would fall into an anomalous position in the GST framework. The clause 26B definition resolves this by including such Union territories within "State" for the specific GST-related provisions, without altering the meaning of "State" for any other constitutional purpose.

**"Goods and Services Tax" (clause 12A)** — Inserted by the 101st Amendment, this definition establishes the constitutional floor for the GST's scope: "any tax on supply of goods, or services or both except taxes on the supply of the alcoholic liquor for human consumption." The carve-out for alcoholic liquor was a political concession to the States, which retain VAT-based taxing authority over alcohol — a significant revenue source. The constitutional definition means that the boundaries of GST can be determined against this standard: a tax that exceeds the constitutional scope of "goods and services tax" as defined, or that tries to capture alcohol within it, would be constitutionally invalid. The definition also interacts with clause 26A ("Services" means anything other than goods), ensuring that "goods" and "services" are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive for GST purposes.

**"Anglo-Indian" (clause 2)** — Historically significant as the definition governing reserved seats for the Anglo-Indian community in Parliament under Article 331 and in State Assemblies under Article 333. The definition turns on paternal European descent in the male line combined with domicile and birth in India. The 104th Amendment (2020) removed Articles 331 and 333, ending the nominated Anglo-Indian seats in Parliament and State Legislatures. The clause 2 definition remains in Article 366 but its constitutional significance is now primarily historical.

**"Proclamation of Emergency" (clause 18)** — Ties the constitutional expression to Article 352(1), ensuring that wherever the term "Proclamation of Emergency" appears in the Constitution, it carries the full procedural and substantive conditions of Article 352. This prevents a narrower or broader usage from developing in different articles and ensures the emergency framework operates as an integrated whole.

**"Existing law" (clause 10)** — Governs interpretive questions about the constitutional validity of laws that were in place before the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950. Under Articles 13 and 372, pre-constitutional laws continue in force subject to fundamental rights challenges and adaptation to the constitutional framework. Clause 10's definition of "existing law" determines which laws are subject to this transitional regime.

## Amendments to Article 366

The constitutional amendment history of Article 366 is itself a record of the Constitution's substantive evolution. The Twenty-sixth Amendment (1971) revised the definition of "Ruler" in clause 22 following the abolition of privy purses. The Forty-fourth Amendment (1978) made consequential changes following the removal of property rights from Part III. The Forty-second Amendment (1976) and subsequent adjustments addressed various definitional issues in the Emergency period. The Eighty-sixth Amendment (2002) and subsequent education-related amendments had indirect effects on definitions used in Part IV-A. The One Hundred and First Amendment (2016) inserted clauses 12A, 26A, and 26B for the GST framework. The One Hundred and Second Amendment (2018) inserted references relevant to Article 342A. The One Hundred and Fifth Amendment (2021) inserted clause 26C for the backward classes definition.

Each of these amendments reflects a substantive policy change in the Constitution's operative provisions and the need to give that change a settled definitional anchor. Reading the amendment history of Article 366 is therefore a way of reading the constitutional history of India's substantive concerns — from the integration of the princely states, through the Emergency and its aftermath, to the GST reform and the evolving politics of reservation categories.

## Key cases

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992) 3 SCC 217 (Mandal case)** — The nine-judge bench's landmark ruling on reservations for Other Backward Classes engaged directly with the meaning of "backward classes" as a constitutional category. While the provision primarily under examination was Article 16(4), the Court's interpretation of social and educational backwardness and its relationship to caste identity set the doctrinal framework within which the subsequent definitions in Article 366 — including the eventual insertion of clause 26C — must be read. The ruling's exclusion of the "creamy layer" from OBC reservation benefits, and its fifty percent ceiling on total reservations, established constitutional constraints that any definition of backward classes must be consistent with.

**E V Chinnaiah v State of Andhra Pradesh (2005) 1 SCC 394** — The five-judge bench held that Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, as defined in Article 366 clauses 24 and 25, constitute a homogeneous class for the purposes of Part III and Part XVI, and that a State cannot sub-classify within the presidential SC/ST list to provide differential benefit to particular sub-groups. The ruling directly engaged the Article 366 definitions, holding that the cross-reference to presidential orders meant the SC/ST category was a unified constitutional class that could not be administratively fragmented. This ruling was later reconsidered and substantially overruled by State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024).

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench overruled EV Chinnaiah and held that States do have power to sub-classify within the SC/ST lists to give preferential treatment to more disadvantaged sub-groups, provided the sub-classification is based on empirical data about relative disadvantage. The ruling required Courts to examine the Article 366 clauses 24 and 25 definitions afresh — holding that the cross-reference to presidential orders determines which castes and tribes are in the category, but does not prescribe how States must distribute benefits among the members of those categories. The judgment is the most recent and most significant ruling on the constitutional scope of the Article 366 SC/ST definitions.

**Janhit Abhiyan v Union of India (2022) 3 SCC 1 (EWS reservation case)** — The five-judge bench upheld the Constitution (One Hundred and Third Amendment) Act, 2019, which introduced ten percent reservation for Economically Weaker Sections (EWS) not covered by existing SC/ST/OBC reservations. The majority held that the EWS reservation did not violate the basic structure. The dissenting opinions engaged with the relationship between "backward classes" (clause 26C) and the EWS category, examining whether economic criterion alone can be a valid constitutional basis for reservation. The case remains the leading authority on the scope and limits of the backward classes definition inserted in Article 366.

**Venkataramana Devaru v State of Mysore (1958) SCR 895** — An early case engaging the Article 366 definition of "Scheduled Castes" and the relationship between SC status and temple entry. The Court held that SC identity for constitutional purposes was determined by the presidential order under Article 341, and that the constitutional definition governed over other criteria. This early affirmation of Article 366's definitional primacy established the principle that the presidential order — cross-referenced from Article 366 — creates a conclusive constitutional determination of SC status.

## Reading the definitions alongside operative provisions

The definitions in Article 366 are inert in isolation — they become consequential only when read together with the operative provisions that use the defined terms. Courts interpreting Articles 15, 16, 340, 341, 342, 342A, 246A, and 279A regularly turn to Article 366 as the first port of call for the meaning of constitutional categories. The interplay between the definitions here and the presidential notification power in Articles 341, 342, and 342A is a perennial source of litigation over inclusion and exclusion of specific communities from SC, ST, and OBC lists.

## Current significance

Article 366 is constitutionally active in several areas of contemporary litigation. The sub-classification of Scheduled Castes and Tribes under the Davinder Singh framework requires courts and states to engage with the Article 366 definitions to understand the boundaries of permissible administrative differentiation within constitutionally defined categories. The GST litigation — about the scope of "goods," "services," and "goods and services tax" as defined in clauses 12, 26A, and 12A — is a source of ongoing constitutional and statutory disputes before the GST Council and the courts. And the backward classes definition in clause 26C, read with Articles 342A and 16(4), continues to generate litigation about the respective powers of Centre and States to determine OBC status.

The "unless the context otherwise requires" escape valve in the preamble to Article 366's list has been invoked in several modern cases where a mechanical application of a definition would produce results inconsistent with the scheme of the provision being interpreted. Courts have used this escape valve to avoid definitional absurdities while maintaining the general rule that Article 366 definitions are presumptively applicable throughout the constitutional text.
