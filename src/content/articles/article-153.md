## What this Article does

Article 153 creates the constitutional office of Governor for every State, making it the structural pivot around which the entire Part VI framework revolves. The office is not optional or contingent: the mandatory language "there shall be" imposes an unqualified obligation on the Union to ensure every State has a Governor at all times. All subsequent provisions — dealing with appointment (Article 155), qualifications (Article 157), conditions of office (Article 158), oath (Article 159), discretionary powers (Article 163), legislative role (Articles 168, 175, 176), and executive functions — flow from this foundational office.

The Constituent Assembly debated whether to have elected Governors, as in the United States model. B.R. Ambedkar and other framers ultimately chose presidential appointment to avoid conflicts between a directly elected Governor and the elected state legislature, and to maintain coordination within the federal scheme. The single sentence of the main clause reflects this deliberate design choice.

## The proviso: one Governor for multiple States

The proviso — inserted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956 — allows the appointment of the same person as Governor for two or more States. It was introduced contemporaneously with the States Reorganisation Act 1956, which dramatically redrawn India's map along linguistic lines. The proviso was a practical device to manage the transition and fill gubernatorial vacancies without delay. In subsequent decades it became standard practice for the Union government to deploy this arrangement when vacancies accumulated.

The proviso is textually permissive, not mandatory: it says "nothing in this article shall prevent" such an appointment, leaving the decision entirely to the Centre's discretion. Critics argue this carries a federal cost — a Governor simultaneously serving multiple States cannot give each full constitutional attention, and a charge-holding Governor seen as the Centre's appointee across adjacent States may lack the institutional legitimacy needed to act as a neutral constitutional authority in each.

## Governor as constitutional head, not Centre's agent

The tension built into Article 153 — a Governor who is a constitutional figure but is appointed by and removable at the pleasure of the Centre — has generated sustained constitutional controversy. The Sarkaria Commission (1988) recommended that Governors should be persons of stature not connected with the politics of the state to which they are appointed, and genuinely non-partisan. The Punchhi Commission (2010) further recommended a fixed, non-renewable five-year term to reduce dependence on the Centre's pleasure. Neither recommendation has been enacted, leaving the tension inherent in Article 153 unresolved by constitutional amendment.

## Comparison with Article 52 (President)

Article 153 parallels Article 52 at the State level: just as there shall be a President of India, there shall be a Governor for each State. The parallel extends to the constitutional character of both offices as constitutional heads who act on the aid and advice of their respective Councils of Ministers. However, where the President is elected by an electoral college under Articles 54-55, the Governor's appointment under Article 155 is entirely in the hands of the Union executive — a structural asymmetry that has no counterpart in the presidential provisions and which makes the Governor's federal role inherently more contested.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court's seven-judge Constitution Bench held that the Governor, as a constitutional head created by Article 153, must act on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers in virtually all matters. The judgment settled that the office is not an independent political executive but a constitutional functionary operating within the parliamentary framework.

**B.P. Singhal v Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court examined the conditions under which a Governor may be removed before the expiry of five years, holding that while the President has unfettered discretion to appoint a Governor, removal cannot be arbitrary or for political reasons. The case gave content to the constitutional status of the office established by Article 153 and remains the leading authority on the limits of the Centre's power over sitting Governors.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Court held that a Governor cannot act in a manner that subverts the democratic mandate, specifically that recommending President's Rule on false or fabricated grounds violates the Constitution. The judgment reinforced that the office created by Article 153 is premised on constitutional propriety rather than partisan preference.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The five-judge bench's strict reading of gubernatorial discretion was premised on the character of the office under Article 153: a Governor who is not directly elected and is not directly accountable to the people of the State cannot legitimately act as a political actor in state affairs except within the very narrow constitutional space specifically carved out.

## Current significance

The Article 153 office has come under repeated scrutiny as tensions between Governors and elected state governments have intensified across multiple States, producing a steady stream of Supreme Court interventions. The proviso permitting a single Governor for multiple States continues to be invoked, drawing criticism that charge-holding arrangements dilute the focused constitutional oversight each State deserves. These structural tensions have prompted calls for a constitutional amendment to align the Article 153 office more clearly with its non-partisan role, including by incorporating the Sarkaria and Punchhi criteria into the article itself rather than leaving them as unenforceable recommendations.
