## Why it matters

Public employment is not merely a livelihood — it is one of the most powerful channels of social mobility in India, and historically, access to the civil services has been deeply shaped by caste and class privilege. Article 16 brings constitutional equality doctrine into the domain of public appointments, but it does so with full awareness that a guarantee of formal equal opportunity, applied to a society with profound structural inequalities, can perpetuate rather than correct those inequalities.

The reservation provisions in Article 16 — clauses (4), (4A), and (4B), all introduced or expanded through constitutional amendments — reflect the founding judgment that communities historically excluded from state services must be actively represented in them, and that representation in the bureaucracy, police, and judiciary is itself a social justice objective of constitutional significance. Maneka Gandhi's dissent in the Constituent Assembly debates makes this explicit: reservation in services was not, in the framers' view, a departure from equality but an expression of it — equality of result rather than merely equality of formal opportunity for groups starting from positions of severe disadvantage.

The political salience of Article 16 reservation is immense. Changes to the reservation framework in public employment directly affect lakhs of appointments, promotional decisions, and seniority positions in central and state government services. The cases that interpret Article 16 are therefore among the most politically sensitive constitutional decisions in India, and several of them have produced both judicial and legislative responses — a cycle of court decisions, constitutional amendments, new court decisions, and further amendments that reflects the ongoing democratic contestation about the proper limits of affirmative action.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) guarantees "equality of opportunity" for all citizens in "matters relating to employment or appointment to any office under the State." The phrase "equality of opportunity" was deliberately chosen over "equality" tout court — it acknowledges that the starting point of equal opportunity is the relevant constitutional guarantee, not equal outcomes. The distinction matters because reservation provisions are constitutionally justified precisely as instruments of equalising opportunity, not as violations of equality.

Clause (2) adds a specific anti-discrimination mandate: no citizen shall be "ineligible for" or "discriminated against" in public employment on specified grounds. The list of grounds in Article 16(2) is longer than in Article 15(1) — it adds descent, place of birth, and residence to the religion, race, caste, and sex of Article 15. The addition of "descent" is particularly significant: it prevents states from excluding persons from employment based on genealogical factors, reinforcing the anti-caste dimension of the provision.

Clause (4) — the foundational reservation provision — uses the phrase "in the opinion of the State." This phrase grants the government significant discretion in identifying backward classes and in determining when they are inadequately represented. However, courts have held that the discretion is not unfettered: the opinion must be based on facts, must involve an inquiry, and must not be coloured by political considerations unrelated to backwardness and representation. The Indra Sawhney and Nagaraj decisions have imposed progressively more stringent procedural requirements on the exercise of this discretion.

Clauses (4A) and (4B) were added by the Constitution (77th Amendment) Act 1995 and the Constitution (81st Amendment) Act 2000 respectively. Clause (4A) extends reservation to promotions within the services for SCs and STs where they are inadequately represented. Clause (4B) allows "carry-forward" of unfilled reserved vacancies to subsequent years without those carried-forward vacancies counting toward the fifty-percent ceiling for the year in which they are filled. These amendments were responses to specific Supreme Court decisions that had constrained promotional reservation and carry-forward arrangements — another iteration of the amendment-in-response-to-judgment cycle.

## Key cases

### The Mandal verdict: Indra Sawhney

**Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992)** — The nine-judge bench verdict on the Mandal Commission recommendations is the foundational precedent on Article 16. Prime Minister V P Singh's government had issued the Mandal Commission Implementation Order in 1990, reserving 27 percent of central government posts for Other Backward Classes, in addition to the existing 22.5 percent for SCs and STs. The order triggered massive social unrest, court challenges, and eventually the reference to a nine-judge bench.

The Court upheld the 27 percent OBC reservation under Article 16(4) but imposed five critical conditions. First, the aggregate of reservations under Article 16(4) together with reservations for SCs and STs may not ordinarily exceed fifty percent of available posts in a year — this ceiling was derived from the equality guarantee of Articles 14 and 16(1) and reflects the Court's view that reservations above fifty percent cannot claim to be a compensatory equality measure without becoming a general denial of equal treatment. Second, the creamy layer — the more economically and socially advanced members of OBC communities — must be excluded from the benefits of Article 16(4), because they no longer suffer the social disability that the provision is designed to address. Third, promotional reservations for OBCs are not constitutionally permissible under Article 16(4) — that provision covers appointments, not promotions; this part of Indra Sawhney led directly to the 77th Amendment inserting Article 16(4A). Fourth, no separate reservation is permissible for the "most backward" classes within the OBC category (the "backward among the backward") in the form of a sub-quota within the OBC quota — though the Court carved an exception for Scheduled Tribes in some contexts. Fifth, reservation is permissible only in initial appointments, not in every selection within service.

The Indra Sawhney decision is the doctrinal foundation on which all subsequent Article 16 cases are built. The fifty-percent ceiling and the creamy layer exclusion are both derived from it and have been consistently applied, though states have regularly tested their limits.

### Quantifiable data for promotional reservations: Nagaraj

**M. Nagaraj v. Union of India (2006)** — The constitutional amendments inserting Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) were challenged before a five-judge bench. The Court upheld the validity of both clauses — the constituent power is not limited by Article 16's equality guarantee in the way that ordinary legislation is — but imposed conditions on the exercise of the power created by these clauses. Before a state can make provision for promotional reservation with consequential seniority for SCs/STs under Article 16(4A), it must first collect quantifiable data demonstrating: (i) the backwardness of the class, (ii) the inadequacy of their representation in the service, and (iii) the compliance of the reservation with the overall efficiency of administration (Article 335).

The Nagaraj conditions had the practical effect of requiring states to commission empirical studies before implementing promotional reservations — a procedural burden that proved difficult for many states to satisfy. The judgment also retained the fifty-percent ceiling and the creamy layer requirement for SC/ST promotional reservations, though the Court acknowledged that applying a creamy layer concept to Scheduled Castes and Tribes is constitutionally more complex than applying it to OBCs, given that SC/ST backwardness is constitutionally recognised whereas OBC backwardness requires individual determination.

### Reading down Nagaraj: Jarnail Singh

**Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018)** — A five-judge bench re-examined the Nagaraj conditions in light of subsequent experience. The Court held that Nagaraj was wrong to require states to re-demonstrate the "backwardness" of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes before making promotional reservations — the constitutional presumption of their backwardness, which underlies their placement in the SC/ST lists in the first instance, cannot be dislodged by a requirement of individual state-level proof. The constitutional specification of SCs and STs as backward is itself a finding of backwardness.

However, the Court upheld the remaining two Nagaraj conditions: states must still demonstrate the inadequacy of SC/ST representation in the service (based on quantifiable data), and must assess whether promotional reservation is compatible with the efficiency of administration under Article 335. The Jarnail Singh decision struck a balance — easing the backwardness-proof burden while retaining the representational data requirement. It also expressly did not extend the creamy layer concept to SC/ST promotional reservations, noting that the question raised constitutional issues of the highest complexity that would require separate determination.

### The EWS reservation: Janhit Abhiyan

**Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (2022)** — The Constitution (103rd Amendment) Act inserted Article 16(6), permitting up to ten percent reservation in appointments for economically weaker sections, in addition to existing reservations under clauses (4) and (4A). The five-judge bench upheld Article 16(6) by a three-to-two majority. The majority held that economic disadvantage is a constitutionally permissible criterion for reservation under Article 16, that the EWS reservation does not breach the fifty-percent ceiling because it was inserted as a separate constitutional provision outside the framework addressed by Indra Sawhney, and that the exclusion of OBCs, SCs, and STs from EWS benefits is constitutionally justified.

The two dissenting opinions represent the most sustained critique of the EWS reservation from a constitutional standpoint. Justice Chandrachud held that the amendment violates the basic structure because it introduces a reservation framework disconnected from the social justice and representational equality rationale that Article 16(4) embodies — it treats reservation as a tool of economic upliftment rather than as a remedy for caste-based historical exclusion from state services. He also argued that excluding the most disadvantaged communities from EWS benefits while making them available to the more socially privileged sections of society produces an outcome fundamentally at odds with equality doctrine.

### Sub-classification for promotional purposes: Davinder Singh

**State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held that states may sub-classify within the Scheduled Caste reservation to create preferential quotas for more deprived sub-groups, as long as the sub-classification is based on empirical data on inadequate representation. For Article 16 purposes, this means states can allocate, for example, a portion of the overall SC quota specifically to communities within the SC list that have historically received fewer government jobs than other SC communities. The decision overruled E V Chinnaiah (2004), which had held that the SC list is a homogeneous constitutional class and cannot be further classified.

The Davinder Singh approach recognises that Article 16's equity goal requires looking beyond categorical equality (all SCs treated alike) toward substantive representation (ensuring the most underrepresented within a category actually benefit). The condition of empirical data on inadequate representation links the sub-classification power to Nagaraj-style procedural requirements, ensuring that sub-classification is not driven by electoral politics rather than genuine representational deficits.

## Current significance

The sub-classification framework from Davinder Singh is the most active area of Article 16 litigation. Multiple states have enacted sub-classification legislation and these laws are before High Courts and the Supreme Court at various stages. The critical question — what quantum and quality of data is sufficient to support a sub-classification claim — is being worked out in real time, and the Supreme Court has not yet provided definitive guidance on the evidentiary standard.

The creamy layer question for SC/ST communities — whether the same income- and social-advancement-based exclusion that applies to OBCs should also apply to Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes within their reserved categories — has been repeatedly flagged but not resolved. Jarnail Singh explicitly deferred it. As the social and economic profile of SC/ST communities becomes more differentiated, the pressure to apply a creamy layer principle to promotional reservations for these communities is increasing, and the Court will eventually have to decide it.

The interaction between Article 16 and Article 335 — which says that the claims of SCs and STs to services must be taken into consideration consistently with the maintenance of efficiency of administration — has become more salient in the context of promotional reservations. Nagaraj and Jarnail Singh both require an assessment of administrative efficiency before promotional reservation is implemented, but the standard for what constitutes an acceptable efficiency impact has not been precisely defined. States take widely varying views on this requirement.

The data requirements under Nagaraj and Davinder Singh create significant administrative burdens that have led to years-long gaps in promotional reservation implementation. Several states have either failed to collect the required data or have had their promotional reservation orders stayed by courts for want of adequate data. This administrative difficulty is producing constitutional pressure to either relax the data requirements or to establish standardised methodologies for collecting and presenting the required information.
