Disqualifications for membership.—(1) A person shall be disqualified for being chosen as, and for being, a member of either House of Parliament— [(a) if he holds any office of profit under the Government of India or the Government of any State, other than an office declared by Parliament by law not to disqualify its holder;]

## Why it matters

Article 102 is the constitutional gate on parliamentary membership. Its grounds for disqualification serve distinct constitutional purposes: the office-of-profit bar prevents the executive from placing MPs in financially dependent positions, the citizenship and allegiance rules protect national sovereignty, and the Tenth Schedule disqualification under clause (2) — added by the 52nd Amendment — attempts to stabilise governments by penalising elected members who defect from the party on whose platform they were elected.

The article has generated sustained litigation because its grounds are politically charged. The office-of-profit test determines whether parliamentary membership can coexist with government-adjacent roles. The anti-defection clause has, paradoxically, concentrated power in the hands of party leadership and presiding officers in ways the framers did not fully anticipate when they introduced the Tenth Schedule.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)(a) — office of profit:** A person holding a government-remunerated office is disqualified unless Parliament has declared that particular office exempt. The "office of profit" test asks whether the position carries pecuniary advantage at the government's cost — salary, facilities, or powers that confer financial benefit. Ministers are expressly excluded by the Explanation.

**Clauses (1)(b) and (1)(c) — incapacity and insolvency:** Disqualification for unsound mind requires a formal court declaration. Insolvency disqualification lapses when discharge is obtained, allowing the former insolvent to stand for election again.

**Clause (1)(d) — citizenship and allegiance:** Voluntary acquisition of foreign citizenship or acknowledgement of allegiance to a foreign state disqualifies the person immediately, even without a formal proceeding. The Election Commission typically determines whether the disqualification applies when the question is referred.

**Clause (1)(e) — statutory disqualification:** Parliament may by law create additional grounds. The Representation of the People Act 1951 is the principal statute, providing for conviction-based disqualification (section 8) and other electoral offences.

**Clause (2) — Tenth Schedule:** Added by the 52nd Amendment, this makes Tenth Schedule anti-defection disqualification a constitutional ground. The Tenth Schedule is administered by the Speaker of the relevant House, and the Speaker's decision is subject to judicial review on constitutional grounds.

## Key cases

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992):** The Supreme Court upheld the constitutional validity of the Tenth Schedule (Article 102(2)) but struck down paragraph 7, which had barred judicial review of the Speaker's disqualification decisions. The Court held that the Speaker, acting as a tribunal, is subject to judicial review on grounds of illegality, irrationality, and procedural impropriety.

**Jaya Bachchan v. Union of India (2006):** The Supreme Court held that Jaya Bachchan held an office of profit as Chairman of the Uttar Pradesh Film Development Corporation and was accordingly disqualified under Article 102(1)(a), despite having resigned before the Election Commission's declaration. The judgment clarified the multi-factor test for identifying an "office of profit."

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007):** The Constitution Bench held that Parliament has the power to expel its members, but examined the overlap between expulsion and disqualification under Article 102, confirming that disqualification is a distinct constitutional concept from expulsion for misconduct.

**Subramanian Swamy v. Election Commission of India (2013):** The Court reaffirmed that disqualification under Article 102(1)(e) read with the Representation of the People Act is a field where Parliament has broad legislative discretion, but the constitutional floor set by Article 102 itself cannot be undermined.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016):** A five-judge bench held that a Speaker who is themselves subject to a removal notice cannot adjudicate Tenth Schedule disqualification petitions — addressing the structural conflict of interest endemic to the Article 102(2) regime.

## Current significance

The anti-defection provisions in Article 102(2) have become one of the most litigated areas of Indian constitutional law. Disqualification petitions have languished for years before Speakers who are themselves members of the ruling party, raising acute conflicts of interest. The Supreme Court has repeatedly called for a time-limit on Speaker decisions and has examined whether courts may directly decide disqualification when a Speaker fails to act within a reasonable time.

The office-of-profit ground resurfaces periodically when government-adjacent positions held by sitting MPs generate controversy, and Parliament has responded by periodically expanding the exemptions list — a practice that critics argue blunts the constitutional bar. The interaction between Article 102(1)(e) and Section 8 of the Representation of the People Act continues to test the boundaries of conviction-based disqualification, particularly after the Supreme Court's 2013 ruling in Lily Thomas that removed the appeal-pending buffer.
