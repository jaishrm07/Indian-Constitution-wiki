## Why it matters

Article 223 ensures that a High Court never lapses into constitutional
leaderlessness. The Chief Justice performs functions — allocating work between
benches, constituting larger benches to hear constitutional questions,
representing the court in administrative dealings with the government — that
cannot simply be suspended because the office holder is absent or the post is
vacant. Article 223 fills the gap by authorising the President to designate
an acting Chief Justice immediately.

The article matters beyond routine administrative continuity because extended
acting Chief Justice tenures have become a symptom of the broader appointments
crisis. When a Chief Justice retires and months pass without a permanent
successor being confirmed, the acting arrangement stretches from a brief bridging
measure into a prolonged period during which the most senior judge exercises
the full powers of the Chief Justice without having been appointed through the
collegium process for that specific role.

## Reading the provision

Article 223 covers two distinct triggers. The first is vacancy — the Chief
Justice's office is empty because the previous occupant has retired, resigned,
died, or been removed. The second is inability — the Chief Justice is absent on
leave, ill, or otherwise unable to perform duties, but the office itself is not
vacant. For vacancies, the acting arrangement continues until a permanent
appointment is made. For inability, it ends when the Chief Justice resumes.

The article vests the designation power in the President without expressly
requiring consultation with the Chief Justice of India. In practice, however,
the Chief Justice of India recommends the designee, and the President acts on
that recommendation. The convention that the most senior puisne judge serves as
acting Chief Justice is followed almost invariably, and any deviation would
require a specific recommendation from the Chief Justice of India with
accompanying justification.

An acting Chief Justice appointed under Article 223 exercises the complete
powers of the office — judicial and administrative. This includes presiding
over full benches, constituting special benches for constitutional matters,
allocating cases, and exercising all statutory powers vested in the Chief
Justice by High Court rules and legislation. The acting appointment does not
create any independent claim to permanent appointment. The collegium considers
the acting Chief Justice's record during the acting tenure, but the permanent
appointment is made on the collegium's recommendation independently of the
acting arrangement.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court held that
presidential powers under provisions of this character — including Article 223
— are exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers in the formal
sense, but that the judicial conventions governing the recommendation of the
designee must be respected. The case reinforced that constitutional form
(presidential action) and constitutional substance (collegium recommendation)
can be distinguished without inconsistency.

**State of Maharashtra v Labhchand Nanuram Bhavsar** — The Court confirmed that
an acting Chief Justice appointed under Article 223 has full authority to
exercise all constitutional and administrative powers of the Chief Justice
during the period of the appointment. There is no category of Chief Justice's
powers that an acting Chief Justice cannot exercise. The acting appointment is
not a diminished form of the office.

**Union of India v Sankalchand Himatlal Sheth (1977)** — The broader principle
from this transfer case — that the President acts on the recommendation of the
Chief Justice of India in all matters relating to the composition and leadership
of High Courts — has been applied by courts to confirm that the Article 223
designation also follows the Chief Justice of India's recommendation, even
though the article does not expressly say so.

## Current significance

Article 223 is invoked multiple times each year across India's 25 High Courts
as Chief Justices retire, take leave, or await succession. The frequency and
duration of acting Chief Justice tenures have become a reliable indicator of
how smoothly the Article 217 appointment pipeline is functioning. When the gap
between a Chief Justice's retirement and their successor's appointment stretches
beyond two or three months, it is a sign that the collegium and the government
are not processing recommendations promptly.

Extended acting tenures — sometimes exceeding six months — raise institutional
questions about legitimacy and accountability. An acting Chief Justice has not
been confirmed through the full collegium process for that role, yet exercises
all the powers of a substantive office-holder. Reform proposals have included
setting a maximum duration for acting arrangements and requiring the collegium
to expedite permanent appointments whenever an acting arrangement exceeds a
specified period, though no legislative or procedural mechanism implementing
such a requirement has been adopted.

The counterpart provision for the Supreme Court is Article 126. The structural
difference is that Article 126 uses the phrase "capable of performing the duties
of the office," suggesting a judgment about suitability, while Article 223
simply says "such one of the other Judges as the President may appoint." In
practice this distinction has not mattered — both provisions result in the
senior-most available judge being designated — but it reflects the greater
political sensitivity surrounding the Chief Justice of India's office compared
to that of a State High Court.
