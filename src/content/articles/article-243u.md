officialText: |-
  243U. Duration of Municipalities, etc.—(1) Every Municipality, unless sooner dissolved under any law for the time being in force, shall continue for five years from the date appointed for its first meeting and no longer:

  Provided that a Municipality shall be given a reasonable opportunity of being heard before its dissolution.

  (2) No amendment of any law for the time being in force shall have the effect of causing dissolution of a Municipality at any level, which is functioning immediately before such amendment, till the expiration of its duration specified in clause (1).

  (3) An election to constitute a Municipality shall be completed,—

  (a) before the expiry of its duration specified in clause (1);

  (b) before the expiration of a period of six months from the date of its dissolution:

  Provided that where the remainder of the period for which the dissolved Municipality would have continued is less than six months, it shall not be necessary to hold any election under this clause for constituting the Municipality for such period.

  (4) A Municipality constituted upon the dissolution of a Municipality before the expiration of its duration shall continue only for the remainder of the period for which the dissolved Municipality would have continued under clause (1) had it not been so dissolved.

## Why it matters

Article 243U is the constitutional guarantee of continuous municipal democracy. Before the 74th Amendment, state governments could dissolve municipal bodies by executive order, extend their terms indefinitely, or impose administrator's rule without any constitutional constraint on the duration. The history of Indian urban governance is replete with episodes of municipal dissolution followed by long periods of administrator-led governance, often serving the political interests of the state government rather than the administrative needs of the city. Article 243U changes this landscape fundamentally by embedding the five-year term and the mandatory election timeline directly in the Constitution.

Three provisions are particularly protective. First, the requirement that a Municipality be given a "reasonable opportunity of being heard" before dissolution (proviso to clause 1) imports natural justice directly into the dissolution process at the constitutional level — a municipality cannot be dissolved without being informed of the grounds and given a chance to respond. Second, clause (2) prevents state legislatures from engineering a dissolution by simply amending the relevant Municipalities Act mid-term; a functioning Municipality cannot be ended merely because the law under which it was constituted is changed. Third, clause (3)'s six-month election deadline applies even after dissolution, preventing prolonged administrator's rule from becoming a de facto substitute for elected governance.

Clause (4) — the remainder-term rule for post-dissolution municipalities — prevents states from resetting the clock by dissolving and reconstituting municipalities on a political timetable rather than the constitutional one.

## How courts use it

Article 243U has been litigated extensively in contexts where state governments have delayed municipal elections, dissolved municipal corporations for ostensibly administrative reasons, or sought to extend the period of administrative rule beyond the six-month deadline. Courts have consistently held that the six-month election obligation is mandatory, that the State Election Commission's constitutional independence under Article 243ZA is the mechanism for enforcing it, and that neither the state government nor the state legislature can override these constitutional requirements.

Several High Courts have held that delays in publishing census-based ward delimitation or OBC reservation orders — which states have used as justifications for postponing municipal elections — do not override the constitutional election timeline of Article 243U. The Supreme Court, following its Panchayat election jurisprudence, has generally maintained that elections must be held within the six-month period regardless of incomplete delimitation or reservation processes, which can be completed through interim arrangements.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — Though predating the 74th Amendment, this case's analysis of the protection of elected institutions from arbitrary executive action provides the doctrinal background against which Article 243U's protections were designed. The 74th Amendment's mandatory election and dissolution protections were a direct constitutional response to the kind of executive override of local democratic institutions that this earlier jurisprudence had only partially constrained.

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004)** — The Supreme Court held that the constitutional obligations under Part IX (and by analogy Part IXA) to hold timely elections are not subject to administrative or political convenience. This judgment has been extensively cited in Article 243U litigation to prevent state governments from indefinitely postponing municipal elections on various grounds.

**Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Corporation of the City of Ahmedabad (2006)** — The Gujarat High Court applied Article 243U to strike down administrative delays in holding municipal elections after a corporation's term expired, holding that the State Election Commission must proceed with elections in compliance with the constitutional timetable even if ward delimitation or reservation orders have not been finalised. The case became a leading authority on the primacy of the Article 243U election obligation.

**Vikas Kishanrao Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021)** — The Supreme Court addressed the OBC reservation issue in the context of municipal elections, and while the primary focus was on the triple test for OBC reservation, the Court's analysis necessarily engaged with the Article 243U election timeline. The Court held that elections could not be indefinitely delayed pending OBC reservation orders, and directed that elections proceed with reduced OBC quotas if necessary to comply with the constitutional timeline.

## Current significance

Article 243U remains actively litigated. The intersection of municipal election timelines with the politically sensitive processes of ward delimitation, OBC reservation determination, and administrative reorganisation of municipal areas has generated repeated controversies. State governments — across party lines — have shown a persistent tendency to delay municipal elections through a sequence of pending procedural steps, using each incomplete step as justification for deferring the next.

The Supreme Court's 2021-22 jurisprudence on OBC reservations in local body elections, which required a "triple test" (dedicated commission, proportionality, 50% ceiling compliance) before OBC seats could be reserved, significantly complicated the Article 243U election timeline for many states. Several states — including Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, and Uttar Pradesh — ran their municipal bodies under administrator's rule for extended periods pending compliance with the triple test requirement, generating fresh Article 243U litigation about the constitutional boundary between permissible election preparation and unconstitutional delay.
