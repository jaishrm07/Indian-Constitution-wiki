## Why it matters

Article 21 is the most litigated, most interpreted, and most transformed provision in the Indian Constitution. In twenty-two words, it has become the constitutional foundation for rights that were not explicitly drafted into the original text — from the right to privacy to the right to a clean environment, from the right to bail for undertrials to the right to free legal aid, from the right to live with dignity to the right to choose one's partner. The history of Article 21 is the history of the Supreme Court's relationship with individual liberty and with the executive power that can threaten it.

The framers deliberately chose "procedure established by law" rather than the American formula "due process of law." Dr. Ambedkar and the Constituent Assembly, aware of how American courts had used "due process" to strike down social legislation under the Lochner-era substantive due process doctrine, wanted to prevent the Indian judiciary from similarly voiding economic regulation. The deliberate rejection of "due process" in favour of a procedural formula was a specific constitutional choice — and it produced the AK Gopalan era from 1950 to 1977, in which Article 21 was read narrowly as requiring only that some procedure exist, not that it be fair.

The 1978 watershed in Maneka Gandhi v Union of India changed everything. The Court held that the procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable — not any procedure enacted by Parliament, but one that satisfies an independent constitutional standard of fairness. More importantly, the Court held that Articles 14, 19, and 21 are not independent silos but form an integrated guarantee: a law that affects life or liberty must satisfy the reasonableness standard of Article 14, the freedom requirements of Article 19, and the procedural fairness requirement of Article 21. This golden triangle doctrine, as it came to be known, is the doctrinal foundation of modern Indian constitutional law.

From Maneka Gandhi forward, Article 21 has been the vehicle for a constitutional project of remarkable ambition: recognising rights that the text does not enumerate but that the Court has held to be necessary for a meaningful right to life. The list now spans hundreds of precedents. Taken together, they reflect the Court's view that the right to "life" in Article 21 is not mere physical survival — it is a right to live with dignity, health, freedom, and the conditions that make human flourishing possible.

## Reading the provision

The text is deceptively short. Four key terms carry the entire weight of the article: "person," "deprived," "life and personal liberty," and "procedure established by law."

**"Person"** — unlike Article 19, which is limited to citizens, Article 21's protection extends to "any person" including non-citizens, stateless persons, and foreign nationals. A foreign national facing deportation retains Article 21 rights. A prisoner retains Article 21 rights (though certain restrictions flow from the fact of imprisonment). Corporate entities, however, cannot claim Article 21 rights — life and personal liberty are inherently natural-person rights.

**"Deprived"** — the deprivation must be by the State or by an authority acting under state power. Private invasions of liberty, however serious, do not engage Article 21 (though they may generate rights to state protection under Article 21's positive obligations). The word "deprived" has been interpreted broadly to cover not just physical detention but any severe interference with the conditions that make life meaningful — forced eviction from shelter, denial of essential medical care, and custodial torture have all been treated as deprivations of life under Article 21.

**"Life and personal liberty"** — "life" includes not just the body but the quality of life: Olga Tellis held that the right to livelihood is part of the right to life, since without a means of earning one's living, life cannot be sustained. "Personal liberty" covers the entire range of individual freedom short of death — freedom from arbitrary detention, from surveillance, from coercive interference with private choices. After Maneka Gandhi, the phrase is read expansively: the right to travel abroad, the right to move freely, the right to carry on a profession, and the right to privacy have all been anchored in "life and personal liberty."

**"Procedure established by law"** — the seminal interpretive question. AK Gopalan (1950) held that any law enacted by Parliament satisfies this requirement; the Constitution only requires that a procedure exist, not that it be just. Maneka Gandhi (1978) overruled this approach and held that the procedure must itself be fair, just, and reasonable. The Court held that a procedure that is "arbitrary, oppressive or unreasonable" cannot constitute "procedure established by law" in a constitutional sense — it is not law at all, because it violates the substantive requirements of Article 14. This construction effectively imports a substantive due process standard through the back door of procedural review.

## The AK Gopalan era (1950-1977)

The early Supreme Court read Article 21 in strict isolation. In AK Gopalan v State of Madras (1950), a six-judge bench upheld the Preventive Detention Act, holding that "procedure established by law" meant only that there was a law prescribing a procedure — the content, fairness, or reasonableness of that procedure was irrelevant to Article 21. The majority refused to read Articles 14, 19, and 21 together, treating each as an independent provision covering separate subject matter. The result was that the State could deprive a person of personal liberty under any law that prescribed a procedure, however summary or unfair, as long as the enactment itself was within Parliament's competence.

The Gopalan era was not static. In subsequent years, the Court began qualifying the broad reading in individual cases, developing limited exceptions and applying natural justice principles to particular detention regimes. But the fundamental framework — that Article 21 required only procedural form, not substantive fairness — persisted until Maneka Gandhi.

The damage done by Gopalan was starkly exposed during the Emergency of 1975-77. When the Habeas Corpus case (ADM Jabalpur) came before the Supreme Court in 1976, the Gopalan framework meant that the mass detention of political prisoners under the Maintenance of Internal Security Act could not be challenged under Article 21 — because the procedure established by that Act was formally valid and the Presidential Order under Article 359 had suspended the right to move courts under Article 21. Only Justice H R Khanna dissented, arguing that the right to life exists independently of any constitution and that no executive order can suspend it. His dissent, which cost him the position of Chief Justice, is regarded as one of the most significant acts of judicial courage in Indian history.

## The Maneka Gandhi revolution (1978)

The Emergency ended, the detainees were released, and the 44th Amendment restored and strengthened Article 21. But the doctrinal revolution came first from the Court itself, in Maneka Gandhi v Union of India (1978).

Maneka Gandhi challenged the impoundment of her passport on undisclosed grounds under the Passports Act. The Government argued that the right to travel abroad was not a fundamental right and that the passport authority had followed the procedure prescribed by the Act. Justice Bhagwati's judgment for the majority fundamentally changed the constitutional landscape. The Court held that:

- The right to travel abroad is an element of personal liberty under Article 21;
- The procedure under the Passports Act, which did not require notice to the passport holder of the grounds for impoundment or an opportunity to be heard, was not a fair procedure;
- "Procedure established by law" does not mean any procedure — it means a procedure that is fair, just, and reasonable;
- Articles 14, 19, and 21 are not independent compartments but flow into and illuminate each other — a law affecting personal liberty must pass muster under all three simultaneously.

The last holding — the golden triangle doctrine — is the most important structural contribution of Maneka Gandhi. By holding that a law touching Article 21 must also satisfy the reasonableness requirements of Article 14 (equality) and Article 19 (specific freedoms), the Court created a comprehensive substantive review framework for laws affecting liberty. A law that is formally valid under Article 21's procedural requirement may still be struck down as unreasonable under Article 14 or as disproportionately restricting Article 19 freedoms. The result was to import, through the intersection of three articles, the substantive due process protection that the framers had deliberately refused to write into Article 21 alone.

## The expansion of Article 21 rights

The decades following Maneka Gandhi saw an extraordinary expansion of Article 21 through public interest litigation. Courts recognised that the right to "life" encompasses conditions necessary for dignified survival. The following rights have been judicially derived from Article 21:

- **Right to livelihood** (Olga Tellis v Bombay Municipal Corporation, 1985): Pavement dwellers cannot be evicted without notice and rehabilitation alternatives.
- **Right to health and emergency medical care** (Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Samity v State of West Bengal, 1996): State hospitals cannot turn away emergency patients for want of beds or facilities.
- **Right to speedy trial** (Hussainara Khatoon v State of Bihar, 1980): Undertrials detained beyond the maximum sentence for their offence must be released; the right to a speedy trial is fundamental.
- **Right to free legal aid** (Hussainara Khatoon; Khatri v State of Bihar, 1981): A person who cannot afford a lawyer must be provided one by the State before trial.
- **Right against custodial torture and police brutality** (D K Basu v State of West Bengal, 1997): Issued detailed guidelines for arrest and detention that now bind all police officers; custodial violence is a violation of Article 21.
- **Right to a clean environment** (Rural Litigation and Entitlement Kendra v State of UP, 1985; MC Mehta cases): Pollution that endangers life and health is a violation of Article 21; the State has a positive duty to prevent environmental degradation that threatens the right to life.
- **Right to privacy** (Justice K S Puttaswamy v Union of India, 2017): Unanimously confirmed as a fundamental right within the core of Article 21; includes informational, decisional, and physical privacy.
- **Right to dignity** (Francis Coralie Mullin v Union Territory of Delhi, 1981): The right to life includes the right to live with human dignity; denial of basic necessities of life to detainees violates Article 21.
- **Right to education** (Unni Krishnan v State of Andhra Pradesh, 1993; later given express textual basis in Article 21A): Education up to the age of 14 is a fundamental right within Article 21.
- **Right to shelter** (Chameli Singh v State of UP, 1996): The right to life includes the right to a minimum standard of shelter; forcible eviction without rehabilitation affects Article 21.
- **Right to reputation** (Kiran Bedi v Committee of Inquiry, 1989): Unjustified adverse findings that damage a person's reputation engage Article 21.

## Key cases

**AK Gopalan v State of Madras (1950)** — The founding case that set the narrow trajectory of Article 21 for a generation. Gopalan, a communist leader, challenged preventive detention under the Preventive Detention Act. The Court upheld the detention, holding that "procedure established by law" meant only that Parliament had passed a law prescribing a procedure — the fairness or content of that procedure was irrelevant. The majority refused to read Articles 14, 19, and 21 as a single integrated guarantee. The judgment effectively left Parliament free to enact any detention procedure it liked and deprive any person of liberty, as long as the law's form was regular. This reading was repudiated in Maneka Gandhi but shaped constitutional practice for twenty-eight years.

**ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla (1976)** — The Habeas Corpus case of the Emergency. The Supreme Court, by a four-to-one majority, held that during a national emergency with the right to move courts under Article 359 suspended, there was no constitutional right to challenge detention before a court — not even on the ground that the detention was mala fide, served on the wrong person, or procedurally defective. The majority opinion by Chief Justice Ray is widely regarded as the nadir of Indian constitutional jurisprudence. Justice H R Khanna's solitary dissent — arguing that the right to life is pre-constitutional, that no executive order can extinguish it, and that "even in grave emergencies the right to life and liberty is not taken away" — was vindicated by subsequent constitutional development but cost Khanna the Chief Justiceship. The decision was effectively overruled by Maneka Gandhi and then formally repudiated in Puttaswamy's privacy ruling.

**Maneka Gandhi v Union of India (1978)** — The revolution. Justice Bhagwati's judgment fundamentally reoriented Article 21 by holding that the procedure prescribed by law must be fair, just, and reasonable, not arbitrary, fanciful, or oppressive. The golden triangle doctrine — that Articles 14, 19, and 21 form an integrated guarantee — gave the Court comprehensive tools for reviewing any law that restricts liberty. The case transformed Article 21 from a narrow procedural provision into the constitutional home of substantive due process in India, and launched the public interest litigation movement that followed.

**Hussainara Khatoon v State of Bihar (1979-80)** — The foundational case for undertrial rights and the right to speedy trial. Letters sent by a journalist describing the appalling conditions in Bihar jails — thousands of undertrials detained for years beyond the maximum sentence their alleged offences could carry — were converted by the Supreme Court into a writ petition. The Court held that the right to a speedy trial is a fundamental right under Article 21 and ordered the release of undertrials whose detention had exceeded the maximum sentence. The case also established that the State's failure to provide legal aid to indigent accused violates Article 21 and Article 39A. It was the first major instance of epistolary jurisdiction and established the template for using Article 32 and Article 21 together to address systemic rights violations.

**Olga Tellis v Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985)** — Pavement dwellers and slum residents challenged the Bombay Municipal Corporation's demolition of their homes. The Court held that the right to livelihood is part of the right to life under Article 21, since without a means of earning a living, one cannot survive. The City's duty to give reasonable notice before eviction was held to be a constitutional requirement. The case is the foundational precedent for the right to livelihood and has been cited in every subsequent forced eviction case.

**D K Basu v State of West Bengal (1997)** — Responding to a public interest petition about custodial deaths and police torture, the Court issued eleven specific guidelines that every police officer and person responsible for arrest must follow: written arrest memo countersigned by the arrestee and a witness, notification of family, medical examination, right to consult a lawyer, and posting of prescribed procedures at all police stations. Violation of these guidelines was held to be actionable as contempt of court. The judgment is the primary constitutional instrument against custodial violence and has been incorporated into the Code of Criminal Procedure through subsequent legislative reform.

**Francis Coralie Mullin v Union Territory of Delhi (1981)** — The Court held that the right to life includes the right to live with human dignity and all that goes along with it — minimum necessities of life such as adequate nutrition, clothing, reading material, and other basic needs. Restrictions on a detainee's rights to meet family and have access to reading material were held to engage Article 21. The case established that Article 21 has a positive dimension — the State must provide conditions that allow a person to live with dignity, not merely abstain from actively killing them.

**Justice K S Puttaswamy v Union of India (2017)** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that the right to privacy is a fundamental right protected under Article 21. The six concurring opinions located privacy at the intersection of life, liberty, dignity, and personal autonomy. The judgment overruled the earlier M P Sharma and Kharak Singh decisions that had questioned whether privacy was a fundamental right, and it expressly repudiated the reasoning in ADM Jabalpur that had cast doubt on unenumerated rights within Article 21. Privacy was held to include informational privacy (control over personal data), decisional privacy (autonomy in intimate choices), and bodily integrity (protection from non-consensual physical interference). The ruling provided the constitutional foundation for the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 and for challenges to state surveillance programmes.

## Current significance

Article 21 remains the most active provision in Indian constitutional litigation. Challenges to state action across almost every domain — surveillance and data collection, environmental regulation, healthcare access, custodial conditions, right to housing, rights of LGBTQ+ persons, access to justice — arrive at courts framed as Article 21 claims. The Puttaswamy privacy ruling has opened up an entire new front of constitutional litigation about biometric databases, mass surveillance, and the right to informational self-determination.

The positive obligations dimension of Article 21 — the State's duty not merely to refrain from killing but to affirmatively protect conditions that make life meaningful — has become one of the most contested areas of judicial policymaking. When courts direct hospitals to ensure emergency care, prisons to reduce overcrowding, states to address drought and starvation, or governments to respond to a public health crisis, they are exercising Article 21's positive dimension. The boundary between legitimate judicial intervention to protect fundamental rights and judicial overreach into executive and legislative domains is a live constitutional question.

The relationship between Article 21 and criminal procedure has also deepened. The Court has progressively expanded bail rights, ruled against indefinite pre-trial detention under stringent anti-terror and anti-drug laws (UAPA, NDPS), held that bail conditions that effectively deny liberty are themselves Article 21 violations, and engaged with the question of whether the State's failure to investigate and prosecute violence against women violates Article 21's guarantee of life and dignity. Each of these developments reflects the Court's continuing project of giving substantive content to the twenty-two words of Article 21.

The digital era has created a new generation of Article 21 questions. Government use of facial recognition technology in protests, mandatory Aadhaar linkage for social benefits, retention of biometric data, and interception of communications without adequate judicial oversight are all being litigated as Article 21 violations. The Court's answers will define the constitutional limits of the surveillance state in India — the most important ongoing constitutional project since the Puttaswamy ruling itself.
