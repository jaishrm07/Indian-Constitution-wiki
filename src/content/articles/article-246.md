officialText: |-
  246. Subject-matter of laws made by Parliament and by the Legislatures of States.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in clauses (2) and (3), Parliament has exclusive power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List I in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "Union List").

  (2) Notwithstanding anything in clause (3), Parliament, and, subject to clause (1), the Legislature of any State also, have power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List III in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "Concurrent List").

  (3) Subject to clauses (1) and (2), the Legislature of any State has exclusive power to make laws for such State or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "State List").

  (4) Parliament has power to make laws with respect to any matter for any part of the territory of India not included [in a State] notwithstanding that such matter is a matter enumerated in the State List.

## Why it matters

The power to make law — to command behaviour, allocate resources, create rights, and impose obligations — is the most consequential power that a constitutional system distributes. Article 246 is the provision that distributes this power in India. It decides whether it is Parliament or a State Legislature (or both) that can regulate banking, taxation, education, land, police, marriage, environment, telecommunications, and every other domain of governance. Getting the distribution right determines whether India functions as a genuine federation or as a centralised state with nominal provincial governments.

The Indian federal distribution was designed by the Constituent Assembly with the experience of colonial centralisation fresh in its memory, but also with the experience of Partition and the challenge of national integration shaping its priorities. The result is a federation deliberately tilted toward the Union. Parliament has exclusive power over a long and important Union List; Parliament and State Legislatures share the Concurrent List but the Union prevails in case of conflict; States have the State List. Moreover, Parliament can legislate on State List subjects when a national emergency is proclaimed (Article 250), when two or more States consent (Article 252), when Parliament needs to implement international obligations (Article 253), and when the Rajya Sabha passes a special resolution (Article 249). The residuary power — matters not in any of the three Lists — also belongs to Parliament under Article 248.

The practical consequence of Article 246 is that major litigation — about what subject a contested law falls under, about whether a State law conflicts with a Union law on a Concurrent List subject, about whether a new technology or economic activity falls within an existing List entry or requires new legislative action — almost always begins with this article. The pith and substance doctrine, the doctrine of repugnancy under Article 254, the incidental legislation doctrine, and the occupied field doctrine are all tools courts use to resolve Article 246 disputes. They are some of the most frequently applied doctrines in Indian constitutional law.

## Reading the provision

**The "notwithstanding" hierarchy**: Clauses (1) and (2) each use the word "notwithstanding" to establish their place in the hierarchy. Clause (1) — the Union List — prevails over clauses (2) and (3). Clause (2) — the Concurrent List — prevails over clause (3) alone, not over clause (1). Clause (3) — the State List — is residual and subject to both (1) and (2). This cascade means that the Union List always takes priority, the Concurrent List takes priority over the State List, and the State List is purely exclusive within the space that remains after (1) and (2) are satisfied.

**Parliament's power for Union territories**: Clause (4) gives Parliament the power to legislate on State List subjects for Union Territories. This is why Parliament can pass laws on land, police, local government, and other State List subjects for Delhi, Puducherry, and other Union Territories. The clause also explains why the Government of NCT of Delhi controversies involve questions about whether Delhi's elected government has the kind of exclusive State-like power that States have or whether Parliament's clause (4) power gives the Centre a continuing override.

**The three lists and their practical coverage**: The Union List (List I) has 97 entries covering defence, atomic energy, foreign affairs, communications, banking, insurance, income tax, customs, and major constitutional institutions. The State List (List II) has 66 entries covering public order, police, land, agriculture, local government, public health, water, and state taxes. The Concurrent List (List III) has 47 entries covering criminal law, civil procedure, marriage and divorce, bankruptcy, social security, education (after the 42nd Amendment shifted it from State to Concurrent), labour, and economic and social planning. The number of entries in each List is less significant than the content — Union List entries tend to be broader and more economically significant, while State List entries are more locally salient.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's Sub-Committee on Union Powers and the Union Constitution Committee wrestled with the three-list model at length. The basic architecture — a three-list system borrowed from the Government of India Act 1935 — was adopted almost unanimously as the right structure, though the content of each list was extensively debated. Dr. Ambedkar emphasised that the Concurrent List was especially important — it was the space in which the Union and States would share legislative authority in domains where national standards mattered but local variation was also legitimate.

The debate about residuary powers was significant. Several members argued that residuary powers should go to the States (as in the United States, where the Tenth Amendment reserves unenumerated powers to the States). Dr. Ambedkar and Nehru rejected this, arguing that residuary power in a rapidly developing society would most commonly be needed by the Union — which was building new institutions, regulating new industries, and entering new international agreements. The decision to give Parliament the residuary power under Article 248 reflects this Union-tilted conception of Indian federalism.

## The pith and substance doctrine

Courts resolve Article 246 competence disputes using the pith and substance doctrine — they ask what the true nature and character (the "pith and substance") of the impugned legislation is, regardless of how it is titled or ostensibly framed. A State law that appears to regulate agriculture (State List Entry 14) but in substance regulates banking (Union List Entry 45) would be held to be beyond State competence. Conversely, incidental impact on a Union List subject does not destroy State legislative power if the dominant purpose and effect fall within the State List.

The doctrine was developed in the pre-constitutional era by the Privy Council in Prafulla Kumar Mukherjee v Bank of Commerce, Khulna (1947) and was adopted wholesale by the Supreme Court. It gives courts the tool to navigate inevitable overlaps between the Lists — since no list entry can be defined so precisely that it does not touch neighbouring entries — without the consequence that every piece of legislation becomes a constitutional dispute. The question "what is this law really about?" is asked with reference to the law's scheme, operative provisions, purpose, and practical effect.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v Union of India (1963)** — The Supreme Court confirmed Parliament's supremacy in the Union List and rejected the argument that federal balance required an equal footing for all levels of government. The Court held that the Constitution itself distributes power through Article 246 and the Seventh Schedule, and courts must give effect to that distribution as written. This case established that Indian federalism is not one of equal sovereignties — the Union was designed to be stronger, and Article 246's structure reflects that design.

**Prafulla Kumar Mukherjee v Bank of Commerce, Khulna (1947)** — The Privy Council's decision on the pre-constitutional Bengal Money Lenders Act articulated the pith and substance doctrine for resolving legislative competence disputes. Courts look to the dominant purpose and true character of a challenged law to assign it to the correct List entry, even if it incidentally touches another List. This judgment became the foundational doctrinal tool for every Article 246 case.

**ITC Ltd v Agricultural Produce Market Committee (2002)** — A Constitution Bench applied the pith and substance doctrine to resolve a conflict between the Union's power to levy market fees (Entry 52, Union List) and the State's power to regulate agricultural produce markets (Entry 28, State List). The Court confirmed that single legislation may legitimately cover ground touching both Lists so long as its predominant subject matter falls within the enacting legislature's competence.

**GVK Industries Ltd v Income Tax Officer (2011)** — A seven-judge bench addressed the extra-territorial dimension of Article 246 legislative competence. Parliament's power under the Union List does not extend to legislation that operates entirely outside India's territory unless the law has a real and substantive connection with India. The case clarified that Article 246 competence is not unlimited even for Parliament — territorial nexus remains a constitutional constraint on legislative power.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd v State of Haryana (2017)** — A nine-judge bench reconsidered and overruled the compensatory tax doctrine — which had given States an escape from Part XIII free-trade obligations if they levied taxes "compensating" for specific public services. The ruling affected the relationship between Article 246 (taxation competence) and Article 301 (freedom of trade), drawing clearer limits on how States can use their State List taxation powers without violating the federal free-trade norm.

**State of Jharkhand v Brahmaputra Metallics Ltd (2022)** — The Supreme Court addressed the interaction between State List Entry 50 (taxes on mineral rights) and Union legislation under Entry 54 (mines and minerals). The Court held that States retain power to levy mineral-rights taxes even after Parliament's Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, provided the State tax does not conflict with specific parliamentary provisions — a significant ruling for resource-rich States' fiscal autonomy in the natural resources domain.

## Current significance

The Goods and Services Tax's constitutional amendment — Article 246A, inserted in 2016 — created India's most significant restructuring of the Article 246 framework since the Constitution came into force. Article 246A gives Parliament and State Legislatures concurrent power to tax goods and services, superseding the previous regime in which Parliament could tax production (central excise) and services, while States could tax the sale of goods (Entry 54, State List). The three-list framework now has an important exception — GST operates through Article 246A, not through the original List entries.

The continuing relevance of Article 246 and the State List is most visible in domains that are not part of GST — petroleum products, alcohol, real estate (to varying degrees), and electricity. State governments generate most of their own-tax revenue from these sectors, and Union attempts to bring them under GST are strongly resisted as incursions on State List competence. The fiscal federalism dimension of Article 246 — who can tax what — is the most practically important current context for the provision.

The digitisation of the economy has created a range of new legislative competence questions that Article 246 and the Seventh Schedule were not designed to resolve. Questions about data regulation, online gaming, cryptocurrency, and platform-economy workers sit uncomfortably across multiple List entries. The government's approach to each new technology — whether through Union legislation (asserting a Union List entry), through the GST regime, or through concurrent legislative action — shapes the federal architecture for a growing segment of the Indian economy.
