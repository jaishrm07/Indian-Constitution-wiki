## What this Article does

Article 7 is the mirror image of Article 6: it provides that a person who migrated from India to Pakistan after 1 March 1947 is not deemed to be a citizen of India at commencement, notwithstanding Articles 5 and 6. The article thus excludes those who left for Pakistan, preventing dual-benefit claims. The proviso carves out an exception for persons who returned to India under a permit for resettlement or permanent return, treating them as having migrated after 19 July 1948 for the purposes of Article 6(b)(ii).

## The automatic exclusion principle

Article 7's exclusion is automatic — it operates "notwithstanding anything in articles 5 and 6" and does not require any formal act by the State to extinguish the citizenship of a person who migrated to Pakistan. The Supreme Court confirmed in Shanno Devi that the loss of Indian citizenship occurred by the mere fact of migration, not by a subsequent declaration or order. This design was intentional: the framers wanted a clear, self-executing rule that prevented persons who had chosen to make their life in Pakistan from simultaneously claiming Indian citizenship.

The date of 1 March 1947 — approximately five months before Partition on 14 August 1947 — was chosen to capture movements that predated the official division but that took place in anticipation of it. Persons who left India for Pakistan before 1 March 1947 were not covered by Article 7 and would retain whatever citizenship status they had under Article 5 or 6 if they also maintained a domicile or registration link to India.

## The permit proviso — returning migrants

The proviso creates a partial exception for persons who, after migrating to Pakistan, returned to India "under a permit for resettlement or permanent return issued by or under the authority of any law." The Government of India issued such permits under the Influx from Pakistan (Control) Act, 1949. A permit-holder who returned was treated, for Article 6(b)(ii) purposes, as having migrated to India after 19 July 1948 — meaning they had to register as a citizen under the Article 6 procedure rather than simply proving continuous residence. This ensured that even permit returnees were subject to an administrative screening process before citizenship was confirmed.

## The permit system in practice

The permit system was administered from 1948 onwards and allowed Indian authorities to regulate the flow of returnees from Pakistan. Not everyone who wished to return could obtain a permit; the system gave the government discretion to admit returnees selectively. From a citizenship law perspective, possession of a valid permit was essential: a person who returned without a permit could be treated as an illegal entrant and was not protected by Article 7's proviso. The Ebrahim Vazir Mavat case established that such unpermitted returnees had no constitutional citizenship claim.

## Key cases

**Ebrahim Vazir Mavat v. State of Bombay (1954) AIR 1954 SC 229** — One of the earliest Supreme Court cases on Partition-era citizenship. The Court held that a person who had voluntarily gone to Pakistan and returned without a valid permit was not protected by the Article 7 proviso and could be treated as a foreigner subject to deportation proceedings.

**Shanno Devi v. Mangal Sain Wason (1961) AIR 1961 SC 58** — The Court confirmed that Articles 6 and 7 together form a complete code for Partition migrants, and that Article 7's exclusion operates automatically without any further act of the State — citizenship was lost by the mere fact of migration to Pakistan after 1 March 1947.

**State of Bihar v. Amar Singh (1955) AIR 1955 SC 282** — The Court examined the interaction between Articles 5 and 7, confirming that Article 7 carves out a specific exclusion from Article 5's general grant and that the date of migration to Pakistan — not the date of return — is determinative for the application of the article.

## Legacy and current significance

Article 7's logic — that voluntary departure to a newly constituted foreign state forfeits Indian citizenship — was carried forward into section 9 of the Citizenship Act, 1955, which terminates citizenship on voluntary acquisition of foreign nationality. The article is invoked in NRC proceedings in Assam where the question is whether a person's ancestor migrated to East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) at some point between 1947 and 1971 and thereby lost Indian citizenship, potentially making their descendants foreigners. The evidentiary complexity of tracing Partition-era movements through surviving documents is one of the central challenges in NRC litigation.
