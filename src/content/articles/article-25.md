  25. Freedom of conscience and free profession, practice and propagation of religion.—(1) Subject to public order, morality and health and to the other provisions of this Part, all persons are equally entitled to freedom of conscience and the right freely to profess, practice and propagate religion.

  (2) Nothing in this article shall affect the operation of any existing law or prevent the State from making any law—

  (a) regulating or restricting any economic, financial, political or other secular activity which may be associated with religious practice;

  (b) providing for social welfare and reform or the throwing open of Hindu religious institutions of a public character to all classes and sections of Hindus.

  Explanation I.—The wearing and carrying of kirpans shall be deemed to be included in the profession of the Sikh religion.

  Explanation II.—In sub-clause (b) of clause (2), the reference to Hindus shall be construed as including a reference to persons professing the Sikh, Jaina or Buddhist religion, and the reference to Hindu religious institutions shall be construed accordingly.

## Why it matters

India is constitutionally secular — the State neither identifies with nor favours any religion — but its population is deeply religious. Article 25 navigates this tension by guaranteeing religious freedom as a fundamental right while simultaneously ensuring that the State retains enough authority to pursue social reform, prevent exploitation in the name of religion, and protect other fundamental rights where they conflict with religious claims. The provision is the primary constitutional instrument through which Hindu temple-entry reform, caste discrimination in religious institutions, and the rights of religious minorities are adjudicated.

The right protected is threefold: freedom of conscience (the inner realm of belief), the right to profess religion (publicly declaring one's faith), the right to practise religion (performing religious acts and observances), and the right to propagate religion (sharing one's faith with others). The last element was contested in the Constituent Assembly, with some members arguing it should be removed to prevent coercive conversion. The majority retained it, understanding "propagation" as persuasion rather than coercion — the right to share one's faith does not include the right to convert by force, fraud, or undue influence.

The qualifications in clause (1) are simultaneous limits on all four aspects of the right. Public order, morality, and health are the explicit limits — a religious practice that causes violence against others, undermines public health, or offends basic constitutional morality can be restricted. "The other provisions of this Part" adds a further qualification: Article 25 does not exist in isolation. If a religious practice discriminates on grounds of sex, caste, or untouchability, Articles 14, 15, and 17 override the religious-freedom claim. The Sabarimala case was precisely about this conflict — between Article 25(1)'s protection of religious practice and Articles 14 and 17's guarantees of equality and prohibition of untouchability.

Clause (2)'s saving provisions are as important as the right itself. Article 25(2)(a) prevents religious institutions from claiming that their secular activities — financial management, commercial enterprises, employment practices — are exempt from State regulation by virtue of their religious character. Article 25(2)(b) is the provision that made temple-entry legislation constitutionally possible — by explicitly authorising laws that throw open Hindu religious institutions of a public character to all Hindus (with Explanation II extending this to Sikhs, Jains, and Buddhists). This was a direct constitutional mandate for social reform of a specific kind: the elimination of caste-based exclusion from places of public Hindu worship.

## Reading the provision

**"All persons" not citizens**: Article 25(1) extends to all persons, not just citizens. Non-citizens in India — foreign nationals, refugees, stateless persons — are equally entitled to freedom of conscience and religious practice. This is consistent with the view that religious freedom is a fundamental human right rather than a political right reserved for the national community.

**Freedom of conscience as the inner right**: The "freedom of conscience" element of Article 25(1) protects the internal dimension of religion — the right to hold a belief without being compelled to profess one's faith, to remain non-religious, or to change one's religion. The State cannot require a person to declare their religion, adhere to a state-approved interpretation of their religion, or perform acts that violate their religious conscience. This inner dimension of the right is what protected the Jehovah's Witnesses' children in Bijoe Emmanuel from compelled participation in the national anthem — a coercive state demand on religious conscience.

**The secular activity distinction in Article 25(2)(a)**: The distinction between religious activity (protected by Article 25) and secular activity associated with religion (regulable under Article 25(2)(a)) is analytically difficult and practically important. Managing the finances of a religious institution, employing workers in temple kitchens, running educational institutions attached to a mosque — all of these are secular activities that happen to be associated with religion. The State can regulate them through ordinary legislation (labour law, taxation, educational standards) without infringing Article 25. Courts have interpreted this provision broadly, allowing substantial state regulation of the organisational and managerial aspects of religious activity.

**Article 25(2)(b) and the reform power**: The throwing-open provision was drafted with the Hindu caste system's exclusion of Dalits from temples directly in mind. It gives legislative force to the social-reform agenda of the Constitution — the view that religious doctrine cannot immunise discriminatory social practices from State intervention. Explanation II's extension to Sikhs, Jains, and Buddhists reflects the Constituent Assembly's view that these faiths, while distinct from Hinduism, share enough of the social context of caste to require similar legislative protection. The provision does not extend to Muslim or Christian institutions, a disparity that has generated academic criticism.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debate on Article 25 was shaped by two competing anxieties. The first was the anxiety of religious minorities — particularly Muslims, Christians, and Sikhs — that a Hindu-majority Parliament would use legislative power to alter or suppress their religious practices. The second was the anxiety of social reformers — particularly those from the Dalit movement and the left — that religious freedom would become a shield behind which caste discrimination in Hindu religious institutions would be perpetuated.

The "propagation" clause was the most contested. Christian missions were particularly concerned that this right be preserved, given their long tradition of proselytising work in India. The eventual compromise was that "propagation" is constitutionally protected but does not include coercion or fraud. Several States have subsequently passed anti-conversion laws that prohibit conversion by allurement, inducement, or force — laws whose constitutionality under Article 25(1) has been repeatedly challenged. The Supreme Court in Rev. Stainislaus v State of Madhya Pradesh (1977) upheld such laws, holding that the right to propagate does not include the right to convert against the other person's will.

The social-reform provision in Article 25(2)(b) was championed by Dr. Ambedkar as a constitutional guarantee that the reform agenda would not be frustrated by Article 25(1) claims. Ambedkar argued that unless the Constitution explicitly authorised temple-entry legislation and similar reforms, religious institutions would use Article 25(1) to block every attempt at social reform. The provision was included over the objections of those who argued that State interference in religious institutions would violate the autonomy of religious communities.

## Key cases

**Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments, Madras v Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar of Sri Shirur Mutt (1954)** — The foundational Article 25 judgment. The Court held that the Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act, which vested management of certain religious institutions in State-appointed trustees, violated Article 26's right to manage religious affairs. In doing so, the Court developed the essential religious practices test — Article 25 protects only practices that are essential or integral to a religion. Practices that are merely associated with a religion but not integral to it are not constitutionally protected. This test has shaped every subsequent Article 25 case, for better or worse.

**Ratilal Panachand Gandhi v State of Bombay (1954)** — The Court drew the fundamental distinction between religious affairs (protected) and secular activity associated with religion (regulable). The State may regulate how a religious trust's funds are managed, how its property is administered, and how its organisational structure operates — but it cannot direct what religious practices are performed or how religious doctrine is to be interpreted. The management of a temple is partly secular (accounts, administration) and partly religious (rituals, worship) — the State can legislate for the former but not the latter.

**Rev Stainislaus v State of Madhya Pradesh (1977)** — The Supreme Court upheld anti-conversion laws passed by Madhya Pradesh and Orissa, holding that the right to propagate religion under Article 25(1) does not include the right to convert others by force, fraud, or inducement. The Court held that such laws protect the religious freedom of the person being approached for conversion — their right not to be coerced into changing religion. This judgment has been criticised for allowing legislatures to restrict propagation broadly and for giving State governments power to determine what counts as illegitimate inducement.

**Bijoe Emmanuel v State of Kerala (1986)** — The Court upheld the right of Jehovah's Witnesses children not to sing the national anthem during school assembly, as their faith forbids saluting any flag or singing anthems other than those directed at Jehovah. The Court held that not singing the anthem — remaining standing in respectful silence — did not disrespect the anthem and fell within the Article 25(1) protection of religious conscience. The case established that personal religious conviction, even in a minority faith not practised by most Indians, attracts full constitutional protection.

**Acharya Jagdishwaranand Avadhuta v Commissioner of Police Calcutta (1983 and 1984)** — The Court considered whether the Ananda Marga's practice of tandava dance (with skulls and live snakes) in public processions was an essential religious practice of the Ananda Marga. The Court held in the first round that it was not, and the High Court ban was upheld. The case became a template for how courts apply the essential-practices test — by inquiring into the religious texts and traditions of a faith to determine whether a practice is truly central to that faith, rather than accepting the community's own characterisation.

**Indian Young Lawyers Association v State of Kerala (2018) — The Sabarimala Case** — The Supreme Court, by four to one, struck down the Travancore Devaswom Board's practice of excluding women aged 10–50 from entering the Sabarimala temple, holding it violated Articles 25(1) and 17. The majority held the exclusion was not an essential religious practice of the Ayyappa devotees and that even if it were, it could not be permitted to continue because it violated the right to equality and the prohibition on untouchability. Justice Indu Malhotra dissented, arguing that the Court should not adjudicate on what is or is not an essential practice of a religion — that assessment should be left to the religious community. In 2019, a five-judge bench referred the case to a nine-judge bench, where it remains pending along with questions about the Shia-Sunni division in mosques, Parsi women's right of entry into the agiary, and FGM in the Dawoodi Bohra community.

**Shafin Jahan v Asokan K.M. (2018) — The Hadiya Case** — A young woman converted to Islam and married a Muslim man. Her father challenged the marriage and conversion, and the Kerala High Court annulled the marriage. The Supreme Court reversed the High Court, holding that the right to choose one's religion and one's life partner are core dimensions of personal liberty under Articles 19, 21, and 25. The State cannot investigate or annul religious conversion by a competent adult on the petition of a displeased parent. The case confirmed that Article 25's freedom of conscience includes the right to convert — both away from and to a religion — as a personal decision beyond State or family interference.

## Current significance

The nine-judge bench reference in Sabarimala is the most consequential pending constitutional matter in the Article 25 domain. The bench has been framed to address the fundamental question of whether the essential religious practices test — a judicially created test that requires courts to determine what is essential to a religion — should continue to be used, be replaced by a different doctrinal framework, or be abandoned entirely in favour of deference to religious communities' own understanding of their practices. The outcome will reshape how every future Article 25 case is argued and decided.

The anti-conversion law controversy continues to generate litigation. Several States have enacted or are in the process of enacting laws that require prior government permission for religious conversion, or that prohibit conversion by a defined list of inducements that critics argue is drawn so broadly as to restrict any form of proselytising. These laws present a direct conflict with the Article 25(1) right to propagate religion, and petitions challenging them are pending before the Supreme Court.

The hijab controversy in Karnataka (2022) produced a Karnataka High Court judgment holding that wearing the hijab is not an essential religious practice of Islam and that State educational institutions can require a uniform dress code without violating Article 25. This judgment was appealed to the Supreme Court, where a two-judge bench was divided and referred the matter to a larger bench. The Article 25 question — whether a religious practice that is sincerely held, even if not essential to the religion, merits constitutional protection — goes to the heart of the essential-practices test debate and connects directly to the Sabarimala nine-judge bench reference.
