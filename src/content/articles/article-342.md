## Why it matters

Article 342 is the gateway to the constitutional protections for Scheduled Tribes — a population of approximately 104 million persons belonging to more than 700 communities spread across every region of India, from the Andamanese islanders to the Bhils of Rajasthan, from the Nagas of Nagaland to the Gonds of Madhya Pradesh. Membership of the ST list triggers reservation in public employment under Articles 16(4), 16(4A), and 16(4B); reservation in educational institutions under Article 15(4); reservation in elected seats under Articles 330 and 332; the protections of the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act; the land protection provisions of the Fifth and Sixth Schedules; and the forest rights framework under the Forest Rights Act, 2006. The definition of who is on the list is therefore consequential across every domain of public life that the Constitution addresses.

The parallel with Article 341 for Scheduled Castes is structural but not substantive. SC status is rooted primarily in caste-based untouchability — a social phenomenon that is geographically widespread and relatively consistent in its identifying features. ST status is rooted in a more heterogeneous set of characteristics — geographical isolation, cultural distinctiveness, tribal governance traditions, forest-dependent livelihoods, and historical exclusion from the mainstream — that vary enormously across regions, ecosystems, and cultural settings. The Tiger-nomad communities of Rajasthan, the sea-faring communities of the Andaman Islands, the rice-cultivating communities of the northeast, and the forest-dwelling communities of central India are all "Scheduled Tribes" but have almost nothing in common except constitutional classification and historical marginalisation.

The article's practical importance is inseparable from the broader legal framework governing tribal land, forest, and governance rights. Unlike Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes have a dedicated territorial governance framework in the Fifth and Sixth Schedules of the Constitution. The question of who is on the ST list therefore determines not only who benefits from employment and education reservation, but also who is protected by Fifth Schedule land alienation prohibitions, who has rights under the Forest Rights Act, and who participates in Fifth Schedule gram sabhas with their special powers over land and natural resources.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)** establishes the Presidential notification as the definitional instrument for Scheduled Tribe status. The consultation with the Governor of the relevant State is mandatory when the notification covers a State (not a Union territory). As with the SC notification under Article 341, the consultation is a procedural requirement not a substantive veto: the President must consult the Governor but is not bound by the Governor's view. The notification specifies "tribes or tribal communities or parts of or groups within tribes or tribal communities" — a formulation that parallels Article 341 in allowing sub-tribal groups and parts of communities to be included or excluded.

**The state-specificity of the notification** is a critical structural feature. A community is a Scheduled Tribe "in relation to" a particular State or Union territory. A community that is on the ST list of Jharkhand has ST status in Jharkhand but not in Maharashtra unless it is separately notified for Maharashtra. For migrant workers and students who move from states where they have ST status to states where they do not, this creates significant disadvantage: they lose eligibility for reservation benefits in their destination state, and their children born in the destination state may grow up without any connection to the ST framework that would have protected their parents.

**Clause (2)** gives Parliament exclusive power to alter the Presidential notification by law. The same structural logic applies as under Article 341: no executive modification is possible, transparency and parliamentary accountability are mandated for any change, and the ordinary legislative process (simple majority in both Houses) suffices. There is no special majority or State ratification requirement, but the necessity of parliamentary action creates a meaningful procedural hurdle and political visibility.

## Lokur Committee criteria and tribal identity

The criteria for inclusion in the Scheduled Tribes list were articulated by the Advisory Committee on the Revision of the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes Lists (Lokur Committee, 1965). The Committee identified five characteristics that define a Scheduled Tribe: (a) indications of primitive traits; (b) distinctive culture; (c) geographical isolation; (d) shyness of contact with the community at large; and (e) backwardness. These criteria have been applied inconsistently across the administrative process, and courts have noted that the guidelines are not themselves constitutionally specified — they are administrative standards that inform the Presidential notification process rather than legally enforceable membership criteria.

The absence of a clear, consistently applied definitional framework has contributed to the large backlog of pending inclusion claims: communities that believe they meet the Lokur criteria have petitioned State and Central governments, but the process of investigation, recommendation, and parliamentary amendment is slow. The tribal identity tests also interact with the sub-classification question raised by Davinder Singh (2024): if the characteristics that define tribal identity are as diverse as the Lokur Committee criteria suggest, it follows that communities on the ST list differ enormously in the extent to which they currently experience marginalisation, making the case for differentiated treatment within the ST category analytically coherent even if politically contested.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's discussions of Article 342 closely tracked those on Article 341. The same design choices — Presidential notification, consultation with Governors, exclusive parliamentary amendment power — were applied to Scheduled Tribes. The substantive question debated was whether tribal communities should be identified primarily by anthropological and geographical criteria or by social and economic backwardness, with the latter approach potentially capturing some communities outside the traditional "primitive tribe" category. The Constituent Assembly effectively deferred this question to the post-enactment process of Presidential notification and parliamentary modification, embedding the two-stage structure rather than specifying criteria.

Members from the northeast pressed for recognition of the special character of tribal governance in the hill areas, which resulted in the Sixth Schedule framework (schedule-vi) operating alongside the ST list. The Sixth Schedule creates autonomous district councils with legislative powers, a governance structure that goes well beyond the reservation-focused framework of Part XVI and that requires ST status as a prerequisite for the community-level protections it provides.

## Key cases

**Bal Patil v Union of India (2005)** — The Supreme Court considered the Jain community's claim to ST status, holding that the criteria for inclusion under Article 342 are primarily related to geographical isolation, cultural distinctiveness, and backwardness associated with primitive conditions of life. The Court held that religious community membership alone does not qualify a group for inclusion, and courts cannot direct the executive or Parliament to add a community to the ST list — these are matters of legislative discretion constrained by the Lokur Committee criteria.

**Kumari Madhuri Patil v Additional Commissioner, Tribal Development (1994)** — The Court laid down detailed guidelines for the verification of ST caste certificates, confirming that the integrity of the Presidential list under Article 342 requires robust State machinery to prevent false claims. The judgment specified a multi-level scrutiny process involving vigilance cells, district-level committees, and scrutiny committees, and required that certificates be verified against the actual communities listed in the relevant State notification.

**State of Maharashtra v Milind (2001)** — A Constitution Bench held that the Presidential ST notification under Article 342 is exhaustive and that a community not notified for a particular State cannot claim ST status in that State merely because it appears in the ST list of another State. The territorial specificity of each notification is fundamental to the article's design, and no amount of social or cultural similarity to a notified ST community can substitute for formal Presidential notification covering the specific State.

**Samatha v State of Andhra Pradesh (1997)** — The Supreme Court held that land transfers in Scheduled Areas to non-tribals and private corporations are unconstitutional, drawing on the Fifth Schedule's protective framework and the constitutional commitment to tribal welfare expressed across Articles 338A, 339, 342, and the Fifth Schedule. The ST list under Article 342 determines which communities benefit from the Fifth Schedule's protective land provisions, making the definitional question directly relevant to land rights litigation.

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024)** — The seven-judge Constitution Bench's ruling on SC sub-classification was explicitly extended to Scheduled Tribes. States now have constitutional power to sub-classify the ST list for the purpose of directing preferential allocation within the reserved ST quota to the most backward tribal sub-groups, subject to the same empirical justification requirement applicable to SCs. The ruling requires States to demonstrate, based on adequate data, that the targeted sub-group is relatively more backward within the overall ST category and that the sub-classification is reasonably designed to address that differential backwardness.

**National Commission for Scheduled Tribes — Annual Reports on ST list pending claims** — The NCST's reports have documented the large backlog of inclusion claims, the inconsistency of the investigation process, and the parliamentary time required for amendment Bills. These reports provide an institutional record of the practical functioning — and limitations — of the Article 342 mechanism.

## Forest rights linkage

The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006 (Forest Rights Act) links the ST list directly to forest rights in two ways. First, "Scheduled Tribes" as defined by the Presidential notification under Article 342 are one of the two beneficiary categories for individual forest rights (rights to cultivate, reside in, and access forest areas that tribal families have traditionally used). Second, community forest rights — rights over entire forest areas for the benefit of a community — are accessible to ST communities. The territorial specificity of Article 342 therefore determines which communities can claim Forest Rights Act benefits in which States. Communities that are on the ST list of one State but not of a neighbouring State cannot benefit from Forest Rights Act provisions on the forest land they may have migrated to, even if they have been dependent on those forests for generations.

## Current significance

The Davinder Singh (2024) ruling's explicit extension to STs is particularly significant given the enormous internal diversity within the tribal population. Forest-dwelling communities in Jharkhand and Chhattisgarh, including Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups such as the Pahadiyas and Birhors, have long argued that numerically dominant or more educationally advanced tribal communities corner most ST reservation benefits in government employment and higher education. Sub-classification legislation targeting genuinely primitive tribal groups is now constitutionally permissible, and States with Fifth Schedule areas are expected to be active in enacting such legislation and defending it before the High Courts.

The ST list inclusion backlog remains a significant constitutional and human rights concern. Communities whose claims have been pending for decades cannot benefit from any constitutional reservation or Fifth Schedule protection during the delay. The NCST has recommended streamlining the investigation and parliamentary amendment process, including creating a standing parliamentary committee specifically for ST and SC list modification Bills to reduce the legislative processing time.

The question of tribal identity and conversion — analogous to the Dalit-Christian and Dalit-Muslim issue for SCs — also arises for STs, though in a different form. Tribal Christians in northeastern States continue to be recognised as STs regardless of religious conversion, because the Scheduled Castes Order 1950 religion restriction applies only to SCs, not to STs. The Presidential notifications for ST communities do not include any religion-based exclusion, meaning Christian Nagas, Christian Mizos, and Christian Khasis all retain full ST status. This constitutional difference between SC and ST treatment on the religion question is an anomaly that has been noted in litigation but not yet resolved by a definitive Supreme Court ruling on the relevant distinction.
