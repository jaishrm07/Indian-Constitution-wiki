  197. Restriction on powers of Legislative Council as to Bills other than Money Bills.—(1) If after a Bill has been passed by the Legislative Assembly of a State having a Legislative Council and transmitted to the Legislative Council—

  (a) the Bill is rejected by the Council; or

  (b) more than three months elapse from the date on which the Bill is laid before the Council without the Bill being passed by it; or

  (c) the Bill is passed by the Council with amendments to which the Legislative Assembly does not agree; the Legislative Assembly may, subject to the rules regulating its procedure, pass the Bill again in the same or in any subsequent session with or without such amendments, if any, as have been made, suggested or agreed to by the Legislative Council and then transmit the Bill as so passed to the Legislative Council.

  (2) If after a Bill has been so passed for the second time by the Legislative Assembly and transmitted to the Legislative Council—

  (a) the Bill is rejected by the Council; or

  (b) more than one month elapses from the date on which the Bill is laid before the Council without the Bill being passed by it; or

  (c) the Bill is passed by the Council with amendments to which the Legislative Assembly does not agree; the Bill shall be deemed to have been passed by the Houses of the Legislature of the State in the form in which it was passed by the Legislative Assembly for the second time with such amendments, if any, as have been made or suggested by the Legislative Council and agreed to by the Legislative Assembly.

  (3) Nothing in this article shall apply to a Money Bill.

## What this Article does

Article 197 is the state legislature's deadlock-resolution mechanism for ordinary (non-Money) Bills, serving a function analogous to Article 108's joint-sitting provision at the Union level — but without any joint sitting. Where the Union Parliament resolves deadlock by convening both Houses together, Article 197 resolves it by making the Legislative Assembly's will conclusive after a two-pass process.

The sequence works as follows. Once the Assembly passes a Bill and sends it to the Council, the Council has three months to act. If the Council rejects the Bill, lets three months lapse without acting, or passes it with amendments the Assembly refuses to accept, the Assembly may re-pass the Bill (clause 1). After this second passage and transmission, the Council has only one month to respond. If the Council again rejects, delays beyond one month, or insists on amendments the Assembly does not accept, the Bill is deemed passed in the form the Assembly last adopted it, incorporating only those Council amendments the Assembly was willing to accept (clause 2). There is no third stage and no further Council veto.

The asymmetry is deliberate. Legislative Councils in Indian States are not directly elected in the same way as Assemblies — their composition includes elected members, nominated members, and members elected by local bodies, graduates, and teachers — and the constitutional framers intended the directly elected Assembly to be the dominant legislative chamber. Article 197 operationalises that dominance by capping the Council's delaying power: a maximum delay of roughly four months (three under clause 1 plus one under clause 2) before the Assembly's second-passed text becomes law.

Clause (3) explicitly excludes Money Bills from this Article, since Money Bills are handled by the separate and even more Assembly-dominant procedure in Article 198.

## Key cases

**State of Karnataka v. State of Andhra Pradesh (various)** — Challenges to state legislation passed under the Article 197 deemed-passage route have occasionally raised procedural questions about whether the three-month and one-month periods were correctly computed, and whether the Assembly's second passage sufficiently complied with its own procedural rules. Courts have generally held that the constitutional time periods are mandatory conditions, but that minor procedural variations within the House's internal rules are protected from judicial scrutiny by Article 212 (the state-level equivalent of Article 122).

**Purushothaman Nambudiri v. State of Kerala (1962)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the deemed-passage provisions in the state bicameral architecture are genuine constitutional alternatives to bicameral agreement — not devices to circumvent the requirement of both Houses acting — and that a Bill deemed passed under the prescribed procedure is as validly enacted as one passed by express agreement of both Houses.

**Bihar Legislative Council abolition litigation (2000s)** — When the Bihar government sought to abolish the Legislative Council through an Assembly resolution, litigation touched on the relationship between the Council's constitutional role under Articles 197–198 and the State's power to abolish it under Article 169. The cases underscored that the Council's delaying and recommending functions under Article 197, while constitutionally protected during its existence, are subject to the State's power to eliminate the second chamber entirely.

## Current significance

Article 197 has been invoked most actively in States where the ruling Assembly majority faces a hostile Council. In Maharashtra and Bihar in particular, governments have passed contentious Bills — including farm laws, reorganisation measures, and social welfare legislation — through the Assembly, watched the Council delay or amend them, and then re-passed the Bills to trigger the deemed-passage route. Critics argue this process reduces the Council to an advisory body capable only of suggesting amendments the Assembly can freely ignore, which in their view defeats the purpose of a bicameral state legislature.

The three-month delay window under clause (1) has also been used strategically in reverse: governments that wish to avoid legislative controversy before elections have occasionally introduced Bills in the Assembly, transmitted them to the Council, and allowed the three-month clock to expire without pushing for a second Assembly passage — effectively using the Council's inaction as a procedural holding mechanism rather than pushing through deemed passage.
