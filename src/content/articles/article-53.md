  53. Executive power of the Union.—(1) The executive power of the Union shall be vested in the President and shall be exercised by him either directly or through officers subordinate to him in accordance with this Constitution.

  (2) Without prejudice to the generality of the foregoing provision, the supreme command of the Defence Forces of the Union shall be vested in the President and the exercise thereof shall be regulated by law.

  (3) Nothing in this article shall—

  (a) be deemed to transfer to the President any functions conferred by any existing law on the Government of any State or other authority; or

  (b) prevent Parliament from conferring by law functions on authorities other than the President.

## Why it matters

Article 53 is the constitutional engine of Union executive power. Without it, the declaration in Article 52 that "there shall be a President" would create an office without authority. Article 53(1) supplies that authority by vesting the Union's entire executive power in the President. The article then performs three additional structural tasks: it separates formal vesting from practical exercise (the President may act "directly or through officers subordinate to him"), it gives the President supreme command of the armed forces while making that command subject to legislative regulation, and it contains a savings clause that prevents the sweeping vesting of power from cannibalising State executive authority or foreclosing Parliament's ability to distribute executive functions by statute.

The relationship between formal vesting and practical exercise is the article's most consequential contribution to Indian constitutional law. It creates a deliberate gap between the nominal repository of executive power (the President) and its real wielder (the Cabinet). This gap is filled by Article 74, which requires the President to act on Cabinet advice. Together, Articles 53 and 74 create the architecture of India's parliamentary executive: power formally vested in an elected constitutional head, but substantively exercised by a Cabinet commanding parliamentary confidence.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) uses the word "vested" — a term of constitutional art. Vesting means that executive power belongs to the President as a matter of constitutional entitlement, not merely delegation. The President does not receive power from Parliament or the Cabinet; the Constitution grants it directly. This matters when Parliament attempts to extinguish or substantially diminish Presidential authority — the vesting clause acts as a constitutional floor.

"In accordance with this Constitution" is the important qualifier in clause (1). The President exercises executive power not at large, but constrained by every other relevant constitutional provision — including Article 74's requirement of Cabinet advice, Article 355's duty of the Union to protect States, and the fundamental rights in Part III. Executive action that violates any of these constraints is not merely politically objectionable but constitutionally void.

Clause (2)'s vesting of supreme command in the President is a significant constitutional statement about civilian control of the military. The phrase "regulated by law" means Parliament has authority to define the structure of military command and the conditions under which forces may be deployed — but the ultimate command authority remains in the constitutional head of state, not in any officer of the armed forces.

Clause (3) is a double savings clause. Sub-clause (a) ensures that the Union does not absorb State executive functions merely by virtue of the general vesting in Article 53(1). Sub-clause (b) preserves Parliament's legislative freedom to create executive bodies — such as regulators, commissions, and tribunals — whose authority comes from statute rather than Presidential delegation. Without sub-clause (b), a rigid reading of the vesting clause might have suggested that Parliament could not confer executive-type powers on non-Presidential authorities.

## Key cases

**Rai Sahib Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab (1955)** — The first major judicial exposition of Article 53. The Supreme Court held that executive power under Article 53 is co-extensive with the Union's legislative competence: the executive can act in all areas where Parliament can legislate, even without a specific statute authorising the particular executive act, provided the act does not trench on existing law. This "co-extensiveness doctrine" remains foundational to understanding Union executive power.

**Union of India v. Sankalchand Himatlal Sheth (1977)** — The Court confirmed that the Union's executive power under Article 53 extends across the Union List without requiring a statute for each individual act. The case also touched on the interplay between Articles 53 and 74, affirming that acts done formally in the President's name are the Cabinet's acts in substance.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)** — The Constitution Bench held that Article 53 vests executive power in the President but that the President is bound by Cabinet advice under Article 74 in all but a narrow set of constitutionally specified situations. Clause (3)(b) was invoked to confirm that Parliament's routine practice of conferring functions on subordinate authorities — departments, statutory commissions, tribunals — is constitutionally regular.

**In re: Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal (1992)** — The Supreme Court examined the boundaries of Union executive power in an inter-State water dispute, distinguishing the President's Article 53 power from State executive powers and affirming that clause (3)(a) preserves State executive autonomy from being absorbed into the Union executive sphere.

## Current significance

The co-extensiveness doctrine from Ram Jawaya Kapur has become the foundation for a large body of Union executive action that bypasses Parliament: executive policies, administrative schemes, and executive orders are routinely issued without explicit statutory backing on the theory that Union executive power is co-extensive with legislative competence. Critics argue that this effectively allows the Cabinet to legislate by executive order in Union List subjects without parliamentary scrutiny. The tension between executive efficiency and parliamentary accountability is built into the architecture of Article 53 itself.

The military dimension of clause (2) has acquired new salience with proposals for theatre commands that would create integrated joint commands under single commanders. The constitutional question is whether Parliament must legislate to authorise this structural reorganisation of military command, or whether the executive can reshape military organisation under the President's Article 53(2) supreme command authority without fresh legislation. This question remains unresolved and has significant implications for civil-military relations and parliamentary oversight of defence policy.
