## What this Article originally did

Article 257A was inserted by the Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) Act, 1976 — the Emergency-era amendment that made sweeping changes to the constitutional distribution of power between the Union and the States. In its original form, the article authorised the Union government to deploy armed forces or other forces of the Union in any State to assist the civil administration. It placed the cost of such deployment on the State government, gave Union forces deployed in a State full protection from prosecution in State courts for acts done in the discharge of their duties, and made the Union's determination of when such assistance was needed largely unreviewable.

The provision was part of a cluster of 42nd Amendment changes — including the insertion of Article 31D, the expansion of Parliament's powers under Article 31C, and changes to Article 368 — that substantially altered the federal balance in favour of the Union. By allowing Central force deployment in States without the need to invoke Article 356 (President's Rule) or the emergency provisions of Part XVIII, Article 257A created a new instrument of Union intervention in State law and order that bypassed the constitutional safeguards attached to the emergency framework.

## The 42nd and 44th Amendments

The Forty-second Amendment Act, 1976 was enacted by Parliament under Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's government during the Internal Emergency declared under Article 352. It is widely regarded as the most comprehensive single alteration to the Indian Constitution, touching 59 articles and inserting new provisions across multiple Parts. Many of its provisions significantly strengthened Union power at the expense of the States and the judiciary.

The Forty-fourth Amendment Act, 1978, enacted by the Janata Party government that came to power after the Emergency's end, sought to restore the constitutional balance. It reversed or modified many of the 42nd Amendment's most controversial changes. Article 257A was one of the provisions omitted by the 44th Amendment, effective from 20 June 1979. Its omission reflected the post-Emergency consensus that the Union's ability to deploy forces in States without emergency proclamation was incompatible with federal principles and the rule of law.

## The underlying constitutional question

The omission of Article 257A left the constitutional position on Union force deployment in States where it had been before 1976: governed primarily by Article 257(2) (Union's power to give directions to States for maintenance of communication arteries) and Article 355 (Union's duty to protect States against internal disturbance). Under the pre-257A framework, the Union's ability to deploy paramilitary forces in States requires either the State government's request, a Presidential proclamation under Article 356, or the implicit authority under Article 355.

This framework — and its adequacy when a State government is unwilling to accept Union assistance or when law and order has broken down in ways short of full Emergency — has remained a source of constitutional controversy. The deployment of central paramilitary forces in Manipur, Jammu and Kashmir, and during electoral violence episodes has repeatedly raised questions about the constitutional basis for Union force deployment without explicit State consent, questions that Article 257A would have answered but its omission leaves to be worked out through Articles 355, 257, and executive arrangements.

## Key cases

**Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v. Union of India (1998) 2 SCC 109** — The Supreme Court upheld the constitutionality of the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act, 1958 applied in the North-East, addressing the Union's power to deploy armed forces in disturbed areas under the authority derived from Article 355 and the Concurrent List entry on preventive detention and maintenance of public order. The case demonstrates the constitutional landscape that Article 257A briefly sought to explicitly regulate and that Article 355 continues to govern after the 44th Amendment's repeal.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court's landmark federalism judgment clarified the conditions under which the Union can intervene in State governance. The Court's insistence on judicial review of Presidential proclamations under Article 356 and its emphasis on cooperative federalism inform the understanding of why an unchecked Article 257A power — deployment without proclamation or review — was seen as constitutionally incompatible with the federal structure.

## Current significance

Article 257A is spent. Its brief presence and rapid omission make it a case study in constitutional excess during the Emergency period and the subsequent restoration of constitutional safeguards. The article is cited in scholarship on the 42nd and 44th Amendments, on the history of Indian emergency constitutionalism, and on the federal dimension of internal security — particularly in debates about Central Paramilitary Forces deployment in States where the State government has not requested assistance or where the State-Centre relationship has broken down.
