## What this Article does

Article 131 vests the Supreme Court with exclusive original jurisdiction — meaning no other court can entertain these suits — over legal disputes between the Government of India and one or more States, or between two or more States inter se. The jurisdiction is confined to disputes that involve the existence or extent of a legal right; political or administrative grievances that do not crystallise into a legal right are excluded. A proviso preserves the position of pre-Constitution treaties and instruments that expressly exclude this jurisdiction.

## Why it matters

Article 131 is the Constitution's primary federal dispute resolution mechanism for inter-governmental legal conflicts. India's federal design distributes legislative, administrative and financial powers between the Union and the States, but disputes about the boundaries of those powers need a neutral forum. Ordinary civil courts were not designed for suits where a sovereign government is a party and where the questions litigated concern constitutional allocation of powers. Article 131 solves this by giving the Supreme Court — the apex court that stands above both Union and State judiciaries — exclusive and mandatory original jurisdiction. "Exclusive" means no other court, including any High Court, can entertain the same dispute. "Mandatory" means the Supreme Court cannot decline to hear a dispute that genuinely falls within the Article's scope. The provision thus fills a structural gap that would otherwise leave federal disputes without a constitutional adjudicator.

## How courts use it (the "legal right" requirement test)

The pivotal gateway condition is that "the existence or extent of a legal right" must be at issue. Courts have consistently held that this phrase excludes purely political or administrative grievances — a State's unhappiness with a Central policy, or a disagreement over resource allocation that has not crystallised into a legal entitlement, is not enough.

**The "legal right" test in practice.** The plaintiff government must identify a specific legal right — rooted in the Constitution, a statute, or a legally binding instrument — that it claims the defendant has infringed. Vague invocations of federal comity or cooperative governance do not suffice. If the dispute is political at its core (for example, a State objecting to the Centre's choice of policies that fall squarely within Union power), the Court will decline jurisdiction under Article 131.

**Article 131 versus Article 32 / Article 226.** Article 32 gives individuals and organisations the right to move the Supreme Court for fundamental rights violations; Article 226 is the High Court's writ jurisdiction. States cannot use Article 131 as a substitute for Article 32 — constitutional validity of Central legislation is not, by itself, a legal right of the State that Article 131 protects, though this boundary has been contested in several suits. Where a State acts as parens patriae arguing on behalf of its residents' fundamental rights, the Court examines carefully whether the State itself has a cognisable legal right or is merely piggybacking a challenge more properly filed under Article 32.

**Article 131 versus Article 136 / SLP route.** In routine litigation, a State aggrieved by a court decision appeals through the Special Leave Petition route under Article 136. Article 131 is for original first-instance suits between governments, not for inter-governmental disputes that have already been adjudicated at some lower level.

**Treaty and agreement bar.** The Proviso excludes disputes arising from pre-Constitution treaties and agreements that exclude this jurisdiction, reflecting the carve-out under Article 363 for pre-constitutional princely-state arrangements.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — Seven States challenged the Union's power to dissolve their Assemblies under Article 356 by filing original suits under Article 131. A nine-judge Constitution Bench held that the dispute was essentially political: the dissolution of an Assembly is an exercise of constitutional discretion by the executive and does not infringe any legal right of the State government as such. The Court ruled Article 131 jurisdiction was not available for such a challenge. This remains the leading authority for the proposition that Article 131 is not a vehicle for challenging political or discretionary executive acts of the Union that do not amount to an infringement of a definable legal right of the plaintiff State.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (1978)** — Karnataka challenged a commission of inquiry established by the Union as beyond the Union's legislative competence and as violating State autonomy. A seven-judge Bench examined the Article 131 scope in detail, with several judges writing separately. The Court accepted that a State could invoke Article 131 to defend its constitutional domain from legislative or executive overreach. It clarified that the "legal right" whose existence or extent is in question need not be a common-law right — constitutional rights and powers vested in States by the constitutional distribution of powers qualify. This case significantly broadened the possible range of disputes cognisable under Article 131.

**State of Haryana v. State of Punjab (2004)** — Haryana sued Punjab under Article 131 for Punjab's failure to construct and deliver agreed river water flows under a Satluj-Yamuna Link canal award. The Court held the suit maintainable because the allocation of an agreed share of inter-state river water to Haryana constituted a legal right capable of enforcement. The case is significant for two reasons: it confirmed that river water sharing arrangements grounded in binding legal instruments create enforceable legal rights within Article 131, and it demonstrated that Article 131 suits can lead to mandatory directions against State governments.

**State of Himachal Pradesh v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2011)** — The Court revisited the scope of Article 131 and confirmed that original suits between States over statutory and constitutional rights arising from territorial arrangements or resource disputes are fully justiciable. The judgment also reiterated that the requirement of a legal right is a threshold test to be applied at the admissions stage, so the Court must form a prima facie view on whether the plaintiff has identified a cognisable legal right before the matter proceeds to full hearing.

## Current significance

**Cauvery water dispute.** The Cauvery river water dispute between Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Kerala and Puducherry spawned both tribunal proceedings and Supreme Court original jurisdiction references over decades. The eventual Supreme Court judgment in 2018 (following a Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal award) redrew allocation figures and directed the Central Government to frame a scheme — demonstrating that Article 131-adjacent original jurisdiction proceedings can produce binding operational orders affecting water infrastructure and agricultural economies of multiple States.

**Inter-state border disputes.** Border tensions between States — most notably Assam and Mizoram (which erupted into violence in 2021) and Assam and Meghalaya — are candidates for Article 131 suits if they crystallise into competing claims of legal right over territory. At present these disputes are handled largely through political and executive channels; Article 131 remains available if a State formalises its territorial claim as a legal right derived from boundary instruments or constitutional provisions.

**GST Council and fiscal federalism.** The Goods and Services Tax Council allocates tax revenues and sets rates through decisions that bind both Union and States. Legal scholars and State finance ministers have raised the question of whether a State that disputes a GST Council decision it considers a violation of its constitutionally guaranteed fiscal autonomy could file an Article 131 suit. The Supreme Court's observation in Union of India v. Mohit Minerals (2022) — that GST Council recommendations are not binding but carry persuasive weight — opens a possible pathway where a State could argue a legal right to a share of tax revenue is being infringed. No such suit has yet reached final adjudication on this theory.

**Challenges to Central legislation.** In 2020 and 2021 several States (including Rajasthan, Punjab and Chhattisgarh) filed original suits under Article 131 challenging the farm reform laws enacted by Parliament, arguing the laws encroached on State List Entry 14 (agriculture). The farm laws were repealed in November 2021 before the suits were decided, but the episode illustrated that Article 131 has become an active constitutional battleground for federalism disputes rather than a dormant provision.
