## Why it matters

Financial independence is a necessary condition for judicial independence. A
judge who fears that an unpopular decision might lead to a reduction in salary,
withdrawal of a house, or loss of pension is not fully independent, regardless
of what the rest of the Constitution says. Article 221 addresses this by making
Parliamentary legislation — not executive discretion — the mechanism for
determining judge salaries, and by constitutionally prohibiting any
post-appointment reduction in allowances or pension entitlements.

The charged-expenditure mechanism means salaries are drawn from the Consolidated
Fund without requiring annual parliamentary approval. Unlike an ordinary budget
line that must be voted on each year, a charged expenditure cannot be blocked
or reduced by a parliamentary majority in a given year — it is automatically
payable. This design mirrors Article 125 for Supreme Court judges and places
judicial salaries in the same protected category as the salary of the President
and the repayment of public debt.

## Reading the provision

Article 221(1) provides that judges of each High Court shall be paid such
salaries as Parliament determines by law. Until Parliament acts, the Second
Schedule specifies the amounts. In practice, Parliament has acted through the
High Court Judges (Salaries and Conditions of Service) Act, 1954, which is
periodically amended to implement Pay Commission recommendations. The Second
Schedule retains its function only as a constitutional backstop in the event
that the Act is somehow repealed without replacement.

Article 221(2) guarantees allowances and rights in respect of leave and pension
as determined by Parliament. The crucial protection is in the proviso: neither
allowances nor pension rights may be varied to a judge's disadvantage after
appointment. This is an absolute constitutional bar. Parliament retains the
power to revise salary upwards or to restructure the scheme for judges appointed
in the future; what it cannot do is reduce the entitlements of a sitting judge.

The proviso operates prospectively with respect to each judge individually. If
Parliament reduces a judge's allowance, the reduction cannot apply to judges
already on the bench at the time of the amendment. Future appointees are bound
by whatever Parliament specifies at the time of their appointment, but those
appointment-time entitlements are then themselves protected against subsequent
reduction by the proviso.

## Key cases

**All India Judges Association v Union of India (2002)** — The Supreme Court
directed enhancement of service conditions for judges at the district and
subordinate levels and linked improvements to those at the High Court level,
treating adequate remuneration as constitutionally connected to the effective
delivery of justice and the ability to attract qualified lawyers to the bench.
The judgment invoked the principle that underfunded courts cannot deliver
constitutional justice.

**State of Bihar v Bal Mukund Sah (2000)** — The Court reaffirmed that the
non-reduction clause in Article 221(2) is an absolute constitutional protection.
Once a judge is appointed, any change to allowances or pension rights that
places the judge in a worse position than at the time of appointment is
constitutionally impermissible, regardless of Parliament's general power to
revise service conditions for public servants.

**P. Ramachandra Rao v State of Karnataka (2002)** — In the context of delays
in criminal trials, the Court noted that underfunding of judicial infrastructure
— including inadequate compensation for judges — is a systemic cause of the
denial of justice. The judgment treated Articles 221 and 125 together as forming
the constitutional framework that Parliament must honour to maintain a
functioning judiciary capable of vindicating constitutional rights.

## Current significance

Article 221 operates through the High Court Judges (Salaries and Conditions of
Service) Act, 1954, as periodically amended. Pay Commission recommendations for
the higher judiciary are implemented through amendments to this Act rather than
through direct executive action, preserving the parliamentary character of
salary determination. The Act governs not just salary but also housing, medical
facilities, travel entitlements, and post-retirement pension — all within the
proviso's non-reduction protection.

Debates about judicial salaries have intensified as the compensation gap between
senior advocates at the Bar and newly appointed High Court judges has widened
substantially. A successful advocate in a major city may earn in a week what a
newly appointed High Court judge earns in a year. The Law Commission and various
judicial reform bodies have pointed to this gap as a structural deterrent to
attracting the highest-calibre candidates to the bench.

The non-reduction proviso has been invoked in challenges to changes in pension
schemes for judges affected by broader public service reforms. Courts have
consistently upheld the proviso against such challenges, reinforcing that the
constitutional protection of judicial emoluments is not merely a privilege of
office but an element of the structural independence of the judiciary that
neither the executive nor the legislature can erode once a judge has assumed
office.
