  324. Superintendence, direction and control of elections to be vested in an Election Commission.—(1) The superintendence, direction and control of the preparation of the electoral rolls for, and the conduct of, all elections to Parliament and to the Legislature of every State and of elections to the offices of President and Vice-President held under this Constitution shall be vested in a Commission (referred to in this Constitution as the Election Commission).

## Why it matters

Democratic government depends on elections, and elections depend on administration that is trusted to be independent and impartial. In the Indian constitutional design, the Election Commission of India is the institution given constitutional responsibility for that administration. Article 324 is its constitutional charter — the provision that creates the Commission, defines its powers, and guarantees the independence without which its power would be politically captive.

The framing of the power in Article 324(1) was deliberate. "Superintendence, direction and control" is a more comprehensive formulation than "supervision" or "administration." It means the Commission has the power not merely to implement the rules Parliament has made but also to act in situations not covered by legislation — to fill gaps in the statutory scheme on its own initiative when the requirements of free and fair elections demand it. This gap-filling power, confirmed by the Supreme Court in Mohinder Singh Gill, is the constitutional source of the Model Code of Conduct, the Commission's power to take decisions on disputed symbols, its authority to transfer officials, and numerous other executive actions that have no specific parliamentary mandate.

The Commission's independence from the executive is structurally guaranteed by two provisions. First, the Chief Election Commissioner can only be removed by the same process as a Supreme Court judge — by a parliamentary address in each House on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity. No Chief Election Commissioner has ever been removed in the seven-plus decades since the Constitution came into force. Second, the conditions of service of the Chief Election Commissioner cannot be varied to their disadvantage after appointment. These provisions insulate the Commission's head from financial coercion or threat of removal — the same institutional design used for judges.

The gap in the provision — that other Election Commissioners (besides the Chief Election Commissioner) have lighter removal protection — created a constitutional vulnerability that was exploited in the 1990s when the government attempted to remove Commissioner T N Seshan through a constitutional manoeuvre. The Supreme Court in T N Seshan v Union of India (1995) confirmed that the multi-member Commission functions collegially and that the Chief Election Commissioner does not have unilateral power to override other Commissioners — a ruling that both constrained Seshan's independent activism and protected the multi-member structure from becoming a tool to dilute the Chief's independence.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the jurisdiction**: The Commission's jurisdiction covers elections to Parliament (both Houses), elections to State Legislatures (both houses where they exist), and elections to the offices of President and Vice-President. The preparation of electoral rolls is specifically included — meaning the Commission's control begins before the election campaign and extends to the foundational question of who is entitled to vote. Voter registration, delimitation of constituencies (though not constituency delimitation itself — that is a parliamentary function under Articles 82 and 327), and the conduct of polling are all within the Commission's jurisdiction.

**Clause (2) — composition and appointment**: The Constitution leaves the number of Election Commissioners to presidential discretion and leaves the appointment of all Commissioners (Chief and others) to the President. "Subject to the provisions of any law made in that behalf by Parliament" is the crucial qualifying phrase — it means Parliament can and should legislate to govern the appointment process. For decades, no such legislation was made, and appointments were made by presidential order on Cabinet recommendation. The absence of legislation meant appointments were effectively controlled by the executive — a structural weakness that the Anoop Baranwal judgment addressed by holding that constitutional courts can fill legislative gaps with constitutional directions in the interim.

**Clause (5) — the asymmetric removal protection**: The Chief Election Commissioner's removal protection (Article 218-equivalent, via the proviso to clause 5) is constitutionally entrenched and cannot be altered by ordinary legislation. Other Election Commissioners and Regional Commissioners can be removed on the Chief Election Commissioner's recommendation — a structure designed to prevent the executive from pressuring subordinate Commissioners without the Chief's knowledge, but one that also creates a potential vulnerability if the Chief is politically compliant. The Law Commission's 255th Report recommended constitutional amendment to bring all Election Commissioners within the same removal protection as the Chief.

**Clause (6) — staff on demand**: The Election Commission has no permanent field cadre of its own. It relies on deputed officers — returning officers, polling officials, central police forces, State police, and other government personnel — made available by State governments and the Centre on the Commission's request. This structural dependence on the executive for implementation has been a persistent tension: the Commission has the power but lacks the machinery, and the machinery's cooperation is indispensable. The Commission's ability to transfer and deploy officials during elections flows partly from Article 324's general power and partly from the Representation of the People Acts.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly considered two models for election administration: a parliamentary committee model (elections supervised by Parliament itself) and an independent commission model. Jawaharlal Nehru, B.R. Ambedkar, and most members strongly supported the independent commission model, arguing that elections administered by Parliament — where the ruling party would have significant influence — would lack credibility. T.T. Krishnamachari moved the final version of Article 289 (as it was then numbered), which became Article 324.

The debate focused significantly on whether the Commission should be a single-member or multi-member body. The original draft provided for a single Chief Election Commissioner. Dr. Ambedkar argued that a single-member Commission was more efficient and that the removal protection provided sufficient independence. The multi-member structure was adopted later, after the 1993 constitutional amendment, leading to the T N Seshan controversy. Dr. Ambedkar also emphasised that the Commission's constitutional status — unlike an ordinary statutory body — was essential to ensure that Parliament itself could not strip the Commission of its independence through ordinary legislation.

## Key cases

**Mohinder Singh Gill v Chief Election Commissioner (1978)** — A Constitution Bench held that Article 324(1) is a plenary grant of power to the Election Commission that is not dependent on, and cannot be limited to, what Parliament has specifically enacted. The Commission can act under Article 324 directly, filling gaps in legislation by its own directions, as long as those directions are consistent with the Constitution and the Act's framework. The case arose from the cancellation of a Haryana Assembly constituency poll, and the Court confirmed that the Commission's constitutional powers are co-extensive with the electoral process — not merely incidental to what Parliament has authorised.

**T N Seshan v Union of India (1995)** — A Constitution Bench settled the question of how a multi-member Election Commission functions. The government had created two additional Election Commissioners specifically to dilute Seshan's independent exercise of power. The Court confirmed that the Commission functions as a collegial body when multi-member, with the Chief acting as Chairman. The majority principle governs — the Chief cannot act alone over the objection of the other Commissioners. The case confirmed that the multi-member structure is constitutionally valid and does not undermine the Commission's independence, as long as all Commissioners have the same procedural standing in Commission decisions.

**S S Dhanoa v Union of India (1991)** — The Supreme Court held that the power of the Election Commission under Article 324 extends beyond conducting elections in the narrow sense. The Commission's "superintendence, direction and control" includes the power to issue directions on election schedules, to postpone elections when necessary to ensure free and fair polling, and to take action during elections that falls outside the specific statutory powers. The case confirmed the gap-filling rationale — Article 324 is not a rubber-stamp provision but a substantive constitutional grant.

**Anoop Baranwal v Union of India (2023)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that the process of appointing Election Commissioners through Cabinet recommendation to the President — without any independent mechanism — was constitutionally inadequate to guarantee the institutional independence that Article 324 requires. The Court held that until Parliament enacts a law governing appointments, they shall be made on the advice of a three-member committee: the Prime Minister, the Leader of the Opposition in the Lok Sabha (or the leader of the largest opposition group), and the Chief Justice of India. The judgment was significant for its reasoning — that constitutional institutions derive their legitimacy from independence, not just from their constitutional creation, and that executive control over appointments undermines independence structurally even if no individual appointment is corrupt.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India (2024)** — The Supreme Court unanimously struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme, holding that anonymous electoral funding violated voters' right to information under Article 19(1)(a) and that the scheme's design — allowing shell companies, companies with government contracts, and loss-making entities to make secret donations — could not be justified. While the primary holding was on free speech and transparency, the judgment has significant implications for Article 324's institutional framework: the Commission must now receive and publish donation information that reveals the nexus between political funding and electoral outcomes.

## Current significance

The constitutionality of the Election Commissioners (Conditions of Service) Act 2023 is before the Supreme Court. The Act governs appointments and conditions of service for Election Commissioners, filling the legislative gap that the Anoop Baranwal judgment said needed to be filled. But it fills the gap by creating an appointment committee from which the Chief Justice of India has been removed and replaced by a Cabinet Minister — a committee on which the government has an effective majority of two to one over the Opposition. Challengers argue this legislative choice is contrary to the constitutional principle of independence affirmed in Anoop Baranwal and that Parliament cannot use its power under Article 324(2) to create an appointment mechanism that gives the executive dominant control over who runs elections.

The Commission's conduct in recent elections has generated sustained criticism about use of the Model Code, scheduling decisions, and voter-roll management. These criticisms do not directly engage Article 324's text but go to the independence and impartiality that Article 324's structural design was meant to guarantee. The question of whether Article 324's power can be used by a Commission that is perceived as not independent — or whether institutional independence is a constitutional precondition for the valid exercise of Article 324's powers — is an important theoretical question that the current litigation may address.

The digitisation of elections, the introduction of EVMs and VVPATs, and the Commission's handling of challenges to electronic voting have produced a persistent litigation stream under Article 324. Courts have generally upheld the Commission's technical decisions while requiring transparency in the VVPAT audit process. The Commission's power to regulate social media conduct during elections, to govern the conduct of political parties' digital campaigns, and to enforce the Model Code in online spaces are now active Article 324 questions that lack settled answers.
