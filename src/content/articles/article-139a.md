## What this Article does

Article 139A, inserted by the Constitution (Forty-fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, grants the Supreme Court two distinct transfer powers:

**Clause (1) — Withdrawal for legal uniformity:** Where cases involving the same or substantially the same questions of law are pending before the Supreme Court and one or more High Courts, or before two or more High Courts, the Supreme Court may withdraw those cases and decide them itself, if satisfied (on its own motion or on application by the Attorney-General or a party) that the questions involved are substantial questions of general importance.

**Clause (2) — Inter-High Court transfer:** The Supreme Court may transfer any case, appeal, or proceeding pending before any High Court to any other High Court, if it considers such transfer expedient in the interests of justice.

## The two-stage withdrawal mechanism in Clause (1)

Clause (1) contains a distinctive two-stage option through its proviso. The Supreme Court may:

- Withdraw the case and decide it fully itself — in which case its judgment resolves both the legal questions and the specific dispute; or
- Withdraw the case, decide only the questions of law, and then return the case to the originating High Court for disposal in conformity with the Supreme Court's judgment.

The second option preserves the fact-finding role of the High Court in cases where the legal question is the only matter warranting Supreme Court attention. The proviso serves judicial economy: the Supreme Court settles the law nationally without needing to conduct a full re-trial or re-examination of facts.

## Who can apply under Clause (1)

Applications for withdrawal under Clause (1) can be made by:

- **The Supreme Court suo motu:** The Court can act on its own initiative without any application.
- **The Attorney-General of India:** The government's principal law officer can apply, reflecting the national interest in uniform legal standards.
- **A party to any such case:** Any litigant in the pending cases can apply.

The restriction to cases pending "before the Supreme Court and one or more High Courts or before two or more High Courts" means there must be at least two concurrent sets of proceedings involving substantially the same legal question — this prevents the provision from being used as a general transfer device where no national uniformity interest is present.

## The "substantial question of general importance" threshold

Clause (1) requires that the legal questions be "substantial questions of general importance" — a higher threshold than merely arguing that the same question arises in multiple jurisdictions. The Court must be satisfied that the question is: (a) genuinely contested (not settled), and (b) of importance to a sufficiently large number of persons or to the national interest. This prevents Article 139A from being used as a tactical device to remove cases from High Courts whose likely outcome is unfavourable.

## Clause (2) and the ends-of-justice transfer

Clause (2) is a broader and more flexible provision. It allows transfer of any case from one High Court to another "if it deems expedient so to do for the ends of justice." This does not require a common legal question or an application by the Attorney-General. The grounds for transfer include:

- **Fair trial concerns:** A reasonable apprehension that a party will not receive a fair hearing in the originating High Court.
- **Convenience of parties or witnesses:** Where most parties and witnesses are located in a different state.
- **Connected proceedings:** Where related cases are pending in another High Court and consolidation is in the interests of justice.

The "ends of justice" standard gives the Court broad discretion, but the Court has held that the mere convenience of one party or a general preference for a different forum is insufficient — there must be a tangible justice concern.

## Key cases

**Nahar Industrial Enterprises v. Hong Kong and Shanghai Banking Corporation (2009):** The Supreme Court clarified the conditions for withdrawal under Clause (1), emphasising that the questions of law must be common or substantially so, and that the Court should ordinarily settle the legal question and return the case to the High Court for disposal, rather than retaining the entire matter unless the facts are inextricably tied to the legal question.

**Subramaniam Swamy v. Ramakrishna Hegde (1990):** The Court addressed the Clause (2) transfer power and affirmed that inter-High Court transfer requires a compelling justice ground. The applicant's discomfort with a particular court or forum preference is not sufficient. The Court restated that its discretion under Clause (2) must be exercised to correct a demonstrated risk to justice, not to forum-shop.

**Abdul Nabi v. High Court of M.P. (1975):** An early case, predating the insertion of Article 139A, in which the Supreme Court addressed the principles for transfer of cases in the context of fair trial and convenience. The Article 139A framework subsequently codified these principles at the constitutional level.

**Union of India v. Cipla Ltd. (2017):** The Court considered Article 139A withdrawal in the context of conflicting High Court judgments on the same question of pharmaceutical patent law, providing a modern example of how the provision functions to achieve legal uniformity in commercial and intellectual property cases — the precise national-interest scenario for which Clause (1) was designed.

## Relationship with Article 136 and Article 222

Article 139A operates alongside other constitutional mechanisms for managing caseload and jurisdictional overlap:

- **Article 136 (special leave):** Allows the Supreme Court to intervene in any case anywhere, but as an appellate remedy rather than a withdrawal and transfer mechanism.
- **Article 222 (transfer of judges):** Operates on the judicial officer rather than the case, and serves different purposes.

Article 139A is unique in providing a forward-looking, pre-appeal mechanism: cases do not need to have been decided in the High Court first; the Supreme Court can intervene while they are still pending. This distinguishes it sharply from the appeal-based Article 136 route.

## Current significance

Article 139A has been used with increasing frequency in cases where divergent High Court judgments on the same question of law — particularly in commercial law, taxation, competition law, and public health regulation — have created legal uncertainty across states. The provision is also invoked in high-profile cases where a transfer from one High Court to another is sought on fair trial grounds, including in cases involving prominent parties or politically sensitive matters. The suo motu withdrawal power in Clause (1) has been used by the Supreme Court to consolidate major constitutional challenges — for example, petitions challenging central legislation filed across multiple High Courts — enabling the Court to give a single authoritative ruling rather than awaiting conflicting decisions from different courts.
