officialText: |-
  243ZE. Committee for Metropolitan planning.—(1) There shall be constituted in every Metropolitan area a Metropolitan Planning Committee to prepare a draft development plan for the Metropolitan area as a whole.

  (2) The Legislature of a State may, by law, make provision with respect to—

  (a) the composition of the Metropolitan Planning Committees;

  (b) the manner in which the seats in such Committees shall be filled:

  Provided that not less than two-thirds of the members of such Committee shall be elected by, and from amongst, the elected members of the Municipalities and Chairpersons of the Panchayats in the Metropolitan area in proportion to the ratio between the population of the Municipalities and of the Panchayats in that area;

  (c) the representation in such Committees of the Government of India and the Government of the State and of such organisations and institutions as may be deemed necessary for carrying out the functions assigned to such Committees;

  (d) the functions relating to planning and coordination for the Metropolitan area which may be assigned to such Committees;

  (e) the manner in which the Chairpersons of such Committees shall be chosen.

  (3) Every Metropolitan Planning Committee shall, in preparing the draft development plan,—

  (a) have regard to—

  (i) the plans prepared by the Municipalities and the Panchayats in the Metropolitan area; (ii) matters of common interest between the Municipalities and the Panchayats, including coordinated spatial planning of the area, sharing of water and other physical and natural resources, the integrated development of infrastructure and environmental conservation; (iii) the overall objectives and priorities set by the Government of India and the Government of the State; (iv) the extent and nature of investments likely to be made in the Metropolitan area by agencies of the Government of India and of the Government of the State and other available resources whether financial or otherwise;

  (b) consult such institutions and organisations as the Governor may, by order, specify.

  (4) The Chairperson of every Metropolitan Planning Committee shall forward the development plan, as recommended by such Committee, to the Government of the State.

## Why it matters

Metropolitan areas are India's most economically consequential geography. They account for a disproportionate share of GDP, tax revenue, formal employment, and infrastructure demand — and they are the units whose governance failures have the most concentrated social and economic costs. The Seventy-Fourth Amendment's architects recognised that the internal administrative boundaries of a metropolitan area — the boundaries between one municipal corporation and another, between urban municipalities and peri-urban panchayats, between the core city and the expanding fringe — create coordination failures that no single urban local body can solve. Water catchments, transport corridors, air pollution, solid waste flows, and housing markets do not stop at ward or municipal boundaries. Article 243ZE is the constitutional answer: a mandatory Metropolitan Planning Committee for every metropolitan area, which must prepare an integrated development plan for the metropolitan area as a whole.

The two-thirds elected-member requirement is substantive, not merely procedural. It ensures that the MPC is driven by locally elected representatives — the municipal councillors and panchayat chairpersons who are accountable to the residents of the area — rather than by technocrats, State officials, or Central government nominees. The proportionality rule (seats filled in proportion to the municipal-to-panchayat population ratio) means that peri-urban panchayat areas, which are often the fastest-growing parts of a metropolitan region, have a guaranteed voice in metro-level planning proportionate to their population.

## The gap between mandate and reality

Article 243ZE is among the most widely disregarded constitutional mandates in modern India. Every city with a population of ten lakh or more is constitutionally required to have a Metropolitan Planning Committee. In practice, no Indian metropolitan area has a functioning MPC that exercises the integrated planning role the Constitution envisages. State governments have strongly resisted constituting effective MPCs because metro-level planning authority is currently exercised by State-controlled development authorities — the Mumbai Metropolitan Region Development Authority, the Chennai Metropolitan Development Authority, the Delhi Development Authority, the Bangalore Development Authority, and equivalent bodies — whose boards are appointed by the State government and whose planning decisions are not subject to elected local body oversight. Constituting an effective MPC would require States to cede a substantial portion of that planning authority to a body that is constitutionally two-thirds composed of elected municipal and panchayat members — a devolution that no State government has willingly undertaken.

The result is that India's largest and fastest-growing cities are planned by State-controlled authorities that are accountable upward to the State government rather than outward to elected urban residents. The constitutional promise of an elected metropolitan planning institution has not been delivered in any metropolitan area.

## How courts use it

Petitions seeking mandamus to compel State governments to constitute Metropolitan Planning Committees have been filed in High Courts in Mumbai, Chennai, Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Kolkata. Courts have uniformly accepted that the word "shall" in clause (1) — "there shall be constituted in every Metropolitan area a Metropolitan Planning Committee" — creates a mandatory obligation and that the State's failure to constitute an MPC is unconstitutional. However, courts have been reluctant to order immediate constitution of a fully functional MPC, often directing States to file compliance affidavits and providing extended time for constitutional compliance. The enforceability problem is that even a court order cannot easily compel the devolution of substantive planning power from a State development authority to a newly constituted elected body: the State retains legislative authority over the structure and powers of the MPC, and can comply formally with Article 243ZE by constituting an MPC that is nominal — holding infrequent meetings and producing a plan that is not linked to actual budget decisions.

## Relation to Article 243ZD

Article 243ZE is the metropolitan-area counterpart of Article 243ZD, which mandates District Planning Committees. Both provisions use the same mandatory language, require the same elected-member majorities, and require the plan to be forwarded to the State government. The structural difference is that the MPC operates across what may be a multi-district metropolitan region (the Mumbai Metropolitan Region, for example, spans parts of Mumbai, Thane, and Raigad districts), while the DPC operates within a single district. In practice, neither institution has fulfilled its constitutional role: the DPC is non-functional in most States, and the MPC has never been meaningfully constituted in any major metropolitan area.

## Current significance

India's metropolitan areas are undergoing rapid peripheral expansion, with the peri-urban fringe — the zone where formal urban jurisdiction blurs into rural panchayat territory — being the site of intense land conversion, infrastructure investment, and population growth. This is precisely the zone that Article 243ZE's integrated metropolitan planning framework was designed to manage. In the absence of MPCs, peri-urban land use change is governed by a patchwork of State development authority notifications, individual project approvals, and State master plans that lack democratic accountability and coordinated spatial logic. The constitutional gap is increasingly consequential: uncoordinated metropolitan expansion generates severe infrastructure deficits, informal settlement growth, and environmental externalities that impose large costs on urban and peri-urban residents alike. The debate about reviving effective metropolitan governance under Article 243ZE has been taken up by the Fifteenth Finance Commission's recommendations, which emphasised the need to strengthen metropolitan planning institutions as a condition for improved grant allocation to large cities.
