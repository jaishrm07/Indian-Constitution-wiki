  143. Power of President to consult Supreme Court.—(1) If at any time it appears to the President that a question of law or fact has arisen, or is likely to arise, which is of such a nature and of such public importance that it is expedient to obtain the opinion of the Supreme Court upon it, he may refer the question to that Court for consideration and the Court may, after such hearing as it thinks fit, report to the President its opinion thereon.

## Why it matters

Article 143 creates a formal channel through which the executive can bring constitutional questions directly before the Supreme Court, bypassing the adversarial case-by-case process. It allows hard questions to be resolved before a crisis forces them, and it has been used at several genuinely pivotal constitutional moments — on delegated legislation, on minority educational rights, on inter-state water disputes, and on the collegium. But because the President acts on the advice of the Council of Ministers, the reference power is in practice an executive tool, and its use has not been free of political controversy.

The advisory opinion mechanism also serves a comparative constitutional function: it allows the Constitution to develop without waiting for private parties to have the requisite standing and interest to bring a case. Some of India's most foundational constitutional holdings — including the territorial cession rule in Berubari and the collegium's five-member composition from the Third Judges Case — came through Article 143 references rather than adversarial litigation. This is a significant departure from the common-law tradition in which constitutional principles develop only from resolved disputes between parties.

The Article 143 mechanism is also significant for what it is not. It is not available to States, to Parliament, or to private parties. Only the President — in practice, the Union Cabinet — can initiate a reference. This executive monopoly on the referral power means the device is not available as an impartial mechanism for resolving constitutional uncertainty unless the executive itself chooses to seek clarity, which it may be reluctant to do when the answer might constrain its own powers.

Comparative constitutional practice is instructive. Canada's Supreme Court has a similar reference jurisdiction exercisable by the federal or provincial governments and has answered references on the patriation of the constitution, the secession of Quebec, and same-sex marriage. India's Article 143 mechanism has produced equally foundational opinions, but it has been used far less frequently relative to the total volume of constitutional questions that have arisen since 1950. The result is that many fundamental constitutional questions have been resolved through adversarial litigation rather than through the more deliberate process that an Article 143 reference permits.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — discretionary reference, discretionary opinion:** The President may refer "a question of law or fact" of "such a nature and of such public importance" that it is expedient to obtain the Court's opinion. Both conditions — that it be a question of law or fact and that it be of public importance — must be satisfied. The Court's role is expressed as permissive — it "may" report its opinion — preserving the Court's discretion to decline. In practice the Court has answered almost all clause (1) references, though it has occasionally declined where the question was too abstract, where no factual substratum existed, or where the identical question was already pending before the Court in adversarial proceedings.

The scope of "question of law or fact" in clause (1) is wide. It encompasses questions of constitutional interpretation, questions of statutory validity, and factual questions of public importance. The requirement of "public importance" excludes private disputes but is otherwise broadly construed. Because the President acts on ministerial advice, virtually any constitutional or legal question can be brought before the Court without the constraints of standing, locus, or the requirement of a concrete dispute that govern ordinary litigation.

**Clause (2) — mandatory opinion on pre-Constitution treaty disputes:** Clause (2) is narrower and obligatory. It deals with disputes over pre-Constitution treaties and agreements that are excluded from the Court's original jurisdiction under the proviso to Article 131. When the President refers such a dispute, the Court "shall" report its opinion — there is no discretion to decline. This ensures that inter-State disputes rooted in pre-constitutional arrangements — which would otherwise have no judicial forum — can still receive authoritative resolution.

**Relationship with Article 141:** Advisory opinions under Article 143 are not judgments and do not technically bind as law declared under Article 141. However, every significant Article 143 opinion has been treated as authoritative by subsequent courts. The Berubari opinion was treated as constitutionally imperative; the Third Judges Case opinion was treated as binding on High Courts; the Kerala Education Bill opinion shaped minority education jurisprudence for decades. The practical consensus is that a unanimous or majority Article 143 opinion from a Constitution Bench carries very nearly the same weight as a Constitution Bench judgment.

**Procedure under Article 145:** Article 143 references are heard before a bench of at least five judges, delivered in open court, and by majority. Dissents are permissible under Article 145(5). A fractured plurality opinion — as in the Delhi Laws Act case — reduces the practical authority of the reference considerably, since there is then no clear majority position courts can apply as law. The open-court requirement under Article 145(4) applies: the opinion must be delivered in open court.

**Prospective quality:** Article 143 references are not governed by the Limitation Act and there is no requirement that they relate to an existing or imminent dispute. The President may refer a question that "is likely to arise" — making the device particularly suited to seeking constitutional guidance before the executive or legislature takes irreversible action. This prospective quality distinguishes the reference jurisdiction from ordinary appellate jurisdiction, which requires a concluded dispute.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

B. N. Rau, the constitutional adviser, drew on Article 143 of the draft from comparative precedents including Section 60 of the Government of India Act, 1935, which gave the Federal Court a similar advisory role. In the Assembly, there was broad agreement that such a mechanism was valuable for a new constitution whose provisions would generate genuine uncertainty. The debate focused primarily on whether the Court's opinions should be binding — some members argued that non-binding opinions were of limited value, while others maintained that binding advisory opinions would blur the distinction between judicial and executive authority. The non-binding formulation prevailed, reflecting the view that the Court's institutional authority would give its opinions persuasive effect even without legal compulsion.

Members also debated whether State governments should have independent access to the reference mechanism, particularly for federalism questions that might pit the Union against the States. The decision to vest the power exclusively in the President — that is, the Union executive — was deliberate, reflecting the Constitution's preference for channelling inter-governmental constitutional questions through a centrally controlled mechanism rather than allowing any government to directly invoke the Court's advisory function.

## Key cases

**In re The Berubari Union (1960):** The President referred whether Parliamentary legislation sufficed to implement the Nehru-Noon Pact on the transfer of the Berubari Union to Pakistan, or whether a constitutional amendment was required. The Court held that a constitutional amendment was necessary because territory forming part of India could not be ceded by ordinary legislation alone. The opinion directly shaped the Ninth Amendment Act and showed Article 143 at its most consequential — the Court's advice set the constitutional terms on which the executive could act. The case also produced foundational pronouncements on the theory of constituent power and the relationship between territory and sovereignty.

**In re The Delhi Laws Act (1951):** A seven-judge Constitution Bench delivered nine separate opinions on the permissibility of delegated legislation, producing prolonged uncertainty about the essential-legislative-functions doctrine. The fractured result illustrated both the power and the limits of Article 143 — it can bring a question to the Court swiftly, but the Court cannot always produce a clear answer on a genuinely contested constitutional question. The ambiguity generated by this reference was only partially resolved by later decisions and contributed to decades of litigation about the constitutional limits of subordinate legislation.

**In re The Kerala Education Bill (1958):** The President asked whether several clauses of the Kerala Education Bill were inconsistent with the Constitution. The Court's opinion on the rights of minorities under Article 30 and on the State's regulatory power over those institutions became foundational precedent in the long line of minority education cases. The reference allowed the Court to give advance constitutional guidance before the legislation was enacted, preventing a potential constitutional crisis.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1998 — Collegium Reference (Third Judges Case):** The President referred questions about the composition of the collegium and the norms governing its operation following the Second Judges Case. A nine-judge bench answered comprehensively — expanding the collegium to five members, laying down the reiteration rule, and elaborating norms for consultation. This is the clearest example of Article 143 being used to entrench judicial interpretation of the Constitution: the Third Judges Case was not a judgment in adversarial proceedings but a Presidential Reference, yet it gave the resulting opinion the full authority of a nine-judge Constitution Bench pronouncement.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 2012 — Coal Block Allocations:** In the aftermath of the coal block allocation controversy, the President referred questions about the powers of the Court under Article 142 to cancel coal block allocations and the extent to which the executive could direct the Court's remedial jurisdiction. The Court's opinion confirmed the independence and breadth of its Article 142 powers while noting their limits. This reference arose in part from the political controversy over judicial directions in a matter of significant economic and administrative consequence.

**In re Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal (1992):** The President referred questions arising from the implementation of the Cauvery Waters Disputes Tribunal's interim order, including questions about the constitutional obligations of State governments to comply with tribunal orders and the role of the executive in enforcement. The reference underlined how Article 143 can be used to address inter-State disputes with a political dimension that makes ordinary adversarial resolution difficult.

## Procedure and hearing

An Article 143 reference is heard like any other Constitution Bench matter. The Court issues notice, invites submissions from the Attorney-General and any interested parties, and typically allows intervention by State governments and civil society organisations. The open-court requirement under Article 145(4) applies. The opinion may be unanimous or by majority, with dissents permissible under Article 145(5).

The absence of a formal lis — no plaintiff, no defendant, no res judicata — means an Article 143 reference does not bind parties in the way a judgment does. However, the government that sought the reference is in practice committed to accepting the opinion. On the rare occasion when an opinion is politically inconvenient, governments have responded by seeking legislation or by constitutional amendment — as in the Berubari case, where the Court's opinion led directly to the Ninth Amendment Act.

## The court's discretion to decline

The Court's discretion to decline a clause (1) reference is constitutionally significant. The Court has indicated that it would be reluctant to answer references framed in the abstract rather than tied to a concrete factual substratum, because advisory opinions given in a vacuum risk being over-broad or may not anticipate the real complexities that arise in practice. Where the same question is already pending in adversarial proceedings before the Court, answering a reference may pre-judge a live dispute — which is both procedurally inappropriate and potentially unfair to parties who have argued the question in that litigation.

In Special Reference No. 1 of 2002 — the Ayodhya Reference — the President asked whether a Hindu temple had existed at the disputed site before the demolition of the Babri Masjid. The Supreme Court declined to answer, holding that the question was neither a "question of law" nor one that it was appropriate for the Court to answer in the abstract, particularly given the extensive pending adversarial litigation on the same subject. This is the clearest instance of the Court refusing a Presidential Reference, and it demonstrates that the Court treats its advisory jurisdiction as genuinely discretionary rather than as an obligation to answer whatever question the executive chooses to frame.

## Current significance

The Presidential Reference power is used rarely — fewer than fifteen major references have been made since 1950 — but at critical constitutional junctures. Conventions govern its use: it would be inappropriate for the executive to use Article 143 to seek an advisory opinion on a question already sub judice before the Supreme Court in adversarial proceedings. The Court has not definitively ruled on when it will decline a reference on this ground, leaving the boundary uncertain. There is also a structural tension: since the reference is made on the advice of the Council of Ministers, the executive can frame questions in ways that favour its own position, a concern that has shadowed several references and reinforces the importance of the Court's retained discretion.

A Presidential Reference on the constitutional issues surrounding the electoral college for the Vice-President and related structural questions has been discussed in recent years, reflecting ongoing governmental interest in using the Article 143 mechanism to address constitutional questions before they generate adversarial litigation. The Court's receptivity to such prospective references — and its willingness to answer questions framed by an executive with interests in the outcome — remains a subject of constitutional debate.

The contrast between the comprehensive opinions produced in major Article 143 references and the fragmented, incremental development of constitutional doctrine through adversarial litigation illustrates a broader tension in constitutional design. Article 143 represents the Constitution's recognition that some questions are too important and too structural to be left entirely to the accidents of litigation, and that the executive should have a mechanism to put those questions to the Court directly when the public interest demands it. The relative underuse of Article 143 in recent decades suggests that this mechanism's potential has not been fully exploited — and that there may be value in thinking more systematically about when a Presidential Reference would produce better constitutional outcomes than waiting for a suitable adversarial case to arrive.
