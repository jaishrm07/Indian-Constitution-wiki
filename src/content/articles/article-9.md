## What this Article does

Article 9 enshrines the principle of single citizenship: any person who voluntarily acquired the citizenship of a foreign state could not be or be deemed to be a citizen of India under Articles 5, 6, or 8. The word "voluntarily" is central — it excludes persons who acquired foreign nationality under compulsion or as a minor without choice. This provision was part of a broader constitutional design that rejected dual citizenship at commencement, a position carried forward by section 9 of the Citizenship Act, 1955.

## Single citizenship as a constitutional value

India opted for single citizenship at commencement, in contrast to the dual State-and-national citizenship characteristic of some federal systems. Article 9 is the enforcement mechanism: it ensures that a person who has chosen to belong to another polity by acquiring that polity's citizenship cannot simultaneously hold Indian citizenship. The provision was particularly important in the Partition context — thousands of persons had acquired Pakistani citizenship by registering with the Pakistani government or obtaining Pakistani passports, and Article 9 ensured that such persons did not also count as Indian citizens.

The automatic operation of Article 9 is one of its most important features. Loss of Indian citizenship occurs "by virtue of" the voluntary acquisition of foreign nationality — no formal order or notification is needed. The State does not have to take any step; the citizenship extinguishes itself on the acquisition of foreign nationality.

## The meaning of "voluntarily"

The adverb "voluntarily" does real constitutional work. Courts have interpreted it to mean that the person must have exercised free choice in acquiring foreign nationality. Acquisition of foreign citizenship:
- by fraud or under duress is not "voluntary" and does not automatically extinguish Indian citizenship;
- by a minor at the initiative of parents is not "voluntary" by the minor — the minor retains the option to renounce the foreign nationality on reaching adulthood;
- through registration or naturalisation in a foreign country on a person's own application is plainly voluntary.

The distinction has practical importance for children in diaspora communities who may have acquired dual nationality by birth or parental action, and who wish to reclaim Indian citizenship.

## Article 9 and the Partition cases

The most litigated context for Article 9 in the early Supreme Court was the interaction with Pakistani citizenship. When persons who had migrated to Pakistan registered as Pakistani citizens or obtained Pakistani passports, they brought themselves within Article 9. The Supreme Court held in Izhar Ahmad Khan that such registration was voluntary and that it extinguished any Indian citizenship that might otherwise have vested under Article 5 or 6.

## Key cases

**Izhar Ahmad Khan v. Union of India (1962) AIR 1962 SC 1052** — The Supreme Court held that the acquisition of Pakistani citizenship by a person who had migrated to Pakistan and registered there was "voluntary" for the purposes of Article 9, extinguishing any Indian citizenship that might otherwise have vested under Article 5.

**Bhawani Prasad Singhania v. State of Bihar (1957) — Patna High Court** — The High Court held that Article 9 does not apply automatically to minors whose parent acquired foreign citizenship, since "voluntary" acquisition requires the act of the person concerned.

**Central Inland Water Transport Corporation v. Brojo Nath Ganguly (1986) 3 SCC 156** — The Court confirmed that loss of Indian citizenship under Article 9 is automatic on voluntary acquisition of foreign nationality and does not require a formal declaration by the State.

## OCI and the single-citizenship tension

The Overseas Citizen of India scheme introduced by the Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2005 creates a form of quasi-citizenship that tensions the single-citizenship principle. OCI cardholders — who hold foreign passports and are therefore not Indian citizens by Article 9 — enjoy many rights that citizens enjoy: the right to live and work in India indefinitely, to hold property (subject to agricultural land restrictions), and to access public services. They are, however, excluded from voting, holding constitutional offices, and serving in the civil services. Critics argue that OCI creates a de facto partial dual-status regime that sits uneasily with Article 9's absolute rule. Supporters counter that OCI is a statutory scheme that falls short of conferring citizenship, and Article 9 is therefore not engaged.

## Current significance

Article 9 is regularly invoked in citizenship-stripping and naturalisation-fraud cases where the Central Government alleges that a person concealed their foreign nationality when applying for an Indian passport or OCI card. It is also a live issue for second-generation diaspora who acquire foreign citizenship at birth and later wish to return to India as citizens rather than OCI cardholders.
