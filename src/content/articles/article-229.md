plainEnglish: >-
  Article 229 gives the Chief Justice of a High Court the power to appoint the court's officers and servants, subject to a State Governor's power to require consultation with the State Public Service Commission for specified categories of external appointments. The conditions of service for court staff are prescribed by rules made by the Chief Justice — but rules relating to salaries, allowances, leave, and pensions require the Governor's approval. All administrative expenses of the High Court, including staff salaries and pensions, are charged on the Consolidated Fund of the State, insulating the court's operational finances from year-to-year legislative appropriation pressure. The structure reflects the Constitution's broader commitment to judicial independence — a court that cannot staff itself and whose budget requires annual legislative approval would be institutionally vulnerable to executive and legislative influence.
officialText: |-
  229. Officers and servants and the expenses of High Courts.—(1) Appointments of officers and servants of a High Court shall be made by the Chief Justice of the Court or such other Judge or officer of the Court as he may direct:

  Provided that the Governor of the State may by rule require that in such cases as may be specified in the rule no person not already attached to the Court shall be appointed to any office connected with the Court save after consultation with the State Public Service Commission.

  (2) Subject to the provisions of any law made by the Legislature of the State, the conditions of service of officers and servants of a High Court shall be such as may be prescribed by rules made by the Chief Justice of the Court or by some other Judge or officer of the Court authorised by the Chief Justice to make rules for the purpose:

  Provided that the rules made under this clause shall, so far as they relate to salaries, allowances, leave or pensions, require the approval of the Governor of the State .

  (3) The administrative expenses of a High Court, including all salaries, allowances and pensions payable to or in respect of the officers and servants of the Court, shall be charged upon the Consolidated Fund of the State, and any fees or other moneys taken by the Court shall form part of that Fund.

## Why it matters

Judicial independence is not only a matter of security of tenure for judges. It also depends on the court's ability to control its own internal administration without being subject to executive patronage or financial pressure. Article 229 secures two dimensions of this institutional independence. First, it gives the Chief Justice — not the State government — the power to appoint court staff, ensuring that the personnel who staff the Registry and support the court's daily operation are not placed there at the government's behest. Second, it charges the court's administrative expenses on the Consolidated Fund of the State, meaning those expenses are automatically met without needing to be voted on by the State Legislature each year.

The parallel with the Supreme Court's financial arrangements (where expenses are similarly charged on the Consolidated Fund of India under Article 146) reflects the Constitution's consistent design principle — courts that depend on annual legislative appropriation for their operating budgets are potentially subject to legislative pressure in ways that compromise their independence.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) vests appointment power in the Chief Justice but permits delegation to another judge or a senior officer. The Governor's power to require PSC consultation applies only when an outsider is being appointed to an office "connected with the Court" — it does not apply to promotions within the existing court staff. This distinction limits the Governor's intrusion into court staffing to the point of external entry, leaving internal career progression within the court's administrative authority.

Clause (2) creates a two-level rule-making structure. The Chief Justice makes rules prescribing conditions of service for court staff, subject to any State Legislature legislation. But for the most financially significant aspects of service — salaries, allowances, leave, and pensions — the Chief Justice's rules require the Governor's approval before they have effect. This approval requirement gives the State executive a voice in conditions that carry budgetary implications, since those costs are ultimately charged on the State's Consolidated Fund.

Clause (3) is the financial independence provision. Charging administrative expenses on the Consolidated Fund means they are paid without an annual vote — unlike ordinary departmental expenditures that must be included in the State budget presented to and voted on by the Legislature. Court fees received by the High Court also form part of the Consolidated Fund, preventing the court from building a separate financial base outside State financial controls.

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (1993) (Second Judges Case)** — The Court's comprehensive treatment of judicial independence included the administrative and financial dimensions of that independence. The judgment confirmed that the administrative independence of High Courts — including control over their own staffing — is an integral component of judicial independence and cannot be subordinated to executive convenience.

**High Court of Judicature at Allahabad v. Raj Kishore Yadav (1997)** — The High Court addressed the scope of the Chief Justice's power to prescribe conditions of service and the extent to which the Governor's approval requirement under clause (2) limits that power. The ruling affirmed that the Chief Justice's rule-making authority is primary and the Governor's role is limited to the specific categories of pay, allowances, leave, and pensions specified in the proviso.

**State of Maharashtra v. Laboratories P.E. (1979)** — The Supreme Court's observations on the financial independence of the judiciary — in the context of Consolidated Fund charging of judicial expenses — are directly relevant to the principle underlying Article 229(3).

## Current significance

Tensions between the Chief Justice's administrative authority under Article 229 and State government preferences for staffing — particularly for senior administrative posts like Registrar General — have arisen in several States. The constitutional allocation of appointment power to the Chief Justice is a direct barrier to State government attempts to place their preferred officials in court administration roles.

The Governor's approval requirement for salary and allowance rules occasionally becomes a friction point when the State government is slow to process approvals, affecting the court's ability to revise pay structures for its staff in line with pay commission recommendations or to address anomalies in service conditions. Courts have in such circumstances sought directions from the Supreme Court to compel timely processing of pending approval requests.

The Consolidated Fund charging of High Court administrative expenses is a direct guarantee of institutional financial independence, but it does not eliminate all financial pressure. State governments retain significant leverage through their control of capital expenditure for court infrastructure — new court buildings, technology upgrades, and physical expansion of court premises require budgetary allocations subject to normal legislative appropriation. The operational independence guaranteed by Article 229(3) thus coexists with ongoing dependence on executive good-will for physical infrastructure, an asymmetry that the Law Commission and judicial administration reformers have periodically flagged as a constraint on High Court modernisation.
