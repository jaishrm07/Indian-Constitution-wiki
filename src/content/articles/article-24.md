## Why it matters

Article 24 is absolute in character — it admits no exceptions, no balancing with competing considerations, and no reasonable-restriction framework of the kind that qualifies rights under Article 19. Its simplicity is deliberate. The Constituent Assembly understood that child labour in hazardous industries was categorically incompatible with the constitutional project of building a society of equal and educated citizens. A right subject to exceptions could be hollowed out by economic necessity arguments; only an absolute prohibition could break the cycle of poverty, illiteracy, and exploited childhood that had characterised colonial India's industrial workforce.

The provision applies against both private employers and the state, making it — like Article 23 — a fundamental right with horizontal application. This was necessary because child labour was overwhelmingly a phenomenon of private industry: match factories, fireworks manufacturers, carpet weavers, brick kilns, and construction sites employed children not because the state directed them to but because private economic calculations made child labour cheap and convenient. Constitutional prohibition required that this calculation be overridden without exception.

The relationship between Article 24 and the broader constitutional framework for childhood reflects a coherent vision. The directive principles in Article 39(e) require the state to ensure that the health and strength of workers, including children, are not abused and that citizens are not forced by economic necessity into occupations unsuited to their age or strength. Article 39(f) requires that children are given opportunities and facilities to develop in a healthy manner and in conditions of freedom and dignity. Article 21A, added by the Eighty-Sixth Amendment in 2002, guarantees free and compulsory education for children between six and fourteen — making school attendance the constitutional alternative to the hazardous employment that Article 24 prohibits. These provisions read together establish that the Constitution treats childhood as a protected status, not merely an age category.

## Reading the provision

The operative words of Article 24 are simple, but each carries significant interpretive content.

"No child below the age of fourteen years" — The age threshold of fourteen was chosen as the minimum age at which a child could be presumed capable of making meaningful choices about work and education, consistent with international labour standards of the era. The Constituent Assembly debates reflect awareness that this was a baseline, not a ceiling, and that subsequent legislation could raise the effective protection. The Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act 1986 and its 2016 amendment continue to use the fourteen-year threshold as the absolute cut-off for factory and hazardous employment, while separately restricting adolescents (fourteen to eighteen) in certain categories of hazardous work.

"Shall be employed to work in any factory or mine" — "Factory" and "mine" are mentioned as exemplars of hazardous industrial settings, not as an exhaustive list. The use of "any other hazardous employment" immediately following confirms this. Courts have consistently treated the specific mention of factories and mines as illustrative — drawing on the historical prominence of child labour in these industries at the time of drafting — and the real scope of the prohibition as extending to all hazardous employment regardless of the industrial setting.

"Engaged in any other hazardous employment" — Courts have interpreted "hazardous" broadly to cover industries where children face risk of injury, toxic exposure, or physical conditions harmful to their development. The Supreme Court has included fireworks and match manufacturing (Sivakasi), chemical production, bidi rolling in enclosed spaces, construction work, stone quarrying, carpet weaving in low-light conditions, and several agricultural operations involving machinery or pesticide exposure. The Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act 2016 defines hazardous occupations and processes through a Schedule and empowers the Central Government to add further categories. The constitutional prohibition under Article 24 is broader than the statutory list — courts are not constrained by the statutory schedule when applying the fundamental right.

The article does not prohibit all work by children under fourteen, only work in hazardous employment. This textual limit has created legislative space for permitting children to work in family enterprises and non-hazardous, non-factory settings — a policy choice embodied in the original 1986 Act. The 2016 Amendment significantly narrowed this space by prohibiting children from working even in family enterprises in hazardous processes, and by restricting the "family enterprise" exception to work that does not interfere with school attendance or harm the child's health. Critics have argued that any exception carries risks of evasion and that the constitutional spirit points toward a complete prohibition on work by children below fourteen in all settings.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly placed Article 24 within the chapter on fundamental rights rather than confining child-labour protection to the directive principles. This location signals constitutional priority — unlike the directive principles, Article 24 is directly enforceable. Speakers in the debates drew on the report of the Child Labour in India (Rege Committee) and on international standards, noting that India's ratification of ILO conventions on minimum age committed the country to progressively raising the effective minimum age of employment. The decision to make the prohibition absolute — without a "public interest" or "economic necessity" exception — reflected hard-won consensus that any exception would be exploited.

## Key cases

**MC Mehta v State of Tamil Nadu (1996) 6 SCC 756** — The landmark public interest petition on child labour in the match and firecracker industry in Sivakasi. The Supreme Court held that Article 24, read with Articles 21, 39(e), and 39(f), imposes positive obligations on the state to ensure children are not employed in hazardous conditions and to provide meaningful alternatives. The Court directed factory inspections in child-labour endemic districts, removal of child workers, deposit of Rs. 20,000 per affected child into a Child Labour Rehabilitation-cum-Welfare Fund to be used for the child's education and welfare, and admission of removed children to schools. It required the employer who employed the child to pay to the fund, and imposed obligations on the District Collector to oversee implementation. The ruling transformed Article 24 from a declaratory guarantee into a framework for judicially supervised rehabilitation and has been applied in every major child-labour case since.

**Labourers Working on Salal Hydro Project v State of Jammu and Kashmir (1983) 2 SCC 181** — The Court held that children employed at a major hydroelectric dam construction site were engaged in hazardous employment within Article 24, regardless of the absence of specific legislation covering construction. The provision is self-executing — its prohibition does not require prior legislative elaboration. The state is obligated to remove child workers and enforce the prohibition wherever hazardous conditions are identified. This case established the self-executing character of Article 24 and its applicability to infrastructure projects.

**People's Union for Democratic Rights v Union of India AIR 1983 SC 1473** — In the Asiad Workers case, the Court noted alongside its Article 23 holdings that children below fourteen were employed on the Delhi Asian Games construction sites, violating Article 24. The Court confirmed that Article 24 operates in conjunction with Article 23's forced-labour prohibition and the labour welfare directives in Part IV, creating an integrated framework of constitutional protection for child workers.

**Bandhua Mukti Morcha v Union of India AIR 1984 SC 802** — The Court extended its bonded-labour analysis to children, observing that child workers in hazardous industries were frequently bonded through debt arrangements imposed on their families. Article 24 requires not only removal of child workers but rehabilitation — a positive obligation connected to Article 21's requirement of life with dignity. The judgment was instrumental in establishing that compliance with Article 24 requires substantive follow-through, not merely formal removal.

**Bachpan Bachao Andolan v Union of India (2011) 5 SCC 1** — The Court directed the Central and state governments to survey industries in child-labour endemic districts, identify child workers, register them in schools, and produce implementation reports. It directed that the Ministry of Labour coordinate with state labour departments and school authorities to provide bridge education for children removed from hazardous employment. The judgment reinforced that Article 24's obligations are ongoing and subject to continuing judicial supervision.

**Unni Krishnan v State of AP (1993) 1 SCC 645** — Though primarily an Article 21A precursor case establishing the right to education as implicit in Article 21, the Court's reasoning directly connected child labour prohibition under Article 24 to access to schooling. Effective enforcement of Article 24 requires a functioning school system — the two provisions are functionally inseparable.

## Legislative framework

The Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act 1986 originally prohibited child labour only in specified hazardous occupations and processes listed in the Schedule while permitting and regulating — rather than prohibiting — child work in non-hazardous settings. This bifurcated approach was criticised by child rights advocates as inconsistent with the constitutional spirit of an absolute prohibition. The Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act 2016 substantially strengthened the framework: it prohibits employment of children below fourteen in all hazardous industries, prohibits employment of "adolescents" (fourteen to eighteen) in hazardous occupations and processes, and eliminates most exceptions for family enterprises. Violations are cognisable offences. Repeat offences attract enhanced penalties.

The National Child Labour Project (NCLP) provides special training centres, bridge education, and vocational training for rehabilitated child workers in child-labour endemic districts before mainstreaming them into the regular school system. The scheme operates under the Ministry of Labour and Employment and is the principal rehabilitation mechanism contemplated by the MC Mehta directions. The Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009, implementing Article 21A, requires all schools to provide free education and treats child labour as an obstacle to school attendance — placing the obligation on the state to remove that obstacle.

## Current significance

Despite constitutional prohibition and progressively strengthened legislation, child labour in informal sectors — agriculture, domestic service, brick kilns, rag picking, and small-scale manufacturing — persists at significant scale in India. The 2011 Census identified over 10 million child workers; more recent survey data suggests continued underreporting. The informal economy's scale, the paucity of labour inspectors, and the economic pressures on families — particularly in rural poverty and migrant communities — mean that the formal prohibition is frequently unimplemented where it matters most.

The intersection of Article 24 with Article 21A means that failures in the schooling system directly translate into failures of the child labour prohibition. When the alternative to hazardous employment is a dysfunctional school that does not offer meaningful education, the constitutional guarantee on paper cannot be delivered in practice. This systemic connection has driven courts to supervise not only the removal of child workers but the quality and accessibility of the educational alternatives provided. Mid-day meal programmes, residential school schemes for migrant children, and bridge education centres are all part of the judicially supervised implementation architecture that Article 24 has generated.

The impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on child labour trends — school closures, family income shocks, and migration disruption — renewed concerns that enforcement gains of the 2000s and 2010s were partly reversed. Court monitoring committees and civil society organisations reported increased engagement of children in agricultural and domestic work during the pandemic period. Article 24's absolute prohibition provides the constitutional baseline against which these regressions must be measured and corrected, and the continuing jurisdiction of courts supervising schemes under the MC Mehta framework ensures that the executive cannot treat enforcement as discretionary.
