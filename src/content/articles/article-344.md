officialText: |-
  344. Commission and Committee of Parliament on official language.—(1) The President shall, at the expiration of five years from the commencement of this Constitution and thereafter at the expiration of ten years from such commencement, by order constitute a Commission which shall consist of a Chairman and such other members representing the different languages specified in the Eighth Schedule as the President may appoint, and the order shall define the procedure to be followed by the Commission.

  (2) It shall be the duty of the Commission to make recommendations to the President as to—

  (a) the progressive use of the Hindi language for the official purposes of the Union;

  (b) restrictions on the use of the English language for all or any of the official purposes of the Union;

  (c) the language to be used for all or any of the purposes mentioned in article 348;

  (d) the form of numerals to be used for any one or more specified purposes of the Union;

  (e) any other matter referred to the Commission by the President as regards the official language of the Union and the language for communication between the Union and a State or between one State and another and their use.

  (3) In making their recommendations under clause (2), the Commission shall have due regard to the industrial, cultural and scientific advancement of India, and the just claims and the interests of persons belonging to the non-Hindi speaking areas in regard to the public services.

  (4) There shall be constituted a Committee consisting of thirty members, of whom twenty shall be members of the House of the People and ten shall be members of the Council of States to be elected respectively by the members of the House of the People and the members of the Council of States in accordance with the system of proportional representation by means of the single transferable vote.

  (5) It shall be the duty of the Committee to examine the recommendations of the Commission constituted under clause (1) and to report to the President their opinion thereon.

  (6) Notwithstanding anything in article 343, the President may, after consideration of the report referred to in clause (5), issue directions in accordance with the whole or any part of that report.

## Why it matters

The Constituent Assembly rejected the idea of embedding a detailed language transition timetable in the Constitution itself. The framers recognised that how fast Hindi should replace English in government was a question too politically explosive to answer in advance, and too dependent on social conditions — literacy rates, administrative capacity, the growth of Hindi as a technical language — that could only be assessed over time. Article 344 is the constitutional solution to that problem: it creates an expert advisory structure to evaluate these conditions periodically and recommend next steps, while subjecting those recommendations to parliamentary scrutiny before any presidential action is taken.

The article's design deliberately places the language question in the domain of evolutionary politics rather than constitutional command. The Commission recommends; the Parliamentary Committee filters; the President directs. No single actor can unilaterally accelerate the Hindi transition, and the institutional requirement that the Commission include representatives of all Eighth Schedule languages ensures that the advisory body cannot be dominated by Hindi speakers. This is why the Constituent Assembly accepted the clause without the rancour that surrounded Article 343 itself.

The Commission mechanism was also Article 349's gatekeeper: Parliament could not enact laws changing the language of the courts or official legislative texts during the fifteen-year transitional period without first going through the Article 344 advisory process. This interlocking design meant that both executive and legislative action on the language question during the critical first fifteen years required prior institutional consideration of the non-Hindi communities' interests.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) requires the President to constitute a Commission at five years (1955) and at ten years (1960) from commencement. The Commission must include a chairman and members representing each of the languages in the Eighth Schedule — a requirement that ensures the advisory body reflects India's full linguistic diversity rather than being an instrument of Hindi-speaking States. Only two such Commissions were ever constituted: the First Official Language Commission (1955, chaired by B.G. Kher) and the Second (1960, chaired by M.C. Chagla). No further Commissions have been appointed under this article, because the Official Languages Act 1963 and the 1967 amendment created a statutory framework that rendered the quinquennial commission mechanism redundant as a periodic review body.

Clause (2) lists the Commission's terms of reference. Item (a) — "the progressive use of the Hindi language for the official purposes of the Union" — sets the directional mandate: the Commission advises on the pace and scope of Hindi's expansion. Item (b) — "restrictions on the use of the English language" — is the corollary question: as Hindi expands, English is correspondingly restricted. Item (c) extends the Commission's remit to the language of the Supreme Court and High Courts under Article 348, making the Commission the body that advises on changes to the most sensitive domain of English use. Items (d) and (e) cover numerals and any other language matter referred by the President.

Clause (3) is the constitutional safeguard that gives Article 344 its minority-protection character. The Commission must give "due regard" to the "just claims and interests" of persons from non-Hindi-speaking areas in public services. This is not a veto but it is a structural constraint — the Commission cannot recommend language changes that would effectively disqualify non-Hindi speakers from competitive examinations or central government posts without providing constitutionally adequate justification. Courts have treated clause (3) as a substantive obligation on the Commission and, derivatively, on the executive when acting on Commission recommendations.

Clauses (4) and (5) establish the Parliamentary Committee of thirty members (twenty from Lok Sabha, ten from Rajya Sabha, elected by proportional representation) to examine the Commission's report and advise the President. The proportional representation election method ensures the Committee's composition reflects the party balance in Parliament, making it a genuinely national body rather than a rubber stamp for the Hindi lobby. Clause (6) allows the President to issue directions on the basis of the Committee's report, notwithstanding Article 343's default position — the critical mechanism by which Commission recommendations are translated into presidential action.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's treatment of Article 344 was notably less acrimonious than the fight over Article 343, because the Commission mechanism was seen as a way of deferring the most difficult decisions rather than resolving them. Members who had fought over whether Hindi should be made the sole immediate official language could accept a process that would generate recommendations over time, because each faction believed the process would eventually vindicate its preferred outcome.

Non-Hindi members secured the clause (3) safeguard for public service interests as the price of their acceptance. T.T. Krishnamachari and other southern members insisted that any Commission advising on Hindi expansion must be structurally constrained to protect the career prospects of non-Hindi-speaking civil servants — a requirement that reflected the concrete fear that replacing English with Hindi in competitive examinations and official communications would amount to a systemic advantage for Hindi speakers in all central government employment. The safeguard in clause (3) was accepted by the Hindi bloc as a reasonable concession, setting the template for the Commission-based approach to language transition.

The requirement that Commission members represent all Eighth Schedule languages was also a southern concession — a guarantee that the Commission advising on Hindi expansion would hear from speakers of Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam, Bengali, Marathi, and Gujarati, not only from the Hindi belt. This structural requirement has been consistently honoured in the appointments to both Commissions actually constituted, though it did not prevent both Commissions from recommending progressive Hindi expansion in their reports.

## Key cases

**First Official Language Commission Report (1956) and Parliamentary Committee Response** — The First Commission chaired by B.G. Kher recommended fairly rapid expansion of Hindi in official Union work. The Parliamentary Committee, chaired by Govind Ballabh Pant, accepted some recommendations and rejected others — particularly the more aggressive proposals for replacing English in technical and scientific terminology. The Committee's tempered report became the political template for the Official Languages Act 1963, demonstrating that the Article 344 mechanism successfully filtered expert recommendations through democratic deliberation.

**Anti-Hindi Agitation, Tamil Nadu (1965)** — When the fifteen-year English-use period under Article 343(2) expired on 26 January 1965 without Parliament having enacted adequate protections, protests erupted in Tamil Nadu. The violence — which left dozens dead — illustrated the catastrophic cost of allowing the language question to be resolved without sufficient political consensus. The 1967 amendment to the Official Languages Act, guaranteeing English's continuation as long as any non-Hindi State requires it, was Parliament's direct response. Courts have since confirmed that this statutory guarantee cannot be reversed unilaterally by executive action on Language Commission recommendations.

**Rajbhasha Vibhag Circular Cases (various High Courts, 1980s–2000s)** — Multiple High Court challenges to circulars from the Department of Official Language requiring central government offices to conduct specified percentages of correspondence in Hindi have turned on whether such executive directions are consistent with both the Official Languages Act and the clause (3) safeguards. Courts have generally upheld directives where they leave room for English responses, but struck down those requiring non-Hindi speakers to communicate only in Hindi, treating clause (3) as a continuing constraint on executive implementation of Commission recommendations.

**Rajbhasha Samiti 2022 Report Controversy** — The Parliamentary Committee on Official Language's 2022 recommendations that Hindi replace English as the medium of instruction in IITs, IIMs, and medical colleges drew constitutional challenges from non-Hindi States on the basis that implementing such recommendations without adequate transitional arrangements would violate the clause (3) safeguard. The controversy illustrated that Article 344's institutional machinery remains politically active more than seven decades after the Constitution came into force.

## The two Commissions and their legacy

Only two Commissions under Article 344 were ever actually constituted. The First Official Language Commission (1955–1956), chaired by B.G. Kher, produced a detailed report recommending substantial expansion of Hindi in Union government work. The Pant Committee's response was considerably more cautious, feeding into the drafting of the Official Languages Act 1963. The Second Commission (1960), chaired by M.C. Chagla, submitted its report before the 1963 Act was passed and its recommendations were largely overtaken by the statutory regime and the 1967 amendment.

No third Commission has been constituted under Article 344(1). The Parliamentary Committee on Official Language (Rajbhasha Samiti), constituted under clause (4), became the permanent body, meeting and reporting regularly without the quinquennial re-constitution cycle. This institutional evolution — from periodic constitutional commission to permanent parliamentary committee — reflects the recognition that language policy requires continuous monitoring rather than episodic review.

## The clause (3) safeguard in practice

Clause (3)'s requirement that the Commission have "due regard" to the interests of non-Hindi speakers in public services has been treated by courts as a substantive constraint on executive action. The Department of Official Language's circulars directing central government offices to progressively increase Hindi correspondence have been challenged on the ground that they disadvantage non-Hindi speakers in service conditions and career advancement. Courts have held that compliance targets for Hindi correspondence must be implemented in a way that does not penalise officials from non-Hindi backgrounds for responding to correspondence in English, and that performance evaluations cannot negatively assess an official's failure to use Hindi if that official is from a non-Hindi-speaking area and was never trained in Hindi.

These judicial glosses on the Official Languages Act and executive circulars derive their constitutional weight from clause (3)'s safeguard, which embeds fair-dealing toward non-Hindi speakers into the very mandate of the language-promotion machinery. The safeguard has proved more durable in practice than critics of the language compromise expected, because it gives courts a textual handle for reviewing the implementation of Hindi-promotion policies without having to address the underlying constitutional choice in favour of Hindi itself.

## Current significance

The Parliamentary Committee on Official Language (Rajbhasha Samiti) continues to operate as the permanent institutional successor to the periodic Commission mechanism. Its voluminous multi-part reports to the President, periodically tabled in Parliament, contain recommendations ranging from requiring Hindi as the medium of IIT and IIM entrance examinations to mandating Hindi signage in ports and airports. The Committee's 2022 report chapter recommending Hindi as the medium of instruction in technical and medical colleges drew sharp criticism from the Chief Ministers of Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and West Bengal and was withdrawn from immediate implementation following political pressure.

The clause (3) safeguard for non-Hindi public service aspirants remains the central constitutional constraint invoked whenever new language mandates are proposed. The tension between the Rajbhasha Samiti's progressive-Hindi mandate and the Official Languages Act's "as long as non-Hindi States require" guarantee is the central unresolved dynamic in India's official language policy, and it plays out through the Article 344 institutional mechanism every time a new set of recommendations is tabled.

The article's design — placing language policy in the hands of an advisory commission subject to parliamentary oversight rather than constitutional command — has proved resilient precisely because it has allowed political negotiation to substitute for constitutional confrontation on what remains India's most divisive domestic cultural question. Whether the permanent Rajbhasha Samiti model fully honours the periodic-commission architecture envisaged by the framers is an open constitutional question, as is whether a third Commission under clause (1) should be constituted to assess language conditions seventy-five years after the Constitution came into force.
