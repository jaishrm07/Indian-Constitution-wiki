## What this Article did

Article 380 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that made provision for the President during the transitional period. It has been omitted.

The article dealt with the presidency in the period between the Constitution's commencement on 26 January 1950 and the election of a President under the normal constitutional procedure in Article 62. It provided for who would serve as President — or perform presidential functions — during this gap period and how the transition to the constitutionally elected President would be managed.

Dr Rajendra Prasad was elected as the first President by the Constituent Assembly on 24 January 1950 — two days before the Constitution's commencement — and was sworn in on 26 January 1950. Article 380 made provision for this transitional presidential arrangement, ensuring that the constitutional requirement for a President was met from day one without waiting for a full election under the new procedure.

## Why it matters

The constitutional grounding of the presidency from day one of the Republic was symbolically and legally essential. The President is the constitutional head of the Indian Republic — signifying the transfer of sovereignty from the British Crown to the people of India, represented by the elected President. Article 380 ensured that this symbolic transfer was constitutionally valid from the first moment of the Republic's existence. Without it, the legal basis for Dr Prasad's presidency during the transitional period could have been questioned, potentially casting doubt on the validity of every presidential act — signing bills, making appointments, summoning Parliament — during 1950-1952.

## Why it was omitted

Article 380 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, together with Articles 379 and 381-392, all of which had served their transitional purposes. By 1956, the presidency had functioned normally under the constitutional procedure for several years, and the transitional provisions for the first President's assumption of office were entirely spent.

## Historical context

The election of Dr Rajendra Prasad as the first President of India was a landmark event. His election by the Constituent Assembly (sitting as an electoral college) was conducted just before the Constitution formally came into force. Article 380 ensured the constitutional continuity of the presidency across that transition. The President is central to the constitutional framework — signing bills into law, summoning and proroguing Parliament, and exercising executive authority through the Council of Ministers — so ensuring a legally valid President from the moment of commencement was constitutionally essential.

## Key cases

No constitutional challenge to the validity of the first President's election or continuation in office is recorded. The transition was legally managed by the provisional arrangements in Article 380 and proceeded without litigation.

## Current significance

Article 380 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in securing the constitutional legitimacy of the first President of India and ensuring that the executive branch of the new Republic had a constitutionally grounded head from the moment of commencement on 26 January 1950.

Dr Rajendra Prasad served as President from 26 January 1950 until 13 May 1962, being re-elected for a second term in 1957 — the only President of India to have served two terms. His election by the Constituent Assembly under the transitional arrangement of Article 380 was the starting point for a constitutional presidency that has since been exercised by over a dozen incumbents. Article 380's role in securing that uninterrupted constitutional chain from the first moment of the Republic is its lasting historical significance.
