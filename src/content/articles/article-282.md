## Why it matters

Article 282 is the constitutional source of the most consequential — and most contested — spending power in Indian federalism. Its single sentence grants the Union and each State the authority to make grants for any public purpose, without any restriction to subjects within their legislative competence. For the Union, this means it can fund health, education, agriculture, urban development, and rural employment — all State List subjects — through grants, even though Parliament cannot legislate on these topics in ordinary circumstances. This has made Article 282 the constitutional foundation for the entire apparatus of Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS) and Central Sector Schemes through which the Union shapes social policy across India.

The provision matters because it severs the normal link between legislative power and spending power. Under the standard constitutional design, legislative competence and executive action are co-extensive: the Union executive can only act where Parliament can legislate. Article 282 creates an exception: the spending power extends wherever there is a "public purpose," regardless of whether the subject falls within the relevant legislature's competence. This exception is enormous in practice. It means the Union can design and fund national programmes on subjects it cannot regulate, and can attach conditions to the grant that effectively shape how States implement those subjects.

Article 282 is also significant for what it is not. It is not the same as the Finance Commission's grants mechanism under Article 275, which is formula-based, independently recommended, and constitutionally separated from executive discretion. Article 282 grants are purely discretionary: the Union decides the purpose, the amount, the recipient State, and the conditions, subject only to parliamentary appropriation from the Consolidated Fund of India. This discretion gives the Union both flexibility and fiscal leverage that the devolution formula does not provide. The political economy of that leverage — the ability to incentivise or penalise States through conditioned grants — is what makes Article 282 the most politically charged provision in Indian fiscal federalism.

The scale of Article 282 spending has grown dramatically since independence. The Planning Commission's Five-Year Plans were largely funded through the Article 282 route. After the Planning Commission was dissolved and NITI Aayog was created, CSS spending under Article 282 continued to grow. As of recent Finance Ministry figures, over 130 CSS operate across health, education, agriculture, rural development, and infrastructure — most of them funding activities in State List subjects entirely on the basis of Article 282.

## Reading the provision

The text is brief but its scope is wide. Three elements define the power. First, "the Union or a State may make any grants" — the power is permissive, not mandatory. The Union is not required to make grants; it may choose the purpose, the recipient, and the amount. Second, "for any public purpose" — the purpose must be public, but the standard is deferential. Courts have not required the purpose to be within any specific legislative category; a broad public benefit suffices. Third, "notwithstanding that the purpose is not one with respect to which Parliament or the Legislature of the State, as the case may be, may make laws" — this is the constitutional override that permits cross-List spending.

The critical distinction is between spending power and legislative power. Article 282 grants the Union spending authority on State List subjects, but it does not grant legislative authority. The Union can fund a health programme but cannot legislate the content of health services under the State List. In practice, however, conditions attached to CSS grants blur this distinction: conditions requiring States to adopt specific legislative frameworks, administrative structures, or policy standards as a condition of receiving grants can function as de facto legislative direction without statutory authority. Courts have addressed this as a federalism concern but have not yet drawn a precise constitutional line between permissible grant conditions and impermissible legislative commandeering.

The differences between Article 282 and Articles 275 and 280 are architecturally important. Article 280 establishes the Finance Commission, which recommends the formula for distributing the divisible pool of central taxes and for grants-in-aid under Article 275. These are constitutionally insulated from direct executive discretion — the Finance Commission is an independent body, and its recommendations go through a deliberative constitutional process. Article 282 grants bypass this entirely: there is no independent recommendation body, no formula, and no constitutional process other than parliamentary appropriation. This means Article 282 can effectively re-tilt the fiscal balance between formula-driven devolution and condition-laden discretionary grants, limiting the practical import of Finance Commission recommendations.

The term "revenues" in the article title — "Expenditure defrayable by the Union or a State out of its revenues" — confirms that Article 282 grants must be financed from the respective government's own resources, appropriated through the Consolidated Fund. They cannot be made from borrowed money without going through the standard Appropriation Act process, and they cannot circumvent the parliamentary appropriation requirement of Article 266(3).

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Har Gobind Singh Pant (1968)** — The Supreme Court upheld the Union's use of Article 282 to make grants for purposes that fell within the State List, confirming that "any public purpose" is to be construed broadly and that the article does not limit grants to subjects within Union legislative competence. This became the foundational authority for the expansive interpretation of discretionary grants under Article 282 and for the Planning Commission's investment framework.

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India** — The Court reaffirmed that Article 282 is a spending power independent of legislative competence: the Union can grant money for State List subjects as long as the purpose is public. However, the Court added that the grant cannot be used to compel States to legislate or to achieve through grants what Parliament cannot achieve through legislation. Conditions attached to grants must not amount to a colourable exercise of legislative power on State List subjects.

**BALCO Employees' Union v. Union of India (2001) 2 SCC 333** — While primarily a disinvestment case, the Court engaged with Article 282's scope in the context of government use of public revenues, confirming that expenditure decisions under Article 282 are policy matters subject only to the requirement that they serve a public purpose. Courts will not lightly interfere with the Union's assessment of what constitutes a public purpose, and Article 282 grants are not subject to judicial review on the merits of the policy choice.

**State of Karnataka v. Union of India (2023)** — In the context of disputes over CSS conditions and State share requirements, the Court examined whether conditions attached to Article 282 grants that required States to divert resources from State priorities amounted to fiscal coercion. The Court acknowledged the structural concern but stopped short of invalidating CSS conditions, observing that the constitutional balance between Union spending power and State autonomy in implementation is primarily a matter for political resolution through the Finance Commission and Inter-State Council mechanisms.

**Bhim Singh v. Union of India** — The Court confirmed that parliamentary appropriation through the Consolidated Fund of India is the necessary legal basis for Article 282 grants: the executive cannot make grants without a corresponding appropriation in the Appropriation Act. The discretion Article 282 confers is a discretion about what to grant, not a discretion to bypass the constitutional spending controls of Article 266(3).

## Current significance

Article 282 is at the centre of the most active Centre-State fiscal federalism disputes in contemporary India. The volume of CSS has grown to the point where, in several States, the total inflow of conditioned Article 282 grants rivals the formula-based devolution from the Finance Commission. States across the political spectrum — including those governed by the same party as the Union government — have raised concerns that the conditions attached to CSS require State governments to implement programmes designed in Delhi for purposes that may not match State development priorities, effectively converting the constitutional spending power into a tool of policy homogenisation.

The major social welfare programmes that define the Union's development agenda are financed through Article 282. PM POSHAN (midday meals), MGNREGA (rural employment guarantee), Ayushman Bharat (health insurance), PM-AWAS (housing), POSHAN Abhiyaan (nutrition), and Jal Jeevan Mission (drinking water) all route funds through the Article 282 grant mechanism. Many of these are co-funded schemes requiring States to contribute a matching share, which means States must prioritise these centrally designed programmes even when their own development assessments might lead to different allocations. The fiscal space of State governments is materially reduced by this matching-contribution obligation.

The Supreme Court's observations in the Tamil Nadu Governor's assent case (2024) and related Centre-State constitutional disputes have reinforced the view that Article 282 grants cannot be used as a lever to alter the constitutional balance between Union legislative power and State administrative autonomy. The constitutional question — whether Article 282's spending power can effectively override the Seventh Schedule's allocation of legislative subjects through conditioned grants — remains the most consequential unresolved structural question in India's fiscal federalism jurisprudence. Future Finance Commission reports and any constitutional revision of the CSS architecture will need to grapple with the tension between the Union's constitutionally authorised discretionary grant power and the States' constitutional interest in fiscal autonomy and administrative sovereignty over their legislative subjects.
