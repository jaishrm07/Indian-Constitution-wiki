officialText: |-
  357. Exercise of legislative powers under Proclamation issued under article 356.—(1) Where by a Proclamation issued under clause (1) of article 356, it has been declared that the powers of the Legislature of the State shall be exercisable by or under the authority of Parliament, it shall be competent—

  (a) for Parliament to confer on the President the power of the Legislature of the State to make laws, and to authorise the President to delegate, subject to such conditions as he may think fit to impose, the power so conferred to any other authority to be specified by him in that behalf;

  (b) for Parliament, or for the President or other authority in whom such power to make laws is vested under sub-clause (a), to make laws conferring powers and imposing duties, or authorising the conferring of powers and the imposition of duties, upon the Union or officers and authorities thereof;

  (c) for the President to authorise when the House of the People is not in session expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of the State pending the sanction of such expenditure by Parliament. [(2) Any law made in exercise of the power of the Legislature of the State by Parliament or the President or other authority referred to in sub-clause

## Why it matters

Article 356 is the provision under which the President may proclaim President's Rule in a State, declaring that the State's constitutional machinery has broken down. The proclamation alone, however, creates a governance vacuum: the elected State Legislature is suspended or dissolved, but the State still needs legislation and funded administration. Article 357 fills that vacuum by specifying the legislative machinery that operates during President's Rule.

Without Article 357, the suspension of the State Legislature would leave the State without a law-making body, unable to enact legislation on the full range of State List subjects. With it, Parliament assumes that legislative role, can delegate it to the President, and the President can sub-delegate further. The result is a chain of authority that ensures legislative continuity throughout the period of central rule.

The continuance clause in Article 357(2) is the most constitutionally significant element. Laws made under this framework are not temporary emergency measures that lapse when President's Rule ends. They continue in force as valid State laws until the restored or re-elected State Legislature amends or repeals them. This means an executive act during President's Rule can have lasting legislative effect — a result that raises fundamental questions about democratic legitimacy and accountability.

## Reading the provision

**Clause 1(a) — delegation to the President.** Parliament may confer on the President the power of the State Legislature to make laws. This is a delegation of Parliament's Article 357 authority, not a direct exercise of that authority by Parliament itself. The President may in turn sub-delegate to any authority, which in practice has meant the Governor acting on the advice of the Union Council of Ministers.

**Clause 1(b) — conferring powers on Union officers.** Parliament, the President, or another delegated authority can make laws that confer powers or impose duties on Union officers with respect to the State's affairs. This enables a parallel Union administrative structure to function in the State during the period of central rule, reinforcing the suspension of normal State administration.

**Clause 1(c) — expenditure without Parliament in session.** The President may authorise expenditure from the State's Consolidated Fund when Parliament is not in session. This is the executive-budget equivalent of Article 116 (votes on account at the Union level), allowing the State's administration to be funded continuously without waiting for parliamentary sanction.

**Clause (2) — the continuance principle.** Laws made under Article 357(1) that the relevant authority would not have been competent to make but for the Article 356 proclamation continue in force after the proclamation ceases. They remain valid until a competent legislature (the restored State Legislature or Parliament where applicable) alters, repeals, or amends them. This is a deliberate design choice: it prevents the legal discontinuity that would result if all legislation enacted during President's Rule automatically expired. The cost is that laws made without a democratically elected state legislature acquire permanent status unless actively reversed.

**Validity contingent on the proclamation.** Because Article 357 powers are derivative of the Article 356 proclamation, the constitutional validity of any law made under Article 357 depends on the validity of the underlying proclamation. If the proclamation is held unconstitutional, the laws made under Article 357 during its operation are also without constitutional authority.

## Key cases

**S R Bommai v Union of India (1994)** — The landmark nine-judge bench ruling on Article 356 has direct implications for Article 357. The Court confirmed that Article 356 proclamations are subject to judicial review and can be struck down if based on irrelevant material, mala fides, or no material at all. Where a proclamation is invalidated, the legislation enacted under Article 357 during that period falls with it. Bommai also held that floor tests should precede dismissal of State governments, thereby reducing the occasions on which Article 357's legislative machinery would be legitimately activated.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court invalidated the President's Rule proclaimed in Bihar in 2005 on the ground that the dissolution of the Assembly before the formation of a government following elections was unconstitutional. The judgment confirmed that laws enacted under Article 357 during an unconstitutional proclamation have no legal standing, reinforcing the derivative nature of Article 357 powers. The Court also reiterated Bommai's requirement that presidential satisfaction must be based on relevant and credible material.

**Minerva Mills v Union of India (1980)** — The Court's holding that emergency-era legislative and executive action must remain within constitutional limits and is subject to judicial review is the foundational principle constraining the exercise of Article 357 powers. In particular, the Court's rejection of the argument that any power granted to Parliament during an emergency is unlimited ensures that even Article 357 legislation must conform to fundamental rights (to the extent not suspended) and the basic structure.

## Current significance

Article 357 has been invoked numerous times since 1950, paralleling India's extensive and contested use of Article 356. President's Rule has been proclaimed more than a hundred times in various States, and legislation and executive orders made under Article 357 in each instance have varying degrees of ongoing effect. Several States have on returning to elected government confronted the legacy of laws, administrative arrangements, and financial commitments made during President's Rule and had to decide whether to continue, amend, or challenge them.

The provision also raises broader constitutional questions about the democratic legitimacy of laws made without an elected legislature. When a State has been under President's Rule for an extended period — as with Jammu and Kashmir between 2018 and 2019 — the volume of legislation enacted under Article 357 by the Parliament and President can be substantial. The reorganisation of Jammu and Kashmir into two Union Territories by the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act 2019 was effected while the State was under President's Rule, concentrating in Parliament legislative powers that would ordinarily require the State Legislature's participation. That exercise, while technically within the Article 357 framework, generated constitutional debate about whether reorganisation of a State's territory could legitimately be accomplished through the Article 357 route.
