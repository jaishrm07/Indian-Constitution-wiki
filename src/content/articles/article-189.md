  189. Voting in Houses, power of Houses to act notwithstanding vacancies and quorum.—(1) Save as otherwise provided in this Constitution, all questions at any sitting of a House of the Legislature of a State shall be determined by a majority of votes of the members present and voting, other than the Speaker or Chairman, or person acting as such. ## What this Article does

Article 189 lays down the fundamental decision-making rules for State Legislature proceedings. The default rule is simple majority of those present and voting, excluding the presiding officer, who holds only a casting vote to break ties. Clause (2) contains an important curative provision: vacancies in membership do not invalidate the House's capacity to act, and subsequent discovery that an unqualified or disqualified person participated in proceedings does not retroactively void those proceedings. Clauses (3) and (4) set the quorum at ten members or one-tenth of total membership — whichever is greater — and impose a duty on the presiding officer to adjourn or suspend the sitting if quorum is lost.

Article 189 is the State-level counterpart to Article 100, which governs voting and quorum in the Houses of Parliament. The two provisions are structurally identical, and the case law developed under either article informs the interpretation of the other.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Voting rule and the casting vote**

The standard voting rule is majority of members present and voting. Abstentions do not count as votes; a member who is present but does not vote is effectively neutral on the question. The presiding officer — Speaker of the Assembly or Chairman of the Council — is excluded from the count of those voting in the first instance. This reflects the convention of the presiding officer's neutrality: the Chair does not take sides on questions before the House.

The casting vote is available only "in the case of an equality of votes" — that is, a tie. It is not a second vote; it is a tie-breaking device. The convention, derived from parliamentary practice, is that the Speaker or Chairman uses the casting vote in favour of the status quo — against change when the House is divided — so that no measure passes by an accidental combination of the Chair's vote with a bare majority of members. In practice, speakers have sometimes used the casting vote for government measures, attracting criticism.

"Save as otherwise provided in this Constitution" preserves the special majority requirements elsewhere in the Constitution. Constitutional amendments under Article 368 require special majorities; resolutions for removal of certain constitutional authorities require prescribed majorities; certain questions in bicameral legislatures require joint sittings or concurrent resolutions. Article 189's simple majority rule is the default, not an override of these express departures.

**Clause (2) — Validity of proceedings despite vacancies and unqualified participation**

Two distinct validating rules operate under clause (2). First, vacancies do not incapacitate the House: a seat left unfilled by death, resignation, disqualification, or delayed by-election does not stop the House from functioning or passing legislation. This is necessary because seats frequently fall vacant mid-session, and requiring the House to be at full strength before acting would be unworkable.

Second — and more consequentially — subsequent discovery that an unqualified person sat, voted, or participated does not void the proceedings. This rule operates even if the person was never entitled to be a member at all: if they were under age, lacked citizenship, or were disqualified at the time of sitting. The practical necessity is obvious: a disqualification adjudication may take years; if the member's participation voided proceedings in the meantime, entire legislative sessions could be challenged.

The clause does not say that disqualification has no effect; it says proceedings already concluded remain valid. Prospectively, once a disqualification is declared, the member can no longer sit or vote. The clause protects the enacted law, not the continuing membership.

**Clauses (3) and (4) — Quorum**

The quorum is the higher of ten members or one-tenth of total House membership. For a House of 200 members, the quorum is twenty. For a small House of seventy members, the quorum is still ten. The State legislature may by law provide a different quorum under clause (3), but in practice few States have done so.

Clause (4) imposes a duty on the presiding officer to adjourn or suspend the meeting when quorum is lost. The language is mandatory — "it shall be the duty" — not discretionary. However, the presiding officer has no mechanism to enforce quorum attendance; the clause can only be given effect by the Chair acting proactively when members absent themselves in numbers. In practice, quorum calls are raised by members as a procedural tactic rather than as genuine enforcement of attendance norms.

## Division of votes and recorded votes

The Constitution specifies the voting rule but not the method of recording votes. Standing Orders of State Legislatures provide for voice votes (ayes and noes on a voice call), division votes (members physically divide into lobbies or press electronic buttons), and recorded votes (names and votes recorded in the official record). A division is demanded when a member challenges the Chair's assessment of the voice vote. Recorded votes are particularly significant for trust votes and constitutional amendment resolutions, where the voting record becomes evidence in subsequent legal proceedings.

## The casting vote in practice

The Speaker's casting vote has figured in several historic State-level confidence crises. When a confidence motion results in a tie — a rare but politically explosive event — the Speaker's casting vote determines whether the government survives. The constitutional convention that the casting vote favours the status quo (i.e., the government remains in office if a no-confidence motion ties) has not been consistently followed across States, and the partisan affiliation of presiding officers has made these moments constitutionally contentious.

## Key cases

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992) 1 SCC 309** — The Supreme Court's landmark anti-defection ruling engaged Article 189's voting rules directly. The Court held that until a disqualification is formally adjudicated and declared, a member's votes in the House are constitutionally valid and proceedings are not vitiated retrospectively under clause (2). This is the foundational precedent for the relationship between ongoing disqualification proceedings and the parliamentary validity of votes cast in the meantime.

**Rajendra Singh Rana v. Swami Prasad Maurya (2007) 4 SCC 270** — The Supreme Court held that when defection-based disqualification petitions are pending, the Speaker cannot sit on the petition indefinitely while the members continue to vote on confidence motions. The Court's analysis of the majority required under Article 189 to constitute a government was conducted against the backdrop of what the genuine constitutional membership of the House should be, taking defection into account — a significant structural observation about how Article 189 voting interacts with the anti-defection provisions.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court, in a nine-judge bench ruling on the Arunachal Pradesh Assembly crisis, addressed the quorum and voting rules of Article 189 in the context of a purported Assembly session convened by the Governor without the Chief Minister's advice. The Court's analysis confirmed that Article 189's procedural rules cannot be deployed in a constitutionally irregular session and that the curative provision in clause (2) does not validate proceedings of a House convened in breach of constitutional procedure.

**Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v. Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2020) 2 SCC 595** — In the Karnataka floor-test crisis, the Court examined the voting rights of members against whom disqualification petitions were pending, ultimately holding that disqualified members lose their right to vote from the date of the disqualification order. The relationship between that prospective disqualification order and Article 189's validation of prior proceedings was central to assessing the constitutional validity of the confidence vote.

## Current significance

Article 189's quorum provisions have been routinely disregarded in most State Assemblies. It is common for fewer than one-tenth of members to be present for ordinary legislative business; proceedings continue without challenge because no member raises a quorum call, and the Chair does not act suo motu. The mandatory language of clause (4) — directing the presiding officer to adjourn for want of quorum — is widely treated as directory rather than imperative in practice, raising questions about legislative legitimacy that courts have been reluctant to adjudicate.

More consequentially, clause (2)'s protection of proceedings from retrospective invalidation is a vital bulwark against the unravelling of legislation whenever disqualification proceedings are initiated against members who voted on major bills. In States with fragile coalition governments and frequent floor-crossing controversies, the constitutional insurance provided by clause (2) ensures that enacted laws do not collapse retroactively when membership disputes are eventually resolved. This function of Article 189 is likely to remain highly relevant as coalition politics and anti-defection proceedings continue to characterise State-level governance.
