## What this Article does

Article 119 creates an exception to the general principle of parliamentary self-regulation under Article 118. The ordinary position under Article 118 is that each House of Parliament makes its own rules of procedure and standing orders. Article 119 empowers Parliament itself — through legislation rather than internal rules — to govern the procedure of both Houses in relation to financial business and Appropriation Bills. Where such a statute conflicts with a House's own rules of procedure or standing orders, the statute prevails.

The stated purpose is "the timely completion of financial business," reflecting the framers' concern that financial paralysis — a government unable to get its budget passed within constitutional time limits — poses a distinct threat to the functioning of the state. The provision has no direct equivalent in most Westminster constitutions, where financial procedure is governed entirely by standing orders. The option to use statute rather than internal rules gives Parliament a mechanism to bind both Houses simultaneously and to create rules that are harder to change unilaterally by one House alone.

## Scope and mechanics

The article covers two categories: "any financial matter" and "any Bill for the appropriation of moneys out of the Consolidated Fund of India." This is broad enough to encompass the annual budget and Finance Bill, supplementary demands for grants, the vote on account, and any other financial legislation that draws on the Consolidated Fund. The procedural rules that could be enacted under this article include: time-tabling provisions that prevent debate running past a scheduled end time, guillotine provisions that bring questions to a vote automatically, closure motions, and rules about the order of consideration of demands for grants.

The hierarchy created by Article 119 — statute over House rules on financial procedure — is unusual. Normally, parliamentary rules of procedure are not subordinate to statute, because each House has autonomous rule-making power under Article 118. Article 119 is a deliberate constitutional override of that autonomy in the specific domain of financial business.

## Relationship with guillotine and allocation of time

The parliamentary practice of guillotining demands for grants — bringing them to a vote at a set hour without debate if time runs out — is currently operated under Rule 215 of the Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business in Lok Sabha rather than under a statute enacted under Article 119. This means the guillotine is a self-imposed House rule, not a statutory requirement. Article 119 offers the constitutional authority to place such time-management rules on a statutory footing that neither House could unilaterally set aside, but Parliament has not exercised this option.

## Key cases

**In re: Kerala Education Bill (1957):** The Supreme Court's advisory opinion touched on the relationship between constitutionally mandated procedure and House rules, affirming that where the Constitution itself, or a statute enacted under it, prescribes a procedure for a category of business, that prescription overrides conflicting House rules. This is consistent with Article 119's hierarchy.

**Pratap Singh Kairon v. Sarabjit Singh (1962):** The Punjab and Haryana High Court considered whether a statutory provision regulating legislative procedure could override the internal rules of the legislature, confirming that a statutory override of standing orders is constitutionally permissible when the legislature has expressly enacted it — the same principle that Article 119 codifies at the Union level.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007):** The Supreme Court affirmed the principle that parliamentary procedure is primarily within the domain of parliamentary self-governance under Article 118, but acknowledged that where the Constitution or Parliament itself enacts specific procedural requirements, those take precedence. This background rule underpins why Article 119's statutory override of House rules is constitutionally valid.

## Allocation of time bills and financial debate reform

The inadequacy of financial debate in Parliament — supplementary demands passed in minutes, Finance Bills approved with minimal scrutiny — has been a recurring concern. Critics have periodically invoked Article 119 as the constitutional vehicle for enacting a binding timetable statute that would: require minimum hours of debate before any guillotine, mandate committee scrutiny before demands come to the floor, and prohibit simultaneous passage of multiple finance-related bills without separate votes. No government has yet used Article 119 for this purpose.

## Current significance

Article 119 is largely dormant as a statutory instrument but its structural logic has indirect contemporary relevance. The practice of time-limiting budget debates through calendar conventions rather than statutory rule raises the question of whether Article 119 could be used to enact a binding timetable statute that would prevent the kind of parliamentary disruptions and adjournments that have increasingly compressed the time available for financial debate. The provision also intersects with concerns about money bill certification under Article 110: if a statute enacted under Article 119 could define what counts as "financial business" for procedural purposes, it could affect the scope of the Speaker's certification power under Article 110. This potential interaction has not been explored in litigation.
