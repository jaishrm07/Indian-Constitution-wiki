## What this Article did

Article 381 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that dealt with the Council of Ministers advising the President during the transitional period. It has been omitted.

The Constitution's Article 74 requires a Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister to aid and advise the President. When the Constitution came into force on 26 January 1950, Jawaharlal Nehru was already serving as Prime Minister of the Dominion of India. Article 381 made provision for the continuation of the existing Council of Ministers — which had been formed under the Government of India Act, 1935 framework — as the Council of Ministers under the new constitutional framework, without requiring a fresh appointment process.

This ensured that the executive government was seamlessly constituted from 26 January 1950, without a gap in the Prime Minister's and Cabinet's authority to govern. The Council of Ministers continued under the new constitutional framework until Parliament was properly constituted after the first general elections.

## Why it was omitted

Article 381 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, along with other transitional articles that had served their purpose. By 1956, the Council of Ministers had operated normally under the constitutional framework for several years and was reconstituted after the first general elections in 1952. The transitional provision was entirely spent.

## Historical context

The continuity of Jawaharlal Nehru's government across the transition from the Dominion of India to the Republic of India was constitutionally and politically essential. Nehru had been Prime Minister since 1947 and commanded a large parliamentary majority in the provisional Parliament. Article 381 gave constitutional sanction to the continuation of his ministry under the new constitutional framework, recognising that the practical functions of government needed to continue without interruption.

This was also important symbolically: the Republic's government on its first day was not a provisional or caretaker arrangement but a fully constituted constitutional Council of Ministers with the authority to act on behalf of the Republic of India.

## Key cases

No constitutional challenge to the validity of the transitional Council of Ministers is recorded. The provision worked smoothly and did not generate litigation.

## Current significance

Article 381 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance is in demonstrating how the framers ensured the seamless transfer of executive authority from the dominion framework to the new Republic, treating the existing Council of Ministers as constitutionally valid under the new Constitution from day one.

The constitutional validation of the Nehru ministry under the new framework was also significant because the Constitution's Article 75 requires that the Prime Minister be a person who commands the confidence of the Lok Sabha. With only a provisional Parliament (not an elected Lok Sabha) in existence from 26 January 1950, the exact constitutional basis for confidence and ministerial responsibility was complex. Article 381 resolved this by treating the existing Council of Ministers as fully constituted, avoiding the need to apply the normal confidence requirement immediately. Once the First Lok Sabha was constituted in 1952, the normal constitutional procedures for ministerial responsibility applied fully.

The central government's legislative agenda during 1950-1952 — conducted under the constitutional framework that Article 381 helped create — included some of the most consequential early legislation of the Republic. The Preventive Detention Act, the Representation of the People Act, early Planning Commission decisions, and the First Five Year Plan framework were all developed under ministerial authority validated by Article 381's transitional provisions. The constitutional legitimacy of these actions and decisions rested, at least in part, on the firm constitutional foundation that Article 381 provided.
