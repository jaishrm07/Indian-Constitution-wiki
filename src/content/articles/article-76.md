  76. Attorney-General for India.—(1) The President shall appoint a person who is qualified to be appointed a Judge of the Supreme Court to be Attorney-General for India.

  (2) It shall be the duty of the Attorney-General to give advice to the Government of India upon such legal matters, and to perform such other duties of a legal character, as may from time to time be referred or assigned to him by the President, and to discharge the functions conferred on him by or under this Constitution or any other law for the time being in force.

## Why it matters

Every government that participates in litigation, seeks constitutional advice, and appears before the highest court needs a chief legal officer. Article 76 constitutionalises that office rather than leaving it to statute, placing it alongside the President, the Cabinet, and Parliament as a named constitutional institution. The qualification requirement — Supreme Court judge-equivalent — ensures that the AG is not a political appointee with minimal legal background but must be among the most senior and technically accomplished lawyers in the country. The right of audience in all courts, and the right to speak in Parliament under Article 88, give the AG a unique constitutional footprint that no purely statutory officer could possess.

The AG occupies an unusual position in the constitutional order — part lawyer, part adviser, part parliamentarian-without-a-vote, but formally none of these in the ordinary sense. The AG is not a judge, not a Minister, not a Member of Parliament. The AG is a constitutional officer of the executive, accountable only to the President (and through Article 74, to the Cabinet), yet appearing before the Supreme Court in a quasi-judicial forum and speaking in Parliament in a quasi-legislative one. This tripartite presence raises important questions about where the AG's professional and constitutional loyalty ultimately lies — and those questions have become more acute in an era of high-profile constitutional litigation where the government's positions are themselves contested before the Court.

The AG's constitutional status matters for another reason: the government's participation in hundreds of cases before the Supreme Court every year — including the most politically sensitive constitutional challenges — depends on the AG's ability to command the Court's confidence. An AG without professional standing or judicial credibility would be ineffective regardless of political connection. The qualification requirement is therefore not merely a symbol of dignity but a functional prerequisite for the office's core job.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Qualification:** The President shall appoint a person qualified to be a Supreme Court judge. Article 124(3) sets three routes to that qualification: being or having been a High Court judge for at least five years, being or having been an advocate of a High Court for at least ten years, or being in the President's view a distinguished jurist. All three routes require either substantial judicial or advocacy experience, ensuring that the AG has the professional standing to argue cases before the Supreme Court and to command the Court's respect when advancing the government's constitutional positions. In practice, Attorneys-General have almost universally been senior advocates of decades' standing.

**Clause (2) — Duties:** The Attorney-General's duties are defined in deliberately open-ended terms — to give legal advice as referred by the President and to discharge constitutional and statutory functions as conferred. The phrase "referred or assigned to him by the President" means (by virtue of Article 74) referred by the Cabinet. The clause also extends to "functions conferred on him by or under this Constitution or any other law", making the AG's mandate expandable by statute without constitutional amendment. Several criminal statutes require the AG's written consent before prosecution can be launched — this is a quasi-prosecutorial function that rests on the Article 76(2) foundation.

**Clause (3) — Right of audience:** The AG has a constitutional right of audience in all courts in the territory of India — not just the Supreme Court. This is a grant from the Constitution itself, not from any court's permission. A senior advocate's right of audience is court-specific; the AG's extends from the Supreme Court to every district court and tribunal without the need for local enrolment or any court's leave. This provision enables the AG to intervene at any level of the judicial hierarchy where the Union's legal interests are engaged.

**Clause (4) — Tenure and remuneration:** The AG holds office during the pleasure of the President — which means, in practice, the pleasure of the Cabinet. There is no fixed tenure, no removal procedure, and no hearing requirement before dismissal. This is the same tenure arrangement as Cabinet Ministers under Article 75(2). In practice, Attorneys-General typically resign when the government changes, treating the office as coextensive with the political tenure of the administration. This convention is not constitutionally required but is well-established. Remuneration is set by the President — again, the Cabinet — and may be changed at will, unlike the salary protections for judges and the CAG.

## The AG and the law officer hierarchy

Article 76 constitutionalises only the AG. The Solicitor General of India (SG) and the Additional Solicitors General (ASGs) are statutory officers whose appointment and powers are governed by executive order. The Law Officers (Conditions of Service) Rules, 1987 sets out the terms of the SG and ASGs' appointment. In practice, the SG and ASGs carry the bulk of day-to-day work in the Supreme Court — appearing in hundreds of cases each term — while the AG reserves appearances for the most significant constitutional cases, Article 143 references, and matters of highest public importance.

This hierarchical arrangement means that the constitutional dignity of Article 76 attaches only to the AG. Courts treat the AG as speaking with particular constitutional authority precisely because of Article 76's placement and the qualification requirements it imposes. When the AG addresses the Court on a constitutional question, the Court treats the submission as reflecting the government's considered legal position, not merely the advocacy strategy of a retained counsel.

The AG also plays a coordinating role among the law officers: in the most significant constitutional matters, the AG briefs the SG and ASGs, agrees the government's legal strategy, and retains final control over the submissions made in the government's name. The AG may also designate Senior Advocates or standing counsel to appear in specific cases, and the AG's chambers serves as the centre of the Union government's litigation strategy across the Supreme Court's docket.

## The Article 88 parliamentary right

Under Article 88, the Attorney-General has the right to speak in, and otherwise take part in the proceedings of, either House of Parliament and any joint sitting of the Houses and any committee thereof of which he may be named a member — but he is not entitled to vote. This constitutional right sets the AG apart from all other legal officers and advocates. No Solicitor General, Additional Solicitor General, or any other lawyer has the right to address Parliament by constitutional grant — only the AG does.

In practice, the AG attends Parliament principally when matters of legal importance arise — including the introduction of constitutional amendments, the tabling of legislation on which the government has sought the AG's advice, and questions about the government's legal positions. The AG may also be called before parliamentary committees to explain the government's legal stance on pending legislation or constitutional disputes. This parliamentary presence gives the AG a role in constitutional development that goes beyond courtroom advocacy: the AG participates in the legislative process as well as the judicial one, making the office a genuine constitutional actor rather than a mere litigation functionary.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debated the qualification threshold for the Attorney-General at some length. Some members favoured a requirement of actual judicial service, reasoning that the AG should be someone with the temperament and experience of a judge rather than merely a successful advocate. Others argued that the most eminent advocates — who had never been judges — were precisely the people best qualified to argue the government's cases before the Supreme Court. The final formulation, importing the Supreme Court judge qualification, was a compromise: it admitted advocates of ten years' High Court standing and distinguished jurists alongside former judges, while ensuring that the AG had professional standing commensurate with the importance of the office. The Constituent Assembly also discussed whether the AG should have security of tenure akin to a judge, but the view prevailed that as a legal officer of the government the AG should hold office at the government's pleasure, as was the practice in comparable common-law jurisdictions.

## Key cases

**In re Special Courts Bill (1978) 1 SCC 380** — An Article 143 reference in which the Attorney-General submitted the government's legal position on the constitutional validity of a proposed Special Courts Bill for emergency-era offences. The case exemplifies the AG's core constitutional function: in reference jurisdiction, the AG represents the government's considered legal position before the full Court on the most sensitive constitutional questions, distinct from the adversarial role in ordinary litigation.

**A.K. Roy v. Union of India (1982) 1 SCC 271** — The AG's arguments on the scope of preventive detention under the National Security Act and Article 22 played a central role in the Court's analysis. The case illustrates the AG as a participant in constitutional development — the AG's submissions shape the Court's understanding of contested constitutional provisions and become part of the interpretive record.

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (1993) 4 SCC 441** — The AG appeared in the Second Judges Case to defend the government's position on judicial appointments and the primacy of the executive in the appointment process. The AG's role in collegium-related litigation illustrates the office's function in the most politically sensitive area of constitutional litigation — where the government's legal interests directly conflict with the judiciary's claimed autonomy — and shows how the AG must balance advocacy for the government with maintaining a relationship of trust with the Court.

**Union Carbide Corporation v. Union of India (1989) 3 SCC 38** — The question of the government's legal capacity to represent the interests of Bhopal victims and to compromise criminal proceedings raised fundamental issues about the scope of the AG's authority. The AG's participation illustrated both the breadth of Article 76(2)'s mandate and the limits imposed by the Court's oversight of settlements affecting public interest.

**K.K. Venugopal as Attorney-General (2017–2022)** — AG Venugopal's tenure generated significant commentary when he declined to give consent to prosecution under the sedition law in certain cases, citing the Supreme Court's pending re-examination of the law's validity. He also publicly expressed reservations about aspects of the government's litigation positions on press freedom cases. These episodes reopened the constitutional question of whether the AG is an independent constitutional officer with professional obligations to the court and to the law, or a client-bound advocate who must advance whatever position the government instructs — a question that Article 76 does not resolve.

**T.R. Andhyarujina as Amicus Curiae in Presidential Reference Cases** — In multiple Article 143 references, former AG T.R. Andhyarujina appeared as amicus curiae, illustrating the practice in which a former AG assists the Court on constitutional questions — a role that demonstrates the AG's long-term relationship with the Court beyond the tenure of any particular government.

## Current significance

The tension between the AG's role as government advocate and as constitutional officer is the defining unresolved question about Article 76. When the government advances a constitutional position that the AG privately believes is untenable, is the AG professionally obligated to withdraw or qualify the argument? The Bar Council of India Rules on professional conduct apply to the AG as to any advocate, but their application is complicated by the office's political accountability. AG Venugopal's public statements during his tenure suggested that an AG can exercise independent professional judgment on questions of constitutional principle — a position that, if accepted as a constitutional convention, would give the AG something closer to the independence of a law officer in common-law systems who can decline to advise on untenable positions.

The relationship between the AG and the consent-to-prosecute power — under statutes such as the Official Secrets Act and the Prevention of Corruption Act — raises distinct issues about the AG's quasi-prosecutorial role. These statutory functions rest on Article 76(2)'s broad formulation and give the AG an executive decision-making role that goes beyond courtroom advocacy. The question of how the AG should exercise this power — and whether it is a political or professional judgment — has not been settled.

The AG's relationship to Attorney-General's functions in constitutional litigation beyond the Union government's interests is also significant. In Article 143 references on questions of constitutional importance, the AG represents the executive's view but the Court also considers it appropriate for the AG to assist the Court in analysing the full constitutional picture. The dual role — advocate for the government and assistant to the Court — is a product of the AG's constitutional status under Article 76 and is one of the most important but least publicly understood aspects of the office.
