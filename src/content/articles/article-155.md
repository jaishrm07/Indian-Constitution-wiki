## Why it matters

Article 155 provides that the Governor of a State shall be appointed by the President by warrant
under hand and seal, making clear that the Governor is a Union appointee rather than a directly
elected official. This mode of appointment is what distinguishes the Indian Governor from the
directly elected governors in the United States model. The Constituent Assembly debated whether
Governors should be directly elected but ultimately settled on presidential appointment to maintain
coordination within the federal scheme and to avoid conflicts between an elected Governor — who
would derive legitimacy from a State-wide mandate — and the elected Chief Minister who heads the
State executive. The framers wanted the Governor to be a constitutional functionary capable of
acting as a bridge between the Union and the State, not a rival power centre with its own electoral
base.

The phrase "by the President" is constitutionally significant. Under Article 74, the President acts
on the aid and advice of the Union Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister in all matters.
The appointment of a Governor under Article 155 is therefore in substance an exercise of the Union
Cabinet's discretion, even though the formal act of appointment is the President's warrant. This
structural reality — that a State's Governor is chosen by the Union executive without any
constitutional requirement of consultation with the State government — is the root cause of the
recurring federal tensions surrounding gubernatorial appointments. It also explains why the Governor
has been described in Supreme Court judgments as a "constitutional functionary" who owes allegiance
to the constitutional order, even while being a creature of the Union executive's choice.

The requirement that the appointment be made "by warrant under his hand and seal" is a formal
constitutional requirement drawn from the tradition of royal prerogative instruments in the
Westminster system. It ensures a documented, authenticated act of appointment and precludes informal
or verbal designations. The warrant establishes the date from which the Governor's term runs under
Article 156 and from which the oath under Article 159 must be administered.

## Reading the provision

The article is brief to the point of terseness — twelve words establish the entire appointment
mechanism for the constitutional head of every State. Four aspects deserve close attention. First,
the President is the appointing authority, but this is nominal under the Article 74 convention.
Second, "warrant under his hand and seal" is the prescribed instrument — no lesser form of executive
action can validly appoint a Governor. Third, the article imposes no conditions on the selection —
it does not require consultation with the Chief Minister, does not bar active politicians, and does
not mandate any particular professional background. Fourth, nothing in Article 155 expressly states
a term or permits removal — those are dealt with in Article 156 — leaving the appointment power as
the sole operative content of this article.

The contrast with the appointment of the President under Article 54 (by an Electoral College
including elected representatives of Parliament and State Assemblies) is stark. The Governor, in
every State, is chosen entirely without reference to the State's own elected institutions. This
design makes the Governor constitutionally dependent on the Union executive from the moment of
appointment — a dependence that is structurally in tension with the Governor's expected role as an
impartial constitutional head who acts on the advice of the State Council of Ministers under
Article 163.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's debates on the Governor's position were among the most contested in the
entire framing process. Several members — including K.T. Shah and A.K. Ayyar — favoured direct
election of Governors on the American model, arguing that an appointed Governor would inevitably
be seen as the Centre's agent rather than as the State's constitutional head. B.R. Ambedkar and
Jawaharlal Nehru defended presidential appointment on the ground that a directly elected Governor
would create a second locus of democratic legitimacy in each State, potentially paralyzing State
governance whenever the Governor and the Chief Minister belonged to different parties.

The final compromise was to make the Governor a presidential appointee while expecting — through
convention rather than legal obligation — that the appointment would be of a person with standing
and impartiality adequate to the office. The absence of any consultation requirement in the text
was a deliberate omission — the framers chose to rely on executive propriety rather than to embed
a procedural constraint that might have created justiciable rights for State governments to
challenge appointments. That reliance on convention has not, in practice, been sufficient to prevent
politically motivated appointments.

## Key cases

**B.P. Singhal v. Union of India (2010) 6 SCC 331** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling on
gubernatorial appointment and removal confirmed that while the President's power to appoint Governors
under Article 155 is broad and the selection process is not justiciable, the removal power implied
in Article 156(1) carries constitutional constraints. The Court held that removal cannot be for
purely political reasons — such as a change of government at the Centre — but acknowledged that
courts cannot review the wisdom of the Union Cabinet's choice at the appointment stage provided the
Article 157 qualifications are met. The judgment is the leading authority on the interplay between
the appointment power in Article 155 and the tenure protection (however limited) in Article 156.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench's broad
discussion of constitutional executive power included observations that the Governor, though
appointed under Article 155, is expected to act in a manner consistent with the constitutional
scheme of parliamentary government — not as a political agent of the appointing authority. The Court
articulated the principle that constitutional functionaries, including the Governor, must subordinate
personal or partisan interests to constitutional duty once in office, even though the appointment
process may have been political.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994) 3 SCC 1** — The nine-judge bench dealing with the scope of
Article 356 discussed the Governor's role in recommending Presidential Rule at length. The Court
emphasised that a Governor who acts as the Centre's political instrument — rather than as a
constitutional head acting on the State Cabinet's advice — violates the constitutional scheme,
regardless of the broad discretion the appointment mechanism in Article 155 gives the Centre. The
judgment indirectly underlines that the mode of appointment does not determine the constitutional
standard of conduct expected once in office.

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker (2016) 8 SCC 1** — The Court examined the Governor's power to
summon the Assembly and held that the Governor acts on the aid and advice of the Chief Minister in
most constitutional functions, reinforcing that the presidential appointment under Article 155 does
not confer independent political authority. The judgment is the latest major ruling on the limits
of gubernatorial discretion, treated against the background of an appointment process that the
Court has consistently held to be beyond judicial review.

## The Sarkaria and Punchhi Commission recommendations

The Sarkaria Commission on Centre-State Relations (1988) recommended a set of conventions governing
the appointment process under Article 155 that remain the most comprehensive articulation of the
normative expectations around gubernatorial selection: the Governor should be a person not connected
with the politics of the State to which they are appointed; they should generally not be an active
politician of the ruling party at the Centre; eminent persons from public life rather than retiring
politicians should be preferred; and — most significantly — the Chief Minister of the concerned
State should ordinarily be consulted before the appointment is made. The Punchhi Commission on
Centre-State Relations (2010) reiterated all of these recommendations with greater urgency, noting
that the appointment process had in practice been used to place politically affiliated figures in
the office, compromising the constitutional neutrality the role requires.

Neither commission's recommendations have been incorporated into Article 155 or into any statute.
All of them operate as non-binding aspirational standards. Courts have consistently held that
challenges to appointments on the ground of failure to follow Sarkaria conventions are
non-justiciable — the appointment power under Article 155 is an executive discretion that courts
will not oversee at the selection stage.

## Current significance

The process by which the Union government nominates Governors under Article 155 has been a subject
of sustained democratic debate. Opposition-governed States frequently allege that appointments are
politically motivated — that Governors are chosen from the ruling party at the Centre or from
political figures aligned with it, and that this compromises their constitutional neutrality. The
post-2014 period and the post-2004 period both saw waves of new gubernatorial appointments shortly
after changes of government at the Centre, as incoming administrations effectively replaced the
incumbents with their own nominees. State governments led by parties in opposition to the Centre
have frequently complained that Governors act as agents of the Union rather than as impartial
constitutional heads. The Tamil Nadu government's protracted conflict with its Governor over
refusals to act on Cabinet advice, and the Kerala government's disputes over gubernatorial
appointments to State university chancellorships, illustrate how the appointment mechanism's
lack of safeguards creates structural friction in federal governance.

The question of whether constitutional amendment is needed to make prior consultation with the State
Chief Minister mandatory, or to bar recent politicians from appointment, or to create an independent
selection mechanism — as several constitutional commissions and parliamentary standing committees
have recommended — has become a live issue in federalism debates. As long as Article 155 remains
as it stands, the appointment power remains one of the most consequential — and least constitutionally
constrained — executive discretions in the Indian federal system.
