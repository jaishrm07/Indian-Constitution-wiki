## Why it matters

Article 39 contains the most substantive economic policy obligations in Part IV. Its six sub-clauses collectively constitute a constitutional charter for economic justice: equal access to livelihood, equitable distribution of the nation's resources, prevention of wealth concentration, equal pay regardless of gender, protection of workers and children from economic exploitation, and the right of children to develop in conditions of freedom and dignity. The framers placed these in Article 39 rather than the Fundamental Rights chapter because they understood that a resource-scarce state in 1950 could not immediately guarantee them as judicially enforceable entitlements — but they wanted the constitutional direction on record as a binding guide to policy.

The six sub-clauses of Article 39 interact with each other and with the Fundamental Rights in ways that have generated the most sustained constitutional jurisprudence of any provision in Part IV. The property rights cases, the nationalisation cases, the equal pay cases, and the child rights cases are all rooted in Article 39.

Sub-clauses (b) and (c) are constitutionally the most consequential. Clause (b) — that the ownership and control of the material resources of the community be so distributed as best to subserve the common good — was the primary constitutional warrant for land reform legislation in the 1950s and 1960s, for nationalisation of banks, coal mines, and other industries in the late 1960s and 1970s, and for the entire post-Nehru development model of State ownership of the commanding heights of the economy. Clause (c) — that the economic system must not result in concentration of wealth and means of production to the common detriment — added a preventive dimension: the State is not merely permitted to redistribute; it is directed to prevent concentrations of private economic power that harm the broader community.

Together, 39(b) and (c) were placed in the Ninth Schedule by early governments to protect land reform legislation from judicial review, generating one of the most sustained constitutional controversies in Indian history — the conflict between property rights under Articles 19(1)(f) and 31 (now repealed) and the distributive programme that Article 39 represents. The Minerva Mills (1980) and Waman Rao (1981) judgments ultimately held that even Ninth Schedule legislation must conform to the basic structure, effectively reasserting judicial review over laws that claimed Article 39(b)/(c) warrant.

Sub-clause (f) — that children be given opportunities and facilities to develop in a healthy manner in conditions of freedom and dignity and that childhood and youth be protected against exploitation — became the judicial foundation, alongside Article 45, for recognising free and compulsory education as a fundamental right in Unni Krishnan J.P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993). That judicial reading preceded and precipitated the 86th Amendment (2002) inserting Article 21A.

## Reading the provision

The provision opens with a direction to the State to "direct its policy towards securing" the listed objectives — language that is deliberately policy-oriented rather than rights-conferring. The sub-clauses use the word "that," each introducing a state of affairs the State must work to bring about: equal livelihood, distributed ownership, non-concentrated wealth, equal pay, protected workers and children, and a healthy environment for child development. This structural choice means that Article 39 is a programme for economic governance, not a set of individual entitlements — but courts have shown that the programme is not merely hortatory.

The distinction between 39(b)'s positive redistribution mandate and 39(c)'s anti-concentration directive is significant. Clause (b) permits and requires the State to redistribute material resources toward the common good; clause (c) targets the private accumulation of economic power that operates to the community's detriment. These are related but distinct constitutional obligations. A government could comply with (b) by ensuring State ownership of key resources while still failing (c) if it permitted monopolistic private concentration elsewhere in the economy. Conversely, breaking up private monopolies satisfies (c) without necessarily distributing resources equitably under (b).

The word "subserve" in clause (b) — that material resources be distributed to "subserve" the common good — is deliberately strong: not merely serve but actively promote the common good. This requires not merely a neutral allocation of resources but one oriented toward the welfare of the community as a whole. The Supreme Court has used this language to assess whether privatisation and natural resource allocation regimes genuinely serve the public interest or merely benefit private actors at the community's expense.

The equal-pay directive in clause (d) — equal pay for equal work for both men and women — has been interpreted by courts not merely as a prohibition on sex-based wage discrimination but as a broader principle that equivalent labour must attract equivalent remuneration regardless of the identity of the person performing it. This reading connects 39(d) to Article 14's equal protection guarantee and has been the basis for extending the principle beyond the male-female comparison to address pay disparities between regular and contractual employees performing the same work. In State of Punjab v. Jagjit Singh (2017), the Supreme Court held that equal pay for equal work is a constitutional right not merely a directive aspiration, taking the principle to near-enforceable status when invoked alongside Articles 14 and 16.

## Key cases

The cases below span the full range of Article 39's sub-clauses — from equal pay, to resource distribution, to education, to child protection — illustrating how each clause has generated its own stream of constitutional jurisprudence.

**Randhir Singh v. Union of India (1982)** — The Supreme Court read Article 39(d) alongside Articles 14 and 16 to hold that employees performing the same or substantially similar duties are entitled to equal remuneration. The Court treated the directive as supplying the substantive content of the equality guarantee, holding that the State cannot maintain pay scales that treat workers performing identical work differently without violating both the constitutional directive and the fundamental right to equality. This judgment established equal pay for equal work as a constitutional principle derived from both Part III and Part IV.

**Unni Krishnan J. P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993)** — A Constitution Bench derived the fundamental right to free and compulsory education for children up to age 14 by reading Articles 41 and 45 alongside Article 39(f). The Court held that a Constitution committed to securing opportunities for children to develop in freedom and dignity, and to protecting them against exploitation and abandonment, necessarily implies an enforceable right to the foundational education without which those constitutional goals are unreachable. The judgment was the direct judicial precursor to the 86th Amendment and Article 21A.

**State of Karnataka v. Ranganatha Reddy (1977)** — In a challenge to the nationalisation of stage carriage services, Justice Krishna Iyer's celebrated concurring opinion gave Article 39(b) and (c) their broadest constitutional reading. He held that "material resources of the community" in 39(b) encompasses all resources — including private ones — that serve the community's needs, and that the State has a constitutional mandate to redistribute them toward the common good. This expansive reading influenced a generation of land reform and nationalisation jurisprudence and was revisited in Jilubhai Nanbhai Khachar (1994) and again in the Property Owners Association case (2024).

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India (1980)** — While the principal holding concerned the basic structure, the Court's treatment of Articles 39(b) and (c) was critical. The majority held that Parliament cannot use these clauses — or any directive principle — to abrogate fundamental rights, and that the harmony between Part III and Part IV is itself a feature of the basic structure. Laws claiming Article 39(b)/(c) protection through Ninth Schedule insertion are not immune from review for violation of the basic structure, meaning the distributive programme the article represents cannot be used as a constitutional sword to destroy the rights framework the Constitution simultaneously guarantees.

## Current significance

Article 39(d)'s equal pay principle is actively litigated in contract labour, scheme workers (anganwadi workers, ASHA workers), and gig economy contexts, with courts regularly asked to extend the principle to informal and semi-formal workers performing functions indistinguishable from regular employees. The principle has been invoked to challenge the two-tier pay structures that have proliferated under government outsourcing. The Supreme Court's rulings in State of Haryana v. Charanjit Singh (2006) and State of Punjab v. Jagjit Singh (2017) have progressively strengthened the equal pay principle across contractual and casual government employment.

Article 39(b) and (c) continue to frame debates about natural resource allocation — spectrum, coal blocks, river water — following the Supreme Court's decision in the 2G Spectrum case (2012), which annulled licences granted without competitive bidding and cited the constitutional obligation to distribute national resources in the common good. The Property Owners Association case, argued before a nine-judge bench, revisited the foundational question of what counts as a "material resource of the community" under 39(b), with the majority in 2024 endorsing a more restrained reading than Justice Krishna Iyer's broad formulation. The majority held that not every privately owned resource automatically becomes a material resource of the community subject to redistribution under 39(b); the determination requires context, nature, and public impact of the resource in question. This recalibration represents a significant shift in the property-redistribution jurisprudence that 39(b) had anchored for decades.

The protection of children under 39(e) and (f) is the constitutional foundation for the prohibition on child labour and for the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009, and is regularly invoked in challenges to State failures to provide quality elementary schooling. Article 39(f)'s requirement that children develop in conditions of freedom and dignity has also been used to challenge corporal punishment in schools, child marriage legislation gaps, and the exclusion of children from quality schooling due to economic circumstance or caste discrimination.

Article 39(e)'s protection of workers from being forced by economic necessity into avocations unsuited to their age or strength is the constitutional basis for minimum-age requirements in hazardous occupations and for the prohibition on bonded labour — connecting directly to Article 23's prohibition on forced labour and the Supreme Court's jurisprudence treating economic compulsion as constitutional coercion.

The interaction between Article 39(b)/(c) and the basic structure doctrine is the most legally significant ongoing question surrounding Article 39. While Minerva Mills held that directive principles cannot override fundamental rights, the Supreme Court in the Property Owners Association case (2024) clarified that not every privately owned resource constitutes a "material resource of the community" and that a State seeking to redistribute private property under the 39(b) banner must demonstrate that the resource in question genuinely serves the community in a way that warrants redistribution. This calibration of the scope of 39(b) has major implications for land acquisition, nationalisation, and resource allocation jurisprudence going forward.

The constitutional significance of Article 39(a)'s right to an adequate means of livelihood grows as automation, platform labour, and the gig economy restructure the relationship between work and income. When workers lack access to an adequate livelihood because of the structure of labour markets rather than individual failure, the State's Article 39(a) obligation to direct policy toward securing that right becomes a constitutional mandate for labour market regulation. Courts have used Article 39(a) in conjunction with Article 21 to require the State to address structural unemployment and to maintain programmes that guarantee access to livelihood for those excluded from formal employment.

Article 39 in combination with Articles 14, 15, and 16 creates a constitutional framework for addressing gendered economic inequality. The equal-pay directive in clause (d) intersects with the equality guarantees to require not merely formal wage equality but attention to the structural forces — occupational segregation, care burdens, informal employment — that produce gendered wage gaps. Courts and legislative committees have used this intersection to argue for pay transparency requirements, equal value assessments, and targeted measures to address the structural determinants of the gender pay gap. Article 39 thus remains one of the most active directive principles in contemporary constitutional adjudication — its clauses invoked across labour law, property law, environmental law, and children's rights to give constitutional content to India's ongoing struggle with economic inequality.
