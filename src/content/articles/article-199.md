## What this Article does

Article 199 defines what counts as a "Money Bill" for the purposes of the state legislature's financial procedure in Articles 198 and 207. It is the state-level counterpart to Article 110, which defines Money Bills at the Union level. The two definitions are materially identical in their seven-category enumeration, reflecting the constitutional design's symmetry between Union and State financial procedure.

A Bill is a Money Bill only if it contains provisions dealing exclusively with one or more of: taxation; State borrowing and guarantees; the Consolidated Fund or Contingency Fund of the State; appropriation from the Consolidated Fund; expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund; receipts and custody in the State's public accounts; or matters incidental to the foregoing. The crucial word is "only": a Bill that deals with financial matters alongside other substantive policy matters — such as creating a new regulatory body that also has funding provisions — is not a Money Bill under Article 199, because it does not contain "only" financial provisions.

Clause (2) carves out from the definition Bills that merely impose fines, require payment of licence fees, or regulate local taxation — ensuring that routine regulatory legislation with financial aspects is not swept into the Money Bill category. Clause (3) vests in the Speaker of the Legislative Assembly the final and conclusive power to certify whether a Bill is a Money Bill. Clause (4) requires the Speaker's signed certificate to be physically endorsed on the Bill when it is transmitted to the Council and when it is presented to the Governor for assent — a formal requirement that makes the certification visible in the legislative record.

The Speaker's certification under clause (3) is declared "final" — language that raises the same question as Article 110(3)'s identical formulation at the Union level: whether the finality of the Speaker's decision means it is entirely beyond judicial review, or merely that it cannot be questioned in routine proceedings, while remaining subject to review for constitutional infirmity.

## Key cases

**K. S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2019) — Aadhaar Act (Union-level parallel)** — While decided under Article 110 (the Union definition), the five-judge bench's divided opinion on whether a Bill certified as a Money Bill was validly so certified is directly relevant to the interpretation of Article 199. The majority upheld the Speaker's certification; Justice D. Y. Chandrachud's dissent argued that the "only provisions" test must be genuinely applied and that a Speaker cannot certify a Bill as a Money Bill if it plainly contains provisions outside the enumerated categories. The same analytical framework governs Article 199.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019)** — A five-judge bench referred to a larger bench the question of what can legitimately be included in a Union Money Bill under Article 110. The constitutional questions raised — about the scope of the "only" requirement and the reviewability of the Speaker's certificate — apply with equal force to Article 199 at the state level, and the eventual ruling of the larger bench will likely shape how state-level Money Bill certifications are assessed.

**State Finance Act challenges** — In several states, Opposition parties in Legislative Councils have challenged the inclusion of non-financial provisions in State Finance Bills certified as Money Bills, arguing that the certification was colourable and designed solely to bypass Council scrutiny. Courts have declined to interfere in most such cases on the basis of the finality clause in Article 199(3), but the Puttaswamy dissent has emboldened counsel to renew such challenges.

## Current significance

The practical significance of Article 199 lies in the boundary it draws between legislation that can be processed under the Assembly-dominant procedure of Article 198 and legislation that must go through the full bicameral process (and, in the event of disagreement, the Article 197 override route). As State governments have become more sophisticated about using the State Finance Bill as a vehicle for substantive policy changes — inserting provisions about regulatory bodies, service conditions, or administrative reorganisation into annual Finance Acts — the "only provisions" test in Article 199(1) has come under increasing strain.

The Speaker's final certification also raises a conflict-of-interest concern at the state level: the Speaker is a member of the ruling party in the Assembly, and certifying a Bill as a Money Bill directly benefits the Assembly majority by removing Council scrutiny. In States where the ruling coalition commands the Assembly but not the Council, the incentive to use Money Bill certification as a procedural bypass is substantial. The constitutional correction for this structural tension — a genuinely independent Speakership or an external body to assess Money Bill classification — has not been implemented in any Indian State.
