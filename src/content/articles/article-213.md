  213. Power of Governor to promulgate Ordinances during recess of Legislature.—(1) If at any time, except when the Legislative Assembly of a State is in session, or where there is a Legislative Council in a State, except when both Houses of the Legislature are in session, the Governor is satisfied that circumstances exist which render it necessary for him to take immediate action, he may promulgate such Ordinances as the circumstances appear to him to require:

  Provided that the Governor shall not, without instructions from the President, promulgate any such Ordinance if—

  (a) a Bill containing the same provisions would under this Constitution have required the previous sanction of the President for the introduction thereof into the Legislature; or

  (b) he would have deemed it necessary to reserve a Bill containing the same provisions for the consideration of the President; or

  (c) an Act of the Legislature of the State containing the same provisions would under this Constitution have been invalid unless, having been reserved for the consideration of the President, it had received the assent of the President.

  (2) An Ordinance promulgated under this article shall have the same force and effect as an Act of the Legislature of the State assented to by the Governor, but every such Ordinance—

  (a) shall be laid before the Legislative Assembly of the State, or where there is a Legislative Council in the State, before both the Houses, and shall cease to operate at the expiration of six weeks from the reassembly of the Legislature, or if before the expiration of that period a resolution disapproving it is passed by the Legislative Assembly and agreed to by the Legislative Council, if any, upon the passing of the resolution or, as the case may be, on the resolution being agreed to by the Council; and

  (b) may be withdrawn at any time by the Governor.

  Explanation.—Where the Houses of the Legislature of a State having a Legislative Council are summoned to reassemble on different dates, the period of six weeks shall be reckoned from the later of those dates for the purposes of this clause.

  (3) If and so far as an Ordinance under this article makes any provision which would not be valid if enacted in an Act of the Legislature of the State assented to by the Governor, it shall be void:

  Provided that, for the purposes of the provisions of this Constitution relating to the effect of an Act of the Legislature of a State which is repugnant to an Act of Parliament or an existing law with respect to a matter enumerated in the Concurrent List, an Ordinance promulgated under this article in pursuance of instructions from the President shall be deemed to be an Act of the Legislature of the State which has been reserved for the consideration of the President and assented to by him.

## Why it matters

Article 213 is the State-level counterpart of Article 123, which confers the same ordinance power on the President at the Union level. Both articles represent a constitutional exception to the principle that law-making authority belongs to the legislature: they allow the executive to promulgate temporary legislation when the legislature is not in session and circumstances genuinely require immediate action. The power is structurally time-limited — an Ordinance ceases to operate after six weeks of the Legislature's reassembly unless the Legislature approves it — and functionally constrained: the Governor cannot promulgate an Ordinance making provisions that could not validly be enacted as a State Act. The most important judicial contribution in this area has been the Supreme Court's condemnation of re-promulgation — the practice of withdrawing an Ordinance just before the Legislature reassembles and immediately re-issuing it, indefinitely avoiding legislative scrutiny — as a fraud on the Constitution.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) authorises the Governor to promulgate an Ordinance when he is "satisfied that circumstances exist which render it necessary for him to take immediate action" and the Legislature is not in session. The satisfaction requirement is not purely subjective; it is justiciable, though courts apply a narrow standard of review. The proviso to clause (1) restricts the Governor: for the categories of legislation that require either prior Presidential sanction for introduction, reservation for Presidential consideration, or Presidential assent to be valid, the Governor cannot promulgate an Ordinance without Presidential instructions. This means that an Ordinance in a Concurrent List area touching Union law supremacy, or on a State List subject affecting Union interests, requires prior clearance from the Centre. Clause (2)(a) requires every Ordinance to be laid before the Legislature on reassembly; it ceases to operate after six weeks from that reassembly unless approved by a resolution, or earlier if the Assembly passes a disapproving resolution. Clause (2)(b) allows the Governor to withdraw an Ordinance at any time. Clause (3) provides that an Ordinance is void to the extent it makes provisions that would be unconstitutional if enacted by the State Legislature.

## Key cases

**D.C. Wadhwa v State of Bihar (1987)** — The Supreme Court condemned the Bihar government's systematic practice of re-promulgating Ordinances session after session without presenting them to the Legislature, some having run for up to fourteen years through consecutive re-promulgation. The Court held that this practice constitutes a fraud on the Constitution and a subversion of the democratic legislative process. The power to promulgate an Ordinance is an emergency power, not a parallel legislative route, and the Governor's continued satisfaction cannot substitute for legislative approval. This case is the foundational authority on the unconstitutionality of serial re-promulgation.

**Krishna Kumar Singh v State of Bihar (2017)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench revisited and significantly deepened D.C. Wadhwa. The Court held: (i) the Governor promulgates Ordinances on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers, and that advice is subject to judicial review to determine whether the preconditions for Ordinance power — legislative recess and immediate necessity — are met; (ii) where an Ordinance lapses or is disapproved, rights and obligations created or extinguished under it do not automatically survive and may require fresh legislative validation; (iii) a Chief Minister or Cabinet that advises re-promulgation to avoid legislative scrutiny acts unconstitutionally; and (iv) courts can examine whether the satisfaction requirement was genuinely met. The judgment applies fully to State Governors under Article 213.

**T. Venkata Reddy v State of Andhra Pradesh (1985)** — The Andhra Pradesh case raised the question whether fundamental rights challenges could be brought against Ordinances under Article 213. The Court confirmed that an Ordinance has the same force as a State Act and is therefore subject to the same fundamental rights constraints as legislation, meaning Ordinances can be challenged on Articles 14, 19, and 21 grounds just as State Acts can.

**K.T. Plantation Private Ltd v State of Karnataka (2011)** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench addressed the ordinance-making power under Articles 123 and 213 together and held that the Governor's satisfaction is subject to judicial review on the grounds of whether there was a genuine emergency requiring immediate action, even though the review standard is narrow. The power is not a parallel legislative track available at executive convenience; it is strictly conditioned on the absence of a legislative session and the existence of circumstances requiring action that cannot await the Legislature's reassembly.

## Current significance

The re-promulgation vice condemned in D.C. Wadhwa has not been eliminated. Several States have replicated the Bihar pattern of extending Ordinances through serial re-promulgation on politically sensitive subjects — including land acquisition, religious conversion regulation, and reservation policy — where the government anticipates legislative resistance. Krishna Kumar Singh (2017) has raised the constitutional cost of re-promulgation by questioning the survival of rights created under lapsed Ordinances, but enforcement depends on litigation that may come years after the relevant transactions. The Article 213(1) proviso requiring Presidential instructions for certain categories of Ordinance creates friction in States where the Governor and the elected government are in political conflict: a Governor can decline to seek or act on Cabinet advice to promulgate an Ordinance, and without Presidential instructions for reserved categories, the Ordinance cannot be issued. The Supreme Court in Krishna Kumar Singh confirmed that Cabinet advice on Ordinance promulgation is justiciable, but real-time enforcement of the duty to follow that advice remains constitutionally underdeveloped.
