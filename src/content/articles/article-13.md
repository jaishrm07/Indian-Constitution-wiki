Laws inconsistent with or in derogation of the fundamental rights.—(1) All laws in force in the territory of India immediately before the commencement of this Constitution, in so far as they are inconsistent with the provisions of this Part, shall, to the extent of such inconsistency, be void.

## Why it matters

Every right declared in Part III would be hollow without a provision that converts the declaration into operative law. Article 13 provides that conversion. It gives constitutional rights their bite by specifying that any law — past or future — that conflicts with fundamental rights is void to the extent of the conflict. Without Article 13, Parliament could theoretically enact statutes that nullified the rights guaranteed in Articles 14 to 32, and courts would have no textual basis for review. With Article 13, the supremacy of fundamental rights over ordinary legislation is constitutionally mandated, not merely asserted.

The practical consequence of Article 13 is that every person who believes a law has violated their fundamental right has a constitutional basis to ask a court to declare that law void. This is not a discretionary power of courts but a constitutional obligation. When the Supreme Court reviews the validity of an Act of Parliament against Part III, it is exercising the power and discharging the duty that Article 13(2) creates. India has strong-form judicial review of legislation — courts can strike down laws — and Article 13 is the textual foundation of that power.

Article 13 also has a temporal dimension that makes it unusual among constitutional provisions. Clause (1) addresses the vast body of law that existed when the Constitution came into force in 1950 — the colonial-era statutes, the provincial regulations, the customs and usages that had governed India for generations. It says that these laws survive but are infected — they become void to the extent they conflict with fundamental rights. This produced two important doctrines: the doctrine of eclipse (the pre-constitutional law is not destroyed but is temporarily eclipsed and can revive if the relevant fundamental right is later amended or removed) and the doctrine of severability (only the offending part is void; the rest of the law survives if it is capable of separate operation).

The provision has one profound omission that became the source of the most important constitutional crisis in Indian legal history: it does not explicitly address whether constitutional amendments themselves are "laws" subject to Article 13. That silence was the subject of prolonged conflict between Parliament and the Supreme Court, resolved eventually through a compromise that shaped the constitutional order to the present day.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) deals with pre-constitutional laws — the laws "in force" immediately before 26 January 1950. The definition of "laws in force" in clause (3)(b) is deliberately broad, encompassing even laws that were not then in operation in all areas. This ensures that dormant or regionally-limited pre-constitutional laws cannot escape Part III scrutiny simply because they were not actively applied at the time of commencement.

The operative consequence of clause (1) is automatic voidness to the extent of inconsistency. The pre-constitutional law does not need to be formally repealed or struck down by a court — the constitutional voidness operates immediately on commencement. The doctrine of eclipse, developed in Bhikaji Narain Dhakras v. State of Madhya Pradesh (1955), adds a nuance: the void pre-constitutional law remains dormant but is not entirely erased from the legal universe. If the fundamental right with which it conflicted is later amended to remove or narrow the right, the previously void law can revive to the extent that the conflict no longer exists.

Clause (2) operates differently. It prohibits the State from making future laws that abridge fundamental rights. Unlike clause (1), which treats pre-constitutional laws as automatically void at commencement, clause (2) voids post-constitutional laws at the moment of their enactment — they are void from birth, not just from the date of a court declaration. The court declaration merely acknowledges the already-existing voidness.

The definition of "law" in clause (3)(a) is exceptionally broad. It includes ordinances, orders, by-laws, rules, regulations, notifications, customs, and usages — effectively the entire regulatory and norm-producing output of the state apparatus. This means that delegated legislation, executive instructions, administrative circulars having binding force, and even long-standing customary practices can be tested against Part III through Article 13. The breadth of this definition has significant practical consequences for the enforcement of constitutional rights against regulatory action.

The doctrine of severability — drawn from the phrase "to the extent of such inconsistency" in clauses (1) and (2) — is itself a significant doctrinal tool. Where part of a legislative provision is consistent with fundamental rights and part is not, courts sever the void part and allow the rest to survive if it is operationally capable of standing alone. The question of what can be severed requires examining whether the valid and invalid portions are so interwoven that the legislature would not have enacted the valid part without the invalid part — if so, the entire provision falls.

## Key cases

### Establishing judicial review: the early years

**State of Madras v. V.G. Row (1952)** — The Criminal Law Amendment Act gave the government power to declare any association unlawful, with no opportunity for the association to be heard before the declaration was made and no judicial review of the declaration. The Supreme Court struck down the provision as violating Article 19(1)(c)'s freedom of association. More significantly, the Court confirmed that Article 13 is the textual anchor of judicial review of legislation — courts are constitutionally required, not merely permitted, to review the validity of post-constitutional law against Part III. Justice Patanjali Sastri observed that this power of judicial review is the most important function of the Supreme Court and must be exercised with strictness commensurate with its grave responsibility.

**Bhikaji Narain Dhakras v. State of Madhya Pradesh (1955)** — The C.P. and Berar Motor Vehicles (Amendment) Act 1947, a pre-constitutional law, was challenged as violating Article 19(1)(g)'s right to trade. The Court upheld the challenge but articulated the doctrine of eclipse: the pre-constitutional law was not permanently void — it was merely eclipsed by the inconsistent fundamental right. If the right were amended, the eclipsed law would re-emerge. This doctrine was important in the early decades when fundamental rights were subject to frequent constitutional amendments and the revival question was practically significant.

**R.M.D.C. (Mysore) Private Ltd. v. Union of India (1957)** — The Court articulated the doctrine of severability in clear terms: when part of a statute is unconstitutional and part is not, the court should sever the unconstitutional portion and preserve the constitutional remainder, provided the statute is capable of such surgical operation. The test for severability is legislative intent — would the legislature have enacted the valid part independently of the invalid part? If yes, severance is appropriate; if no, the whole statute falls.

### The great contest: amendments as "law"

**I.C. Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967)** — The Punjab Security of Land Tenures Act 1953 had been inserted into the Ninth Schedule and the Constitution had been amended to place it beyond challenge. The question was whether a constitutional amendment enacted under Article 368 was "law" within the meaning of Article 13(2) and could therefore be struck down if it abridged fundamental rights. An eleven-judge bench held, by six to five, that it was. Chief Justice Subba Rao, writing for the majority, reasoned that Article 13(2) speaks of "any law" made by "the State," and Article 12 defines "the State" to include Parliament. Parliament acts as the State even when exercising its constituent power. Constitutional amendments are therefore "laws" within Article 13(2) and void if they abridge fundamental rights. The majority prospectively overruled its earlier decisions and held that Parliament had no power to amend Part III.

The Golaknath decision created a constitutional crisis. If Parliament could not amend fundamental rights, the entire project of land reform, bank nationalisation, and poverty-alleviation legislation — which Courts had repeatedly struck down as violating the right to property — was constitutionally impossible. The political response was forceful: the Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act 1971 inserted clause (4) into Article 13, specifically providing that nothing in Article 13 applies to any amendment made under Article 368. This was a direct parliamentary response to Golaknath, seeking to remove constitutional amendments from the scope of Article 13.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The most consequential case in Indian constitutional history. A thirteen-judge bench was assembled to reconsider Golaknath. By seven to six, the Court overruled Golaknath and upheld the 24th Amendment's insertion of Article 13(4) — constitutional amendments are not "law" within Article 13 and cannot be struck down for abridging fundamental rights. In this respect, Parliament's legislative response to Golaknath succeeded. However — and this is what transformed constitutional history — the Court simultaneously held that while Article 13 does not limit amendment power, there is an inherent limitation on that power: Parliament cannot use Article 368 to destroy the "basic structure" of the Constitution. The basic structure doctrine, created in Kesavananda as a replacement for the Golaknath rule, holds that certain features of the Constitution are so fundamental — the supremacy of the Constitution, judicial review, federalism, separation of powers, fundamental rights as a class — that they cannot be abrogated even by a constitutional amendment. Article 13(4) removed amendments from the scope of Article 13, but Kesavananda created a constitutional limit that operates independently of Article 13.

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India (1980)** — The Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act 1976, enacted during the Emergency, had among other things inserted Article 31C in a form that immunised laws implementing Directive Principles from any challenge under Part III, and had amended Article 368 to declare that no court could question the validity of any constitutional amendment. The Court struck down both these provisions. Article 31C as amended was struck down because it effectively destroyed judicial review of legislation — any law, however unrelated to the Directive Principles it purported to implement, was beyond challenge. The amendment to Article 368 was struck down because removing judicial review of constitutional amendments destroyed the basic structure itself. Article 13's general scope was not directly in issue, but the case confirmed that the basic structure doctrine is an independent, judicially-enforceable limit on Parliament's constituent power, operating alongside (and partly replacing) Article 13's limitation on ordinary legislative power.

### Subordinate legislation and executive action

**State of Punjab v. Dalbir Singh (2012)** — The Court reaffirmed that rules and regulations made under statutes fall within the definition of "law" in Article 13(3)(a) and must conform to Part III. A rule made under a parent Act that conflicts with a fundamental right is void even if the parent Act itself is constitutionally valid. This has significant practical implications for delegated legislation — rule-making authorities cannot, through subordinate instruments, do what the parent statute itself could not do.

**State of Bihar v. Bihar Distillery Ltd. (1997)** — The Court confirmed that executive orders and notifications having the force of law also fall within Article 13(3)(a) and are subject to fundamental rights review. The State cannot evade constitutional scrutiny by framing measures as executive orders rather than formal legislation — if the order has binding legal force, it is "law" within Article 13.

## Current significance

Article 13 remains the textual anchor for every constitutional challenge to legislation in India. Its continuing importance is felt in at least three distinct areas.

The debate over subordinate legislation continues to expand. As regulators, tribunals, and government departments issue binding directions, standards, and circulars without following formal legislative process, the question of whether these instruments qualify as "law" within Article 13 — and are therefore subject to Part III challenge — arises with increasing frequency. The Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules 2021, SEBI regulations, TRAI directives, and similar instruments are all potentially subject to Article 13 scrutiny if they affect the freedoms guaranteed by Part III.

The Ninth Schedule controversy — the practice of inserting laws into the Ninth Schedule to immunise them from Article 13 challenge — was substantially resolved by the Supreme Court in I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007), which held that laws inserted into the Ninth Schedule after Kesavananda Bharati are subject to review to the extent they violate the basic structure, including the fundamental rights chapter as it stood after Kesavananda. This decision means that the Ninth Schedule is no longer a complete immunity from constitutional review, though the precise limits of its operation remain under discussion.

The relationship between Article 13 and the personal laws — Hindu, Muslim, and other religious personal laws that were not formally enacted by a legislature but derive from custom, usage, and religious text — is an area of continuing doctrinal uncertainty. Article 13(3)(a) includes "custom or usage having in the territory of India the force of law," which would bring personal law usages within the scope of fundamental rights review. The Court has hesitated to apply this reasoning fully, partly on grounds of religious freedom and partly from institutional caution about adjudicating religious doctrine. The question whether personal law customs that discriminate on grounds of sex or caste are "laws" void under Article 13 remains one of the most important unresolved issues in Indian constitutional law.
