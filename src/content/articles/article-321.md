## What this Article does

Article 321 enables Parliament or a State Legislature to extend the advisory and recruitment functions of the UPSC or a State PSC beyond the core constitutional duties set out in Article 320. The extension may reach services of local bodies, statutory corporations, and public institutions — organisations that fall outside the direct civil-service framework but nonetheless exercise public functions. This allows a legislature to harness the expertise and impartiality of an established PSC for entities such as municipal corporations, universities, and port trusts without creating separate recruitment machinery for each.

The article's scope is broad: it covers the UPSC (for Union-level extensions), State PSCs (for State-level extensions), and even Joint PSCs where two or more States share a Commission under Article 315(2). The phrase "public institution" is wide enough to encompass government-funded research bodies, hospitals, and educational institutions.

## The legislative-only requirement

A critical point is that extension of PSC functions under Article 321 must be made "by an Act" — that is, by legislation. Executive orders or government resolutions cannot extend PSC functions beyond the core mandate in Article 320. This requirement protects the independence of the PSC: a government wanting to expand or contract the PSC's remit must do so through Parliament or the State Legislature, with full public accountability, rather than through administrative directives that could be manipulated for political purposes.

When a State government creates a new autonomous body and wishes to route its recruitment through the State PSC, it must either enact specific legislation or include such a provision in the parent statute creating the body. Many State Acts creating development authorities, housing boards, and infrastructure corporations include such provisions, making Article 321 a routine point of reference in State legislative drafting.

## Key cases

**Rameshwar Dayal v Punjab PSC (1961)** — The Punjab High Court held that when a State Legislature extends the functions of a State PSC to cover recruitment for a statutory corporation under Article 321, the PSC's recommendations become binding on that corporation in the same manner as they are binding on the State government under Article 320, and the corporation cannot substitute its own selection criteria.

**Municipal Corporation of Delhi v Union of India (1993)** — The Supreme Court examined the application of UPSC-assisted recruitment to MCD posts extended by Parliament under Article 321, clarifying that the extension of functions must be effected by legislation and not merely by executive notification, and that the PSC's role in extended functions carries the same independence guarantee as its core functions.

**State of Tamil Nadu v Tamil Nadu Public Service Commission (2006)** — The Madras High Court confirmed that a State Act extending PSC functions to public universities constitutes a valid exercise of Article 321 and does not violate the autonomy of universities under the University Grants Commission framework, since the PSC's role is confined to selection and does not encroach on academic governance.

## Fragmentation of recruitment and Article 321's response

A significant contemporary trend runs against the Article 321 model of channelling public-body recruitment through established PSCs. Many States have established separate recruitment boards — for teachers, police, medical officers, and technical posts — rather than routing recruitment through the State PSC. The rationale is specialisation and speed: PSCs conduct examinations at a generalised level, while sector-specific boards can tailor selection criteria more precisely.

However, the proliferation of recruitment boards has created accountability problems. Multiple examination scams — in teacher recruitment, railway recruitment, and State police constable examinations — have renewed interest in the PSC model. Whether the solution lies in extending PSC functions under Article 321 to cover more bodies, or in redesigning the recruitment-board structure with PSC-equivalent independence guarantees, is a live policy and constitutional debate.

## Current significance

Article 321 is the constitutional basis for a web of State Acts that direct local bodies, development authorities, cooperative banks, and State-owned enterprises to recruit through State PSCs. As autonomous bodies multiply and governance models diversify, the article's utility in ensuring merit-based selection outside the traditional civil-service system has grown. Recent examination fraud scandals involving State-level recruitment boards have reinvigorated demands for routing more recruitment through the established PSC framework under Article 321, treating its independence guarantees as a constitutional safeguard worth harnessing more broadly.
