officialText: |-
  243ZD. Committee for district planning.—(1) There shall be constituted in every State at the district level a District Planning Committee to consolidate the plans prepared by the Panchayats and the Municipalities in the district and to prepare a draft development plan for the district as a whole.

  (2) The Legislature of a State may, by law, make provision with respect to—

  (a) the composition of the District Planning Committees;

  (b) the manner in which the seats in such Committees shall be filled:

  Provided that not less than four-fifths of the total number of members of such Committee shall be elected by, and from amongst, the elected members of the Panchayat at the district level and of the Municipalities in the district in proportion to the ratio between the population of the rural areas and of the urban areas in the district;

  (c) the functions relating to district planning which may be assigned to such Committees;

  (d) the manner in which the Chairpersons of such Committees shall be chosen.

  (3) Every District Planning Committee shall, in preparing the draft development plan,—

  (a) have regard to—

  (i) matters of common interest between the Panchayats and the Municipalities including spatial planning, sharing of water and other physical and natural resources, the integrated development of infrastructure and environmental conservation; (ii) the extent and type of available resources whether financial or otherwise;

  (b) consult such institutions and organisations as the Governor may, by order, specify.

  (4) The Chairperson of every District Planning Committee shall forward the development plan, as recommended by such Committee, to the Government of the State.

## Why it matters

The Seventy-Fourth Amendment's architects recognised that urban and rural planning cannot be carried out in isolation — water supply, spatial planning, waste management, infrastructure, and environmental conservation cross the rural-urban boundary and require integrated district-level coordination. Article 243ZD creates the District Planning Committee as the constitutional institution for that integration. The DPC must consolidate the plans of Panchayats and municipalities within a district and produce a single draft development plan. The four-fifths elected-member requirement ensures that the DPC is driven by locally elected representatives rather than State officials, in proportion to the rural and urban populations of the district. The DPC's draft plan goes to the State Government, creating a constitutional channel through which local priorities are formally fed into State planning. In practice, DPCs have been among the most neglected of the 73rd and 74th Amendment institutions: many States have not constituted them at all, those that exist often lack staff, funds, and effective planning authority, and State governments routinely bypass them through sector-specific departments and Union-sponsored missions.

## How courts use it

Courts have received petitions seeking mandamus to compel States to constitute DPCs, on the basis that the word "shall" in Article 243ZD(1) creates a mandatory obligation. High Courts have generally agreed that the obligation to constitute a DPC is not discretionary and have issued directions to State governments to comply. The more difficult question — whether a DPC, once constituted, has enforceable planning authority that can bind the State government's sectoral decisions — has not been definitively resolved. Courts have tended to read the DPC's plan-forwarding function as consultative rather than binding on the State government, though they have acknowledged that ignoring DPC recommendations entirely may be inconsistent with the constitutional design. The overlap between DPC jurisdiction over spatial planning and the powers of State development authorities, urban development authorities, and industrial corridor bodies creates recurring jurisdictional disputes.

## Key cases

**Mohd. Aslam v. Union of India (2003) — Local body elections and planning structures** — The Supreme Court's observations about the mandatory character of the 74th Amendment's institutional requirements — including the obligation to constitute planning committees — have been applied by High Courts to compel States to establish DPCs. The ruling stands for the broader proposition that the constitutional obligation to constitute the institutions of urban and rural self-government is non-derogable and is subject to judicial enforcement.

**State of Karnataka v. Rajashree Rangarajan (2017) — DPC composition and elected representation** — The Karnataka High Court, dealing with a challenge to the composition of a District Planning Committee, held that the four-fifths elected-member requirement under Article 243ZD(2) is a mandatory constitutional floor that State law cannot undercut. The State's attempt to fill DPC positions primarily with State officials and nominated members was struck down as inconsistent with the constitutional provision requiring election by and from among elected Panchayat and municipal members.

**In re: District Planning Committees (various High Court proceedings, 2015–2022)** — Multiple High Courts, including those of Kerala, Maharashtra, and Himachal Pradesh, have issued directions to State governments to constitute or reconstitute DPCs, to provide them with dedicated staff and funding, and to ensure that the DPC plans are actually prepared and forwarded to the State government as required by Article 243ZD(4). These cases reflect the persistent gap between the constitutional mandate and administrative reality.

## Current significance

Article 243ZD sits in structural tension with the Union Government's flagship urban development programmes. The Smart Cities Mission, AMRUT, and housing mission projects are designed as centrally-sponsored schemes that flow through Special Purpose Vehicles or State nodal agencies, bypassing the DPC framework entirely. In Smart Cities, the SPV is typically a company under the Companies Act with a State-level board, not an institution accountable to the elected local body or integrated into the district planning process mandated by Article 243ZD. Critics have argued that the constitutional requirement for integrated district planning is functionally nullified when large-scale urban infrastructure investment is channelled through parallel structures that do not report to the DPC. The constitutional validity of these arrangements has not been directly tested before the Supreme Court, but the argument that they conflict with Article 243ZD's mandatory planning integration has been raised in public interest litigation. The DPC model also represents an unrealised opportunity for managing peri-urban growth and the rural-urban fringe, which is increasingly where India's urban expansion is occurring — precisely the kind of spatial integration across municipal and panchayat jurisdictions that Article 243ZD was designed to enable.
