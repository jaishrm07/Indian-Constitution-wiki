  [338A. National Commission for Scheduled Tribes.—(1) There shall be a Commission for the Scheduled Tribes to be known as the National Commission for the Scheduled Tribes.

  (2) Subject to the provisions of any law made in this behalf by Parliament, the Commission shall consist of a Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson and three other Members and the conditions of service and tenure of office of the Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson and other Members so appointed shall be such as the President may by rule determine.

  (3) The Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson and other Members of the Commission shall be appointed by the President by warrant under his hand and seal.

  (4) The Commission shall have the power to regulate its own procedure.

  (5) It shall be the duty of the Commission—

  (a) to investigate and monitor all matters relating to the safeguards provided for the Scheduled Tribes under this Constitution or under any other law for the time being in force or under any order of the Government and to evaluate the working of such safeguards;

  (b) to inquire into specific complaints with respect to the deprivation of rights and safeguards of the Scheduled Tribes;

  (c) to participate and advise on the planning process of socioeconomic development of the Scheduled Tribes and to evaluate the progress of their development under the Union and any State;

  (d) to present to the President, annually and at such other times as the Commission may deem fit, reports upon the working of those safeguards;

  (e) to make in such reports recommendations as to the measures that should be taken by the Union or any State for the effective implementation of those safeguards and other measures for the protection, welfare and socio-economic development of the Scheduled Tribes; and

  (f) to discharge such other functions in relation to the protection, welfare and development and advancement of the Scheduled Tribes as the President may, subject to the provisions of any law made by Parliament, by rule specify.

  (6) The President shall cause all such reports to be laid before each House of Parliament along with a memorandum explaining the action taken or proposed to be taken on the recommendations relating to the Union and the reasons for the non-acceptance, if any, of any such recommendations.

  (7) Where any such report, or any part thereof, relates to any matter with which any State Government is concerned, a copy of such report shall be forwarded to the Governor of the State who shall cause it to be laid before the Legislature of the State along with a memorandum explaining the action taken or proposed to be taken on the recommendations relating to the State and the reasons for the non-acceptance, if any, of any of such recommendations.

  (8) The Commission shall, while investigating any matter referred to in sub-clause (a) or inquiring into any complaint referred to in sub-clause (b) of clause (5), have all the powers of a civil court trying a suit and in particular in respect of the following matters, namely:—

  (a) summoning and enforcing the attendance of any person from any part of India and examining him on oath;

  (b) requiring the discovery and production of any document;

  (c) receiving evidence on affidavits;

  (d) requisitioning any public record or copy thereof from any court or office;

  (e) issuing commissions for the examination of witnesses and documents;

  (f) any other matter which the President may, by rule, determine.

  (9) The Union and every State Government shall consult the Commission on all major policy matters affecting Scheduled Tribes.]

## Why it matters

Article 338A creates the constitutional oversight body for Scheduled Tribes — communities whose constitutional protection spans a significantly wider legal and geographical landscape than that of Scheduled Castes. While SC protection is primarily focused on employment, education, and anti-atrocity law, ST protection additionally encompasses territorial governance (Fifth and Sixth Schedule frameworks), forest rights (Forest Rights Act, 2006), PESA (Panchayats Extension to Scheduled Areas Act, 1996), and the administration of Scheduled Areas under Article 339. The NCST is constitutionally mandated to oversee all of these intersecting regimes.

The 89th Amendment's decision to create a separate NCST was driven by the recognition that the combined SC and ST Commission established by the 65th Amendment was structurally unable to give adequate attention to both communities' distinct needs. SC communities face primarily urban and semi-urban issues of discrimination, reservation implementation, and atrocity; ST communities face primarily rural and forest-area issues of displacement, alienation of tribal lands, denial of forest rights, and inadequate implementation of the Fifth and Sixth Schedule governance frameworks. The separation has allowed the NCST to develop specialised expertise on these forest-and-land-rights questions that would have been diluted in a combined Commission.

The NCST's consultation mandate under clause (9) is particularly significant in the context of large infrastructure and development projects affecting Scheduled Areas. The Commission has repeatedly flagged that environmental and forest clearances, dam projects, mining leases, and highway construction in Fifth Schedule areas are approved without the constitutionally required consultation with tribal communities — violations that the NCST's monitoring function is designed to identify and report.

## Reading the provision

**Clauses (1) to (3)** establish the Commission's constitutional existence and five-member composition, with appointments by Presidential warrant giving the Commission a status equivalent to that of the NCSC under Article 338.

**Clause (4)** gives the NCST procedural autonomy to regulate its own procedure.

**Clause (5)** defines the Commission's duties across six sub-clauses: investigating and monitoring safeguards under the Constitution and other laws; inquiring into specific complaints; advising on socio-economic development planning; reporting annually to the President; recommending measures for effective implementation of safeguards; and performing such other functions as the President may by rule specify. The inclusion of "under any other law" in sub-clause (a) extends the NCST's monitoring mandate beyond the Constitution itself to the Forest Rights Act, PESA, the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, and land acquisition laws — all of which significantly affect tribal communities.

**Clause (6)** mandates the tabling of NCST reports in Parliament with a memorandum explaining action taken and reasons for non-acceptance of any recommendations. This accountability mechanism mirrors the NCSC framework under Article 338(6).

**Clause (7)** requires the Governor to lay State-related portions of NCST reports before the State legislature with a corresponding memorandum — an important federal dimension given that Fifth Schedule governance is primarily a State-level responsibility under the Governor's superintendence.

**Clause (8)** gives the NCST civil-court powers for investigations, including the power to summon witnesses, require document production, and receive evidence on affidavits.

**Clause (9)** imposes the mandatory consultation obligation: the Union and every State Government must consult the NCST on all major policy matters affecting Scheduled Tribes. This is the Commission's most proactive tool, requiring prior engagement before policies are finalised rather than merely post-hoc complaint-handling.

## The 89th Amendment: separating SC and ST oversight

The Constitution (Eighty-ninth Amendment) Act, 2003 bifurcated the National Commission for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (established by the 65th Amendment in 1990) into the NCSC under Article 338 and the NCST under the new Article 338A. The separating rationale was three-fold. First, the volume and complexity of the complaints and monitoring work for each community was sufficient to warrant dedicated institutional attention. Second, the policy advisory functions of the two Commissions required different expertise — the NCSC needed expertise in employment law, atrocity law, and social welfare schemes, while the NCST needed expertise in forest law, Fifth and Sixth Schedule governance, tribal land rights, and indigenous community governance. Third, the parliamentary accountability mechanism — annual reports tabled with action memoranda — would be more focused and informative if each Commission presented reports specific to its community rather than combined reports that often meant one community's issues received less attention.

## Key cases

**Samatha v State of Andhra Pradesh (1997)** — Decided before the NCST's creation, the Supreme Court held that land transfers in Scheduled Areas to non-tribals and private corporations are unconstitutional, and pointed to the need for a dedicated institutional watchdog for tribal rights — a gap that the NCST was subsequently created to fill. The case is foundational to the NCST's oversight of land and forest rights in Scheduled Areas.

**State of Andhra Pradesh v Lavu Narendranath (various)** — Cases challenging State-level inclusion and exclusion of communities in the ST list have reiterated that the President's notification under Article 342 is the definitive source of ST status, and the NCST's role in evaluating compliance with safeguards operates on top of that definitional framework.

**Orissa Mining Corporation v Ministry of Environment and Forests (2013)** — The Supreme Court directed the Central Government to obtain consent of gram sabhas in Scheduled Areas under PESA before granting forest clearance for mining in the Niyamgiri hills. The Court cited the constitutional safeguards for Scheduled Tribes, the role of the Fifth Schedule, and the Forest Rights Act — all areas within the NCST's monitoring mandate. This case exemplifies the intersection of the NCST's oversight function with judicial enforcement of tribal rights.

**Writ Petitions on Forest Rights Act implementation** — The NCST's annual reports on Forest Rights Act implementation have been placed before the Supreme Court in the consolidated FRA writ petitions, where the Commission's findings on the scale of wrongful rejections of tribal forest rights claims have been cited as evidence of systematic State-level non-compliance with the Act.

## Interaction with the Fifth and Sixth Schedules

The Fifth Schedule (schedule-v) establishes the Scheduled Areas framework for central and eastern India, under which Governors have special powers, the Tribes Advisory Council advises on tribal welfare, and laws applying to Scheduled Areas can be restricted or modified. The Sixth Schedule (schedule-vi) applies to tribal areas in Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram, creating autonomous district councils with legislative, judicial, and administrative powers. The NCST's mandate to monitor safeguards for Scheduled Tribes under "any other law" and "any order of the Government" encompasses both Schedule frameworks. In practice, the NCST monitors whether Fifth Schedule Governors are exercising their powers to protect tribal land rights, whether State PESA-conforming legislation is in place and implemented, and whether autonomous district councils in the Sixth Schedule States are functioning effectively.

## Current significance

The NCST operates at the interface of tribal rights, forest law, and development policy — arguably the most contested constitutional terrain in twenty-first century India. Large-scale infrastructure projects, mining operations, and forest-area development increasingly affect Scheduled Areas, and the constitutional requirement of consultation under clause (9) is frequently violated or bypassed. The Commission's reports have documented systematic failures to consult gram sabhas before forest clearances, inadequate implementation of Forest Rights Act individual and community titles, and non-compliance with PESA provisions by State legislatures that have not enacted conforming legislation.

The Davinder Singh (2024) ruling's application to STs opens new questions for the NCST. If States enact sub-classification legislation to direct preferential quotas within ST reservations to genuinely primitive tribal groups, the Commission will need to provide the empirical foundation — data on the relative conditions of ST sub-groups — that the constitutional framework now requires. The NCST's data collection and reporting capacity will need to evolve to meet this demand. The Commission has already flagged in its annual reports that some numerically large or more educationally advanced tribal groups benefit disproportionately from ST reservation in certain States, suggesting that the underlying empirical concern driving sub-classification demands is real and documented.
