## What this Article does

Article 178 requires every State Legislative Assembly to elect two of its own members as Speaker and Deputy Speaker as soon as it constitutes. The Speaker presides over the Assembly, certifies bills as money bills under Article 199, administers the oath of office to members, decides questions of order and privilege, and — most controversially — adjudicates disqualification petitions under the Tenth Schedule (anti-defection law). The Deputy Speaker performs these functions in the Speaker's absence. Whenever either office falls vacant the Assembly must elect a replacement without undue delay.

The article is the constitutional foundation for the presiding officer institution in State Legislatures, conferring institutional legitimacy on whoever the House chooses. By requiring the Assembly to elect from its own membership, the Constitution ties the office to democratic mandate and House accountability rather than to executive appointment — a deliberate contrast with the Governor's appointment by the President.

## Comparison with Article 93 (Lok Sabha Speaker)

Article 178 is the State-level counterpart of Article 93, which establishes the same office for the Lok Sabha. The two provisions are structurally identical — both use "shall choose," both require replacement when vacancies arise, and neither specifies the procedure for election or the majority required. The difference lies in the context: the Lok Sabha Speaker's decisions have national constitutional consequences, while the State Assembly Speaker's decisions operate within the State's legislative domain, though they can have significant national implications when they concern anti-defection proceedings affecting government stability.

## The Speaker's functions under the Tenth Schedule

The most litigated aspect of the Article 178 office in recent constitutional history is the Speaker's role as the sole adjudicating authority under paragraph 6 of the Tenth Schedule (anti-defection provisions). The Tenth Schedule, inserted by the 52nd Amendment (1985), vests the power to decide disqualification petitions in the "Chairman or Speaker of such House." This means the Speaker elected under Article 178 by a majority of the ruling coalition must decide, in a quasi-judicial capacity, whether members of the ruling coalition's rival factions have defected from the party.

The structural tension — a Speaker elected by the ruling majority deciding disqualifications against members whose defection the ruling majority may have encouraged — has been repeatedly flagged by the Supreme Court as a constitutional infirmity that the legislature has not yet corrected.

## The obligation to fill vacancies promptly

The phrase "so often as the office... becomes vacant, the Assembly shall choose another member" imposes a continuing constitutional obligation on the Assembly. A legislature cannot operate without a Speaker indefinitely. Courts and constitutional scholars have noted that where a Speaker resigns in the midst of a political crisis, the obligation to elect a new Speaker falls immediately on the Assembly, and an Acting Speaker's powers under Article 180 are inherently limited to the period before that election.

## Key cases

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v The Hon'ble Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — A Constitution Bench held that when a Speaker is the Tenth Schedule authority in a disqualification dispute in which the Speaker is personally implicated, unreasonable delay in deciding petitions amounts to a constitutional violation. The Court issued time-bound directions, directly connecting the Article 178 office to constitutional obligations under the anti-defection law.

**Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v Hon'ble Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2019)** — The Supreme Court reaffirmed that the Speaker's power under the Tenth Schedule is subject to judicial review for procedural compliance, and cautioned that the presiding officer's role in anti-defection matters must be discharged independently of the ruling party's political interests, even though the Speaker is elected from party ranks under Article 178.

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court, dealing with Maharashtra's political crisis, addressed the consequences of a Speaker's resignation and the powers of an Acting Speaker, confirming that the obligations created by Article 178 survive the individual officeholder, and that decisions made by an improperly constituted or conflicted Speaker are subject to judicial scrutiny.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — The Court's analysis of the Deputy Speaker's powers in a crisis confirmed that the Article 178 office creates a continuing institutional obligation: the Assembly cannot function without a constitutionally elected presiding officer, and attempts to circumvent the office — whether by the executive or by a faction within the legislature — are constitutionally impermissible.

## Current significance

The office created by Article 178 sits at the intersection of parliamentary procedure and democratic accountability. The structural tension between the Speaker's electoral origin — chosen by the ruling majority — and the quasi-judicial role under the Tenth Schedule has made it one of the most contested offices in contemporary State constitutional practice. Multiple simultaneous political crises in Maharashtra, Manipur, Goa, and other States have all turned on the Article 178 office and its interaction with the anti-defection law. Reform proposals — including vesting anti-defection jurisdiction in a statutory tribunal or the Election Commission — continue to be debated, with the Supreme Court repeatedly noting the structural problem while stopping short of ordering legislative action.
