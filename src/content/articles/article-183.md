  183. Vacation and resignation of, and removal from, the offices of Chairman and Deputy Chairman.—A member holding office as Chairman or Deputy Chairman of a Legislative Council—

  (a) shall vacate his office if he ceases to be a member of the Council;

  (b) may at any time by writing under his hand addressed, if such member is the Chairman, to the Deputy Chairman, and if such member is the Deputy Chairman, to the Chairman, resign his office; and

  (c) may be removed from his office by a resolution of the Council passed by a majority of all the then members of the Council:

  Provided that no resolution for the purpose of clause (c) shall be moved unless at least fourteen days' notice has been given of the intention to move the resolution.

## Why it matters

Article 183 is the tenure provision for the presiding officers of the State Legislative Council — the upper house of a State Legislature in the six States that retain bicameralism. It mirrors Article 179, which serves the same function for the Speaker and Deputy Speaker of the Legislative Assembly. The parallel architecture reflects the Constitution's symmetrical treatment of the two houses: in a bicameral State Legislature, both presiding officers must have equivalent constitutional protections for their independence and equivalent mechanisms for their removal. The principal distinction from Article 179 is the absence of the Assembly-dissolution proviso, which is logical because the Council is a permanent house and is never dissolved.

## Reading the provision

Clause (a) creates automatic vacation: if the Chairman ceases to be a member of the Council — whether through resignation, disqualification, or expiry of term — the office of Chairman falls vacant immediately.

No separate removal process is needed for automatic vacation; the event of ceasing membership is itself the operative fact.

Clause (b) allows voluntary resignation: the Chairman writes to the Deputy Chairman; the Deputy Chairman writes to the Chairman.

This mutual-resignation-address structure, identical to Article 179(b), prevents the resignation being addressed to a subordinate officer or to an external authority. A Chairman cannot be required to address his resignation to the Governor, to the government of the day, or to any officer of the State executive — protecting the presiding officer's constitutional independence from executive pressure.

Clause (c) is the only mechanism for involuntary removal: a resolution passed by an absolute majority of all then members of the Council, with at least fourteen days' notice of the intention to move such a resolution.

The fourteen-day notice period serves two purposes: it allows the Chairman time to respond publicly to any allegations motivating the removal motion, and it gives Council members time to deliberate without being stampeded into a snap vote. The absolute majority requirement — not just a majority of those present and voting — means that even a numerically strong majority present in the house may fall short of the removal threshold if many members are absent, making removal practically difficult and reinforcing the Chairman's functional independence.

## Key cases

**Kihoto Hollohan v Zachillhu (1992)** — The Supreme Court's foundational ruling on the Tenth Schedule held that the Speaker or Chairman performing anti-defection adjudication functions acts as a tribunal whose orders are subject to limited judicial review. The security of tenure provisions in Article 183 are part of the constitutional architecture that is meant to ensure the Chairman's independence as both presiding officer and disqualification adjudicator; the Court's endorsement of judicial review of Tenth Schedule decisions is premised in part on the existence of that independence.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh Legislative Assembly (2016)** — The Supreme Court established that a presiding officer facing active removal proceedings cannot in the meantime exercise consequential powers — specifically, disqualifying members. While the facts concerned an Assembly Speaker under Article 179, the principle has been applied to Council Chairmen under Article 183 in subsequent state legislative disputes, because the constitutional logic is identical.

**Raja Ram Pal v Hon'ble Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — Although concerning the Lok Sabha Speaker, the Court's analysis of the scope of judicial review over presiding officers' decisions — affirming that the expulsion or removal of members by presiding officers is subject to judicial review on constitutional grounds — applies equally to Council Chairmen exercising analogous powers, reinforcing that Article 183's tenure protections do not insulate the Chairman from accountability for unconstitutional conduct.

## Distinction from Article 179

The most practically significant difference between Articles 183 and 179 concerns what happens during a dissolution of the Assembly. Article 179 contains a proviso stating that a Speaker or Deputy Speaker does not vacate office solely because the Assembly is dissolved; they continue until the Assembly first meets after the general election. No equivalent proviso appears in Article 183, but none is needed: the Legislative Council is a permanent body under Article 169 that is never dissolved. Membership of the Council changes through biennial retirements, not dissolution, so the continuity of the Chairman's office through a Council election cycle does not raise the same issues as an Assembly dissolution. The Chairman continues to hold office as long as he remains a Council member and has not resigned or been removed, making the Article 183 tenure structure straightforwardly stable compared to the Assembly-dissolution complications in Article 179.

## Current significance

The six States retaining Legislative Councils — Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Bihar, Telangana, and Uttar Pradesh — each operate under Article 183's framework. The Chairman's role has become particularly significant in anti-defection adjudication under the Tenth Schedule, where Chairmen of Councils — like Speakers of Assemblies — face structural incentives to protect members of their own party. Allegations of partisan delay or decision-making in Council-level anti-defection proceedings have followed patterns similar to those observed with Assembly Speakers. The absolute-majority removal threshold in clause (c) provides strong practical protection against retaliatory removal of a Chairman who acts impartially, but it also makes it very difficult to remove a Chairman who acts in a partisan manner, since the ruling party that elected the Chairman is unlikely to vote for his removal. Constitutional reform proposals to vest anti-defection jurisdiction in an independent body — a former judge or election commissioner — would require amendment of Articles 179, 183, and the Tenth Schedule; no such amendment has been enacted despite strong judicial encouragement in Keisham Meghachandra Singh (2020) and Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil (2019).
