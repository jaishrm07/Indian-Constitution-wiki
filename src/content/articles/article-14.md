## Why it matters

Article 14 is the constitutional bedrock of the rule of law in India. Its twenty-two words — borrowed from the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution and from the traditions of English common law — do two distinct constitutional jobs. "Equality before the law" is a negative, rule-of-law principle: the State cannot claim a special immunity from the legal standards it applies to others; the executive officer who breaks the law is as much subject to legal consequences as the private individual; and the legal process must be followed regardless of who the affected party is. "Equal protection of the laws" is a positive, equality-of-treatment principle: persons who are similarly situated must be treated similarly, and any differential treatment requires a constitutionally sufficient justification.

The article covers every person within the territory of India — citizens and non-citizens alike. Unlike Article 15 (which is limited to discrimination on specific grounds) and Article 16 (which addresses employment), Article 14 is general: any State action — legislative, executive, or administrative — that denies equality before the law or equal protection can be challenged under it. This universality makes Article 14 the default equality provision, the constitutional anchor to which litigants return whenever Articles 15 and 16 do not precisely fit their case.

The history of Article 14 doctrine is the history of an expanding constitutional guarantee. In the 1950s and 1960s, the Court treated Article 14 primarily through the classification framework: the State could make distinctions between groups of persons as long as the classification had an intelligible differentia and the differentia had a rational nexus to the object sought to be achieved. This was a relatively modest standard, allowing Parliament and the executive wide latitude to regulate differently in pursuit of legitimate social objectives. From the mid-1970s, the Court added an independent anti-arbitrariness standard: any State action that is manifestly arbitrary — made without application of mind, excessive, unpredictable, or disproportionate to the objective — violates Article 14 regardless of whether it involves a formal classification.

The anti-arbitrariness doctrine has proved more powerful in practice than the classification doctrine, because it allows courts to examine the quality of State decision-making across the full range of administrative, regulatory, and legislative action without needing to identify a comparison group that is being discriminated against. It is the constitutional basis for much of Indian administrative law — the requirement of reasoned decisions, the doctrine of proportionality, the prohibition on unreasonable conditions in contracts and licences, and the control of arbitrary exercise of discretionary power.

## Reading the provision

The phrase "equality before the law" derives from A V Dicey's rule of law doctrine — the idea that every person, regardless of their status or position, is subject to the ordinary law of the land and equally amenable to judicial process. In the Indian context, it has been interpreted to mean that the State cannot grant immunities from legal process to persons or classes of persons in ways that are arbitrary or constitutionally unjustified. It prevents the State from placing itself above the law and from selectively exempting some categories of persons from ordinary legal obligations.

The phrase "equal protection of the laws" is borrowed directly from the American Fourteenth Amendment. It is understood as a positive command: the law must be applied equally to all who fall within a given legal category. If the State wishes to treat different persons differently, it must justify that differentiation. The early Court developed the classification test — intelligible differentia (some rational basis for grouping persons) plus rational nexus (the grouping must bear a reasonable relationship to the objective of the law) — as the primary mechanism for determining whether differential treatment was constitutionally valid.

Critically, Article 14 permits, and indeed requires, classification. Perfect formal equality — treating all persons exactly the same regardless of their different circumstances — would itself produce injustice. The Constitution allows the State to legislate differently for different categories of persons as long as the categorisation is non-arbitrary. Zamindars and tenant farmers may be treated differently; organised sector and informal sector workers may be treated differently; persons with disabilities may be treated differently in their favour. The equality guarantee under Article 14 is therefore an equality of treatment within applicable categories, not an equality of outcomes across all persons.

The anti-arbitrariness doctrine, as it developed from E P Royappa (1974) forward, adds an independent standard: even if a law does not involve any discrimination between identifiable groups, it may still violate Article 14 if it is manifestly arbitrary. Manifest arbitrariness means being capricious, irrational, not informed by reason, or disproportionate to the mischief it seeks to address. The Supreme Court in Shayara Bano v Union of India (2017), when striking down instantaneous triple talaq, held that the practice was manifestly arbitrary because it allowed a husband to dissolve a marriage whimsically, without cause, and without any recourse for the wife — a form of asymmetric power that was constitutionally unsustainable under Article 14.

The relationship between Article 14 and Articles 15 and 16 is hierarchical: Articles 15 and 16 deal with specific forms of discrimination (based on religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth) and are the more specific provisions. Article 14 is the general provision that underlies them all and provides a residual guarantee where Articles 15 and 16 do not precisely apply. A person who cannot satisfy the conditions of Article 15 (for example, because the discrimination they face is based on language or class rather than religion or caste) may still succeed under Article 14 by showing manifest arbitrariness.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v Anwar Ali Sarkar (1952)** — Among the earliest significant Article 14 cases, the Court struck down the West Bengal Special Courts Act because it conferred on the State Government an unfettered discretion to refer cases to special courts for a speedier trial without providing any objective criteria for exercise of that discretion. The absence of any guidance on when the power would be exercised rendered the classification arbitrary. The case established that conferral of unguided discretion on the executive itself violates Article 14, because it effectively means that similarly situated persons may be treated differently depending entirely on the whim of the authority.

**Budhan Choudhry v State of Bihar (1955)** — The Court consolidated the intelligible differentia and rational nexus tests as the standard of review under Article 14. The two requirements operate conjunctively: the grouping must have a rational basis, and that rational basis must connect to the purpose of the law. The case also made clear that the legislature is afforded a wide margin of appreciation in making classifications — courts do not require proof that the distinction was the best available; they only require that it is reasonably possible to see a constitutional justification for it.

**E P Royappa v State of Tamil Nadu (1974)** — The first major doctrinal evolution. Justice Bhagwati, in a concurring opinion in which he was joined by Justice Krishna Iyer, held that equality under Article 14 is not merely the absence of discriminatory classification between groups. It is a dynamic concept with many dimensions. "Equality and arbitrariness are sworn enemies," and any state action "coloured by arbitrariness" is necessarily in conflict with the rule of law and equality. The anti-arbitrariness principle introduced in Royappa has since become an independent limb of Article 14 jurisprudence, more frequently invoked than the original classification test in many areas of administrative law review.

**Maneka Gandhi v Union of India (1978)** — While primarily an Article 21 case, Maneka Gandhi fundamentally influenced Article 14 doctrine by confirming that Articles 14, 19, and 21 form an integrated guarantee. Any law that restricts liberty must pass all three simultaneously. The Court held that a law that is arbitrary violates Article 14 even if it does not discriminate between identifiable groups, because arbitrariness itself is inconsistent with the equality norm. This decision linked the anti-arbitrariness doctrine to the broader project of substantive rights protection and confirmed that the State cannot act capriciously in any domain that touches fundamental rights.

**D S Nakara v Union of India (1983)** — The Court held that a pension scheme that drew a distinction between retirees who retired before a certain date and those who retired after, giving the latter more favourable benefits, violated Article 14. The classification was held to be arbitrary because all pensioners form a homogeneous class and there was no rational basis for treating them differently based purely on the date of retirement. The case is a foundational precedent for the application of Article 14 to welfare entitlements and State service conditions.

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — The nine-judge bench engaged Article 14 extensively in upholding and defining the limits of OBC reservations. The Court held that reservations for socially and educationally backward classes are consistent with Article 14's equality guarantee because they represent an equality of result rather than a denial of equality — the Constitution recognises that formal equality of treatment produces substantive inequality when some groups start from positions of severe disadvantage. At the same time, the Court imposed a fifty-percent ceiling on aggregate reservations, using Article 14 to hold that reservations beyond this level cannot claim the justification of compensatory equality and instead become a denial of equal treatment to the unreserved.

**Shayara Bano v Union of India (2017)** — The five-judge Constitution Bench that struck down instantaneous triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) did so on several grounds, including Article 14's anti-arbitrariness doctrine. Three of the five judges held that a practice that permitted a husband to dissolve a marriage instantly and irrevocably without any cause, without any notice to the wife, and without any possibility of reconciliation or recourse, was manifestly arbitrary. The power given to the husband — absolute, unguided, and one-sided — could not survive the Article 14 scrutiny that requires State-recognised legal arrangements to be non-arbitrary.

**Navtej Singh Johar v Union of India (2018)** — In striking down Section 377 of the IPC as applied to consensual same-sex relations between adults, the five-judge bench also held that the provision violated Article 14. It drew an irrational distinction between homosexual and heterosexual conduct for purposes of criminalisation, a distinction that had no rational nexus to any legitimate state objective and that was based entirely on historical prejudice and moral condemnation. The case is significant for applying the anti-arbitrariness and manifest-unreasonableness standards under Article 14 to penal legislation.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India (Electoral Bonds) (2024)** — The Constitution Bench struck down the Electoral Bond Scheme on multiple grounds. On Article 14, the Court held that the scheme's grant of unlimited, anonymous political donations violated the equal protection of voters' right to information about the sources of political funding. By creating a mechanism under which donors could make large anonymous donations, the scheme created an arbitrary asymmetry between voters (who could not know who funded which party) and the State Bank (which could know and could theoretically disclose to the government). The case extended Article 14's anti-arbitrariness doctrine into the domain of electoral finance.

## Article 14 and administrative law

The most practical impact of Article 14 in day-to-day constitutional practice is in administrative law. Every exercise of discretionary power by the executive — granting or revoking licences, making appointments, awarding contracts, issuing administrative orders — is subject to Article 14 scrutiny. The central requirements that courts derive from Article 14 in this domain are:

**Reasoned decisions**: An administrative authority that does not give reasons for its decision makes it impossible to assess whether the decision was reasoned or arbitrary. The Supreme Court has held that the requirement of reasoned decisions is implied by Article 14 in many contexts, and has required reasons from authorities even where the relevant statute does not expressly mandate them.

**Non-selective enforcement**: If the State enforces a law against some persons but not against others who are identically situated, the selective enforcement itself violates Article 14, regardless of whether the enforcement against those selected is otherwise lawful. Courts can issue mandamus directing uniform enforcement.

**Proportionality**: The 2020 Internet Shutdown cases and subsequent decisions have moved Indian constitutional review toward a proportionality standard under Article 14 — the restriction must be no more than necessary to achieve the legitimate objective. This standard, derived from comparative constitutional practice, has begun displacing the more deferential "rational nexus" test in cases involving fundamental rights restrictions.

**Legitimate expectation**: Where a person has a legitimate expectation — arising from a representation or an established practice — that they will be treated in a certain way, departure from that treatment without reason may violate Article 14. This doctrine, imported from English administrative law, has been extensively developed in Indian constitutional jurisprudence.

## Current significance

Article 14 remains among the most frequently invoked constitutional provisions in the Supreme Court and High Courts. Challenges to executive orders, administrative policies, tender conditions, service rules, electoral bond schemes, and penal legislation all routinely raise Article 14 as a primary or ancillary ground. The expansion of the anti-arbitrariness doctrine into the domain of proportionality — requiring that measures be not just rationally connected to their objectives but no more restrictive than necessary — has made Article 14 review significantly more demanding than it was in the Budhan Choudhry era.

The relationship between Article 14 and affirmative action policy is the most politically charged area of current Article 14 litigation. The 103rd Amendment's EWS reservation (for economically weaker sections of the general category) was upheld in Janhit Abhiyan v Union of India (2022) against Article 14 challenges, with the majority holding that economic disadvantage is a constitutionally permissible basis for classification and that the EWS category does not violate the equality norm. The dissenting opinions disputed whether the economic criterion alone, without reference to the deep-rooted social disadvantages that the reservation framework was designed to address, was consistent with Article 14's substantive equality vision.

The Davinder Singh (2024) sub-classification ruling also raised important Article 14 questions — whether a State government could sub-classify reserved categories to prefer more disadvantaged sub-groups within the SC or ST list, and whether doing so was consistent with the equality guarantee applicable to the SC/ST lists. The Court upheld sub-classification in principle, holding that treating all within the SC/ST categories as a homogeneous group when some are far more disadvantaged than others is itself inconsistent with substantive equality under Article 14.

The digital and regulatory state has created new Article 14 frontiers: challenges to algorithmic decision-making by government agencies (whether an algorithm that processes welfare applications denies equal treatment to those it disadvantages), to mandatory biometric linkage requirements that disadvantage those who cannot provide biometrics, and to differential regulatory treatment of platform workers versus formal employees. Each of these generates Article 14 claims that require courts to apply constitutional equality standards to novel factual contexts.
