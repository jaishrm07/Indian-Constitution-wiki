plainEnglish: Article 360 allows the President to declare a Financial Emergency if satisfied that the financial stability or credit of India, or of any part of its territory, is threatened. Once proclaimed, the declaration must be placed before Parliament and lapses after two months unless both Houses approve it by resolution. During a Financial Emergency, the Union can give binding directions to States on financial matters, including requiring them to reduce the salaries of their employees and to send all money bills to the President for approval. The President may also reduce the salaries of judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts. No Financial Emergency has ever been proclaimed in India's history.

  360. Provisions as to financial emergency.—(1) If the President is satisfied that a situation has arisen whereby the financial stability or credit of India or of any part of the territory thereof is threatened, he may by a Proclamation make a declaration to that effect. [(2) A Proclamation issued under clause (1)—

  (a) may be revoked or varied by a subsequent Proclamation;

  (b) shall be laid before each House of Parliament;

  (c) shall cease to operate at the expiration of two months, unless before the expiration of that period it has been approved by resolutions of both Houses of Parliament:

  Provided that if any such Proclamation is issued at a time when the House of the People has been dissolved or the dissolution of the House of the People takes place during the period of two months referred to in sub-clause (c), and if a resolution approving the Proclamation has been passed by the Council of States, but no resolution with respect to such Proclamation has been passed by the House of the People before the expiration of that period, the Proclamation shall cease to operate at the expiration of thirty days from the date on which the House of the People first sits after its reconstitution unless before the expiration of the said period of thirty days a resolution approving the Proclamation has been also passed by the House of the People.]

  (b) it shall be competent for the President during the period any Proclamation issued under this article is in operation to issue directions for the reduction of salaries and allowances of all or any class of persons serving in connection with the affairs of the Union including the Judges of the Supreme Court and the High Courts. ## Why it matters

Article 360 is the most dormant of India's three constitutional emergency provisions — a National Emergency under Article 352 has been proclaimed three times, President's Rule under Article 356 has been imposed well over a hundred times, but a Financial Emergency under Article 360 has never once been proclaimed since the Constitution came into force in 1950.

The provision nonetheless occupies significant constitutional space. Its existence shapes the negotiating position of States in fiscal disputes with the Union, conditions the design of Finance Commission awards, and provides a background guarantee that the Union has the constitutional authority to intervene if a fiscal crisis genuinely threatens the nation's financial stability or credit. The mere availability of this power is part of the architecture of credibility that keeps India's sovereign credit position stable.

The threshold is deliberately broad — "financial stability or credit of India or of any part of the territory thereof" — which makes it applicable not only to a nationwide economic collapse but potentially to a regional default or a State government's insolvency of sufficient scale to affect national creditworthiness. The absence of a judicial gloss on this threshold (because the provision has never been invoked) means its precise contours remain undefined.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the proclamation.** The President must be "satisfied" that financial stability or credit is threatened. The standard of presidential satisfaction is the same as under Article 352 and is subject to the post-Bommai principle that satisfaction must rest on relevant material and is subject to limited judicial review.

**Clause (2) — parliamentary approval.** Unlike a National Emergency (which requires approval within one month), a Financial Emergency lapses after two months unless approved by resolutions of both Houses. No special majority is required — an ordinary resolution by both Houses suffices. This is a weaker parliamentary gate than Article 352 but stronger than Article 354 (which merely requires laying before Parliament). The Lok Sabha proviso applies if the Lok Sabha is dissolved during the two-month window.

**Clause (3) — Union directions to States.** During a Financial Emergency, the Union executive's authority extends to giving binding directions to States on financial propriety. These can cover any aspect of the State's financial management that the President deems necessary. This is a sweeping power that goes well beyond ordinary Article 256 directions.

**Clause (4)(a) — reduction of State salaries and reservation of money bills.** Directions may include requirements to reduce the salaries and allowances of persons serving the State at any level, and to send all money bills (and equivalent financial legislation) to the President for consideration. The reservation of money bills is particularly significant: it effectively transfers the State Legislature's budget-making authority to the President during the emergency.

**Clause (4)(b) — reduction of Union salaries including judges.** The President may also direct reductions in salaries of persons serving the Union, including Judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts. This is the only constitutional provision that allows judicial salaries to be reduced (Article 125 and 221 protect judicial salaries from reduction in ordinary times as a guarantee of judicial independence). The availability of this power during a Financial Emergency is a deliberate constitutional choice: the gravity of a financial crisis is treated as sufficient justification to override even judicial salary guarantees.

**Hypothetical triggers.** Constitutional commentators have identified several scenarios that might plausibly lead to a Financial Emergency proclamation: a catastrophic collapse of the banking system with systemic sovereign exposure; a State government's default on its debt obligations so large as to trigger a national sovereign credit downgrade; a severe balance of payments crisis requiring emergency International Monetary Fund intervention of the kind that nearly occurred in 1991; or a prolonged global financial shock that drives India's debt-to-GDP ratio to levels threatening default.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v Union of India (1963)** — While not a Financial Emergency case, the Court examined the relationship between the Union and States in matters of fiscal and legislative authority. The judgment's analysis of constitutional protections for States in the federal fiscal structure provides the baseline against which the breadth of Article 360(3) directions would be assessed. States have constitutionally protected fiscal interests, and Union directions during a Financial Emergency must still serve the purpose of addressing the financial crisis rather than accomplishing collateral political objectives.

**Minerva Mills v Union of India (1980)** — The Court's foundational holding that emergency powers are subject to judicial review and that the basic structure constrains even emergency-era executive action applies to Article 360 as much as to Articles 352 and 356. Presidential satisfaction under Article 360(1) is not immune from review for mala fides or for being based on irrelevant material.

**State of Kerala v Union of India (original suit filed 2024)** — The State of Kerala filed a suit before the Supreme Court challenging Union-imposed net borrowing ceilings and conditions on State debt, arguing that fiscal coercion by the Centre through Fiscal Responsibility legislation and discretionary withdrawal of borrowing space effectively replicates the consequences of a Financial Emergency without the formal proclamation and without the parliamentary oversight Article 360 requires. The suit has raised foundational questions about the constitutional limits of informal Union fiscal control over States and what kinds of financial constraint require a formal constitutional basis under Article 360.

## Current significance

The 1991 balance of payments crisis — when India pledged gold reserves to avert a sovereign default — is the closest the country has come to conditions that might have warranted a Financial Emergency proclamation, but the government addressed the crisis through IMF borrowing and economic reforms rather than a constitutional proclamation. This suggests that political and reputational costs act as a strong deterrent to invoking Article 360, even when the economic conditions might technically satisfy the threshold.

Contemporary relevance comes from two directions. First, the growing fiscal divergence between well-performing and distressed States, combined with Union fiscal centralisation through GST administration and discretionary grant-making, raises questions about whether the conditions for a partial Financial Emergency (threatening the credit of "any part" of India) could arise from severe State-level fiscal stress. Second, the Kerala borrowing dispute in the Supreme Court directly challenges whether the Union is effectively exercising Article 360-equivalent powers informally, without the safeguards — most importantly the two-month parliamentary approval requirement — that a formal proclamation would require. The Court's resolution of that case will have significant implications for how Article 360 is understood even in its dormant state.
