## Why it matters

Article 272's omission is itself a constitutional event worth understanding. The article was not erased because it was unconstitutional or unworkable, but because it had been superseded by a more comprehensive and coherent mechanism. Its removal in 2000 completed a structural rationalisation of the fiscal federalism provisions that had been brewing since the Tenth Finance Commission recommended unifying the divisible pool. Understanding what Article 272 did — and why it needed to be removed — illuminates both the fragility of the pre-2000 sharing architecture and the constitutional logic of the unified pool system that replaced it.

In the original Constitution, fiscal federalism for shared taxes operated through two separate tracks: Article 270 governed the sharing of income tax proceeds (the dominant direct tax), while Article 272 governed the distribution of Union excise duties (the dominant indirect tax). Each track had its own percentage formula, its own Finance Commission input process, and — crucially — its own legislation by Parliament prescribing distribution ratios. The result was that a Finance Commission examining the fiscal needs of States had to make separate recommendations for income tax sharing and excise duty sharing, and Parliament had to enact separate legislation for each. The two sharing formulas could and did diverge over time, creating complexity without constitutional justification.

The deeper structural problem was that separate sharing formulas for different tax heads created perverse incentives. If income tax sharing was at a higher percentage than excise duty sharing, the Union had a fiscal incentive to raise more revenue through excise duties (lower sharing) than through income tax (higher sharing). This distorted tax policy: the optimal tax structure from a revenue-efficiency standpoint diverged from the optimal tax structure from a Union-retention standpoint. Article 272's existence as a separate, lower-sharing track for excise duties contributed to this incentive problem. Eliminating it and merging excise into the Article 270 unified pool removed that distortion.

## What this Article originally provided

Article 272 originally provided that Parliament could, by law, distribute a portion of Union-levied taxes (principally Union excise duties) between the Union and the States. It operated in tandem with the pre-2000 version of Article 270, which was limited to income tax, and Article 272, which covered Union excise duties. Parliament used the enabling power to enact separate sharing legislation for each category of tax, resulting in a fragmented devolution architecture with different formulas for different tax heads.

Under the pre-Eightieth Amendment framework, the Centre used its Article 272 power to prescribe the percentage of Union excise duty proceeds that would be shared with States. The Finance Commission made recommendations on this percentage, but the ultimate determination rested with Parliament's legislation. This meant that the divisible pool for excise duties and the divisible pool for income tax (under Article 270) operated independently, with different sharing ratios and different State-wise distribution formulas potentially applicable to each tax head.

The complexity went further: not all excise duties were equally shareable under Article 272. The Union could and did use various rate structures — basic excise, special excise, additional excise — with different degrees of sharing, giving the executive considerable room to manoeuvre in ways that affected State revenues without formally altering the stated sharing percentage under Article 272. This flexibility in the tax architecture was a recurring source of Union-State fiscal friction in the 1970s and 1980s.

## Reading the provision

The original Article 272 gave Parliament the power to prescribe by law "the share of the net proceeds of duties in the nature of excise duties" that would be distributed to States. The "may be distributed" language was permissive rather than mandatory in its original form — Parliament could choose whether to share at all, and if so, at what percentage and by what distribution principles. This contrasted with the language of Article 270, which spoke in more mandatory terms about income tax distribution. The Finance Commission's recommendations on Article 272 distribution were thus advisory inputs to a parliamentary discretion, rather than mandatory inputs to a presidential order as in the Article 270 regime.

## Why it was omitted

The Constitution (Eightieth Amendment) Act 2000 — enacted to give effect to the recommendations of the Tenth Finance Commission — omitted Article 272 entirely and rewrote Article 270 to create a single unified divisible pool covering all Union taxes and duties (except those specifically excluded). This rationalised the fiscal federalism framework by eliminating the patchwork of separate sharing laws and replacing them with one Article 270-based pool, the distribution of which is determined by successive Finance Commissions.

The Tenth Finance Commission's central recommendation — adopted by Parliament through the Eightieth Amendment — was to merge all shareable taxes into one pool and assign a single percentage of that pool to States. This required removing Article 272's separate excise-duty distribution mechanism, because retaining it alongside a unified Article 270 pool would have created confusion about which formula governed which tax. The omission of Article 272 was therefore a constitutional housekeeping measure to align the text with the new unified devolution model.

## Key cases

**Kedar Nath Bajoria v. State of West Bengal** — An early case interpreting the scope of Parliament's distribution power under the predecessor provisions to Article 272, establishing that the power to prescribe distribution principles carries with it the obligation to actually make provision, and cannot be exercised to exclude States from sharing proceeds indefinitely. The Court's reasoning that constitutional distribution powers impose a corresponding duty of distribution informed later arguments about the mandatory character of Finance Commission recommendations.

**Union of India v. H.S. Dhillon (1971)** — While primarily about the scope of the Union List, the case engaged with the architecture of Articles 268-272 as they stood before the Eightieth Amendment, providing interpretive context for how the pre-amendment distribution scheme operated and confirming that Parliament's exercise of its Article 272 distribution power was subject to constitutional review even though the power was formally discretionary.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (Revenue Sharing Disputes, pre-2000)** — State challenges to the excise duty sharing ratios prescribed by Parliament under Article 272 established that the Finance Commission's recommendations on those ratios, though not binding, created a strong constitutional expectation that Parliament would not depart from them without transparent justification. This principle — that Finance Commission recommendations generate constitutional expectations rather than binding obligations — continues to govern the post-Eightieth Amendment unified pool framework.

## Current significance

Although Article 272 has been omitted since 2000, it retains documentary importance as the textual predecessor to the unified divisible pool under Article 270. Legal historians and Finance Commission reports regularly reference the pre-Eightieth Amendment architecture to explain why certain legacy adjustment mechanisms — such as transitional grants and protected shares for specific States — were built into the Tenth Finance Commission's award.

The pre-Article 272 era illustrates the dangers of tax-specific sharing formulas. Because each tax had its own sharing percentage, the Union had an incentive to raise revenue through taxes with lower sharing percentages, distorting tax policy in ways that were not fiscally neutral. The unified pool system introduced by the Eightieth Amendment, by removing the separate Article 272 mechanism, eliminated this incentive and forced fiscal policy decisions about the overall level of taxation to be separated from the question of which particular taxes to use. This structural improvement in fiscal federalism made possible by Article 272's omission is now a standard reference point in academic and policy debates about the adequacy of the divisible pool framework.

Understanding what Article 272 said — and why it was removed — provides essential context for evaluating contemporary proposals to reform the divisible pool formula, add new tax categories, or address the cesses-and-surcharges problem that has emerged within the Article 270 unified pool framework. The lesson of Article 272's omission is that constitutional tidiness sometimes requires removing provisions that have been superseded by more comprehensive mechanisms, and that the long-term structural health of fiscal federalism sometimes requires accepting short-term disruption to entrenched legislative arrangements. That lesson is directly relevant to present debates about whether the cess proliferation problem under Article 270 requires a constitutional amendment similar in kind to the Eightieth Amendment that removed Article 272.
