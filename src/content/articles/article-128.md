  128. Attendance of retired Judges at sittings of the Supreme Court.—Notwithstanding anything in this Chapter, [the National Judicial Appointments Commission] may at any time, with the previous consent of the President, request any person who has held the office of a Judge of the Supreme Court or of the Federal Court [or who has held the office of a Judge of a High Court and is duly qualified for appointment as a Judge of the Supreme Court] to sit and act as a Judge of the Supreme Court, and every such person so requested shall, while so sitting and acting, be entitled to such allowances as the President may by order determine and have all the jurisdiction, powers and privileges of, but shall not otherwise be deemed to be, a Judge of that Court:

  Provided that nothing in this article shall be deemed to require any such person as aforesaid to sit and act as a Judge of that Court unless he consents so to do.

## What this Article does

Article 128 allows the body responsible for judicial appointments — described in the current text as the NJAC, but in operative practice since 2015 the collegium — with the President's prior consent, to invite retired Supreme Court or Federal Court Judges, and qualified retired High Court Judges, to sit and act as Supreme Court Judges. Unlike the ad hoc mechanism under Article 127, which is triggered by a quorum failure, Article 128 can be activated at any time. The consent proviso is important: no retired Judge can be compelled to return, meaning the article preserves personal autonomy while providing an institutional resource.

While sitting under Article 128, a retired Judge exercises the full jurisdiction, powers, and privileges of a Supreme Court Judge, but is not "deemed to be" a Judge of the Court for other purposes. This distinction — functional equivalence without full status — means that service under Article 128 does not restart a retired Judge's tenure or alter pension and post-retirement employment restrictions under Article 124(7). The retired judge is entitled to allowances determined by presidential order while sitting, but resumes their ordinary retired status on completion of the assignment.

## Three categories of eligible persons

Article 128 covers three classes of retired judges:

1. **Former Supreme Court judges:** Any person who has held the office of Judge of the Supreme Court. These are the most natural candidates, having already served at the Court.
2. **Former Federal Court judges:** The Federal Court existed from 1937 to 1950 under the Government of India Act, 1935. This category is now purely historical — all former Federal Court judges have long since died.
3. **Qualified retired High Court judges:** Those who have retired from a High Court and are "duly qualified for appointment as a Judge of the Supreme Court" — meaning they meet the constitutional eligibility requirements in Article 124(3) (having been a judge of a High Court for at least five years, or an advocate of a High Court for at least ten years).

The third category reflects the same eligibility standard as Article 127 for ad hoc judges, treating retired High Court judges who qualify for Supreme Court appointment as part of the available reserve pool.

## The parallel with Article 224A

Article 128 has a direct High Court counterpart in Article 224A, which allows the Chief Justice of a High Court, with the previous consent of the National Judicial Appointments Commission and the President, to request a retired judge of that court or any other High Court to sit and act as a judge. Article 224A has been used more frequently than Article 128. The Supreme Court in State of Rajasthan v. Prakash Chand (1998) considered the scope of Article 224A and the conditions under which retired judges can exercise jurisdiction, with the reasoning applicable by analogy to Article 128.

## The NJAC reference and its post-2015 effect

The article currently refers to the NJAC as the requesting body. Following the striking down of the NJAC in 2015, this reference is inoperative. The collegium process governs by default, but no constitutional amendment has substituted the collegium for the NJAC in the article's text. This creates the same inoperative-text problem as Articles 124A, 124B, 124C, and 127. In practice, any Article 128 invocation would require the Chief Justice (acting for the collegium) to seek the President's consent, replicating the pre-NJAC procedure.

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (1993):** The collegium judgment discussed Articles 127 and 128 together as part of the constitutional architecture for maintaining a functional bench, confirming that even these ad hoc and retired-judge mechanisms must operate consistently with the primacy of the Chief Justice and the independence of the judiciary.

**K. Veeraswami v. Union of India (1991):** The Court considered the scope of protections and privileges that attach to a judge's office, touching on the distinction between a judge in office and a person acting as a judge under provisions like Article 128, and the extent to which such a person falls within the institutional protection afforded to the judiciary.

**State of Rajasthan v. Prakash Chand (1998):** While addressing Article 224A (retired High Court judges sitting at High Courts), the Court laid down principles about the jurisdiction and independence of retired judges sitting on additional assignment that apply by analogy to Article 128 Supreme Court assignments.

## Independence concerns

The requirement of presidential consent has been identified as a structural tension in Article 128. The executive's role in approving the return of a specific retired judge creates at least the perception that the government may prefer to consent to some former judges and not others. This concern has been one reason the provision is used so rarely — the judiciary is unwilling to create a pattern of executive selection that could erode the independence it has built through the collegium system. Critics have argued that the consent requirement should be reformed to require only the collegium's decision, with the presidential role being purely formal.

## Current significance

Article 128 has been invoked very rarely in the Supreme Court's history. Its existence as a constitutional reserve capacity is significant, but the combination of institutional reluctance, independence concerns, and the inoperative NJAC reference has made it a largely theoretical provision. The provision is periodically discussed as a potential response to severe judicial vacancy crises, particularly when the Court's backlog reaches levels that make the failure to use every available constitutional mechanism difficult to justify.
