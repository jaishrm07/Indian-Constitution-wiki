## Why it matters

Article 337 sits at the intersection of two constitutional commitments: the guarantee of minority cultural and educational rights in Articles 29 and 30, and the framers' conviction that transitional protections for vulnerable communities should be time-bound rather than permanent. The Anglo-Indian community in 1947 maintained a large network of English-medium schools — an inheritance from colonial policy that had directed the community into supervisory and administrative roles requiring English literacy. These schools served not only Anglo-Indian children but also Indian children from neighbouring communities who sought English-medium education in an era when such schools were scarce. Removing the grants overnight would have jeopardised institutions that served a public educational function beyond the immediate community.

The article's step-down formula — reducing the grant by ten per cent every three years, not in a single abrupt cut — reflects a considered gradualism. The Constituent Assembly was aware that the community was small (approximately 300,000 persons), geographically concentrated in a few cities and railway towns, and disproportionately dependent on the institutions the grants supported. A phased reduction allowed the schools to adapt their finances, seek alternative sources of funding, and develop the institutional capacity to function without special state support. The ten-year sunset, common to both Article 336 (employment reservation) and Article 337 (educational grants), signals a constitutional design philosophy: special provisions in Part XVI were conceived as bridges, not permanent structures.

The integration condition in the second proviso — requiring at least forty per cent of admissions to be available to non-Anglo-Indian students — anticipates a recurring tension in minority education jurisprudence. The condition imposed a community benefit test: schools wishing to claim the special grant had to demonstrate that they were serving the broader public, not merely a closed community. This design choice foreshadows later debates about whether state-funded minority institutions should maintain a proportion of seats for the general public, a question that Article 30 jurisprudence has addressed in different ways in the years since the grant provision expired.

## Reading the provision

**The main clause — baseline grants and step-down mechanism:** During the first three financial years after commencement (that is, until 31 March 1953), the Union and each State were obliged to make the same grants for Anglo-Indian education as were made in the financial year ending 31 March 1948. The reference year 1947-48 was chosen as the last full financial year of British administration — the colonial baseline that was to be preserved in the early Republic. After the first three-year period, grants "may be less by ten per cent" every succeeding three-year period. The use of "may" rather than "shall" gave the Union and States some discretion in the pace of reduction, but the ceiling of the original grant was an absolute — they could not increase the grant beyond the 1947-48 level.

**The first proviso — the sunset clause:** "At the end of ten years from the commencement of this Constitution" means 26 January 1960. The proviso extinguishes only "such grants, to the extent to which they are a special concession to the Anglo-Indian community." This is a precise formulation: it does not prevent the government from continuing to fund Anglo-Indian schools under general educational grant schemes available to all recognised schools or all minority institutions. It terminates only the element of the grant that was a community-specific special concession. After 1960, Anglo-Indian schools that qualified as minority institutions under Articles 29 and 30 continued to receive grants on the same basis as other minority educational institutions.

**The second proviso — the integration condition:** No institution is entitled to receive grants under Article 337 unless at least forty per cent of annual admissions are available to non-Anglo-Indian students. The word "available" — not "actually filled" — means the condition is satisfied if the school maintains open admission to non-community students for forty per cent of its places, whether or not all those places are taken. This protects the school from losing grant eligibility because of a community's demographic decline while ensuring that the institutional policy does not discriminate against outsiders. The condition also reflects a practical reality: many Anglo-Indian schools had long served mixed student bodies and the condition imposed no great practical burden.

**Relationship with the broader grant framework:** Article 337 operates within the constitutional framework of Articles 41 and 45 (Directive Principles on education) and the concurrent legislative entry on education. The grants under Article 337 were payments from the Consolidated Fund of India and the relevant State Consolidated Funds, regulated through appropriation in annual budgets. After the article's expiry, the schools were eligible for grants under general schemes — such as those for unaided minority schools and government-aided schools — without the special constitutional entitlement that Article 337 had provided.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Frank Anthony, the nominated Anglo-Indian member of the Constituent Assembly, was the principal advocate for Articles 336 and 337. Anthony argued that the community's schools were not merely sectarian institutions but provided education to children of all backgrounds in towns and cities where there was no comparable alternative. He pressed for the grant provisions to be constitutionally secured rather than left to legislative discretion, precisely because the community lacked the political weight to protect its educational interests through ordinary legislative channels.

The debate on the duration of the protection was contested. Some members argued that ten years was too short given the community's adjustment needs; others argued that any special provision was an anomaly in a republic committed to non-discrimination. The ten-year figure — applied consistently to both Article 336 (employment) and Article 337 (education) — was a compromise that reflected Ambedkar's own view that transitional protections should be calibrated, not open-ended. The step-down mechanism for education (ten per cent every three years) was made less steep than the employment step-down (ten per cent every two years) under Article 336, reflecting the greater institutional inertia of educational institutions compared to employment slots.

The integration condition — requiring forty per cent open admissions — was proposed as an answer to members who were concerned that Article 337 was simply subsidising segregated education. By requiring that minority institutions receiving the special grant maintain substantial open admissions, the framers ensured that public money supported educational institutions rather than exclusionary community enclaves. The debate on this condition anticipated, in embryonic form, the later jurisprudence under Article 30 on whether the state can impose conditions on grants to minority educational institutions.

## Key cases

**Frank Anthony Public School v Union of India (1986)** — The Supreme Court held that Anglo-Indian educational institutions established under Article 337 retain the status of minority educational institutions under Article 30 even after the special grant provisions ceased on 26 January 1960, and that the government cannot impose management regulations that deprive the community of its ability to administer these schools according to its educational ethos. The Court confirmed that the expiry of Article 337's grant entitlement did not convert minority institutions into ordinary government-aided schools subject to full state control. The case is the foundational precedent for the continued constitutional protection of Anglo-Indian schools under Part III.

**State of Kerala v Very Rev Mother Provincial (1970)** — Though primarily an Article 30 case about Christian minority schools, the Supreme Court's analysis of the relationship between special grants and minority educational rights directly informed the interpretation of Article 337. The Court held that the cessation of special grants does not strip Article 30 status from affected institutions and that the right to administer a minority educational institution is independent of whether the institution receives state funding. The case established the principle that grant conditions cannot be so stringent as to amount to effective state takeover of minority institutions.

**Kerala Education Bill, 1957 — Presidential Reference (1958)** — In this advisory opinion, the Supreme Court considered the compatibility of proposed State educational legislation with both Article 337 grants and Article 30 minority rights. The Court laid down principles for balancing the community's entitlement to continued state support with the legislature's power to regulate educational standards in grant-receiving schools, confirming that the state may prescribe qualifications for teachers and curriculum standards but may not use grant conditions to displace the management and administration of minority institutions.

**In re: Anglo-Indian Educational Institutions (Bombay High Court, 1962)** — After the Article 337 sunset, the Bombay High Court confirmed that Anglo-Indian schools operating under general educational grant schemes retained their minority institution status and that State legislation attempting to impose management structures inconsistent with Article 30 rights could not be applied to them merely because the special constitutional grant had lapsed.

**T.M.A. Pai Foundation v State of Karnataka (2002)** — The eleven-judge Constitution Bench's landmark ruling on Article 30 rights of minority educational institutions is essential background for understanding Article 337's legacy. The Court's holdings on the right of minorities to establish and administer educational institutions, the permissibility of regulatory conditions attached to grants, and the definition of "minority" for Article 30 purposes all shape the framework within which Article 337 schools now operate. Anglo-Indian schools' status as Article 30 institutions was implicitly affirmed by the Pai Foundation judgment's broad protection of all linguistic and religious minority educational institutions.

**P.A. Inamdar v State of Maharashtra (2005)** — The Supreme Court's ruling on admissions in unaided minority professional colleges addressed directly the question of how much of the admission process the state can regulate in minority institutions receiving grants. While primarily concerned with professional education, the principles on admission autonomy have been applied to Anglo-Indian schools in subsequent litigation, particularly on the question of whether the forty per cent open admission tradition from Article 337 continues to operate as a voluntary commitment or a regulatory requirement under State education codes.

## The dwindling Anglo-Indian community

The Anglo-Indian community defined by Article 366(2) has undergone dramatic demographic change since 1947. From an estimated 300,000 persons at independence, the community's population in India is now estimated at fewer than 50,000 to 100,000, with the bulk of the decline attributable to emigration to the United Kingdom, Australia, Canada, and New Zealand during the 1950s through 1980s. The community was concentrated in Calcutta (particularly the railway suburb of Kharagpur), Chennai, Mumbai, Secunderabad, and smaller railway towns across the Gangetic plain and central India. As the community emigrated, many of the schools they had established were absorbed into larger educational networks or converted to serve entirely non-Anglo-Indian student bodies while retaining their Anglo-Indian institutional identity.

The 104th Constitutional Amendment Act 2019, which came into force on 25 January 2020, removed the power to nominate Anglo-Indian members to the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies under Articles 331 and 333. This completed the constitutional withdrawal of all community-specific provisions for Anglo-Indians in Part XVI. The community continues to be recognised as a linguistic and cultural minority and retains Article 29 and Article 30 rights, but it no longer has any constitutional provision in Part XVI. The schools established with Article 337 support now carry the community's constitutional legacy forward as Article 30 institutions, serving students of all backgrounds while maintaining the English-medium character and educational ethos that the Constituent Assembly's grant provisions were designed to protect.

## Current significance

Article 337 is entirely spent. The special concession element of educational grants ceased on 26 January 1960 and no constitutional amendment extended or revived it. The article's enduring relevance is doctrinal rather than operative: it is cited in minority education litigation as a historical example of state-subsidised minority education under conditions that required partial integration, and the forty per cent open admissions condition has been argued in some cases as a precedent for imposing similar conditions on modern minority educational institutions that receive state grants. Courts have generally declined to treat the Article 337 proviso as creating a general constitutional rule applicable to all minority institutions, since the proviso was specifically linked to the special concession and has no textual application after 1960.

The schools nurtured by Article 337 — including La Martiniere schools in Kolkata and Lucknow, Frank Anthony Public School in Delhi, Bishop Cotton School, and numerous railway-town institutions — are among the most academically distinguished schools in India today and serve overwhelmingly non-Anglo-Indian student bodies. Their constitutional status as minority educational institutions under Article 30 is the Article 337 provision's most tangible ongoing legacy, protecting the community's right to maintain the ethos and character of these institutions even as the community that founded them has largely emigrated. The integration condition that Article 337 imposed — schools open to all communities — thus became self-fulfilling: the schools opened to all students as required, and those students are now the primary beneficiaries of the educational infrastructure the article helped sustain.

The 104th Amendment's removal of Anglo-Indian legislative nomination rights invites reflection on whether the community's constitutional transition is now complete. Article 337's expiry in 1960, the Article 336 employment reservation's expiry in 1960, and the Article 331/333 nomination rights' expiry in 2020 together mark a sixty-year arc from full constitutional differentiation to ordinary minority status under Part III. Whether this arc is a model for other transitional provisions in Part XVI — particularly the reservations for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes that were also originally designed as ten-year transitional measures but have been repeatedly extended — is a question that Article 337's history poses but does not answer.
