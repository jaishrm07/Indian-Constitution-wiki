## What this Article did

Article 386 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that dealt with Councils of Ministers for States in Part B of the First Schedule. It has been omitted.

Just as Article 384 dealt with the continuation of Councils of Ministers in Part A States, Article 386 made the corresponding provision for Part B States (the former acceding princely states). In those States, existing governments — which had been constituted under the integration arrangements — continued as Councils of Ministers under the new Constitution, with the existing head of government serving as the constitutional Chief Minister.

The Part B States' governments had been formed through a variety of processes following the integration of princely states. Rajpramukhs (former rulers or their nominees) served as constitutional heads in several Part B States, corresponding to the Governor's role in Part A States. Article 386 gave constitutional sanction to the continuation of existing State executive arrangements pending the full operation of the elected-government framework.

## Why it matters

The constitutional grounding of executive authority in the former princely states was essential because these states had only recently — in some cases, only months before — been integrated into India. Their governments were still establishing the institutions and procedures of democratic governance, and uncertainty about the constitutional basis for their executive authority could have undermined governance at a critical moment. Article 386 provided that certainty, ensuring that Chief Ministers and State governments in Part B States operated on solid constitutional foundations from 26 January 1950.

## Why it was omitted

Article 386 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, along with the other Part B State transitional provisions, when the entire Part A/B/C State categorisation was abolished and States were reorganised on linguistic lines.

## Historical context

The governance of former princely states during the transition to elected democratic government was a delicate matter. Some former rulers had been promised roles in the new constitutional order, and the transitional Councils of Ministers in Part B States sometimes included former rulers or their nominees alongside elected politicians. Article 386 provided a constitutional framework for this mixed arrangement to continue until fresh elections could be held.

The transitional executive arrangements in Part B States were particularly important for large former states like Hyderabad (where the Nizam had recently been removed from power following the Police Action of 1948) and Mysore (whose Maharaja had signed the instrument of accession and played a cooperative role in the integration process).

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge to the transitional executive arrangements in Part B States under Article 386 is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 386 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in providing constitutional sanction for the executive governance of former princely states during the transition to elected government, as part of the broader constitutional integration of India's diverse political units into a unified federal republic.
