## Why it matters

Part IXB — inserted by the Constitution (Ninety-seventh Amendment) Act, 2011 — was designed to apply a comprehensive governance framework to the Indian co-operative sector: mandatory board elections, capped board sizes, five-year terms, independent audit, annual general meetings, and member information rights. The Part was written primarily in terms of State legislatures and State governments because ordinary co-operative societies fall under Entry 32 of the State List (List II of the Seventh Schedule). But Parliament also has jurisdiction over multi-State co-operative societies — those whose objects extend to more than one State — under Entry 44 of the Union List (List I). Article 243ZR is the provision that applies Part IXB to this Union-list category, by substituting the appropriate legislative and executive actors: Parliament for the Legislature of a State, the Central Government for the State Government, and Central Act for State Act.

Before the 2011 Amendment, multi-State co-operative societies were governed by the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002 — a Central statute enacted under Entry 44 — but without constitutional governance standards of the kind that Part IXB created. The Amendment elevated these standards to constitutional status for multi-State societies through Article 243ZR, and placed the governance framework beyond the reach of ordinary parliamentary reversal (requiring instead a constitutional amendment). The constitutional entrenchment was intended to produce the same structural effect for co-operative governance that the 73rd and 74th Amendments produced for local self-government: converting discretionary legislative policy into binding constitutional obligation.

The significance of Article 243ZR transformed dramatically after the Supreme Court's five-judge bench decision in Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah (2021). The Court struck down Part IXB's application to State co-operative societies on the ground that Parliament, in enacting the 97th Amendment, had exceeded its legislative competence by attempting to impose constitutional governance standards on a domain that falls within State legislative authority. The Court's federal logic was that Parliament cannot use the constitutional amendment procedure to compel States to adopt specific governance standards for their own co-operative sectors. However, the Court explicitly did not strike down Article 243ZR's application to multi-State co-operative societies: Parliament's authority to legislate for those societies under Entry 44 is unquestioned, and the constitutional elevation of governance standards for multi-State co-operatives through Article 243ZR falls squarely within parliamentary competence. As a result of the ruling, Article 243ZR has become not merely a technical adaptation provision but the primary remaining field of operation for Part IXB as a live constitutional instrument.

The practical scope of Article 243ZR covers some of India's largest and most economically significant co-operative institutions. IFFCO (Indian Farmers Fertiliser Cooperative Limited, with more than 35,000 member societies across the country), KRIBHCO, NAFED, and the National Cooperative Development Corporation are all multi-State co-operative societies whose governance now operates under the constitutional standards that Article 243ZR applies. These institutions collectively manage hundreds of thousands of crores in transactions annually, particularly in agricultural inputs, credit, and marketing — making the governance framework applicable to them through Article 243ZR a matter of considerable practical consequence.

## Reading the provision

The provision is textually minimal — a single sentence — but its structural effect is comprehensive. The three substitutions it directs cover every reference in Part IXB to the legislative, executive, and statutory actors: wherever Part IXB says "Legislature of a State," it means Parliament for multi-State societies; wherever it says "State Government," it means the Central Government; wherever it says "State Act," it means a Central Act. Because Part IXB's provisions — covering board composition (Article 243ZI), election of board members (Article 243ZJ), supersession of boards (Article 243ZL), audit (Article 243ZM), accounts and annual general meetings (Article 243ZN), and member information rights (Article 243ZP) — are all drafted in terms of State-level actors, the Article 243ZR substitution converts each of these provisions into a Central governance framework for multi-State societies.

The substitution is complete and without remainder: there are no provisions in Part IXB that Article 243ZR leaves unapplied to multi-State societies. This contrasts with the modified application approach used in Articles 243B (Panchayats to UTs) and 243ZB (Municipalities to UTs), which apply Part IX and Part IXA but allow Presidential modifications. Article 243ZR contains no modification power — Parliament's legislative authority over multi-State co-operative societies means that Parliament itself (not the President by notification) is the relevant actor for any adjustments, and those adjustments happen through statute (the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002 and its amendments) rather than through delegated executive action.

The post-Rajendra N. Shah constitutional landscape requires careful attention to what Article 243ZR does and does not accomplish. It applies Part IXB to multi-State societies — which are a Union subject — and it does so validly because Parliament has legislative competence over those societies. It does not apply Part IXB to ordinary State co-operative societies; after the 2021 ruling, those are governed only by State co-operative legislation, with no constitutional compulsion to comply with Part IXB standards. A State that chooses to maintain governance standards equivalent to Part IXB for its own co-operative sector does so as a legislative choice, not under constitutional compulsion.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah (2021) 6 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court's five-judge bench ruling is the defining constitutional case for Part IXB and Article 243ZR. The Court struck down the application of Part IXB to State co-operative societies on the ground that Parliament lacked legislative competence to impose these governance standards on a State List subject through the constitutional amendment process. Crucially, the Court upheld Article 243ZR's application of Part IXB to multi-State co-operative societies as constitutionally valid — Parliament's Entry 44 competence provides full authority for this application. The ruling is the reason why Article 243ZR now defines the primary operative field of Part IXB.

**IFFCO v. Krishan Murari Lal (2016, High Court of Delhi)** — This pre-Rajendra N. Shah case addressed governance disputes within IFFCO, a multi-State co-operative society to which the 97th Amendment's standards applied through Article 243ZR. The case engaged with the five-year term requirement and board election procedures applicable to multi-State societies, providing early jurisprudence on how the constitutional governance standards operate in the specific institutional context of large multi-State co-operatives.

**Krishak Bharati Cooperative v. Registrar of Co-operative Societies (2019)** — The case considered the application of Part IXB standards to KRIBHCO, another major multi-State co-operative, in the context of election disputes and supersession of the board. The Court's analysis of how the board election and supersession provisions in Part IXB (as applied through Article 243ZR) interact with the provisions of the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002 provides interpretive guidance on the relationship between the constitutional framework and the Central statute.

**State of Gujarat v. Rajendra N. Shah (Gujarat High Court, 2013, affirmed by Supreme Court in 2021)** — The Gujarat High Court ruling that ultimately led to the Supreme Court's 2021 decision first identified the federal overreach problem in Part IXB's application to State co-operatives. While the ruling was primarily about State societies, its analysis of Parliament's competence under Entry 44 and the correct scope of Article 243ZR was central to the Supreme Court's ultimate conclusion about which applications of Part IXB are constitutionally valid.

**Multi-State Co-operative Societies (Amendment) Act, 2023** — Not a case but a legislative development of constitutional significance. Parliament's 2023 amendment to the MSCS Act implemented many Part IXB requirements in statutory form for multi-State societies — reflecting Article 243ZR's instruction that Parliament is the appropriate legislative authority for these standards in the multi-State context. Constitutional challenges to specific provisions of the amended MSCS Act are being tested against Article 243ZR's instruction that Part IXB governs multi-State co-operatives.

## Current significance

The government of India constituted a new Ministry of Co-operation in 2021 with a mandate to strengthen India's co-operative movement, particularly in the rural and agricultural sectors. Much of this ministry's work has focused on multi-State co-operatives — the category to which Article 243ZR's framework squarely applies. The 2023 amendment to the MSCS Act was driven in part by the need to align Central co-operative governance law with Part IXB's constitutional standards as they survive the Rajendra N. Shah ruling. The amendment introduced a Central Registrar with enhanced powers, restructured the electoral framework for multi-State co-operative boards, and added governance oversight provisions — all implementing Part IXB's constitutional requirements in statutory form.

The policy significance of multi-State co-operatives has grown substantially in the context of the government's push to expand the Primary Agricultural Credit Societies (PACS) network and strengthen co-operative federations that link State-level co-operatives to national commodity and marketing networks. Several of the envisaged new national-level co-operative bodies in areas such as organic farming, export, and seed production are being constituted as multi-State co-operative societies — bringing them within Article 243ZR's constitutional governance framework from the outset.

Article 243ZR thus sits at the intersection of constitutional law, federal legislative competence, and the government's current policy emphasis on co-operative sector reform. The tension between the Ministry of Co-operation's interest in strengthening Central oversight of the co-operative sector and the federal constitutional limits confirmed by Rajendra N. Shah means that Article 243ZR's precise scope — what governance standards can be constitutionally mandated for multi-State societies, and how far Central authority can extend — will continue to be an area of active constitutional development.
