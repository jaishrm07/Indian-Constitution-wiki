## Why it matters

The guarantee of universal adult franchise is among the most transformative provisions the Constituent Assembly enacted. In the mid-twentieth century, adult suffrage was not a given in newly independent nations — many retained property qualifications, literacy requirements, or other filters inherited from colonial arrangements. India's framers, led by B.R. Ambedkar and Jawaharlal Nehru, chose a clean break: every adult citizen, regardless of wealth, education, caste, or religion, would vote on equal terms. At the time of the first general elections in 1951–52, this enrolled approximately 173 million voters — the largest democratic electorate in history.

The political stakes of Article 326 are not merely historical. The franchise is the constitutional instrument through which citizens exercise sovereignty — it is what makes governments derive their just powers from the consent of the governed. Any restriction on who may vote that operates outside Article 326's enumerated grounds strikes at the foundation of democratic legitimacy. This is why courts have been consistently protective of franchise rights even as they have deferred to the election petition procedure for challenges to specific election outcomes. And it is why proposals that could operationally disenfranchise voters — whether through Aadhaar linkage, biometric de-registration, or complex residency requirements — generate constitutional challenge under Article 326 before they generate litigation under any other provision.

The 61st Amendment's lowering of the voting age from 21 to 18 in 1989 reflected an evolving understanding of the relationship between civic maturity and citizenship. Young people between 18 and 21 could be conscripted, could enter contracts, could marry, and bore the same civic responsibilities as their older compatriots — yet had no say in choosing the governments that made the laws binding them. The amendment was passed with broad cross-party support and expanded the electorate by an estimated 40 to 50 million at the time of enactment. It is now the largest youth voter cohort in the world.

## Reading the provision

**"The elections to the House of the People and to the Legislative Assembly of every State shall be on the basis of adult suffrage":** Article 326 applies specifically to elections to the Lok Sabha and to State Legislative Assemblies. It does not directly regulate elections to the Rajya Sabha (elected by State Assemblies), State Legislative Councils (where they exist), or elections to local bodies. The franchise for those bodies is governed by different constitutional provisions and statutes. This means the universal adult franchise guarantee in its direct constitutional form covers the two most important democratic elections — national and State.

**"Every person who is a citizen of India":** The franchise is tied to citizenship, not residence, domicile, or taxpayer status. Non-citizens — including Overseas Citizens of India — do not have a constitutional right to vote. Citizenship is the constitutional threshold, and Article 5 through 11 govern citizenship; Article 326 then operates on that citizenship base to guarantee franchise. Stateless persons and non-citizen permanent residents are not constitutionally entitled to register as voters under this provision.

**"Who is not less than [eighteen years] of age":** The bracketed figure reflects the 61st Amendment substitution from twenty-one to eighteen. The qualifying age is fixed on a date to be determined by law, allowing Parliament and State Legislatures to specify a reference date for age calculation — typically the date the electoral roll is finalised or a specified cut-off date.

**"And is not otherwise disqualified ... on the ground of non-residence, unsoundness of mind, crime or corrupt or illegal practice":** These four grounds are exhaustive. Non-residence means the person is not ordinarily resident in the constituency. Unsoundness of mind means legal incapacity as determined by competent authority. Crime covers disqualifications imposed by the Representation of the People Act and similar statutes for specified offences. Corrupt or illegal practice covers persons disqualified for proven electoral corruption. Parliament can give content to these grounds through legislation, but it cannot add new categories of disqualification beyond these four.

**"Shall be entitled to be registered as a voter":** The entitlement is to registration — not to actual voting at a specific election on a specific date. A citizen who meets the criteria is entitled to be on the roll; the practical exercise of the vote at a particular election depends on being on the roll for that constituency and presenting themselves at the polling booth. Courts have treated the entitlement to registration as itself a constitutional right, not merely a statutory privilege, meaning that arbitrary refusal of registration or deletion from the roll may be challenged as a constitutional violation.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The decision to include universal adult franchise without any property or literacy qualification was debated at length. Some members argued that the illiterate rural poor could not be expected to make an informed electoral choice and that a literacy requirement was a reasonable prerequisite. Ambedkar, Nehru, and the majority responded that literacy was itself a product of the inequalities the Constitution was meant to remedy — to exclude the illiterate from the franchise was to exclude the very people who had the most at stake in democratic government. If the poor and illiterate were left off the voter roll, governments would face no democratic accountability for maintaining their poverty and illiteracy. The argument won decisively, and India chose universal adult franchise at a moment when most of its electorate was non-literate.

The original age threshold of twenty-one was a conventional default — most democracies at the time used twenty-one as the adult franchise threshold. The case for lowering it to eighteen developed over subsequent decades as the age of legal majority for most other purposes settled at eighteen, making the asymmetry between civic responsibility and political participation increasingly difficult to justify.

## Key cases

**Vijay Narain Singh v State of Bihar AIR 1954 SC 202** — The Supreme Court examined whether restrictions on voting rights imposed by provincial electoral laws were consistent with the Article 326 guarantee of adult suffrage. The Court upheld the principle that Article 326 is a self-operative constitutional guarantee and that any statutory restriction on the franchise must fall squarely within one of the four enumerated grounds of disqualification. Restrictions falling outside those grounds are unconstitutional regardless of their administrative rationale. This remains the foundational authority for the proposition that the four disqualification grounds are exhaustive.

**Jyoti Basu v Debi Ghosal (1982) 1 SCC 691** — The Court explained that the right to vote, while rooted in Article 326, is a statutory right in the sense that its exercise is regulated by the Representation of the People Act. Article 326 provides the constitutional floor of entitlement; statutory regulation implements rather than restricts that entitlement when it operates within constitutional bounds. The distinction matters because election petitions — which are creatures of the RPA — are the proper forum for contesting violations of the franchise in specific elections, while Article 326 remains the constitutional standard against which all franchise legislation is measured.

**People's Union for Civil Liberties v Union of India (2013) 10 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court held that voters have a constitutional right to reject all candidates by recording a NOTA (None of the Above) vote. The Court anchored this right in Article 19(1)(a) read with Article 326, reasoning that meaningful exercise of the adult franchise requires that voters be able to express dissatisfaction with all available candidates and that this expression of dissent is a form of constitutionally protected speech. The judgment led to the introduction of the NOTA option on electronic voting machines. NOTA votes do not change the election outcome — the highest polling candidate wins regardless — but they create a publicly recorded measure of voter dissatisfaction.

**Anoop Baranwal v Union of India (2023) 6 SCC 161** — The five-judge Constitution Bench reinforced that the independence of the Election Commission, which administers the franchise under Article 326, is itself a constitutional requirement flowing from the Part XV framework. The Court held that Article 326's universal suffrage guarantee is only meaningful if the body administering it is genuinely independent of the executive and political parties whose electoral fortunes depend on how the franchise is administered. The direction on the appointment process for Election Commissioners was grounded in this structural logic.

**Kuldip Nayar v Union of India (2006) 7 SCC 1** — While primarily addressing Rajya Sabha election procedures, the five-judge bench confirmed that Part XV's universal franchise framework — including Article 326 — creates a unified democratic scheme that Parliament may develop but not dismember. The Court treated the adult franchise guarantee as constitutional bedrock against which the validity of parliamentary election legislation is assessed.

## Aadhaar linkage and disenfranchisement concerns

One of the most significant contemporary debates about Article 326 concerns the proposal to link Aadhaar numbers to electoral rolls under amendments to the Representation of the People Act enacted in 2021. The stated purpose is to eliminate duplicate and ghost registrations that inflate the rolls and facilitate impersonation. Critics argue that any technology-driven deletion of voter entries — whether due to biometric failure, data mismatches, or administrative errors — risks disenfranchising genuine voters in violation of the Article 326 entitlement to registration. Courts have required that such programmes be implemented with procedural safeguards and that deletion not proceed without adequate notice and an opportunity to contest. The Supreme Court is yet to finally rule on whether the compulsory linkage mechanism is consistent with Article 326 read with the right to privacy under Article 21.

## Current significance

Article 326's ongoing significance manifests across several active debates. The question of voter roll accuracy — whether the Electoral Photo Identity Card programme and Aadhaar linkage are administered in a way that reduces genuine voter registrations along with fraudulent ones — directly tests whether the constitutional entitlement is being administratively fulfilled or administratively curtailed. The situation of internal migrant workers, who are registered in home constituencies but resident elsewhere, raises questions about whether the non-residence disqualification is being applied in a manner that effectively disenfranchises mobile workers. The NOTA jurisprudence continues to evolve as courts consider whether NOTA majorities should have any legal consequence for the election outcome — a debate that turns on the scope of the Article 326 franchise as a vehicle for positive electoral choice versus mere rejection.

Proposals for simultaneous elections to Parliament and all State Assemblies implicate Article 326 by potentially extending or abbreviating legislative terms, affecting when newly eligible eighteen-year-old voters can first exercise their constitutional entitlement. Any reform that delays an election beyond a legislature's normal term effectively deprives those newly eligible voters of the franchise for a longer period — a concern that must be weighed against any administrative efficiency justification for simultaneity.
