  77. Conduct of business of the Government of India.—(1) All executive action of the Government of India shall be expressed to be taken in the name of the President.

## Why it matters

Article 77 provides the constitutional form for all Union executive action. The vesting of executive power in the President under Article 53 would be legally incoherent without a rule specifying how that power is exercised in practice through a bureaucratic government with hundreds of departments, thousands of officers, and millions of daily decisions. Article 77 supplies that rule: every executive act must be expressed in the President's name, authenticated according to rules made by the President, and transacted according to rules that allocate ministerial responsibilities. Together, these three clauses create the constitutional architecture for India's administrative state.

The attribution requirement in clause (1) performs a function that is both formal and substantive. At the formal level it ensures legal coherence: there is a single constitutional locus — the President — from which all executive authority flows, even if the practical decision-making rests with Ministers and officials. At the substantive level it creates a constitutional accountability mechanism: because executive action is attributed to the President, and the President acts on Cabinet advice under Article 74, every executive decision is implicitly endorsed by the Cabinet. A Cabinet that takes a decision without following the Transaction of Business Rules cannot later disclaim responsibility by arguing the action was taken by a subordinate officer in the President's name — the attribution traces the action back to the Cabinet's constitutional responsibility.

The authentication requirement in clause (2) resolves a constitutional tension that would otherwise be acute: the President cannot personally sign or authorise every order issued by the Government of India, yet those orders must be constitutionally valid. Clause (2) declares that properly authenticated orders cannot be challenged on the ground that the President did not personally make or execute them. The authentication system — typically requiring the signature of a Joint Secretary or a higher official under the Presidential Authentication Rules — substitutes for personal presidential participation while maintaining the constitutional attribution.

The rule-making power in clause (3) — both the Transaction of Business Rules and the Allocation of Business Rules — is the constitutional basis for the operational structure of the Union government. The Transaction of Business Rules define the procedures for taking government decisions: which matters must go to the Cabinet, which can be decided by individual Ministers, which require Prime Ministerial approval, and what procedures govern inter-ministerial consultation. The Allocation of Business Rules define which Ministry has jurisdiction over each subject, preventing overlap and establishing clear lines of ministerial responsibility. These rules have constitutional force: their violation can render executive action invalid.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1)'s phrase "shall be expressed to be taken in the name of the President" is a constitutional command, not a directory guideline. "Expressed to be taken" means the attribution must appear on the face of the order or instrument — it is not enough that the order is impliedly authorised. Every written executive instrument of the Government of India must carry an express attribution to the President. The consequence of missing this attribution is that the instrument is not a valid exercise of executive power under Article 53, making it susceptible to challenge as lacking constitutional foundation.

Clause (2)'s authentication framework has two components. First, the President makes rules specifying how orders must be authenticated — typically by the signatures of senior officials under the Authentication (Orders and Other Instruments) Rules, 1958. Second, an instrument that is so authenticated "shall not be called in question" on the ground that the President did not personally make or execute it. The "shall not be called in question" language is peremptory — it forecloses a specific class of challenge. Courts have consistently held that while the authentication bars the "not personally made" challenge, it does not bar challenges on other grounds — such as that the order was made in violation of the Transaction of Business Rules, or that it exceeded the constitutional powers of the executive.

Clause (3) uses "shall make rules" — a mandatory obligation, not a discretionary power. The President must make rules for the convenient transaction of business and for ministerial allocation. This obligation has been discharged through the Government of India (Transaction of Business) Rules, 1961, and the Government of India (Allocation of Business) Rules, 1961. Courts have treated these rules as constitutional instruments: a decision taken in violation of their procedures is not merely administratively irregular but constitutionally defective. The Article 77(3) obligation creates an enforceable procedural baseline for Cabinet governance.

## Attribution versus personal authorisation

The most important interpretive principle arising from Article 77 is the distinction between constitutional attribution and personal authorisation. All executive orders are attributed to the President by constitutional mandate, but the vast majority are made by Ministers, officials, or delegated authorities under the rules made under Article 77(3). Article 77(2) expressly forecloses any challenge to the validity of a duly authenticated order on the ground that the President did not personally make it.

This distinction is constitutionally important because it clarifies the nature of executive power in a parliamentary democracy: real decision-making authority rests with the Council of Ministers under Articles 74 and 75, but constitutional and legal form require that authority to be expressed through the President's name. The attribution creates legal coherence — there is a single constitutional locus for all executive action — while the authentication rules provide practical mechanisms for delegating that action through the administrative hierarchy.

The distinction also matters for understanding the relationship between Article 77 and presidential discretion. When the President returns a Bill under Article 111 or withholds assent, the formal attribution requirement of Article 77 applies — but those particular acts are constitutional exercises of presidential authority, not delegated administrative decisions. Presidential acts where the President exercises personal constitutional judgment (as opposed to formal attribution of Cabinet decisions) are in a different constitutional category from routine administrative orders issued in the President's name.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Constitution Bench's analysis of the executive power of the Union included a detailed examination of Article 77, confirming that the requirement to act in the President's name is a matter of constitutional form rather than personal Presidential participation. The Court held that acts done by constitutional or statutory authorities, if done in accordance with the rules made under Article 77(3), are valid executive acts. The President's constitutional role is that of formal head and nominal author of all executive action, while the Council of Ministers is the real author whose decisions are attributed to the President by constitutional convention and Article 77's mandatory attribution rule.

**State of Punjab v Sodhi Sukhdev Singh (1961)** — The Supreme Court held that an order passed in the name of the President but in violation of the rules made under Article 77(3) could be challenged as invalid. This is a critical limitation on the scope of clause (2)'s protection: while clause (2) bars the "not personally made" challenge, it does not protect orders that violate the Transaction of Business Rules. The authentication bars one class of challenge; violations of the rules themselves constitute a separate and separately cognisable ground of invalidity. Courts have set aside executive decisions made without following the prescribed allocation of business or inter-ministerial consultation requirements.

**Union of India v Brigadier P.S. Gill (2012)** — The Court examined the scope of Presidential orders under service law in the defence forces context, engaging with Article 77's requirement that defence service orders be expressed in the President's name. The judgment confirmed that the constitutional attribution requirement in Article 77(1) applies to defence services orders, making the President the formal constitutional commander-in-chief of all such actions. The Court confirmed that the constitutional attribution requirement is substantive rather than merely ceremonial — orders that fail to comply with it are not constitutionally valid executive acts under Article 53.

**Bachan Singh v State of Punjab (1980)** — The Supreme Court's constitutional examination of capital punishment and presidential clemency under Article 72 engaged with the relationship between Articles 72, 74, and 77. The Court confirmed that clemency orders under Article 72 must be expressed in the President's name and authenticated under the rules made pursuant to Article 77(2), to constitute valid legal grants of clemency. The interaction between the clemency power and the Article 77 attribution and authentication framework establishes that even the most personal of presidential constitutional powers — the mercy power — is subject to the formal requirements of Article 77.

**Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973)** — The Constitution Bench's examination of the basic structure of the Constitution included observations about the nature of executive power and the rules of its exercise. While Article 77 was not a central provision in the Kesavananda debate, the Court's recognition that the rules governing executive action have constitutional rather than merely statutory force — and that they cannot be violated even by a Cabinet with overwhelming parliamentary support — traces back to the mandatory character of Article 77(3) and the constitutional foundation it gives to the Transaction of Business Rules.

## Current significance

Article 77 has quiet but significant practical importance in administrative law. When executive orders are challenged before courts — whether in service matters, regulatory decisions, or policy notifications — the question of whether they were properly made in the name of the President and authenticated in accordance with the prescribed rules is a threshold issue. Decisions taken without following the Transaction of Business Rules have been set aside by courts, making Article 77(3) an important accountability mechanism for orderly Cabinet governance.

The Allocation of Business Rules enacted under Article 77(3) have acquired contemporary significance in disputes about which Ministry has competence over emerging policy areas that cut across traditional departmental boundaries — artificial intelligence regulation, space commerce, digital economy, and data governance all involve multiple Ministries. When the rules have not been updated to clearly allocate jurisdiction over new subjects, courts applying Article 77(3) must determine which Ministry's orders are constitutionally valid, since conflicting orders from different departments cannot all be valid exercises of the single executive authority vested in the President.

The relationship between Article 77 and political accountability is one of the most significant but underappreciated dimensions of Indian constitutional law. Because all executive action is attributed to the President and the President acts on Cabinet advice, every executive act creates a paper trail of constitutional attribution — from the President's name on the order, through the authenticating official, to the Ministry that proposed it under the Transaction of Business Rules. This chain of attribution, when enforced by courts applying State of Punjab v Sodhi Sukhdev Singh's principle, provides a constitutional framework for holding the executive accountable through the procedural requirements of Article 77 even when substantive constitutional challenges are difficult to mount.
