plainEnglish: >-
  Parliament can by law bring a new State into the Union — either by admitting an existing external territory or by establishing a wholly new one — and may attach whatever terms and conditions it considers appropriate. The power is outward-facing — it applies to territory not yet part of India rather than to the reorganisation of existing Indian territory, which is Article 3's domain. "Admit into the Union" contemplates bringing in a political entity that already exists as a separate unit, while "establish" allows Parliament to create a new State from scratch. The phrase "on such terms and conditions as it thinks fit" grants Parliament enormous latitude — it can attach special autonomy arrangements, cultural protections, resource reservations, or migration controls. The most celebrated use of Article 2 was Sikkim's absorption in 1975, which also inserted Article 371F with permanent protective provisions for Sikkim's people and laws. Together with Article 4, any law under Article 2 that amends the First or Fourth Schedule is deemed not to be an amendment of the Constitution, enabling admission by ordinary legislation. Article 2 remains latent — it is the constitutional mechanism for any future incorporation of territory into India, whether by negotiated merger, treaty, or voluntary accession.

## Why it matters

Article 2 is the constitutional embodiment of India's open territorial architecture. The framers crafted it with deliberate breadth, conscious that the Union had not yet stabilised: hundreds of princely states were still being integrated, border questions with Pakistan and China were unresolved, and territories like Pondicherry (then a French possession), Goa (Portuguese), and Sikkim (a protectorate) remained outside the First Schedule. A short, capacious provision that gave Parliament maximum flexibility was exactly what the moment required.

The article's significance goes beyond the accidents of post-colonial geography. It reflects a foundational principle of the Constitution — that the Union's territorial extent is not frozen at commencement but is capable of deliberate growth. This is what distinguishes Article 2 from the detailed reorganisation machinery in Article 3: Article 3 is about reshaping what already exists inside India; Article 2 is about bringing the outside in. The two provisions together make India's territorial architecture one of the most legally flexible in the world, enabling expansion by ordinary legislation rather than the cumbersome referendum processes that other constitutions require for significant territorial changes.

The "terms and conditions" power embedded in Article 2 has had lasting doctrinal consequences. The asymmetric autonomy provisions that characterise India's north-east — Article 371A for Nagaland, Article 371C for Manipur, Article 371F for Sikkim, Article 371G for Mizoram — all trace their origin to conditions attached at the point of admission or full statehood grant. Understanding those special provisions requires understanding their source in Article 2's latitude. When the Supreme Court in 2023 upheld the abrogation of Article 370, the status of conditions-at-admission as a category of constitutional commitment was inevitably implicated: could Parliament unilaterally revoke terms it had earlier imposed on itself as a precondition of admission?

## Reading the provision

**"Parliament may by law"** — The power is vested in Parliament acting through legislation, not in the executive by executive order. The use of "law" rather than "ordinance" or "executive action" confirms that the admission of a new State requires the full democratic process of parliamentary enactment. An ordinary majority suffices; Article 2 does not attract the special-majority requirement of Article 368, and Article 4 reinforces this by deeming such laws not to be constitutional amendments.

**"admit into the Union"** — This limb addresses territory that already exists as a distinct political unit but stands outside the Union. A protectorate, a treaty-state, a neighbouring sovereign's territory that has voted to merge — all would be admitted, not established. Sikkim in 1975 is the paradigm. The admission power operates on an entity that already has a political identity; Parliament's act is one of incorporation rather than creation.

**"or establish, new States"** — The establishment power is wider. It allows Parliament to create a State where none existed in that form — most commonly by elevating a Union Territory to full Statehood. When Goa, Daman and Diu became a Union Territory in 1961 (after the military action to end Portuguese control) and eventually became a State in 1987, the process drew on both the integration-by-legislation technique and the statehood-grant power. Similarly, the north-eastern States that moved from Union Territory or Part C State status to full Statehood passed through this limb.

**"on such terms and conditions as it thinks fit"** — This is the most consequential phrase in the article. It allows Parliament to impose conditions that remain binding on both the Centre and the new State. Such conditions can include: special protections for pre-existing local laws (Article 371F preserved Sikkim's laws and customs, and its pre-Union treaties); reserved legislative powers; restrictions on acquisition of property by outsiders; protections for employment of local people; and fiscal guarantees. The phrase was interpreted in Maganbhai Patel to give Parliament near-plenary flexibility and to require no concurrence from the State legislatures of other States.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

Article 2 attracted relatively little controversy in the Constituent Assembly compared to the intense debates on Article 3 (reorganisation). The drafting committee's position, reflected in B.R. Ambedkar's explanatory interventions, was that the provision should be brief and enabling: the Constitution would set the framework, Parliament would handle the specifics. The Assembly accepted that any conditions attached at the point of admission would need to be worked out case by case and that locking detailed terms into the Constitution itself would create rigidity better avoided.

The more contested question was the relationship between Articles 2 and 3. Early drafts drew a less sharp line between the two. The final demarcation — Article 2 for external territories, Article 3 for internal reorganisation — was clarified in successive committee revisions. The Constituent Assembly also debated whether admission of new States should require ratification by existing State legislatures, on the model of some federal constitutions. The proposal was rejected: Parliament alone would have the power, consistent with the general centripetal tendency of the Constitution's federal design. The Advisory Committee's report on this point emphasised that given the uncertainty of the post-Partition situation, requiring multi-State ratification would be an unacceptable constraint on the Centre's capacity to manage territorial integration.

## Key cases

**In re Berubari Union (1960) AIR 1960 SC 845** — The Supreme Court examined whether the Nehru-Noon Pact, which ceded part of the Berubari Union to Pakistan, required a constitutional amendment or could be done by executive agreement. The Court held that cession of Indian territory to a foreign country required a constitutional amendment under Article 368, not an executive act, and drew the critical distinction between cession (territory going out) and admission (territory coming in). The ruling clarified that Article 2 is an inward-facing provision — it empowers Parliament to bring territory in, but provides no basis for sending Indian territory out.

**Maganbhai Ishwarbhai Patel v. Union of India (1970) 3 SCC 400** — The Supreme Court held that Parliament's power under Article 2 to set "terms and conditions" for admission is wide and does not require ratification by State legislatures or a constitutional amendment. The Court confirmed that even a significant relinquishment of Indian sovereign rights in a border demarcation treaty was a matter of executive prerogative under the foreign affairs power, not requiring a referendum or State consent. The case established that the Article 2 "terms" power is Parliament's alone to exercise and define.

**State of Sikkim v. Surendra Prasad Sharma (1994) 5 SCC 282** — After Sikkim's admission in 1975, disputes arose over whether Article 371F's protections (particularly the guarantee of Sikkim's existing laws and the representation arrangements for different communities) were justiciable. The Supreme Court held that the protective conditions attached at the time of Sikkim's admission under Article 2 and embodied in Article 371F are enforceable and cannot be whittled down by ordinary Central legislation that fails to address the special constitutional status Sikkim was accorded at admission.

**State of Manipur v. Surjakumar Okram (2022) 1 SCC 558** — The Court examined the constitutional history of Manipur's transition from Part C State status to full statehood and confirmed that conditions Parliament attaches under Article 2 at the point of admission or statehood grant remain binding until expressly altered by law. The case also considered whether the transitional provisions that accompanied Manipur's statehood continued to have effect after the State's first elections under the new constitutional status.

**In re Article 370 (Constitution Bench, 2023)** — While primarily about the abrogation of Article 370's operative provisions, the Constitution Bench's analysis engaged directly with the nature of conditions-at-admission and the competence of Parliament to alter them. The Court's reasoning about the Centre's power to reshape the constitutional status of a formerly special-status territory carries implications for Article 2's "terms and conditions" power wherever those terms were embedded in the Constitution (as with Article 371F for Sikkim) rather than merely in statute.

**Pratul Kumar Seal v. Chief Settlement Commissioner (1969)** — An early case testing the constitutional mechanics of territory integration through law under Articles 2 and 3. The Court held that in cases of disputed territorial status the legislation passed by Parliament pursuant to these articles is definitive, and Courts cannot re-examine the underlying political determinations about which territory should be part of India.

## Current significance

Article 2 is dormant but not obsolete. As of 2025 there are no territories undergoing active admission proceedings, but the provision remains the constitutional mechanism for any future expansion of the Union. The situation of territories on India's periphery — whether in the context of boundary negotiations with China over the Line of Actual Control, the status of Aksai Chin, or the legal position of territories administered but not internationally recognised — all potentially engage Article 2 if they were ever to be formally incorporated.

The article also feeds into the ongoing constitutional debate about the reversibility of conditions-at-admission. After the abrogation of Article 370 in 2019, academic and judicial discourse has sharpened around the question: when Parliament uses Article 2 to admit a territory on stated terms — including terms that Parliament later embeds in the Constitution as special provisions — can those terms be unilaterally altered by Parliament without the consent of the State that was admitted on the strength of those terms? The 2023 Constitution Bench judgment addressed this obliquely but did not resolve it comprehensively for all contexts.

Finally, Article 2 gives constitutional grounding to the north-east's special autonomy arrangements. Every time a court in Nagaland, Manipur, Sikkim, or Mizoram is asked to assess the scope of a protective provision under the Article 371 cluster, the interpretive question traces back through that cluster to the original Article 2 "terms and conditions" at admission. The article is therefore not historical curiosity but an active source of live constitutional rights.
