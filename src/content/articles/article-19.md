  (b) to assemble peaceably and without arms;

## Why it matters

Article 19 is the constitutional home of the freedoms that define civic and economic life. The right to speak, to gather, to organise, to move, to settle, and to work are not merely individual liberties — they are the preconditions for democratic citizenship, collective action, economic activity, and human dignity. Together, they form the constitutional infrastructure through which people participate in public life, challenge power, and shape their own futures.

The article's design reflects a sophisticated understanding of the relationship between rights and their limitations. Unlike many constitutional systems that guarantee rights and then leave it to the legislature to define permissible restrictions, Article 19 specifies in the text itself both the content of each right and the exhaustive list of grounds on which restrictions may be imposed. The State cannot justify restricting assembly, for instance, by pointing to public health — that ground is not in Article 19(3); it can only restrict assembly on grounds of sovereignty and integrity or public order. This specificity makes Article 19 one of the most structurally precise rights provisions in any constitution.

The "reasonable restrictions" standard is the principal battlefield in Article 19 litigation. A restriction may fall within the permitted grounds — a law regulating speech in the interests of "public order" is within Article 19(2)'s scope — but still be struck down as unreasonable if it is overbroad, disproportionate, vague, or more extensive than necessary to achieve the permitted purpose. The Court has increasingly moved toward a proportionality standard that requires not just formal compliance with the grounds of restriction but a demonstration that the restriction is the least restrictive means of achieving the legitimate governmental purpose.

Article 19 is limited to citizens — unlike Articles 14 and 21, which extend to all persons. Non-citizens and corporations cannot invoke Article 19 rights as their own, though they may receive protection through derivative claims (for example, a corporation can raise a challenge under Article 14, and the impact on its employees' Article 19 rights may be a relevant consideration). The phrase "all citizens" also means that the protections apply throughout the territory of India — a citizen exercising their Article 19 rights in Kashmir, Manipur, or Nagaland has the same constitutional protection as one in Delhi or Mumbai.

## Reading the provision

### Article 19(1)(a) — Freedom of speech and expression

Freedom of speech and expression is the most litigated and most consequential of the Article 19 freedoms. The Court has construed it to include not just the content of speech but the right to receive information, the right to know, the freedom of the press, the right to broadcast, the right to communicate through the internet, and — as held in Navtej Singh Johar — the right to express one's identity and intimate choices.

The permitted grounds for restricting speech under Article 19(2) are exhaustive: sovereignty and integrity of India, security of the State, friendly relations with foreign States, public order, decency or morality, contempt of court, defamation, and incitement to an offence. A law that restricts speech for any other reason — say, administrative inconvenience, economic competition, or sensitivity of public figures — is beyond Article 19(2) and is invalid. The Court has also held that even within permitted grounds, the restriction must have a proximate (not remote) connection to the harm the ground is designed to prevent. A restriction on speech in the interests of "public order" must address a direct and immediate threat to public order — not any conceivable downstream risk.

The right to silence (not to speak) has been held to be within Article 19(1)(a). In Bijoe Emmanuel v State of Kerala (1986), three Jehovah's Witness children were held entitled to refuse to sing the national anthem on religious grounds — their refusal to speak was protected by the same right that protects the choice of speech.

### Article 19(1)(b) — Right to peaceful assembly

The right to assemble peaceably and without arms is the constitutional basis for the right to protest, demonstrate, and take collective public action. Assembly that is armed or that is not peaceful does not attract constitutional protection. The permitted ground for restricting assembly is sovereignty and integrity of India or public order — notably narrower than the list for speech restrictions (there is no ground of "decency or morality" in Article 19(3)). Laws requiring prior permission for public assemblies must therefore be justified under one of these two grounds, and blanket prohibitions on assembly in particular areas must show a nexus to sovereignty/integrity or public order.

The intersection of Article 19(1)(b) with the right to protest has been directly engaged in challenges to the Section 144 of the Code of Criminal Procedure — the magistrate's power to prohibit assemblies in specified areas. The Supreme Court has upheld Section 144 in principle as a reasonable restriction, but has held that an order under Section 144 must be supported by material facts showing a specific threat to public order and cannot be used as a routine tool to prevent lawful assemblies.

### Article 19(1)(c) — Freedom to form associations

The right to form associations, unions, and co-operative societies is the constitutional foundation for trade unions, political parties, professional associations, civil society organisations, and all forms of collective organisation. The restriction grounds are sovereignty/integrity, public order, and morality — again narrower than speech restrictions.

The right to form an association does not carry with it the right to have that association recognised or given particular legal status by the State, and it does not prevent the State from regulating the internal governance of associations for reasons of public order or morality. But the right to organise collectively and advance common interests is constitutionally protected, and a law that effectively prevents the formation of any association in a particular sector or domain must satisfy the reasonable restriction standard.

### Article 19(1)(d) and (e) — Freedom of movement and residence

The rights to move freely throughout India and to reside and settle anywhere in India are protected under Article 19, with restrictions permitted in the interests of the general public or for the protection of Scheduled Tribes. The Scheduled Tribes carve-out reflects the constitutional concern that unrestricted entry into scheduled areas by non-tribal populations could undermine the tribal autonomy that the Fifth and Sixth Schedules are designed to protect.

These rights have been relied on in challenges to laws that exclude certain categories of persons from particular territories (such as restrictions on non-residents settling in certain States or Union territories) and in challenges to externment orders that require specific individuals to leave or stay away from particular areas. The right to movement has also been engaged in challenges to curfews and restrictions on movement imposed during civil unrest.

### Article 19(1)(g) — Right to practise any profession or carry on any trade or business

The freedom to practise a profession or carry on a trade, occupation, or business is one of the most litigated of the Article 19 rights in the economic domain. It covers not just formal professions (medicine, law, engineering) but all commercial activity including retail trade, manufacturing, and service businesses. The restriction ground is the general public interest, and the Article specifically allows the State to prescribe professional or technical qualifications and to carry on any trade or business through a State monopoly.

The reasonableness of professional licensing requirements has been extensively litigated: requirements that are rationally connected to protecting the public from incompetent practitioners (medical qualifications, engineering licensing) are valid, but requirements that merely protect incumbents from competition without any public-safety rationale are not. The right also interacts with Article 14 (the State cannot grant licences or concessions to some practitioners on arbitrary grounds while denying them to others) and with freedom of trade under Part XIII.

## The "reasonable restrictions" standard

The term "reasonable restrictions" in clauses (2) through (6) is the constitutional standard against which every restriction on Article 19 rights is measured. Reasonableness has two dimensions in the Court's jurisprudence.

The first is substantive reasonableness — is the restriction rationally related to a legitimate state objective, and is the limitation on the right proportionate to the benefit achieved? A law that restricts speech by anyone who criticises any government official is both overbroad (it sweeps in constitutionally protected speech) and disproportionate (the restriction is far greater than necessary to prevent any concrete harm). Such a law is unreasonable regardless of which permitted ground it invokes.

The second is procedural reasonableness — does the law or order restricting the right include adequate safeguards against arbitrary exercise? A law that gives the executive absolute, unguided discretion to prohibit speech, assembly, or movement is unreasonable because the discretion itself creates the risk of arbitrary restriction. The Court has consistently required that restrictions on Article 19 rights be defined in advance, be judicially reviewable, and be subject to procedural checks.

The proportionality standard, increasingly influential since Puttaswamy (2017), requires the Court to ask: (a) does the restriction pursue a legitimate aim? (b) is the restriction rationally connected to that aim? (c) is it the least restrictive means available? (d) do the benefits of the restriction outweigh its costs to the right? This four-part test, derived from international human rights law and comparative constitutional practice, is stricter than the earlier "not excessive" or "not arbitrary" standards and has been applied in internet shutdown cases (Anuradha Bhasin) and in challenges to the Personal Data Protection legislative framework.

## Key cases

**Romesh Thappar v State of Madras (1950)** — Among the earliest Article 19 cases, the Court struck down a Madras law that permitted the State Government to prohibit the circulation of a journal without judicial order. The law was held to be beyond the permitted grounds for restricting speech (the 1950 Constitution allowed restrictions for "undermining the security of the State" but not for the less serious ground of "public order" — this was subsequently amended by the First Amendment). The case established from the very beginning that Article 19's list of permitted grounds is exhaustive and that the State cannot justify speech restrictions by reference to any other rationale.

**Sakal Papers v Union of India (1962)** — The Court struck down a newspaper industry regulation that restricted the number of pages and prevented papers from charging below a minimum price. The State argued this was a regulation of the newspaper industry, not a restriction on speech. The Court held that a law that has the direct effect of preventing a newspaper from reaching its readers restricts freedom of the press, and that it must be justified under Article 19(2)'s grounds, not under any general regulatory power. This case established the principle that Article 19(1)(a) is violated by indirect as well as direct restrictions on expression.

**Bennett Coleman and Co v Union of India (1972)** — The Newsprint Control Order, which allocated newsprint between papers and effectively limited the size of larger papers, was challenged as a restriction on press freedom. The Court held that freedom of the press is included within Article 19(1)(a) and that any restriction that directly curtails the reach or circulation of a newspaper is a restriction on that freedom. The State's defence that the restriction was imposed to ration a scarce resource (newsprint) was held to be an invalid justification under Article 19(2).

**Bijoe Emmanuel v State of Kerala (1986)** — Three children belonging to the Jehovah's Witnesses were expelled from school for refusing to sing the national anthem. The Court held that the right to freedom of speech and expression in Article 19(1)(a) includes the right not to speak — the right to remain silent. Compelled expression violates Article 19(1)(a) just as much as prohibition of expression. The judgment is the foundational case on freedom of conscience in the Article 19 context.

**SP Gupta v Union of India (1981)** — The Court held that the right to know, to receive information about matters of public interest, is implicit in Article 19(1)(a). Citizens whose ability to exercise their rights depends on access to information about the actions of government have a constitutional interest in that information. This was the foundation for the later development of the right to information under Article 19(1)(a), which provided much of the constitutional argument for the Right to Information Act 2005.

**Shreya Singhal v Union of India (2015)** — The most important internet-era Article 19(1)(a) case. Section 66A of the IT Act, which criminalised online messages that caused "annoyance, inconvenience, danger, obstruction, insult, injury, criminal intimidation, enmity, hatred or ill will" was struck down as an unconstitutional restriction on speech. The Court held that the provision's terms were too vague to give adequate notice of what was prohibited (violating the "reasonable" restriction standard), that it covered constitutionally protected speech including political criticism and social commentary, and that the permitted grounds in Article 19(2) did not extend to "annoyance" or "inconvenience." The Court also read down Section 79 (intermediary liability safe harbour) to require takedown only on court or government order, not on private complaint. Shreya Singhal remains the most consequential judicial statement on internet freedom in India.

**Anuradha Bhasin v Union of India (2020)** — Following the August 2019 revocation of Jammu and Kashmir's special status, the government imposed a complete internet shutdown that lasted months. The Supreme Court held that the freedom of the press under Article 19(1)(a) and the right to carry on trade through the internet under Article 19(1)(g) are both fundamental rights that the State cannot restrict by indefinite shutdown orders. The Court held that such orders must satisfy the proportionality standard — they must be necessary, no broader than required, and subject to judicial review. It directed that all shutdown orders be published and be reviewed by a designated authority. The case opened new constitutional ground on the right to access the internet as a medium for the exercise of existing fundamental rights.

**Navtej Singh Johar v Union of India (2018)** — The Constitution Bench read down Section 377 IPC's application to consensual same-sex relations and held that sexual orientation and gender identity are protected under Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21. The Article 19(1)(a) dimension was specific: the freedom of expression encompasses the right to express one's identity, including intimate choices and the expression of sexual orientation. A law that criminalises conduct expressive of identity restricts the freedom to express that identity and must satisfy the Article 19 reasonable restriction standard.

## Sedition and Article 19 — an ongoing controversy

Section 124A of the Indian Penal Code (sedition) criminalises words, signs, or visible representations that bring the government into hatred, contempt, or disaffection. The colonial-era provision, which was used extensively against independence activists, has been the subject of sustained constitutional challenge under Article 19(1)(a).

The Supreme Court in Kedar Nath Singh v State of Bihar (1962) saved Section 124A from invalidity by reading it down to cover only speech that incites violence or has a tendency to cause public disorder — not mere criticism or even harsh condemnation of the government. On the narrowed reading, sedition prosecutions must show that the speech in question was likely to produce violence or public disorder, not merely that it expressed strong disapproval of the government.

In 2022, the Supreme Court placed further sedition prosecutions on hold and invited the government to reconsider the provision. The central constitutional question is whether Section 124A, even as narrowed by Kedar Nath, is a constitutionally permissible restriction on speech under Article 19(2)'s "security of the State" and "public order" grounds, or whether it gives excessive discretion to prosecuting authorities and has a chilling effect on legitimate political speech. The Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (2023), which replaced the IPC, repealed Section 124A and replaced it with a broader provision on acts endangering sovereignty, unity, and integrity — which itself now faces Article 19 challenges.

## Current significance

Article 19 litigation is among the most politically significant constitutional activity in contemporary India. The freedom of speech strand is engaged in: challenges to internet shutdowns (ongoing in Jammu and Kashmir and periodic shutdowns in other States); challenges to laws that criminalise "fake news" or require government registration of digital news outlets; challenges to the Unlawful Activities Prevention Act's application to journalists and social activists; challenges to sedition prosecutions; and challenges to platform regulation rules that require intermediaries to identify originators of messages (the traceability requirement).

The proportionality framework from Anuradha Bhasin has given courts a more structured and demanding tool for reviewing restrictions on Article 19 rights. Instead of asking only whether a restriction is "reasonable" in a general sense, courts now examine whether it is the least restrictive means of achieving a legitimate objective. This has raised the bar for both legislative and executive action that restricts speech, assembly, or movement.

The right to protest — at the intersection of Articles 19(1)(a) and 19(1)(b) — was directly engaged in cases arising from the Shaheen Bagh protest against the Citizenship Amendment Act and the farmers' protest on Delhi's borders in 2020-21. The Court's handling of these cases — acknowledging the right to protest in principle while also authorising restrictions on specific protest locations — illustrates the ongoing tension between the constitutional protection for collective expression and the State's interest in maintaining order and movement.

The digital economy has created a new generation of Article 19(1)(g) questions about the freedom to practise a profession or carry on a business through digital platforms. Regulations on gig economy platforms, app store policies, cryptocurrency trading prohibitions, and mandatory licensing of digital services all raise questions about whether they constitute reasonable restrictions on the freedom to carry on a trade or business in the interests of the general public.
