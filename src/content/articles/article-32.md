## Why it matters

Dr. B. R. Ambedkar called Article 32 "the very heart and soul of the Constitution" — stronger language than he used for any other provision. His reasoning was structural: rights without remedies are promises without enforcement. If Part III guaranteed freedom of speech, equality, and personal liberty but offered no mechanism for enforcing those guarantees against the State, those rights would be aspirational documents rather than justiciable claims. Article 32 converts the aspiration into a legally enforceable demand, and it does so at the highest judicial level, bypassing the ordinary civil court system.

The right to move the Supreme Court under Article 32 is itself a fundamental right — not a procedural facility that Parliament can restrict by ordinary legislation. Article 32(4) makes this explicit: the right cannot be suspended except as provided by the Constitution, meaning only through an emergency proclamation under Articles 352 and 359. This constitutional entrenchment distinguishes Article 32 from ordinary rights of appeal. A person whose fundamental rights are violated can approach the Supreme Court directly; they do not need to exhaust lower-court remedies first. The Supreme Court cannot refuse to hear an Article 32 petition on the ground that an alternate remedy exists, though in practice it may suggest the more appropriate forum.

The High Courts have a parallel writ jurisdiction under Article 226, which is in some ways broader — High Courts can issue writs for violations of legal rights (not just fundamental rights) and can issue writs against private bodies performing public functions. The relationship between Articles 32 and 226 has been one of the enduring jurisdictional questions in Indian constitutional practice. The two jurisdictions are concurrent for fundamental rights violations; the choice of forum is the petitioner's, though the Supreme Court may transfer cases to High Courts when the facts are contested or when the matter is primarily of regional significance.

The combination of Article 32 with Article 142 (complete justice) and Article 32A (now omitted, but historically the power to issue writs against State laws) has given the Supreme Court an extraordinary remedial arsenal. When the Court exercises Article 32 jurisdiction, it can issue not just declaratory orders but structural injunctions, mandatory directions to State agencies, monitoring committees, and performance benchmarks — a range of remedies that no statute precisely authorises but that the Court has developed to make constitutional rights effective in practice.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — guaranteed access**: The right to move the Supreme Court by "appropriate proceedings" is guaranteed. This means the Court cannot decline jurisdiction on jurisdictional grounds; if a petitioner frames a genuine fundamental rights claim, the Court must hear it. "Appropriate proceedings" is deliberately open-ended: it encompasses writ petitions in the traditional sense, letters from prisoners (epistolary petitions), petitions from public-spirited individuals challenging systemic violations, and applications for contempt when court orders are not complied with.

**Clause (2) — the five writs**: The Constitution names five writs in the nature of the English prerogative writs: habeas corpus (bring the body — challenges unlawful detention), mandamus (we command — requires a public authority to perform a legal duty), prohibition (prevents an inferior court from exceeding its jurisdiction), quo warranto (challenges a person's title to hold public office), and certiorari (quashes an inferior tribunal's decision for error of law). The phrase "in the nature of" is significant — these are constitutional writs adapted to Indian conditions, not exact replicas of their English counterparts. The Court may also issue any other direction or order necessary to enforce fundamental rights, making the list non-exhaustive.

**Clause (3) — empowering other courts**: Parliament may by law give other courts (High Courts already have Article 226 jurisdiction; subordinate courts could in theory be empowered) the power to issue constitutional writs within their local limits. This has been used sparingly: the primary writ courts remain the Supreme Court (under Article 32) and the High Courts (under Article 226).

**Clause (4) — suspension**: The right under Article 32 can be suspended only as provided by the Constitution — specifically, by a Presidential Order under Article 359 issued during a Proclamation of Emergency under Article 352. During the 1975-77 Emergency, such an order suspended the right to move courts under Articles 14, 19, 21, and 22, which is what made the Habeas Corpus case (ADM Jabalpur) possible — petitioners could not invoke Article 32 to challenge their detention because the right to do so had been suspended. The 44th Amendment subsequently made it constitutionally impossible to suspend the right to move courts under Articles 20 and 21, ensuring that the right to challenge detention for violation of Article 21 can never be suspended even during an emergency.

## Public Interest Litigation and the transformation of Article 32

The most consequential development in the history of Article 32 was the Supreme Court's invention of public interest litigation (PIL) in the late 1970s and early 1980s. PIL transformed Article 32 from a remedy for individual victims into a vehicle for systemic constitutional change.

Under traditional writ jurisdiction, only the person whose rights were violated could petition the Court. Standing rules required the petitioner to show personal injury. PIL broke this requirement: Justice Bhagwati and Justice Krishna Iyer, in a series of landmark judgments, held that any public-spirited person could petition the Court on behalf of those who could not access the courts — prisoners, bonded laborers, slum dwellers, women workers in factories, and other groups whose practical inability to litigate was itself a form of constitutional deprivation.

The first PIL cases addressed the appalling conditions of undertrial prisoners and bonded labourers. The Court received letters from journalists and social activists describing systematic rights violations and converted those letters into writ petitions. In Bandhua Mukti Morcha v Union of India (1984), Justice Bhagwati confirmed that Article 32's "appropriate proceedings" expressly includes such epistolary petitions, and that the State's obligation to provide constitutional remedies cannot be frustrated by the petitioner's poverty or illiteracy.

PIL then expanded into structural rights — environmental pollution, forest governance, urban planning, child labour, custodial deaths, electoral corruption, and administrative accountability. The Court began appointing commissioners, monitoring compliance, and issuing continuing mandamus orders that required the executive to report to the Court on its progress. This supervisory function transformed the Court into a constitutional oversight body with a role that goes beyond adjudication in the ordinary sense.

The PIL jurisdiction has also attracted criticism. Critics argue that Article 32 PIL proceedings have become a vehicle for judicial activism that encroaches on executive and legislative functions, that they are prone to capture by well-resourced interests who can disguise private litigation as public interest litigation, and that the Court's monitoring role in complex governance matters exceeds its institutional competence. The Court itself has periodically reasserted standards requiring genuine public interest and has dismissed frivolous PILs as abuse of process. The proper scope of PIL under Article 32 remains a live debate about the separation of powers in India.

## The five writs in practice

**Habeas corpus** is the most politically significant of the five writs. It requires a person detaining another to produce the detainee before the court and justify the detention. In cases of arbitrary preventive detention under MISA, NSA, UAPA, or NDPS, habeas corpus petitions are the primary constitutional remedy. The detention must be supported by a specific legal basis; vague or formulaic grounds are insufficient. The Emergency-era Habeas Corpus case (ADM Jabalpur, 1976) suspended habeas corpus jurisdiction, and its overruling established that the right to challenge detention cannot be extinguished even in emergencies affecting Articles 14 and 19 — though Article 359 orders can still theoretically suspend Article 21's application to court proceedings.

**Mandamus** directs a public authority to perform a non-discretionary legal duty. It has been used extensively in cases where government agencies fail to make appointments, disburse funds, process applications, or comply with court orders. Mandamus does not lie against purely discretionary decisions or against private parties. In PIL cases, the Court has issued continuing mandamus — ongoing directions with reporting requirements — to compel the executive to implement rights-protective programmes over time.

**Certiorari** quashes the decision of an inferior tribunal that has exceeded its jurisdiction, violated principles of natural justice, or made a manifest error of law on the face of the record. Its scope in Indian constitutional law has been much expanded compared to its narrow English origins — the Supreme Court reviews not just questions of jurisdiction but the substantive legality of administrative action affecting fundamental rights.

**Prohibition** prevents an inferior court or tribunal from proceeding beyond its jurisdiction. It is issued before a proceeding is concluded, unlike certiorari which quashes after the fact. The distinction has become blurred in practice, since Indian courts often issue "certiorari cum prohibition" — quashing a completed order and directing the inferior authority not to exercise the jurisdiction it has exceeded.

**Quo warranto** challenges the entitlement of a person to hold a public office. It requires the officeholder to show by what warrant they hold the office. It has been used to challenge appointments to constitutional offices, statutory bodies, and positions requiring specific qualifications. The Court considers whether the appointment conditions were met, whether the appointing authority acted within its power, and whether the person's continued tenure is constitutionally valid.

## Key cases

**Romesh Thappar v State of Madras (1950)** — One of the earliest Article 32 cases, it established that the right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights is itself a fundamental right and cannot be curtailed by ordinary legislation. The decision was also significant for its holding on Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of speech), demonstrating from the very beginning that Article 32 petitions were the vehicle through which the fundamental rights chapter would be interpreted and applied.

**Bandhua Mukti Morcha v Union of India (1984)** — The foundational PIL case for Article 32 epistolary jurisdiction. A letter from a social activist describing bonded labourers in stone quarries in Haryana was converted into a writ petition. Justice Bhagwati held that "appropriate proceedings" under Article 32(1) includes any communication — a letter, a postcard, even a newspaper article — that brings to the Court's attention a matter of fundamental rights violation, provided the petitioner is acting in good faith for the benefit of those who cannot themselves petition. The Court appointed a commissioner to investigate conditions and ordered the state government to comply with the Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act. This case defined the modern scope of public interest litigation.

**ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla (1976)** — The Habeas Corpus case. Following the Emergency Proclamation and a Presidential Order suspending the right to move courts under Articles 14, 19, 21, and 22, the Court by a 4-1 majority held that habeas corpus petitions could not be maintained to challenge detention under the Maintenance of Internal Security Act. The majority held that when Article 32 rights are suspended, there is no surviving right to move the Court. Justice Khanna's dissent — that the right to life is pre-constitutional and cannot be suspended by executive order — was vindicated by subsequent constitutional evolution, and the majority reasoning was effectively overruled by the 44th Amendment and by Puttaswamy.

**Justice K S Puttaswamy v Union of India (2017)** — The privacy case arose as an Article 32 petition challenging the mandatory Aadhaar biometric database. While the substantive outcome was the recognition of privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21, the case also illustrated how Article 32 functions as the gateway for constitutional questions of fundamental importance that require the Court to articulate new doctrine. The nine-judge bench that sat to resolve the foundational questions was constituted in response to an Article 32 petition, demonstrating that the provision enables not just individual rights enforcement but the development of constitutional law at its most abstract level.

**Sheela Barse v State of Maharashtra (1983)** — A letter from a journalist about the custodial torture of women prisoners in Bombay police lock-ups was converted into a writ petition. The Court issued detailed directions about the conditions of police custody, the rights of female detainees, and the state's duty to prevent custodial violence — all derived from Article 32 read with Article 21. The case was seminal in establishing the Court's power to issue monitoring directions in custody cases.

**Vishaka v State of Rajasthan (1997)** — A gang rape on a government premises provoked an Article 32 petition for systemic protection from sexual harassment at the workplace. In the absence of legislation, the Court used Article 32 read with Articles 14, 15, and 21 to issue the Vishaka guidelines — binding directions specifying the obligations of employers, the composition of complaints committees, and the procedures for addressing sexual harassment. The guidelines were enforceable as orders of the Supreme Court until legislation (the POSH Act 2013) replaced them. The case is the landmark example of the Court using Article 32 PIL to fill a legislative gap on constitutional rights.

**MC Mehta v Union of India (1987 and subsequent)** — A series of Article 32 petitions addressing industrial pollution of the Ganges, air pollution in Delhi, and hazardous waste management became the foundation for Indian environmental constitutional law. The Court used Article 32 read with Article 21's right to a clean environment to develop the absolute liability doctrine for hazardous industries, to close polluting industries in and around Delhi, to mandate conversion of diesel public transport to compressed natural gas, and to establish the National Green Tribunal. The Mehta cases remain the most sustained exercise of the Court's structural injunction powers under Article 32 in the environmental domain.

## Current significance

Article 32 PIL continues to be the primary mechanism for systemic constitutional challenge in India. Cases on prison conditions, rights of transgender persons, rights of the elderly and disabled in state institutions, recognition of same-sex relationships, surveillance and data protection, rights of manual scavengers, child marriage, and environmental enforcement are all being litigated as Article 32 petitions. The Court's engagement with these systemic issues through PIL has made it, in effect, a co-participant in governance — monitoring executive compliance with constitutional norms across a vast range of policy domains.

The legitimacy of this role remains contested. Critics from both the progressive left and the constitutional right argue that the Court's PIL jurisdiction has expanded beyond what Article 32 authorises — that the provision was designed for individual rights enforcement, not for systemic governance oversight. Defenders argue that when the executive fails to deliver rights to those who cannot access courts, the only constitutionally available response is judicial intervention through Article 32's broad guarantee of access.

The relationship between Article 32 and Article 226 has become more complex as High Courts have developed their own PIL jurisprudence and as cases move between jurisdictions. The Supreme Court's guidelines on when to transfer Article 226 PIL cases to itself (or vice versa) have created a complex framework for allocating systemic rights litigation between the two tiers of writ jurisdiction.

The digital era has opened new Article 32 frontiers. Petitions challenging internet shutdowns, social media content moderation, algorithmic profiling by government agencies, and mandatory biometric linkage for state benefits are all arriving as Article 32 petitions. The Court's responses will determine whether Article 32 can adapt to protect fundamental rights in the digital environment with the same effectiveness it has shown in physical-world contexts.
