## Why it matters

Article 300A is the constitutional residue of what was once the centrepiece of Indian property rights — Articles 19(1)(f) and 31. From 1950 to 1978, the Constitution guaranteed citizens a fundamental right to acquire, hold, and dispose of property (Article 19(1)(f)) and protected against compulsory acquisition without compensation (Article 31). These provisions generated an enormous body of jurisprudence — and an equally large volume of constitutional conflict with the socialist programmes of successive governments. Land reform legislation, zamindari abolition, bank nationalisation, and property ceiling laws were all challenged as violations of the fundamental right to property. Parliament responded with repeated constitutional amendments inserting the Ninth Schedule to protect certain laws from judicial review, inserting Articles 31A, 31B, and 31C to shield acquisition and socialisation legislation, and ultimately — through the 44th Amendment in 1978 — removing the right to property from Part III altogether.

The 44th Amendment was the culmination of three decades of conflict between the property right and India's redistributive constitutional project. By moving property from Part III (Fundamental Rights) to Part XII (Finance, Property, Contracts and Suits) as a simple constitutional right, Parliament resolved — or at least transformed — that conflict. The enforcement asymmetry is the key practical change: a constitutional right under Part XII is enforceable by writ in a High Court under Article 226, or through ordinary suit in civil courts, but does not ground a direct petition to the Supreme Court under Article 32 — which is itself a fundamental right that applies only to violations of Part III rights. This structural demotion was intended to allow land reform and redistribution legislation to proceed without constant constitutional challenge in the Supreme Court.

Yet Article 300A has proved more resilient than the 44th Amendment's proponents anticipated. Its terse formulation — "No person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law" — has been read by the Supreme Court to contain requirements that go far beyond mere formal legality. The Court has extracted from the phrase "authority of law" an implicit requirement of public purpose, adequate compensation, and procedural due process — requirements that were explicit in the former Article 31 but that the 44th Amendment deliberately omitted from Article 300A. This interpretive move has partially restored the substantive protection that the Amendment sought to remove, making the article one of the most important and most litigated provisions in the entire Constitution.

## The 44th Amendment and its constitutional context

The 44th Amendment 1978 was enacted by the Janata government that came to power after the Emergency. The amendment aimed to accomplish several constitutional corrections — restricting the power to proclaim emergency, restoring some fundamental rights that had been suspended during the Emergency, and removing property from the fundamental rights chapter to prevent its use as a tool to block redistributive legislation.

Before the Amendment, the story of property rights in India was one of sustained constitutional conflict. The Constituent Assembly had included the right to property but framed it carefully — the right to hold and dispose of property under Article 19(1)(f) was subject to reasonable restrictions in the public interest, and Article 31 permitted compulsory acquisition for a public purpose with compensation. Almost immediately after the Constitution came into force, state land reform legislation was challenged. The Patna High Court struck down the Bihar Land Reforms Act in 1950; the Supreme Court in State of Bihar v Kameshwar Singh (1952) voided land reform legislation. Parliament responded with the First Amendment (1951), which inserted Articles 31A and 31B and the Ninth Schedule to protect land reform laws from Article 19(1)(f) and Article 31 challenge.

This amendment-and-litigation cycle continued through the 1950s, 1960s, and 1970s. The Bank Nationalisation case (1970) and the Privy Purses case (1970) saw the Supreme Court strike down executive action affecting property rights. The Twenty-Fifth Amendment (1971) inserted Article 31C to protect laws implementing directive principles from Articles 14, 19, and 31. Kesavananda Bharati (1973) held that the basic structure of the Constitution limited Parliament's amendment power, creating uncertainty about whether even the basic structure of the property right was protected.

The 44th Amendment resolved this uncertainty by removing Articles 19(1)(f) and 31 from the Constitution and substituting the minimal Article 300A. The Constituent Assembly's original architecture — a constitutionally protected property right in Part III — was dismantled. What remained was a constitutional right (not a fundamental right) in Part XII with no explicit requirement of public purpose or compensation.

## Reading the provision

"No person" — The protection extends beyond citizens to all persons, including companies, corporations, and foreign nationals with property in India. This broader coverage is consistent with Article 300A's location in Part XII rather than Part III, which contains several provisions limited to citizens.

"Shall be deprived" — "Deprivation" is interpreted broadly to encompass not just permanent acquisition but also temporary dispossession, physical destruction, requisition of property for temporary use, and regulatory action that substantially destroys the property's economic value or the owner's ability to use it. The Supreme Court has distinguished between mere regulation of property use (which may be lawful without compensation) and deprivation (which requires legal authority and, per the Court's reading, public purpose and compensation). The boundary between regulation and deprivation is contested in many categories of cases, including development regulations, coastal zone restrictions, and heritage conservation orders.

"Of his property" — "Property" is given a broad interpretation that goes well beyond land and buildings. It includes moveable property, intellectual property rights, contractual entitlements, statutory licences and permits, vested pension and gratuity rights, compensation amounts awarded by courts or tribunals before payment, and any other legally recognised entitlement with economic value. Courts have held that a vested right — one that has accrued before the relevant legislative change — is property for Article 300A purposes even if it is a purely personal right. The breadth of the property concept is one of the reasons Article 300A generates such extensive litigation.

"Save by authority of law" — This phrase is the provision's operative requirement. At minimum it means that the deprivation must be authorised by a valid enacted statute of a competent legislature — executive orders, notifications, and administrative circulars that exceed their parent statute's scope do not constitute "authority of law." But the Supreme Court has gone significantly further, holding that "authority of law" imports requirements of public purpose, fair compensation, and procedural due process as implicit constitutional standards. An acquisition formally authorised by statute but serving no public purpose, or providing no compensation, or proceeding without notice to the affected person, may violate Article 300A even though it has a statutory basis.

## The judicial enlargement of Article 300A

The transformation of Article 300A from a bare procedural guarantee into a substantive property right is the work of the Supreme Court's post-1978 jurisprudence. Three doctrinal moves have been central.

First, the Court held that "authority of law" requires the authorising statute itself to be constitutionally valid — not merely formally enacted by a competent legislature. Laws that authorise deprivation of property in ways that are arbitrary (Article 14), violate free speech (Article 19), or deprive persons of life and liberty without due process (Article 21) may fail constitutional scrutiny even when they formally authorise the deprivation.

Second, the Court read implicit requirements of public purpose and compensation into "authority of law" in several decisions, most significantly KT Plantation v State of Karnataka (2011) and Vidya Devi v State of HP (2020). The reasoning is that deprivation without public purpose is arbitrary and thus fails Article 14, and deprivation without compensation is itself a form of arbitrary property taking that no constitutional system can permit without explicit enabling text.

Third, the Article 300A and Article 21 interaction has generated procedural due process requirements. Where a deprivation of property also affects the right to livelihood or shelter — slum demolitions, eviction of street vendors, cancellation of licences that constitute primary livelihoods — courts have held that Article 21 is engaged alongside Article 300A, importing requirements of notice, hearing, and reasoned orders that Part III enforcement demands.

## Key cases

**Waman Rao v Union of India AIR 1981 SC 271** — The Supreme Court upheld the 44th Amendment's removal of Articles 19(1)(f) and 31 from Part III and the insertion of Article 300A in Part XII. The Court held that this did not violate the basic structure of the Constitution because property retained constitutional (if not fundamental-rights-grade) protection. The judgment is the foundational authority for Article 300A's constitutional status and confirmed that the right to property, while no longer a fundamental right, is not a mere statutory entitlement.

**Jilubhai Nanbhai Khachar v State of Gujarat (1994) Supp (1) SCC 596** — The Court held that deprivation of property without valid statutory authority violates Article 300A, and clarified that the authorising statute must itself satisfy constitutional standards including non-arbitrariness under Article 14. This decision brought the property right into dialogue with the equality guarantee and established that formal statutory backing is necessary but not sufficient — the law must also be constitutionally sound.

**KT Plantation Pvt Ltd v State of Karnataka (2011) 9 SCC 1** — A landmark expansion of Article 300A's scope. A Constitution Bench held that the phrase "authority of law" in Article 300A carries implicit requirements: the deprivation must be for a public purpose and must be accompanied by payment of compensation unless a specific constitutional provision dispenses with it. The Court acknowledged that these requirements had been explicit in the original Article 31 and held that their removal from the text by the 44th Amendment did not eliminate them from the constitutional order — they survived as implicit requirements of "authority of law" under Article 300A. This decision substantially narrowed the gap between the former fundamental right and the current constitutional right.

**Vidya Devi v State of HP (2020) 2 SCC 717** — The Court held that property cannot be taken from a person who has occupied and used it without paying compensation, regardless of the period of deprivation. The State of Himachal Pradesh had taken land for a road without paying compensation for decades. The Court directed compensation on the basis that the State's failure to pay for land it effectively took constituted a violation of Article 300A as a continuing wrong.

**Kolkata Municipal Corporation v Bimalendu Das (2018) 8 SCC 376** — The Court held that a municipal demolition order issued without notice and without following the statutory procedure for demolition of private property violated Article 300A even though the municipal authority had a general statutory power to demolish unsafe structures. Procedural compliance with the "authority of law" requirement — meaning compliance with the statutory procedure, not just existence of the statutory power — is essential.

**Property Owners Association v State of Maharashtra (2024) SCC OnLine SC 2728** — A nine-judge Constitution Bench resolved the long-standing question about Article 39(b) — the directive principle requiring distribution of material resources to subserve the common good — and its relationship with Article 300A. The majority held that not all privately held property constitutes "material resources of the community" within Article 39(b), and that the Directive Principle cannot justify blanket State expropriation of private property without satisfying the requirements of valid law, public purpose, and compensation under Article 300A. This ruling was constitutionally significant in limiting the scope of the socialisation directive that had been the principal constitutional justification for post-1978 nationalisation measures.

**Bishamber Dayal Chandra Mohan v State of UP AIR 1982 SC 33** — An early Article 300A decision holding that executive action without statutory backing — in this case, a state government order taking over a shop — violated Article 300A. The case established that even after the 44th Amendment, property cannot be taken by executive order without legislative authority, maintaining the rule-of-law baseline.

## The LARR Act 2013 and the statutory framework

The Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act 2013 (LARR Act) replaced the colonial-era Land Acquisition Act 1894 as the principal statute for compulsory land acquisition. It dramatically raised the compensation standard (typically two to four times the market value), required social impact assessments, mandated consent of a specified percentage of affected families for private-sector and PPP projects, and established procedures for rehabilitation and resettlement. The LARR Act operationalises the implicit requirements the Supreme Court has read into Article 300A — public purpose, fair compensation, and procedural process — at the statutory level.

Disputes about LARR Act compliance — the adequacy of social impact assessments, the calculation of market value, the sufficiency of rehabilitation packages, and the scope of the urgency provision that exempts certain acquisitions from the consent requirement — are litigated under Article 300A as constitutional challenges to the acquisition process. The interaction between the statutory framework and Article 300A's constitutional floor has been particularly significant in cases involving tribal and forest land, where the LARR Act's protections intersect with the Forest Rights Act 2006 and Article 244's protections for Scheduled Areas.

## Article 300A and Article 21

An increasingly significant strand of case law holds that where deprivation of property also affects the right to livelihood — the agricultural land of a farming family, the workspace of a self-employed artisan, the home of a long-term resident in an urban settlement — the deprivation engages Article 21 alongside Article 300A. Where Article 21 is engaged, the full enforcement machinery of Part III becomes available, including the direct petition to the Supreme Court under Article 32. Courts have imposed requirements of notice, opportunity of hearing, and reasoned orders — procedural protections that go beyond what Article 300A alone demands — in cases where demolitions and dispossessions are found to implicate the right to livelihood. This convergence between the property right and the right to life is the dominant trend in recent slum demolition, eviction, and licence-cancellation litigation.

## Current significance

Article 300A is one of the most frequently litigated constitutional provisions in India. Land acquisition for infrastructure — highways, railways, smart cities, industrial corridors, and defence installations — generates constant disputes about statutory validity, public purpose, and compensation adequacy. Urban redevelopment, slum clearance, and regularisation of unauthorised constructions raise Article 300A in combination with Article 21 in High Court and Supreme Court PIL. Forest rights, tribal land alienation, and the cancellation of mining and quarrying licences that are primary community livelihoods regularly invoke the article.

Emerging issues include the application of Article 300A to digital property — data, domain names, social media accounts, and digital assets subject to government blocking or removal orders — and to regulatory takings in the financial sector, where the Reserve Bank's power to write down or cancel bank licences and debt instruments affects holders of financial property. The question of whether these constitutional protections extend to non-physical forms of property — consistent with the broad definition courts have adopted — is likely to generate significant litigation in the years ahead.

The Supreme Court's willingness to read substantive requirements into Article 300A's bare text ensures that every major land and property acquisition case becomes a constitutional case. The dialogue between the article's minimal formulation and the Court's expansive interpretation continues to define the effective scope of the Indian property right — and the boundary between constitutional democracy and the redistributive state.
