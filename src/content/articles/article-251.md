## Why it matters

Article 251 is the constitutional traffic rule that applies whenever Parliament invades the State List under the special powers of Articles 249 and 250. Article 249 permits Parliament to legislate on State List subjects when the Rajya Sabha passes a resolution by a two-thirds majority declaring that the subject has become a matter of national importance. Article 250 enables the same during a proclaimed national Emergency under Article 352. Both provisions represent exceptional departures from the ordinary federal division of legislative authority. Article 251 determines what happens when Parliament exercises these exceptional powers and existing or future State laws are on the same subject: Parliamentary law prevails for as long as the Parliamentary law remains in force, but the State law is not permanently destroyed — it merely becomes inoperative and is automatically restored once the Parliamentary power lapses.

This design reflects a deliberate federal choice. The framers understood that emergency or national-interest-based invasions of the State List should not permanently extinguish State legislative competence. A Parliament acting under Article 249 for a year, or under Article 250 during a brief Emergency, should not be able to permanently occupy a State subject merely by enacting a law. Article 251 closes this gap by treating the State law's inoperability as co-extensive with the Parliament's exceptional law — temporary, conditional, and self-reversing.

The contrast with Article 254, which governs Concurrent List repugnancy, illuminates what is at stake. Under Article 254, once Parliament occupies a Concurrent List field after a State law has received Presidential assent, the question of which law prevails becomes complex and can require fresh Parliamentary action or fresh Presidential assent. Under Article 251, the relationship is simpler: Parliamentary exceptional law displaces State law while it operates, and the State law revives automatically when it does not. This simplicity serves the federal purpose — it makes the invasive Parliamentary law temporary in its real-world effect, not merely in its nominal validity.

Article 251 also preserves the State Legislature's dignity in a doctrinal sense. The opening words — "Nothing in articles 249 and 250 shall restrict the power of the Legislature of a State to make any law which under this Constitution it has power to make" — affirm that the State Legislature remains competent to pass laws on the subject even while a Parliamentary law under Article 249 or 250 is in force. The State law is not ultra vires; it is merely rendered inoperative in so far as it is repugnant. This distinction between constitutional invalidity and temporary inoperability matters: it means the State Legislature's act of passing the law is constitutionally unobjectionable and the law will revive in full when the occasion for its suppression ends.

## Reading the provision

The Article is structured in a single sentence with two operative parts.

**First part — preservation of State legislative competence:** "Nothing in articles 249 and 250 shall restrict the power of the Legislature of a State to make any law which under this Constitution it has power to make." This confirms that Articles 249 and 250 disable Parliament's usual List-based incompetence to legislate on State subjects — they do not strip States of their own legislative power on the same subjects. A State Legislature may continue to legislate even while Parliament is in the field under either Article.

**Second part — the primacy rule with temporal limitation:** "If any provision of a law made by the Legislature of a State is repugnant to any provision of a law made by Parliament which Parliament has under either of the said articles power to make, the law made by Parliament, whether passed before or after the law made by the Legislature of the State, shall prevail, and the law made by the Legislature of the State shall to the extent of the repugnancy, but so long only as the law made by Parliament continues to have effect, be inoperative."

Several elements of this second part demand attention:

**"Whether passed before or after"** — Parliament's law under Article 249 or 250 prevails regardless of chronological priority. A State law enacted after the Parliamentary law is no less subject to displacement than a pre-existing State law. Similarly, a Parliamentary law enacted after a State law displaces the State law retrospectively from the moment the Parliamentary law comes into force.

**"To the extent of the repugnancy"** — Inoperability is proportionate, not total. If a State law has twenty provisions and only three are repugnant to the Parliamentary law, only those three become inoperative. The remaining seventeen provisions continue to operate. This proportionality principle, established by analogy from Article 254 jurisprudence, prevents the blunt nullification of an entire State law when only limited conflicts exist.

**"But so long only as the law made by Parliament continues to have effect"** — This is the temporal condition. The State law's inoperability is strictly contingent on Parliament's law remaining in force. When the Parliamentary law lapses — whether because the Article 249 Rajya Sabha resolution expires (resolutions under Article 249 have a one-year life, though renewable), or because the Emergency under Article 250 comes to an end — the Parliamentary law ceases to "continue to have effect" and the State law's inoperability ends automatically.

**"Be inoperative"** — The choice of "inoperative" rather than "void" or "invalid" is crucial. It signals that the State law is not constitutionally defective; it is merely suspended. Courts do not strike down inoperative provisions as ultra vires — they recognise them as valid but temporarily without effect. On revival, the law operates as if the period of inoperability had not occurred.

**No Presidential assent saving mechanism** — Unlike Article 254(2), where a State law can prevail over an earlier Central law on Concurrent List subjects if it has received Presidential assent, Article 251 provides no equivalent. States cannot route their laws through the President to protect them against Parliamentary invasion under Article 249 or 250. The reason is that these powers are qualitatively different: Parliament is not legislating on a shared Concurrent List subject but encroaching into exclusively State territory under exceptional circumstances. Permitting a Presidential-assent escape route would partially neutralise the purpose of Articles 249 and 250.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — Although primarily addressing the scope of Presidential power under Article 356, this nine-judge Constitution Bench extensively examined the scheme of federal distribution of legislative power and the nature of Parliamentary override. The judgment confirmed that Parliamentary incursion into State legislative territory under emergency-type powers is inherently temporary and that State legislative authority is constitutionally resilient. The principle that States retain their legislative power even while Parliamentary exceptional laws operate in their territory is grounded in the architecture the Court described. While Article 251 was not the ratio, the judgment provides authoritative doctrinal support for treating the inoperability under Article 251 as suspension rather than permanent displacement.

**Hoechst Pharmaceuticals Ltd. v. State of Bihar (1983) 4 SCC 45** — In this five-judge bench decision, the Supreme Court examined Article 254 repugnancy in detail and its analysis is the primary analogical authority for Article 251 disputes. The Court held that repugnancy under the Constitution is a provision-by-provision inquiry — a State law is not rendered wholly inoperative merely because some clauses conflict with Parliament's law. Repugnancy must be assessed with specificity; only the conflicting provisions are displaced. The proportionality principle articulated in Hoechst applies equally to Article 251, ensuring that States do not lose their entire law when Parliament's exceptional law touches only part of the same field.

**M. Karunanidhi v. Union of India (1979) 3 SCC 431** — The Supreme Court established a general test for repugnancy that governs both Article 254 and Article 251: (a) there must be a direct conflict between specific provisions; or (b) Parliament must have intended to cover the whole field so exhaustively that State legislation on any aspect of it is excluded; or (c) the two laws must be so inconsistent that giving effect to one must necessarily result in disobeying the other. Mere overlapping subject matter without actual conflict does not create repugnancy. This framework is the standard diagnostic tool for repugnancy questions under Article 251.

**Bharat Coking Coal Ltd. v. State of Bihar (1983) 4 SCC 27** — This case required the Court to examine the relationship between Central mining legislation and State revenue laws, and while decided primarily on the Union List competence ground, the Court's treatment of temporary displacement principles is instructive for Article 251. The judgment noted that displacement of State law by Central law does not affect the State's legislative title to the subject and that if Central competence on the subject lapses, State laws revive to operate on their own terms.

## Current significance

Article 251 remains a structurally important provision even though Article 249 has rarely been invoked. The Rajya Sabha resolution mechanism has been used sparingly — the resolution in 1952 on the Essential Services Maintenance Act, and debates about invoking Article 249 for subjects like cross-border terrorism and water disputes remain academic because Parliament has generally preferred to rely on the Union List or the Concurrent List to justify Central legislation, avoiding the procedural hurdle of a Rajya Sabha two-thirds resolution. Article 250 invocations are tied to Emergency proclamations, which have themselves been rare since the constitutional amendments following the Emergency of 1975-77.

The automatic revival principle embedded in Article 251 remains constitutionally significant as a statement of federal resilience. It confirms that the Indian federal structure is not one where temporary Central override permanently alters the distribution of legislative power. States that had laws on a subject at the time Parliament invoked Article 249 or 250 do not lose those laws permanently — they get them back when the exceptional period ends. This feature distinguishes India's approach from federal systems where legislative displacement, once effected, may require fresh State action to re-occupy the field.

As debates continue about invoking Article 249 for matters like climate change coordination, cybersecurity, and river water governance, Article 251's framework becomes relevant again. Any Parliamentary law enacted under Article 249 would trigger Article 251's proportionate inoperability rule for conflicting State laws and, crucially, would be subject to the temporal limit that expires with the Rajya Sabha resolution — returning the legal landscape to its pre-invocation state automatically. Understanding Article 251 is therefore essential for anticipating the real-world consequences of any future use of Parliament's Article 249 national-interest power.
