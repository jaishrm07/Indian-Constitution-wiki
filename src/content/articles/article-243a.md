## Why it matters

Article 243A is the constitutional foundation for participatory democracy in rural India. Its significance lies in the distinction it draws between representative and participatory governance. The elected Gram Panchayat under Article 243B is representative — it governs on behalf of the village through chosen members. The Gram Sabha is participatory — it is the entire electorate of the village, assembled as a constitutional institution. By giving the Gram Sabha a constitutional definition in Article 243 and a constitutional mandate in Article 243A, the 73rd Amendment placed direct village democracy at the base of the Panchayati Raj structure, not merely as a consultative appendage but as a body with legally conferrable powers and functions.

The article's permissive language ("may exercise such powers...as the Legislature...may, by law, provide") is a deliberate choice. The drafters of the 73rd Amendment debated whether to prescribe a mandatory list of Gram Sabha functions, but ultimately left this to state discretion to accommodate the variety of agrarian structures, land tenure systems, and administrative traditions across Indian states. This deference to State legislatures has had significant consequences: state laws vary dramatically in the powers they vest in the Gram Sabha, ranging from token consultative roles to substantial decision-making authority over local development plans and social audit proceedings.

## How courts use it

Courts have used Article 243A in two broad ways. First, in challenges to state laws that either do not constitute a Gram Sabha or create a body that does not correspond to the constitutional definition, courts have treated Article 243A as imposing a duty on states to enact legislation giving the Gram Sabha some meaningful powers rather than leaving it entirely inoperative. A state that constitutes a Gram Panchayat but takes no legislative steps to empower the Gram Sabha is inconsistent with the constitutional scheme, even if the text only says "may."

Second, and more consequentially, courts have used Article 243A and the broader PESA framework to test whether state laws in Scheduled Areas adequately protect the Gram Sabha's role. PESA (the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996) is the parliamentary legislation enacted under Article 243M(4)(b) that extends Part IX to Scheduled Areas with modifications. PESA vests mandatory consent functions in the Gram Sabha — including prior informed consent for land acquisition and for issuing mining leases. When state PESA-conforming legislation bypasses or dilutes the Gram Sabha's consent role, courts have intervened.

## Key cases

**Samatha v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1997)** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling on tribal land rights in Scheduled Areas placed the Gram Sabha at the centre of any constitutionally valid governance process in those areas. The Court held that no transfer of land in Scheduled Areas to non-tribals or private companies was permissible without compliance with the constitutional protections for tribal communities, and it treated the Gram Sabha as the institutional embodiment of those protections. The judgment powerfully reinforced the significance of Article 243A in Scheduled Area governance.

**Nandini Sundar v. State of Chhattisgarh (2011)** — The Supreme Court's ruling on state-sponsored village defence groups in Chhattisgarh engaged with the question of whether local governance institutions, including the Gram Sabha, were being bypassed. The Court emphasised that governance measures affecting tribal areas must work through, and not around, constitutionally mandated local institutions.

**Orissa Mining Corporation v. Ministry of Environment and Forests (2013)** — The Supreme Court held that the Gram Sabha's consent, as required under PESA, was a mandatory prerequisite before forest clearances could be granted for mining in Scheduled Areas. The judgment directly applied the Article 243A framework — as extended and amplified by PESA — to block a major industrial project. It is the most significant judicial enforcement of Gram Sabha consent rights in Indian constitutional history.

## Current significance

The gap between the constitutional promise of Article 243A and its implementation remains one of the deepest structural problems in Indian local governance. Most states have enacted laws vesting some powers in the Gram Sabha, but in practice Gram Sabhas meet infrequently, lack administrative support, and are often dominated by local power structures rather than functioning as genuinely participatory bodies. Social audit provisions — which vest in the Gram Sabha the power to scrutinise expenditure on government programmes — have been more effectively implemented in a few states, particularly Rajasthan, but remain weak elsewhere.

In the Scheduled Areas context, the Gram Sabha's PESA consent role continues to be heavily litigated. State governments have repeatedly attempted to streamline or circumvent Gram Sabha consent processes for infrastructure and mining projects, generating ongoing conflicts between the constitutional framework of Article 243A and the imperatives of industrial development policy.
