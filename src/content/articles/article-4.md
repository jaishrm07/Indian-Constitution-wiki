## Why it matters

Article 4 is the constitutional mechanism that makes territorial reorganisation workable as ordinary legislation. Without it, every admission of a new State or reorganisation of existing States would require not only a parliamentary majority but also the two-thirds majority and State ratification demanded by Article 368 for constitutional amendments. Since reorganisation necessarily involves amending Schedule I (the list of States and Union territories) and Schedule IV (the allocation of Rajya Sabha seats), and since those are constitutional provisions, every reorganisation law would constitute a constitutional amendment if Article 4 did not intervene.

The framers' choice to permit ordinary legislation to accomplish this was deliberate and consequential. The framers had watched the Government of India Act 1935's provincial structure prove difficult to adjust to post-Partition realities. They wanted to give Parliament the flexibility to reorganise territory as demands from linguistic communities, administrative considerations, or political necessity required — without the veto that Article 368's ratification requirement would effectively give to an assembly of incumbent State legislatures whose members had every incentive to protect existing boundaries.

Article 4(1)'s authorisation of "supplemental, incidental and consequential provisions" makes reorganisation legislation genuinely self-contained. A State reorganisation Act does not merely change a name or a boundary — it must deal with the distribution of assets and liabilities accumulated over decades, the status of government employees, the jurisdiction of courts, the continuation of State legislation in successor territories, the electoral arrangements for the new State's first elections, and dozens of other transitional matters. The article permits Parliament to address all of this in a single instrument without any of it being characterised as constitutional amendment.

Article 4(2)'s non-amendment declaration has made the Constitution's territorial provisions among the most flexible in the democratic world. No federally organised democracy — the United States, Canada, Australia, Germany — permits the creation or abolition of constituent units by ordinary majority legislation. India does. The political consequences are significant: State governments have no constitutional protection against reorganisation, and a parliamentary majority determined to bifurcate or merge States needs only to comply with Article 3's procedural requirements (President's reference, State legislature's views obtained, though not binding), pass an ordinary law, and amend Schedules I and IV in that same law.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) imposes a duty and confers a power simultaneously. The duty is expressed in the word "shall contain" — any law made under Article 2 or 3 must include the First and Fourth Schedule amendments necessary to give effect to the territorial changes. Parliament cannot lawfully pass a reorganisation law that changes the constitutional map without also updating the two schedules in the same enactment. The power is the "may also contain" authority — authorising the inclusion of supplemental, incidental, and consequential provisions, including electoral representation for newly formed States or affected States.

The parenthetical in clause (1) — "including provisions as to representation in Parliament and in the Legislature or Legislatures of the State or States affected" — is an important clarification. Parliamentary representation is governed by Schedule IV and Article 81, which are constitutional provisions. Without Article 4, any adjustment to Rajya Sabha seat allocation or Lok Sabha boundaries in a reorganisation law would require Article 368 procedure. The parenthetical confirms that such adjustments can be made in the reorganisation law itself.

"Supplemental, incidental and consequential provisions" has been interpreted broadly. Courts have found this phrase wide enough to cover: transfers of government employees with protection of service conditions, allocation of public sector undertakings and development authorities between successor States, assumption of the predecessor State's debts, continuation in force of State legislation in successor territories, provision for the High Court's jurisdiction during the transition, and arrangements for completing pending elections in affected constituencies. The breadth prevents the argument that any provision beyond the territorial change itself requires a separate statute — the reorganisation law is presumed to be the complete constitutional vehicle for the transition.

Clause (2)'s non-amendment declaration removes these laws from the purview of Article 368 entirely. The declaration operates categorically — it does not require any inquiry into what the law does or whether it touches a "basic structure" element of the Constitution. Reorganisation laws made under Articles 2 and 3 are simply not constitutional amendments for Article 368 purposes. This is a constitutional text-based carve-out, not a judicial doctrine. Its consequence is that reorganisation laws are subject to judicial review under ordinary constitutional principles — separation of powers, fundamental rights, federalism — but are not subject to the special safeguards that Article 368 provides.

## The three major uses of Article 4

**The States Reorganisation Act, 1956** was the first and most consequential exercise of Articles 3 and 4. It dissolved the Pt. A/B/C/D States architecture, created 14 States and 6 Union territories broadly on linguistic lines, and amended Schedule I entirely. The Act's supplemental provisions were extensive — distributing the assets of the former Part B States, dealing with the service conditions of princely-State employees, and establishing new High Court arrangements for Kerala and other newly created States.

**Bifurcation cases 1999-2014**: The creation of Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and Uttarakhand in 2000, Telangana in 2014, and Andhra Pradesh's bifurcation in 2014 each used the Article 3/Article 4 mechanism. Telangana's creation from the former Andhra Pradesh was the most complex — the supplemental provisions of the Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2014 ran to 108 sections dealing with the allocation of water resources, power projects, debts, and service conditions, all legally valid as "supplemental, incidental and consequential" under Article 4(1).

**Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019** exercised the most contested use of Articles 3 and 4, converting the State of Jammu and Kashmir into two Union territories. This was constitutionally unusual: Article 3's Explanation I permits conversion to Union territory status, but the J&K case involved a State whose constitutional position was already special under Article 370, and the Parliament was acting as the State Legislature by Presidential Proclamation under Article 370(1)(b) rather than through normal consultation under Article 3's proviso. Critics argued this amounted to the Parliament reorganising a State without any genuine State legislature consultation, using the Presidential rule period to bypass the procedural safeguard in the Article 3 proviso.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly debated the status of territorial changes extensively. Some members — particularly those from regions that believed the colonial administrative map had divided natural linguistic or cultural communities — wanted State reorganisation to be easier, arguing that the Constituent Assembly was creating an interim territorial structure that would need rapid revision once the linguistic and cultural logic of India's regions was given constitutional effect. Others were concerned that easy reorganisation would encourage secessionist movements and that the Centre needed to be able to create and change States without giving incumbent State legislatures a veto.

The decision in Article 4(2) to exclude reorganisation laws from the Article 368 framework resolved this debate firmly in favour of flexibility. The framers recognised that the list of States in Schedule I was inherently provisional — the integration of princely States was still underway when the Constitution commenced — and that rigidity in territorial provisions would soon generate political pressure for constitutional crisis. The non-amendment declaration was intended to channel territorial disputes into parliamentary politics rather than constitutional amendment battles.

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar noted that the provision was essential given the unfinished character of territorial integration at the time of the Constitution's commencement. He was aware that the first decade would see major reorganisation, and he did not want every adjustment to require the super-majority machinery of Article 368.

## Key cases

**Mangal Singh v Union of India (1967) AIR 1967 SC 944** — The Supreme Court confirmed that a State reorganisation law passed under Articles 2 and 3 that adjusts Rajya Sabha seat allocations in the Fourth Schedule is valid under Article 4(1) and does not require the Article 368 procedure. This settled the legislative route for all subsequent reorganisations and removed the argument that changing Schedule IV was inherently a constitutional amendment.

**In re Berubari Union (1960) AIR 1960 SC 845** — The Presidential Reference addressed Article 4(2) and clarified that the exemption from Article 368 applies only to laws genuinely made under Articles 2 and 3. Any agreement that would cede territory to a foreign State still requires a constitutional amendment; Article 4(2) does not provide a shortcut for territorial cession, only for territorial internal reorganisation.

**Pradeep Kumar Biswas v Indian Institute of Chemical Biology (2002) 5 SCC 111** — The Court noted in passing that "supplemental, incidental and consequential provisions" in Article 4(1) are broad enough to cover transitional service conditions for government employees affected by reorganisation, illustrating that Article 4's scope extends well beyond purely territorial provisions.

**In re Article 370 (2023) — Jammu and Kashmir reorganisation challenge** — The Supreme Court upheld the J&K Reorganisation Act, 2019, including the conversion of J&K from a State to a Union territory. The majority avoided deciding whether a State can be permanently converted to Union territory through ordinary legislation under Articles 3 and 4, instead resting the decision on the special constitutional position of J&K under Article 370. The judgment left open the deeper question of whether Article 4(2)'s non-amendment declaration has limits — and whether basic structure principles can constrain what Parliament can do even through a law that is formally valid under Articles 3 and 4. The Court directed that J&K's statehood be restored, implicitly suggesting that permanent downgrade without restoration may be constitutionally questionable.

**Babulal Parate v State of Bombay (1960) AIR 1960 SC 51** — The Supreme Court considered whether the State legislature's view under Article 3's proviso had been properly obtained and observed that the proviso requires only that Parliament obtain the State legislature's views — Parliament is not bound by those views. This confirmed the asymmetry between the procedural form of consultation and substantive veto: Article 4 gives Parliament the power to reorganise; Article 3's proviso requires the form of consultation without the substance of consent.

## Current significance

The Article 4 non-amendment declaration has direct contemporary relevance in two contexts. First, the ongoing discussion about whether Jammu and Kashmir should regain full State status following the 2019 reorganisation turns on whether Articles 3 and 4 permit downgrade of a State to Union territory as a permanent measure, or only as a temporary measure with restoration implied. The Supreme Court's 2023 direction to restore statehood, while not deciding the constitutional question definitively, suggests that permanent downgrade through ordinary legislation may face future challenge.

Second, the question of State bifurcation — demands for separate States in Andhra's coastal region, in Vidarbha (Maharashtra), in Poorvanchal (Uttar Pradesh), and elsewhere — continues to be resolved through the Article 3/Article 4 mechanism. The creation of new States through ordinary legislation means these demands can in principle be met by a parliamentary majority at any time, creating political pressure at the centre of Centre-State relations. The ease of bifurcation under Articles 3 and 4 is a structural feature of Indian federalism that distinguishes it from all other major federal systems.

The interaction between Article 4(1)'s supplemental-provisions authority and the Seventh Schedule's List distribution is also an active question. Can a reorganisation law include provisions that touch upon List subjects — for instance, assigning a river's waters between two successor States — without this being characterised as a law on a List subject? The Telangana bifurcation generated ongoing disputes about whether the AP Reorganisation Act's water-allocation provisions were valid as supplemental or whether they constituted a law on inter-state river waters requiring Article 262 procedures. Courts have generally read Article 4(1) broadly, but the boundaries of "supplemental" remain contested.
