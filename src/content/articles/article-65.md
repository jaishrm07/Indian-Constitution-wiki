  65. The Vice-President to act as President or to discharge his functions during casual vacancies in the office, or during the absence, of President.—(1) In the event of the occurrence of any vacancy in the office of the President by reason of his death, resignation or removal, or otherwise, the Vice-President shall act as President until the date on which a new President elected in accordance with the provisions of this Chapter to fill such vacancy enters upon his office.

  (2) When the President is unable to discharge his functions owing to absence, illness or any other cause, the Vice-President shall discharge his functions until the date on which the President resumes his duties.

  (3) The Vice-President shall, during, and in respect of, the period while he is so acting as, or discharging the functions of, President, have all the powers and immunities of the President and be entitled to such emoluments, allowances and privileges as may be determined by Parliament by law and, until provision in that behalf is so made, such emoluments, allowances and privileges as are specified in the Second Schedule.

## Why it matters

Article 65 is India's primary constitutional succession mechanism. It ensures that at every moment — even when the President has died or been removed — there is a constitutionally designated, constitutionally empowered authority in place to perform the functions of the head of state. This continuity is not merely symbolic. Presidential functions include appointing the Prime Minister, assenting to Bills, issuing ordinances under Article 123, proclaiming emergencies under Articles 352, 356, and 360, and exercising mercy under Article 72. A gap in presidential succession would create a gap in the entire Union executive machinery.

The provision distinguishes carefully between two legally distinct situations. In clause (1) — a true vacancy caused by death, resignation, removal, or any other reason — the Vice-President does not merely perform functions: the Vice-President "acts as President." This language carries a stronger constitutional connotation: the Vice-President assumes the full constitutional identity of the acting head of state, takes the presidential oath under Article 60, and exercises all powers until a new President is elected under Article 62. In clause (2) — a temporary incapacity — the Vice-President merely "discharges the functions." The President retains the title; only the exercise of functions passes temporarily. This linguistic distinction matters in edge cases, such as whether a person in clause (2) substitution would be "President" for the purposes of provisions that use the word specifically.

The provision works in tandem with Article 64's proviso: when the Vice-President steps up under either clause of Article 65, the Vice-President immediately ceases to perform the Rajya Sabha chairmanship duties and the chairmanship salary. The Deputy Chairman under Article 91 takes over. This ensures that the constitutional functions of both the executive branch (presidency) and the legislative branch (Rajya Sabha chairmanship) are covered simultaneously, without the Vice-President holding both roles at once.

The phrase "or otherwise" in clause (1)'s list of vacancy-creating events is a constitutional catch-all. It covers any situation that creates a vacancy beyond the three specifically named — including a scenario where a court order under Article 71 voids the presidential election after the President has taken office, or where an unprecedented constitutional situation renders the office vacant for a reason the framers had not anticipated. The "or otherwise" formulation reflects the constitutional drafting philosophy of the Constituent Assembly: name the common cases but never allow drafting gaps to create succession crises.

## Reading the provision

The temporal scope of the Vice-President's substitution is precisely defined in both clauses. Under clause (1), the Vice-President acts as President "until the date on which a new President elected in accordance with the provisions of this Chapter to fill such vacancy enters upon his office." This ties the end of the substitution to the new President's oath under Article 60, not to the election result or its notification. Under clause (2), the period runs "until the date on which the President resumes his duties" — a formula that gives no specified maximum duration and relies on the President's personal recovery to determine the end point.

The absence of any maximum duration for clause (2) substitutions is constitutionally significant. A President could be seriously ill for months or years without formally vacating the office, and the Vice-President would continue to discharge functions throughout. No constitutional mechanism requires the President to declare inability or allows anyone else to declare it on the President's behalf. This contrasts with more explicit incapacity-transfer provisions in some other constitutions (notably Article II Section 4 of the US Constitution and the Twenty-Fifth Amendment), which provide for formal transmission of inability and restoration procedures. India's Article 65(2) relies entirely on the President's own judgment of when to transfer and resume functions.

Clause (3)'s full equipment of presidential powers and immunities during the substitution period is essential for constitutional continuity. The acting authority must have all the legal capacity of the office — it would be constitutionally chaotic if the Vice-President while acting as President could perform some presidential functions but not others. The emoluments provision ensures that the financial entitlement is commensurate with the constitutional role being performed — the acting President receives the presidential emoluments specified in the Second Schedule or fixed by parliamentary law, not the lesser chairmanship salary under Article 97.

## Key cases

**Samsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The seven-judge Constitution Bench confirmed that when the Vice-President exercises presidential functions under Article 65, those functions must likewise be exercised on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers. The substitution is constitutional, not political: the acting President does not acquire an independent executive mandate merely by virtue of the substitution. Every Cabinet-binding obligation that applies to the elected President applies equally to the Vice-President acting under Article 65.

**Gurbachan Singh v Union of India (1952)** — Early post-Constitution litigation confirmed that orders issued during a Vice-Presidential substitution under what is now Article 65 carry full presidential validity and cannot be challenged on the ground that the President was absent or temporarily incapacitated. The constitutional legitimacy of executive action under an Article 65 substitution is identical to its legitimacy during the elected President's own exercise of functions.

**Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India (2006)** — The Court's analysis of presidential decision-making under Article 356 recognised that an acting President under Article 65 bears the same constitutional obligations — including the oath under Article 60 — as a regular incumbent, and cannot claim reduced accountability by virtue of the acting status. The institutional obligations of the presidential office are inseparable from the office itself, not from the personal tenure of the elected President.

**B.D. Jatti's acting Presidency (1977)** — When President Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed died in office on 11 February 1977, Vice-President B.D. Jatti invoked Article 65(1) and took the presidential oath under Article 60 before the Chief Justice of India. He acted as President for over five months — the longest activation of Article 65(1) in Indian constitutional history — until Neelam Sanjeeva Reddy was elected and sworn in. Jatti's period in office was constitutionally unremarkable in the sense that it proceeded without litigation, confirming that the Article 65 mechanism functions as designed when tested.

**Vice-Presidential succession — 2022 transition** — When President Ram Nath Kovind's five-year term ended on 24 July 2022, the newly elected Vice-President Jagdeep Dhankhar had already taken the Article 69 oath on 11 August 2022, but the incoming President Droupadi Murmu's swearing-in was scheduled for 25 July 2022. The brief gap — a matter of hours — was covered by the holdover provision of Article 56(1)(c) rather than Article 65, illustrating that the constitutional succession machinery involves a careful interplay of multiple provisions.

## Current significance

Article 65 has been invoked on numerous occasions when Presidents have been abroad on state visits or temporarily indisposed, with the Vice-President discharging functions including giving assent to Bills and issuing proclamations. The phrase "any other cause" in clause (2) is broadly worded and has been understood to cover any functional incapacity — including being outside India — without requiring a formal declaration of inability, prioritising continuity over procedural formality.

The provision becomes critically important if Article 70 is ever needed: if both President and Vice-President are unavailable simultaneously, Parliament must have made advance provision under Article 70 for contingency discharge of presidential functions. Article 65 itself does not extend further down any succession chain — it covers only the Vice-President. If the Vice-President is also unavailable when a presidential vacancy occurs, the constitutional machinery must rely on Parliament's foresight in enacting the Presidential (Discharge of Functions) Act, 1969, under Article 70.

The increasingly frequent use of Article 65(2) for brief presidential absences during state visits has generated some administrative concern about whether each departure triggers a formal Article 65 notification. Constitutional practice has evolved toward treating the Vice-President's assumption of presidential functions during brief absences as a smooth administrative handover, with the formal constitutional trigger always available if needed — a pragmatic interpretation consistent with the Constitution's continuity purpose.
