## Why it matters

Article 371E was inserted by the Constitution (Thirty-second Amendment) Act, 1973, as part of a constitutional package designed to address the political crisis created by the Telangana agitation of 1969–73. The agitation was rooted in the perception — borne out by data on government employment and educational opportunity — that the Telangana region, which had been merged with the Andhra State in 1956 to form Andhra Pradesh, was being systematically disadvantaged relative to the Andhra and Rayalaseema regions. Educated Telangana youth found themselves outcompeted for places in the state's universities and for government jobs by candidates from the more educationally developed coastal Andhra region. The demand for a separate central university that would not be subject to the same intra-State admission competition was one of the specific, concrete educational grievances driving the agitation.

The provision matters constitutionally because it overrides the ordinary federal distribution of legislative powers. Education — specifically the establishment of universities — is primarily a State subject under Entry 32 of the State List (List II) of the Seventh Schedule. Parliament's general power to establish universities by legislation rests on Entry 63 of the Union List (which covers institutions of national importance, and certain specific named universities), and on the Concurrent List Entry 25 (which covers education generally including universities). But for a specific State, Article 371E creates an unambiguous, direct constitutional authorisation for Parliament to establish a university without needing to rely on a general Union List entry or on the argument that the institution is of national importance. This constitutional specificity matters both symbolically — it signals that the educational commitment to Telangana is constitutionally protected, not merely a matter of political will — and practically, as it gives the establishing legislation an unchallengeable constitutional pedigree.

The thirty-second Amendment also inserted Article 371D — the more operationally complex provision dealing with employment and admission reservations for Telangana within Andhra Pradesh — alongside Article 371E. Together, these two articles represented the constitutional dimension of the "Mulki Rules" settlement: Article 371D addressed employment access (through an Administrative Tribunal mechanism and specified reservation requirements), while Article 371E addressed educational access at the highest level through a national institution insulated from intra-State competition. They were conceived as a package, and both arose from the same political settlement that ended the most intense phase of the Telangana agitation without acceding to the demand for a separate State — a demand that would ultimately be fulfilled forty years later in 2014.

The provision is brief to the point of terseness, but the brevity is functional. Parliament is given a power and a target; the detail of how that power is exercised — the university's constitution, governance, statutes, financial arrangements — is left to the implementing legislation. This is consistent with the approach taken in other Article 371-series provisions: the Constitution creates the constitutional authority or protection, and the legislative or executive action fills in the operational content.

## Reading the provision

The article does two things. First, it grants Parliament the legislative power to establish a university in Andhra Pradesh. This is a grant of a specific power that sits outside the Seventh Schedule distribution, overriding the ordinary State legislative competence over universities in Andhra Pradesh on this one subject. Second, it implicitly defines the territorial scope: the university is to be established "in the State of Andhra Pradesh." This territorial reference, written in 1973 and based on the then-existing State of Andhra Pradesh (comprising the Andhra, Rayalaseema, and Telangana regions), became constitutionally significant in 2014 when Andhra Pradesh was bifurcated into the State of Telangana and the residual State of Andhra Pradesh.

Parliament exercised this power by enacting the Central University of Hyderabad Act, 1974, which established the University of Hyderabad in Hyderabad. The University was established in Hyderabad — then the capital of the undivided Andhra Pradesh — and was explicitly intended to serve the Telangana region's educational needs. The University of Hyderabad Act created a central university with its own statutes, Court, Executive Council, and Academic Council, funded by the University Grants Commission and the central government rather than the State government. This central funding and governance model was the operative mechanism for insulating the institution from intra-State political competition over admissions and appointments.

The article does not specify that the university must be located in Telangana specifically, or that it must serve Telangana's needs primarily — the text simply says Andhra Pradesh. This ambiguity, benign in 1973, became pointed after bifurcation: Hyderabad (where the University of Hyderabad is located) is in Telangana, not in the residual Andhra Pradesh. The question of whether Article 371E's reference to "the State of Andhra Pradesh" after bifurcation refers to Telangana (the successor State in whose territory the university sits), to the residual Andhra Pradesh (the State that inherited the name), or to the geographical area that was formerly Andhra Pradesh, is not resolved by the article's text and depends on the interpretation of the Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2014.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**The Telangana Agitation and the Thirty-second Amendment (1969–1973)** — The constitutional incident behind Article 371E is the Jai Telangana movement. The movement emerged from the 1969 agitation in Telangana, which was sparked by the government's decision to retain the Mulki Rules (which reserved government jobs for Telangana residents) but which quickly escalated into a broader demand for a separate Telangana State. The Mulki Rules themselves were challenged before the Andhra Pradesh High Court and the Supreme Court — with the Supreme Court in T. Devadasan v. Union of India and in the Mulki Rules cases of 1972 ultimately upholding the rules as consistent with the Constitution. But the agitation's specific educational grievance — the absence of a dedicated university giving Telangana students a genuine alternative to competing with coastal Andhra students — was addressed legislatively rather than judicially, through the Thirty-second Amendment's insertion of Articles 371D and 371E. The parliamentary history of the amendment records that Article 371E was specifically intended to address Telangana's demand for a central university free from regional competition within the State.

**The Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2014 and Article 371E** — The bifurcation of Andhra Pradesh into Telangana and the residual Andhra Pradesh by the Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2014 is the most significant event in Article 371E's post-enactment history. The Act, enacted under Article 3 of the Constitution, bifurcated the State without a corresponding constitutional amendment to Article 371E. Section 94 of the Reorganisation Act addressed the University of Hyderabad specifically, providing that it shall continue in Telangana and shall be available to students from the residual Andhra Pradesh on terms to be determined. The continued existence of the University of Hyderabad in Telangana under the Central University of Hyderabad Act, 1974 — legislation enacted under Article 371E — means that Article 371E operates as the constitutional basis for an institution that now primarily serves the State of Telangana rather than the State of Andhra Pradesh. This is an unusual constitutional situation: a provision drafted to address Telangana's educational disadvantage within Andhra Pradesh now underpins an institution in a separate State.

**Residual Andhra Pradesh's demand for a new central university** — After bifurcation, the residual State of Andhra Pradesh, whose capital Amaravati was under construction and which lost the University of Hyderabad to Telangana, sought its own central university. Parliament responded by establishing the Central University of Andhra Pradesh at Anantapur under the Central Universities Act, 2009 (as amended). This was done under Parliament's general power to establish central universities rather than under Article 371E. The existence of this new institution raises the question of whether Article 371E has been exhausted by the original exercise of the power (in the University of Hyderabad Act, 1974) or whether it could be used as a basis for establishing a further university in the residual Andhra Pradesh. The constitutional text does not limit Parliament to a single exercise of the power, and Article 371E could in principle authorise further central university legislation for Andhra Pradesh.

## Current significance

After the bifurcation of Andhra Pradesh in 2014, Article 371E occupies an ambiguous constitutional position. The institution it enabled — the University of Hyderabad — is located in Telangana, a State that did not exist when the provision was enacted. The residual State of Andhra Pradesh, which retained the constitutional name, no longer has the University of Hyderabad within its territory. This territorial dislocation means that Article 371E's original political purpose — ensuring educational access for the Telangana region within Andhra Pradesh — has been overtaken by bifurcation: Telangana now has its own government that can directly address educational policy, and the University of Hyderabad in Hyderabad is now a national institution serving the post-bifurcation Telangana State rather than a compensatory mechanism for a disadvantaged region within a larger State.

The University of Hyderabad's continued operation under the Central University of Hyderabad Act, 1974 does not formally depend on Article 371E for its day-to-day functioning. Parliament now has a well-established general power to establish central universities, and the institution's continued existence is grounded in the original Act and subsequent amendments rather than in a fresh exercise of Article 371E. However, Article 371E remains the constitutional pedigree of that original Act, and any challenge to the Act's validity — for instance, an argument that Parliament exceeded its competence in establishing the university — would be defeated by reference to Article 371E's direct grant of parliamentary power. In this sense, Article 371E functions as a continuing constitutional guarantee of the validity of the university's legislative foundation.

The provision's broader significance lies in what it illustrates about the Indian Constitution's approach to regional educational grievances. The decision to address Telangana's demand for a university through a constitutional amendment rather than through ordinary legislation — elevating the commitment to a level that cannot be undone by a simple Parliamentary majority without a further constitutional amendment — reflects the framers' recognition that some regional grievances are deep enough to warrant constitutional entrenchment. Whether that constitutional entrenchment continues to serve any practical purpose in the post-bifurcation landscape, or whether Article 371E is now a constitutional fossil — a provision whose political purpose has been superseded by events without being formally removed — is itself a question about how the Constitution ages and how it should be maintained as the country's political geography evolves.
