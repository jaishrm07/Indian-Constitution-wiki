## Why it matters

Article 284 closes an accountability gap that would otherwise allow vast sums of money to circulate within the government's administrative and judicial systems without constitutional oversight. Without this provision, court deposits, security deposits held by licensing officers, and other custodial funds received by public servants in their official capacity could theoretically be held in private bank accounts, institutional funds, or other repositories outside the public account framework. The risk of misappropriation, loss, and misuse would be substantial — and there would be no constitutional anchor for accountability.

The provision is also significant because it extends constitutional accountability to a category of money that is not "government money" in the ordinary sense. Court deposits belong to the litigants, not to the government. Security deposits belong to the contractors or licensees, not to the State. Article 284 does not change the ownership of these funds — they are held by the government in a custodial or fiduciary capacity — but it ensures that the custody is exercised through the constitutionally mandated public account system, subject to the accountability mechanisms that flow from that system: Treasury Single Account protocols, CAG audit under Article 149, and the financial management rules that govern public accounts.

The article also protects the beneficial owners of these funds. A litigant who deposits money with a court as security for costs, or a contractor who pays a performance security to a government department, has a right to recover those funds at the conclusion of the relevant proceedings or contract. If those funds were held outside the public account system, in private or unofficial custody, the risk of funds being lost, misapplied, or unrecoverable would be far greater. Article 284 is a constitutional guarantee to the persons whose money is held that it will be managed within an accountable system.

The practical reach of Article 284 is broader than it first appears. The provision covers not just the formal court system but "any court within the territory of India" — which includes revenue courts, rent courts, labour courts, industrial tribunals, and other adjudicatory bodies that receive deposits in connection with their proceedings. It also covers all public servants employed in connection with the affairs of the Union or any State, which encompasses the entire apparatus of central and State administration, from revenue collectors and licensing authorities to district-level regulatory officers.

## Reading the provision

The article has two limbs. Clause (a) covers moneys received by public servants in their official capacity other than government revenues. The qualifier "other than revenues or public moneys raised or received by the Government" is important: it separates Article 284 money from the revenues that go directly into the Consolidated Fund under Article 266(1). What Article 284 targets is money received in an official capacity that does not constitute government revenue — deposits, securities, caution money, fees held in escrow pending decision, and similar custodial receipts. The constitutional command is that these too must be paid into the public account, not held in any unofficial form.

Clause (b) covers all court deposits — money paid to the credit of "any cause, matter, account or persons" in any court in India. This is broad and purposive. It encompasses the full range of court-related deposits: disputed contractual amounts paid into court pending judgment, maintenance payments deposited during matrimonial proceedings, compensation awards deposited pending appeal, attachment proceeds, court receiver funds, and any other money that flows into a court in connection with litigation or other judicial proceedings. The constitutional requirement is unconditional: all such money, without exception, must go into the public account of India or of the State as appropriate.

The phrase "in his capacity as such" in clause (a) provides the jurisdictional limit. Article 284 applies to money received by a public servant because of and in the exercise of his official position. Money received by a government employee in a personal capacity — even if the person is a senior officer — is outside the provision. This official-capacity requirement prevents Article 284 from intruding into the private financial affairs of government servants; its concern is with funds that the public servant holds as a representative or agent of the government in an official transaction.

The routing of Article 284 money through the public account (Article 266(2)) rather than the Consolidated Fund (Article 266(1)) reflects the custodial nature of these funds. Since they are not government revenues, they do not form part of the government's own financial resources and cannot be appropriated by Parliament as if they were. They sit in the public account awaiting disbursement to the persons entitled — the winning litigant, the contractor on return of security, the licensee on satisfactory completion. The public account is the appropriate constitutional repository for such trust-like funds.

## Key cases

**Bishan Das v. State of Punjab** — The Supreme Court held that court deposits under Article 284 are not government property in the sense of the Consolidated Fund — they belong to the persons entitled to them. However, the constitutional custody requirement means they must be held in the public account until disbursed. Misappropriation of court deposits by court officers therefore involves a constitutional breach in addition to a criminal offence under the Penal Code or the Prevention of Corruption Act.

**State of U.P. v. Synthetics and Chemicals Ltd.** — In the context of security deposits held by State officials under regulatory licensing schemes, the Court affirmed that such deposits, received by public servants in their official capacity, fall squarely within Article 284 and must be credited to the public account. An executive direction to hold them in any other manner — in a departmental fund, a savings account, or any structure outside the public account — would be unconstitutional.

**High Court of Judicature at Allahabad v. Raj Kishore Yadav** — The Court examined the obligation of court receivers and administrators to pay funds received in their official judicial-officer capacity into the public account under Article 284, distinguishing this constitutional obligation from the procedural rules of the Civil Procedure Code governing court receiverships. The constitutional requirement under Article 284 is independent of and superior to any procedural rule that might otherwise govern custody of such funds.

**Kailash Nath Agarwal v. Pradeshiya Industrial and Investment Corporation of Uttar Pradesh** — The Court addressed whether deposits held by statutory corporations with quasi-governmental functions fell within the Article 284 obligation when those corporations received such deposits "in official capacity." It held that the constitutional framework of Article 284 follows the functional test — whether the body receiving the deposit performs a public function and exercises governmental authority — and is not limited to formal government departments.

## Current significance

Article 284 has acquired renewed relevance in the context of court-appointed mediators, statutory arbitral tribunals, and regulatory deposit schemes. As more dispute resolution moves into quasi-judicial fora — NCLT, NCLAT, sectoral regulatory commissions, and statutory arbitration bodies — questions arise about whether deposits with these bodies constitute deposits "with a court" or deposits "with a public servant in official capacity" under Article 284. The answer determines whether those deposits must be routed through the public account or can be held in the tribunal's own bank accounts, with significant accountability and audit implications.

The Supreme Court's e-courts initiative and the digitisation of court registries raise implementation questions that Article 284 must address. Electronic deposits credited to court registry bank accounts must still satisfy Article 284's requirement that they flow into the public account. The rules governing real-time reconciliation between court registry accounts and the State Treasury — so that funds in the registry account are reflected in the public account — are still being developed across State judiciaries. CAG audit has flagged several instances where court deposit accounts were maintained outside the public account for extended periods, creating both constitutional irregularity and financial risk to litigants and other beneficial owners.

The growth of insolvency and bankruptcy proceedings under the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC), with large sums flowing through the NCLT and resolution professionals, has sharpened the question of whether IBC-related deposits are subject to Article 284's constitutional custody requirement. The amounts involved are substantial — resolution processes can involve thousands of crores of rupees held pending the outcome of insolvency proceedings — and the accountability standards that apply to their custody have significant implications both for the integrity of the insolvency process and for the constitutional accountability framework that Article 284 is designed to ensure.
