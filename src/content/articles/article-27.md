## Why it matters

Article 27 expresses constitutional secularism in its fiscal dimension. The Indian state does not merely tolerate all religions equally: it is constitutionally prohibited from compelling citizens to financially underwrite the promotion of any religion through earmarked taxation. The provision converts the secular character of the state from an aspiration into a concrete fiscal constraint. It sits alongside Article 25 (freedom of conscience), Article 26 (freedom to manage religious affairs), and Article 28 (prohibition on religious instruction in state-funded schools) as part of the Constitution's multi-pronged secularism framework.

The practical context in which Article 27 operates is distinctive. India has a long tradition of state involvement in religious endowments, pilgrimage infrastructure, and temple administration — all predating the Constitution. Article 27 does not require the state to become indifferent to religion; it targets the specific mischief of compelled financial support through taxes whose proceeds are earmarked for religious promotion. This precision makes the line-drawing task for courts both important and difficult.

The stakes are concrete. A Hindu taxpayer objecting to state expenditure on a mosque, a Muslim taxpayer objecting to government grants for temple renovation, or a secular citizen opposing Haj subsidies paid from general revenues — each reaches for Article 27. The provision also has a structural function: it reinforces the idea that the state's engagement with religion must be regulatory and welfare-oriented, not confessional. When the state crosses from regulating religious institutions to actively propagating a faith using public money extracted from unwilling citizens, it threatens the religious neutrality that is a basic feature of the Constitution.

The asymmetric reality of Indian religious law makes Article 27 especially important. Most major endowment legislation in southern and western India applies only to Hindu institutions. Administrative levies on temple income, surcharges on religious charities, and contributions to endowment boards are collected primarily from Hindu religious bodies. If those levies are classified as taxes appropriated for the maintenance of Hindu religion, they could violate Article 27. Conversely, if the state exempts Muslim or Christian institutions from similar levies, an equality challenge under Articles 14 and 15 arises. Article 27 thus sits at the intersection of secularism and equal treatment of religions.

A further dimension is the growing scale of state expenditure on pilgrimage infrastructure. The Kashi Vishwanath Corridor, Amarnath Shrine Board grants, and state funding of the Char Dham highway project involve substantial Consolidated Fund outlays on infrastructure centred on Hindu pilgrimage. Critics argue these expenditures promote Hinduism at general taxpayer expense, while supporters distinguish between tourism infrastructure and religious promotion. Article 27 frames this debate even where it may not furnish a direct remedy, because the question of when state spending crosses from legitimate development into unconstitutional religious promotion is the question Article 27 was designed to address.

The provision is deceptively short — a single sentence — but it encodes a sophisticated constitutional philosophy. It does not prohibit all state expenditure connected to religion. It does not require the state to treat religion as a purely private matter. It targets compulsion and earmarking together: citizens must be forced to pay, and the proceeds must be specifically routed to religious promotion. This double trigger makes the provision more precise than a general non-establishment clause, but also easier to circumvent by structuring expenditure through general appropriations rather than dedicated levies.

## Reading the provision

The operative phrase is "specifically appropriated in payment of expenses for the promotion or maintenance of any particular religion or religious denomination." Every element of this phrase has been litigated. "Compelled to pay" means that voluntary religious contributions or community endowment management fees do not engage Article 27 at all. If citizens choose to contribute to a religious trust of their faith, the constitutional prohibition is simply inapplicable: Article 27 reaches only the coercive power of the state acting through compulsory taxation. This reading leaves intact the entire apparatus of voluntary religious giving — zakat, tithe, daan — as matters of personal religious freedom under Article 25.

"Taxes" has been read narrowly in the case law. Fees charged for services rendered, regulatory levies, and charges for the use of state-administered religious endowment oversight machinery are distinguished from taxes in the strict sense. The distinction between a tax and a fee is well-established in Indian constitutional law: a tax is a compulsory levy whose proceeds flow into general revenues, while a fee is a charge exacted for identifiable services rendered and whose proceeds are applied to the cost of those services. If a State endowment board charges temples an annual contribution for the board's regulatory and supervisory activities, that charge may be characterised as a fee rather than a tax, placing it outside Article 27's scope entirely.

"Specifically appropriated" is the most critical qualifier. If the proceeds of a levy flow into the Consolidated Fund and are thereafter allocated by appropriation law to general state purposes — some of which may incidentally support religious bodies — Article 27 is not violated. It is violated only when the tax proceeds are earmarked at the point of collection for expenditure on religious promotion. This reading creates a structural loophole: a government that wishes to fund religious activity can do so by routing funds through the Consolidated Fund and enacting an appropriation rather than a dedicated religious levy. The earmarking requirement thus invites evasion through clever fiscal architecture, and critics argue that a purposive interpretation should look at the functional effect rather than the formal designation.

"Promotion or maintenance of any particular religion" requires that a religion or denomination be identifiable. A general contribution to "religion in India" or to "all faiths equally" does not trigger Article 27, though such a scheme might engage Article 15 or Article 14 if it systematically excludes or disadvantages particular groups. Courts have drawn a distinction between maintenance — keeping existing religious infrastructure operational — and promotion — actively propagating faith or increasing its adherents. Both are within Article 27's prohibition when funded by compelled taxation. An important corollary is that the prohibition attaches to any particular religion, including the majority religion. The framers designed Article 27 symmetrically: no citizen, regardless of faith, can be compelled to fund religious promotion for any denomination, not merely minorities.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The provision that became Article 27 had a relatively smooth passage through the Constituent Assembly compared with the more contested religious freedom articles. The Drafting Committee's principal concern was to prevent the possibility of a Hindu-majority legislature using its power to raise taxes in a manner that would compel minority religious communities — Muslims, Christians, Parsis, Sikhs — to fund the promotion of Hinduism, or vice versa. B.R. Ambedkar, presenting the draft to the Assembly, emphasised that the prohibition was a corollary of the principle of a secular state: a state that does not endorse any religion should not use its fiscal machinery to promote one. The provision was understood as the financial counterpart of the freedom of conscience guaranteed in Article 25.

A significant debate concerned the relationship between Article 27 and the existing system of religious endowments. Several members from the southern states noted that temple administration had long been subject to state oversight and that endowment boards charged contributions from religious institutions as a condition of their administrative supervision. These members sought assurance that the new constitutional prohibition would not invalidate the entire endowment administration architecture. The Drafting Committee addressed this concern by framing the prohibition around "taxes" and "specifically appropriated," leaving room for regulatory fees charged in return for genuine public services to religious institutions. This drafting choice anticipated the subsequent distinction drawn by the Supreme Court in Shirur Mutt between taxes and fees.

A further strand of debate addressed the Haj subsidy and analogous practices under which the colonial government had extended travel concessions and administrative assistance to Muslim pilgrims. Some members argued that continuation of such practices would violate the spirit of Article 27 even if the funds flowed from general revenues rather than dedicated taxes. Others argued that welfare expenditure benefiting religious communities was consistent with a secular state's obligation to protect the interests of all citizens. The Assembly did not resolve this tension definitively, and it persists in contemporary litigation over pilgrimage subsidies and religious infrastructure funding. The ambiguity is not accidental: the framers were aware that Indian secularism would be a form of principled engagement with religion rather than strict separation, and they calibrated Article 27 to prohibit compelled confessional taxation while leaving space for general welfare expenditure that incidentally connects to religious communities.

## Key cases

**Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments, Madras v. Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar of Sri Shirur Mutt AIR 1954 SC 282** — A mutt challenged the levy imposed under the Madras Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act as a compulsory tax appropriated for religious purposes in violation of Article 27. The Supreme Court held that the contribution was not a tax but a regulatory fee for services rendered by the state in supervising religious endowments, and that the proceeds were applied to the administration of the oversight mechanism rather than to promote Hinduism. The Court established the foundational tax-versus-fee distinction that has governed Article 27 analysis ever since, and held that Article 27 does not prevent the state from recovering the costs of genuine regulatory services from the institutions being regulated.

**Ratilal Panachand Gandhi v. State of Bombay AIR 1954 SC 388** — Jain religious trusts challenged provisions of the Bombay Public Trusts Act 1950 that subjected denominational funds to state supervision and required that they be applied to purposes approved by a public authority. The Court struck down the provisions primarily on Article 26(b) grounds, holding that they displaced the denomination's right to manage its own religious affairs. Although Article 27 was not the primary ground, the judgment clarified the relationship between Articles 26 and 27: the fiscal constraint in Article 27 and the autonomy protection in Article 26 are complementary aspects of denominational freedom, reinforcing each other rather than occupying separate constitutional compartments.

**Azeez Basha v. Union of India AIR 1968 SC 662** — The Supreme Court considered challenges to the Aligarh Muslim University Act and acknowledged, in passing, that Article 27 prohibits compulsory religious taxation but does not prevent the state from making general grants-in-aid that incidentally benefit religious or minority institutions. The critical constitutional line is between general-purpose state funding for educational or charitable purposes — which may flow to faith-linked bodies — and taxes whose proceeds are specifically earmarked to promote a faith. The case confirmed that the earmarking requirement is the key constitutional trigger, not the mere fact that state money reaches a religious body.

**Prafull Goradia v. Union of India (2011) 2 SCC 705** — The petitioner directly challenged government expenditure from the Consolidated Fund on Haj pilgrimage subsidies as violating Article 27. The Supreme Court rejected the challenge on the ground that the amount spent was neither a compulsory levy on any person nor the proceeds of a tax specifically appropriated for religious promotion: it was a discretionary government expenditure from general revenues. The ruling confirmed that Article 27 targets the collection and earmarking mechanism, not the spending side of a general appropriation. The Court noted that if every government expenditure that benefited a religious community violated Article 27, the state could not fund charitable or educational activities carried out by religious bodies, a result inconsistent with Indian secular practice.

**Tilkayat Shri Govindlalji Maharaj v. State of Rajasthan AIR 1963 SC 1638** — The Court considered the constitutionality of the Nathdwara Temple Act and the extent to which the state could impose administrative control over a private religious institution. While the primary issues concerned Articles 25 and 26, the Court's analysis of permissible state involvement in temple administration is directly relevant to Article 27: state administrative oversight funded by temple revenues does not constitute a tax appropriated for the promotion of religion, provided the oversight serves genuine regulatory purposes rather than becoming a mechanism for the state to propagate the faith.

**Sri Adi Visheshwara of Kashi Vishwanath Temple v. State of Uttar Pradesh AIR 1997 SC 2065** — The Supreme Court upheld the UP Shri Kashi Vishwanath Temple Act, which brought the famous temple under state administration. The Court held that the administrative framework did not violate Article 27 because the levies collected were regulatory in character and the state's role was managerial rather than confessional. The judgment is significant because it illustrates the practical limits of Article 27: even where the state takes extensive administrative control over a major Hindu pilgrimage site, the provision does not bar that control provided there is no dedicated tax on the general public whose proceeds are appropriated for Hinduism's promotion.

## Current significance

Article 27 remains actively litigated in several recurring contexts. Hindu religious endowment statutes in multiple states levy contributions and administration fees on temple income; petitioners argue these constitute taxes appropriated for the maintenance of Hindu religion, while governments defend them as regulatory fees. The High Courts of Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, and Karnataka have seen repeated challenges to contribution slabs under Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Acts, with outcomes turning on the factual question whether the charges correspond to services actually rendered.

State funding of pilgrimage infrastructure presents the sharpest contemporary challenge. The Kashi Vishwanath Corridor, funded substantially from state and Central budgets, the Amarnath Shrine Board receiving annual grants, the Sabarimala development fund, and the Char Dham all-weather road project involve large Consolidated Fund outlays at sites of religious significance to one community. Critics raise Article 27 concerns alongside Article 14 equality arguments: if the state invests this scale of resources in Hindu pilgrimage infrastructure, is there comparable investment in minority religious sites? Courts have generally deflected Article 27 challenges to infrastructure spending by holding that the expenditure comes from general revenues rather than earmarked taxes, but the functional equivalence argument — that general revenue spending achieves the same religious promotion as earmarked taxation — remains undecided.

The asymmetry of Indian endowment legislation is a structurally unresolved problem. Most Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Acts apply only to Hindu institutions; Waqf Acts govern Muslim religious properties under different administrative frameworks; and Christian and other minority religious bodies fall under general trust law. This differentiation means that the Article 27 regulatory-fee argument operates almost exclusively against Hindu institutions, since they alone are subject to the contribution levies that generate Article 27 challenges. Whether this asymmetry itself violates the secular principle underlying Article 27 — by imposing financial obligations on one religion's institutions that analogous institutions of other faiths do not bear — is a live question before several courts.

The open constitutional question is whether Article 27 can be given purposive bite against the modern practice of routing religious promotion expenditure through the Consolidated Fund and general appropriations, thereby avoiding the technical earmarking that triggers the provision. A purely formal reading renders Article 27 largely irrelevant to the most significant contemporary forms of state support for religion. A purposive reading that asks whether the functional effect of state spending is to appropriate public funds for religious promotion would give the provision much more force, but would also require courts to make difficult empirical judgments about the religious character of state spending decisions. That tension between formal and purposive interpretation of Article 27 is the central doctrinal question the Supreme Court has yet to resolve directly.
