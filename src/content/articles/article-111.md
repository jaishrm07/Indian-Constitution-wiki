## Why it matters

Article 111 is the constitutional moment at which the legislative process becomes law — or does not. It defines the President's role in the final stage of Bill passage and determines the balance of power between Parliament and the head of state. The article's design is deliberately asymmetric — the President's return power is only a suspensive veto, not an absolute one; if Parliament repasses a returned Bill, the President must assent. This ensures that Parliament, as the representative body, retains the final legislative word while the President retains a meaningful deliberative function.

The article's silence on a timeline for presidential action has generated the "pocket veto" controversy — whether a President (or Governor, under the parallel Article 200) who simply fails to act on a Bill is effectively vetoing it indefinitely without facing any constitutional remedy. The Supreme Court's 2023 and 2024 rulings have begun to fill this gap, establishing an implied reasonable-time obligation that the constitutional text does not spell out.

## What this Article does

Article 111 governs the final stage of the central legislative process.
Once both Houses of Parliament have passed a Bill, it is presented to the
President, who has three options: (1) assent to the Bill, whereupon it
becomes law; (2) withhold assent, which kills the Bill; or (3) for
non-Money Bills only, return it to the Houses with a message requesting
reconsideration and specifying any recommended amendments. The crucial
asymmetry is in the proviso: if both Houses reconsider and pass the Bill
again — with or without the President's suggested amendments — the
President is constitutionally obliged to assent. Parliament therefore has
the last word, and the return power functions as a suspensive rather than
an absolute veto.

Money Bills are excluded from the return procedure. Because a Money Bill
can only originate in the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha's power over it is
merely advisory, adding a Presidential reconsideration loop would create
an additional procedural hurdle that the Constitution's financial
provisions do not contemplate.

## Reading the provision

Three features of the text deserve close attention.

**No time limit.** Article 111 imposes no deadline. The words "as soon as
possible" appear only in the proviso governing a return for
reconsideration, not in the main clause dealing with assent or withholding
of assent. This drafting gap has generated the "pocket veto" controversy:
a President (or, by analogy under Article 200, a Governor) who simply
declines to act at all is technically not "withholding assent" but is also
not assenting, leaving the Bill in constitutional limbo indefinitely.

**Reasoned message required for return.** The power of return is limited
to non-Money Bills and requires the President to accompany the return with
a reasoned message. The President cannot return a Bill for tactical delay
without specifying the grounds; the return must at minimum identify the
provisions or amendments under consideration. This requirement connects
the return power to the advice process under Article 74 — the President
acts on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers, and any return
message will reflect the Cabinet's assessment of the Bill.

**Absolute duty to assent after repassing.** Once a returned Bill is
repassed by both Houses, the obligation to assent is absolute and
unqualified. The Constitution uses the words "shall not withhold assent
therefrom," placing the President under a positive duty with no
discretionary exception. The only open question is whether this duty also
extinguishes any power to reserve the repassed Bill for the opinion of the
Supreme Court under Article 143 — generally regarded as still available in
principle but effectively foreclosed by convention.

## Article 74 and the role of Cabinet advice

Because the President acts on the binding aid and advice of the Council of
Ministers under Article 74, the practical decision to assent, withhold
assent, or return a Bill is the Cabinet's decision. The President is not a
free agent. This has implications for the pocket-veto problem: where a
Cabinet that previously passed a Bill has since been replaced by a new
government that opposes the Bill, the new Cabinet could in theory advise
the President to decline action indefinitely. Courts have not yet resolved
whether Article 74 advice can be used to achieve what Article 111 does not
permit by text.

## Key cases

**K. Nagaraj v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1985)** — The Supreme Court
confirmed that assent is a constitutive step in the legislative process,
not a ministerial formality. A law does not come into existence until the
President has assented to it, so any challenge to the validity of a
pre-assent Bill is premature. The Court also noted that the assent process
is integral to the constitutional scheme of checks between Parliament and
the executive head of state.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)** — A seven-judge Constitution
Bench held that the President acts on the binding aid and advice of the
Council of Ministers in all constitutional functions save those that the
Constitution itself expressly reserves to Presidential discretion. This
ruling anchors the Article 111 process firmly in Cabinet government: the
decision to assent, withhold assent, or return a Bill is in practice the
Cabinet's decision, communicated through the President.

**State of Punjab v. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab
(2023)** — The Supreme Court held that indefinite inaction on Bills
pending before the Governor (and, by analogy, the President) is
constitutionally impermissible. The Court read a reasonable-time
requirement into the silence of the text, reasoning that the Governor's
constitutional role is to facilitate, not obstruct, the legislative
process. The judgment directly confronts the pocket-veto problem.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. Governor of Tamil Nadu (2024)** — A Constitution
Bench went further, holding that a Governor who withholds assent from
Bills passed by the State Legislature without returning them acts
unconstitutionally. Drawing on Article 111 principles to interpret Article
200, the Court declared assent to ten Tamil Nadu Bills to be deemed given
from the date of presentation to the Governor, effectively supplying the
constitutional remedy the text had omitted.

## Relationship with Article 200

Article 200 is the State-level mirror of Article 111: it governs assent by
the Governor to State Legislature Bills and adds a third option —
reservation of the Bill for the President's consideration. Presidential
assent to State Bills is then governed by Article 201. The controversies
litigated under Article 200 in Punjab and Tamil Nadu directly inform the
interpretation of Article 111, because both provisions share the same
structural logic and the same drafting silence on timelines.

## Current significance

The "pocket veto" — indefinite inaction, neither assenting nor formally
withholding nor returning — has emerged as one of the most contested
constitutional questions in Indian federalism. The 2023 and 2024 Supreme
Court decisions have moved toward a doctrine of implied reasonable-time
obligation, but the precise remedy (deemed assent, mandamus, or
declaration of unconstitutionality) remains unsettled at the Union level.
The question of whether a President can return a Bill when the government
that originally passed it has since changed also tests the boundaries of
Article 74, since the current Cabinet's advice may be to stall a Bill that
a predecessor Cabinet had moved through Parliament.
