## Why it matters

Article 19 is the Constitution's central catalogue of civil liberties — the freedoms of speech and expression, peaceful assembly, forming associations, free movement across India, residence anywhere in India, and carrying on any occupation or business. Each freedom is subject to reasonable restrictions that Parliament and State Legislatures may impose under clauses (2) through (6). But a law going beyond those limits — say, a blanket prohibition on all political meetings, total press censorship, or a sweeping restriction on movement — would ordinarily be struck down under Article 19 as disproportionate or not within the permitted heads of restriction.

Article 358 creates a categorically different regime for the duration of a war or external aggression emergency. During such an emergency, Article 19 ceases to constrain the State's legislative or executive power. The State may impose total press censorship, ban public meetings, prohibit freedom of movement and residence, or shut down associations — none of these measures can be challenged on Article 19 grounds while the emergency is in force. The constitutional justification is the extreme national exigency of warfare or external attack: in such circumstances, the State must be capable of acting swiftly and comprehensively without individual civil liberties litigation obstructing the defence effort.

The most important structural feature of Article 358 is that it is automatic. Once an emergency on grounds of war or external aggression is proclaimed under Article 352, Article 19 is suspended by operation of the Constitution itself — no further presidential order is required. This automatic character sharply distinguishes Article 358 from Article 359, which requires a specific presidential order naming the rights whose judicial enforcement is to be suspended. The distinction matters enormously in practice — under Article 358, the moment the emergency proclamation is issued, every law and executive action touching Article 19 subjects is immunised, without any separate government decision or notification.

**The 44th Amendment's critical narrowing.** The original Constitution allowed an emergency proclamation on grounds of "internal disturbance," and the original Article 358 suspended Article 19 on any such proclamation. In 1975, Prime Minister Indira Gandhi's government used an internal disturbance proclamation to declare an emergency aimed at suppressing domestic political opposition — a situation having nothing to do with external threat — and the automatic suspension of Article 19 was then used to impose press censorship, ban opposition meetings, jail political opponents, and suppress civil society. The 44th Amendment of 1978 responded by replacing "internal disturbance" with "armed rebellion" as a ground for proclamation under Article 352, and by confining Article 358's automatic suspension to emergencies proclaimed on grounds of war or external aggression only. An emergency proclaimed on the armed rebellion ground no longer automatically suspends Article 19 — the government must instead seek a specific presidential order under Article 359 naming the rights to be suspended.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the automatic suspension.** While a war or external aggression emergency is in operation under Article 352, nothing in Article 19 restricts the State's power to make laws or take executive action. The State's competence to legislate or act is expanded to include any measure that would otherwise be barred by Article 19's protections. Laws made on this expanded competence are, however, sunset laws — to the extent they are incompatible with Article 19 they cease to have effect as soon as the emergency ends. The saving clause provides that things done or omitted during the emergency period remain valid even after the emergency is revoked; completed executive acts are not retrospectively invalidated by the end of the emergency.

**The recital requirement — clause (2).** This clause was inserted by the 44th Amendment and is a significant safeguard. A law must contain a recital — a statement on the face of the legislation — declaring that it is enacted in relation to the emergency proclamation then in operation. Executive action must be taken under a law containing such a recital. If a law lacks this recital, Article 358(1) protection does not apply to it even if it was enacted during an emergency. This requirement is an important accountability mechanism — it prevents the government from using the shadow of an emergency to immunise legislation that has no genuine connection to the national security crisis. Parliament and the executive are put on notice, and the public is informed, that emergency powers are being invoked, and the nexus with the proclamation must be expressly acknowledged.

**Geographic limitation.** The proviso to clause (1) deals with partial emergencies — situations where an emergency operates only in part of the territory of India. In such a case, laws made and executive actions taken under Article 358 in areas outside the emergency zone are valid only if the security of India is threatened by activities connected to the emergency zone. This prevents a localised emergency from being used as a pretext for nationwide suppression of Article 19 freedoms. The geographic limitation mirrors the structure of the proviso to Article 353 and the geographic scope provisions elsewhere in Part XVIII.

**Saving of completed acts.** The survival of completed acts after the emergency lapses means the consequences of emergency-era restrictions on civil liberties can outlast the emergency itself. A person who was arrested, whose property was seized, or whose publication was banned under an emergency law still has no Article 19 claim even after the emergency ends. The courts may, however, apply other constitutional grounds of challenge — Articles 14 (equality), 21 (life and liberty), or the basic structure doctrine — to such completed acts, depending on the circumstances.

**Interaction with Article 359.** Article 358 and Article 359 operate in parallel but are structurally different. Article 358 applies only to war or external aggression emergencies and operates automatically to suspend Article 19 itself — the rights are simply unavailable. Article 359 requires a presidential order and suspends the right to move courts for enforcement of specified rights — the rights technically survive but cannot be enforced judicially. For an armed rebellion emergency, where Article 358 does not apply, the government may obtain an Article 359 order to suspend enforcement of Article 19 rights, but it must make that specific decision rather than relying on automaticity.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The original draft of Article 358 attracted debate in the Constituent Assembly primarily on the question of how far civil liberties should yield to national security during an emergency. Dr B R Ambedkar defended the automatic suspension of Article 19 on grounds of practical necessity — a state at war must be capable of acting decisively without the delays of constitutional litigation. Several members, including K T Shah and Shibban Lal Saxena, raised concerns that the "internal disturbance" ground in the original Article 352 was too broad and that tying Article 358's automatic suspension to such a broad ground risked normalising emergency suppression of legitimate political activity. These concerns proved prescient. The framers did not amend the draft at the time, but the subsequent history of the 1975 Emergency vindicated the critics, and the 44th Amendment's reform was a belated enactment of the limiting principle those critics had advocated.

T T Krishnamachari in the Assembly emphasised that the Article 358 mechanism was modelled on wartime constitutional practice in parliamentary democracies — the idea that in genuine warfare, the state needs a free hand. The key premise was that an external security emergency is qualitatively different from an internal political crisis, a distinction the 44th Amendment ultimately embedded in the constitutional text itself.

## Key cases

**Makhan Singh Tarsikka v State of Punjab AIR 1964 SC 381** — The Supreme Court decided this case during the emergency proclaimed in 1962 following the India-China war. The Court drew the fundamental constitutional distinction between Articles 358 and 359 — Article 358 operates automatically on proclamation and suspends Article 19 itself, whereas Article 359 requires a specific presidential order and suspends only the right to move courts. The Court confirmed that Article 358 operates from the moment of proclamation, covers both legislative and executive action that would otherwise violate Article 19, and that laws made under the emergency authority cease to apply prospectively when the emergency ends but completed actions remain valid. Makhan Singh remains the foundational procedural map for understanding the two emergency rights provisions.

**Bhut Nath Mete v State of West Bengal AIR 1974 SC 806** — The Court clarified the scope of Article 358's protective immunity. Only laws and executive actions that could not have been undertaken but for the Part III restrictions fall within Article 358's protection — meaning only measures that would have violated Article 19 in normal times. Article 358 is not a general licence for all State action during an emergency; it does not immunise laws or actions that were already competent under ordinary law without any Article 19 impediment. This limitation prevents the emergency regime from being stretched to protect conduct that had nothing to do with the exercise of emergency powers.

**ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla AIR 1976 SC 1207** — Decided during the 1975 Emergency, the Supreme Court majority ruled that when the right to move courts under Article 21 had been suspended by presidential order under Article 359, no habeas corpus petition could be heard — the courts were wholly excluded from reviewing the legality of detention. The broader political context of this case — an emergency proclaimed on internal disturbance grounds and Article 358 used to impose press censorship and ban political activity — was the immediate backdrop for the 44th Amendment's reforms. Justice H R Khanna's solitary dissent, arguing that the executive cannot take life or liberty without the authority of law regardless of any emergency order, was subsequently vindicated. The case remains the most significant demonstration of what an unconstrained reading of the emergency provisions could produce.

**A K Roy v Union of India (1982) 1 SCC 271** — The Court addressed the constitutionality of preventive detention legislation enacted in the post-Emergency period. Reviewing the deliberate legislative history of the 44th Amendment, the Court noted Parliament's express choice to confine Article 358's automatic suspension to external emergencies and to exclude armed rebellion emergencies from its scope. This exclusion was a direct corrective to the 1975 Emergency experience, and the Court emphasised that interpreting Article 358's scope in a manner that restored the pre-Amendment breadth would defeat the purpose of the constitutional reform.

**Minerva Mills Ltd v Union of India (1980) 3 SCC 625** — While primarily dealing with the basic structure doctrine and Parliament's amending power, the Court's examination of the 44th Amendment's reforms to the emergency provisions confirmed the constitutional validity of the narrowing of Article 358 to external emergencies only. The Court held that the post-Amendment framework reflected a deliberate rebalancing between emergency necessity and protection of fundamental rights, and that this rebalancing itself expressed the basic structure values of democracy and rule of law.

**Naga People's Movement of Human Rights v Union of India (1998) 2 SCC 109** — The Court examined the relationship between emergency-adjacent legislation — specifically the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act — and the fundamental rights framework. While not directly on Article 358, the judgment's analysis of when security legislation may restrict Article 19 freedoms outside the formal emergency framework is directly relevant to understanding the practical boundary of Article 358's domain and the question of whether emergency-like conditions can be generated without a formal proclamation.

## Current significance

The 44th Amendment's limitation of Article 358 to war and external aggression emergencies is widely regarded as one of the most important structural safeguards against repetition of the 1975 Emergency experience. Since the third National Emergency ended in 1977, no National Emergency has been proclaimed, and Article 358 has not been operationally invoked. Nevertheless, the provision's constitutional significance persists on several fronts.

First, in a genuine war scenario involving a major armed conflict with a neighbouring state, Article 358 would automatically remove Article 19 protection across the country — including for civilians in areas entirely unaffected by the fighting. The geographic proviso in clause (1) mitigates but does not eliminate this concern, because a court asked to review whether a particular law or action was valid under Article 358 would still have to defer to the State's assessment that activities in the emergency zone threaten security nationwide. Second, the recital requirement, while meaningful, does not guarantee proportionality — a law that contains the required recital but goes far beyond what the security emergency demands could still shelter behind Article 358. Courts reviewing such a law could not apply Article 19's proportionality analysis, though other grounds of challenge including Article 14 remain available.

Third, the relationship between Article 358 and security legislation that achieves similar effects without an emergency proclamation — such as the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act, the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, and preventive detention laws — raises the question of whether the practical effect of Article 358 can be approximated through ordinary legislation without the constitutional safeguards the emergency framework provides. The Article 358 regime at least requires a war or external aggression ground, Cabinet resolution in writing, parliamentary approval by special majority, and statutory recitals — conditions that shadow security legislation need not satisfy. This asymmetry continues to inform constitutional debates about the proportionality of security legislation.

Finally, the Article 358 and Article 359 relationship leaves open the question of what happens during an armed rebellion emergency where an Article 359 order suspends enforcement of Article 19 rights but Article 358's automatic protection is unavailable. In that scenario, the legislature enacting Article 19-incompatible laws would need to rely on Article 359's clause (1A) mechanism — substantively similar to Article 358 but requiring prior presidential order and recital, creating a more deliberate and checked process. This structural design reflects the 44th Amendment's judgment that armed rebellion emergencies are less clearly justified as occasions for comprehensive civil liberties suspension than warfare against a foreign enemy.
