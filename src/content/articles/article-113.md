  113. Procedure in Parliament with respect to estimates.—(1) So much of the estimates as relates to expenditure charged upon the Consolidated Fund of India shall not be submitted to the vote of Parliament, but nothing in this clause shall be construed as preventing the discussion in either House of Parliament of any of those estimates.

  (2) So much of the said estimates as relates to other expenditure shall be submitted in the form of demands for grants to the House of the People, and the House of the People shall have power to assent, or to refuse to assent, to any demand, or to assent to any demand subject to a reduction of the amount specified therein.

  (3) No demand for a grant shall be made except on the recommendation of the President.

## Why it matters

Article 113 is where Parliament's financial sovereignty is most directly exercised — it gives Lok Sabha the power to assent to, reduce, or refuse each demand for expenditure, and reserves to the executive the exclusive right to initiate spending proposals. The distinction between charged expenditure (which is not voted on, preserving the independence of institutions like the judiciary and the Comptroller and Auditor General) and voted expenditure (subject to democratic scrutiny) is a structural choice that reflects both democratic accountability and institutional independence values.

The constitutional right to cut or refuse a demand has been the central mechanism of opposition strategy in budget politics. In coalition governments, threatened cut motions have been bargaining tools for extracting concessions. In majority governments, the guillotine that passes all outstanding demands in a single bloc vote at session's end means the constitutional power to scrutinise individual grants is almost never exercised in practice.

## Reading the provision

Article 113 operationalises the annual financial statement by setting out how Parliament actually handles budget estimates. It draws a critical distinction between charged expenditure (which Parliament can debate but not vote to reduce) and voted expenditure (which is submitted as "demands for grants" to Lok Sabha). Rajya Sabha plays no voting role in demands for grants; this is exclusively a Lok Sabha function, mirroring the Money Bill asymmetry. Clause (3) adds a further constraint: no demand for a grant can be made without the President's recommendation, ensuring that the executive retains the initiative over public spending proposals.

**Charged expenditure** includes the salary and allowances of the President, Judges of the Supreme Court and High Courts, the CAG, and debt servicing — all items that the Constitution's framers considered should not be made vulnerable to annual parliamentary whittling. These items appear in the estimates and are open to debate but not to a vote to reduce or refuse.

**Demands for grants** cover all other expenditure. Lok Sabha can assent, refuse, or assent subject to a reduction. A cut motion — either a token cut (signalling disapproval without changing the amount), a policy cut (cutting to a symbolic Rs 1 to censure a policy), or an economy cut (proposing a specific reduction) — is the formal mechanism through which opposition scrutiny of government expenditure operates.

## Key cases

**Ram Jawaya Kapur v. State of Punjab (1955)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of executive power and parliamentary financial control reinforced that Article 113 demands for grants are the constitutional mechanism through which Parliament authorises executive spending, and that expenditure without such authorisation violates the constitutional framework even if technically possible under another statutory head.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — The foundational basic structure judgment, while primarily about amendment power, confirmed that parliamentary financial control — including the demands-for-grants mechanism — is an aspect of the Constitution's essential structure that cannot be abrogated even by constitutional amendment.

**In re: Special Reference No. 1 of 2002 (Gujarat Assembly dissolution)** — The Supreme Court's examination of the relationship between the Governor's address, the financial statement, and demands for grants confirmed that the Article 112–113 sequence is a mandatory constitutional routine that the executive cannot bypass or defer indefinitely.

## Current significance

The guillotine of demands for grants — where Parliament approves months of government spending in a matter of minutes at the end of the budget session — is widely criticised as a structural failure of parliamentary financial oversight. With over a hundred demands often passed without debate in a single guillotined vote, Parliament's Article 113(2) power to refuse or reduce grants has become more theoretical than practical. Parliamentary committees on estimates and public accounts have repeatedly recommended reforms to give standing committees more time for pre-vote scrutiny of departmental demands, but the constitutional framework of Article 113 itself does not require such scrutiny — leaving the adequacy of financial oversight largely to parliamentary convention and committee practice rather than constitutional compulsion.
