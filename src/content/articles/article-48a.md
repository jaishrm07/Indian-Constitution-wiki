## Why it matters

Article 48A was inserted by the 42nd Constitutional Amendment Act, 1976, during the Emergency period, and it represents one of the most consequential additions ever made to Part IV. Its insertion reflected two developments: growing international recognition of environmental protection as a state responsibility following the 1972 Stockholm Conference on the Human Environment, and mounting evidence within India of the destruction caused by rapid industrialisation, deforestation, and the commercialisation of forest resources. The article directs the State to endeavour to protect and improve the environment and to safeguard the forests and wildlife of the country — a mandate that is simultaneously a policy directive, a constitutional value, and a substantive standard against which government action and inaction can be measured.

Together with Article 51A(g) — which imposes a corresponding duty on every citizen to protect and improve the natural environment, including forests, lakes, rivers, and wildlife — Article 48A forms the constitutional bedrock of India's environmental law framework. Parliament has enacted major legislation under this framework: the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act (1974), the Environment (Protection) Act (1986), the Forest (Conservation) Act (1980), the Wildlife (Protection) Act (1972), and the Biological Diversity Act (2002). None of these statutes could have the constitutional significance they do without Article 48A providing their constitutional anchor.

The deeper significance of Article 48A lies in what it says about intergenerational responsibility. The environment is not a resource to be depleted for the benefit of the present generation; it is a public trust that the State holds on behalf of all generations, present and future. Courts have used this understanding — drawing on the doctrine of public trust and on Article 48A's mandate — to limit private exploitation of natural resources, to restrain government licensing of environmentally destructive projects, and to recognise that the constitutional obligation to protect the environment is not discharged by enacting laws but requires active, ongoing enforcement.

The article's significance extends beyond litigation to the design of executive institutions. The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change; the Central Pollution Control Board and its State counterparts; the Wildlife Crime Control Bureau; and India's commitments under the Paris Agreement all find their constitutional grounding in Article 48A. The National Green Tribunal, established in 2010 under the National Green Tribunal Act, is structurally premised on the constitutional duty embodied in Article 48A — its mandate is to enforce the environmental protection obligation that the article imposes, and its jurisdiction extends precisely to the categories of environmental harm that the article's three limbs — environment, forests, wildlife — identify.

## Reading the provision

**The principal judicial technique.** Courts read Article 48A through the lens of Article 21. The Supreme Court has held repeatedly that the right to life under Article 21 includes the right to live in a clean and healthy environment, and Article 48A is the directive that confirms the constitutional basis for this expansion. Because the right to a healthy environment is enforceable as part of Article 21, individuals and communities can litigate violations even though Article 48A itself is not directly enforceable as a Fundamental Right. This Article 21 + Article 48A combination is now the standard judicial technique for environmental constitutional litigation in India.

**The three limbs of the article.** Article 48A contains three distinct but related mandates:

- The **environment** limb is the broadest and has been applied to air pollution, water pollution, soil contamination, noise pollution, coastal regulation, and climate change. It encompasses both natural and built environments and requires the State to take affirmative steps to improve degraded conditions, not merely maintain the status quo.
- The **forests** limb has been given an extraordinarily wide interpretation in the Godavarman litigation, where the Court defined "forest" to include all areas satisfying the dictionary definition regardless of legal classification, extending constitutional protection to millions of hectares not formally notified as Reserved or Protected Forests under the Indian Forest Act.
- The **wildlife** limb provides the constitutional basis for the Wildlife Protection Act's prohibition on hunting and trade in protected species, and for the designation and management of wildlife sanctuaries, national parks, and tiger reserves.

**Importing international environmental law principles.** Courts have incorporated international environmental law principles through Article 48A, reasoning that India's treaty commitments are consistent with and required by the constitutional directive. The following principles have been incorporated into Indian constitutional law through this route:

- The **precautionary principle**: when scientific uncertainty exists about whether an activity will cause environmental harm, the State must err on the side of prevention rather than wait for proof of damage;
- The **polluter-pays principle**: those who cause environmental pollution bear the costs of remediation, not the affected communities or the State;
- The doctrine of **sustainable development**: development that meets present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs;
- The principle of **intergenerational equity**: natural resources must be conserved for future generations, not exhausted for present economic gain;
- The **public trust doctrine**: natural resources such as rivers, forests, and seashores are held in trust by the government for present and future citizens, and cannot be permanently alienated to private parties.

**Continuing mandamus.** The Supreme Court and the National Green Tribunal have used Article 48A to develop a doctrine of continuing mandamus — issuing ongoing judicial directions that monitor compliance with environmental protection orders over years or decades. The Yamuna pollution litigation, the Ganga cleaning orders, the vehicular pollution directions in Delhi, and the Godavarman forest case are all examples where courts have issued directions and then monitored compliance over many years through follow-up hearings, court-appointed committees, and periodic reports from executive agencies.

## The M.C. Mehta petitions — the constitutional spine of environmental litigation

No discussion of Article 48A is complete without the M.C. Mehta litigation, which spans four decades and has produced more constitutional environmental law than any other set of cases in any jurisdiction. The key M.C. Mehta petitions and their contributions are:

- **Oleum Gas Leak (1987)**: Established the principle of absolute liability for hazardous industries — an enterprise carrying on a hazardous activity that causes harm is liable absolutely, without any exception. Grounded in Article 48A's constitutional condemnation of environmental harm.
- **Ganga Pollution (1988)**: Directed the closure of tanneries discharging effluents into the Ganga without treatment. Began the long-running Ganga cleaning litigation that has produced hundreds of orders over three decades.
- **Taj Trapezium (1997)**: Ordered the relocation of coal-burning industries around Agra to prevent acid-rain damage to the Taj Mahal. Read Article 48A together with Article 49's obligation to protect nationally important monuments.
- **Vehicular Pollution in Delhi (1998 onwards)**: Directed conversion of Delhi's bus fleet to CNG, phase-out of old diesel vehicles, and tightening of emission standards. Established that Article 48A can justify major economic costs on industry and government in service of environmental protection.
- **Kamal Nath (1997)**: Developed the public trust doctrine — held that the State cannot alienate public trust resources (rivers, forests, seashores) to private parties in a way that destroys the resource for the public.
- **Badkal and Surajkund Lakes (1997)**: Directed restrictions on construction and commercial activities around Delhi-area lakes to protect their ecological character, applying Article 48A's mandate to protect natural water bodies.
- **Brick Kilns in Delhi (1996)**: Directed closure of brick kilns operating within specified zones around Delhi, contributing to the broader scheme of pollution control that the Court developed through Article 48A and Article 21.

## The National Green Tribunal and Article 48A

The National Green Tribunal Act, 2010 created a specialised judicial body to address environmental disputes. The Tribunal's constitutional foundation rests substantially on Article 48A, which provides the constitutional mandate that Parliament is implementing when it creates specialised environmental adjudication. The NGT's jurisdiction covers:

- Disputes relating to enforcement of any legal right relating to environment;
- Questions of substantial environmental importance;
- Matters relating to Forest (Conservation) Act, Water Act, Air Act, Environment Protection Act, Public Liability Insurance Act, and Biological Diversity Act.

The NGT has used Article 48A's constitutional framework to develop its own jurisprudence on environmental standards, operating on the principle that the constitutional obligation to protect and improve the environment requires active judicial enforcement rather than merely passive resolution of disputes. The Tribunal has issued landmark orders on river cleaning, industrial pollution, solid waste management, and protection of wetlands and mangroves.

## Key cases

**Rural Litigation and Entitlement Kendra v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1985)** — Known as the Doon Valley case, this was the first instance of the Supreme Court directly enforcing an environmental protection directive against economic activity. The Court ordered the closure of limestone quarries in the Mussoorie hills that were causing severe deforestation and ecological damage to the Doon Valley watershed. Relying on Articles 48A and 51A(g), it held that the constitutional duty to protect the environment is not a mere aspiration but an obligation enforceable against the State and against private actors operating with State permission. The judgment established the template for environmental public interest litigation — a citizens' petition, reliance on constitutional directives, and Court-supervised closure of destructive activity.

**Vellore Citizens' Welfare Forum v. Union of India (1996)** — Tanneries in Tamil Nadu were discharging untreated effluents into the Palar river, causing severe contamination of drinking water sources and destruction of farmland. The Supreme Court, treating Articles 48A and 51A(g) as the constitutional foundation, explicitly incorporated the precautionary principle and the polluter-pays principle into Indian law, holding that these international norms are part of the environmental law of India. The Court directed the tanneries to pay compensation, ordered the creation of an environmental protection fund, and required the State to restore the damaged environment. The judgment is the authoritative source for the proposition that international environmental law principles have constitutional force in India through Article 48A.

**T.N. Godavarman Thirumulkpad v. Union of India (1997 onwards)** — The Godavarman forest case began as a challenge to timber felling in the Nilgiris but evolved into what is perhaps the most expansive exercise of judicial supervision of environmental governance in any democracy. The Supreme Court defined "forest" in its broadest dictionary sense, stopping timber operations across all forest land in India regardless of ownership, and has subsequently issued hundreds of directions on forest governance, wildlife corridors, compensatory afforestation, and the diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes. Article 48A is the constitutional foundation for this ongoing litigation, which treats the Court as a continuing authority with constitutional responsibility for ensuring that the State does not abdicate its duty to safeguard forests.

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India — Oleum Gas Leak (1987)** — Following a gas leak from the Shriram Food and Fertilisers plant in Delhi, the Supreme Court developed the principle of absolute liability for hazardous industries, holding that an enterprise engaged in a hazardous or inherently dangerous activity that causes harm is liable absolutely, without any exception or defence. The Court drew explicitly on Article 48A as evidence that the Constitution treats environmental harm from industrial activity as presumptively unacceptable, requiring a standard of accountability higher than traditional common law strict liability.

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India — Taj Trapezium (1997)** — The Supreme Court ordered the relocation or conversion of iron foundries, glass factories, and other coal-burning industries within the Taj Trapezium zone around Agra, on the ground that particulate emissions were causing irreversible chemical damage to the marble surface of the Taj Mahal. Relying on Article 48A and Article 49's obligation to protect nationally important monuments, the Court held that the State's constitutional duties required proactive industrial regulation rather than waiting for visible damage. The judgment is a key example of Article 48A operating in concert with other constitutional provisions to generate obligations that neither provision would generate alone.

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India — Vehicular Pollution in Delhi (1998 onwards)** — In a series of orders spanning several years, the Supreme Court directed the conversion of Delhi's public transport fleet to compressed natural gas, the phase-out of old diesel vehicles, and the tightening of emission standards, in response to Delhi's catastrophic air quality. The constitutional basis was the right to a healthy environment under Article 21 read with Article 48A. The Court held that the failure to control vehicular emissions violated the constitutional obligation to protect the environment and thereby the fundamental right to life. These orders established that Article 48A can justify the imposition of major economic costs on industry and government in the service of environmental and public health protection.

## Current significance

Article 48A is among the most litigated constitutional provisions of the twenty-first century. The National Green Tribunal processes thousands of environmental cases annually, and its entire institutional existence is premised on the constitutional duty embodied in Article 48A. Contemporary issues where the article has direct constitutional bearing include:

- Climate change mitigation and adaptation obligations of the State;
- Coastal regulation zone enforcement and protection of mangroves and wetlands;
- Protection of biodiversity hotspots from mining and infrastructure projects;
- Air quality standards in cities, including the continuing crisis of particulate pollution in the Indo-Gangetic plain;
- Riverbank encroachment and the restoration of urban waterbodies;
- Rights of forest-dwelling communities under the Forest Rights Act, 2006.

The article is at the centre of debates about the constitutionality of amendments to environmental governance processes that reduce public participation or fast-track industrial clearances. Courts examining such measures must weigh the government's development interest against the constitutional obligation under Article 48A — a tension structurally built into the Indian constitutional framework and showing no sign of resolution through legislative settlement rather than judicial supervision.

The Supreme Court in M.K. Ranjitsinh v. Union of India (2024) addressed the conflict between conservation obligations under Article 48A and renewable energy development objectives, holding that the constitutional duty to protect wildlife and forests must be balanced against — but cannot be overridden by — the government's clean energy transition programme. The judgment illustrates how Article 48A generates constitutional constraints even on programmes that are themselves environmentally motivated.

India's commitments under the Paris Agreement and its Nationally Determined Contributions to greenhouse gas reduction have generated discussion about whether Article 48A creates a constitutional obligation to take meaningful climate action. The Supreme Court, in M.K. Ranjitsinh and related cases, acknowledged that the right to be free from the adverse effects of climate change is part of the constitutional right to life under Article 21 read with Article 48A. Several pending environmental petitions have raised climate change as a dimension of this rights framework, arguing that government failure to regulate carbon-intensive industries or to meet climate commitments constitutes a violation of the constitutional right to live in a sustainable environment. The Supreme Court's engagement with these petitions will likely shape the constitutional law of climate for the coming decades, with Article 48A serving as the provision that bridges international climate obligations and domestic constitutional rights.

Article 48A also interacts importantly with Schedule VII's distribution of legislative competence. Environment as such is not a single List entry — legislative power over forests, mines and mineral development, wildlife, and pollution control is distributed across Union and Concurrent Lists. Article 48A provides a constitutional value that informs the interpretation of all these List entries, directing courts and Parliament to read them in a way that facilitates rather than obstructs environmental protection. The result is that even when the Union and States compete for legislative primacy on environmental subjects, Article 48A's constitutional mandate pushes toward the interpretation that better serves environmental protection — a form of constitutional environmental federalism that operates through interpretive principle rather than rigid allocation of legislative power.

Tensions between environmental protection under Article 48A and other constitutional values — particularly the right to development and to livelihood under Article 21, the rights of scheduled tribe communities under Articles 15 and 46, and the property rights framework — have generated an increasingly sophisticated body of balancing jurisprudence:

- **Article 48A vs. Article 21 livelihood**: The Court in Narmada Bachao Andolan v. Union of India (2000) held that displacement caused by the Sardar Sarovar Dam did not violate Article 21 or Article 48A when adequate rehabilitation was provided, acknowledging that development projects can proceed even when they cause environmental and social disruption, as long as mitigation measures are adequate.
- **Article 48A vs. tribal rights**: Forest-dwelling communities protected by the Forest Rights Act, 2006 have rights to use and cultivate forest land. Courts have held that Article 48A's environmental protection mandate and the rights of forest communities are not inherently in conflict — forest communities are often the most effective guardians of forest ecology — but tensions arise when conservation objectives require restricting community forest use.
- **Article 48A vs. infrastructure development**: The Court's approach to environmental clearances for highways, power projects, and urban development reflects an ongoing calibration between Article 48A's constitutional mandate and the government's legitimate development objectives, mediated through environmental impact assessment procedures and judicial oversight of those procedures.
