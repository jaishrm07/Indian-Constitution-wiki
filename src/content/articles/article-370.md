## Why it matters

Article 370 was the constitutional cornerstone of J&K's special status for nearly seven decades. It defined the terms on which the erstwhile princely state joined the Indian Union and embedded those terms in the Constitution itself. Its abrogation in 2019 and the Supreme Court's endorsement of that abrogation in 2023 raise fundamental questions about the scope of executive and legislative power under Article 370, the limits of constitutional interpretation, and whether federalism protections for a constituent unit can be dissolved without that unit's consent. The controversy sits at the intersection of federalism, basic structure doctrine, and the constitutional law of accession.

## Reading the provision

Article 370 as originally enacted operated on a layered basis. Clause (1)(a) disapplied Article 238 (which governed Part B States) to J&K. Clause (1)(b) confined Parliament's legislative power over J&K to the subjects in the Instrument of Accession and such further subjects as the State government concurred in. Clause (1)(d) was the mechanism by which other constitutional provisions were extended to J&K: the President could make orders applying any provision of the Constitution "subject to such exceptions and modifications," but orders relating to matters beyond the Instrument of Accession required the State government's concurrence, and if given before the J&K Constituent Assembly was convened, had to be placed before that Assembly.

Clause (3) gave the President power to declare Article 370 inoperative entirely, but only on the recommendation of the J&K Constituent Assembly. When that Assembly dissolved in 1956 having adopted the J&K Constitution, it did not recommend abrogation. The standard constitutional view thereafter was that the recommendation condition had become impossible to fulfil, making Article 370 functionally permanent despite the word "temporary" in its heading.

## The 2019 abrogation

The Union government's 2019 approach moved in two steps. First, Constitutional Order 272 (5 August 2019), issued under Article 370(1)(d), applied all provisions of the Constitution to J&K and simultaneously amended Article 367 (the interpretation article) in its application to J&K to provide that references to "Constituent Assembly of the State" in Article 370(3) should be read as references to the "Legislative Assembly of the State." Since J&K was under President's Rule, the Governor exercised the State government's powers — meaning Union executive concurrence effectively stood in for State concurrence. Parliament then, sitting as the State Legislature under President's Rule, passed a resolution recommending that Article 370 cease to operate. The President issued that notification on 6 August 2019.

Simultaneously, Parliament passed the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019 under Article 3, bifurcating the State into two Union Territories: Jammu and Kashmir (with a legislature) and Ladakh (without). The critics of the abrogation raised four main arguments: that Article 367 cannot be used to amend Article 370 since interpretation provisions cannot substitute for the Article 368 amendment procedure; that the Governor under President's Rule is not the "Government of the State" for purposes of concurrence under Article 370; that Parliament acting as the State Legislature cannot simultaneously act as Parliament to pass the resolution; and that downgrading a State to Union Territory requires the State Legislature's views under Article 3, which could not meaningfully be obtained given the President's Rule situation.

## Key cases

**In re: Article 370 (Supreme Court, December 2023)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the abrogation. The Court held that Article 370 was a temporary provision that did not acquire permanence simply because the J&K Constituent Assembly dissolved without recommending its removal. The President's power under Article 370(3) could be exercised without a fresh Constituent Assembly recommendation once the original Assembly had concluded its work. On the Union Territory question, the Court held that J&K's statehood would be restored, directed elections to the J&K Assembly, and noted the Centre's undertaking on restoration of statehood. However, the Court did not conclusively resolve whether Parliament acting under President's Rule could validly provide the State concurrence.

**Sampat Prakash v State of Jammu and Kashmir (1969)** — The Supreme Court upheld the continued validity of Presidential Orders extending the Constitution to J&K, rejecting the argument that Article 370 had lapsed once the J&K Constituent Assembly dissolved. The Court characterised the provision as still operative, a position that was ultimately relied upon in 2023 to support the government's power to act under it.

**State Bank of India v Santosh Gupta (2016)** — The Supreme Court considered whether the SARFAESI Act (a central law on secured creditors) validly applied to J&K given that it had not been extended by a Presidential Order under Article 370(1)(d). The Court confirmed that central legislation applied to J&K only if specifically extended under the Article 370 framework, illustrating the practical scope of the special constitutional arrangement before 2019.

## Current significance

J&K holds elections to its Legislative Assembly in September–October 2024 and now has an elected government as a Union Territory. Ladakh remains a Union Territory without a legislature, with demands from the Ladakhi political community for Sixth Schedule protections and statehood. The question of restoring full statehood to J&K remains a live constitutional and political commitment. The entire body of Indian legislation now applies directly in J&K without the need for separate Presidential Orders, changing the legal landscape for property rights, business regulation, and fundamental rights enforcement in the former State. China's continued refusal to recognise the 2019 reorganisation, especially as it relates to areas it claims in Ladakh, gives the abrogation an ongoing external dimension. The 2023 Supreme Court judgment, while settling the immediate constitutional challenge, left important questions about the limits of executive power under President's Rule and the relationship between Articles 367, 368, and 370 open for future litigation.
