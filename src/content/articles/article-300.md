## Why it matters

Article 300 is the constitutional foundation for the entire law of government liability in India — the provision that determines whether citizens can sue the state in contract and tort, in what courts, and for what categories of wrong. Every dispute about government negligence in building roads, every claim for compensation when government vehicles injure pedestrians, every suit for breach of a government contract, and every writ seeking compensation for police brutality ultimately traces its constitutional roots to Article 300.

The article's design reflects a deliberate transitional choice. Rather than starting from scratch with a new doctrine of government liability suited to an independent republic, the framers anchored the scope of suability to the pre-constitutional practice of the Dominion of India and the Provinces. This had two advantages: it prevented a gap in litigation rights that might have arisen if the Constitution had created government liability from zero without specification, and it preserved whatever protection the inherited framework provided while leaving Parliament and State legislatures free to modify and improve it. The disadvantage — one that persists — is that the inherited Crown-era baseline carried with it the doctrine of Crown immunity in respect of sovereign functions, a colonial relic that proved extraordinarily difficult to dislodge from Indian government liability law.

The Supreme Court's progressive dismantling of Crown immunity under Article 300 — starting with State of Rajasthan v Vidhyawati's rejection of broad immunity arguments, continuing through Kasturilal's reinstatement of a limited sovereign function doctrine, and culminating in N Nagendra Rao's characterisation of the sovereign/non-sovereign distinction as an anachronism — reflects the inherent tension in Article 300's design. A provision that anchors government liability to a colonial baseline in a democratic republic will inevitably require judicial renovation as the republic matures. That renovation is still incomplete, and the absence of comprehensive government liability legislation leaves courts with inadequate tools for the task.

## Reading the provision

Sub-clause (1) establishes the formal legal personality of government entities for litigation purposes. The Union Government sues and is sued as "Union of India" — not as "Government of India" or "President of India" — and each State sues and is sued by its state name. This formal naming rule is more than a procedural convenience: it defines the juridical persona of the State for the purposes of the law of obligations. When a citizen sues "the State of Maharashtra" for negligence, they are invoking Article 300's conferral of legal personality on that entity, which cannot be a party to litigation without such conferral.

The key phrase "in the like cases as the Dominion of India and the corresponding Provinces or the corresponding Indian States might have sued or been sued" is Article 300's substantive content. It establishes the pre-constitutional practice as the starting baseline for suability — the Union and States are suable in the same circumstances as their predecessors, no more and no less, unless Parliament or a State legislature has modified the position by legislation. This reference to the Dominion and Province baseline imports the entire body of law on Crown liability that had developed in India under the Government of India Act 1858, the Government of India Act 1935, and the colonial common law inherited from English practice.

Under that inherited baseline, the Crown (and its successor governments) was liable in contract in the same way as private persons, but in tort only for acts performed in a "non-sovereign" or commercial-administrative capacity. Acts performed in a "sovereign" capacity — including the exercise of statutory powers involving the use of force, the imposition of criminal sanctions, the exercise of executive prerogative, and the administration of military affairs — were immune from tort liability. This sovereign/non-sovereign distinction, developed in English law and applied in colonial India, became the central doctrinal problem of Indian government liability law after the Constitution.

"Subject to any provisions which may be made by Act of Parliament or of the Legislature of such State" is the modification clause. Parliament and State legislatures may by legislation expand or contract the scope of government suability. Parliament could enact a comprehensive Torts Claims Act, making the government fully liable for negligence without any sovereign immunity defence — comparable to the Federal Tort Claims Act in the United States. It has not done so. Some States have enacted limited legislation modifying their suability in specific contexts. The absence of comprehensive central legislation is the primary reason the inherited Crown immunity doctrine continues to operate.

Sub-clause (2) is purely transitional. It preserves the continuity of pending litigation by automatically substituting the new constitutional entities — Union of India, State governments — for the Dominion and Provinces that were parties to proceedings at the Constitution's commencement. Without this provision, all pending suits against the colonial government would have had to be refiled naming the new parties, with potential limitation period consequences. The provision was practically important in 1950 when hundreds of cases against the Dominion government and Provincial governments were pending.

## Sovereign immunity in Indian constitutional law

The doctrine of Crown immunity — that the Crown is not liable for torts committed in the exercise of sovereign functions — was well established in English common law by the time of Indian independence. The classic English case, Tobin v The Queen (1864), established that the Crown could not be sued in tort at all without consent; the Crown Proceedings Act 1947 subsequently watered this down significantly for England, making the Crown liable in tort in the same way as a private person. India did not enact equivalent legislation, leaving the inherited baseline in place.

The critical question under Article 300 is whether the inherited baseline included the sovereign immunity doctrine and whether that doctrine survived the transition from Crown to Republic. Kasturilal Ralia Ram Firm v State of UP (1965) answered yes on both counts — the immunity for sovereign functions was part of the pre-constitutional baseline and was preserved by Article 300's reference to pre-constitutional suability. The result was a doctrine that exempted the state from liability for torts committed in the exercise of statutory powers involving compulsion, force, and the core functions of governance.

N Nagendra Rao v State of AP (1994) substantially revised this position. A Constitution Bench (five judges) held that the distinction between sovereign and non-sovereign functions was a relic of feudal and colonial thinking incompatible with the nature of a modern democratic state. The welfare state permeates every aspect of citizens' lives — health, education, infrastructure, law enforcement — and the State cannot be allowed to escape liability for negligent exercise of these pervasive powers by labelling them "sovereign." The Court announced a much narrowed scope for sovereign immunity: functions that are peculiarly governmental — the making of laws, the levying of taxes, the maintenance of military forces — might retain some residual immunity, but the vast range of welfare state activities would not.

The N Nagendra Rao position has not been consistently applied. Subsequent cases have reached different conclusions about which functions are "sovereign," and the lack of comprehensive legislation has left the doctrine uncertain. Cases involving police negligence, prison conditions, and public health infrastructure continue to involve contested sovereign immunity arguments, with outcomes varying across High Courts and between different benches of the Supreme Court.

## Constitutional compensation through writ jurisdiction

The most important development in government liability doctrine since 1994 has come not through Article 300 at all, but through the parallel track of constitutional compensation under Article 32 and Article 226. In Nilabati Behera v State of Orissa (1993) 2 SCC 746, the Supreme Court held that a writ court exercising jurisdiction under Article 32 or 226 can award monetary compensation to a victim of fundamental rights violation by the state — without needing to establish a cause of action in tort or to defeat a sovereign immunity defence. The constitutional remedy in public law operates on different principles from the private law remedy in tort: the state's liability arises from its constitutional duty to protect fundamental rights, not from the law of negligence.

The Nilabati Behera remedy has been elaborated in cases involving custodial deaths, encounter killings, and other police violence. The Court has held that the State bears strict liability for the death or injury of persons in its custody — no sovereign function defence is available, because the constitutional obligation to protect the life and liberty of persons in state custody is non-delegable and cannot be shielded by the immunity that Article 300's inherited baseline imports. This constitutional compensation track has effectively created a two-tier liability regime: for ordinary government negligence (road accidents, contract breaches, non-custodial torts), Article 300's inherited tort law applies with its contested sovereign immunity defence; for fundamental rights violations by the state (custodial violence, encounter killings, arbitrary detention), Nilabati Behera constitutional compensation operates without that defence.

## The Constituent Assembly and the baseline choice

The framers' decision to anchor government suability to the pre-constitutional baseline rather than defining it afresh was a practical choice driven by time pressure and the desire to maintain legal continuity at a moment of constitutional transition. Discussions in the Constituent Assembly show awareness of the Crown immunity problem — members noted that the English Crown Proceedings Act 1947 had just reformed Crown liability in Britain and that India would eventually need to do the same. But the immediate priority was to ensure that the Constitution's commencement did not create a gap in existing legal rights and pending litigation. Article 300's reference to the Dominion baseline was the simplest way to achieve this while leaving reform to Parliament.

The irony is that Parliament's failure to legislate a comprehensive government liability framework has left courts working with an eighteenth-century doctrine — Crown immunity — in a twenty-first-century welfare state. The reform that the framers anticipated would come from Parliament has instead come incrementally from the judiciary, through a series of decisions that have progressively narrowed the immunity without entirely abolishing it.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v Vidhyawati AIR 1962 SC 933** — The Supreme Court held that the Union and State Governments are vicariously liable for the tortious acts of their servants in the same way as a private employer, rejecting broad Crown immunity arguments in the context of a government vehicle accident. The Court drew on Article 300's reference to pre-constitutional suability and then reasoned that the transformation from colonial Crown to a democratic republic founded on popular sovereignty removed the philosophical justification for maintaining the immunity intact. This was the first significant post-Constitution step toward government liability in tort.

**Kasturilal Ralia Ram Firm v State of UP AIR 1965 SC 1039** — The Court upheld a sovereign immunity defence in the context of lost property seized by police — holding that policing is a sovereign function and that torts committed in its exercise are immune from liability. The judgment distinguished between acts in a commercial or non-sovereign capacity (liable) and acts in a sovereign or governmental capacity (immune). Though repeatedly criticised and gradually eroded, Kasturilal remained a leading authority for three decades and continues to be invoked by State governments claiming immunity from tort liability.

**Nilabati Behera v State of Orissa (1993) 2 SCC 746** — The landmark Supreme Court decision creating the constitutional compensation remedy for fundamental rights violations by the state. A young man died in police custody. The Court held that the state bears strict liability for custodial deaths and directed compensation without requiring the petitioner to overcome the sovereign immunity defence. The remedy was grounded in the state's constitutional obligation under Article 21 rather than in tort law, bypassing Article 300's inherited immunity framework. This decision has been the basis for constitutional compensation awards in hundreds of custodial violence, encounter killing, and arbitrary detention cases.

**N Nagendra Rao v State of AP (1994) 6 SCC 205** — A Constitution Bench substantially restricted the sovereign immunity doctrine, characterising the sovereign/non-sovereign function distinction as an anachronism inconsistent with the welfare state. The Court held that the State cannot claim immunity for torts committed in the exercise of duties that affect the lives and livelihoods of citizens — immunity should be limited to functions that are peculiarly governmental in the narrowest sense. The decision was a significant step in aligning Article 300's inherited baseline with constitutional values, though subsequent case law has not consistently followed its approach.

**Chairman, Railway Board v Chandrima Das (2000) 2 SCC 465** — The Supreme Court held that the Railways, as a commercial enterprise of the state, could not claim sovereign immunity for the rape of a Bangladeshi national by railway employees on railway premises, awarding constitutional compensation. The ruling confirmed that the state's constitutional obligations under Article 21 extend to foreign nationals and that the Nilabati Behera remedy is available against commercial as well as purely governmental state enterprises.

**State of AP v Challa Ramkrishna Reddy (2000) 5 SCC 712** — The Court held that the state was liable for the death of an undertrial prisoner due to prison authorities' negligence, rejecting the sovereign immunity argument. The state's duty to protect persons in its custody is non-delegable and absolute — sovereign immunity has no application to custodial deaths.

## Current significance

Article 300's practical importance is felt in every case of government contractual breach and government tortious liability — a vast field encompassing construction contract disputes, road accident compensation claims against state transport undertakings, land acquisition compensation defaults, police and custodial violence claims, hospital negligence by government hospitals, and public health infrastructure failures. The question of whether a particular State action constitutes a "sovereign function" immune from suit continues to be litigated, with different outcomes in different High Courts reflecting the unsettled state of the doctrine after Kasturilal and N Nagendra Rao.

Parliament's failure to enact a comprehensive government liability statute remains the central structural problem in this area of law. In the absence of such legislation, courts are forced to develop government liability doctrine incrementally, case by case, under a constitutional framework that was designed only to preserve continuity — not to create a comprehensive liability regime. The Law Commission of India has repeatedly recommended enactment of a Government Liability Act, but Parliament has not acted. This legislative gap leaves citizens who suffer negligent harm from government activity dependent on a judge-made doctrine of uncertain scope and inconsistent application.

The interaction between Article 300's tort liability framework and the constitutional compensation track established by Nilabati Behera raises an important question of remedial choice. A victim of police negligence who has suffered harm short of fundamental rights violation must establish tortious liability under Article 300's rules, confronting possible sovereign immunity arguments. A victim of custodial violence who can establish a fundamental rights violation can seek constitutional compensation directly through writ jurisdiction. The boundary between these two tracks — and the question of whether constitutional compensation is available for negligence that falls short of deliberate fundamental rights violation — continues to generate significant litigation.

The intersection of Article 300 with the digital state is an emerging area. Government digital services, data breaches involving government-held personal information, and algorithmic administrative decisions that cause financial or personal harm to citizens raise questions about whether the state's liability in digital tort can be claimed under Article 300 or must instead be pursued through the constitutional compensation route if a fundamental right is engaged. As the Indian state increasingly operates through digital platforms and automated systems, Article 300's inherited liability framework will need adaptation to address the new categories of government-caused harm.
