officialText: |-
  190. Vacation of seats.—(1) No person shall be a member of both Houses of the Legislature of a State and provision shall be made by the Legislature of the State by law for the vacation by a person who is chosen a member of both Houses of his seat in one house or the other.

  (2) No person shall be a member of the Legislatures of two or more States specified in the First Schedule and if a person is chosen a member of the Legislatures of two or more such States, then, at the expiration of such period as may be specified in rules1 made by the President, that person's seat in the Legislatures of all such States shall become vacant, unless he has previously resigned his seat in the Legislatures of all but one of the States.

  (3) If a member of a House of the Legislature of a State—

  (a) becomes subject to any of the disqualifications mentioned in [clause (1) or clause (2) of article 191]; or [(b) resigns his seat by writing under his hand addressed to the speaker or the Chairman, as the case may be, and his resignation is accepted by the Speaker or the Chairman, as the case may be,] his seat shall thereupon become vacant:

  [Provided that in the case of any resignation referred to in sub-clause (b), if from information received or otherwise and after making such inquiry as he thinks fit, the Speaker or the Chairman, as the case may be, is satisfied that such resignation is not voluntary or genuine, he shall not accept such resignation.]

  (4) If for a period of sixty days a member of a House of the Legislature of a State is without permission of the House absent from all meetings thereof, the House may declare his seat vacant:

  Provided that in computing the said period of sixty days no account shall be taken of any period during which the House is prorogued or is adjourned for more than four consecutive days.

## Why it matters

Article 190 is the mechanism through which the constitutional conditions for legislative membership are enforced in practice. It converts abstract disqualifications and breaches of membership conditions into the concrete legal consequence of a vacant seat, triggering a by-election and the loss of the member's political position. The most consequential clause in recent State politics has been clause (3)(b) — resignation — and its proviso empowering the presiding officer to refuse a resignation not found to be voluntary or genuine. This proviso, inserted by constitutional amendment, was designed precisely for situations in which a ruling party uses pressure, inducement, or intimidation to manufacture resignations from opposition or rebel members, and gives the Speaker or Chairman a quasi-judicial gate-keeping role that courts have been called upon to review.

Article 190 also connects the seat-forfeiture mechanism to the anti-defection scheme of the Tenth Schedule. When a member is disqualified under the Tenth Schedule by the Speaker, the disqualification order under Article 191(2) triggers the seat becoming vacant under Article 190(3)(a). The two provisions operate together: Article 191 determines whether the disqualification ground is made out; Article 190 determines the consequence. This interplay means that a challenge to a Tenth Schedule disqualification order is simultaneously a challenge to the seat vacation that follows under Article 190.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) prevents double membership of both Houses in a bicameral State Legislature.

Where a person is elected to both Houses, the State Legislature must by law provide for which seat he vacates; until he vacates one, the situation of double membership persists as an anomaly the Constitution tolerates briefly but requires the legislature to resolve.

Clause (2) prevents simultaneous membership of the Legislatures of two or more States — if a person is elected to multiple State Legislatures, all seats fall vacant at the end of a period fixed by Presidential rules unless he has resigned from all but one.

Clause (3)(a) automatically vacates the seat when a member becomes subject to a disqualification under Article 191(1) or (2), making disqualification and seat-forfeiture a single event rather than two separate proceedings.

Clause (3)(b) requires that a resignation be accepted by the Speaker or Chairman. Under the inserted proviso, acceptance is withheld if the presiding officer is satisfied after inquiry that the resignation is not voluntary or genuine — a judgment call that has produced extensive litigation in recent political crises. The Speaker must record reasons for acceptance or rejection, and those reasons are subject to judicial review.

Clause (4) provides for vacation through prolonged absence: sixty days of absence from all meetings of the House without permission. This period is computed excluding prorogation intervals and adjournments of more than four consecutive days — so genuinely short sessions between long recesses do not unfairly trigger the sixty-day count.

## Key cases

**Subhash Desai v Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court's extensive ruling on the Maharashtra political crisis examined the circumstances of mass resignation by Shiv Sena MLAs, the Speaker's powers under Article 190(3)(b), and the interaction with Tenth Schedule disqualification petitions. The Court held that resignations must be independently verified for voluntariness and that the Speaker cannot mechanically accept mass resignations filed in the context of an ongoing political crisis without examining whether they are genuinely voluntary.

**Rajendra Singh Rana v Swami Prasad Maurya (2007)** — The Supreme Court considered the relationship between incurring disqualification under the Tenth Schedule and the vacation of seat under Article 190(3)(a). The Court held that once a member has incurred disqualification under the Tenth Schedule, the seat falls vacant immediately — the Speaker's order is declaratory, not constitutive, of the vacation — and the Speaker cannot indefinitely defer deciding the disqualification petition to allow the member to continue sitting and voting.

**Election Commission of India v Subramanian Swamy (1996)** — The Court drew a clear procedural distinction between disqualifications adjudicated by the Election Commission under Article 192 (office of profit and related Article 191(1) grounds) and those decided by the Speaker under the Tenth Schedule. Both trigger seat vacation under Article 190(3)(a), but the pathways and appellate routes differ, and conflating the two regimes is constitutionally erroneous.

## Current significance

The voluntariness-of-resignation inquiry under the Article 190(3)(b) proviso has become one of the most heavily litigated issues in State Legislature law. In Rajasthan (2020), Karnataka (2019), Manipur, and Maharashtra (2022), members of rebel or breakaway groups either resigned or were alleged to have been coerced into resigning, and Speakers were faced with contested quasi-judicial determinations about the genuineness of those resignations. The Supreme Court has repeatedly directed Speakers to decide such matters expeditiously and on the basis of evidence, but there is no mandatory time limit on the Speaker's inquiry. The interaction of Article 190 with the Tenth Schedule creates a further complication: a member who has resigned but also faces a defection petition may argue that his resignation pre-empts the disqualification, while the party seeking disqualification argues the reverse. Courts have not definitively resolved this sequencing question across all factual configurations, making Article 190 a persistent source of constitutional uncertainty in State Legislature membership law.
