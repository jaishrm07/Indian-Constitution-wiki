## Why it matters

Article 327 is the constitutional power source for India's entire body of election law. Without it — or with only the fragmented State-by-State powers that existed before the Constitution — India's elections to national and State legislatures would be governed by a patchwork of State laws, creating opportunities for State governments to manipulate procedures in their favour, establishing inconsistent voter eligibility standards, and fragmenting the administrative machinery for what is constitutionally a national exercise in democratic self-governance. By vesting comprehensive legislative authority over elections in Parliament, Article 327 ensures that the basic framework for electoral democracy is uniform, centrally legislated, and subject to parliamentary scrutiny rather than being left to the legislatures whose own composition is at stake.

The breadth of Article 327 is deliberate. The phrase "all matters relating to, or in connection with, elections" is expansive and has been consistently construed to reach virtually every aspect of the electoral process — voter registration, qualification and disqualification of voters, the size and shape of constituencies, delimitation, the nomination process, the conduct of campaigns, voting procedures, counting methods, the declaration of results, post-election petitions, and the administrative infrastructure of returning officers and election observers. Parliament has exercised this power primarily through the Representation of the People Act 1950 (which deals with electoral rolls and delimitation) and the Representation of the People Act 1951 (which deals with the actual conduct of elections and election petitions). These two Acts, read together with the constitutional provisions of Part XV, constitute the principal statutory framework for Indian elections.

The constraint — "subject to the provisions of this Constitution" — is equally important. Article 327 does not give Parliament a free hand to design elections any way it chooses. The constitutional provisions that limit its exercise include the fundamental rights in Part III, the specific provisions of Part XV itself, and the basic structure doctrine. The interplay between Parliament's Article 327 power and the Election Commission's Article 324 superintendence is a major field of constitutional litigation and has produced the principle that Parliament can occupy the field through legislation, but the Commission retains residual constitutional power to fill gaps where Parliament has not spoken and where the requirements of free and fair elections demand action.

## Reading the provision

**"Subject to the provisions of this Constitution":** This is the foundational limit on the entire power. Parliament cannot use Article 327 to override Article 324 (Election Commission independence), Article 325 (single-roll guarantee), or Article 326 (universal adult franchise). It cannot violate the fundamental rights in Part III — election legislation that discriminates on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth is ultra vires even if Parliament claims the legislative power under Article 327. And it cannot violate the basic structure — the Indira Gandhi ruling confirmed that retrospective validation of a specific election result through ordinary Article 327 legislation amounts to an exercise of judicial power by Parliament and is unconstitutional.

**"From time to time":** The phrase confirms that Parliament's election legislation power is iterative. Parliament may enact, amend, and replace election legislation as democratic needs evolve — it need not enact a single comprehensive and permanent code. This has allowed Parliament to respond to electoral developments through targeted amendments to the Representation of the People Acts rather than comprehensive re-legislation. It also means that election law can be updated with each Parliament, reflecting the understanding that the mechanics of democracy must be capable of evolution.

**"All matters relating to, or in connection with, elections":** This is the widest formulation the provision could have taken. Courts have confirmed that the phrase is not confined to the mechanics of voting and counting; it extends to the preparatory and consequential processes. The preparation of electoral rolls (a pre-election matter), the delimitation of constituencies (a structural matter), the disqualification of candidates (a qualification matter), and the grounds for election petitions (a post-election matter) all fall within this phrase. The Supreme Court's consistent approach has been to interpret Article 327 liberally in favour of parliamentary competence, checking its exercise only against the constitutional limits expressly identified.

**"Including the preparation of electoral rolls, the delimitation of constituencies":** The specific mention of electoral rolls and delimitation is illustrative, not exhaustive. These are the two most structurally important elements of electoral administration — who votes and in what constituency they vote — and their specific mention confirms that Parliament's power over these matters is constitutionally grounded, not merely implied. Parliament's creation of Delimitation Commissions through the Delimitation Act is the principal exercise of this specific power.

**"And all other matters necessary for securing the due constitution of such House or Houses":** This residual catch-all ensures that nothing functionally essential to a properly constituted legislature falls outside Parliament's reach. "Due constitution" means properly constituted in accordance with the Constitution and applicable law — elected in a manner that is free, fair, and in accordance with the democratic principles the Constitution presupposes. Parliament may use Article 327 to legislate on any matter functionally necessary to achieve a properly constituted legislative House, even if that matter is not explicitly mentioned.

**Article 327 and the Seventh Schedule:** The provision notably overrides the ordinary legislative competence scheme of the Seventh Schedule for its specific subject matter. Election law cuts across the constitutional divide between Union and State matters — Article 327 grants Parliament the specific power to legislate on elections to both Parliament and State Houses. A State cannot invoke its List II powers to enact election law that conflicts with Parliament's Article 327 legislation. This is why Parliament can regulate the conduct of elections to State Assemblies — a matter that would otherwise fall squarely within State legislative competence under the general distribution of powers.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's decision to vest primary election law-making power in Parliament rather than distributing it to States reflected a clear lesson from pre-independence experience. Provincial governments with control over election administration had demonstrated a tendency to manipulate procedures for political advantage. The framers were also influenced by the federal models of other constitutional democracies — even in federations with strong State powers, election administration for the national legislature typically remained under central authority. Ambedkar's arguments for a constitutionally independent Election Commission were complemented by the decision to keep the legislative framework for elections in parliamentary hands, creating a two-tier guarantee of independence — an independent administrative body (Article 324) and a Parliament-made statutory framework (Article 327) rather than a system dependent on State Legislatures.

The debate also considered whether the Constitution should set out election law principles in detail or leave them to Parliament. The framers chose the latter, believing that the flexibility to adapt election law through ordinary parliamentary legislation was more valuable than constitutional rigidity, provided the foundational guarantees (single roll, adult franchise, Election Commission independence) were constitutionally anchored.

## Key cases

**Kuldip Nayar v Union of India (2006) 7 SCC 1** — A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld Parliament's power under Article 327 to amend the system of Rajya Sabha elections from secret ballot to open ballot, introduced to prevent cross-voting. The Court held that the breadth of "all matters relating to, or in connection with, elections" is sufficient to cover changes to voting procedure provided they do not offend other constitutional provisions. The judgment established that Article 327's power extends to procedural redesign of the electoral process and is not confined to administrative regulation of the mechanics of voting.

**Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain (1975) 2 SCC 159** — The Constitution Bench, while deciding the foundational basic structure case, also considered whether Parliament could retrospectively validate electoral irregularities through ordinary legislation under Article 327. The Court held that clause 4 of the Thirty-ninth Amendment, which retrospectively validated the 1971 election result then under challenge in an election petition, amounted to an exercise of judicial power by Parliament — beyond the scope of Article 327 and offensive to the separation of powers. The judgment established that Article 327 cannot be used to interfere with or validate specific sub-judice elections or to immunise a sitting legislator's election from judicial scrutiny.

**People's Union for Civil Liberties v Union of India (2003) 4 SCC 399** — The Supreme Court upheld the Election Commission's order directing candidates to disclose criminal antecedents, assets, and educational qualifications, even in the absence of specific parliamentary legislation under Article 327 requiring such disclosure. The Court held that voters' right to know — grounded in Article 19(1)(a) — is an essential component of free and fair elections and that Parliament's failure to legislate under Article 327 does not extinguish the right; the Election Commission's Article 324 power fills the gap. The case defined the relationship between the Article 327 legislative power and the Commission's residual constitutional authority.

**Mohinder Singh Gill v Chief Election Commissioner (1978) 1 SCC 405** — Although primarily an Article 324 case, the Court's analysis of the boundary between Parliament's Article 327 legislative powers and the Election Commission's Article 324 superintendence remains the governing framework. Parliament can occupy the field through Article 327 legislation, but the Commission retains residual power to fill gaps where Parliament has not legislated. Where Parliament has spoken through Article 327 legislation, the Commission must work within that framework; where Parliament is silent, the Commission's constitutional power fills the vacuum.

**Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India (2002) 5 SCC 294** — The Supreme Court ordered disclosure of criminal records, assets, and educational qualifications by election candidates, finding that voters' right to know is an essential component of free and fair elections that Parliament is obligated to secure under Article 327. When Parliament failed to legislate the required disclosures, the Court sustained its order through the Election Commission's Article 324 power. The case is the authority for the proposition that voters' constitutional right to information about candidates must be secured through the Article 327 legislative framework, and that courts may direct the Commission to fill gaps Parliament has left.

**Lily Thomas v Union of India (2013) 7 SCC 653** — The Court struck down section 8(4) of the Representation of the People Act 1951, which allowed convicted MPs and MLAs to retain their seats during the appeal of their conviction. The Court held that Parliament had exceeded its Article 327 competence by enacting a provision that effectively modified — through statutory means — the disqualification scheme that the Constitution itself mandates under Articles 102 and 191. The judgment confirmed that Article 327 is subject not only to fundamental rights but also to the Constitution's own structural provisions, including the disqualification architecture for members of Parliament and State Legislatures.

**Anoop Baranwal v Union of India (2023) 6 SCC 161** — The five-judge Constitution Bench's direction on Election Commissioner appointments observed that Parliament's failure to exercise its Article 327 (and Article 324(2)) legislative power to establish an independent appointment mechanism had created a constitutional vacuum that the Court was required to fill. The judgment is significant for Article 327 because it establishes that the failure to legislate on a matter within Parliament's Article 327 competence when that failure undermines constitutional values can be addressed by the courts through directions to fill the gap.

## Current significance

Article 327 is directly invoked whenever Parliament enacts or amends election-related legislation. The most active current debate is the "One Nation One Election" proposal — simultaneous elections to Parliament and all State Assemblies — which would require comprehensive Article 327 legislation restructuring the election cycle, aligning the terms of all elected Houses, and establishing a mechanism for dealing with midterm dissolution. The Ramnath Kovind Committee's 2024 report concluded that Article 327 provides sufficient legislative power for certain aspects of this reform, though constitutional amendments to Articles 83, 85, 172, and 174 would also be needed. The proposal remains among the most significant potential exercises of Article 327 power since the Constitution came into force.

Campaign finance regulation — specifically the replacement of the Electoral Bonds Scheme struck down by the Supreme Court in 2024 as violative of Article 19(1)(a) — is a pressing Article 327 question. Any replacement mechanism for political funding will constitute "matters relating to elections" within Article 327's scope and will need to satisfy the constitutional requirement of transparency derived from voters' right to information. The constitutional tension between disclosure requirements and claims of donor privacy falls squarely within the Article 327 legislative field that Parliament must now occupy.

Delimitation — the redrawing of constituency boundaries following each decennial census — is an Article 327 matter that has been frozen since the 1976 Constitution (Forty-second Amendment) froze constituency numbers until 2001, then extended until 2026. The delimitation exercise that will follow the 2021 census is among the most consequential exercises of Article 327 power in the Constitution's history. Southern States, which have had lower population growth rates due to more successful family planning programmes, face significant loss of parliamentary representation relative to northern States. The interaction between Article 327 delimitation power and Articles 81, 82, 170, and 171 will define the political geography of Indian democracy for the next twenty-five years.
