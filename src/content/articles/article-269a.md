  [269A. Levy and collection of goods and services tax in course of inter-State trade or commerce.— (1) Goods and services tax on supplies in the course of inter-State trade or commerce shall be levied and collected by the Government of India and such tax shall be apportioned between the Union and the States in the manner as may be provided by Parliament by law on the recommendations of the Goods and Services Tax Council.

  (2) The amount apportioned to a State under clause (1) shall not form part of the Consolidated Fund of India.

  (3) Where an amount collected as tax levied under clause (1) has been used for payment of the tax levied by a State under article 246A, such amount shall not form part of the Consolidated Fund of India.

## What this Article does

Article 269A is the constitutional cornerstone of the Integrated Goods and Services Tax (IGST) system. It was inserted by the Constitution (One Hundred and First Amendment) Act 2016 as part of the package of constitutional changes needed to give the GST reform a firm textual foundation in Part XII.

Under the pre-GST regime, the Constitution did not permit the Union to levy a tax on the sale of goods — that was a State subject under Entry 54 of the State List. Conversely, States could not tax services, and the Union could not levy tax on intra-State trade. The pre-existing Central Sales Tax on inter-State sales was a Union levy, but its revenue went entirely to the originating State, creating incentives for tax cascading and State-level trade barriers.

Article 269A resolves this by creating a single constitutional slot for inter-State supplies. The Union levies and collects IGST on all inter-State supplies (clause 1). Imports are treated as inter-State supplies by the Explanation, ensuring that the IGST framework captures international trade without a separate constitutional provision. Crucially, the proceeds are apportioned between the Union and the States — not on the Finance Commission formula but on a mechanism prescribed by Parliament on the recommendation of the GST Council under Article 279A.

Clauses (2), (3), and (4) address the accounting mechanics of the input tax credit chain. When a taxpayer in State A pays SGST to State A and uses that credit to discharge IGST, the SGST-funded portion of IGST does not become part of the Consolidated Fund of India — it notionally remains in State A's account. The symmetrical clause (4) operates when IGST credit is used to discharge State GST. This prevents the constitutional funds framework (Articles 266 and 283) from becoming a barrier to the seamless cross-credit mechanism that is the operational heart of the GST system.

Clause (5) empowers Parliament to determine the "place of supply" — the rule that assigns an inter-State transaction to a particular State and thereby determines which State receives the IGST share. The Integrated Goods and Services Tax Act 2017 implements this power.

## How courts use it

Article 269A has been tested in litigation about the constitutionality of the GST regime and the place-of-supply rules. Courts have been asked whether the Union's power to levy and collect IGST under Article 269A imposes a corresponding duty to apportion proceeds promptly to States, or whether the Union can hold IGST proceeds in the Consolidated Fund of India pending settlement.

The High Courts of Kerala, Gujarat, and Madhya Pradesh considered challenges to specific aspects of IGST apportionment and the treatment of transitional credit under the GST legislation, with petitioners arguing that the Union was retaining IGST proceeds in violation of Article 269A(2). The Supreme Court has also addressed the competence of Parliament to prescribe place-of-supply rules under Article 269A(5) and whether such rules displace State taxes on cross-border digital services.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Mohit Minerals Pvt. Ltd. (2022)** — The Supreme Court's landmark ruling on the GST Council's powers under Article 279A also engaged with Article 269A, confirming that the Council's recommendations on IGST apportionment are not merely advisory in the political sense but are the constitutional mechanism that gates Parliament's legislative power to prescribe the distribution formula. The Court read Article 269A together with Articles 246A and 279A to understand the cooperative federal design of the GST architecture.

**Filco Trade Centre v. Union of India (2022)** — The Supreme Court directed the reopening of the GST transitional credit window, in proceedings that involved examining the interplay between Article 269A's IGST framework and the transitional provisions of the CGST Act, confirming that constitutional protections for cross-credit flows could not be extinguished by executive notification alone.

**Suo motu proceedings on GST compensation to States (2021-22)** — Several State governments invoked the constitutional framework of Article 269A in arguments before the Supreme Court about the Union's obligation to make GST compensation payments — arising from the GST (Compensation to States) Act 2017 — even when IGST and CGST collections fell short of projections. While the Court did not resolve these as Article 269A questions, the proceedings clarified the constitutional architecture of inter-State GST revenue flows.

## Current significance

Article 269A sits at the epicentre of India's most significant fiscal-federalism reform since independence. The GST compensation regime — under which the Union guaranteed States a 14 percent annual growth in GST revenues for five years — expired in June 2022, generating political controversy about whether the Union had fully discharged its obligations under the IGST settlement framework. After the compensation period ended, several States reported that IGST settlement delays were creating short-term cash flow problems, raising questions about the operational adequacy of the Article 269A apportionment mechanism.

The place-of-supply rules for digital and cross-border services — made under Article 269A(5) — are increasingly contested as the digital economy grows: determining which State "receives" an online service or a data-driven transaction requires increasingly complex rules, and Article 269A(5)'s mandate to Parliament to make such rules is being tested by the pace of economic change. The Sixteenth Finance Commission has been asked to examine whether IGST settlement mechanisms should be reviewed to prevent temporary Union retention of State shares from distorting State cash flows.
