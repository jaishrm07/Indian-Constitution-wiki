## What this Article does

Article 367 is the Constitution's general interpretation provision. Its three operative clauses each address a distinct interpretive problem. Clause (1) imports the General Clauses Act 1897 — India's default statutory interpretation statute — to supply rules of construction for the Constitution itself, subject to adaptations under Article 372. Clause (2) extends references to Acts of Parliament or State Legislatures to cover Ordinances issued by the President or a Governor. Clause (3) defines "foreign State" for constitutional purposes and gives the President power to declare a State non-foreign for specified purposes. Clause (4), which related to an interpretation of "Scheduled Castes", was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) is the most consequential. The General Clauses Act 1897 is a short statute of about twenty provisions that supplies interpretive defaults for legislative text: masculine pronouns include feminine; singular includes plural; "shall" is mandatory and "may" is permissive; definitions given in an earlier section govern later sections unless context otherwise requires; repeal of an amending Act does not revive the repealed provisions; and offences against a repealed law may still be prosecuted for acts done during the law's operation. By importing the General Clauses Act into constitutional interpretation, Article 367(1) means that unless the constitutional text or context says otherwise, all of these defaults apply when reading the Constitution's own provisions.

The interaction between Article 367(1) and Article 366 is important. Article 366 provides a comprehensive set of definitions that govern the entire constitutional text. Article 367(1) then provides the General Clauses Act's general rules as a residual toolkit for everything not addressed by Article 366 or any specific constitutional provision. Together the two articles create a layered interpretive framework: Article 366 specific definitions govern first; Article 367 imports General Clauses Act defaults for everything else; and the constitutional context can override both.

Clause (2) has significant practical effect. Many constitutional provisions refer to "laws made by Parliament" or "laws made by the Legislature of a State". Without clause (2), one could argue that Presidential or Gubernatorial Ordinances — which are executive instruments rather than Acts of a legislature in the formal sense — do not count as "law" for the purposes of those provisions. Clause (2) forecloses that argument by directing that all references to Acts of Parliament or State Legislatures include references to Ordinances of the President or the Governor.

The most dramatic use of Article 367 in constitutional history came in August 2019 when the Central Government issued the Constitution (Application to Jammu and Kashmir) Order 2019 (Constitution Order 272). The Order purported to modify Article 367 as it applied to Jammu and Kashmir by adding a new clause 4, which provided that references in Article 370 to the "Constituent Assembly of Jammu and Kashmir" were to be read as references to the "Legislative Assembly of Jammu and Kashmir". Since Jammu and Kashmir's Legislative Assembly had been suspended under President's Rule, its functions were being performed by the President acting on the advice of the Union Council of Ministers. The effect of Constitution Order 272 was therefore to route the constitutional concurrence requirement of Article 370(3) — which required the recommendation of the Constituent Assembly before the President could abrogate Article 370 — through the Union executive itself. On the same day, a resolution was passed under Article 370(3) on this basis, and Article 370 was effectively rendered inoperative. This Article 367 mechanism — using an interpretation clause to achieve what critics said was a substantive amendment — was one of the central legal issues before the Supreme Court in the Article 370 reference.

## Key cases

**D S Nakara v Union of India (1983) 1 SCC 305** — The Supreme Court drew on Article 367's import of the General Clauses Act, specifically the principle that "person" includes a group of persons and that singular includes plural, in construing the scope of Articles 14 and 16 as applied to pension rules. The case illustrates how the General Clauses Act defaults imported by Article 367 operate as a background interpretive toolkit in cases that appear to be purely about substantive constitutional rights rather than constitutional interpretation methodology.

**Kuldip Nayar v Union of India (2006) 7 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court's examination of the constitutional validity of the system of open balloting for Rajya Sabha elections required interpretation of "elected" and "election" as they appear in the constitutional text. Article 367's import of the General Clauses Act's section 3 definitions of standard terms was relevant background to the Court's analysis of whether the constitutional framework for elections required a particular form of ballot secrecy. The case shows how Article 367 supplies definitional and interpretive defaults for constitutional terms that are not defined in Article 366.

**Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973) 4 SCC 225** — Multiple judges in the thirteen-judge Bench engaged with how the General Clauses Act, imported by Article 367, bears on the meaning of "amendment" in Article 368. The General Clauses Act's section 6A, dealing with amendment and repeal, was cited in the course of arguments about whether constitutional amendments authorised by Article 368 could be treated as equivalent to legislative repeals with saved rights. The broader interpretive methodology employed by the Court — purposive reading of the Constitution — was informed by Article 367's framework even where the General Clauses Act defaults were being adjusted to fit the constitutional context.

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024)** — The seven-judge Bench's analysis of the scope of Article 341 engaged implicitly with Article 367's interpretive framework in construing the words "include" and "exclude" in Article 341(2) and in reading Article 341 alongside Article 16(4). The Court drew on the General Clauses Act's rule that a power to do something includes a power to do it in part, as part of the interpretive toolkit supplied by Article 367(1).

**In re Article 370 of the Constitution (Supreme Court of India, 2023)** — The five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously upheld the abrogation of Article 370 but engaged extensively and critically with the Article 367 mechanism used in Constitution Order 272. The Court characterised the use of Article 367 to modify Article 370's terms as a substantive modification of the Constitution, but held it was within the President's power under Article 370(1)(d) during the period of President's Rule. The judgment is the leading authority on the limits of using an interpretation clause to achieve substantive constitutional change, and raises the question — which the Court did not fully resolve — of whether Article 367 can serve as a vehicle for bypassing the special-majority and ratification requirements of Article 368.

## Relationship with Article 366

Article 366 and Article 367 together form the Constitution's interpretive infrastructure. Article 366 provides a glossary of defined terms used throughout the constitutional text — covering "agricultural income", "Anglo-Indian", "Consolidated Fund", "Scheduled Castes", "Scheduled Tribes", "State", and many others. These definitions are operative wherever the defined term appears in the Constitution unless the particular article specifies a different meaning. Article 367 then provides the residual interpretive toolkit — the rules for reading constitutional language that is not governed by any specific Article 366 definition or context-specific provision. The relationship is complementary: Article 366 answers the question "what do specific constitutional terms mean?", while Article 367 answers the question "how should constitutional language generally be read?". Together they ensure that the Constitution is not interpreted in a vacuum but against an established set of definitions and interpretive defaults. Both articles are located in Part XIX (Miscellaneous), reflecting their character as general housekeeping provisions applicable across the entire constitutional text rather than to any particular subject matter.

## Article 367 and the General Clauses Act toolkit

The General Clauses Act 1897 imported by Article 367(1) provides a set of interpretive rules that are of constant but largely invisible relevance to constitutional construction. Among the most significant for constitutional purposes are: section 3 (definitions of standard terms such as "person", "public officer", "State Government", and "writing"); section 6 (effect of repeals — the repeal of a provision does not revive an earlier repealed law, and existing rights and obligations under the repealed law survive); section 8 (construction of subordinate legislation after amendment of the parent power); section 13 (gender and number — masculine includes feminine, singular includes plural); and section 16 (where a power is given to do an act, all powers necessary to do that act are implied). Section 16 has been particularly significant in constitutional interpretation: it supports the inference that where the Constitution gives Parliament or the executive a power, ancillary powers reasonably necessary to effectuate that power are implied. The General Clauses Act's section 21 (power to issue orders or directions includes power to rescind or modify them) is similarly significant when reading constitutional powers to issue notifications or orders. These default rules operate silently in the background of constitutional construction unless the specific context of a provision displaces them.

## Current significance

Article 367's continuing importance lies primarily in clause (1), which makes the General Clauses Act the default interpretive toolkit for every provision of the Constitution. The 2019 use of Article 367 to modify Article 370 illustrated that this quiet interpretation article sits at the centre of potentially transformative constitutional events. The Supreme Court's 2023 ruling in the Article 370 case — upholding the abrogation while acknowledging the interpretive modification was of substantive character — leaves open the broader question of the limits of executive use of Article 367 to reshape constitutional meaning. That question has implications for any constitutional provision whose operative meaning could be altered by redefining a term through a Presidential Order under Article 367, bypassing the special majority requirements of Article 368. The case thus elevates Article 367 from a technical housekeeping provision to a site of first-order constitutional controversy about the limits of executive power over constitutional interpretation.
