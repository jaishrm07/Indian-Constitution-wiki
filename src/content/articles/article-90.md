plainEnglish: >-
  Article 90 completes the constitutional framework for the Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha by specifying how the office becomes vacant — automatically when the holder ceases to be a member, voluntarily by written resignation to the Chairman, or forcibly by a resolution passed by an absolute majority of all the Rajya Sabha's then members on fourteen days' notice. The absolute-majority requirement makes removal practically difficult, functioning more as a deterrent than a regularly exercised accountability tool. The fourteen-day notice period protects the Deputy Chairman from surprise political manoeuvres. officialText: |-
  90. Vacation and resignation of, and removal from, the office of Deputy Chairman.—A member holding office as Deputy Chairman of the Council of States—

  (a) shall vacate his office if he ceases to be a member of the Council;

  (b) may at any time, by writing under his hand addressed to the Chairman, resign his office; and

  (c) may be removed from his office by a resolution of the Council passed by a majority of all the then members of the Council:

  Provided that no resolution for the purpose of clause (c) shall be moved unless at least fourteen days' notice has been given of the intention to move the resolution.

## Why it matters

Article 90 provides the accountability mechanism for the Deputy Chairman — the only Rajya Sabha presiding officer whose tenure is entirely within the Council's own control. Unlike the Chairman (the Vice-President), who can be removed only through the joint process under Article 67(b), the Deputy Chairman can be removed by the Rajya Sabha itself on an absolute majority. This means the Deputy Chairman, while insulated against casual political moves by the notice requirement and the absolute-majority threshold, is ultimately answerable to the chamber. The article also has doctrinal significance because of the Nabam Rebia line of cases, which used its structure to develop a broader constitutional principle about when presiding officers facing removal must suspend discretionary powers.

The design of Article 90 reflects the Constitution's theory of presiding-officer accountability: it should be possible to remove an officer who has lost the confidence of the chamber, but removal should not be easy, tactical, or sudden. The fourteen-day notice requirement and the absolute-majority threshold together ensure that removal requires genuine and sustained political will across the House, not a temporary majority assembled for an expedient purpose. This balance — accountability without instability — is the same principle that governs the parallel provisions for the Speaker under Article 94.

The interaction between Article 90 and the anti-defection framework under the Tenth Schedule has become one of the most practically important aspects of Indian constitutional law. The Deputy Chairman exercises the same disqualification powers as the Chairman when acting in that capacity, and those powers are inherently sensitive when the political balance of the House is in flux. The Nabam Rebia doctrine's extension of Article 90's logic to constrain the Deputy Chairman's discretionary powers during removal proceedings protects the integrity of both the removal process and the disqualification process.

## Reading the provision

The three modes of vacation reflect different constitutional circumstances. Automatic vacation under clause (a) ensures the office cannot be held by someone who has lost the democratic mandate of a Rajya Sabha seat — the Deputy Chairman must always be a current member. This is a condition that cannot be waived: a person who is no longer a Rajya Sabha member cannot continue as Deputy Chairman even temporarily. The office falls vacant automatically and without any further constitutional formality.

Voluntary resignation under clause (b) is addressed to the Chairman rather than to the House, reflecting the formal hierarchy of presiding officers. The resignation takes effect when it is written and addressed, without requiring any further acceptance or House approval. The Chairman receives the resignation as the constitutional head of the Rajya Sabha's presiding structure, and the vacancy arises on the communication of the written notice. In the Chairman's absence, the question of to whom resignation should be addressed has been managed by convention and rules of procedure.

Removal under clause (c) requires an absolute majority — not a bare majority of members present and voting but a majority of all then members of the Council. This is a deliberately high threshold. If the Rajya Sabha has 245 members, removal requires at least 123 votes, regardless of how many members are actually present and voting. A government that controls, say, 130 seats in the Rajya Sabha can by itself remove the Deputy Chairman; a divided opposition would need near-total unity. The absolute-majority requirement thus aligns the removal threshold with the threshold required for significant legislative acts.

The fourteen-day notice requirement serves two functions. It gives the Deputy Chairman time to prepare a defence and address the House before the vote. It also prevents the removal mechanism from being used as an emergency political weapon in fast-moving parliamentary situations. A political crisis that erupts on a Monday cannot be resolved by an immediate removal vote — the House must wait at least two weeks, by which time the crisis may have been resolved by other means. This cooling-off period is a structural protection for the Deputy Chairman's independence.

## Parallel with the Speaker's removal under Article 94

Article 90's structure mirrors Article 94, which governs the vacation, resignation, and removal of the Speaker and Deputy Speaker of the Lok Sabha. Both articles require an absolute majority for removal and both impose a fourteen-day notice requirement. This symmetry reflects the Constitution's design of a coherent presiding-officer accountability framework across both Houses. The difference lies in the identity of the primary presiding officer: the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha is the Vice-President (who faces a different removal mechanism under Article 67), whereas the Speaker of the Lok Sabha is elected by and removable by the Lok Sabha alone under Article 94.

In the Lok Sabha, removal of both the Speaker and the Deputy Speaker follows the Article 94 procedure. In the Rajya Sabha, the Chairman (Vice-President) is effectively shielded from Rajya Sabha-only removal because Article 67(b) requires a Rajya Sabha resolution plus Lok Sabha concurrence, while the Deputy Chairman faces the easier Article 90 absolute-majority mechanism. This asymmetry means the Deputy Chairman is the only Rajya Sabha presiding officer over whose tenure the Rajya Sabha has sole constitutional authority.

## The Nabam Rebia extension

The Supreme Court's 2016 judgment in Nabam Rebia used the constitutional logic of Articles 90 and 94 as the basis for a broader principle. The Court reasoned that a presiding officer against whom a proper removal notice has been admitted cannot exercise discretionary, quasi-judicial powers — specifically, the power to disqualify members under the Tenth Schedule — that could affect the composition of the House and thereby the outcome of the removal vote. The principle is rooted in the natural-justice maxim nemo judex in causa sua and the structural logic of the accountability mechanism: a presiding officer who can manipulate the membership of the House that is voting on their removal can in effect protect themselves from accountability.

The practical effect of the Nabam Rebia doctrine is that once a removal notice satisfying Article 90's requirements is admitted, the Deputy Chairman's powers are constrained in real time, even before the fourteen-day notice period expires and the formal removal vote occurs. This has made the submission of removal notices a political instrument in its own right, used not always to actually remove a presiding officer but to paralyse the exercise of discretionary powers at a critical moment. The doctrine has been criticised as an overextension of Article 90's literal text but has been consistently applied by courts and is now a settled feature of Indian constitutional law.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The five-judge Constitution Bench held that a presiding officer against whom a notice of removal has been admitted cannot exercise the power to disqualify members under the Tenth Schedule until the removal notice is resolved. The principle was developed from the constitutional logic of Articles 90 and 94 — both of which structure the removal mechanism for presiding officers. The Court reasoned that allowing a presiding officer to reshape the House's membership while facing removal would undermine the constitutional accountability mechanism the articles provide.

**Kihoto Hollohan v. Zachillhu (1992)** — The constitutional bench that upheld the Tenth Schedule examined the immunities and powers of presiding officers, clarifying that the tenure and removal provisions in articles like Article 90 must be read alongside anti-defection powers, generating a coherent framework: the presiding officer's powers are extensive but the officer remains accountable to the House through removal. The bench's reasoning that Article 90's accountability mechanism justifies the constitutionality of presiding-officer adjudication powers remains the foundation of the Tenth Schedule's legitimacy.

**Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court's extended engagement with Speaker disqualification jurisdiction and its suspension during removal proceedings directly applied Nabam Rebia's interpretation of Articles 90/94 logic, confirming that the doctrine operates whenever a valid removal notice is before the House, regardless of whether the notice is likely to succeed.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Court's comprehensive analysis of presiding officer powers confirmed that the constitutional succession and accountability provisions, including removal under Article 90, create a framework of checked authority — presiding officers have extensive powers but those powers are balanced by the accountability mechanism, and courts may review whether the removal procedure was followed correctly even if they cannot review substantive presiding decisions.

## Current significance

Article 90 has direct relevance in periods of hung or coalition-driven parliamentary arithmetic. The Deputy Chairman's removal threshold — absolute majority of all members — makes removal genuinely difficult even for a determined opposition, but not impossible if substantial cross-party support is assembled. The Nabam Rebia doctrine has added a tactical dimension — a removal notice, once admitted, limits what the Deputy Chairman can do even before the removal vote occurs. This has made the submission of removal notices a political instrument in its own right, used not always to actually remove a presiding officer but to constrain the exercise of quasi-judicial powers on pending anti-defection and related matters.

The Rajya Sabha's prolonged failure to elect a Deputy Chairman in recent Parliaments means the Article 90 removal framework has been dormant by reason of vacancy rather than by reason of stability, leaving the Council's succession chain under Article 91 exposed to the risk of a Presidential appointment if the Chairman also becomes unavailable. This vacancy has itself become a constitutional controversy, with opposition parties arguing that the government is constitutionally obliged to support election of a Deputy Chairman under Article 89(2)'s "as soon as may be" directive.

The intersection of Article 90 with the Tenth Schedule's anti-defection provisions is the most live constitutional question in the article's contemporary operation. As floor-crossing crises and government-formation disputes proliferate at both the central and state levels, the question of whether a Deputy Chairman facing a removal notice can adjudicate pending disqualification petitions — and if not, who can adjudicate in their place — has become an urgent issue that Article 90's text alone cannot answer. The Nabam Rebia doctrine's answer is that the power is suspended, but the substitute mechanism is still being worked out through subsequent litigation and political convention.
