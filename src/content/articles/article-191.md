## Why it matters

Article 191 is the membership-integrity provision for State Legislatures. It ensures that those who sit in a State Legislative Assembly or Council satisfy minimum standards of legal capacity, national loyalty, financial standing, and freedom from conflicting governmental obligations. The article has two distinct halves: the four enumerated substantive disqualifications in clause (1), adjudicated through the Governor-and-Election-Commission process under Article 192; and the Tenth Schedule anti-defection disqualification in clause (2), adjudicated by the Speaker or Chairman. The design of two separate adjudication regimes for two distinct types of disqualification reflects the Constitution's recognition that partisan defection requires a legislative-house-internal remedy, while legal incapacity and loyalty questions require an arm's-length electoral body.

## Reading the provision

The office-of-profit disqualification in clause (1)(a) is the most litigated of the five substantive grounds.

A person holding an "office of profit under the Government of India or the Government of any State" is disqualified unless the State Legislature has by law declared that office not to be disqualifying. The Explanation expressly exempts Ministers — Union or State — from being treated as holders of offices of profit, recognising that Ministers are both executive office-holders and legislators by constitutional design. The multi-factor test for "office of profit" asks whether the office carries remuneration or financial benefit from government, whether appointment and removal are controlled by government, and whether the functions performed are governmental in nature.

Clauses (b) and (c) reflect basic requirements of legal capacity (no person of unsound mind or undischarged insolvent may sit as a legislator). Clause (d) requires undivided national allegiance: citizenship of another State or acknowledgment of allegiance to a foreign State disqualifies a person from holding legislative office.

Clause (1)(e) — disqualification "by or under any law made by Parliament" — is the gateway through which the Representation of the People Act, 1951 operates. Conviction for specified offences, corrupt practices at elections, and other grounds in that Act flow into Article 191(1)(e) as constitutional disqualifications, adjudicated through the Governor-and-Election-Commission process under Article 192.

Clause (2), inserted by the Fifty-Second Amendment in 1985, brought Tenth Schedule defection disqualifications within the Article 191 framework, making defection a constitutional-level disqualification rather than a mere party-political matter adjudicated outside the membership framework.

## Key cases

**Jaya Bachchan v Union of India (2006)** — The Supreme Court interpreted the office-of-profit test under Article 191(1)(a) (in the context of the parallel Rajya Sabha provision, Article 102) and held that an office carries "profit" even if the holder does not in fact draw a salary, so long as the office is capable of yielding remuneration. The judgment restated the multi-factor test for identifying a disqualifying office of profit and has been consistently applied to State Legislature membership disputes under Article 191(1)(a).

**Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil v Hon'ble Speaker, Karnataka Legislative Assembly (2019)** — The Supreme Court upheld disqualification orders against rebel MLA members under the Tenth Schedule (Article 191(2)) while also recording — though not deciding — the larger constitutional issue of whether it was appropriate for the Speaker, an elected party member, to decide disqualification petitions involving his own party. The Court reiterated the need for reform of the adjudication mechanism.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v Hon'ble Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — The Court went further than Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil, strongly recommending that Parliament amend the Constitution to vest Tenth Schedule (Article 191(2)) adjudication in an independent body such as a former Chief Justice, and directed expeditious decision-making pending such reform. The judgment highlighted the structural conflict of interest inherent in Speaker-led anti-defection adjudication.

**Election Commission of India v Subramanian Swamy (1996)** — The Court confirmed that the two disqualification regimes under Article 191 — clause (1) governed by Article 192 and clause (2) governed by the Tenth Schedule — operate independently. A challenge under one regime does not subsume or displace the other, and conflating them produces erroneous results both procedurally and substantively.

## The two adjudication tracks

Article 191's most practically important structural feature is the division of adjudicatory authority between two entirely separate institutional tracks. Disqualification under clause (1) — the four substantive grounds plus parliamentary statute — is decided through the Governor-and-Election-Commission process under Article 192. Disqualification under clause (2) — the Tenth Schedule — is decided by the Speaker or Chairman. These tracks cannot be substituted for each other: a disqualification question arising under Article 191(1)(e) by virtue of criminal conviction under the Representation of the People Act cannot be routed through the Speaker's Tenth Schedule process, and a defection-based disqualification under Article 191(2) cannot be referred to the Election Commission under Article 192. High Courts have struck down disqualification proceedings that mistakenly applied the wrong track, reinforcing the constitutional rigidity of the dual-regime design.

## Current significance

Article 191(1)(a) — office of profit — continues to generate frequent High Court litigation as State Legislatures designate statutory board chairmanships, advisory committee memberships, and other government-attached posts for their members. The exemption lists vary by State and are often outdated, leading to challenges against sitting members on the ground that they hold newly created posts not yet included in the State's exemption legislation. Article 191(2) and the Tenth Schedule together remain the primary arena for constitutional disputes about legislative membership, with the Speaker's conflict-of-interest problem identified in Keisham Meghachandra Singh yet to be addressed by legislative reform. The broader framework of Article 191 reflects the Constitution's underlying premise that legislative chambers must be composed of members who are legally competent, financially solvent, and free from divided governmental loyalties — a premise that is enforced through different institutional routes depending on which disqualification ground is engaged.
