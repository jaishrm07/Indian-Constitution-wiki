## What this Article did

Article 373 is a transitional bridging provision that operated for the first year of the Constitution's life. Article 22 of the Constitution governs preventive detention, and clauses (4) and (7) of that article require Parliament to make laws specifying the maximum period of preventive detention and constituting advisory boards for reviewing such detentions. Until Parliament enacted that legislation — or until one year from commencement, whichever came first — Article 373 substituted the President for Parliament in those clauses. This meant the President could issue orders providing the relevant protections during the transitional gap.

The Preventive Detention Act, 1950 was enacted by Parliament within the first year of the Constitution, so Article 373's operation was short-lived in practice. Once Parliament legislated under Article 22(7), the substitution mechanism became unnecessary and the article was spent.

## Historical context

The provision reflects the framers' concern that no gap exist in the preventive detention framework between the Constitution's commencement on 26 January 1950 and Parliament's first legislative session under the new order. Given the security context of 1950 — partition-related unrest, communist insurgencies, and significant border tensions — an operative framework for preventive detention was considered essential from day one.

Article 22(4) requires that a detained person be produced before an advisory board within five weeks of detention, and Article 22(7) requires Parliament to prescribe the maximum period for preventive detention and the composition and procedure of advisory boards. Before Parliament acted, Article 373 filled this gap by empowering the President to make orders performing those functions. This ensured that persons detained immediately after 26 January 1950 had the procedural protections of Article 22 even before Parliamentary legislation was in place.

The Preventive Detention Act, 1950 — one of the first major acts of the new Parliament — was enacted rapidly and provided for advisory boards and maximum detention periods. With its enactment, the trigger under Article 373 ("until provision is made by Parliament") was satisfied, and the article ceased to operate.

## Key cases

**A K Gopalan v State of Madras AIR 1950 SC 27** — The Supreme Court's first major judgment on preventive detention under the new Constitution was decided in 1950, in the same year that Article 373 was operative. The Court upheld the Preventive Detention Act, 1950 and interpreted the scope of Article 22 broadly, treating it as a self-contained code for preventive detention. While the judgment did not specifically address Article 373, the context of transitional presidential authority over detention that Article 373 created was directly relevant to the operative framework the Court was interpreting.

No case appears to have specifically tested the validity or scope of a presidential order made under Article 373 itself. Given the very short window of operation and the rapid enactment of the Preventive Detention Act, 1950, Article 373 likely never generated independent litigation.

## Current significance

Article 373 has been a spent provision since at least early 1950, when Parliament enacted the Preventive Detention Act. It has no continuing operational effect. Its historical significance lies in illustrating the care taken by the Constitution's framers to ensure that the transition from the Government of India Act, 1935 framework to the new constitutional order was seamless even in sensitive areas like preventive detention. The provision also reveals the Constituent Assembly's understanding that Parliament might not immediately pass legislation required by the Constitution's new framework, and that executive authority would need to fill those gaps temporarily. It is of primary interest to constitutional historians studying Part XXI's transitional provisions and to scholars examining the early preventive detention jurisprudence in India.

## Why it matters

The structure of Article 373 illuminates the constitutional relationship between executive and parliamentary authority during a transitional period. The Constituent Assembly was willing to vest in the President the power to make orders on subjects — advisory boards, maximum detention periods — that the Constitution expressly assigned to Parliament, but only for the minimum time necessary until Parliament could act. The provision reflects a deep preference for parliamentary legislation over executive orders on rights-affecting matters, while recognising the practical reality that Parliament could not legislate instantaneously on every subject from the moment of constitutional commencement.

The article is one of several in Part XXI that used the formula "until provision is made by Parliament ... or until the expiration of [a specified period], whichever is earlier" — a drafting technique that tied the duration of the transitional provision to the earliest of two triggering events. This approach reflected the framers' preference for provisions that would expire as soon as they were no longer needed, rather than remaining permanently on the books. The pattern of rapid legislative action by the first Parliament in 1950 — including the Preventive Detention Act — meant that most such provisions were spent within months rather than the full transitional periods allotted.
