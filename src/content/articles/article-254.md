  254. Inconsistency between laws made by Parliament and laws made by the Legislatures of States.—(1) If any provision of a law made by the Legislature of a State is repugnant to any provision of a law made by Parliament which Parliament is competent to enact, or to any provision of an existing law with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent List, then, subject to the provisions of clause (2), the law made by Parliament, whether passed before or after the law made by the Legislature of the State, or, as the case may be, the existing law, shall prevail and the law made by the Legislature of the State shall, to the extent of the repugnancy, be void.

  (2) Where a law made by the Legislature of a State with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent List contains any provision repugnant to the provisions of an earlier law made by Parliament or an existing law with respect to that matter, then, the law so made by the Legislature of such State shall, if it has been reserved for the consideration of the President and has received his assent, prevail in that State:

## Why it matters

Article 254 is the single most important provision governing the relationship between Central and State legislation in India's day-to-day constitutional practice. It supplies the rule of precedence — repugnancy — when Parliament and a State Legislature have both legislated on the same subject from the Concurrent List (List III of the Seventh Schedule). Because the Concurrent List spans over fifty subjects including criminal law, criminal procedure, civil procedure, marriage and divorce, insolvency, education, labour, forests, economic and social planning, price control, and legal and medical professions, collisions between Central and State legislation on these subjects are not occasional constitutional events but structural features of Indian governance. Article 254 is therefore the daily operating rule of Indian federalism in its most contested territory.

The provision does far more than simply give Parliament the last word. Clause (2) builds in a State-protective valve: a State law on a Concurrent List subject that has been reserved for and received Presidential assent can prevail over an earlier Central law within that State. This means States are not purely subordinate on Concurrent List subjects — they have a constitutional mechanism to achieve local legislative priority, subject only to Parliament's always-available power to legislate again and override. The Presidential assent route under Article 254(2) has been used by States to enact innovative social policy legislation — rent control laws, agricultural tenancy laws, anti-conversion laws, and various social sector laws — that departs from or supplements the Central framework.

Understanding the repugnancy doctrine is indispensable for constitutional practice. It governs the validity of huge swaths of State legislation and Central legislation simultaneously. Any lawyer advising on the validity of a State law, any policy-maker designing State-level regulatory frameworks, and any judge adjudicating commercial, labour, land, or criminal disputes involving dual statutory regimes must be fluent in Article 254's mechanics. The cases decided under Article 254 — examining when an "occupied field" precludes State law, when Presidential assent saves a State law, and what "repugnancy" actually means — form the backbone of Indian constitutional law on legislative competence.

The provision also illuminates a fundamental design choice the framers made: that the Concurrent List is not simply an area of shared legislative competence where both levels of government legislate independently. It is an area of hierarchically structured dual legislation, where Central law is supreme unless the specific conditions of Article 254(2) are met. The Concurrent List allows States to experiment and adapt — but only within the ceiling Parliament sets, and only with the safety valve of Presidential assent when States wish to go beyond that ceiling. Article 254 is therefore the mechanism that makes the Concurrent List work as a zone of controlled, calibrated State legislative innovation rather than unconstrained State autonomy.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the default repugnancy rule:**

"If any provision of a law made by the Legislature of a State is repugnant to any provision of a law made by Parliament which Parliament is competent to enact, or to any provision of an existing law with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent List, then, subject to the provisions of clause (2), the law made by Parliament, whether passed before or after the law made by the Legislature of such State, or, as the case may be, the existing law, shall prevail and the law made by the Legislature of the State shall, to the extent of the repugnancy, be void."

Several elements of clause (1) require precise analysis:

*"Any provision of a law made by the Legislature of a State is repugnant to any provision of a law made by Parliament"* — The comparison is provision-by-provision, not law-by-law. A State law is not rendered wholly void because a single clause conflicts with a Parliamentary law. The rest of the State law that is consistent with Parliamentary law survives. This proportionality principle — void "to the extent of the repugnancy" — means courts must carefully identify the specific provisions in conflict rather than striking down entire statutes.

*"Which Parliament is competent to enact"* — The Parliamentary law must be one Parliament is competent to make. This typically means the subject must appear in the Union List or the Concurrent List. A Parliamentary law that is itself ultra vires (enacted without competence) does not trigger Article 254 repugnancy, because the Constitutional text requires Parliament to have "competence to enact" the law. This safeguard ensures that Parliament cannot expand State law displacement simply by purporting to legislate on subjects outside its competence.

*"Or to any provision of an existing law with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent List"* — "Existing law" means pre-constitutional law (statutes passed before the Constitution came into force) that continues to operate under Article 372. If a State law enacted after the Constitution conflicts with a pre-constitutional Central law on a Concurrent List subject, the pre-constitutional law prevails under Article 254(1), subject to Article 372's other saving provisions.

*"Whether passed before or after the law made by the Legislature of such State"* — Parliamentary law prevails over State law on the same Concurrent List subject regardless of which was enacted first. A State law that predates the Parliamentary law is as subject to displacement as a State law enacted after. This temporal neutrality means States cannot protect their Concurrent List laws from Parliamentary override simply by enacting them first.

*"Shall, to the extent of the repugnancy, be void"* — Void, not merely inoperative. Under Article 251 (which applies when Parliament legislates on State List subjects under Articles 249 or 250), the State law is "inoperative" — it is valid but suspended and revives automatically when the Parliamentary exceptional power lapses. Under Article 254(1), the State law is void to the extent of the repugnancy. It is constitutionally invalid, not merely temporarily displaced. Revival requires fresh State legislation, not automatic re-operability.

**Clause (2) — the Presidential assent exception:**

"Where a law made by the Legislature of a State with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent List contains any provision repugnant to the provisions of an earlier law made by Parliament or an existing law with respect to that matter, then, the law so made by the Legislature of such State shall, if it has been reserved for the consideration of the President and has received his assent, prevail in that State."

Clause (2) is the most consequential State-protective provision in the Concurrent List framework. Its elements are:

*The State law must be repugnant to an "earlier" Parliamentary law.* The Presidential assent route applies when the State law is inconsistent with a pre-existing Parliamentary law on the same Concurrent List subject. It does not apply when a Parliamentary law is enacted after the Presidential assent State law — the proviso makes clear that a later Parliamentary law always overrides.

*"Reserved for the consideration of the President and has received his assent"* — Both conditions must be met. The State Bill must have been reserved (not merely sent for consideration but actually reserved under Article 200 for the President's consideration) and the President must have granted assent, not withheld it or returned it. A State law that was not reserved for Presidential consideration — for example, because the Governor gave assent without reserving it — cannot claim the Article 254(2) protection even if it is repugnant to an earlier Central law. The reservation and Presidential assent requirement is jurisdictional: without both conditions being met, the clause is simply unavailable.

*"Prevail in that State"* — Presidential assent gives the State law intra-State primacy over the conflicting earlier Central law. Within the State, it is the State law that governs; the Central law is displaced for that State. Other States where the same Central law operates are unaffected — the Presidential assent State law creates a localised departure from the national Central law framework.

*The proviso — Parliament's ever-present override:* "Nothing in this clause shall prevent Parliament from enacting at any time any law with respect to the same matter including a law adding to, amending, varying or repealing the law so made by the Legislature of the State." This is the critical limiting condition on the Presidential assent exception. It is absolute and unconditional. Parliament can, at any time after a State law receives Presidential assent under Article 254(2), enact legislation on the same Concurrent List subject. When Parliament enacts such legislation, the new Parliamentary law prevails over the Presidential-assent State law under Article 254(1)'s default rule. The State law is again void to the extent of the new repugnancy. Presidential assent thus confers only temporary, contingent State law primacy — subject always to Parliamentary re-occupation of the field.

**The "occupied the field" doctrine:**

Repugnancy under Article 254 has two distinct forms. Express conflict — where a provision of the State law directly contradicts a provision of the Parliamentary law — is straightforward to identify. More complex is implied repugnancy, also called the "occupied the field" doctrine. Under this doctrine, repugnancy arises even when there is no direct textual conflict if Parliament has legislated so comprehensively on a Concurrent List subject that it has effectively occupied the entire field, leaving no room for concurrent State legislation on any aspect of that subject.

The occupied-the-field inquiry requires examining: the object, scope, and scheme of the Parliamentary legislation; whether it was intended to be a complete and exhaustive code on the subject; whether State legislation on the same subject, even addressing aspects Parliament did not expressly address, would undermine or undercut the Parliamentary framework; and whether Parliament intended to displace State law entirely or merely to set minimum standards that States could supplement.

The occupied-field doctrine is significantly broader than the express-conflict test and is the source of most controversy in Article 254 litigation. Expansive application of the doctrine can effectively render the Concurrent List a Union List in practice — once Parliament enacts a framework law, States may be precluded from any legislation on the subject even in areas Parliament has not touched. The Supreme Court has recognised this tension and has developed a doctrinal response: a presumption against implied repugnancy. Courts will not readily infer that Parliament intended to occupy a field entirely unless the scheme of the Central legislation clearly indicates that intent.

## Key cases

**Zaverbhai Amaidas v. State of Bombay AIR 1955 SC 297** — One of the earliest Supreme Court examinations of Article 254 repugnancy, decided when Article 254 jurisprudence was still being formulated. The Court examined the relationship between the Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act 1946 and a Bombay State law on the same subject and held that repugnancy requires an actual conflict — where both laws cover the same field and conflict in their commands, the State law is void to the extent of the conflict. The judgment established the foundational framework that repugnancy is not a question of subject matter overlap alone but requires an actual, operative inconsistency between the laws. This early standard-setting remains a reference point for the general test.

**Ch Tika Ramji v. State of Uttar Pradesh AIR 1956 SC 676** — An early Article 254 decision that developed the covered-the-field doctrine alongside the express conflict test. Examining a State sugar cane law and Central sugar legislation, the Court held that Parliament's comprehensive regulation of the sugar industry had occupied the field, rendering State legislative supplementation void even on aspects not expressly addressed in the Central law. The judgment introduced the idea that repugnancy can arise not only from direct conflict but from the totality of Parliamentary legislative occupation of a subject.

**M. Karunanidhi v. Union of India (1979) 3 SCC 431** — This Constitution Bench decision remains the definitive authority on the general test for repugnancy under Article 254. The Court laid down that a conflict between a State law and a Parliamentary law under Article 254 arises in three circumstances: (a) when there is a direct conflict between specific provisions such that complying with one necessarily involves violating the other; (b) when Parliament intends to lay down a complete and exhaustive code on a subject, making any State law on that subject repugnant by implication; (c) when the two laws operate on the same subject matter, cover the same area, and their provisions are so inconsistent that they cannot stand together. The Court also held that mere overlapping subject matter, or the fact that both laws address the same general field without actual operational conflict, is insufficient to constitute repugnancy. Karunanidhi continues to be the starting point for every Article 254 analysis in Indian courts.

**Hoechst Pharmaceuticals Ltd. v. State of Bihar (1983) 4 SCC 45** — A five-judge Constitution Bench examined the Drugs (Price Control) Order made under the Essential Commodities Act (a Central law) and State pharmaceutical regulation in Bihar. The Court applied the Karunanidhi framework and held that repugnancy under Article 254 requires a real and actual conflict — if the two laws can operate simultaneously without conflict, Article 254 is not triggered. The judgment is important for its clear affirmation that the mere existence of a Central law on a broad subject does not automatically nullify State laws addressing distinct aspects of the same general field. Courts must examine each provision alleged to be repugnant with specificity, asking whether complying with the State provision and the Central provision simultaneously is possible.

**ITC Ltd. v. Agricultural Produce Market Committee (2002) 9 SCC 232** — The Supreme Court examined the conflict between the Central Tobacco Board Act and State agricultural produce market committee legislation. The case required the Court to apply both the pith and substance doctrine (to determine which List each law truly belongs to) and Article 254 repugnancy analysis (to address the Concurrent List overlap). The Court held that where the dominant purpose of a State law is to regulate a State subject (agricultural markets) and the dominant purpose of a Central law is a Union subject (regulation of a specific commodity board), the laws are not truly in the same field and repugnancy under Article 254 does not arise. The ITC judgment illustrates the important interaction between pith and substance characterisation and Article 254: repugnancy analysis presupposes that both laws are genuinely operating on the same Concurrent List subject. If proper characterisation reveals that the laws are operating on different lists, Article 254 is not engaged at all.

**State of Kerala v. Mar Appraem Kuri Co. Ltd. (2012) 7 SCC 106** — A Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court examined the conflict between the Kerala Chitties Act 1975 (a State law on chit funds, which had received Presidential assent) and the Chit Funds Act 1982 (a later Central law on the same subject). The Court held that the later Central law prevailed over the Presidential-assent State law under Article 254(1) read with the proviso to Article 254(2) — Parliament had, in enacting the Chit Funds Act 1982, exercised its power under the proviso to supersede the State law even though it had Presidential assent. The judgment is the leading authority on the relationship between Article 254(2) and the proviso: Presidential assent creates only temporary State primacy, and a later Parliamentary law on the same Concurrent List subject automatically prevails, rendering the Presidential assent State law void to the extent of the new repugnancy. Mar Appraem Kuri also clarified that the Presidential assent requirement in Article 254(2) is not merely procedural — it is a jurisdictional condition, and courts will rigorously verify whether the Governor did in fact reserve the Bill for Presidential consideration.

**Government of Andhra Pradesh v. J.B. Educational Society (2005) 3 SCC 212** — The Supreme Court examined whether Presidential assent to a State education law could cure its repugnancy to the Central Institutes of Technology Act. The Court held that Presidential assent under Article 254(2) does indeed "cure" the repugnancy in the sense that the State law prevails in that State over the earlier Central law. The judgment is the clearest authority for the proposition that Presidential assent is not merely a procedural formality — it has the substantive constitutional effect of reversing the normal hierarchy between Central and State law on Concurrent List subjects for that State. J.B. Educational Society is frequently cited for the principle that repugnancy is not a permanent constitutional defect in a State law; it is a defect that can be cured by the Presidential assent mechanism, which is precisely the constitutional function clause (2) is designed to serve.

## Current significance

Article 254 has become the central battleground of Indian constitutional law on economic regulation, labour relations, land acquisition, and social welfare legislation. Parliament has enacted a series of comprehensive framework statutes — the four Labour Codes consolidating twenty-nine labour laws, the Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act 2013, the National Food Security Act 2013, the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016, and the SARFAESI Act — on subjects that are either squarely on the Concurrent List or overlap significantly with it. Each of these laws raises acute Article 254 questions when States enact supplementary or conflicting legislation.

The Labour Codes present a particularly complex Article 254 challenge. Labour as a subject is Entry 22 in the Concurrent List. The four Labour Codes (Code on Wages 2019, Industrial Relations Code 2020, Code on Social Security 2020, Code on Occupational Safety, Health and Working Conditions Code 2020) consolidate the Central framework. Several States — Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat — have enacted their own labour law amendments or enacted new State labour legislation either before or after the Central Codes. Whether these State laws are repugnant to the Codes, whether they received Presidential assent (and therefore temporarily prevail), and whether the Central Codes when they come into full force will displace the State laws under Article 254(1) are live constitutional questions.

The Farm Acts litigation — before the Acts were withdrawn in 2021 — raised a particularly interesting Article 254 dimension. The Farmers (Empowerment and Protection) Agreement on Price Assurance and Farm Services Act 2020 and the Farmers Produce Trade and Commerce (Promotion and Facilitation) Act 2020 were challenged partly on the basis that they encroached on State-domain subjects (agricultural markets, Entry 28 of the State List). Several States enacted laws to protect their APMC frameworks. Whether the Central Farm Acts operated on State List subjects or Concurrent List subjects — and therefore whether Article 254 or the residual exclusive State competence applied — was never fully resolved because of the Acts' withdrawal.

The intersection of Article 254 with GST legislation is structurally novel. Article 246A, inserted by the 101st Amendment, creates a special concurrent power for GST on the supply of goods and services, outside the ordinary Seventh Schedule framework. But disputes about residual State tax powers — on petroleum products, alcohol, and real estate — regularly implicate Article 254 when both Central and State GST-adjacent laws coexist. Courts are increasingly called upon to apply the repugnancy analysis to these novel fiscal co-occupation questions.

The Presidential assent route under Article 254(2) continues to be a significant vehicle for State legislative innovation. Multiple State laws on education, tenancy, rent control, land ceilings, professional regulation, and anti-conversion have received Presidential assent and prevail within their States over earlier Central laws on the same subjects. These State laws represent the constitutional space within which federalism operates in practice — States using the Presidential assent mechanism to implement local social and economic priorities that diverge from the Central framework. The growing importance of this mechanism, and its increasing use in politically contested legislative contexts, makes Article 254(2) one of the most contested provisions in contemporary Indian constitutional litigation.
