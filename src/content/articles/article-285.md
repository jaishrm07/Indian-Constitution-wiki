  285. Exemption of property of the Union from State taxation.—(1) The property of the Union shall, save in so far as Parliament may by law otherwise provide, be exempt from all taxes imposed by a State or by any authority within a State.

## Why it matters

Article 285 is a structural pillar of Indian fiscal federalism. Without it, States and their local bodies could impose property taxes, municipal rates, and other levies on Central government assets — railways, defence establishments, postal buildings, and government offices — potentially generating revenue from Union operations in ways that could financially constrain those operations or create political leverage over them. The immunity preserves the Union's operational and financial independence from sub-national fiscal power, while retaining a safety valve: Parliament can always lift the immunity by law if the balance of federal interests requires it.

The article reflects a constitutional judgment about fiscal sovereignty and comity between levels of government. Neither the Union nor the States should be subject to the other's tax authority in respect of their core governmental assets. Allowing States to tax Union property would mean that a State government hostile to a central programme could use its tax power to raise the cost of running that programme on State territory, undermining the uniform national character of Union activities. Conversely, allowing the Union to tax State property without limit would enable the Centre to impose fiscal burdens on State operations — which is why Article 289 creates a reciprocal immunity for State property from Union taxation.

Article 285 also matters because of the scale of Union property in India. The Indian Railways alone holds one of the largest land banks in the country; the Ministry of Defence controls vast tracts in cantonments across every major city; the postal network, public sector undertakings, national highways, and CPWD housing estates together represent a significant portion of urban and peri-urban land. If all this were subject to State and municipal taxation, the fiscal impact on Union operations — and the corresponding windfall for local bodies — would be enormous. The immunity reflects the framers' judgment that these assets serve national purposes and should not be subject to sub-national fiscal extraction.

The clause (2) transitional saving reflects the practical reality of 1950. Colonial fiscal arrangements had subjected many Union properties to municipal levies under pre-independence law. Rather than abruptly disrupt local government revenue streams — which would have caused immediate fiscal hardship to municipal bodies — the framers allowed pre-existing taxes on specific Union properties to continue until Parliament chose to address them. This pragmatic transitional mechanism has been progressively worked through as Parliament has exercised its powers under individual statutes, but some vestigial pre-1950 arrangements may still technically persist.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) states the default: all Union property is exempt from all State taxes and from all taxes imposed by any authority within a State. The scope of "authority within a State" is deliberately broad. It covers not just the State legislature but also municipal corporations, cantonment boards, panchayats, notified area committees, port trusts, and any other body that has been granted power to levy taxes by or under State law. The immunity is not limited to State-level taxation; it extends to every layer of sub-national fiscal authority.

The immunity attaches to "property of the Union" — meaning property vested in the Government of India in its own name or held through constitutional instruments of the Union. This is a narrower category than might first appear. Companies incorporated under the Companies Act — even wholly government-owned public sector undertakings — are distinct legal persons under company law, and their property is generally not "property of the Union" in the Article 285 sense. ONGC, NTPC, BHEL, and SAIL must therefore ordinarily pay State and municipal taxes on their assets unless Parliament has specifically exempted them by statute. The constitutional immunity is for property held directly by the Union government, not for property held by its corporate subsidiaries.

The phrase "save in so far as Parliament may by law otherwise provide" creates the override mechanism. Parliament can lift the immunity — either comprehensively for a category of property or selectively for a specific tax or jurisdiction — by enacting legislation to that effect. This power has been exercised in railway-specific legislation, postal acts, and other central statutes that make Union property subject to particular State levies as a matter of cooperative fiscal policy. Parliament can also exercise the power in reverse — by conferring immunity on quasi-Union entities that the constitutional provision does not directly cover.

Clause (2) is the transitional savings clause. Only taxes that were "immediately before the commencement of this Constitution" actually being levied on the specific Union property in question benefit from the transitional carve-out. The Court has read this strictly: a general tax imposed on all property of a certain class does not give a local body the transitional right to continue taxing Union property of that class unless the Union property itself was specifically assessed and liable. The savings clause protects vested revenue streams, not hypothetical tax bases.

## Key cases

**Western India Theatres Ltd. v. Cantonment Board AIR 1959 SC 858** — The Supreme Court confirmed that Article 285 immunity extends to local bodies within a State and not just the State government itself. A cantonment board — though exercising its functions within a State's territory — cannot levy any tax on Union property that is not authorised by Parliament. This established that the immunity is comprehensive across all layers of sub-national government.

**New Delhi Municipal Council v. State of Punjab (1997) 7 SCC 339** — A Constitution Bench examined the scope of the immunity for property held by Central entities and the mechanism for waiving it. The Court clarified that the exemption attaches to Union ownership, reaffirmed that Parliament's waiver power under clause (1) must be exercised by express legislation, and held that administrative arrangements or contractual provisions cannot substitute for the statutory waiver that Parliament alone can provide.

**Union of India v. City Municipal Council, Bellary AIR 1963 SC 1195** — The Court applied the clause (2) transitional window carefully, holding that only taxes actually being levied on the specific Union property at the Constitution's commencement qualified to continue under the savings clause. A tax that had been imposed on the class of property generally but not specifically assessed on the Union property in question did not benefit from the transitional carve-out.

**Municipal Corporation of Greater Bombay v. Union of India (1996)** — The Court considered whether buildings belonging to the Government of India could be subjected to municipal property tax. It reaffirmed the default immunity while examining Parliament's power to authorise such taxation and the conditions under which the Union had or had not exercised its waiver power by statute. The decision confirmed that municipal bodies cannot self-help their way around Article 285 by asserting that Union property imposes costs on their services.

**Steel Authority of India Ltd. v. State of Orissa (2001)** — Addressing the PSU boundary question, the Court held that property vested in a government company under the Companies Act is not "property of the Union" for Article 285 purposes, even where the government owns all the shares. The immunity attaches to the legal form of ownership, not to the ultimate economic interest. This has significant implications for Central PSUs that hold large industrial and housing estates across States.

## Current significance

Article 285 is most actively litigated in disputes between Central government departments, PSUs, and municipal bodies over property tax. Large urban tracts — defence cantonments, railway colonies, CPWD housing estates, airport land, and central university campuses — remain exempt from municipal taxation, reducing the tax base of local bodies that bear the infrastructure costs of hosting these facilities. Municipal corporations in major cities have repeatedly contested this position, arguing that they provide water supply, roads, sewerage, and other services to Union properties without being able to recover costs through property tax.

The growing commercialisation of government land has sharpened the immunity question. The National Monetisation Pipeline — under which the Union is monetising railway stations, airports, highways, gas pipelines, and other government assets by leasing them to private operators — raises the question of whether commercially leased Union property retains the Article 285 immunity. Courts are increasingly asked whether a Union property that generates commercial rental income on a long-term lease to a private developer remains "property of the Union" for immunity purposes, or whether the commercial leasing converts it into a taxable asset. Parliament has not yet provided a comprehensive statutory framework for this question, leaving it to be resolved case by case in litigation between Union lessors, private lessees, and State/municipal tax authorities.

The GST era has also added a new dimension to Article 285 questions. Some State GST authorities have sought to apply GST on services provided to Union properties, arguing that the Article 285 immunity is a property tax immunity and does not extend to transaction taxes on commercial services. The Union's position has been that GST levied on transactions in connection with Union property effectively increases the cost of maintaining that property and should be treated as equivalent to a tax on the property itself. This frontier of Article 285 jurisprudence — whether service and transaction taxes can be applied to Union-related activity even where the underlying property is immune from property tax — will be resolved by the Supreme Court as GST implementation matures.
