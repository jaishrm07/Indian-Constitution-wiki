officialText: |-
  109. Special procedure in respect of Money Bills.—(1) A Money Bill shall not be introduced in the Council of States.

  (2) After a Money Bill has been passed by the House of the People it shall be transmitted to the Council of States for its recommendations and the Council of States shall within a period of fourteen days from the date of its receipt of the Bill return the Bill to the House of the People with its recommendations and the House of the People may thereupon either accept or reject all or any of the recommendations of the Council of States.

  (3) If the House of the People accepts any of the recommendations of the Council of States, the Money Bill shall be deemed to have been passed by both Houses with the amendments recommended by the Council of States and accepted by the House of the People.

  (4) If the House of the People does not accept any of the recommendations of the Council of States, the Money Bill shall be deemed to have been passed by both Houses in the form in which it was passed by the House of the People without any of the amendments recommended by the Council of States.

  (5) If a Money Bill passed by the House of the People and transmitted to the Council of States for its recommendations is not returned to the House of the People within the said period of fourteen days, it shall be deemed to have been passed by both Houses at the expiration of the said period in the form in which it was passed by the House of the People.

## Why it matters

Control over taxation and public expenditure is the most direct lever of government power. Article 109 reflects the constitutional judgment that the directly elected chamber — whose members are accountable to voters every five years — should have final authority over these decisions. Rajya Sabha, whose members are elected by State legislatures on staggered six-year terms, participates in the process but cannot block it. The asymmetry is deliberate and is modelled on the relationship between the UK House of Commons and House of Lords after the Parliament Acts of 1911 and 1949.

What makes Article 109 constitutionally explosive today is not the rule itself but its gateway: the Speaker's power under Article 110(3) to certify whether a Bill is or is not a Money Bill. Once that certificate is issued, Article 109's asymmetric procedure kicks in and Rajya Sabha becomes an advisory chamber. If the certificate is wrongly issued — if provisions that do not belong in a Money Bill are smuggled through as "incidental" — Article 109's intended scope is distorted.

Article 109 must be read alongside Article 110 (which defines what a Money Bill is), Article 117 (which governs financial Bills that are not Money Bills), and Article 122 (which limits judicial review of parliamentary proceedings). The interplay between these provisions determines how much of the government's legislative programme can be insulated from Rajya Sabha scrutiny — a question of fundamental importance to the federal and bicameral character of Indian democracy.

The framers debated at length whether the upper chamber should have any role at all in financial legislation. The compromise reached — advisory role only, with a 14-day window to return recommendations — was modelled on British constitutional practice but adapted to India's more assertive federal structure. The extent to which that compromise has been strained by the modern Money Bill controversy was something the framers did not anticipate.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — origin rule:** A Money Bill cannot be introduced in Rajya Sabha. It must originate in Lok Sabha. This is categorical. A Bill introduced in Rajya Sabha can never, even in theory, become a Money Bill.

**Clause (2) — fourteen-day window:** After Lok Sabha passes a Money Bill, it is transmitted to Rajya Sabha. Rajya Sabha has exactly fourteen days to return it with recommendations. The fourteen days begin from the date of actual receipt. The period is not extendable and does not pause for prorogation within the fourteen-day window.

**Rajya Sabha's limited role — recommendations, not amendments:** Rajya Sabha does not amend a Money Bill. It returns it to Lok Sabha with recommendations. The distinction is constitutional: recommendations are suggestions that Lok Sabha may accept or reject. Amendments, by contrast, would give Rajya Sabha co-equal power over the text. The word "recommendations" was chosen deliberately to make clear that Rajya Sabha's role is advisory only.

**Clauses (3) and (4) — Lok Sabha's final say:** Lok Sabha may accept or reject any or all of Rajya Sabha's recommendations. There is no obligation to accept any recommendation. If Lok Sabha accepts some recommendations, the Bill is deemed passed with those accepted amendments. If Lok Sabha accepts none, the Bill is deemed passed in the form Lok Sabha originally passed it. Rajya Sabha has no right to insist, no right to amend, and no right to delay beyond fourteen days.

**Clause (5) — deemed passage:** If Rajya Sabha simply does not act within fourteen days, the Bill is deemed passed in the form Lok Sabha approved. Rajya Sabha's silence is constitutionally irrelevant.

**No joint sitting:** Unlike ordinary Bills under Article 108, there is no provision for a joint sitting over a Money Bill dispute. Lok Sabha's position is final.

**Speaker's certificate required:** Before a Money Bill is transmitted to Rajya Sabha or presented to the President for assent, the Lok Sabha Speaker must endorse a certificate on the Bill stating that it is a Money Bill. This certificate, governed by Article 110(3) and (4), is the legal instrument that triggers the Article 109 procedure for the receiving chamber and for the President.

## Key cases

**K. S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2019) — Aadhaar Act:** A five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the Aadhaar Act's passage as a Money Bill by a 4–1 majority, finding that Aadhaar's core function — linking identity to Consolidated Fund disbursements — brought it within Article 110(1). Justice D. Y. Chandrachud, dissenting, held that provisions creating a universal biometric database and enabling private-sector authentication had no real nexus to the Article 110 definition, and that certifying the Act as a Money Bill was "a fraud on the Constitution" that unconstitutionally excluded Rajya Sabha from its legislative role under Article 109.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019):** A five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously held that the Finance Act 2017, passed as a Money Bill and containing amendments to tribunal legislation (National Company Law Tribunal, Debt Recovery Tribunal), required examination by a larger bench. The Court found that the Finance Act had used the money bill route to amend multiple substantive laws — the Tribunals Reforms Act, the Prevention of Money Laundering Act, and others — without those amendments having any genuine connection to Article 110's definition. The reference to a seven-judge bench remains pending, making this the most consequential unresolved question in Indian parliamentary procedure.

**S. Ramachandra Rao v. Union of India (2012):** The Supreme Court confirmed that the fourteen-day window in clause (2) is mandatory and self-executing: a Money Bill not returned within that period is constitutionally deemed passed regardless of the reason for non-return. The Court also noted, without finally deciding, that the reviewability of the Speaker's certificate is a live question.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019):** A five-judge Constitution Bench unanimously found that the Finance Act 2017 — passed as a Money Bill — had used the procedure to amend a range of legislation (tribunals, anti-money laundering, insolvency) whose connection to Article 110's definition was doubtful. The bench referred the question of reviewability of the Speaker's certificate and the outer limits of the Money Bill definition to a seven-judge bench. The reference is the most consequential pending question in Indian parliamentary law.

## Current significance

The Aadhaar majority's permissive reading of the money bill route opened a significant constitutional fault line. In the years following, the government routed Finance Act amendments — touching foreign funding of political parties (FCRA 2016 amendments), tribunal restructuring, and insolvency law — through the same Money Bill mechanism, bypassing Rajya Sabha on matters that had no obvious connection to taxation or the Consolidated Fund. The effect was to convert the Lok Sabha majority, in some sessions, into an omnibus legislative organ unchecked by the federal chamber. The pending seven-judge bench reference in Rojer Mathew will determine whether the Speaker's certificate under Article 110(3) is unreviewable or whether courts can examine — and potentially void — Money Bill certifications that are used as instruments of bicameral bypass.

The Article 109 controversy also has a federalism dimension that goes beyond bicameralism. Rajya Sabha is not only a revising chamber but also the chamber in which States are represented. When legislation that affects State subjects or State finances is routed as a Money Bill — bypassing Rajya Sabha — the States' representative voice in national legislation is circumvented. Constitutional scholars have argued that this undermines the federal compact that Rajya Sabha is meant to protect, transforming what the framers designed as a robust upper chamber into a largely ceremonial body on fiscal matters. The resolution of the Rojer Mathew reference will therefore have consequences not only for bicameralism but also for Indian federalism as a whole.

Rajya Sabha has, in practice, developed informal mechanisms to signal its displeasure with Money Bills even though it cannot formally block them. Rajya Sabha committees have published critical reports on Bills certified as Money Bills, and Rajya Sabha members have made detailed speeches placing on record arguments that the certification was constitutionally improper. These records have fed into judicial proceedings challenging the certifications, illustrating how the upper house uses its deliberative function even when its legislative veto has been stripped by the Article 109 procedure.

**The procedural flow under Article 109:**

1. Bill introduced in Lok Sabha (not Rajya Sabha).
2. Lok Sabha passes the Bill.
3. Speaker certifies the Bill as a Money Bill under Article 110(3)–(4).
4. Bill transmitted to Rajya Sabha with the Speaker's certificate.
5. Rajya Sabha has 14 days to return with recommendations.
6. Lok Sabha considers recommendations — accepts or rejects any or all.
7. Bill is deemed passed by both Houses in the form Lok Sabha finally approves.
8. Bill presented to President for assent under Article 111 with the Speaker's certificate.

At no stage does Rajya Sabha have the power to withhold passage, demand amendments, or trigger a joint sitting.
