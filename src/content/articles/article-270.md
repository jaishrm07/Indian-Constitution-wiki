## Why it matters

Article 270 is the constitutional heart of fiscal federalism in India. While the Union collects most major taxes — income tax, corporation tax, customs duties, and now integrated GST — the Constitution requires those revenues to be shared with States. Article 270 creates the divisible pool that makes this sharing mandatory and ties its quantification to the independent Finance Commission process under Article 280. Without this provision, the fiscal architecture would be purely unitary: the Union would collect and spend everything, and States would depend entirely on discretionary grants.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) defines what enters the divisible pool: all taxes and duties referred to in the Union List, except:
- Duties and taxes assigned exclusively to States under Articles 268, 269, and 269A (stamp duties on financial instruments, taxes on railway freight, and integrated GST attributable to States)
- Surcharges under Article 271 (which the Union retains entirely)
- Any cess levied for a specific purpose under a parliamentary law (which is kept outside the pool)

This means the divisible pool is structurally residual: everything the Union levies on List I subjects enters the pool unless it is explicitly carved out.

Clauses (1A) and (1B), inserted by the 101st Constitutional Amendment (2016) when GST was introduced, bring the Union's share of integrated GST and certain GST-related collections within the same distributional framework as traditional direct and indirect taxes. This ensures that the shift to GST did not reduce States' overall share of central tax revenues.

Clause (2) provides the sharing mechanism: the President prescribes, by order, the percentage of net proceeds to be distributed among States and the manner of distribution. Clause (3) defines "prescribed" — crucially, after a Finance Commission has been constituted, the President must consider the Finance Commission's recommendations before issuing the distributional order.

## The Finance Commission and the divisible pool

The Finance Commission (Article 280) is constituted every five years and recommends both the vertical share (what percentage of the divisible pool goes to States collectively) and the horizontal share (how that percentage is distributed among States). Recent Finance Commissions have recommended:

- 14th Finance Commission (2015-20): 42% devolution to States
- 15th Finance Commission (2021-26): 41% devolution (one percent reduction to account for Jammu and Kashmir's reorganisation into Union Territories)

The 16th Finance Commission is currently determining the share for 2026-31.

## The cess problem

A major structural tension in Article 270 is the cess exclusion. Parliament has progressively expanded the range of purpose-specific cesses — education cess, Krishi Kalyan cess, infrastructure cess, health cess, Swachh Bharat cess — that are excluded from the divisible pool. States argue that this practice artificially shrinks the pool, reducing their constitutional entitlement. Between 2010 and 2020, the share of cesses and surcharges in Union tax revenues roughly doubled, effectively reducing the divisible pool as a share of gross Union tax collection.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India** — The Supreme Court confirmed that the Finance Commission's recommendations on the divisible pool percentage are constitutionally significant inputs which the President must consider but is not bound to follow verbatim. However, material departures from Commission recommendations require proper justification given the constitutional design of fiscal federalism.

**Kerala v. Union of India (Cess Challenges)** — State governments have repeatedly challenged before the Supreme Court the Union's practice of levying surcharges and cesses that do not enter the divisible pool, arguing this shrinks States' share in violation of the spirit of Article 270. While direct relief has been limited, the Court has acknowledged that systematic cess proliferation may strain the constitutional design.

**Pradeep Jain v. Union of India** — The Court observed that Articles 270 to 275 together form an integrated fiscal federalism scheme, and that the Union's distributional obligations to States have a constitutional character rooted in the Finance Commission mechanism rather than being purely discretionary.

## Current significance

The 16th Finance Commission (2024–29 terms) is currently determining the vertical share of central taxes to be devolved to States, with considerable debate over whether cesses and surcharges have structurally eroded the divisible pool beyond the constitutional design's intent. GST compensation to States under the transitional arrangement lapsed in June 2022, making the Article 270 share even more critical to States' revenues. Several Southern States have raised formal objections to the population-based horizontal distribution formula, arguing it penalises States that performed well on demographic and developmental indicators and rewards States with higher population growth. The relationship between Article 270 devolution and the fiscal deficit targets under the FRBM framework also continues to generate Union-State tension.
