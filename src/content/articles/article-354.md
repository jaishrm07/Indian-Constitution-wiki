  354. Application of provisions relating to distribution of revenues while a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation.—(1) The President may, while a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation, by order direct that all or any of the provisions of articles 268 to 279 shall for such period, not extending in any case beyond the expiration of the financial year in which such Proclamation ceases to operate, as may be specified in the order, have effect subject to such exceptions or modifications as he thinks fit.

## Why it matters

India's federal fiscal architecture is anchored in Part XII of the Constitution. Articles 268 to 279 define which taxes are levied and collected by the Union, which are levied by the Union and collected by the States, and how proceeds are shared or assigned to States. The Finance Commission, constituted every five years under Article 280, recommends the formulae for vertical and horizontal devolution — determining how much of the Union's tax revenues flow to States, and in what proportion among them. These arrangements are designed to give States stable and constitutionally protected revenue streams independent of Union discretion — a deliberate design choice reflecting the Constituent Assembly's understanding that fiscal independence is essential to genuine State autonomy in a federal system.

Article 354 creates an express exception. When a National Emergency under Article 352 is in force, the President may by order modify any or all of Articles 268–279, effectively suspending the Finance Commission's formula and concentrating revenue control at the Union level. The rational basis for this power is that a genuine national security emergency may require the Union to control and redirect fiscal resources on a war footing that cannot wait for the ordinary Finance Commission cycle. A conflict that imposes extraordinary demands on defence, logistics, and relief expenditure may require the Union to retain resources that would in ordinary times flow to States. The check is parliamentary: every such order must be laid before each House of Parliament. The time limit is also important — no order under Article 354 can outlast the financial year in which the emergency ends, preventing the fiscal dislocation from becoming a permanent restructuring of the federal fiscal balance.

The provision has never been invoked in the decades since the Constitution came into force, but its existence structures the political economy of emergency governance in India. Any government declaring a National Emergency under Article 352 would immediately face questions about whether it also intended to invoke Article 354 to retain fiscal resources at the Centre — and the political cost of doing so while States are expected to share the burden of the emergency would be high. The provision is relevant not only as a dormant power but as a signal of the extent to which the Constitution's emergency framework concentrates power at the Union level, across both legislative and fiscal dimensions.

## Reading the provision

**Scope of modification.** The President's power under Article 354 is broad in form — it extends to any or all of Articles 268 to 279 and to any exceptions or modifications "the President thinks fit." This is not a power to make marginal adjustments; in principle, the President could suspend States' entitlement to their share of income tax proceeds, their share of excise duties, or the formulaic grants recommended by the Finance Commission. The key articles that can be modified include:

- **Article 268**: Duties levied by the Union but collected and retained by States;
- **Article 269**: Taxes levied and collected by the Union but assigned to States;
- **Article 270**: Taxes levied and collected by the Union and distributed between Union and States (the central devolution pool);
- **Article 275**: Grants from the Union to certain States (Finance Commission-recommended grants-in-aid);
- **Article 279**: Calculation of "net proceeds" of a tax for distribution purposes.

**Connection with Article 353.** Article 353 expands Parliament's legislative and the Union executive's power during an emergency — Parliament may legislate on State List subjects, and the Union executive may give directions to the State executive. Article 354 is the fiscal counterpart: it addresses the revenue dimension of the same centralisation. Together, they mean that during an emergency the Union can not only legislate on State subjects but also withhold the financial resources States would normally rely upon to administer those subjects. The combination makes a National Emergency a potentially complete supersession of the federal balance, subject only to parliamentary oversight.

**Connection with Article 360.** Article 360 (Financial Emergency) separately empowers the President to direct States to observe canons of financial propriety and to reduce State government salaries and allowances. Article 354 addresses the distribution of centrally collected revenues, while Article 360 addresses the conduct of State finances during a Financial Emergency specifically. A National Emergency could in principle simultaneously trigger both Articles 354 and 360 powers, producing a dual centralisation of both revenue flows and expenditure decisions.

**Parliamentary oversight.** Unlike the proclamation of emergency itself — which requires approval by special majority of each House within one month (Article 352(4)) — orders under Article 354 need only be "laid before" Parliament. There is no requirement of approval by resolution. This means the government of the day can reshape revenue-sharing by executive order without requiring parliamentary sanction — a significant asymmetry in the oversight framework. The laying requirement ensures Parliament knows what has been done, but it does not give Parliament a veto over the modification.

**Time limit.** The constitutional time limit — that the modification cannot extend beyond the financial year in which the emergency ends — is a structural safeguard against permanent fiscal centralisation through the emergency route. This prevents the government from using the emergency as a pretext for a lasting restructuring of the fiscal federal relationship. If a National Emergency ends on 15 November in a given year, an Article 354 order made during the emergency cannot continue past the following 31 March.

## The fiscal federal balance and emergency — context for Article 354

To appreciate the significance of Article 354, it is necessary to understand the normal framework it disrupts. Under Part XII of the Constitution:

- The Divisible Pool consists primarily of personal income tax and union excise duties (later corporation tax) collected by the Union, from which States receive constitutionally mandated shares;
- The Finance Commission, appointed every five years, recommends the vertical devolution ratio (how much of the Divisible Pool goes to States as a whole) and the horizontal distribution formula (how it is divided among individual States);
- In addition to tax devolution, States receive grants-in-aid under Article 275 for post-devolution revenue deficits and for special needs;
- States also have their own taxes — stamp duty, land revenue, State excise, vehicle taxes — that are not part of the central distribution framework.

An Article 354 order could in principle reduce or eliminate States' share of the Divisible Pool, suspend grants-in-aid under Article 275, or modify the definition of "net proceeds" for distribution purposes. Each of these would directly reduce States' revenues and their capacity to fund education, health, and welfare programmes — precisely the programmes most likely to face increased demand in the conditions of a national emergency.

## Article 354 in comparative perspective

India's Article 354 has no direct parallel in most federal constitutions. The Government of India Act, 1935, from which many of India's constitutional provisions descend, did not include a comparable provision because the fiscal relationship between the Centre and the Provinces was less constitutionalised. The comparable provisions in other federal systems — the emergency fiscal powers in Australia's Constitution and the Spending Power under the United States Constitution — operate through different mechanisms and do not directly permit modification of constitutional revenue-sharing formulae. Article 354's combination of broad presidential discretion, minimal parliamentary oversight, and the background of the federal fiscal framework makes it uniquely significant in comparative constitutional law as an example of deliberate fiscal centralisation power in an otherwise genuinely federal fiscal constitution.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v Union of India (1963)** — The Court examined the scope of Union authority over State property and fiscal autonomy in a federal framework. The judgment's analysis of the constitutional protections available to States in fiscal matters provides the background against which the breadth of the presidential power under Article 354 must be assessed. States have a constitutionally protected fiscal interest in devolution, and any modification of the distribution provisions — whether under Article 354 during an emergency or through other mechanisms — must serve a legitimate constitutional purpose and not amount to a permanent dispossession of States' revenue entitlements.

**Minerva Mills v Union of India (1980)** — The Court confirmed that even emergency-era executive action must remain within constitutional limits and that the basic structure — including federalism as a structural feature — constrains the exercise of presidential powers during an emergency. An Article 354 order that permanently or disproportionately distorted the fiscal federal relationship would be vulnerable to challenge under this reasoning. The judgment signals that the emergency powers, including Article 354, cannot be used to achieve what a constitutional amendment could not legitimately achieve — the dismantling of the federal structure as a basic feature.

**State of Kerala v Union of India (2024, pending)** — The State of Kerala filed an original suit before the Supreme Court challenging Union restrictions on its borrowing limits under the Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management Act framework. Kerala argued that fiscal coercion through arbitrary net borrowing ceilings effectively produces the conditions of a Financial Emergency without a formal proclamation and without the Article 360 parliamentary oversight. Though directed at ordinary fiscal legislation rather than Article 354 directly, the suit raises foundational questions about the limits of Union interference with State finances that will shape how any future Article 354 order is scrutinised.

## Current significance

Article 354 has never been invoked, and its non-invocation during the National Emergencies of 1962, 1971, and 1975–77 suggests that the political and fiscal costs of using it were considered too high even when emergency conditions existed. However, the provision's potential activation is more than a theoretical concern. Recurrent Union-State fiscal disputes — over GST compensation, over the devolution ratio recommended by the Finance Commission, and over Centre's discretionary grants and tied transfers — create a political context in which any future government contemplating a National Emergency would face intense scrutiny of whether it also used Article 354 to shift fiscal resources.

The political economy of Article 354 involves at least three dimensions:

- **Deterrence**: The existence of the power deters States from taking positions on national security matters that directly conflict with the Centre, since States know that fiscal support could theoretically be withheld during an emergency;
- **Fiscal centralisation as emergency governance**: A genuine national security emergency — external aggression, large-scale internal disturbance — may require the Centre to mobilise resources rapidly. Article 354's power is the constitutional acknowledgment that federal fiscal arrangements may be temporarily adjusted in such circumstances;
- **Risk of abuse**: The power could theoretically be used to punish recalcitrant States or to permanently shift the fiscal balance under cover of an emergency, even after the genuine security necessity has passed. The time limit (expiry at the end of the financial year in which the emergency ends) and the requirement that orders be laid before Parliament are the constitutional safeguards against such abuse, but their adequacy has been questioned.

The provision's weak parliamentary oversight requirement — laying before Parliament rather than requiring approval — is a design asymmetry that has been noted by constitutional reformers, Finance Commissions, and academic commentators. In a federal system where States depend on constitutionally guaranteed revenue shares for their functional autonomy, the ability of the President to modify those shares by executive order during an emergency represents a concentration of fiscal power that is unmatched in normal times. Finance Commissions from the Tenth onwards have made increasing use of conditional grants that the Union can adjust administratively; the existence of Article 354 in the background arguably legitimises a centralising impulse that manifests in ordinary policy practice even without formal emergency. As Union-State fiscal relations continue to be contested through litigation, legislation, and negotiation in the Finance Commission process, the background presence of Article 354 as an unused but available tool of centralisation is a relevant dimension of the constitutional politics of Indian federalism.

The Sixteenth Finance Commission, constituted in 2023, operates in a context of deepened fiscal tensions between the Union and the States — including disputes about the share of the divisible pool, the treatment of cess and surcharges that fall outside the divisible pool, and the design of conditional grants. Article 354 is not a live issue in the Finance Commission's work, but the provision's existence is a reminder of the constitutional asymmetry that underlies fiscal federal negotiations: States bargain over their constitutionally guaranteed shares knowing that in an extreme scenario those guarantees can be modified by presidential order. Whether this background asymmetry suppresses State assertiveness in fiscal negotiations or is simply too remote to affect practical bargaining outcomes is a question for political economists rather than constitutional lawyers — but its constitutional dimension is grounded in the text of Article 354.
