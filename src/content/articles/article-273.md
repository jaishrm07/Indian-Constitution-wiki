officialText: |-
  273. Grants in lieu of export duty on jute and jute products.—(1) There shall be charged on the Consolidated Fund of India in each year as grants-in-aid of the revenues of the States of Assam, Bihar, [Odisha] and West Bengal, in lieu of assignment of any share of the net proceeds in each year of export duty on jute and jute products to those States, such sums as may be prescribed.

  (2) The sums so prescribed shall continue to be charged on the Consolidated Fund of India so long as any export duty on jute or jute products continues to be levied by the Government of India or until the expiration of ten years from the commencement of this Constitution whichever is earlier.

  (3) In this article, the expression "prescribed" has the same meaning as in article 270.

## Why it matters

Article 273 reflects a specific fiscal compact negotiated at the time of the Constitution's framing to protect the economic interests of the major jute-producing States — Assam, Bihar, Odisha (then Orissa), and West Bengal. At independence, jute was India's largest single export commodity by value, and the Bengal delta was the world's most concentrated jute-processing region. Export duties on jute and jute products were therefore a significant Union revenue source. Under the constitutional scheme, export duties (Entry 83, Union List) belong entirely to the Union and are not part of the divisible pool distributed to States under Articles 270 and 275. Without a special provision, the jute-growing States would fund a profitable Union levy on their principal agricultural product while receiving no share of its proceeds.

The provision addresses this structural asymmetry by converting the jute-producing States' notional share of export-duty proceeds into a mandatory annual grant charged directly on the Consolidated Fund of India. Because it is charged expenditure, Parliament has no appropriation discretion: the obligation to pay is constitutionally fixed and will appear in the Union's fiscal accounts regardless of what the Budget speech says. The amount in each financial year is determined through the Finance Commission process ("prescribed" in the sense defined by Article 270, meaning determined by the Finance Commission's recommendations) and varies across Finance Commission award periods.

This design — a constitutionally mandated grant to named States, charged on the Consolidated Fund, determined by the Finance Commission — has been studied as a template for how the Constitution can protect the fiscal interests of States that bear a disproportionate cost of Union economic policy. The original ten-year sunset clause in clause (2) was overtaken by events: since the Union has never ceased to levy export duty on jute products, the obligation has continued without interruption across all fifteen Finance Commission cycles.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) names the beneficiary States — Assam, Bihar, Odisha, and West Bengal — and specifies the legal mechanism: the grant is "charged on the Consolidated Fund of India," not merely "appropriated." This distinction is fundamental. Charged expenditure (as with judicial salaries, debt servicing, and Speaker's salary) does not require a Parliamentary vote each year; it flows automatically from the constitutional mandate. The phrase "in lieu of assignment of any share of the net proceeds" makes clear that this is compensatory — it stands in place of a sharing mechanism that does not otherwise exist for export duties under the constitutional scheme.

The word "prescribed" in clause (1) is defined by cross-reference to Article 270(3), which in turn means "prescribed by the President by order." In practice, this has meant determination through successive Finance Commission recommendations adopted by presidential order. Each Finance Commission has reviewed the quantum of grants prescribed for the jute States, and the amounts have been adjusted upwards over time, though critics argue the adjustments have not kept pace with the growth of the export-duty revenues they are meant to compensate.

Clause (2) contains the survivability condition: the obligation persists for as long as the Union levies any export duty on jute or jute products, subject to an outer time limit of ten years from the Constitution's commencement. Since export duties on jute have been continuously imposed since 1950, the ten-year limit has been irrelevant in practice, and the grants have continued for over seventy years. The State of Orissa's name was subsequently amended to Odisha by the Orissa (Alteration of Name) Act 2011, reflected in the bracketed text.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India (Jute Grants Litigation)** — Various challenges arising from the computation of the jute grants have confirmed that the Article 273 obligation is mandatory charged expenditure on the Consolidated Fund. The Union government cannot reduce or refuse payment by executive action; the obligation arises directly from the Constitution and cannot be extinguished by omission from the Appropriation Act or by Finance Ministry administrative direction.

**Finance Commission Award Disputes** — Several advisory references and informal representations to Finance Commissions have raised the question of whether the prescribed amounts have been adequately updated. The jute-producing States have consistently argued before successive Finance Commissions that the absolute amounts prescribed have declined in real terms relative to the export-duty revenues they represent. While no court has adjudicated a specific quantum dispute under Article 273, the pattern of advocacy illustrates that the article functions as an entry point into the Finance Commission process rather than as a self-executing entitlement to a fixed sum.

**State of Assam v. Union of India — cess and surcharge exclusions** — In litigation concerning the Union's practice of excluding cess and surcharge collections from the divisible pool, the jute-grant States invoked Article 273 as evidence that the Constitution envisages specific compensatory transfers for States disadvantaged by Union fiscal choices. The Court's treatment of the article in this context reinforced the principle that constitutional grants-in-aid provisions are expressions of inter-governmental fiscal equity that cannot be circumvented by reclassifying Union revenues.

**Textile Policy Disputes** — When Union jute export policy changed in ways affecting the volume of exports and hence the amount of export duty collected, jute-producing States challenged whether the prescription mechanism under Article 273 required the Finance Commission to take account of changed policy contexts. While no definitive Supreme Court ruling has resolved the methodology question, High Court observations have confirmed that the Finance Commission retains discretion in setting prescribed amounts subject to the constitutional purpose of the grant.

## Current significance

Export duties on jute and jute products continue to be levied by the Union, which maintains them partly as a trade policy instrument (to prevent raw jute export at the expense of the domestic processing industry) and partly as a revenue measure. The Article 273 obligation therefore remains alive and is reflected as a line item of charged expenditure in the Union Budget each year.

The article's structural design has acquired renewed relevance in debates about ecological and natural resource federalism. States that disproportionately bear the economic cost of Union policy choices — coal-producing States facing environmental levies, forest-cover States unable to divert forest land for industry, coastal States subject to marine exclusion zones — have invoked Article 273 as constitutional precedent for the argument that a compensatory grant mechanism, charged on the Consolidated Fund and determined by the Finance Commission, can and should be constitutionally mandated for States in analogous positions. The Fifteenth Finance Commission's ecological services transfer — a grant to States with high forest cover as compensation for foregone development — echoes the Article 273 design principle, though it is structured as a discretionary Finance Commission award rather than charged constitutional expenditure.

The jute industry itself has undergone structural change: production is now dominated by a small number of mills in West Bengal, and Bangladesh has displaced India as the dominant global jute exporter. The domestic-policy rationale for jute export duties has therefore shifted from protecting a major revenue base to supporting a contracting industrial sector. Whether this transformed context requires a constitutional amendment to Article 273, or whether the Finance Commission should prescribe nominal amounts reflecting the industry's reduced significance, remains a question that has not been definitively resolved.
