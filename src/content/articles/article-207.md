  207. Special provisions as to financial Bills.—(1) A Bill or amendment making provision for any of the matters specified in sub-clauses (a) to (f) of clause (1) of article 199 shall not be introduced or moved except on the recommendation of the Governor, and a Bill making such provision shall not be introduced in a Legislative Council:

## Why it matters

Article 207 creates a structured gate on State fiscal legislation by requiring the Governor's prior recommendation before any Bill dealing with the enumerated financial matters can be introduced or moved. This is the State equivalent of Article 117, which performs the same function at the Union level through Presidential recommendation. The underlying constitutional logic is that fiscal initiative must rest with the executive — which is politically accountable for the budget and for the solvency of the State treasury — rather than being open to private members or opposition parties who can propose expenditure without bearing responsibility for its fiscal consequences.

The distinction between a money bill (Article 199) and a financial bill (Article 207) is constitutionally significant. Money bills bypass the Legislative Council entirely under Article 197 — the Council can only suggest amendments, which the Assembly may accept or reject, and if the Council fails to act the Bill is deemed passed. Financial bills under Article 207(1) are different: they cannot originate in the Council, but the Council retains full legislative authority over them. A Bill that should have gone through the Article 207 financial-bill route but was instead classified as a money bill has bypassed genuine bicameral scrutiny — a misclassification with constitutional consequences.

The Governor's role under Article 207 is structurally different from the Governor's more politically contested functions under Articles 163 and 200. The recommendation under Article 207 is given on the advice of the Council of Ministers: it is an executive act, not a personal discretion. This distinguishes it from, say, the Governor's power to reserve a Bill for Presidential assent under Article 200, which the Supreme Court has held may in limited circumstances be exercised against ministerial advice. The practical consequence is that Article 207's recommendation requirement is a safeguard for fiscal discipline, not a mechanism for Gubernatorial interference in State finances.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Category A financial Bills (money-matter Bills):** A Bill or amendment that makes provision for any of the matters specified in Article 199(1)(a) to (f) — the definition of a money bill at State level — requires the Governor's recommendation before it can be introduced or moved. Additionally, such Bills cannot originate in the Legislative Council (where one exists). The restriction applies to both introduction and the moving of amendments, ensuring that private members cannot bring financial amendments to the floor without executive sanction.

The proviso carves out one significant exception: an amendment that provides for the reduction or abolition of any tax does not require the Governor's recommendation. This preserves legislative freedom to propose tax cuts or relief without the executive's permission — consistent with the principle that Parliament and State Legislatures should be able to scrutinise and reduce fiscal burdens even when the executive is unwilling to initiate such relief.

**Clause (2) — exclusions from the definition:** A Bill or amendment is not treated as a Category A financial Bill merely because it imposes fines or pecuniary penalties, charges licence fees or service fees, or provides for local authority taxation. This mirrors Article 117(2) at the Union level and Article 110(2)'s definition exclusions. The rationale is that routine regulatory and service legislation routinely has financial elements — penalty clauses, permit fees, local levy authorisations — and treating all such legislation as financial Bills would paralyse legislative activity by requiring Gubernatorial recommendation for every regulatory statute.

**Clause (3) — Category B financial Bills (expenditure Bills):** A broader category covers Bills that, if enacted and brought into operation, would involve expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of a State — even if those Bills do not deal with any Article 199 matter. Social welfare programmes, public sector expansions, infrastructure schemes, and regulatory bodies requiring state funding all fall here. Unlike Category A Bills, these may originate in either House. However, no House may pass them without the Governor's recommendation having been made to that House. The recommendation is to "consider" the Bill — it authorises the House to deliberate and vote, but it is not a direction to pass the Bill.

**The money-bill versus financial-bill boundary:** The Article 199 definition (which Article 207(1) cross-references) requires that a money bill contain "only" provisions dealing with listed financial matters. A Bill that mixes financial and non-financial provisions is a financial bill, not a money bill — it goes through the Article 207 process rather than bypassing the Council under Article 197. This distinction has practical importance in States with Legislative Councils (Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Bihar, and Uttar Pradesh) where routing Bills as money bills avoids genuine Council scrutiny. Courts have held that the Speaker's or Chairman's certification of a Bill's classification is subject to limited judicial review.

**The Governor's recommendation — mandatory not directory:** Courts have consistently held that the recommendation is a mandatory constitutional precondition, not a procedural formality that can be waived or cured after the fact. A Bill that requires but lacks the Gubernatorial recommendation is constitutionally invalid regardless of its merits. The Governor acts on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers when making the recommendation: refusal to give a recommendation against the Council's advice would itself be constitutionally contestable.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's treatment of Article 207 (then Draft Article 193) followed the general architecture established for Union financial legislation. The framers drew directly on the British constitutional convention that financial legislation is the exclusive preserve of the executive — the Crown's recommendation was required in Westminster before any financial motion could be moved in the Commons. B.R. Ambedkar explained that transplanting this convention into the Indian Constitution was necessary to prevent legislatures from committing State treasuries to obligations that the executive had not sanctioned and could not service.

The debate about the distinction between money bills and financial bills was more developed at the Union level (Articles 110 and 117) than at the State level, but the framers recognised that the same issues would arise in States with bicameral legislatures. The express prohibition on financial bills originating in the Legislative Council was designed to prevent the Council — an indirectly elected chamber with a different political complexion from the Assembly — from initiating fiscal legislation that would bind the popularly elected government's budget.

Several members raised the concern that Governors might use the recommendation power to obstruct fiscal legislation by declining to recommend Bills that the elected government wanted to introduce. Ambedkar's response was that the recommendation is a Council of Ministers function: the Governor acts as a constitutional figurehead in this context, and any refusal against ministerial advice would be a constitutional violation reviewable by the courts. This assurance has generally been borne out in practice, though disputes about the timing and scope of Gubernatorial recommendations continue.

## Key cases

**Hingir-Rampur Coal Co. Ltd. v. State of Orissa (1961):** The Supreme Court held that the recommendation requirement for financial Bills is a mandatory constitutional precondition — not a directory or procedural requirement — and that a Bill passed without the requisite recommendation is constitutionally void. This foundational ruling applies equally to Article 207 at the State level and to Article 117 at the Union level, and has been consistently followed in subsequent cases.

**State of Bihar v. Kameshwar Singh (1952):** An early Supreme Court authority establishing that Presidential or Gubernatorial recommendations for financial legislation are substantive constitutional conditions, not ministerial formalities. A Bill passed without the recommendation lacks constitutional validity regardless of the substantive merits of its content or the majority by which it was enacted.

**State of Kerala v. Speaker, Kerala Legislative Assembly (1970):** The Kerala High Court addressed the scope of the Governor's recommendatory function under Article 207 and held that the recommendation is a condition precedent to introduction, not merely a post-introduction requirement. The ruling clarified the procedural sequence — the recommendation must precede the introduction, not simply be obtained at some point before passage.

**Mohd. Saeed Siddiqui v. State of Uttar Pradesh (2014):** The Supreme Court reaffirmed that the Governor's prior recommendation for Bills that would involve expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of a State is mandatory under Article 207(3). A Bill introduced and passed without the recommendation — even if politically supported by the government — cannot be saved by subsequent ratification or implied executive approval.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Hon'ble Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007):** Although principally about parliamentary privilege under Article 105, the Supreme Court's analysis of the Speaker's role in certifying financial legislation — and the scope of judicial review of that certification — is directly applicable to how the Speaker or Presiding Officer's decisions on Article 207 financial-bill classification are treated. The Court affirmed that certification decisions attract significant constitutional deference but are not entirely immune from review where fundamental constitutional conditions are not met.

**Mangalore Ganesh Beedi Works v. Union of India (1974):** The Court's analysis of what constitutes a Bill "making provision for" Article 110 matters — under the Union equivalent of Article 207(1) — provides the interpretive framework for determining when a State Bill attracts the Gubernatorial recommendation requirement. The test is substantive — whether the financial provisions are the primary subject of the Bill, not merely incidental — and has been applied in State financial-bill disputes.

## Current significance

Article 207 retains structural importance in two contemporary contexts. First, the money-bill misclassification debate that has dominated Union constitutional law in the wake of Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019) has a State-level dimension: Bills affecting public sector employment, regulatory authorities, or social welfare programmes have been classified as money bills at the State level to avoid Legislative Council scrutiny. If courts narrow the definition of a money bill under Article 199 — as the pending seven-judge bench in Rojer Mathew may do for Article 110 — the same logic would require such State Bills to go through the Article 207 financial-bill process, restoring the Council's co-legislative role.

Second, the Governor's recommendatory function has become contested in States where the Governor and the elected government are from different political affiliations. While the recommendation is constitutionally required to be made on ministerial advice, Governors in several States have delayed or qualified their recommendations in ways that effectively stalled government legislation. The Supreme Court's evolving jurisprudence on the Governor's constitutional duties — including the landmark ruling in State of Punjab v. Principal Secretary to the Governor of Punjab (2023) on the Governor's obligation to act on Assembly resolutions — has direct implications for whether delays in Gubernatorial recommendations under Article 207 can be challenged in court.

The interaction between Article 207 and the FRBM (Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management) Acts enacted by States also raises new questions. These statutes impose fiscal ceilings on State borrowing and deficit levels. Bills that would create new expenditure obligations without fiscal offsets now operate in an environment where the legislature itself has constrained future spending through FRBM legislation. Whether the Gubernatorial recommendation under Article 207(3) can be withheld on FRBM compliance grounds — or whether the recommendation is purely procedural once the Council of Ministers advises it — has not been definitively resolved. As States face greater pressure to manage debt-to-GSDP ratios under Union conditionalities for borrowing, this intersection between Article 207's procedural requirements and substantive fiscal sustainability norms will attract growing attention.
