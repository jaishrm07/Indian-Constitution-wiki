  [371F. Special provisions with respect to the State of Sikkim.—

  Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution,—

  (a) the Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim shall consist of not less than thirty members;

  (b) as from the date of commencement of the Constitution (Thirtysixth Amendment) Act, 1975 (hereafter in this article referred to as the appointed day)—

  (i) the Assembly for Sikkim formed as a result of the elections held in Sikkim in April, 1974 with thirty-two members elected in the said elections (hereinafter referred to as the sitting members) shall be deemed to be the Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim duly constituted under this Constitution; (ii) the sitting members shall be deemed to be the members of the Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim duly elected under this Constitution; and (iii) the said Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim shall exercise the powers and perform the functions of the Legislative Assembly of a State under this Constitution;

  (c) in the case of the Assembly deemed to be the Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim under clause (b), the references to the period of [five years], in clause (1) of article 172 shall be construed as references to a period of [four years] and the said period of [four years] shall be deemed to commence from the appointed day;

  (d) until other provisions are made by Parliament by law, there shall be allotted to the State of Sikkim one seat in the House of the People and the State of Sikkim shall form one parliamentary constituency to be called the parliamentary constituency for Sikkim;

  (e) the representative of the State of Sikkim in the House of the People in existence on the appointed day shall be elected by the members of the Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim;

  (f) Parliament may, for the purpose of protecting the rights and interests of the different sections of the population of Sikkim make provision for the number of seats in the Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim which may be filled by candidates belonging to such sections and for the delimitation of the assembly constituencies from which candidates belonging to such sections alone may stand for election to the Legislative Assembly of the State of Sikkim;

  (g) the Governor of Sikkim shall have special responsibility for peace and for an equitable arrangement for ensuring the social and economic advancement of different sections of the population of Sikkim and in the discharge of his special responsibility under this clause, the Governor of Sikkim shall, subject to such directions as the President may, from time to time, deem fit to issue, act in his discretion;

  (h) all property and assets (whether within or outside the territories comprised in the State of Sikkim) which immediately before the appointed day were vested in the Government of Sikkim or in any other authority or in any person for the purposes of the Government of Sikkim shall, as from the appointed day, vest in the Government of the State of Sikkim;

  (i) the High Court functioning as such immediately before the appointed day in the territories comprised in the State of Sikkim shall, on and from the appointed day, be deemed to be the High Court for the State of Sikkim;

  (j) all courts of civil, criminal and revenue jurisdiction, all authorities and all officers, judicial, executive and ministerial, throughout the territory of the State of Sikkim shall continue on and from the appointed day to exercise their respective functions subject to the provisions of this Constitution;

  (k) all laws in force immediately before the appointed day in the territories comprised in the State of Sikkim or any part thereof shall continue to be in force therein until amended or repealed by a competent Legislature or other competent authority;

  (l) for the purpose of facilitating the application of any such law as is referred to in clause (k) in relation to the administration of the State of Sikkim and for the purpose of bringing the provisions of any such law into accord with the provisions of this Constitution, the President may, within two years from the appointed day, by order, make such adaptations and modifications of the law, whether by way of repeal or amendment, as may be necessary or expedient, and thereupon, every such law shall have effect subject to the adaptations and modifications so made, and any such adaptation or modification shall not be questioned in any court of law;

  (m) neither the Supreme Court nor any other court shall have jurisdiction in respect of any dispute or other matter arising out of any treaty, agreement, engagement or other similar instrument relating to Sikkim which was entered into or executed before the appointed day and to which the Government of India or any of its predecessor Governments was a party, but nothing in this clause shall be construed to derogate from the provisions of article 143;

  (n) the President may, by public notification, extend with such restrictions or modifications as he thinks fit to the State of Sikkim any enactment which is in force in a State in India at the date of the notification;

  (o) if any difficulty arises in giving effect to any of the foregoing provisions of this article, the President may, by order, do anything (including any adaptation or modification of any other article) which appears to him to be necessary for the purpose of removing that difficulty:

  Provided that no such order shall be made after the expiry of two years from the appointed day;

  (p) all things done and all actions taken in or in relation to the State of Sikkim or the territories comprised therein during the period commencing on the appointed day and ending immediately before the date on which the Constitution (Thirty-sixth Amendment) Act, 1975, receives the assent of the President shall, in so far as they are in conformity with the provisions of this Constitution as amended by the Constitution (Thirty-sixth Amendment) Act, 1975, be deemed for all purposes to have been validly done or taken under this Constitution as so amended.]

## Why it matters

Sikkim's accession was constitutionally unlike any other integration into India. It involved the absorption of a functioning sovereign monarchy — the Chogyal Palden Thondup Namgyal of the Namgyal dynasty — through a referendum in April 1975, followed by the rapid Thirty-sixth Amendment that produced Article 371F. The article had to perform an unusual set of tasks at once: validate a legislature and its members elected under a different constitutional order, create parliamentary representation from scratch, protect the distinct ethnic composition of Sikkim's polity, insulate pre-accession treaty arrangements from judicial challenge, and continue the entire pre-existing legal framework. No other Part XXI provision attempts a constitutional transition of this comprehensiveness in a single article.

Article 371F also has an important international dimension that no other 371-series provision possesses. China continued to describe Sikkim as an independent State occupied by India until 2005, and even the 2005 change came in the context of the India-China joint statement on boundary questions that has itself since been contested. Any future Chinese revisitation of Sikkim's status in the context of Himalayan border negotiations would implicate the constitutional foundation that Article 371F provides. The pre-accession treaty insulation in clause (m) ensures that old Indo-Sikkim treaties cannot be relitigated in Indian courts — but the international law dimension of those agreements lies beyond the constitutional text.

The Sangha constituency — the only directly religion-linked reserved constituency in any Indian State legislature — rests entirely on clause (f)'s authorisation for Parliament to protect the rights and interests of different sections of Sikkim's population through Assembly seat reservation. This unique feature of Sikkimese democracy has been periodically challenged as inconsistent with India's secular constitution, but has been consistently maintained by Parliament under the authority that clause (f) grants.

## Reading the provision

**Legislature and elections (clauses a–c):** The Assembly must have not fewer than thirty members. The existing thirty-two-member Assembly from the April 1974 Sikkim elections was deemed duly constituted under the Constitution. Its term ran for four years from the appointed day rather than the standard five, giving an early opportunity for fresh elections under the full constitutional order.

**Parliamentary representation (clauses d–e):** One Lok Sabha seat was allotted to Sikkim, with the State forming a single parliamentary constituency. Unusually, the first Lok Sabha representative was to be elected by State Assembly members rather than by direct popular vote — an interim measure until the first direct election could be held. This is one of the clearest examples of the article's transitional character: it creates temporary constitutional arrangements explicitly designed to be superseded by normal electoral processes.

**Community protections (clause f):** Parliament may make provision for reserving Legislative Assembly seats for candidates from particular sections of Sikkim's population and delimiting constituencies from which only such candidates may stand. This power has been used to maintain reserved seats for the Bhutia-Lepcha community, seats for the Nepali-origin population, and — most distinctively — the Sangha constituency reserved for representatives of Buddhist monasteries. The Sangha seat has been periodically challenged as inconsistent with the secular character of the Constitution, but clause (f) provides a direct constitutional authorisation that overrides the general secular framework for elections.

**Governor's special responsibility (clause g):** The Governor has special responsibility for peace and equitable social and economic advancement of different sections of the population, and may act in individual discretion subject to Presidential directions. This is a broader and more positive mandate than the equivalent provisions in Articles 371A or 371C — it encompasses not just law and order but also social and economic equity across Sikkim's communities, reflecting the State's particular history of ethnic stratification between Bhutia-Lepcha landowning communities, Nepali-origin majority, and small Limboo, Tamang, and other groups.

**Transitional provisions (clauses h–p):** All State properties vest in the new State government. The existing High Court is deemed the constitutionally valid High Court for Sikkim. Existing courts, authorities, and officers continue. Existing Sikkimese laws remain in force until amended or repealed; the President had two years to adapt them for constitutional conformity. Clause (m) uniquely removes all court jurisdiction (other than the Supreme Court's advisory jurisdiction under Article 143) over disputes arising from pre-accession treaties with the Government of India. Clause (n) allows the President to extend Indian enactments to Sikkim with modifications, providing a mechanism for integrating Sikkim's legal system with the wider Indian statutory framework without requiring immediate comprehensive legislation.

## The 1975 merger and Article 2

The Thirty-sixth Amendment Act, 1975 also amended Article 2 (which deals with Parliament's power to admit new States) and the First Schedule to include Sikkim as the twenty-second State. Article 371F is the constitutional expression of Article 2's exercise in Sikkim's case — but it goes far beyond anything Article 2 itself requires, creating a detailed constitutional charter for the new State rather than simply recording its addition to the Union. The interaction between Article 371F and Article 2 has been discussed in academic commentary: Article 2 gives Parliament power to admit States on "such terms and conditions as it thinks fit," and Article 371F can be read as the constitutional embodiment of those terms and conditions in Sikkim's case, given special constitutional status by being embedded directly in Part XXI rather than left to ordinary legislation.

## Key cases

**Sonam Wangdi v Union of India (Sikkim High Court, 2013; Supreme Court, 2016)** — The pivotal income-tax exemption case. Section 10(26AAA) of the Income Tax Act exempted from tax the income of "Sikkimese" persons — defined primarily as those listed in the register of Sikkim subjects as of 1975. Old Indian settlers in Sikkim (Marwaris and others) who had lived there before 1975 were excluded from the exemption by a 2008 amendment. The Supreme Court held that the exclusion of old Indian settlers from the exemption was discriminatory and unconstitutional, as they had integrated into Sikkimese society. The case illustrates how the personal status questions created by the 1975 merger — who counts as a "Sikkimese subject" — continue to generate significant litigation under the framework Article 371F created.

**Association of Old Settlers of Sikkim v Union of India (2023)** — Following the 2016 ruling, Parliament amended the Income Tax Act to extend the exemption to old Indian settlers; this was challenged by Nepali-origin Sikkimese groups who argued the original exclusion had been necessary to protect the rights of indigenous communities. The case reached the Supreme Court and raised questions about the interaction between Article 371F(f)'s community-protection mandate and equality guarantees under Article 14, confirming that the protections in Article 371F create a special constitutional space for community-differentiated treatment in Sikkim that must be balanced against equality rights.

**Pawan Hans Ltd v Sikkim Government (2016)** — The Supreme Court addressed whether pre-merger Sikkimese laws governing aviation operations continued to be in force under clause (k) and whether they had been superseded by central legislation, applying the framework of the adaptation and continuance provisions in Article 371F(k) and (l). The case illustrated how the continuance of old Sikkimese law under the constitutional framework creates ongoing questions about the interaction between the pre-accession legal order and subsequent Indian legislation.

**Sangha Constituency Challenge (Election Commission of India, multiple proceedings 1990s–2010s)** — The continued existence of the Sangha constituency — a Buddhist monastery reserved seat in the Sikkim Assembly — has been challenged before the Election Commission and in court as inconsistent with the constitutional bar on religion-based discrimination in voting rights under Article 325, and as incompatible with the secular framework of Articles 15 and 16. These challenges have been rejected on the basis that clause (f) of Article 371F provides direct constitutional authority for community-based seat reservations in Sikkim that overrides the general equality provisions.

## Current significance

The Sangha constituency remains the only directly religion-linked reserved constituency in any Indian State legislature, and its constitutional basis rests entirely on clause (f). Income-tax exemptions for Sikkimese subjects remain politically sensitive, representing a substantial fiscal privilege compared to other Indian residents, and have been extended and contracted through parliamentary action. The litigation pattern around the exemption — involving old Indian settlers, Nepali-origin Sikkimese, and Bhutia-Lepcha indigenous community representatives — illustrates the complexity of managing competing community interests within the framework clause (f) creates.

China's historical non-recognition of Sikkim as an Indian State — substantially changed in 2005 but with residual ambiguity — gives Article 371F's merger architecture an ongoing geopolitical dimension. The Himalayan border disputes in the Doklam area (2017) and the Galwan Valley (2020) have raised questions about whether Chinese revisitation of the broader border question could implicate claims about Sikkim's status, and the pre-accession treaty insulation in clause (m) ensures that old Indo-Sikkim treaties cannot be relitigated in Indian courts even if their international law dimensions remain unsettled.

Sikkim's distinctive land rights regime — in which pre-merger land ownership records and community protections continue to operate alongside Indian property law — continues to generate practical questions about development, infrastructure investment, and the application of central land acquisition and forest rights legislation. The President's power in clause (n) to extend Indian enactments to Sikkim with modifications has been used selectively, creating a partly integrated and partly distinct legal order that reflects the careful constitutional transition Article 371F engineered.
