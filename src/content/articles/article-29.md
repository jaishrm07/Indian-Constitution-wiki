## Why it matters

India's Constitution was drafted against the backdrop of partition — a catastrophe directly linked to the failure to protect religious and linguistic minorities. The framers were acutely conscious that the new state's commitment to pluralism had to be more than rhetorical, that cultural minorities needed concrete constitutional guarantees against both direct state persecution and the quieter but equally corrosive force of enforced cultural homogenisation. Article 29 is one of the provisions that embodies this commitment. It translates the abstract principle of pluralism into two specific and enforceable constitutional rights: a collective cultural right and an individual non-discrimination guarantee in education.

Clause (1) recognises something important about the relationship between culture and personhood — that a person's language, script, and cultural practices are not incidental preferences but central aspects of their identity and their community's cohesion. A state that compels assimilation — that forces linguistic minorities to abandon their language, or tribal communities to shed their cultural practices, or religious communities to relinquish their scripts — does a form of violence to those communities even if no physical force is involved. Article 29(1) protects against this constitutional wrong by giving communities themselves a right to conserve what makes them distinct, positioning cultural survival as a matter of fundamental rights rather than legislative grace.

Clause (2) addresses a different but related problem — the use of admission criteria that reflect the dominant community's religious, caste, or linguistic identity to exclude others from educational opportunities provided by or with the support of the state. When government-maintained or government-aided schools and colleges admit students selectively on the basis of religion, caste, or language, they use public resources to perpetuate exclusionary hierarchies. Clause (2) prohibits this: it is a specialised application of the anti-discrimination principle to the educational domain, targeted specifically at the kinds of discrimination that had historically been used to keep Dalits out of schools, religious minorities out of colleges, and linguistic minorities out of the better-funded institutions of the majority community.

The heading of Article 29 — "Protection of interests of minorities" — is misleading in a significant way. Article 29(1) by its own terms applies to "any section of the citizens," not only to minorities. A regional cultural group, even if it is a majority in its own state, can invoke Article 29(1) to resist a central government policy that threatens its distinct language or script. The Arya Samaj has invoked it to protect Sanskrit, Telugu speakers have invoked it against Hindi imposition, and tribal communities invoke it to protect indigenous scripts. This non-minority character of the Article 29(1) right is an underexplored but constitutionally significant aspect of the provision.

Article 29 also matters structurally because it is the platform from which Articles 29(2) and 30 interact. The constitutional tension between the individual non-discrimination guarantee in clause (2) and the collective minority institution-building right in Article 30 is one of the most persistently contested problems in Indian educational law. Does a minority institution's right to maintain its character allow it to give preferential admission to community members in a way that effectively excludes others on the very grounds clause (2) prohibits? The answer — worked out across sixty years of Supreme Court decisions — turns on the word "only" in clause (2) and the distinction between preference for the founding community and exclusion of others on prohibited grounds.

The two clauses together give Article 29 a dual-axis structure that is unusual among constitutional rights provisions. Clause (1) runs vertically, protecting communities over time by guaranteeing the conservation of their cultural heritage. Clause (2) runs horizontally, protecting individuals at the point of access to publicly funded education by prohibiting discriminatory exclusion. Both dimensions are essential to the constitutional architecture of pluralism: without clause (1), cultural minorities face long-run assimilation; without clause (2), individual members of minority and lower-caste communities face exclusion from the educational resources they need to participate equally in civic and professional life.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) confers the right on "any section of the citizens residing in the territory of India or any part thereof having a distinct language, script or culture of its own." The beneficiary is a "section of citizens" — a collective, not merely an individual. The right is to "conserve" — not to develop, expand, or propagate, though those activities may be protected under other provisions. Conservation means maintaining what exists, protecting it against erosion, and transmitting it to future generations. The right does not require a community to prove it is under threat before invoking it; it is preventive as much as remedial.

The triggers for the right — distinct language, distinct script, or distinct culture — are alternatives. A community does not need to have all three; possessing any one distinct feature qualifies the community for the right. Courts have interpreted "distinct" as meaning genuinely differentiated, not merely a minor variant of the majority culture, but the threshold has not been set impossibly high. Tribal communities with indigenous languages, religious communities with distinctive scripts such as Gurmukhi or Perso-Arabic, and regional linguistic communities have all been held to qualify. The word "residing in the territory of India or any part thereof" means the section need not be spread across the whole country; a community concentrated in one region can still invoke the protection.

The nature of the right to "conserve" has been debated in the case law. Does it include a right to demand state support for conservation — state-funded schools teaching in the community's language, state recognition of the community's script for official purposes? Or is it only a negative right to resist state action that would destroy the cultural feature? Courts have generally given Article 29(1) a positive dimension when read alongside the state's duty under Article 350A to provide instruction in the mother tongue at the primary stage, but have been cautious about converting the conservation right into an open-ended affirmative claim on specific government resources beyond the primary level.

Clause (2) uses the words "on grounds only of religion, race, caste, language or any of them." The word "only" is critical — a citizen cannot be denied admission where the sole or dominant reason for denial is one of these specified grounds. If an institution's admission criteria are based on merit and the applicant is excluded on merit, clause (2) is not violated even if the outcome is that students of a particular religion or caste are disproportionately excluded. The "only" qualifier also means that if there are additional legitimate differentiating factors — such as domicile, a qualifying examination, or specialised course requirements — the fact that religion or caste correlates with outcomes does not automatically constitute a clause (2) violation. What the clause prohibits is the explicit use of one of the listed grounds as the operative reason for exclusion.

The institutions covered by clause (2) are "educational institution maintained by the State or receiving aid out of State funds." This covers government schools and colleges, and aided private institutions. Unaided private institutions receive no direct constitutional obligation under clause (2) for admissions, though they remain subject to the general anti-arbitrariness standard under Article 14. The distinction between aided and unaided institutions became crucial in the context of private professional colleges following TMA Pai Foundation, where the Court held that the non-discrimination norm in clause (2) operates differently against institutions of different funding categories.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The debate in the Constituent Assembly on what became Article 29 was shaped by two competing anxieties. Members representing religious and linguistic minorities feared that in a democracy dominated by the Hindu majority, state educational institutions would be used to assimilate minority communities and exclude minority students from educational opportunity. Members representing the national integration vision feared that strong communal cultural rights would fragment the new nation and entrench the very communalism that had produced partition. Article 29 was designed to balance these concerns by separating the collective cultural right from the individual non-discrimination guarantee and limiting the compulsion element rather than the cultural diversity element.

The drafting of clause (2) was directly influenced by Ambedkar's insistence that the non-discrimination principle in public education must be constitutional rather than statutory, so that state governments could not override it through communal reservation orders. The Madras Communal Government Order — which was before the Assembly as a concrete example of what states might do — was the immediate target of clause (2). The "only" qualifier was inserted after deliberation to ensure that merit-based selection criteria, which might produce community-disparate outcomes, were not automatically invalidated: the prohibition was targeted at explicit communal classification as the operative selection criterion, not at any outcome that correlated with community membership.

The relationship between clause (2) and the emerging Article 30 minority institution right was a source of tension that the Assembly did not fully resolve in the text. Members representing Christian missionary institutions and Islamic educational bodies argued that minority institutions receiving state aid should retain the right to give preference to their own community students — otherwise, Article 30 would be emptied of practical meaning if aided minority institutions were required to fill all seats on a purely non-discriminatory basis. The Drafting Committee's position — ultimately reflected in the "only" formulation in clause (2) — was that preference for the founding community in a minority institution is not a denial on the prohibited grounds but an exercise of Article 30 character, while systematic exclusion of all outsiders purely on religious or caste grounds would cross the constitutional line. The courts were left to draw the exact boundary.

## Key cases

**State of Madras v. Srimathi Champakam Dorairajan AIR 1951 SC 226** — The Madras Government's Communal Government Order reserved seats in state medical colleges on grounds of religion and caste. Champakam Dorairajan, a Brahmin woman, was denied admission despite higher marks than admitted candidates from other communities. The Supreme Court struck down the Order as a violation of Article 29(2): the denial of admission to a state-maintained institution was based only on religion and caste, the precisely prohibited grounds. The Court rejected the argument that Directive Principles could override Fundamental Rights. The case had an immediate constitutional consequence — the First Constitutional Amendment 1951 inserted Article 15(4), permitting special provisions for backward classes and Scheduled Castes and Tribes — making Champakam Dorairajan the progenitor of the entire constitutional framework for reservation in education.

**In Re Kerala Education Bill AIR 1958 SC 956** — This advisory opinion is the foundational pronouncement on the relationship between Articles 29 and 30. The Supreme Court held that a minority institution established under Article 30 may give preference in admissions to students from the founding minority community without violating Article 29(2), because such preference is not a denial on the grounds "only" of religion or language in the prohibited sense — it is an exercise of the institution's minority character. The Court also established that state regulation of minority institutions must be limited to ensuring educational standards and cannot extend to displacing the minority community's administrative control. This two-part ruling — on the compatibility of preferential admission with clause (2), and on the limits of state regulation under Article 30 — became the template for all subsequent educational rights adjudication.

**D.A.V. College v. State of Punjab AIR 1971 SC 1731** — The Supreme Court confirmed that Article 29(1) is not confined to minority communities. The Arya Samaj's claim to protect its distinct Sanskrit-based cultural heritage was recognised as a valid invocation of clause (1), establishing that "any section of citizens" includes groups that are numerically significant or regionally dominant. The case broadened the practical scope of the cultural conservation right and confirmed that the article's protective ambit extends to any section with a genuinely distinct cultural feature, regardless of whether it occupies a subordinate position in the national demographic hierarchy.

**T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka (2002) 8 SCC 481** — The eleven-judge Constitution Bench produced a comprehensive restatement of how Articles 29, 30, 14, 15, and 19(1)(g) interact in the context of private and minority professional educational institutions. On Article 29, the Court confirmed that minority status is determined on a state-wise basis and that the right to conserve culture under clause (1) includes the right to establish educational institutions as a vehicle for cultural transmission. On clause (2), the Court held that the non-discrimination guarantee applies fully to state-maintained and aided institutions but that private unaided institutions — whether minority or non-minority — have constitutional protection for their institutional autonomy that modifies how clause (2) operates in their context.

**P.A. Inamdar v. State of Maharashtra (2005) 6 SCC 537** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench held that the state cannot impose its reservation policy on unaided minority or non-minority private professional colleges. The ruling reinforced the distinction between state-maintained institutions — where Article 29(2)'s non-discrimination norm operates with full force alongside reservation obligations — and unaided private institutions, where constitutional autonomy substantially limits the state's ability to dictate admission composition. The case required the 93rd Constitutional Amendment inserting Article 15(5) for the state to impose any reservation requirement on unaided private institutions, confirming that such obligations cannot be derived from Article 29(2) alone.

**Pramati Educational and Cultural Trust v. Union of India (2014) 8 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court held that the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009, insofar as it required unaided minority schools to reserve 25 per cent of seats for economically weaker section students, could not be applied to minority institutions protected by Article 30. The Court held that the 25 per cent obligation would impinge on the minority institution's right to administer its admissions in a manner that preserves its character. The case illustrates the ongoing tension between Article 29(2)'s universalist non-discrimination norm and Article 30's minority-protection autonomy, and shows that the latter can, in the Court's analysis, shield minority institutions from at least some of the obligations that clause (2) might otherwise impose.

## Current significance

The intersection of Article 29(2) with reservation policy in state-aided educational institutions continues to generate litigation, particularly as states expand reservation in professional college admissions to include EWS categories under Articles 15(6) and 16(6). The Supreme Court's 2022 judgment in Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India upholding EWS reservation was partly framed by reference to Article 29(2), with the Court considering whether economic criteria for admission preference fall outside the clause's prohibitions, which list only religion, race, caste, and language. The interaction between expanding reservation categories and the clause (2) non-discrimination norm will continue to generate constitutional litigation.

The rights of linguistic minorities under Article 29(1) are increasingly pressed in the context of the National Education Policy 2020 and its emphasis on mother-tongue instruction. Several state governments have faced legal challenges from communities demanding instruction in Gondi, Tulu, Kokani, Bodo, and other regional languages at primary and secondary levels, relying on clause (1) alongside Article 350A. The adequacy of state provision for linguistic minorities — including migrant communities in metropolitan areas whose mother tongue differs from the dominant language of the state — is an area where judicial engagement with the positive dimension of the conservation right will be necessary.

The question of Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims presents an emerging issue with Article 29(1) dimensions. These communities — members of historically untouchable castes who converted to Christianity or Islam — argue that they retain their caste-based cultural and social identity despite religious conversion, and that the denial of Scheduled Caste status to them violates the constitutional framework. If SC status is recognised as a cultural and social designation under Article 29(1) rather than purely a Hindu-caste identity, the conservation right may support claims by these communities to the protections that attach to that cultural position. A Constitution Bench reference on this question has been pending for years.

The open constitutional question that Article 29 leaves unanswered is whether the right to conserve culture in clause (1) generates any enforceable positive obligation on the state to fund minority cultural and linguistic institutions beyond the narrow area of primary-level mother-tongue instruction, or whether it remains a purely negative right against suppression. As tribal and small linguistic communities face language death from demographic and economic pressure rather than explicit state prohibition, the value of a purely negative conservation right is increasingly questioned. Whether courts should read clause (1) as imposing a minimum positive obligation — perhaps extending the Directive Principle in Article 350A to secondary education and cultural institutions — or whether these are questions for the legislature is the central constitutional question Article 29(1) has not yet resolved.
