## What this Article does — and why it is inoperative

Article 124C was inserted by the Constitution (Ninety-ninth Amendment) Act, 2014, as the third and final article in the NJAC package. Where Article 124A constituted the NJAC and Article 124B defined its functions, Article 124C gave Parliament the authority to regulate the procedure for judicial appointments by ordinary legislation, and further empowered Parliament to authorise the NJAC to make its own internal regulations.

Like Articles 124A and 124B, Article 124C was declared unconstitutional and void in Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015). It remains in the printed constitutional text but has no legal effect.

## The scope of Article 124C's grant

Article 124C had two distinct elements:

**The parliamentary legislation power:** Parliament could enact a law regulating the procedure for appointing the Chief Justice of India, other Supreme Court judges, Chief Justices of High Courts, and other High Court judges. This was the basis for the National Judicial Appointments Commission Act, 2014, which Parliament enacted simultaneously with the constitutional amendment. The Act specified the detailed process by which the NJAC would receive, consider, and make recommendations for judicial appointments.

**The sub-delegation to the NJAC:** Parliament could also empower the NJAC to lay down regulations for the discharge of its own functions, the manner of selecting persons for appointment, and other matters the NJAC considered necessary. This sub-delegation was significant because it would have allowed the NJAC to elaborate its own procedures without requiring parliamentary amendment each time. The NJAC Act included such a delegation, but neither the Act nor the regulations came into operation following the 2015 judgment.

## The constitutional innovation

Article 124C represented a significant constitutional innovation because judicial appointment procedure had previously been governed by the collegium's own informal conventions, the Second Judges case (1993), the Third Judges case (1998), and a Memorandum of Procedure that was not publicly available in full form. Moving appointment procedure from judge-made convention to parliamentary statute — with further delegation to an independent commission — was framed by the amendment's proponents as bringing democratic accountability to a process that had been opaque and self-referential.

Critics argued that placing appointment procedure in parliamentary statute, with the potential for subsequent amendment by a simple majority, would make judicial independence vulnerable to legislative majorities in a way that judge-made convention and basic-structure entrenchment had prevented.

## Key cases

**Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v. Union of India (2015) — the NJAC case:** The Constitution Bench struck down the entire 99th Amendment, including Article 124C. The Court's analysis of Article 124C was principally that the power to empower Parliament to legislate appointment procedure could not be used to create a regime that violated judicial independence, and that the NJAC Act's provisions (built on the Article 124C grant) did precisely that. The Law Minister's membership of the NJAC, the veto powers of non-judicial members, and the absence of any requirement of judicial primacy in the NJAC's decision-making were collectively found to be incompatible with the basic structure.

**Special Reference No. 1 of 1998 (Third Judges case):** The advisory opinion that had established the collegium system for Supreme Court appointments. The 99th Amendment, including Article 124C, was Parliament's direct response to the Third Judges case — an attempt to re-involve the executive in the appointments process through constitutional amendment rather than executive action. The NJAC judgment restored the Third Judges case framework.

**Madras Bar Association v. Union of India (2021):** A subsequent case addressing tribunal reform, in which the Court again emphasised that Parliament's power to legislate the structure and procedure of judicial and quasi-judicial bodies is bounded by the basic structure, and specifically by the requirements of judicial independence — directly applying the principle established in the NJAC case.

## Parliament's residual power after the NJAC judgment

The striking down of Article 124C raised a question about whether Parliament retains any power to legislate judicial appointment procedure in the absence of the article. The Supreme Court's judgment in the NJAC case implicitly answered that the collegium system, as an aspect of the basic structure, cannot be displaced by ordinary parliamentary legislation. However, Parliament retains the ability to enact procedural requirements that are consistent with judicial primacy in appointments — such as transparency requirements, eligibility criteria, and timelines — without displacing the collegium's substantive decision-making power. Whether and how Parliament might do this without reconstituting the NJAC or something equivalent remains an open constitutional question.

## Current significance

The inoperative status of Article 124C leaves a gap in the constitutional text: the article that would have given Parliament explicit authority to legislate appointment procedure no longer has legal effect, but the general question of whether Parliament can legislate on the subject at all — and if so, within what limits — has not been definitively answered. The Memorandum of Procedure between the collegium and the government remains the operative governance document, but it is not a statute and has no binding legal character. Article 124C thus continues to mark the outer boundary of what Parliament attempted and the Supreme Court refused to sanction, making it a significant reference point in any future debate about the constitutional basis for judicial appointments reform.
