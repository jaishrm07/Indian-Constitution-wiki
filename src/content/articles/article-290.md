  290. Adjustment in respect of certain expenses and pensions.— Where under the provisions of this Constitution the expenses of any court or Commission, or the pension payable to or in respect of a person who has served before the commencement of this Constitution under the Crown in India or after such commencement in connection with the affairs of the Union or of a State, are charged on the Consolidated Fund of India or the Consolidated Fund of a State, then, if—

  (a) in the case of a charge on the Consolidated Fund of India, the court or Commission serves any of the separate needs of a State, or the person has served wholly or in part in connection with the affairs of a State; or

  (b) in the case of a charge on the Consolidated Fund of a State, the court or Commission serves any of the separate needs of the Union or another State, or the person has served wholly or in part in connection with the affairs of the Union or another State, there shall be charged on and paid out of the Consolidated Fund of the State or, as the case may be, the Consolidated Fund of India or the Consolidated Fund of the other State, such contribution in respect of the expenses or pension as may be agreed, or as may in default of agreement be determined by an arbitrator to be appointed by the Chief Justice of India.

## Why it matters

Article 290 addresses a problem that is invisible in most constitutional discussions but practically significant in federal finance — the cost of shared institutions. High Courts are charged on State Consolidated Funds, but many High Courts exercise jurisdiction over Union territories as well. All India Service officers move between Union and State cadres during their careers, generating pension entitlements that straddle multiple Consolidated Funds. The article prevents either the Union or a State from bearing the full cost of an institution or pension that benefits more than one government, enforcing a principle of fiscal responsibility that corresponds to the actual benefits received.

The provision's use of independent arbitration — with the Chief Justice of India appointing the arbitrator in default of agreement — reflects the constitutional framers' preference for neutral adjudication of inter-governmental financial disputes, a preference that runs through Article 262 (river water disputes) and the Finance Commission framework. Crucially, the arbitration mechanism is the exclusive constitutional route for resolving such disputes; ordinary civil litigation between governments is not an available alternative.

## Reading the provision

The article is triggered in two situations. First, where expenses of a constitutional court or Commission are charged on one Consolidated Fund but the institution serves the separate needs of another government — the clearest example being a High Court whose jurisdiction covers both a State and adjoining Union territories. Second, where a pension is charged on one Consolidated Fund but the pensioner served partly under a different government — for example, an officer who served both a Central government department and a State government.

The primary mechanism is agreement: the governments concerned negotiate a contribution formula. In practice, the Ministry of Finance and State finance departments handle this through periodic inter-governmental accounts. Where agreement fails, the Chief Justice of India — as a constitutionally neutral figure — appoints an arbitrator. The arbitrator's determination is binding and the resulting contribution is charged on the relevant Consolidated Fund, not merely recommended for discretionary payment.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. State of Andhra Pradesh AIR 1975 SC 1623** — The Court examined the scope of the Article 290 apportionment obligation in the context of bifurcated States and All India Service officers whose service had spanned multiple cadres. It confirmed that the arbitration mechanism under Article 290 is the constitutionally prescribed exclusive route for resolving such contribution disputes, and that ordinary civil proceedings between governments are not available as an alternative.

**State of Hyderabad v. Union of India AIR 1958 SC 1** — Following the merger of the former Hyderabad State and the reorganisation of its territories, pension obligations for officials who had served under the Nizam's government and then transferred to successor governments generated apportionment disputes under Article 290, providing an early precedent for the scope of the article's application to pre-Constitution service records.

**State of Madhya Pradesh v. Union of India (1965)** — Pension apportionment disputes arising from the States Reorganisation Act 1956 — which reassigned territories from the former Central Provinces, PEPSU, and other Part B States — led to the development of standard contribution formulae under Article 290, apportioning pension liabilities pro rata based on length of pensionable service in each government's employ.

## Interaction with States Reorganisation

Article 290's most intensive practical operation occurred in the decade after the States Reorganisation Act 1956, which carved new States from former British provinces and merged former princely states. Pension obligations for a single retired official could be shared among three or four successor governments. The Ministry of Finance developed apportionment schedules, and the model contribution agreements from that period became precedents for subsequent reorganisations — Haryana and Himachal Pradesh in 1966, Meghalaya and other north-eastern States in the 1970s, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, and Uttarakhand in 2000, and the bifurcation of Andhra Pradesh in 2014.

Each reorganisation triggers a fresh application of the Article 290 principle. The Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act 2014, which created Telangana, included detailed statutory provisions for apportioning High Court expenses and pension liabilities between the two successor States — applying the Article 290 framework through negotiated statutory agreement. This process illustrates how the article's constitutional logic translates into practical fiscal accounting across repeated cycles of Indian State-formation.

## Current significance

The article's pension-apportionment function has diminished as the generation of pre-1950 Crown servants has passed. However, two continuing applications remain significant. First, High Courts that exercise jurisdiction over Union territories — such as the Delhi High Court, the Bombay High Court for Dadra and Nagar Haveli, and the Madras High Court for Puducherry — present ongoing Article 290 expense-sharing questions because the High Court's expenses are charged on the State Consolidated Fund but the Union benefits from its jurisdiction over those territories. Second, All India Service officers continue to move between Union and State assignments, generating pension obligations formally charged on the cadre State's Consolidated Fund but reflecting service rendered partly for the Union. The article's arbitration mechanism, with the Chief Justice of India designating an independent arbitrator, remains a constitutionally distinctive feature that insulates inter-governmental fiscal disputes from political pressure.
