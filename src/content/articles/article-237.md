plainEnglish: >-
  Article 237 empowers the Governor to extend Chapter VI's judicial appointment and control framework — which automatically covers civil judicial posts — to any class or classes of magistrates in the State by public notification. The notification can apply the chapter's provisions with exceptions and modifications as the Governor specifies, and takes effect from the date fixed in the notification. The provision exists because the definition of "judicial service" in Article 236 covers civil judicial posts and does not automatically extend to the criminal magistracy. Governors exercising this power bring classes of magistrates under High Court administrative control, insulating their careers from the executive direction that would otherwise apply. Following the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973's mandatory separation of judicial and executive magistracy, most States have issued notifications under Article 237 to formalise the constitutional status of judicial magistrates under High Court control — but the CrPC separation itself is a statutory achievement, and without the Governor's notification, it would lack Article 235's constitutional protection.
officialText: 237. Application of the provisions of this Chapter to certain class or classes of magistrates.—The Governor may by public notification direct that the foregoing provisions of this Chapter and any rules made thereunder shall with effect from such date as may be fixed by him in that behalf apply in relation to any class or classes of magistrates in the State as they apply in relation to persons appointed to the judicial service of the State subject to such exceptions and modifications as may be specified in the notification.

## Why it matters

Article 237 addresses the fundamental question of where, in the constitutional architecture, the criminal magistracy belongs. In the Indian legal system, judicial magistrates exercise criminal judicial functions — conducting trials, taking cognizance of offences, remanding accused persons, recording confessions — that are comparable in importance and sensitivity to those of civil judges. Yet the definition of "judicial service" in Article 236 covers only civil judicial posts. Without Article 237, the entire criminal magistracy below the sessions judge level would fall outside the constitutional protections of Chapter VI, remaining within executive career management and subject to the same patronage risks that Article 235 was designed to eliminate for civil judges.

Article 237 was the Constitution's answer to this gap — not by automatically including magistrates in the judicial service, but by giving the Governor the power to extend the framework by notification. This permissive rather than mandatory approach reflected the complexity of the magistracy system at the time of the Constitution's commencement, where executive and judicial functions were combined in the same officer. The article created the constitutional mechanism for change while leaving the timing and extent to each State's political process.

The CrPC 1973 subsequently made the separation of judicial and executive magistracy compulsory, but that statutory separation is itself subject to legislative reversal. Governor notifications under Article 237 elevate the protection from a statutory policy choice to a constitutionally grounded arrangement — harder to undo and subject to constitutional review if challenged.

## Reading the provision

The provision operates through a public notification — an executive act by the Governor on the advice of the Council of Ministers, under the Shamsher Singh principle that the Governor acts on ministerial advice in all executive functions. The initiative for the notification therefore rests with the State government politically, even though the formal act is the Governor's.

The notification can specify exceptions and modifications — meaning Chapter VI need not be imported wholesale. A Governor's notification might, for instance, apply Article 235's control over postings and leave to judicial magistrates while providing a modified consultation framework for appointments that differs from the full Governor-PSC-High Court consultation required by Article 234. This flexibility allowed States to tailor the extension to the specific circumstances of their magistracy system as the separation from executive magistracy was implemented.

The phrase "any class or classes of magistrates" is potentially broad, but in practice notifications have been confined to the criminal magistracy — judicial magistrates and, in some States, metropolitan magistrates established under the CrPC for large urban areas. The article has never been used to extend Chapter VI to purely executive magistrates (who are IAS or State Civil Service officers) or to adjudicators in non-judicial statutory bodies.

## Key cases

**State of Assam v. Ranga Muhammad (1967)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that prior to a notification under Article 237, magistrates are not covered by the judicial service provisions, and their conditions of service remain in the executive domain. The Governor's notification power is discretionary, not obligatory — the article creates a power to invite the framework, not a duty to deploy it.

**Sushanta Kumar Bose v. High Court of Orissa (1968)** — The Orissa High Court considered whether judicial magistrates were already within the Chapter VI framework by virtue of their judicial functions, or whether a Governor's notification under Article 237 was required. The ruling affirmed that the notification is required, and that the High Court's Article 235 control does not automatically extend to judicial magistrates until the notification brings them within the framework.

**All India Judges' Association v. Union of India (2002)** — The Supreme Court's wide-ranging directions on the subordinate judiciary included reaffirmation of the requirement for States to maintain the separation between judicial and executive magistrates, and directed that judicial magistrates must be governed through the High Court rather than through the State secretariat. The judgment strengthened the constitutional case for all States to have Article 237 notifications in place covering their judicial magistracy.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974)** — The Supreme Court's ruling that the Governor acts on ministerial advice in executive functions is relevant to Article 237 notifications. The Governor's public notification extending Chapter VI to a class of magistrates is an executive act taken on ministerial advice — the political initiative rests with the State government.

## The separation of magistracies and Article 237's historical role

Before the CrPC 1973, the same individual could function simultaneously as a judicial magistrate and as an executive magistrate. In many district administrations, the District Magistrate — a member of the Indian Administrative Service — presided over both civil administration and significant elements of the criminal justice system. The Law Commission's 14th Report (1958) recommended separation of the two functions, and the CrPC 1973 enacted this separation nationally by dividing the magistracy into two streams: judicial magistrates coming under Sessions Court and High Court supervision, and executive magistrates remaining within the general administrative service.

States were required to implement the separation by 1975. Article 237 was the constitutional mechanism by which the newly separated judicial magistracy could be brought fully within the High Court's control framework under Chapter VI. Governor notifications issued during the transition period effectively constitutionalised the separation — placing judicial magistrates within the same governance framework as civil judges and district judges, and making it constitutionally protected rather than merely statutory.

## Current significance

Article 237 was most actively invoked during the transition period following the CrPC 1973. Today the provision is less frequently invoked, as the judicial-executive separation is largely complete across most States. Its continued relevance lies in two areas. First, it serves as a constitutional safety valve allowing extension of judicial independence protections to magistracy classes that remain vulnerable to executive direction. Second, the question of whether particular categories of newly created magistracy-equivalent posts — metropolitan magistrates under special statutes, executive magistrates exercising judicial functions in regulatory contexts — fall within earlier notifications, or require fresh notifications, continues to arise in service litigation.

Article 237 is the last article in Chapter VI, and its placement is structurally significant. The chapter builds from appointment (Articles 233-234) through control (Article 235) to definitions (Article 236) and then to extension (Article 237). This architecture reflects a careful constitutional design: the core framework is mandatory and applies automatically to the civil judicial service; the extension to the criminal magistracy is permissive and operates through executive action. The chapter acknowledges that the separation of judicial and executive functions at the criminal trial level is ultimately a political achievement, consolidated by constitutional mechanism — not a constitutional command that enforces itself without State action.
