officialText: |-
  198. Special procedure in respect of Money Bills.—(1) A Money Bill shall not be introduced in a Legislative Council.

  (2) After a Money Bill has been passed by the Legislative Assembly of a State having a Legislative Council, it shall be transmitted to the Legislative Council for its recommendations, and the Legislative Council shall within a period of fourteen days from the date of its receipt of the Bill return the Bill to the Legislative Assembly with its recommendations, and the Legislative Assembly may thereupon either accept or reject all or any of the recommendations of the Legislative Council.

  (3) If the Legislative Assembly accepts any of the recommendations of the Legislative Council, the Money Bill shall be deemed to have been passed by both Houses with the amendments recommended by the Legislative Council and accepted by the Legislative Assembly.

  (4) If the Legislative Assembly does not accept any of the recommendations of the Legislative Council, the Money Bill shall be deemed to have been passed by both Houses in the form in which it was passed by the Legislative Assembly without any of the amendments recommended by the Legislative Council.

  (5) If a Money Bill passed by the Legislative Assembly and transmitted to the Legislative Council for its recommendations is not returned to the Legislative Assembly within the said period of fourteen days, it shall be deemed to have been passed by both Houses at the expiration of the said period in the form in which it was passed by the Legislative Assembly.

## What this Article does

Article 198 is the state legislature's analog to Article 109, which governs Money Bills at the Union Parliament level. The structure and logic of both Articles is identical: Money Bills originate exclusively in the lower, directly elected chamber; the upper house has a strictly advisory role; and the lower chamber's will is decisive.

Under Article 198, a Money Bill can only be introduced in the Legislative Assembly (clause 1). Once the Assembly passes it, the Bill is transmitted to the Legislative Council, which has fourteen days to return it with recommendations (clause 2). The Assembly may accept or reject those recommendations in whole or in part. Whether the Assembly accepts, rejects, or simply receives no response within fourteen days, the Money Bill is deemed passed — in the Assembly's original form if the Council does not respond, or in the Assembly's form incorporating any Council recommendations the Assembly chose to accept (clauses 3–5). The Council cannot veto, amend, or delay a Money Bill beyond the fourteen-day window.

The rationale mirrors that at the Union level: control over taxation, appropriation, and public finance is treated as the exclusive domain of the elected chamber, which is directly accountable to the people for fiscal decisions. The Council's role is limited to suggesting improvements within a tight timeframe, not to exercising co-equal legislative authority over the State's finances.

The definition of what qualifies as a Money Bill for these purposes is set out in Article 199. The Speaker of the Legislative Assembly certifies whether a Bill is a Money Bill, and that certification is final under Article 199(3). This creates the same structural tension present at the Union level: a Speaker aligned with the ruling Assembly majority may certify Bills as Money Bills to bypass Council scrutiny.

## Key cases

**S. Ramachandra Rao v. Union of India (2012) — (Union-level parallel)** — The Supreme Court's analysis of the fourteen-day mandatory window under Article 109, the Union analog, confirmed that the time period is a constitutional imperative and that a Money Bill not returned within fourteen days is deemed passed as a matter of automatic constitutional operation, not by any further act of the House or the Governor. The same principle applies to Article 198 at the state level.

**Hoechst Pharmaceuticals Ltd. v. State of Bihar (1983)** — The Supreme Court, examining the validity of Bihar state tax legislation, confirmed that where a Money Bill is correctly certified and follows the Article 198 procedure, the resulting enactment is constitutionally valid even if the Council had reservations or returned it with recommendations that the Assembly declined to accept.

**Money Bill certification controversies — State Speakers** — In several states, Opposition parties have challenged the Speaker's certification of finance-related Bills as Money Bills, arguing that provisions inserted into State Finance Bills go beyond the Article 199 definition. Courts have generally applied the principle established in the Union-level Aadhaar litigation (K. S. Puttaswamy, 2019) — that the Speaker's certificate is entitled to significant deference but is not entirely beyond judicial review — though no state-level certification has been definitively struck down.

## Current significance

The practical importance of Article 198 at the state level has grown as State governments have used Money Bill certification to include substantive policy provisions in annual Finance Bills, bypassing Council scrutiny in bicameral states. This mirrors the controversy at the Union level over Finance Act amendments but arises in a different constitutional context: State Councils, unlike the Union's Rajya Sabha, cannot formally record or broadcast their objections to the same degree, making the misuse of the Money Bill route at the state level less publicly visible.

The fourteen-day window also creates administrative pressure. A State Finance Bill transmitted to the Council close to the end of the budget session may effectively expire the fourteen-day period during a recess, triggering the deemed-passage provision by default rather than by conscious legislative action. State governments and Council Chairpersons have periodically disputed whether periods when the Council is not in session count against the fourteen-day clock — an issue that has not been definitively resolved by the Supreme Court in the state-level context.
