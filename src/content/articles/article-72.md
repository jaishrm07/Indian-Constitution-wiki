  72. Power of President to grant pardons, etc., and to suspend, remit or commute sentences in certain cases.—(1) The President shall have the power to grant pardons, reprieves, respites or remissions of punishment or to suspend, remit or commute the sentence of any person convicted of any offence—

  (a) in all cases where the punishment or sentence is by a Court Martial;

  (b) in all cases where the punishment or sentence is for an offence against any law relating to a matter to which the executive power of the Union extends;

  (c) in all cases where the sentence is a sentence of death.

  (2) Nothing in sub-clause (a) of clause (1) shall affect the power conferred by law on any officer of the Armed Forces of the Union to suspend, remit or commute a sentence passed by a Court Martial.

  (3) Nothing in sub-clause (c) of clause (1) shall affect the power to suspend, remit or commute a sentence of death exercisable by the Governor of a State under any law for the time being in force.

## Why it matters

Executive clemency is one of the oldest prerogatives of sovereign power — the correction of judicial outcomes by the state in the name of mercy, justice, or public policy. The Constituent Assembly included Article 72 deliberately, recognising that the judicial process, however careful, produces outcomes that sometimes demand correction: new evidence emerges after conviction, the prisoner's circumstances change dramatically, a judicial error is suspected but not correctable through appeal, or the collective conscience of the nation concludes that a punishment, though legally valid, ought not to be carried out. The President's pardon power provides a constitutional safety valve for all these situations.

The death penalty jurisdiction in clause (1)(c) is the article's most significant and most contested application. Every person sentenced to death in India — regardless of the offence, the court, or whether the case arose under Union or State law — can petition the President for clemency under Article 72. This gives the President a nationwide, universal mercy jurisdiction over capital punishment. The President's decision on such petitions is therefore the final constitutional act before the state takes a human life.

## Reading the provision

The article distinguishes five forms of clemency: pardon (completely absolves the conviction and removes all disabilities), reprieve (delays execution to allow further proceedings), respite (substitution of a lesser sentence for a specific reason, such as pregnancy), remission (reduction of the amount of punishment without changing its character), and commutation (substitution of a less severe form of punishment). These are not interchangeable — a pardon erases the conviction entirely while a commutation from death to life imprisonment still leaves a conviction and a sentence in place.

Clause (2) preserves the pre-existing statutory powers of armed forces officers to exercise clemency in court-martial cases, ensuring that the President's overarching jurisdiction does not extinguish more immediate military disciplinary processes. Clause (3) similarly preserves the Governor's concurrent jurisdiction under Article 161 to commute or remit a death sentence under applicable State law — so both the President and the Governor may in principle have jurisdiction over a particular death sentence, depending on the law under which the conviction arose.

In practice, the Ministry of Home Affairs processes mercy petitions and prepares Cabinet recommendations before the President acts. The petition travels from the convict to the State Government for its views, then to the Ministry of Home Affairs, which prepares a note for the Cabinet, and finally to the President with a Cabinet recommendation. This bureaucratic journey can, as the Supreme Court has documented, take years or even decades.

## Key cases

**Maru Ram v. Union of India (1980)** — A Constitution Bench held that the power under Article 72 is not a personal prerogative of the President but must be exercised on ministerial advice under Article 74. The Court clarified the scope of "sentence of death" under clause (1)(c) to include death sentences imposed by any court in India, not only by Central law courts, and held that this jurisdiction could not be whittled down by Parliament to exclude particular categories of convict.

**Kehar Singh v. Union of India (1989)** — One of the most politically significant mercy-petition cases in Indian history. Kehar Singh, convicted for the assassination of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, sought a pardon. The Supreme Court held that the scope of judicial review of Article 72 decisions is narrow: courts will not sit in appeal over the merits of a clemency decision and cannot substitute their view of what the proper outcome should be. However, the Court confirmed that review is available if the decision was made without application of mind, on extraneous considerations, or in a manner that is wholly arbitrary or mala fide. The petition was rejected, and Kehar Singh was executed.

**Epuru Sudhakar v. Government of Andhra Pradesh (2006)** — A remission granted to a convict on apparent political grounds was challenged. The Supreme Court held that pardons and remissions granted under Articles 72 and 161 are judicially reviewable and that grants made on extraneous political considerations or in breach of constitutional principles can be struck down. The judgment expanded the scope of judicial oversight of the clemency process, treating it as a facet of the rule of law.

**Shatrughan Chauhan v. Union of India (2014)** — A landmark ruling on the rights of death-row prisoners awaiting Presidential mercy decisions. The Court held that inordinate, unexplained delay in deciding a mercy petition is itself a violation of Article 21 and can commute a death sentence to life imprisonment. The Court catalogued systematic failures — mercy petitions pending for ten, fifteen, or more years — and issued directions requiring the government to process petitions within a defined time frame. The judgment transformed the clemency process from a purely discretionary executive act into one subject to constitutional time-discipline.

## Current significance

The Shatrughan Chauhan directions have made the Supreme Court a de facto supervisory body over the Presidential clemency process in capital cases. Petitions challenging delay in mercy decisions now reach the Court regularly, and several death sentences have been commuted on this ground. The tension between the Cabinet's prerogative to recommend rejection and the prisoner's Article 21 right against cruel, degrading, or disproportionate punishment plays out in ongoing litigation.

A deeper constitutional question remains unresolved: whether the mandatory death penalty — applicable in certain terrorism and narcotics offences without judicial discretion to impose a lesser sentence — is compatible with Article 21. If the courts eventually hold that mandatory capital punishment is unconstitutional, the Article 72 clemency process will become the principal forum for relief in those cases until Parliament amends the relevant statutes. The broader question of whether the death penalty itself can survive Article 21 scrutiny is periodically raised and has not yet been definitively answered by a Constitution Bench.
