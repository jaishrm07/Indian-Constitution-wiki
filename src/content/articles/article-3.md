## What this Article does

Article 3 is the primary engine of State reorganisation within India. It authorises Parliament to form new States by separating territory from existing States, merge States, increase or diminish a State's area, alter its boundaries, or change its name — all by ordinary legislation requiring only a simple majority, not the special majority demanded by Article 368. The proviso requires that the relevant State legislature be given a period to express its views before the Bill is introduced, but that opinion is advisory: Parliament may proceed even if the State legislature objects.

## The ordinary-law mechanism

One of the most constitutionally distinctive features of Article 3 is that the power it confers — to restructure the federal map — is exercised by an ordinary Bill, not a constitutional amendment. This means no two-thirds majority, no ratification by State legislatures, and no special procedural hurdles beyond the proviso's consultation requirement. The Constituent Assembly made this choice deliberately: given the scale of reorganisation needed after Partition and the expectation of further linguistic-reorganisation demands, requiring constitutional amendments for every boundary change would have made the process impossibly cumbersome.

Article 4 reinforces this design by declaring that laws under Article 3 that amend the First and Fourth Schedules are not "amendments of the Constitution" for purposes of Article 368.

## Consultation, not consent

The proviso mandates that the President refer the reorganisation Bill to the affected State legislature "for expressing its views thereon." The legislature gets a specified period to respond. But the Supreme Court has consistently held that this is a consultation — Parliament is not bound by the State's views. In practice, State legislatures opposing reorganisation (as Andhra Pradesh did over Telangana) have seen their objections overridden. This procedural asymmetry is one of the sharpest expressions of Indian federalism's centripetal character.

## Key cases

**Babulal Parate v. State of Bombay (1960) AIR 1960 SC 51** — The Supreme Court upheld the States Reorganisation Act, 1956, confirming that Parliament can form new States without the consent of existing States and that the proviso's "views" requirement is satisfied even if those views are overridden.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — The Court held that the word "law" in Article 3 means an Act of Parliament passed in the ordinary way and that the provision does not require a constitutional amendment. The judgment settled any residual doubt about the legislative route for reorganisation.

**Union of India v. Sukumar Sengupta (1990) 1 SCC 428** — The President's reference of a reorganisation bill to State legislatures is a mandatory procedural step; failure to follow it would be a jurisdictional defect. But the period allowed for the State to express views is within the President's discretion.

**Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act proceedings (2013–14)** — When Parliament passed the Act creating Telangana, the constitutional validity was challenged on the ground that the Andhra Pradesh legislature had resolved against bifurcation. Courts consistently held that Article 3 makes the State's views advisory, vindicating Parliament's plenary power.

## Major reorganisations under Article 3

The article has been used in almost every decade since 1950. The States Reorganisation Act, 1956 redrew the map on linguistic lines, creating or altering about 14 States. Subsequent reorganisations produced Nagaland (1963), Haryana and Chandigarh (1966), Himachal Pradesh (1971), Meghalaya, Manipur, and Tripura as full States (1972), Mizoram and Arunachal Pradesh (1987), Uttarakhand, Chhattisgarh, and Jharkhand (2000), and Telangana (2014). Each was effected by ordinary legislation.

The most constitutionally contested use was the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019, which bifurcated the State of Jammu and Kashmir into two Union territories. Critics argued that downgrading a State to a Union territory went beyond the ordinary reorganisation power — particularly because Explanation I of Article 3 states that "State" includes a Union territory in clauses (a) to (e) but not in the proviso, meaning the affected J&K legislature was not consulted (Parliament itself served as J&K's legislature at the relevant time since President's Rule was in force). The Supreme Court upheld the reorganisation in In re Article 370 (2023).

## Pending demands

Multiple demands for new States remain politically active: Vidarbha (from eastern Maharashtra), Saurashtra (from Gujarat), Gorkhaland (from West Bengal's Darjeeling hills), Bodoland (from Assam), Maru Pradesh (from Rajasthan), and Harit Pradesh (from western Uttar Pradesh). Each such demand must ultimately pass through the Article 3 process, with Parliament retaining the final word.

## Current significance

Article 3 sits at the intersection of territorial sovereignty, federalism, and democracy. Its design — powerful Centre, consultative States — reflects the framers' priority of national unity over federal autonomy. The J&K reorganisation has pushed the question of whether the article's ordinary-law mechanism can be used to permanently downgrade a State's constitutional status back onto the docket of constitutional interpretation.
