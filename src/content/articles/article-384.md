## What this Article did

Article 384 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that dealt with the Council of Ministers for Governors during the transitional period. It has been omitted.

Just as Article 381 dealt with the continuation of the central Council of Ministers, Article 384 dealt with State-level Councils of Ministers. Under the Constitution, each State has a Council of Ministers headed by the Chief Minister to aid and advise the Governor (Article 163). When the Constitution came into force, the States already had functioning governments — led by Chief Ministers and Councils of Ministers who had been formed under the Government of India Act, 1935 framework.

Article 384 gave constitutional sanction to the continuation of these existing State Councils of Ministers as the constitutional Councils of Ministers of the corresponding States, without requiring fresh appointment processes. Chief Ministers like Govind Ballabh Pant (UP), B C Roy (West Bengal), and Chakravarti Rajagopalachari (Madras) continued in office under the new constitutional framework.

## Why it was omitted

Article 384 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. By 1956, all State governments had been reconstituted after elections held in 1951-52 and the transitional Council of Ministers provisions had been entirely spent.

## Historical context

The continuity of State Councils of Ministers was essential for the governance of each State from 26 January 1950. Without this provision, there would have been a legal gap in the authority of State governments to function on the day of constitutional commencement. The existing Chief Ministers and Cabinets had been conducting governance under the dominion framework, and Article 384 validated their continuation under the new constitutional framework.

This provision illustrated a pattern throughout Part XXI's transitional articles: the framers recognised that institutions at every level — central and State — needed uninterrupted constitutional grounding on the day of commencement.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge to the validity of transitional State Councils of Ministers is recorded.

## Current significance

Article 384 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in establishing the constitutional validity of State governments during the transition period from the dominion framework to the Republic, ensuring that each State's executive was constitutionally grounded from 26 January 1950.

The governance of States during the two years between constitutional commencement (1950) and the first general elections (1951-52) was a period of extraordinary activity. State governments enacted major land reform legislation, established educational institutions, and managed post-partition demographic and economic challenges — all under the constitutional validity conferred by Article 384 on the transitional Councils of Ministers. The legislative and executive acts of these governments during 1950-1952 form a substantial part of the legal and institutional infrastructure of modern India.

The zamindari abolition laws enacted by States like Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and Madhya Pradesh during this period — which abolished hereditary landlordism and vested agricultural land in cultivators — were among the most socially significant acts of the early Republic. These laws were subsequently challenged before the Supreme Court on the grounds that they violated fundamental rights. The Court's judgments in these cases, particularly State of Bihar v Maharajadhiraja Sir Kameshwar Singh (1952), shaped the entire early constitutional jurisprudence on property rights. The constitutional validity of the State governments that enacted these laws derived in part from the transitional framework of Article 384.
