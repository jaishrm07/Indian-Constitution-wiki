  176. Special address by the Governor.—(1) At the commencement of the first session after each general election to the Legislative Assembly and at the commencement of the first session of each year, the Governor shall address the Legislative Assembly or, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, both Houses assembled together and inform the Legislature of the causes of its summons.

## What this Article does

Article 176 requires the Governor to deliver a special address to the State Legislature at the commencement of the first session after each general election to the Legislative Assembly and at the commencement of the first session of each year. This obligatory address — the State equivalent of the President's Address to Parliament under Article 87 — informs the Legislature of the government's policy agenda and the reasons for which it has been summoned. The article also mandates that legislative rules provide time for discussion of the address, ensuring the elected house can formally deliberate on the government programme.

## The mandatory character of the obligation

Article 176 uses the word "shall" — "the Governor shall address" — in deliberate contrast to Article 175, which uses "may." The special address is not an act of gubernatorial discretion but a constitutional obligation. A Governor who refuses to deliver the address prepared by the state government, or who delivers a fundamentally altered version of it, acts outside constitutional authority. High Courts and the Supreme Court have consistently held that the Governor has no independent power to alter the ministerially approved address text.

The timing requirements are equally non-discretionary: the address must occur at the commencement of the first session after each general election and at the commencement of the first session of each year. A Governor cannot postpone the first session as a device to avoid the Article 176 obligation, and the summoning of that session is itself governed by Article 174.

## What the address contains

The address informs the Legislature of the "causes of its summons" — that is, the government's programme and legislative priorities for the session. In practice, the address is a policy document prepared entirely by the Council of Ministers outlining the government's achievements and plans, read aloud by the Governor in a ceremonial joint sitting of the two Houses (where both exist) or the Assembly alone. The Governor reads the address as a constitutional duty, not as a personal statement of views. The address is then debated by the legislature under clause (2)'s time-allocation mechanism, and the government must respond to the debate, typically through a motion of thanks.

## Clause (2): Mandatory time allocation

Clause (2) obliges the legislature's procedural rules to make provision for time to discuss the address. This is not left to the discretion of the ruling party or the Speaker — the rules must build in discussion time. In practice, state legislatures follow the convention established by their rules of procedure, which typically provide for a motion of thanks to the Governor's address with several days of debate, mirroring the Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha practice under Article 87.

## Comparison with Article 87

Article 176 is substantially identical in structure to Article 87, which governs the President's special address to Parliament. The parallel is intentional: the Constituent Assembly designed the State Legislature's constitutional framework as a scaled-down but structurally consonant version of the Union Parliament framework. Both articles use the mandatory "shall," both require the address at the first post-election session and at the first session of each year, and both require time allocation for discussion. The Governor's address under Article 176 is accordingly subject to the same interpretive principles that apply to the President's address under Article 87, including the principle that content is determined by the elected government.

## Key cases

**Shamsher Singh v State of Punjab (1974)** — The Court's broad holding that the Governor acts on the aid and advice of the Council of Ministers in all non-discretionary matters directly encompasses the special address under Article 176. The content and timing of the address is determined by the government, not by the Governor unilaterally.

**Kerala Governor address controversy (2023)** — The Kerala Governor declined to read out portions of the address prepared by the state government, substituting his own text on some matters. The Kerala High Court and commentators held that this exceeded the Governor's constitutional authority. The episode prompted legislative and legal responses about the enforceability of the Article 176 obligation.

**State of Tamil Nadu v Governor of Tamil Nadu** — Judicial observations in proceedings related to the Tamil Nadu Governor's conduct affirmed that mandatory constitutional acts under articles such as Article 176 cannot be withheld or delayed, as they are obligations rather than discretions vested in the Governor.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Court's analysis of the limits of gubernatorial discretion, read with the Shamsher Singh principle, establishes the framework within which Article 176 must be understood: where the Constitution says "shall," the Governor's personal views are constitutionally irrelevant.

## Current significance

Several Governors have withheld or modified the address prepared by the state government, leading to constitutional confrontations at the start of legislative sessions. These episodes have revived academic and judicial debate about whether Article 176 creates a true obligation enforceable by courts against the Governor, and the emerging consensus from court observations is that the special address is a non-discretionary constitutional duty that must be performed in the form advised by the Council of Ministers. The question of what remedy is available when a Governor refuses — contempt, mandamus, or some other constitutional mechanism — remains unsettled, and further judicial clarification is expected as governor-government tensions persist.
