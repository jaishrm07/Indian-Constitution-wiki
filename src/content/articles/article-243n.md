  243N. Continuance of existing laws and Panchayats.—

  Notwithstanding anything in this Part, any provision of any law relating to Panchayats in force in a State immediately before the commencement of the Constitution (Seventy-third Amendment) Act, 1992, which is inconsistent with the provisions of this Part, shall continue to be in force until amended or repealed by a competent Legislature or other competent authority or until the expiration of one year from such commencement, whichever is earlier:

  Provided that all the Panchayats existing immediately before such commencement shall continue till the expiration of their duration, unless sooner dissolved by a resolution passed to that effect by the Legislative Assembly of that State or, in the case of a State having a Legislative Council, by each House of the Legislature of that State.

## Why it matters

Article 243N performs the essential function of managing the transition from the pre-1992 state-law Panchayati Raj framework to the new constitutional framework of Part IX. The 73rd Amendment was a transformative constitutional intervention: it took Panchayats from being purely statutory creatures of state law to being constitutional institutions with mandatory structures, elected compositions, reserved seats, fixed terms, and independent election commissions. This transformation could not happen instantaneously without disrupting the many Panchayats that were already functioning under state law at the time of the Amendment's commencement.

The article's structure balances two concerns. First, it respects the legitimate expectations of existing Panchayat bodies and their members by allowing them to serve out their terms. Second, it sets a hard deadline — one year from commencement — for states to bring their Panchayat laws into conformity with Part IX. Any provision of existing state Panchayat law that was inconsistent with Part IX would automatically lapse after that one-year period even if the state legislature had not yet enacted conforming legislation. This created a powerful incentive for states to act quickly in amending their laws.

The practical effect of Article 243N was felt most sharply in states with long-standing Panchayati Raj laws — such as Maharashtra's Zilla Parishads and Panchayat Samitis Act, Kerala's Panchayati Raj Act, and Karnataka's Zilla Parishads Act — which required substantial revision to conform with the reservation requirements, election commission requirements, and Finance Commission requirements of Part IX. Most states completed or substantially advanced this legislative work within the one-year window.

## How courts use it

Courts have used Article 243N in two principal ways. First, they have treated it as a negative rule: after the expiry of the one-year transitional period (i.e., after April 1994), no provision of pre-existing state Panchayat law that was inconsistent with Part IX could be relied upon. Attempts by state governments to continue functioning under old law frameworks that were inconsistent with Part IX — for example, by maintaining Panchayat bodies whose composition did not meet Article 243D's reservation requirements — could not succeed after that deadline.

Second, courts have used the provisory clause of Article 243N to distinguish between the continuation of pre-existing Panchayat bodies (which the proviso protects until their terms expire) and the application of the new constitutional requirements to those bodies. A Panchayat that was mid-term when the 73rd Amendment commenced was entitled to complete its term under Article 243N, but elections for the subsequent term had to comply fully with Part IX.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — While predating the 73rd Amendment, the Supreme Court's analysis of the relationship between Parliament's constitutional amendment power and state laws in this case established principles about transitional validity that were applied in the Part IX context. The general principle — that unconstitutional state laws in the field covered by a subsequent constitutional amendment have a transitional life only to the extent the amendment expressly saves them — underlies Article 243N's operation.

**Kishan Singh v. State of Haryana (1993)** — Early High Court decisions addressing the commencement and transitional effect of the 73rd Amendment applied the Article 243N framework to specific aspects of Haryana's pre-existing Panchayat laws, clarifying the one-year sunset provision's operation and confirming that Part IX requirements applied fully to elections held after the transitional period.

**State of Uttarakhand v. Balwant Singh Chaufal (2010)** — The Supreme Court's discussion of Panchayat accountability and the obligations of state governments under Part IX treated the constitutional framework as having fully superseded the pre-Amendment legal landscape. The Court's reasoning confirmed that Article 243N's transitional savings no longer had any ongoing effect and that state Panchayat law must be read entirely in conformity with Part IX.

## Current significance

Article 243N's direct transitional effect exhausted itself by April 1994. Its ongoing relevance is principally interpretive: it confirms that the pre-1992 state-law era of Panchayati Raj is constitutionally superseded by Part IX, and that any attempt to revert to pre-Amendment frameworks — for example, through state laws that effectively reconstruct the pre-Amendment system without the constitutional safeguards — would be unconstitutional.

The provision also has residual significance in the context of states that voluntarily opt in to Part IX under Article 243M(4)(a) — that is, states like Nagaland that are currently excluded from Part IX and might in the future pass the required special majority resolution to extend Part IX to themselves. When such a state opts in, its pre-opt-in Panchayat laws would have the same transitional protection that Article 243N gave to states covered by the original 73rd Amendment, giving the state a one-year period to conform its laws to Part IX requirements.
