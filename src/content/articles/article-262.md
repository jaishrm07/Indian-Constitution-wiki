  When States fight over how to share river water that crosses their borders, Parliament can set up special tribunals to decide those disputes outside the ordinary court system. Parliament can also pass a law saying that even the Supreme Court cannot hear these water allocation disputes — an extraordinary constitutional permission that recognises the technical, political, and hydrological complexity of river-sharing conflicts. In practice, the Inter-State River Water Disputes Act 1956 does exactly this: it creates ad hoc tribunals composed of retired Supreme Court judges for major disputes such as Cauvery, Krishna, Narmada, and Ravi-Beas. The result is a hybrid system in which specialist tribunals decide the substance of water sharing while the Supreme Court retains a watchdog role over procedural legality and implementation.

## Why it matters

Water sharing is among the most intractable of India's inter-State disputes. Rivers flow across State boundaries without regard to administrative maps, and every drop allocated to one State is a drop unavailable to another. The disputes are simultaneously hydrological (how much water is there?), agricultural (who needs it when?), historical (what pre-existing uses and rights exist?), and political (which State government can afford to compromise?). These characteristics make them poorly suited to ordinary adversarial court litigation, which takes evidence, applies legal precedent, and delivers binary verdicts.

Article 262 reflects the framers' recognition of this poor fit. The provision authorises Parliament to create specialist tribunals rather than leaving water disputes to the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction under Article 131 or to the general judicial system. More strikingly, it permits Parliament to oust even the Supreme Court's jurisdiction — a constitutional permission the framers extended with full awareness of its exceptional character. Article 32 makes the Supreme Court the guarantor of fundamental rights and generally protects its jurisdiction from legislative override. Article 262(2), with its "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" language, creates a narrow exception: for this specific category of dispute, Parliament may exclude even the Supreme Court. The framers accepted this exception because they judged that technical water disputes needed a mechanism insulated from the Court's general docket, and because they trusted Parliament to build the right alternative mechanism.

The practical stakes are enormous. The Cauvery dispute has periodically produced violent protests across Tamil Nadu and Karnataka. The Krishna waters dispute involves the agricultural economies of Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Andhra Pradesh/Telangana. The Narmada dispute brought together Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Maharashtra, and Rajasthan in decades of negotiation. The Ravi-Beas dispute between Punjab and Haryana has remained a live political crisis since 1986. Article 262 is not an abstract constitutional provision — it is the foundation on which the entire inter-State water dispute resolution system rests, and its practical adequacy is tested daily.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — the enabling power:**

"Parliament may by law provide for the adjudication of any dispute or complaint with respect to the use, distribution or control of the waters of, or in, any inter-State river or river valley."

Parliament "may" — it is not required to. Parliament exercised this enabling power through the Inter-State River Water Disputes Act 1956 (ISRWDA). The subject matter is broadly defined: disputes about "use, distribution or control" of inter-State river waters. This covers allocation of flows among States, storage in reservoirs, groundwater drawing affecting inter-State rivers, flood management, and environmental flow requirements. The reference to "river valley" extends the provision beyond the river's physical course to the entire hydrological catchment, meaning disputes about land use and water management within a catchment that affects river flows are in principle within scope.

**Clause (2) — the jurisdiction-ouster provision:**

"Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, Parliament may by law provide that neither the Supreme Court nor any other court shall exercise jurisdiction in respect of any such dispute or complaint as is referred to in clause (1)."

"Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" is the strongest possible override language in the Constitution. It expressly overrides even Article 32 (the Supreme Court's fundamental rights jurisdiction) and Article 131 (the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction in inter-State disputes). Parliament has exercised this power in the ISRWDA: Section 11 of the Act bars courts, including the Supreme Court, from exercising jurisdiction over disputes that have been referred to or are before a Tribunal constituted under the Act.

Courts have not read this as an absolute exclusion. The Supreme Court has consistently held that it retains supervisory jurisdiction to: (a) determine whether a dispute falls within the ISRWDA's scope; (b) examine whether a Tribunal has exceeded its terms of reference; and (c) enforce Tribunal awards that have been gazetted and thereby acquire the force of Supreme Court orders. This supervisory residue — though contested in its exact scope — means the jurisdiction ouster operates on the merits of water allocation disputes, not on all judicial oversight of the Tribunal system.

## The Inter-State River Water Disputes Act 1956

Parliament exercised the Article 262(1) power through the ISRWDA, which:

- Creates a framework under which any State may make a request to the Central Government to refer a dispute with another State about an inter-State river to a Tribunal.
- Bars States from making independent references to the Supreme Court under Article 131 once a Tribunal has been constituted or a request has been made.
- Constitutes Tribunals of retired Supreme Court judges appointed by the Central Government. Each Tribunal is ad hoc — created for the specific dispute.
- Requires Tribunals to give awards that, once published in the Official Gazette by the Central Government, have the force of an order of the Supreme Court and are binding on the parties.
- Sets (in the amended ISRWDA) time limits for Tribunal proceedings — the 2002 amendments introduced a three-year outer time limit for final awards, later adjusted by the 2019 amendments.

Active and completed Tribunals include: the Krishna Water Disputes Tribunal (1969, reconstituted 2004), the Narmada Water Disputes Tribunal (1969, award 1979), the Ravi-Beas Water Tribunal (1986), the Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal (1990, final award 2007), the Vansadhara Water Disputes Tribunal (2010), and the Mahadayi Water Disputes Tribunal (2010, award 2018).

The Inter-State River Water Disputes (Amendment) Act 2019 sought to consolidate the multiple ad hoc Tribunals into a single permanent body with specialised benches, with a technical expert committee supporting each bench, and a two-year outer limit for awards. The implementation of the 2019 amendments has been delayed.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

B.R. Ambedkar and Alladi Krishnaswami Ayyar both participated in the debates on what became Article 262. The framers had the Indus Waters dispute and the South Indian river disputes in mind as examples of the technical and political complexity that made ordinary court adjudication unsuitable. The decision to permit Parliament to oust Supreme Court jurisdiction was debated. Some members expressed concern that ousting the apex court's jurisdiction from a whole category of inter-governmental disputes set a dangerous precedent. The framers ultimately accepted the ouster power, convinced that specialist tribunals composed of senior judges would provide adequate adjudicative quality, and that the technical nature of river hydrology required an institutional format different from a general court. The provision reflects a deliberate constitutional choice to prioritise specialist adjudication over hierarchical court integration for this specific subject.

## Key cases

**State of Karnataka v. State of Tamil Nadu (Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal — multiple Supreme Court rounds, 1991–2018)** — The Cauvery dispute produced one of the longest-running and most complex threads of Supreme Court litigation in Indian constitutional history. The Court directed the Union Government to constitute a Tribunal (1990), enforced interim allocations over Karnataka's and Tamil Nadu's objections, examined the Tribunal's jurisdiction over Puducherry's claims, and ultimately reviewed the final Tribunal award in 2018, modifying allocations. The litigation confirmed that the jurisdiction-ouster provision bars the Court from adjudicating water allocation de novo but does not prevent it from directing the Union to constitute a Tribunal, from enforcing Tribunal orders, or from reviewing the Tribunal's legal framework.

**Re: Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal (1992) Supp 1 SCC 44** — The Supreme Court clarified the limits of Article 262(2)'s jurisdiction ouster, holding that a Tribunal's refusal to exercise jurisdiction or a manifest jurisdictional error going to the root of the matter could still attract supervisory review. The clause bars ordinary hearing of the merits of water allocation disputes, not all judicial oversight of the Tribunal's functioning. This case remains the leading authority on the residual supervisory jurisdiction that the Supreme Court retains despite the ouster provision.

**State of Karnataka v. State of Andhra Pradesh (2000) 9 SCC 572** — The Court, dealing with Karnataka's challenge to the Krishna Waters Tribunal's allocation, examined whether the Tribunal had exceeded its terms of reference. The judgment confirmed that Article 262(2)'s ouster operates on the merits of water sharing disputes, not on jurisdictional questions about the Tribunal's mandate. It also confirmed that once a Tribunal's award is gazetted as having the force of a Supreme Court order, the Court can enforce it through contempt jurisdiction and directions to the Central Government.

**State of Haryana v. State of Punjab (SYL Canal — various proceedings including (2002) 2 SCC 507)** — The Supreme Court's repeated interventions in the Sutlej-Yamuna Link canal dispute illustrated the tension between the jurisdiction-ouster provision and the Court's enforcement role. Punjab's construction of the canal was ordered by the Ravi-Beas Tribunal, and the Supreme Court directed completion. Punjab's subsequent statutory action in 2004 — the Punjab Termination of Agreements Act — unilaterally terminating water-sharing agreements was struck down by the Supreme Court as unconstitutional. The Court held that implementation and enforcement of a Tribunal award — as distinct from adjudication of the underlying water dispute — raises constitutional obligation questions that remain within the Court's purview.

**State of Tamil Nadu v. Union of India (Cauvery implementation, 2016)** — The Supreme Court, exercising its Article 32 jurisdiction on petitions connected to the Cauvery dispute, directed the Union Government to notify the Cauvery Management Board, which Parliament had been required to constitute under the Cauvery Tribunal's 2007 award. The Court held that the Union's failure to notify the Board was a failure to implement a constitutional obligation, which the jurisdiction-ouster provision did not insulate from judicial direction.

## Current significance

Several Tribunal awards remain partially unimplemented. The Cauvery final award (modified by the Supreme Court in 2018) has not been fully implemented, with Karnataka and Tamil Nadu in continuing dispute over seasonal flows and drought-year reductions. The Mahadayi award (2024) — allocating waters of the Mahadayi river among Goa, Karnataka, and Maharashtra — immediately generated State-level protests and demands for political intervention, testing the award implementation framework.

The ISRWDA (Amendment) Act 2019's proposal to replace all ad hoc Tribunals with a single permanent body with multiple benches remains pending in full implementation. The proposal has been supported by the Inter-State Council and by successive Finance Commissions as a mechanism for faster resolution and better institutional consistency but has encountered political resistance from States concerned about losing the ability to have their disputes decided by Tribunals constituted at their request.

Climate-driven variability in river flows is generating structural pressure on the Tribunal award system. Several Tribunal awards were calibrated on historical flow data that climate change is systematically invalidating — the Cauvery basin, the Krishna basin, and the Narmada basin all show changes in seasonal distribution and total annual flow that are undermining the assumptions built into existing awards. The ISRWDA's framework for reviewing or modifying Tribunal awards in light of changed circumstances is underdeveloped, and Article 262's silence on periodic review is emerging as a constitutional gap in the water dispute resolution architecture.
