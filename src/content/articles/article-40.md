## Why it matters

Article 40 is the constitutional expression of Gandhian decentralisation: the idea that democracy in India must be rooted in the village and that the State must actively organise and empower rural local self-government. The Constituent Assembly debate on this provision reflected a genuine tension between the Nehruvian vision of a planned, centralised developmental state and the Gandhian vision of village self-sufficiency and gram swaraj. By placing village panchayats in Part IV rather than Part III, the Constitution's framers preserved the centralising developmental state as the primary driver of national policy while acknowledging that it must ultimately organise itself in ways that devolve power to the villages.

The provision is constitutionally unique within Part IV in that it addresses democratic governance rather than economic or social welfare. Where most of Part IV concerns economic rights and social welfare, Article 40 focuses on democratic architecture — specifically on creating institutions close enough to the people to make governance participatory and accountable at the grassroots level. This was not a peripheral concern for the framers; it reflected a considered view that a centralised administrative state would be unable to deliver justice and development to India's vast rural population.

For the first four decades after independence, Article 40 remained largely dormant as a directive. The Balwant Rai Mehta Committee Report of 1957 — the first systematic evaluation of panchayati raj after independence — recommended a three-tier structure of democratic decentralisation and was the intellectual foundation for the wave of State-level panchayati raj legislation in the late 1950s and 1960s. But without constitutional compulsion, these experiments were inconsistent: panchayats were given different powers across states, elections were irregular, finances were negligible, and the bureaucracy remained dominant. The failure to give panchayats real powers, adequate finances, and regular elections was a persistent grievance that Article 40 alone could not remedy.

The 73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992) addressed this directly by inserting Part IX into the Constitution, creating a mandatory framework for panchayati raj institutions — elections every five years, reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and women (at least one third), devolution of functions from the Eleventh Schedule, State Election Commissions, and State Finance Commissions to ensure fiscal transfers. Article 40 is the historical seed from which that entire framework grew: it provided the constitutional legitimacy for the view that decentralisation was not merely a policy preference but a constitutional obligation.

The 73rd Amendment is often described as a "silent revolution" in Indian democracy, bringing over three million elected representatives into local governance — more than any other democratic system in the world. The constitutional mandate of Article 40, converted from a non-justiciable directive into the mandatory framework of Part IX, is directly responsible for this democratic transformation. At least one-third of all panchayat seats are reserved for women, producing the largest single experiment in women's political participation in history. Article 40 is thus not merely a provision about administrative organisation; it is the constitutional foundation for a democratic experiment of global significance.

The article's significance lies not only in what it enabled but in what it tells courts about constitutional intent. When disputes arise about the scope of panchayat powers, the adequacy of State devolution, or the permissibility of State government interference in panchayat elections and administration, Article 40 supplies the interpretive background: the Constitution's preference is for self-governing, empowered, and financially independent local bodies, not token institutions that merely implement State directives.

## Reading the provision

The text of Article 40 is spare but significant. The State shall "take steps to organise village panchayats" — not merely permit them to exist — and must "endow them with such powers and authority as may be necessary to enable them to function as units of self-government." The phrase "units of self-government" is the key constitutional standard. A panchayat that exists on paper but lacks the powers, finances, or administrative capacity to make and implement decisions about local affairs is not functioning as a unit of self-government; it is functioning as an administrative outpost of the State.

This standard also has a democratic dimension. Self-government requires not just formal powers but genuine political autonomy: regularly elected bodies, accountable to their local constituencies, with a secure tenure and a sphere of jurisdiction that State governments cannot arbitrarily override. The Balwant Rai Mehta Report had articulated this vision, and the 73rd Amendment implemented it constitutionally — but the gap between the constitutional standard and the administrative reality is what continues to generate litigation and reform advocacy.

The 73rd Amendment's Eleventh Schedule lists twenty-nine subjects that may be devolved to panchayats, including agriculture, land improvement, land reform implementation, minor irrigation, animal husbandry, rural electrification, rural housing, poverty alleviation programmes, primary and secondary education, health and sanitation, markets and fairs, and maintenance of community assets. The word "may" in the relevant constitutional provision has been read by State governments as discretionary, but courts have invoked Article 40 to resist the view that devolution is purely optional — the constitutional preference for genuine self-government requires that devolution be real and substantial, not nominal.

Article 40 also reflects the particular influence of Gandhian political thought in the Constitution's design. Gandhi's vision of gram swaraj — village self-rule as the foundation of Indian democracy — is given constitutional expression here. The framers chose to place it in Part IV rather than Part III, which was a concession to the view that immediate, uniform implementation was impractical, but they preserved the aspiration as a binding constitutional direction on every successive State. The Ashok Mehta Committee (1977) and the G.V.K. Rao Committee (1985) reinforced and updated the Balwant Rai Mehta vision before the 73rd Amendment finally gave it mandatory constitutional force.

## Key cases

**Gram Panchayat of Village Jamalpur v. Malwinder Singh (1985)** — Before the 73rd Amendment, the Supreme Court treated Article 40 as evidence that the constitutional order values devolved self-governance, reading it to support panchayat authority over local matters and to confirm that legislation conferring powers on village bodies was consistent with the directive's mandate. The case illustrated how even a non-enforceable directive could shape the Court's reading of State and local government relationships, with courts resolving interpretive ambiguities in favour of broader rather than narrower panchayat jurisdiction.

**State of Rajasthan v. Basant Nahata (2005)** — The Supreme Court examined the extent to which State legislatures can restrict the powers of elected panchayats, reading Article 40 alongside Part IX to conclude that the constitutional framework envisages genuine devolution rather than a formal transfer with substantive powers retained by the State. The Court emphasised that Article 40 reflects a constitutional commitment to local self-governance that the State cannot hollow out through legislative devices that preserve the form of panchayati raj while negating its substance. The principle established — that the appearance of devolution without its substance violates the constitutional mandate — has been applied in numerous subsequent challenges to State interference with panchayat autonomy.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** — The Court upheld the constitutional validity of reservations for Other Backward Classes in panchayat elections, relying in part on Article 40's directive that panchayats must be organised as genuine units of self-government for all sections of the population. It held that the inclusion of historically marginalised communities in local governance was consistent with both Part IX's mandatory reservation scheme and the broader constitutional purpose that Article 40 expresses. The judgment confirmed that panchayati raj institutions are not a concession by the State but a constitutional entitlement that requires active measures to be made genuinely inclusive. The Court drew on the history of Article 40 — from Gandhian aspiration through Balwant Rai Mehta's recommendations to the 73rd Amendment — to confirm the constitutional seriousness of the self-government mandate.

## Current significance

Despite the 73rd Amendment's constitutional mandate, the gap between the constitutional vision of Article 40 and the on-the-ground reality of panchayati raj remains wide. State governments have been slow to devolve the full range of subjects listed in the Eleventh Schedule — agriculture, minor irrigation, social forestry, rural housing, poverty alleviation — and financial transfers to panchayats remain inadequate in most states. Elected panchayats are frequently superseded by State governments, their elections delayed, and their administrative staff allocated by and accountable to State bureaucracies rather than to locally elected representatives.

The problem of delayed and manipulated panchayat elections has attracted significant judicial attention. High Courts have regularly issued mandamus directions requiring State Election Commissions to conduct elections within constitutional time limits, reading Article 40 alongside Articles 243E and 243K to hold that the State cannot indefinitely postpone elections to elected local bodies even under the guise of delimitation exercises, reservation order revisions, or administrative reorganisation.

Financial devolution is the other major battleground. The constitutional requirement of State Finance Commissions, drawn from Article 243I, is directly connected to Article 40's standard of genuine self-government — a panchayat without adequate finances is not a unit of self-government but a body performing government functions without government resources. Successive Finance Commission reports at the national level have recommended significant increases in devolution to local bodies, and courts have cited Article 40 to hold that fiscal transfers to panchayats are a constitutional obligation, not a discretionary State bounty.

Article 40 also informs debates about urban local bodies under the 74th Constitutional Amendment and the parallel question of whether city and town governments receive the powers and resources needed to function as genuine units of democratic self-governance. The report of the Second Administrative Reforms Commission and the recommendations of successive State Finance Commissions have consistently cited the constitutional standard of Article 40 in calling for more substantive devolution of fiscal and administrative powers to local bodies. As India urbanises rapidly, the gap between the constitutional vision of self-governing local bodies and the reality of under-resourced municipalities is generating the same litigation pressures that earlier emerged in the panchayati raj context.

The Panchayat (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act 1996 (PESA) extended the panchayati raj framework to tribal areas, drawing directly on Article 40's constitutional mandate while ensuring that tribal customary governance systems were respected. PESA gave tribal communities the right to manage their natural resources, approve development projects, and regulate local affairs — creating a constitutionally grounded model of indigenous self-governance that reflects the original aspiration of Article 40. Litigation under PESA in the Supreme Court and High Courts has reinforced the constitutional seriousness of local self-governance in scheduled areas, with courts treating non-compliance with PESA's consultation and consent requirements as violations of constitutionally protected rights.

The gender dimensions of Article 40 deserve special attention. The 73rd Amendment's mandatory reservation of one-third of panchayat seats for women has produced a fundamental transformation in women's political participation at the local level. Many States have gone further, reserving fifty per cent of seats. The Supreme Court has upheld these reservations in multiple cases, drawing on Article 40's mandate that panchayats be genuine units of self-government for all sections of the community, and reading this requirement as mandating the inclusion of women — historically excluded from political participation — as essential to the constitutional standard of genuine self-government.

The National Disaster Management framework and the management of the COVID-19 pandemic revealed the importance and limitations of panchayati raj institutions in crisis governance. Courts invoked Article 40 to require State governments to devolve genuine authority and resources to panchayats for local disease containment measures, treatment, and relief — illustrating how the constitutional standard of self-governing local bodies becomes most urgent precisely when centralised governance proves inadequate.

The Fourteenth and Fifteenth Finance Commissions have significantly increased the quantum of funds transferred directly to local bodies, including panchayats, as a constitutional requirement. The Fifteenth Finance Commission's recommendations included a tied grant for health infrastructure at the panchayat level and untied grants for local area development. These fiscal transfers are constitutionally grounded in the State Finance Commission provisions of Part IX, which themselves flow from Article 40's mandate for financially empowered self-governing local bodies. The growing body of Finance Commission jurisprudence on local body grants is part of the constitutional maturation of Article 40's vision into a fiscal reality, however imperfect.

The question of panchayat elections and the right to local democratic representation has also generated constitutional litigation under Articles 14, 19, and 21, with courts reading Article 40 to establish that citizens have a constitutional interest in regular, free, and fair panchayat elections. Judicial directions to conduct elections within stipulated timelines, to prohibit unilateral postponements by State governments, and to prevent executive interference in election schedules are all traceable to the fundamental constitutional commitment that Article 40 embodies: that self-government at the local level is not a gift from the State but a constitutional entitlement of the people.

Digital governance and e-panchayat initiatives represent the latest chapter in the Article 40 story, raising questions about whether the constitutional standard of "units of self-government" is met when panchayat functions are increasingly performed by centralised digital platforms designed and operated by State governments rather than by locally elected representatives with genuine administrative authority. The constitutional standard of Article 40 — panchayats endowed with the powers and authority necessary to function as units of self-government — continues to evolve as the administrative landscape changes, ensuring the provision remains a living constitutional norm rather than a historical relic.

The constitutional vision of Article 40 has also been influential in academic and policy debates about the nature of Indian federalism. India is typically described as a quasi-federal system with a strong unitary bias. Article 40 and the 73rd Amendment introduced a third tier of governance that complements — and in some respects corrects — the centralising tendencies of the federal structure. The political scientist Atul Kohli and others have argued that effective democratic deepening in India requires genuine devolution to local bodies, and Article 40's constitutional mandate is cited as the normative foundation for that argument. Whether India can move from formal to substantive panchayati raj remains one of the key constitutional challenges of the coming decades.

Article 40's constitutional legacy is inseparable from the question of how India's democracy has been deepened — or failed to be deepened — at the grassroots. The constitutional mandate is clear and the statutory framework is in place; the challenge is political will and administrative capacity to make it real.

The Gram Sabha — the assembly of all adult voters in a village — is the constitutional bedrock of Article 40's vision of self-government. The 73rd Amendment recognised the Gram Sabha as the primary democratic institution in the panchayat system, and PESA gave it especially strong rights in scheduled areas including the power to approve local plans and exercise control over natural resources. Courts have used Article 40 to protect the Gram Sabha's constitutional status against State laws that circumvent or bypass it, holding that the constitutional commitment to village self-government requires that the Gram Sabha — not merely the panchayat as an elected body — exercise genuine deliberative authority over local decisions.
