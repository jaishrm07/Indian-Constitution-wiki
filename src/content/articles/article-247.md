## What this Article does

Article 247 empowers Parliament to create additional courts specifically to administer Union List laws and pre-constitutional laws on Union subjects, overriding the general Chapter structure of Part XI. It operates as a carve-out from the ordinary scheme under which High Courts exercise supervisory jurisdiction over subordinate courts: Parliament can establish a parallel judicial infrastructure where the subject matter demands speed, specialisation, or uniform national standards. The provision has been used to create dedicated tribunals and courts for customs, income tax, company law, and intellectual property — areas where general civil courts were perceived as too slow or insufficiently specialised.

The Article's phrase "better administration" is purposive: it requires that any additional court created under this power be genuinely directed at improved enforcement of the relevant Union law, not at bypassing High Courts for extraneous reasons. This purposive constraint has fed into the broader tribunal jurisprudence that scrutinises whether Parliament can validly remove subject matter from High Court jurisdiction by transferring it to a statutory body.

## The tribunal system and Article 247

Article 247 is the constitutional root from which India's complex specialist tribunal system has grown. The Income Tax Appellate Tribunal (1941, pre-constitutional but ratified by Article 247 powers), the Customs, Excise and Service Tax Appellate Tribunal, the Intellectual Property Appellate Board, the National Company Law Tribunal, the Debt Recovery Tribunals, the Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal, and the National Green Tribunal all trace their constitutional authority ultimately to Parliament's Article 247 power to provide "for the better administration" of Union laws.

The "notwithstanding anything in this Chapter" opening means that Article 247 operates as an exception to whatever limits the rest of Chapter I of Part XI might otherwise impose on Parliament's ability to establish courts — including limits that might flow from the constitutional position of High Courts. The exception is not absolute, however: it cannot override fundamental constitutional structures such as Article 226 (High Court writs) or basic structure principles that the Supreme Court has elevated beyond Parliament's reach.

## Key cases

**L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India (1997) 3 SCC 261** — The Supreme Court held that the judicial review powers of the High Courts under Article 226 and of the Supreme Court under Article 32 are part of the basic structure and cannot be ousted by any tribunal created by Parliament, including those established under Article 247-type powers. Tribunals created under Parliamentary law may serve as courts of first instance, but their decisions must remain subject to High Court scrutiny. This ruling fundamentally shaped how Article 247 courts and tribunals are integrated into the constitutional hierarchy.

**Madras Bar Association v. Union of India (2014) 10 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court struck down parts of the National Tax Tribunal Act 2005 which had sought to replace High Court jurisdiction over direct and indirect tax matters with a National Tax Tribunal. The Court held that redirecting tax appeals away from High Courts undermined the independence of the judiciary and the basic structure. Although the case arose from the Finance Ministry's tribunal-rationalisation agenda rather than Article 247 directly, it defines the outer limit of Parliament's power to create "additional courts" — they may supplement but cannot supplant High Courts.

**Rojer Mathew v. South Indian Bank Ltd. (2019) 6 SCC 1** — A five-judge bench struck down the Finance Act 2017's provisions on tribunal appointments and service conditions as violating judicial independence. The Court reaffirmed that tribunals established under Parliamentary power, including Article 247, must have appointment, tenure, and salary structures that guarantee independence from executive control, regardless of the legislative source of their creation.

## Independence requirements for Article 247 courts

A sustained line of Supreme Court decisions has established that any court or tribunal Parliament creates under Article 247 must satisfy the same core independence requirements that apply to constitutional courts — namely, independence of appointments from executive preference, security of tenure, and adequate remuneration. The executive cannot, under the guise of creating an "efficient" specialist tribunal, design an appointment or removal mechanism that makes the tribunal susceptible to government pressure.

This independence requirement flows from the interaction of Article 247 with the basic structure doctrine: the rule of law and the independence of the judiciary being basic structure elements, any tribunal that exercises judicial or quasi-judicial functions must be structured in a way consistent with those principles regardless of whether it is formally a "court" or not.

## Current significance

Article 247 has gained renewed relevance with the Tribunals Reforms Act 2021 and the Supreme Court's ongoing oversight of tribunal appointments, tenure, and independence. The Court has repeatedly struck down executive-favourable appointment processes for tribunals as inconsistent with judicial independence, reinforcing that any court or tribunal Parliament creates under Article 247 must satisfy basic constitutional guarantees of impartiality. The rationalisation of 26 tribunals into fewer bodies under the 2021 Act continues to raise questions about whether the merged bodies can exercise the "better administration" function that Article 247 contemplates, or whether consolidation compromises the specialised expertise that justified their original creation.
