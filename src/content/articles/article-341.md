## Why it matters

Article 341 is the gateway to the entire package of constitutional protections for Scheduled Castes. Membership of the SC list triggers eligibility for reservation in educational institutions under Article 15(4), reservation in public employment under Articles 16(4), 16(4A), and 16(4B), reservation in elected seats under Articles 330 and 332, and the protections of Article 17 (abolition of untouchability) and the Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act. The definition of who is on the list is therefore not a technical administrative matter; it is a determination of constitutional entitlement to the most consequential affirmative action package in Indian law.

The two-stage mechanism — Presidential notification followed by exclusive parliamentary amendment — reflects a deliberate constitutional choice to remove SC list management from ordinary executive discretion. Governments change; political pressures vary; the temptation to add or remove communities from the SC list for electoral advantage would be overwhelming if the list could be altered by executive order. By reserving amendment power for Parliament alone, Article 341(2) imposes a transparency and accountability requirement: any change to the SC list must pass through public legislative deliberation, with the government required to state its reasons and accept parliamentary scrutiny.

The article's most enduring constitutional controversy — the religion-based exclusion of Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims under paragraph 3 of the Scheduled Castes Order 1950 — strikes at the heart of the article's purpose. If the social disability that Article 341 is designed to address (untouchability-linked discrimination) persists after conversion to Christianity or Islam, the argument that converts should be denied SC status requires a justification that the constitutional text itself does not provide. The article contains no religion-based qualification; that restriction is entirely an executive creation through the 1950 Order, and the Supreme Court is currently examining whether it is constitutionally sustainable.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1)** establishes the Presidential notification as the operative instrument for SC definition. Three aspects are notable. First, consultation with the Governor is required when the notification covers a State (but not a Union territory) — this is a mandatory procedural requirement, though not a requirement to follow the Governor's advice. Second, the notification specifies communities "in relation to" a State or Union territory: SC status is state-specific, not national. Third, the notification can specify "castes, races or tribes or parts of or groups within castes, races or tribes" — a formulation broad enough to include sub-castes and caste-groups as well as the main named castes. This breadth has become constitutionally significant in the context of the Davinder Singh sub-classification ruling.

**Clause (2)** creates Parliament's exclusive amendment power. The clause is drafted as a carve-out from the general principle that Presidential notifications cannot be changed by subsequent executive notifications: Parliament can alter the list by law, but the executive cannot. The "save as aforesaid" formulation means the only way to vary a Presidential SC notification is through Parliament. There is no special majority requirement: an ordinary law passed by simple majority in both Houses and receiving Presidential assent suffices. But the necessity of going through Parliament — with full committee scrutiny, public debate, and political accountability — is the substantive safeguard.

## Scheduled Castes Order 1950 and the religion exclusion

The Constitution (Scheduled Castes) Order 1950, issued under Article 341(1), is the founding Presidential notification. As originally enacted it restricted SC status to members of Hindu castes only. In 1956 the Order was amended to extend SC status to members of Sikh castes; in 1990 Buddhist converts from SC castes were included. The Scheduled Castes Order's paragraph 3 currently reads: "Notwithstanding anything contained in paragraph 2, no person who professes a religion different from the Hindu, the Sikh or the Buddhist religion shall be deemed to be a member of a Scheduled Caste."

The constitutional text of Article 341 itself contains no religious restriction. The restriction derives entirely from paragraph 3 of the executive order. The Rangnath Misra Commission (2007) and the National Commission for Religious and Linguistic Minorities both recommended extending SC status to Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims, noting that social disabilities rooted in untouchability persist regardless of the convert's religion. The Central Government has not accepted these recommendations, arguing that conversion is a voluntary act that severs the social connection to the SC community. This argument has been contested on empirical grounds — survey data shows that Dalit converts continue to face the same caste-based discrimination as non-converted Dalit Hindus — and on constitutional grounds — Article 25's right to religion does not require the loss of any other constitutional entitlement as a consequence of religious choice.

The case captioned as National Council of Dalit Christians v Union of India (and connected petitions) has been pending before the Supreme Court since 2004. In 2022, the Court directed the Central Government to appoint a new Commission of Inquiry to examine the matter, signalling the constitutional seriousness of the religion-based exclusion. The matter remains pending.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's treatment of Article 341 focused on the mechanism for SC identification rather than the substantive criteria for inclusion, which had already been debated extensively in the context of Articles 15 and 16. The key constitutional design choice was the presidential-notification mechanism: rather than embedding a list in the constitutional text itself (which would require a constitutional amendment every time a community was added or removed), the framers chose a notification mechanism that allowed the list to evolve while maintaining parliamentary control over amendments.

B.R. Ambedkar defended the notification mechanism as providing both flexibility and protection: the Governor consultation requirement provided a state-specific assessment; the President's notification gave the list constitutional status and stability; and Parliament's exclusive amendment power prevented executive manipulation. The debate also touched on the geographical specificity of notifications — whether SC status should be portable across State boundaries. The Constituent Assembly did not resolve this question definitively, leaving the state-specific structure in place with the implicit expectation that Parliament would eventually address the migration problem through legislation.

## Key cases

**Indra Sawhney v Union of India (1992)** — The nine-judge bench affirmed that the Presidential SC list is sacrosanct and cannot be altered except by Parliament. The government cannot, through executive orders, direct that certain castes or sub-groups within the SC list receive preferential treatment over others without parliamentary authorisation. The Court also confirmed that caste is an appropriate proxy for social and educational backwardness under the Indian constitution — a holding that is directly relevant to Article 341's use of caste as the definitional unit for SC identification.

**E.V. Chinnaiah v State of Andhra Pradesh (2005)** — A five-judge Constitution Bench held that a State law sub-classifying the SC list to direct a proportion of SC reservations to more backward sub-groups within the list was unconstitutional. The Court reasoned that the SC list under Article 341 is a single, integrated class created by the President; the State legislature has no power to subdivide it since that amendment power belongs exclusively to Parliament under Article 341(2). This ruling stood as the prevailing precedent for nearly two decades and was the foundation for the settled understanding that sub-classification within SC and ST lists was impermissible.

**State of Punjab v Davinder Singh (2024)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench overruled E.V. Chinnaiah and held that States do have constitutional power to sub-classify the SC list to provide preferential treatment within the reserved quota to sub-groups that are more backward, provided the sub-classification is based on empirical evidence of differential backwardness and does not exclude any caste from the SC category altogether. The majority reasoned that Parliament's exclusive power to alter the Presidential list (adding or removing castes) is distinct from a State's power to identify the most deserving among those already on the list for preferential allocation within the quota. The seven-judge bench's split of 6:1 on sub-classification and the significant concurring opinion of Justice B.R. Gavai (arguing for extending the creamy-layer principle to SCs and STs) means that further constitutional deliberation on these questions is certain.

**National Council of Dalit Christians v Union of India (pending, 2004–present)** — The Supreme Court is examining whether paragraph 3 of the Scheduled Castes Order 1950, which excludes Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims from SC status, is constitutionally valid under Articles 14, 15, 16, and 25. In 2022 the Court directed the Central Government to appoint a new Commission of Inquiry, underscoring the constitutional gravity of the religion-based exclusion. The case has the potential to expand the SC population significantly if the Court holds the exclusion unconstitutional and Parliament responds by amending the Order.

**Action Committee on Issue of Caste Certificate to Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes of Maharashtra v Union of India (1994)** — The Supreme Court laid down detailed guidelines for the verification of SC caste certificates, addressing the problem of fraudulent SC claims. The judgment confirmed that the Presidential list under Article 341 establishes the definitive entitlement, and that certificates must be verified against the actual communities listed in the notification — not against self-declared caste identities.

## Article 341(2) and the exclusive parliamentary amendment power

The clause (2) prohibition on executive amendment of the Presidential SC list is one of the few places in the Constitution where Parliament is explicitly given a power that is at the same time withheld from the executive. The constitutional bar is express: "save as aforesaid a notification issued under the said clause shall not be varied by any subsequent notification." A government that wishes to add a community to the SC list must introduce a Bill in Parliament, pass it by ordinary majority, and receive Presidential assent. This is the same procedural requirement as for any ordinary law — no special majority, no State ratification — but the necessity of going through Parliament creates political visibility and accountability. In practice, SC list additions require the government to state publicly which community is being added, on what evidence of backwardness, and with what political and demographic consequences — a transparency that executive action would not require.

## Article 341 and Article 342 compared

Article 341 and Article 342 are parallel provisions: Article 342 does for Scheduled Tribes what Article 341 does for Scheduled Castes. Both follow the same two-stage structure — Presidential notification followed by exclusive parliamentary power to alter the list — and both are subject to state-wise specification. The key substantive differences reflect the different historical and social bases of the two communities. Scheduled Castes are defined primarily by the traditional social hierarchy of caste-based untouchability — the Dalits, historically occupying the lowest rungs of the jati system. Scheduled Tribes are defined primarily by geographical isolation, cultural distinctiveness, and historical exclusion from the mainstream rather than by untouchability specifically. Article 17's abolition of untouchability applies specifically to SC communities; no equivalent provision applies to STs. The Fifth and Sixth Schedules create governance frameworks for tribal areas that have no SC equivalent. These substantive distinctions mean that the case law on Articles 341 and 342, while structurally parallel, develops on different doctrinal tracks.

## Current significance

The Davinder Singh (2024) ruling has opened a new chapter in SC reservation law by clearing the constitutional path for State governments to enact sub-classification legislation. Several States — including Punjab, Tamil Nadu, and Telangana — have enacted or are reviewing such legislation. The resulting litigation is working through the High Courts, and the Supreme Court can be expected to develop further doctrine on how sub-classification exercises must be conducted, what data is required, and how the efficiency condition in Article 335 applies within the sub-classified framework.

Justice Gavai's concurrence in Davinder Singh arguing for the extension of the creamy-layer principle to SCs and STs is the most consequential unresolved question in the field. If a future Constitution Bench adopts this reasoning as binding ratio, it would require Parliament to amend Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) or for the executive to redefine the eligible class for promotional reservation within SC and ST lists. The political resistance to applying a creamy-layer exclusion to SCs — who face discrimination based on caste identity regardless of economic status — means that any judicial adoption of this principle would be deeply contested.

The pending Dalit-Christian and Dalit-Muslim cases present a potentially transformative moment. If the Supreme Court holds that paragraph 3 of the Scheduled Castes Order 1950 is unconstitutional, Parliament would be required to amend the Order to include these communities. This would substantially increase the SC population, increase the demand on the reserved quota, and require a redesign of reservation allocations across the employment and education sectors. The Article 340 commission mechanism could be activated to provide the empirical foundation for any such redesign.
