plainEnglish: >-
  Article 89 establishes the Rajya Sabha's presiding officers by making the Vice-President its ex officio Chairman — a constitutional officer elected by both Houses of Parliament who is not a member of the Rajya Sabha — and by requiring the Rajya Sabha to elect a Deputy Chairman from among its own members. The choice to link the Vice-Presidency to the Chairmanship gives the upper house a presiding officer with national stature who stands outside the internal politics of the chamber, while the Deputy Chairman is embedded within it and accountable to it. This architecture means the Rajya Sabha has a more insulated and prestigious presiding officer than the Lok Sabha, whose Speaker is elected from and remains a member of the House. The powers the Chairman exercises over the Rajya Sabha — recognising speakers, ruling on points of order, and potentially certifying Bills — make the identity of the Vice-President a matter of significant consequence for parliamentary management.
officialText: |-
  89. The Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Council of States.—(1) The Vice- President of India shall be ex officio Chairman of the Council of States.

  (2) The Council of States shall, as soon as may be, choose a member of the Council to be Deputy Chairman thereof and, so often as the office of Deputy Chairman becomes vacant, the Council shall choose another member to be Deputy Chairman thereof.

## Why it matters

The design choice in Article 89(1) is structurally distinctive and politically consequential. Unlike the Lok Sabha Speaker, who is elected by and from the members of the Lok Sabha under Article 93, the Rajya Sabha Chairman is the Vice-President of India — a constitutional officer elected by an electorate that transcends the Rajya Sabha. Under Article 66, the Vice-President is elected by an Electoral College consisting of members of both Houses of Parliament in a joint sitting, using the system of proportional representation with the single transferable vote. The Chairman is therefore not a member of the Rajya Sabha, does not vote in its proceedings (except to cast a casting vote in the event of a tie under Article 100), and cannot be removed from the Chairmanship by the Rajya Sabha alone. Removing the Vice-President requires a resolution by the Rajya Sabha passed by an absolute majority, which must then be agreed to by the Lok Sabha under Article 67(b). This double-lock on removal gives the Rajya Sabha's presiding officer an independence from internal chamber politics that the Lok Sabha Speaker, removable by an absolute majority of the total membership of the Lok Sabha alone under Article 94, does not possess to the same degree.

This structural independence is not without costs. Because the Vice-President's political identity is formed through a national election contested on party lines, the Chairman may carry strong partisan associations into the Chair. The convention in the Westminster tradition — that a Speaker or Chairman must shed all party affiliations upon election to the presiding office — is difficult to maintain when the presiding officer continues to hold high political office (the Vice-Presidency) and when their removal requires a constitutional process far more cumbersome than what the Lok Sabha uses for its Speaker. The result is an office that is simultaneously more protected and more politically identified than the Lok Sabha speakership.

The Deputy Chairman, by contrast, is elected by the Rajya Sabha from among its own members under Article 89(2) and can be removed by a resolution of the Rajya Sabha under Article 90(c). The Deputy Chairman therefore has a direct accountability to the chamber that the Chairman lacks. This creates a two-tier structure at the apex of Rajya Sabha proceedings: a Chairman whose tenure is insulated from chamber politics and a Deputy Chairman who is embedded within them, making the Deputy Chairman election a recurring site of government-opposition competition for institutional control.

The practical importance of who occupies the Chair of the Rajya Sabha should not be underestimated. The Chairman decides who may speak, how long they may speak, whether a point of order is admitted, whether a matter is referred to a select committee, and how disorder in the House is managed. These are not purely administrative powers — they shape the character and output of legislative deliberation. When the Rajya Sabha is controlled by parties different from those forming the government, the Chairman's management of proceedings becomes politically significant in ways that compound the legislative gridlock that divided House compositions can produce.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1)'s ex officio linkage between the Vice-Presidency and the Chairmanship has practical implications throughout Part V. When the Vice-President is acting as President under Article 65 — because of the President's absence, illness, or otherwise — the Vice-President must cease performing Rajya Sabha duties. Article 65(1) provides that while acting as President, the Vice-President shall not perform the duties of the office of Vice-President. This creates a formal vacancy in the Chairmanship that Article 91(1) fills by activating the Deputy Chairman to act as Chairman. The Deputy Chairman is therefore not merely a parliamentary convenience but a constitutionally necessary office whose activation may be triggered by events entirely outside the Rajya Sabha — the unavailability of the President, which causes the Vice-President to step into the Presidency, which in turn vacates the Chairmanship.

The Chairman's role is not merely ceremonial. The Chairman maintains order in the Rajya Sabha, recognises members to speak, rules on points of order, and administers the Rajya Sabha's Rules of Procedure. The Chairman's rulings on points of order have constitutional significance when they touch on matters such as whether a Bill has been correctly classified as a Money Bill (given that Money Bills can only originate in the Lok Sabha under Article 109, the question occasionally arises in the Rajya Sabha when it receives a Bill for passage or recommends amendments), and whether proceedings comply with constitutional requirements for joint sittings under Articles 108 and 118. In joint sittings convened to resolve deadlock between the Houses under Article 108, the Speaker of the Lok Sabha presides — not the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha — a distinction that reflects the Lok Sabha's primacy in legislation but also means the Rajya Sabha's Chairman has no presiding role in the most constitutionally significant joint assembly in the parliamentary calendar.

Clause (2)'s obligation to choose a Deputy Chairman "as soon as may be" is a constitutional directive carrying a degree of urgency. The Rajya Sabha must fill the post promptly each time it becomes vacant — whether because the previous Deputy Chairman loses their seat, resigns under Article 90(a) or (b), or is removed by resolution under Article 90(c). The phrase "as soon as may be" is the Constitution's standard formulation for a non-immediate but mandatory obligation, and it has been treated by constitutional commentators as creating a directional duty on the House rather than an enforceable hard deadline. Extended periods during which the Rajya Sabha has not elected a Deputy Chairman — which have occurred — raise questions about whether the constitutional directive is being honoured, particularly since the Deputy Chairman is first in the Article 91 succession chain for the Chairmanship.

The Deputy Chairman, unlike the Chairman, votes as a full member of the Rajya Sabha when not presiding. The Deputy Chairman retains all the rights of a member — including the right to vote on legislation — and exercises the Chairman's powers only when the Chairman is absent from a sitting or when the Chairmanship is temporarily vacant. This dual status as member and presiding officer-in-waiting distinguishes the Deputy Chairman from the Lok Sabha's Deputy Speaker, who has a structurally similar position in the Lok Sabha. The Rajya Sabha Rules of Procedure govern the precise occasions on which the Deputy Chairman takes the Chair and the conduct expected of the Deputy Chairman when presiding.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**The removal proceedings against Deputy Chairmen — Parliamentary incidents** — Several attempts to remove a Deputy Chairman through the Article 90(c) resolution procedure have become politically charged episodes. The removal procedure requires giving at least fourteen days' notice of the intention to move the resolution, under the proviso to Article 90(c). This notice requirement has been used tactically — both to signal political pressure on a Deputy Chairman and to mobilise floor management in advance of a vote. Unlike the removal of the Speaker, which is governed by Article 94 and has never been successfully executed, the smaller and more manageable political arithmetic of the Rajya Sabha in certain periods has made Deputy Chairman removal a more credible threat.

**Subhash Desai v. Principal Secretary, Governor of Maharashtra (2023)** — The Supreme Court's examination of the Speaker's role in disqualification proceedings in the Maharashtra context generated important constitutional analysis of the independence required of presiding officers and the limits of judicial review of their decisions. The Court's reasoning about the institutional character of presiding officers — that they derive their authority from the Constitution rather than from the majority that elected them, and that they owe duties to the whole House rather than to the government of the day — applies with equal force to the Rajya Sabha's Chairman and Deputy Chairman. The judgment reinforces that the Chairman's rulings in disqualification proceedings under the Tenth Schedule are subject to judicial review, though within strict scope.

**Manoj Narula v. Union of India (2014)** — The Supreme Court's examination of parliamentary probity and the quality of persons in high constitutional offices engaged the standards expected of constitutional officeholders including presiding officers of Parliament. The Court noted that offices like the Chairmanship and Deputy Chairmanship carry institutional expectations of impartiality and commitment to parliamentary democracy that go beyond their bare legal description. The judgment is relevant to evaluating whether a Chairman's conduct of proceedings meets the constitutional standard or whether it has become an exercise in partisan management.

**Deputy Chairman elections as political contests** — The election of the Deputy Chairman of the Rajya Sabha has on multiple occasions been the site of intense government-opposition rivalry. Because the Deputy Chairman is elected by the Rajya Sabha itself — and because the ruling coalition and opposition have at various times held competing majorities in the Rajya Sabha — the post has become a barometer of the ruling government's control over the upper house. The government's interest in placing a sympathetic person in the Deputy Chairmanship is partly symbolic (institutional control) and partly practical (the Deputy Chairman manages proceedings when the Vice-President is absent, which may be frequent given the Vice-President's other duties).

## Current significance

The Rajya Sabha Chairman's conduct of proceedings has been politically contested in recent sessions. The Chairman exercises significant discretion over which members are recognised to speak, how disruptive proceedings are handled, and how committee referrals are managed — powers that take on partisan significance given that the Rajya Sabha has periodically been controlled by parties different from the government in the Lok Sabha. The Chairman's rulings on points of order raised by opposition members about the propriety of legislation — including the constitutional validity of Bills passed without adequate scrutiny — have been contested both within the House and in commentary, raising questions about whether the Chair is genuinely neutral or systematically favourable to the government's legislative agenda.

The Deputy Chairman election has become a flashpoint for government-opposition rivalry, with the ruling coalition and opposition competing for the post precisely because an unelected or sympathetic Deputy Chairman matters for the Article 91 succession chain. Periods in which the Rajya Sabha has gone without a Deputy Chairman — because no candidate commands a majority or because the process has been politically deadlocked — create a constitutional gap: if the Vice-President becomes unable to perform Chairman's duties, Article 91 activates the Deputy Chairman, but if the post is vacant, the procedure must fall back to a member deputed by the Chairman. This gap has not produced a constitutional crisis but represents an observable weakening of the Article 89(2) directive.

The Vice-President's transition from a background constitutional role to a more active presiding presence has renewed attention to whether the ex officio structure of Article 89 provides adequate institutional distance between the Vice-President's political identity and the Chairman's constitutional impartiality obligations. Statements from the Chair in recent years — including direct commentary on opposition conduct and judicial decisions — have been controversial, with critics arguing that they are inconsistent with the impartiality expected of a presiding officer and supporters arguing that the Chairman is entitled to be a substantive rather than merely procedural presence in parliamentary life. The constitutional text of Article 89 is silent on these expectations, leaving them to convention, and convention has proved insufficient to resolve the debate.
