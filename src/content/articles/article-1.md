## What this Article does

Article 1 names the country "India, that is Bharat" and declares it a Union of States — not a federation of sovereign units. It identifies three categories of territory: the territories of the States, Union territories listed in the First Schedule, and any territory that may be acquired in the future. The word "Union" was chosen deliberately to signal that States have no right to secede and that component units derive their existence from the Constitution rather than from a prior compact between them.

## Union, not federation — the Ambedkar doctrine

The choice between "Union" and "federation" was a deliberate act by the Constituent Assembly. B.R. Ambedkar explained in his closing speech on 25 November 1949 that the term "federation" was not used because it would imply an agreement between sovereign units that retained residual autonomy. In the Indian scheme the States are not indestructible: Parliament can alter their boundaries, merge them, or convert them to Union territories under Articles 3 and 4. The Centre's legislative and executive authority is paramount; States exist within a constitutional framework defined and alterable by Parliament. The "Union of States" formula simultaneously acknowledged the practical federal character of governance while reserving ultimate territorial sovereignty in the Union.

The name clause — "India, that is Bharat" — gives equal constitutional status to both names. Bharat is the Sanskrit name used throughout classical literature and in most Indian languages; India derives from the Indus river through Greek and Latin usage. The Constituent Assembly debated whether to use only one name but settled on the dual-name formula. Periodic demands to replace "India" with "Bharat" in all official use resurfaced in 2023 when presidential correspondence styled the host as "Bharat", triggering fresh constitutional debate about whether a constitutional amendment would be required to remove one of the two co-equal names.

## The three tiers of territory

Article 1(3) draws an important structural distinction. The territories of the States and the Union territories in the First Schedule form the established constitutional map; the third category — "such other territories as may be acquired" — is an open-ended clause allowing India to incorporate territory through treaty, cession, or annexation without a fresh constitutional amendment. Goa was acquired in 1961 and integrated through ordinary legislation relying on this acquisition power. Sikkim was admitted in 1975 under Article 2 (not Article 1(3)(c)), which is the route for adding a pre-existing State to the First Schedule. The distinction matters because Article 2 governs voluntary admission while Article 1(3)(c) covers post-acquisition constitutional status once territory has come under Indian sovereignty.

## Key cases

**In re Berubari Union (1960) AIR 1960 SC 845** — The Supreme Court held that ceding territory covered by Article 1 to a foreign country requires an amendment under Article 368 and cannot be done by ordinary legislation alone. The Union proposed to transfer the Berubari Union area to Pakistan under the Nehru–Noon Pact. The Court ruled this required a constitutional amendment, shaping every subsequent territorial adjustment. The decision remains the leading authority on the relationship between Article 1 and the amending power.

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India (1963) AIR 1963 SC 1241** — The Court affirmed that India is not a federal state in the classical sense and that the Union has paramount authority over States. The judgment reinforced that "Union of States" means the Centre can override State autonomy on matters of national territory and legislative supremacy.

**Rameshwar Prasad v. Union of India (2006) 2 SCC 1** — While addressing the dissolution of the Bihar Assembly, the Court reiterated that territorial integrity of States is a constitutional value and that Union power under Article 1 read with Article 3 does not extend to arbitrary action driven by political considerations.

## Amendment and reorganisation history

The First Schedule has been amended numerous times since 1950. The States Reorganisation Act, 1956 effected the most sweeping redrawing of the map on linguistic lines. Later additions include Nagaland (1963), Haryana (1966), Himachal Pradesh (1971), Meghalaya, Manipur, and Tripura (1972), Sikkim (1975), and Goa as a full State (1987). Most controversially, the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019 converted the State of J&K into two Union territories — J&K (with a legislature) and Ladakh (without one). Each change operated through Articles 3 and 4, allowing ordinary legislation to amend Schedule I rather than requiring the higher threshold of Article 368. The J&K reorganisation renewed academic debate about whether downgrading a State's constitutional status falls within ordinary legislative power.

## Current significance

Article 1 anchors any future merger or acquisition of territory, making it directly relevant to India's unsettled border disputes — with China along the Line of Actual Control and in respect of Aksai Chin, and with Pakistan over the broader J&K question. The Bharat-vs-India naming question remains constitutionally live. The article's "acquired territories" clause has also been invoked in discussions about the constitutional status of uninhabited island territories and India's maritime Exclusive Economic Zone.
