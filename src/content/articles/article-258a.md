## Why it matters

Article 258A is the reciprocal of Article 258. Where Article 258 allows the Union to push executive functions downward to States (or their officers), Article 258A allows a State to push functions upward to the Union. Inserted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act 1956, the provision reflects the bilateral character of cooperative federalism in India's constitutional design — administrative integration between Union and States is not a one-way street in which the Centre always directs and the States always execute. States may also, of their own accord and with Central consent, hand over the management of State subjects to Union agencies better placed to administer them.

The provision matters as a structural counterweight to the perception that the Constitution's federal architecture always advantages the Union. Article 258A demonstrates that the Constitution explicitly contemplates States initiating upward delegation — a point directly relevant to contemporary debates about centralisation versus cooperative federalism.

## Reading the provision

**Consent of the Government of India** — The Union does not automatically accept every upward entrustment a State Governor may wish to make. The Government of India must consent, ensuring that Central administrative machinery is not unilaterally burdened with State functions. Consent may be general or specific and is typically expressed through executive correspondence or notifications rather than formal legal instrument. The consent requirement makes Article 258A a bilateral arrangement — either party can decline or withdraw from the arrangement.

**Conditional or unconditional entrustment** — A State may attach conditions to the entrustment. For instance, a State entrusting certain regulatory functions to a Central agency might condition the entrustment on the Central agency following the State's prescribed procedures, reporting back to State authorities, or limiting its action to specified districts or time periods. Unconditional entrustment, by contrast, transfers the function in full — the entrusted body exercises it as though it were its own.

**No financial compensation clause** — Article 258(3) expressly entitles States to compensation for extra administrative costs when the Union entrusts functions to them. Article 258A contains no equivalent. Any financial implications of upward entrustment must be resolved through executive negotiation. In practice this has not generated litigation, because upward entrustments are voluntarily sought by States that perceive a net administrative benefit from Central management rather than an additional cost.

**Governor as the entrusting authority** — The Governor acts on the advice of the Council of Ministers. The Governor's formal role ensures that entrustment carries the constitutional imprimatur of the State's constitutional head rather than being merely an administrative arrangement between departmental officers. This prevents individual ministers or State officials from unilaterally offloading functions to Central agencies without a cabinet-level decision.

**Absence of a parliamentary legislation route** — Article 258(2) allows Parliament to legislate to confer powers on State officers directly, without a separate consent transaction. Article 258A has no equivalent — there is no mechanism by which Parliament can legislate to impose upward entrustment on a State. Upward delegation is purely executive and purely consensual, reinforcing that Article 258A is an instrument of cooperative federalism rather than Central coercion.

## Key cases

**Jaora Sugar Mills (P) Ltd. v. State of Madhya Pradesh AIR 1966 SC 416** — While not decided directly on Article 258A, the Supreme Court addressed the general principles of inter-governmental entrustment and affirmed that entrustment does not transfer constitutional competence. The entrusting government remains constitutionally responsible for the matter, and the entrusted body acts as agent within the scope of the entrustment.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977) 3 SCC 592** — In the context of President's Rule, the Court examined the constitutional basis for the Union's exercise of executive functions within States and affirmed that both upward and downward administrative integration require either explicit constitutional authorisation or the consent of both parties. Article 258A's consent mechanism was noted as a paradigm example of constitutionally authorised bilateral integration.

**Shamsher Singh v. State of Punjab (1974) 2 SCC 831** — The Supreme Court's authoritative ruling that the Governor acts on ministerial advice applies to Article 258A: when a Governor exercises the power to entrust functions to the Union, he acts on the advice of the Council of Ministers, not in personal discretion. The decision to make an upward entrustment is therefore a State cabinet decision channelled through the constitutional form of a gubernatorial act.

## Current significance

Article 258A remains relevant in cooperative federal arrangements for cross-border infrastructure, disaster response, border management, wildlife conservation, and national-level regulatory schemes where a State may find it advantageous to entrust certain functions to a Central agency with greater resources or national reach. In practice, upward entrustments are administratively documented rather than publicly notified, which limits the visibility of Article 258A's use — many such arrangements are effected through Memoranda of Understanding or executive letters rather than formal Presidential-order-style instruments.

The absence of litigation on Article 258A reflects its consensual character: because both the State and the Union must agree, the adversarial dimension that typically drives constitutional cases does not arise. In contemporary debates about centralisation and cooperative federalism, Article 258A is cited as textual evidence that the Constitution's federal design is not simply a hierarchy with the Union at the top, but a two-directional framework — States can voluntarily delegate upward, as well as the Union delegating downward. This symmetry is directly relevant to disputes about whether Central agencies administering formally State subjects are constitutionally anomalous or constitutionally envisaged.
