## Background: the Indian princely states

At independence India had over 560 princely states whose rulers had accepted British paramountcy but
were not directly under the Crown. Their integration into the Indian Union was achieved through
Instruments of Accession and later through merger agreements by which the rulers agreed to merge with
India in exchange for retention of their personal titles, privileges, and an annual privy purse — a
tax-free payment from the consolidated fund calibrated to each ruler's state revenue and other factors.
These agreements were given constitutional backing in the original Articles 291 and 362, which protected
the privy purses and personal rights respectively.

## The executive de-recognition attempt and judicial defeat

In 1970 the Indira Gandhi government sought to abolish the privy purses by executive order, issuing an
order under Article 366(22) de-recognising all rulers. The Supreme Court struck this down in
*Madhavrao Scindia v Union of India* (1971), holding that recognition and de-recognition of rulers
required parliamentary action and could not be accomplished by executive order alone. The judgment
protected the rulers' constitutional entitlements against unilateral executive action.

## The constitutional amendment response

Parliament then passed the Constitution (Twenty-sixth Amendment) Act, 1971, inserting Article 363A and
amending Article 366. Article 363A achieves through amendment what the executive order had attempted:
it abolishes recognition of all rulers on the commencement of the 26th Amendment (28 December 1971)
and simultaneously extinguishes the privy purse and all rights, liabilities, and obligations associated
with it. The phrase "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" was necessary to override the
pre-existing constitutional protections in Articles 291 and 362, which were simultaneously repealed.

The amendment was passed in both Houses with the required two-thirds majority after the 1971 election
gave the ruling Congress party an overwhelming mandate. The Rajya Sabha had been the stumbling block
in 1970; the reconstituted Houses after 1971 passed the amendment without difficulty.

## Legal effect

Clause (a) of Article 363A formally ends the presidential recognition of rulers, and with it the status
of "Ruler" under the Constitution. Clause (b) simultaneously extinguishes the privy purse and all
associated rights. No court can award compensation or enforce any claim arising from the pre-amendment
privy purse obligation: the extinguishment is total and the article overrides all other constitutional
provisions.

Article 366(22), which had defined "Ruler" and which was the vehicle the executive had tried to use in
1970, was amended by the 26th Amendment to give a historical rather than operative definition of the
term. The Ruler and their successors lost their special status on 28 December 1971 and became ordinary
citizens with no constitutional entitlement to recognition or payment.

## Significance

Article 363A illustrates the relationship between judicial review and constitutional amendment in India.
A Supreme Court ruling that blocked executive action was overcome not by a subsequent executive measure
but by using the amending power under Article 368 to change the constitutional text itself. The episode
also prefigured debates in *Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala* (1973), argued just two years later,
about the limits of Parliament's power to extinguish constitutionally guaranteed rights through the
amendment process.

## Why it matters

The abolition of privy purses was one of the most symbolically charged acts of the post-independence
Indian state. The princely rulers had entered the Union on terms that included explicit guarantees of
their personal privileges and financial payments — guarantees that carried constitutional weight under
Articles 291 and 362. The decision to extinguish those guarantees through constitutional amendment was
not simply a fiscal measure (the total annual outlay on privy purses at abolition was modest by national
budget standards). It was a declaration that the Indian state would not indefinitely maintain a feudal
aristocracy as a constitutionally protected class.

The political economy of the abolition explains much about why it happened when it did. The Congress
party under Indira Gandhi fought the 1971 general election partly on the platform of "Garibi Hatao"
(Remove Poverty), and the retention of hereditary royal payments sat uneasily with that platform.
The electoral sweep of 1971 gave the party the two-thirds majority in both Houses needed for
constitutional amendment — a majority it had lacked in 1970 when the executive order was struck down.
Article 363A therefore represents a case where the constitutional requirement of a special majority
served its intended purpose: the change required genuine democratic endorsement, not just executive will.

For students of constitutional law, Article 363A also matters because it operated in conjunction with
the simultaneous repeal of Articles 291 and 362. This three-part operation — insertion of 363A, repeal
of 291, repeal of 362 — was necessary because Article 363A's "notwithstanding" clause would have been
insufficient to override specific constitutional guarantees without also removing those guarantees from
the text. Parliament did not merely override earlier provisions; it deleted them. This technique of
comprehensive constitutional housekeeping, rather than leaving superseded provisions in place as
constitutional debris, is worth noting as a matter of drafting practice.

## Reading the provision

The operative language of Article 363A is deceptively simple but carefully constructed. The opening
phrase — "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution or in any law for the time being in force" —
creates the broadest possible override. It overrides not just Articles 291 and 362 (which were
simultaneously repealed) but also any other constitutional provision that might otherwise require
compensation for the extinction of property-like rights. In particular, it overrides the right to
property as it then stood (Article 31, which was itself significantly amended over this period) and
prevents any claim for compensation for the abolition of the privy purse.

Clause (a) deals with status: recognition ceases on the commencement of the 26th Amendment. The clause
covers not just the recognised ruler but also any "successor" who had been recognised as such. This
prevents the argument that a successor's recognition, once granted, survived the abolition of the
original recognition. The word "cease" operates automatically on commencement — there is no requirement
for a further presidential act or notification. The rulers became ex-rulers by operation of law on
28 December 1971.

Clause (b) deals with the financial entitlement. The language is unusually emphatic: the privy purse
"is abolished" and "all rights, liabilities and obligations in respect of privy purse are extinguished."
The use of both "abolished" and "extinguished" is deliberate — the first addresses the institution, the
second addresses any residual legal claims that might have survived the abolition. The reference to "any
other person" at the end of clause (b) ensures that payments made to nominees or assignees of rulers
(rather than to the rulers directly) are also cut off. No person of any description retains any
entitlement to payment of privy purse after commencement.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**Madhav Rao Scindia v. Union of India (1971).** This is the foundational case that made Article 363A
necessary. The Supreme Court, by a majority of nine to two, held that the presidential order purporting
to de-recognise all rulers under Article 366(22) was unconstitutional. The Court held that "recognition"
under Article 366(22) and the attendant rights under Articles 291 and 362 were constitutional rights
that could not be extinguished by executive order. The majority's reasoning turned on the distinction
between recognition (a constitutional act that conferred status and financial rights) and de-recognition
(which, if it extinguished those rights, required the higher procedural bar of constitutional amendment).
The dissenting judges, including Chief Justice Sikri, would have held the order valid. The case was
decided just as Parliament was preparing the 26th Amendment; the judgment arrived in early 1971 and
the amendment was passed later that year following the election.

**H.H. Maharaja Pratap Singh v. Union of India (1981).** After the abolition, litigation continued over
the scope of what had been extinguished. In Pratap Singh, the question was whether personal privileges
beyond the privy purse — such as the use of certain honors and the retention of personal property —
had been abolished by Article 363A or only those rights specifically connected to the privy purse.
The Court clarified that Article 363A's scope was limited to the privy purse as a financial payment
and to the formal status of "Ruler" as a constitutional category; it did not abolish other personal
privileges or property rights that rulers held in their individual capacity under ordinary law. The
judgment confirmed the precise boundary of the 26th Amendment's operation and left intact the ordinary
property rights and some honorific uses that had no specific constitutional basis in the pre-amendment
Articles 291 and 362.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973).** While this case did not directly involve Article 363A,
the arguments in Kesavananda were shaped partly by the privy purse episode. Counsel for the petitioners
pointed to the 26th Amendment as an example of Parliament using the amending power to extinguish
constitutionally guaranteed rights without compensation. The Court's decision in Kesavananda —
establishing the basic structure doctrine and holding that Parliament could not amend the Constitution
so as to destroy its basic structure — was a response in part to precisely this kind of use of the
amending power. The privy purse abolition survived the Kesavananda framework because abolishing an
aristocratic financial privilege was not held to attack any basic structural feature of the Constitution;
indeed, the egalitarian thrust of the abolition was consistent with the Constitution's commitment to
equality.

**The political prelude: the Rajya Sabha defeat of 1970.** This is not a court case but a constitutional
incident of the first order. In 1970, before the 1971 general election, the Congress government
introduced a constitution amendment bill to abolish privy purses. The bill passed the Lok Sabha but fell
short of the required two-thirds majority in the Rajya Sabha by a handful of votes. This failure led
the government to attempt the executive route via presidential de-recognition — the route struck down
in Madhav Rao Scindia. The 1971 election, fought explicitly on the privy purse issue among others,
produced the majorities needed for the 26th Amendment. The episode illustrates how the requirement of
special parliamentary majorities for constitutional amendments serves as a genuine constraint that can
delay but ultimately not prevent fundamental change when democratic opinion strongly favours it.

## Current significance

Article 363A is fully spent as an operative provision. All privy purses were abolished on 28 December
1971 and no payment has been made under that head since. The category of "Ruler" for constitutional
purposes no longer exists. In that sense, the article is historical text, not living law.

However, Article 363A retains significance in several contemporary dimensions. First, the former royal
families — the descendants of the princely rulers — remain politically prominent in many parts of India.
Several have been elected to Parliament and State Legislatures. Their public authority now derives
entirely from democratic politics, not from constitutional recognition, but their social standing and
electoral influence in their former territories often reflects the legacy of their historical position.
Article 363A is the constitutional line that separates the historical from the contemporary: on one
side, constitutionally guaranteed status and payment; on the other, ordinary citizenship.

Second, Article 363A's history is a reference point in ongoing debates about the scope of Parliament's
amending power. Can Parliament use Article 368 to extinguish constitutionally guaranteed entitlements
without providing compensation? The answer given by the 26th Amendment, and implicitly endorsed by
the post-Kesavananda Court, is yes — provided the amendment does not destroy the basic structure.
The privy purse abolition has been cited in subsequent property and compensation cases as an authoritative
example of legitimate use of the amending power to achieve redistributive or egalitarian objectives.

Third, some former rulers or their descendants have occasionally revived claims to personal property,
honorific titles, or other residual privileges that they argue were not swept away by Article 363A.
Courts have consistently held that Article 363A operated precisely on those rights that were
constitutionally grounded in Articles 291 and 362 and that ordinary property rights held in personal
capacity were unaffected. These cases, though individually narrow, keep Article 363A in judicial view
as a provision with defined but not unlimited scope.
