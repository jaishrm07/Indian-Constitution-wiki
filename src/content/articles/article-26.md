## Why it matters

Religion in India is not only practised individually. It is also organised institutionally — through temples, mosques, churches, gurudwaras, mutts, waqfs, and countless other collective structures that own property, manage endowments, provide charitable services, and transmit religious tradition across generations. Article 26 is the constitutional provision that protects these collective structures from State interference in their internal religious affairs while simultaneously ensuring that their secular activities remain subject to law.

The practical stakes of Article 26 are enormous. Temple endowments in South India control hundreds of billions of rupees in property accumulated over centuries. Waqf properties constitute one of the largest non-governmental landholdings in the country. Christian educational institutions operate some of the most prominent schools and colleges in India. The constitutional protection these institutions enjoy for their religious autonomy, and the extent to which State governments can regulate their administration, finances, and employment practices, are determined by Article 26.

The provision is also the primary constitutional instrument through which religious institutions have resisted social reform legislation. When Tamil Nadu or Andhra Pradesh enacted laws requiring the State-managed administration of Hindu religious endowments, the affected temples and mutts challenged the laws as violating Article 26's right to manage religious affairs. When the Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Boards appointed executive officers to manage wealthy temples, the hereditary priests and trustees challenged their displacement as a violation of denominational autonomy. These cases have produced one of the most elaborate bodies of constitutional doctrine in India — the religion-secular distinction, the denominational identity test, the essential religious practices test — all tools courts use to adjudicate between religious autonomy and State regulatory power.

The constitutional qualifications on Article 26 are narrower than on Article 25. Article 26 is qualified only by public order, morality, and health — not by "the other provisions of this Part" as Article 25 is. This means the equality guarantees of Articles 14 and 15 do not automatically override Article 26 rights in the way they can override Article 25 rights. Whether equality claims can nonetheless limit Article 26's protection — particularly when denominational practice discriminates on grounds of sex or caste — is an unresolved constitutional question that the Sabarimala case and its ongoing nine-judge bench reference directly raise.

## Reading the provision

**Who holds the right — "religious denomination or any section thereof"**: Not every religious group qualifies as a denomination for Article 26 purposes. Courts have held that a denomination requires (a) a collection of individuals who have a system of beliefs or doctrines which they regard as conducive to their spiritual wellbeing; (b) a common organisation; and (c) designation by a distinctive name. These requirements set a threshold that excludes informal religious communities, individual religious leaders without organised followings, and sects within a religion that have not acquired a sufficiently distinct institutional identity. The test is fact-dependent — courts examine the history, organisation, and self-understanding of each group claiming denominational status.

**Clause (a) — establishing and maintaining institutions**: Denominations have the right to create and sustain institutional structures — temples, churches, mosques, educational institutions attached to religious bodies, hospitals, orphanages, and other charitable enterprises. "Maintain" adds an ongoing right — not just initial establishment but continued operation. State legislation that effectively destroys an established institution by stripping it of its endowments, its governance structure, or its capacity to continue operating would violate this clause. The right is to establish institutions "for religious and charitable purposes" — both religious and charity-connected institutions are protected, reflecting the historical integration of religious and welfare functions in Indian institutional life.

**Clause (b) — managing "matters of religion"**: This is the most contested clause. Courts have held that "matters of religion" includes religious rites and ceremonies, forms of worship, modes of ritual, and the doctrinal content of the faith. It does not include the secular administration of religious institutions — managing finances, maintaining property, employing administrative staff, running the organisational side of the denomination. The State can legislate for the secular dimensions of religious institutional life without violating Article 26(b). The critical (and difficult) question is where the religious ends and the secular begins — a question that recurs in every endowment management case and every temple-entry dispute.

**Clauses (c) and (d) — property and its administration**: The right to own and acquire property is constitutionally guaranteed by clause (c) — subject only to the general qualifications of Article 26 (public order, morality, health). Clause (d)'s protection for administration of property comes with an important qualifier: "in accordance with law." This means State legislation governing how religious institutions hold, account for, and apply their property is constitutionally valid — the guarantee is of administration according to existing law, not administration free from all legal constraint. The clause does not protect denominational property management from regulation; it protects the denomination's right to administer its property (as opposed to having that administration transferred entirely to the State).

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly included Article 26 to meet two distinct concerns. First, religious minority communities — particularly Muslims, Christians, and Parsees — needed constitutional assurance that their institutional structures (mosques, churches, fire temples, waqfs, educational institutions) would not be nationalised or administratively absorbed by a Hindu-majority State legislature. Second, established Hindu religious institutions — particularly the great mutts of South India and the ancient temples of Tamil Nadu, Andhra, Kerala, and Karnataka — had long traditions of autonomous management that their trustees and hereditary priests were anxious to protect.

The balance struck was characteristic of the Constitution's approach to religion — neither establishment nor absolute separation, but a framework that preserves institutional religious autonomy while allowing social reform and regulatory oversight. Dr. Ambedkar was explicit that Article 26 was not intended to prevent legislatures from reforming the administration of religious institutions — it was intended to prevent them from entering the domain of religious doctrine and ritual practice. The line between reforming administration and dictating doctrine has been the subject of litigation ever since.

## Key cases

**Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments, Madras v Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar of Sri Shirur Mutt (1954)** — The foundational Article 26 judgment. The Shirur Mutt challenged the Madras Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act, which vested management of certain religious institutions in State-appointed trustees. The Court held that the denomination has an absolute right under Article 26(b) to manage its own affairs in matters of religion — the State cannot take over the management of the internal religious activities of a mutt. However, the secular aspects — financial administration, property maintenance — are regulable. The Court also developed the essential religious practices test: Article 26(b) protects only those practices that are essential or integral to the religion concerned. This case established the secular/religious distinction that shapes every subsequent Article 26 dispute.

**Ratilal Panachand Gandhi v State of Bombay (1954)** — The Court struck down portions of the Bombay Public Trusts Act that gave State authorities power to direct how charitable religious trusts used their funds. While the State can regulate how trusts account for funds, it cannot direct the religious purposes to which those funds are applied. The case drew the line precisely: procedural and financial regulation is permitted, substantive direction of religious activity is not. The judgment remains the clearest articulation of what Article 26(d)'s "in accordance with law" means — it preserves regulatory power but does not permit substitution of State judgment for denominational judgment on religious matters.

**S P Mittal v Union of India (1983)** — The Auroville case. The Court considered whether the Sri Aurobindo Society and the residents of Auroville constituted a religious denomination entitled to Article 26 protection. The Court held that the Aurobindo movement did not qualify as a religious denomination — it lacked the requisite system of religious beliefs and the distinctive institutional identity that Article 26 requires. The State could therefore take over the management of Auroville under the Auroville Foundation Act without violating Article 26. The case remains the leading authority on what constitutes a "religious denomination" for constitutional purposes.

**Bramchari Sidheshwar Shai v State of West Bengal (1995)** — The Ramakrishna Mission sought to be declared a minority religious denomination distinct from Hinduism in order to claim protection for its educational institutions under Articles 26 and 30. The Supreme Court, by a seven-to-two majority, held that the Ramakrishna Mission is part of the Hindu religion and does not constitute a separate religious denomination. This ruling illustrated the complexity of the denominational identity test in the context of reform movements within Hinduism and the institutional consequences of denominational versus majority-religion status.

**Indian Young Lawyers Association v State of Kerala (2018) — The Sabarimala Case** — The five-judge bench majority held that the Travancore Devaswom Board's practice of excluding women aged 10–50 from Sabarimala was not an essential religious practice of the Ayyappa devotees and that the devotees did not constitute a separate religious denomination (which would give them Article 26(b) protection for internal management). The case directly raised the question whether a practice that was claimed to be essential to a denomination's religious affairs could be overridden by the equality provisions of Part III. Justice Indu Malhotra's dissent argued that the majority had failed to apply the Article 26(b) denomination test correctly and had improperly subordinated the Devaswom Board's denominational autonomy to equality claims. The matter is before a nine-judge bench.

**Waqf Board cases — ongoing**: State Waqf Board legislation has been challenged under Article 26 on the ground that State-appointed Waqf Board officials managing Muslim religious endowments interfere with the denomination's right to manage its own affairs under clause (b). The Waqf (Amendment) Act 2025 has significantly expanded State oversight of Waqf properties and introduced provisions requiring non-Muslim representation on Waqf Boards — generating fresh Article 26 challenges that are now before various High Courts and likely to reach the Supreme Court.

## Current significance

State laws governing Hindu religious endowments remain the most active domain of Article 26 litigation. The Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Karnataka, and Kerala Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Acts each involve State-appointed officers managing major temples, controlling temple finances, and in some cases directing the appointment of priests and the performance of rituals. Challenges under Article 26(b) argue that State officers directing ritual practice violate the denomination's right to manage its own affairs in matters of religion. Counter-arguments invoke the secular/religious distinction and the State's legitimate interest in preventing corruption and mismanagement in endowments that serve millions of worshippers.

The nine-judge bench reference arising from Sabarimala is directly relevant to Article 26's future. The bench is addressing three interlocking questions: whether the essential religious practices test is the right framework for deciding what falls within Article 26(b); whether the equality provisions of Part III can override Article 26(b) denominational autonomy even when the denomination claims a practice is essential; and whether constitutional morality (the values embedded in the Constitution) or popular morality (the religious community's own values) should govern when the two conflict. The answers will reshape the constitutional framework for every Article 26 dispute involving the intersection of religious autonomy and equality.

The Waqf (Amendment) Act 2025 has introduced a new and significant cluster of Article 26 disputes. Non-Muslim members on Waqf Boards, enhanced State oversight of Waqf property, and new registration and titling requirements are all being challenged as violations of Article 26(b) and (d) — the Muslim community's right to manage its own religious affairs and administer its property. These challenges are among the most significant religious-freedom cases currently before Indian courts.
