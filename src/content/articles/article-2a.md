## What this Article did

Article 2A was inserted by the Constitution (Thirty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1974, effective 1 March 1975.
It provided that Sikkim shall be associated with the Union of India as a special category, with Parliament
having the power to make provisions for regulating and managing the association between Sikkim and India.
The constitutional design was novel — Sikkim would be neither a full State of the Union under Article 1
nor a Union Territory, but an "associate" state with a defined relationship governed by a special statute
(the Government of Sikkim Act, which was appended to the Constitution as a new Schedule 10A).

Sikkim at the time was a protectorate of India. Under the Treaty of Tumlong (1973) and subsequent
negotiations, the Chogyal (ruler) of Sikkim and the Indian government had been working toward a closer
relationship. The "associate state" formula was a political compromise: a step toward integration that
stopped short of full merger. The 35th Amendment also amended Article 80 to give Sikkim one seat in
the Rajya Sabha.

## The path to full statehood and Article 2A's omission

The "associate state" arrangement lasted barely months. In April 1975, the Sikkim State Assembly —
elected in April 1974 under the supervision of the Chief Election Commissioner of India — passed a
resolution requesting accession to India as a full State. Following a referendum in which a large majority
voted in favour of merger, Parliament enacted the Constitution (Thirty-sixth Amendment) Act, 1975, which
amended Article 1 and the First Schedule to add Sikkim as the 22nd State, inserted Article 371F
containing special provisions for Sikkim, and omitted Article 2A as no longer necessary.

Article 2A was thus in force from 1 March 1975 to 26 April 1975 — a constitutional provision with a
lifespan of under two months. Its omission by section 5 of the 36th Amendment, effective 26 April 1975,
was straightforward: with Sikkim as a full State, the "associate" status provision had been superseded.

## Constitutional and historical significance

The Sikkim accession through Article 2A and its successor Article 371F illustrates several dimensions of
Indian constitutional practice. First, it demonstrates the Constitution's flexibility in creating
intermediate categories (associate statehood) as a transitional mechanism during a sensitive geopolitical
negotiation. Second, the speed of the transition — from associate state to full State in under two months
— reflected the pace of political change on the ground following the April 1975 resolution.

The accession was and remains politically contested in some quarters. China does not recognise Sikkim
as part of India, and the Chogyal contested the legality of the merger. Article 371F, which preserved
certain Sikkim-specific protections including the special status of Sikkimese subjects and their rights
in Sikkim lands, was the constitutional acknowledgment that accession came with special obligations.
The Supreme Court in Sonam Wangdi v. Union of India (2014) upheld certain Sikkim-specific income tax
exemptions as flowing from Article 371F's special status framework.

Article 2A's tombstone entry in Part I of the Constitution is a reminder that the territorial map of
the Union has been dynamic — shaped by negotiation, referendum, and legislative action rather than fixed
at the moment of independence.

## How to read Article 2A today

Article 2A no longer has any operative effect. It is retained in the constitutional text as a historical
marker, indicating the intermediate constitutional step through which Sikkim transitioned from a
protectorate to an associate state and then to a full State. Reading it alongside Article 371F (which
contains Sikkim's permanent special provisions) and Article 2 (Parliament's power to admit new States)
provides the complete arc of Sikkim's constitutional integration.

## Why it matters

The brief existence of Article 2A is not a footnote to constitutional history — it is a window into
the Constitution's capacity for creative adaptation. India's founders designed Article 2 and Article 3
to allow Parliament to create and alter States, but the Sikkim situation required a category that had
not been anticipated: a territory with its own monarchy, its own legislature, its own foreign treaty
relationships, but seeking a formal constitutional relationship with India short of full merger.
Article 2A invented that category. The fact that it worked — even for just seven weeks — demonstrates
that the amending power under Article 368 can be used to solve immediate political problems with
constitutional precision.

The broader significance of Article 2A lies in what it signals about the Indian Union's relationship
with territories on its borders. The 1950 Treaty of Peace and Friendship between India and Sikkim had
already made Sikkim a protectorate, with India responsible for its external affairs, defence, and
communications. The 35th Amendment translated that diplomatic reality into constitutional form. The
36th Amendment then completed the integration. Together, the two amendments show that constitutional
union can be staged — that full incorporation need not happen in a single legislative step when political
circumstances require a more gradual approach.

Article 2A also matters because its deletion itself carries meaning. The provision was not merely
superseded; it was affirmatively omitted from the text of the Constitution. Unlike the retention of
Article 370 as "[Inoperative]" for decades after its abrogation in 2019 (pending formal reorganisation
proceedings), Article 2A was deleted cleanly because the purpose it served had been completely fulfilled.
This distinction between omission and inoperativeness is itself a constitutional choice about how the
text should present the Union's history.

## Reading the provision

Reading Article 2A requires understanding what it authorised rather than what its text said. The article
as it stood was a framework provision: it declared Sikkim to be "associated" with the Union and gave
Parliament the power to define the terms of that association by legislation. The substance of the
association — the Sikkim Assembly, the joint management of external affairs, the constitutional position
of the Chogyal — was contained in the Government of Sikkim Act, 1974 and the new Tenth Schedule A
appended to the Constitution by the 35th Amendment.

The constitutional design was hierarchical. Article 2A sat in Part I alongside Article 1 (name and
territory of the Union) and Article 2 (Parliament's power to admit new States). Its placement in Part I
made clear that Sikkim's "association" was not merely a treaty arrangement but a constitutional status.
Yet Article 2A carefully avoided saying Sikkim was "part of the territory of India" — that language
belongs to Article 1 and was only applied to Sikkim by the 36th Amendment when Sikkim was listed in
the First Schedule. The distinction between constitutional "association" and constitutional membership
of the Union was deliberate and reflected the political sensitivities of the moment.

The reference to Sikkim's formal status during the brief period of Article 2A's operation is also a
reminder that constitutional text is read in the context of surrounding provisions. The 35th Amendment's
addition of Article 2A alongside its amendment of the Ninth Schedule, Article 80, and the supplementary
Government of Sikkim Act created a micro-regime for Sikkim's governance. No single provision can be
read in isolation when an entire set of amendments operates together to constitute a new constitutional
relationship.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**The 1973 Treaty of Tumlong and the political background.** The immediate precursor to Article 2A was
the tripartite agreement of May 1973 between the Indian government, the Chogyal, and the political
parties of Sikkim (principally the Sikkim National Congress and the Sikkim State Congress). The agreement
followed popular unrest against the Chogyal's autocratic rule and provided for elections under Indian
supervision. India's political control over Sikkim deepened through 1974 and 1975. The constitutional
device of Article 2A formalised an existing reality: India had been exercising near-complete control
over Sikkim's external affairs and increasingly over its internal administration since the protectorate
treaty of 1950.

**The April 1975 Referendum and Resolution.** Following the April 1974 elections to the Sikkim Assembly,
the Assembly — dominated by the Sikkim National Congress, which favoured full merger with India — passed
a resolution on 10 April 1975 declaring Sikkim a constituent unit of India and requesting its
incorporation as a State. A referendum was held on 14 April 1975, and over 97 per cent of voters
reportedly voted in favour of merger. The referendum results and the Assembly resolution formed the
political basis for the 36th Amendment. The Chogyal refused to endorse the resolution and was stripped
of power. The constitutional legitimacy of the referendum was disputed, but the Supreme Court did not
subsequently accept any challenge to the validity of the 36th Amendment.

**Sonam Wangdi v. Union of India (2014).** This case did not directly concern Article 2A, which had
long been deleted, but it arose from the framework Article 2A had been part of. The Court upheld the
income tax exemption applicable to Sikkimese residents under Article 371F(k), which preserved special
tax arrangements that had applied to Sikkim before accession. The judgment confirmed that Article 371F's
special provisions — the permanent successor to the Article 2A framework — were enforceable constitutional
rights, not merely transitional courtesies.

**China's non-recognition and the geopolitical dimension.** Although not a domestic judicial incident,
China's refusal until 2003–2005 to formally acknowledge Sikkim as part of India is a dimension of
Article 2A's constitutional legacy. The provision was partly a response to the geopolitical necessity
of anchoring Sikkim's relationship with India in the Constitution itself, making that relationship harder
to dispute or unwind. The deletion of Article 2A in favour of Article 371F — which places Sikkim
squarely within the Union as an ordinary State with special provisions — was the constitutional
completion of that project.

## Current significance

Article 2A has no operative legal effect today. No rights can be asserted under it and no obligations
flow from it. Its current significance is entirely historical and conceptual. For constitutional scholars,
it remains significant as the only instance in Indian constitutional history of a provision explicitly
creating "associate state" status — a category that scholars of comparative federalism will recognise
from the experience of other federal systems that have created asymmetric or graduated membership
arrangements.

For lawyers and judges, Article 2A's trajectory offers a model for thinking about how constitutional
provisions relate to their context. The provision worked because it was narrowly targeted (creating one
relationship for one territory), time-limited in practical effect (intended to be superseded by full
accession), and connected to detailed subsidiary legislation (the Government of Sikkim Act) that gave
it operational content. Constitutional provisions that serve transitional or bridging purposes are often
the least studied but among the most important in practice.

For policymakers, Article 2A's history poses a question that retains contemporary relevance: what
constitutional mechanism is available if India were to seek a formal relationship with a neighbouring
territory short of full incorporation? Article 2A suggests the answer lies in the amending power under
Article 368. The Constitution can accommodate new categories, but only if Parliament is willing to act
and the political conditions permit. The Sikkim experience also illustrates the risks of intermediate
arrangements: once the "associate" status was established, the pressure for full integration became
irresistible, and the intermediate category lasted barely two months.

Article 2A's successor framework under Article 371F also retains active significance. The special
protections for old settlers in Sikkim — particularly regarding land ownership and reservation of
government employment — have been litigated repeatedly and continue to be politically salient. The
Sikkim High Court and the Supreme Court have been called upon to define who qualifies as a "Sikkimese
subject" for the purpose of these protections, and the relationship between Article 371F's land
protections and the rights of citizens from other States under Article 19(1)(e) has been an ongoing
constitutional tension. None of this post-1975 jurisprudence arises from Article 2A directly, but it
all flows from the integration that Article 2A initiated. Understanding Article 2A is therefore a
prerequisite for fully understanding Article 371F and the litigation around it.

The deletion of Article 2A from Part I of the Constitution also raises a question of constitutional
memory. Part I now reads as if Sikkim was always a State in the ordinary sense, listed in the First
Schedule alongside Andhra Pradesh and Maharashtra. The omitted Article 2A is invisible in the running
text unless the reader knows to look for the tombstone entry. This selective visibility — where the
intermediate step of "associate statehood" is technically present but practically invisible — is
characteristic of how constitutional documents record transformative historical events through text
that presupposes its own completion.
