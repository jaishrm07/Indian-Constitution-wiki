## Why it matters

Article 243W is the functional core of urban constitutional self-government. The Seventy-Fourth Amendment did not simply create new constitutional bodies — it attempted to give those bodies real governing authority over urban life. Article 243W authorises State legislatures to devolve powers necessary for municipalities to function as "institutions of self-government" rather than as subordinate arms of State administration. The Twelfth Schedule lists eighteen subjects — ranging from urban planning, regulation of land use, public health, and sanitation to slum improvement, urban poverty alleviation, and maintenance of public amenities — that municipalities may receive jurisdiction over. The phrase "institutions of self-government" has doctrinal weight: courts have used it to test whether devolution laws genuinely transfer decision-making authority or merely delegate administrative tasks while retaining State control over all consequential decisions.

The urban devolution problem has a distinctive structural dimension that makes it more intractable than its rural counterpart under Article 243G. Cities are centres of State revenue, commercial regulation, and large-scale infrastructure investment. State governments have especially powerful incentives to retain control over urban land, urban planning, and urban development because decisions in these domains generate significant economic rents and political patronage opportunities. This creates a systematic bias against devolving meaningful authority under Article 243W, even in states that have been relatively progressive about rural Panchayati Raj devolution. The resulting governance vacuum is filled by parastatal bodies — development authorities, industrial development corporations, housing boards, water supply and sewerage boards — that perform Twelfth Schedule functions outside the accountability framework of elected municipal government.

The parastatal problem is the defining structural failure of Article 243W's implementation. In most major Indian cities, the metropolitan development authority controls land use and town planning, the water board controls water supply and sewerage, the housing board controls public housing, and the state transport corporation controls mass transit — while the elected municipal corporation is left with street lighting, solid waste collection, and property tax administration as its primary domains. This fragmentation means that the elected body accountable to urban residents is responsible for the least consequential decisions, while the most consequential decisions — where the city grows, who gets water, what gets built — are made by bodies appointed by the State government and answerable only to it. Article 243W's promise of urban self-government has not been realised in any Indian major city in comprehensive form.

Clause (b) of Article 243W extends the devolution framework downward to Committees within municipalities — including the Ward Committees mandated under Article 243S and other intra-municipal committees. This is constitutionally significant because it contemplates a system of participatory neighbourhood governance within cities, with ward-level elected and deliberative bodies receiving genuine powers rather than merely advisory roles. The failure to constitute functional Ward Committees in most Indian cities, and the failure of states to endow them with meaningful authority under clause (b), represents the second tier of the Article 243W devolution deficit.

## Reading the provision

The opening phrase — "Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, the Legislature of a State may, by law, endow" — establishes the permissive character of Article 243W's devolution mechanism. Like its counterpart for Panchayats in Article 243G, this article does not self-execute. A municipality has no constitutional authority over Twelfth Schedule subjects simply because those subjects are listed. Authority vests only when the State legislature passes a positive law. This design reflects the constitutional compromise at the time of the 74th Amendment: urban local government reform was inserted into the Constitution, but State governments were not stripped of their legislative authority over municipalities under Entry 5 of the State List.

Clause (a)(i) — "the preparation of plans for economic development and social justice" — gives municipalities a constitutional planning mandate. This is more than the power to prepare a budget; it envisions municipalities as active institutions that formulate development priorities for their area, integrate land use planning with economic development goals, and align local resource allocation with social justice objectives. In practice, State Town Planning Acts typically retain master planning and development plan functions with the State government or its agencies, making genuine municipal planning authority the exception rather than the rule.

Clause (a)(ii) — "the performance of functions and the implementation of schemes as may be entrusted to them including those in relation to the matters listed in the Twelfth Schedule" — contains the Twelfth Schedule reference. The eighteen subjects include urban planning, regulation of land use, planning for economic and social development, roads and bridges, water supply, public health and sanitation, fire services, urban forestry, slum improvement, urban poverty alleviation, public amenities, and promotion of cultural, educational, and aesthetic aspects of urban life. The phrase "as may be entrusted to them" signals that entrusting requires an affirmative act by the State legislature or executive; functions are not transferred merely because they appear on the Twelfth Schedule.

Clause (b) — the Committee devolution clause — enables State legislatures to endow the committees referred to in Articles 243S and 243T (Ward Committees and other intra-municipal committees) with powers and authority related to Twelfth Schedule subjects. This clause contemplates a sub-municipal layer of participatory governance. Ward Committees constitute the constitutional mechanism for deepening urban democracy below the level of the full municipal council, analogous to the Gram Sabha in rural governance. Their systematic non-constitution or non-empowerment across Indian cities represents a significant constitutional gap.

## Key cases

**Vikas Kishanrao Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021)** — While primarily an OBC reservation case under Article 243T, the Supreme Court's analysis extensively examined the constitutional framework of urban local self-government under Part IXA including Article 243W. The Court reaffirmed that municipalities are constitutional institutions, not merely creatures of statute, and that the State's obligations under Part IXA include ensuring that elected bodies have genuine governing authority. The ruling's broader framework of constitutional accountability for urban governance has been applied in subsequent challenges to parastatal displacement of municipal functions.

**State of Maharashtra v. Municipal Corporation of Greater Mumbai (2012) — Devolution versus parastatal displacement** — The Supreme Court examined the constitutional relationship between elected municipal bodies and State-created development authorities performing overlapping functions. While upholding the existence of parallel bodies, the Court noted that Article 243W creates an affirmative obligation to enable municipalities to function as genuine institutions of self-government, and that State governments cannot use parastatals to systematically hollow out municipal authority over Twelfth Schedule subjects. The ruling drew a distinction between legitimate State oversight and structural displacement of elected governance.

**Bangalore Development Authority v. State of Karnataka (2010)** — The Karnataka High Court, affirmed in principle by the Supreme Court, examined whether functions listed in the Twelfth Schedule could be indefinitely retained by a State development authority rather than transferred to the elected municipal corporation. The Court held that Article 243W read with the Twelfth Schedule created a constitutional expectation of devolution that required the State to justify the continued retention of urban development functions outside the elected municipal structure. The judgment rejected the argument that administrative efficiency considerations alone could justify permanent parastatal retention of Twelfth Schedule functions.

**Municipal Corporation of Delhi v. Birla Cotton (2003) — Scope of municipal powers after devolution** — The Supreme Court considered the extent of a municipal corporation's authority once functions had been devolved under a State law implementing Article 243W. The Court held that devolution creates genuine governing authority, not merely an administrative role, and that municipalities may exercise regulatory and planning powers within the scope of the devolved functions without further State approval for each individual decision. The ruling established that partial devolution — conferring functions while retaining State veto over each exercise of those functions — does not constitute genuine devolution under Article 243W.

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010)** — The Supreme Court's comprehensive review of the 73rd and 74th Amendment framework held that the constitutional design of local self-government was an integrated scheme. The Court's analysis of the functional devolution architecture — including Article 243W and the Twelfth Schedule — established that selective compliance with the 74th Amendment's institutional requirements, such as holding elections without devolving functions or funds, is inconsistent with the constitutional purpose of creating genuine urban institutions of self-government.

## Current significance

The gap between constitutional aspiration and political reality remains the defining problem of Article 243W. More than three decades after the Seventy-Fourth Amendment, most major Indian States have not fully devolved the eighteen Twelfth Schedule subjects to municipalities. Urban planning, land use regulation, and major infrastructure remain with State departments or parastatal bodies in almost every State. The Second Administrative Reforms Commission and successive Finance Commissions have consistently recommended greater functional devolution, and the Fifteenth Finance Commission specifically tied a portion of urban grants to measurable devolution indicators. Compliance has remained patchy, and the transparency requirements around devolution status — which states are required to publish — are poorly enforced.

The Smart Cities Mission launched in 2015 and the AMRUT programme have attracted particular criticism for inverting the constitutional design of Article 243W. Both programmes are administered by the Union Government through the Ministry of Housing and Urban Affairs, with funds channelled to cities through Special Purpose Vehicles in the case of Smart Cities. These SPVs are typically incorporated as companies under the Companies Act, with the state and city governments as shareholders, and are managed by executives accountable to state government rather than to the elected municipal council. Critics argue that this structure systematically bypasses the elected municipal body — the institution that Article 243W is meant to empower — and consolidates urban development decision-making in state-level and Union-level bureaucracies. The Comptroller and Auditor General has flagged governance concerns about SPV accountability in multiple audit reports, and the constitutional question of whether SPV-administered urban development is consistent with the Article 243W framework has not been definitively resolved by the courts.

Looking ahead, the rapid urbanisation of India — with the urban population expected to reach nearly 600 million by 2030 — makes the Article 243W devolution question increasingly urgent. Cities that are governed by fragmented, under-empowered, and fiscally starved municipal bodies cannot make the long-term infrastructure investments, land use decisions, and social service commitments that growing urban populations require. The constitutional promise of urban self-government embedded in Article 243W is not merely an institutional design preference; it reflects a democratic theory about who should make decisions affecting urban residents' lives. The failure of that promise is both a governance failure and a democratic one.
