  311. Dismissal, removal or reduction in rank of persons employed in civil capacities under the
  Union or a State.—(1) No person who is a member of a civil service of the Union or an all-India
  service or a civil service of a State or holds a civil post under the Union or a State shall be
  dismissed or removed by an authority subordinate to that by which he was appointed.

  (2) No such person as aforesaid shall be dismissed or removed or reduced in rank except after an
  inquiry in which he has been informed of the charges against him and given a reasonable opportunity
  of being heard in respect of those charges:

  Provided that where it is proposed after such inquiry, to impose upon him any such penalty, such
  penalty may be imposed on the basis of the evidence adduced during such inquiry and it shall not be
  necessary to give such person any opportunity of making representation on the penalty proposed:

  Provided further that this clause shall not apply—

  (a) where a person is dismissed or removed or reduced in rank on the ground of conduct which has
  led to his conviction on a criminal charge; or

  (b) where the authority empowered to dismiss or remove a person or to reduce him in rank is
  satisfied that for some reason, to be recorded by that authority in writing, it is not reasonably
  practicable to hold such inquiry; or

  (c) where the President or the Governor, as the case may be, is satisfied that in the interest of
  the security of the State it is not expedient to hold such inquiry.

  (3) If, in respect of any such person as aforesaid, a question arises whether it is reasonably
  practicable to hold such inquiry as is referred to in clause (2), the decision thereon of the
  authority empowered to dismiss or remove such person or to reduce him in rank shall be final.

## Why it matters

The civil service of a modern state requires both discipline and protection from capricious authority.
Without Article 311, the pleasure doctrine in Article 310 could be weaponised to dismiss inconvenient
civil servants without any process, turning the bureaucracy into an instrument of whoever holds power.
The historical backdrop is important: pre-independence India experienced the use of dismissal and
removal as tools of political control, and the framers — acutely aware of this — made Article 311 one
of the most detailed due-process provisions in the Constitution. Ambedkar in the Constituent Assembly
emphasised that civil servants needed the confidence that they could carry out their duties without
fear of arbitrary removal, and that this confidence was a precondition for an effective, impartial
public administration.

Article 311 is therefore the Constitution's answer to an ancient problem: how do you maintain the
government's power to remove unfit servants while preventing the removal power from destroying civil-
service independence? The answer is a mandatory inquiry, a communicable charge-sheet, and a genuine
hearing — with three tightly drawn exceptions that the Supreme Court has consistently refused to
expand beyond their constitutional text. The balance is precarious and the litigation it generates is
enormous: Article 311 is among the most litigated provisions in the Constitution, with thousands of
decisions from the Central Administrative Tribunal, High Courts, and the Supreme Court addressing its
meaning and limits in different factual and service contexts.

The article operates at three levels simultaneously. First, it is a procedural guarantee — it
prescribes what the government must do before punishing a servant. Second, it is a substantive
guarantee — courts have read the procedural requirements as implying that the punishment must be
proportionate to the misconduct found at inquiry. Third, it is a structural guarantee — the
requirement that a higher authority cannot be bypassed by a lower one (clause (1)) protects the
hierarchical integrity of the appointment and dismissal process, ensuring accountability runs upward
in the service in a constitutional direction. Together these three dimensions make Article 311 a
comprehensive safeguard against arbitrary executive action in the employment domain.

The article applies to dismissal, removal, and reduction in rank — but not to every adverse action.
Compulsory retirement, transfer, suspension, withholding of increments, and censure are generally
not covered by the full inquiry requirement, though courts have consistently held that if any of
these is in substance a disguised dismissal or reduction in rank, Article 311 applies to the action
in its real character. This boundary between covered and uncovered actions has itself been extensively
litigated, with courts developing a doctrine of "stigma" and "cast of misconduct" to determine
whether an apparently administrative action is in truth a punishment that requires inquiry.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) establishes what is sometimes called the "level rule" or "rank rule": no person can be
dismissed or removed by an authority subordinate to that by which he was appointed. This means that
if the President appointed a civil servant — as is the case for IAS and other Group A officers — no
authority subordinate to the President can remove that person. The clause prevents bypassing the
appointing authority by delegating the removal decision downward in the hierarchy. In practice it
ensures that dismissal of senior officers is handled at the appropriate level with the seriousness
it deserves, and that State governments cannot on their own authority remove officers whose
appointment was an act of the Union.

Clause (2) is the heart of the provision and contains three essential procedural requirements. First,
there must be an inquiry — a formal proceeding in which evidence is gathered and considered. Second,
the servant must be informed of the charges against him — the charge-sheet or memorandum of charges
must be sufficiently detailed to allow the servant to understand exactly what misconduct is alleged.
Third, the servant must be given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in respect of those charges —
this encompasses the right to submit a written reply to the charge-sheet, the right to appear before
the inquiring authority, the right to lead evidence in defence, the right to cross-examine prosecution
witnesses, and (in some circumstances) the right to legal representation. These three requirements
are cumulative: the absence of any one of them vitiates the inquiry.

The first proviso to clause (2) — introduced by the Constitution (Fifteenth Amendment) Act 1963 —
resolves a controversy that had generated conflicting High Court decisions. Before the amendment,
courts were divided on whether, after a completed inquiry that found misconduct, a fresh show-cause
notice on the proposed penalty was also required. The first proviso clarifies that once the inquiry
is complete, no separate opportunity to represent on the penalty is needed. The evidence at inquiry
is the basis for the penalty, and the servant has already had the opportunity to contest that
evidence. This streamlines the process without prejudicing the servant, who had the full inquiry
opportunity.

The second proviso contains the three exceptions that have generated the most litigation in the
history of this provision.

**Exception (a) — Criminal conviction**: Applies where the servant has been convicted on a criminal
charge arising from the same conduct that forms the ground for dismissal. The criminal court's
findings and the process of criminal trial are treated as a substitute for the departmental inquiry.
The exception is narrow: the criminal conviction must be for conduct that is the ground for dismissal;
a conviction on an unrelated matter does not dispense with the inquiry for a separate departmental
misconduct.

**Exception (b) — Impracticability**: Applies where the disciplinary authority is satisfied, for
reasons recorded in writing, that it is not "reasonably practicable" to hold an inquiry. This is the
most frequently invoked and the most litigated exception. Courts have interpreted "not reasonably
practicable" as meaning more than merely inconvenient, difficult, or expensive — it must be genuinely
impracticable in a meaningful sense. The most common instances recognised by courts include: witnesses
unavailable because they are involved in ongoing criminal proceedings; the servant absconding and
cannot be served; witnesses unwilling to depose for fear of harm; and the officer who would serve as
inquiring authority being of the same class as the charged servant. The recording of reasons is
mandatory and substantive — a bald assertion of impracticability without identifying specific facts
relied on will not satisfy the constitutional requirement.

**Exception (c) — Security of state**: Applies where the President or Governor is personally
satisfied that holding an inquiry would be contrary to the security of the State. This is the most
drastic exception and the least frequently applied. It cannot be delegated to any lesser authority.
Courts have held that this exception cannot be invoked merely because the servant holds a sensitive
post or because the misconduct involves classified information. There must be a genuine finding,
reached by the President or Governor personally, that the conduct of a departmental inquiry would
itself compromise security — for instance because the inquiry process would require disclosing
intelligence sources or classified operational methods. Courts have been unwilling to review the
merits of this satisfaction, but have held that the satisfaction must be genuine, not a fiction.

Clause (3) provides that the disciplinary authority's decision on whether it is reasonably practicable
to hold an inquiry is "final." The Supreme Court, however, has consistently read this finality as
subject to judicial review for mala fides, perversity, and non-application of mind. The finality
provision prevents courts from substituting their own assessment of practicability for that of the
disciplinary authority — but it does not immunise a dishonest or unreasoned decision from scrutiny.
This reading preserves judicial review while respecting the operational expertise of disciplinary
authorities and the constitutional design that places the initial judgment with the executive.

## Key cases

**Union of India v Tulsiram Patel (1985)** — A seven-judge Constitution Bench produced the most
comprehensive and authoritative reading of Article 311. The Court upheld all three exceptions to the
inquiry requirement as constitutionally valid and confirmed that each must be construed narrowly. On
the impracticability exception, the Court defined "not reasonably practicable" as something more than
inconvenient but not requiring impossibility — it is a fact-based judgment that specific circumstances
make a fair inquiry genuinely unworkable. The Court held that written reasons are both mandatory and
the basis for judicial review: a court can examine whether the reasons disclose a genuine case of
impracticability or whether they are a pretext for avoiding accountability. The judgment remains the
governing authority on all three exceptions and is cited in virtually every Article 311 case.

**Parshotam Lal Dhingra v Union of India (1958)** — The foundational Constitution Bench decision
distinguishing reversion from punitive dismissal. The Court held that Article 311 applies only to
action that is "by way of punishment" — action that imposes a penalty for misconduct. A reversion to
a permanent substantive post from a temporary higher post is not punishment unless imposed as a
sanction for misconduct. This distinction between administrative action and punitive action — which
turns on whether the action casts a stigma and is imposed because of misconduct — remains the
threshold test for Article 311's applicability in all subsequent cases.

**State of Andhra Pradesh v S Sree Rama Rao (1963)** — The Court held that "reasonable opportunity"
under clause (2) is a substantive guarantee, not a formality. The servant must be told the charges in
enough detail to mount a defence — vague or omnibus charges do not satisfy the constitutional
requirement. The servant must have a real (not illusory) opportunity to cross-examine witnesses and
present evidence. An inquiry that is rushed, that excludes relevant evidence, or that denies effective
participation violates the provision, and the resulting order will be set aside regardless of whether
the servant was ultimately guilty of the misconduct alleged.

**Divisional Personnel Officer, Southern Railway v T R Chellappan (1976)** — The Court examined
the distinction between "punishment" and administrative action in the context of compulsory
retirement, holding that the critical test is whether the action is founded on a finding of misconduct
and whether it carries the character of a penalty. If compulsory retirement is ordered as a sanction
for specific misconduct found in an inquiry, it becomes a punishment and attracts Article 311. If
ordered as a general service administration measure without specific findings of misconduct, it does
not. The case is important for the doctrine that the label on the order is irrelevant to the Article
311 analysis — it is the substance that determines the constitutional characterisation.

**State of Uttar Pradesh v Man Mohan Lal Srivastava (1987)** — The Court considered what constitutes
"adequate opportunity" in the inquiry, holding that the opportunity must be adequate in the specific
circumstances of the case. Adequacy is assessed by asking whether the servant was given a genuine and
real chance to defend himself, not whether some nominal process was completed. A servant who was given
a charge-sheet but denied time to prepare a reply, or denied access to documents on which the charges
are based, has not received adequate opportunity even though an inquiry was technically conducted.
The decision reinforces that Article 311's procedural requirements must be substantively satisfied.

**Kusheshwar Dubey v Bharat Coking Coal Ltd (1988)** — The Supreme Court extended Article 311
safeguards to employees of government-owned corporations in certain circumstances, applying the
principle that where the state discharges public functions through a corporate veil, constitutional
protections travel with the function. This decision opened the question of how far Article 311
applies beyond the formal civil services to the wider public sector — a question that continues to
be litigated as the government's economic footprint through public sector undertakings expands.

**State of Maharashtra v Veerappa R Saboji (1979)** — The Court addressed the situation where a
servant makes it impossible to hold an inquiry by absconding or refusing to participate. It confirmed
that in such circumstances the impracticability exception can validly be invoked, but stressed that
the disciplinary authority must exhaust reasonable attempts to serve the servant and conduct the
inquiry before concluding that it is not reasonably practicable to do so. The exception cannot be
pre-emptively invoked; it requires a genuine prior attempt to hold the inquiry.

## Current significance

Article 311 generates a steady stream of litigation across Central Administrative Tribunal benches,
High Courts, and the Supreme Court. The Central Administrative Tribunals — established under the
Administrative Tribunals Act 1985 for Union services — handle first-instance adjudication of the
majority of Article 311 cases involving Union civil servants, with appeals lying to High Courts and
ultimately to the Supreme Court. The sheer volume of litigation testifies to both the importance of
Article 311 protections and the frequency with which disciplinary authorities fail to comply with
the procedural requirements, either through negligence, haste, or a misunderstanding of the
constitutional standard.

Live issues in current litigation include: the validity of "voluntary retirement" pressures that are
in substance coerced dismissals, where courts apply Article 311 if the compulsion is established; the
use of the impracticability exception in terrorism and extremism cases where the state argues that
inquiry is impracticable because witnesses cannot come forward safely; the scope of Article 311
protection for contractual and daily-wage employees who fall outside the regular civil service; the
procedural standards for inquiries against senior IAS and IPS officers where political dimensions
colour the proceeding; and whether Article 311 applies to officers serving in autonomous bodies and
constitutional commissions. Each of these issues involves the Court in calibrating the degree of
deference owed to executive disciplinary judgment against the constitutional commitment to fair process.

The security-of-state exception under proviso (c) has historically been invoked most frequently in
Jammu and Kashmir. With the reorganisation of that State in 2019 and the altered administrative
landscape in the Union Territory of J&K, the frequency and circumstances of invocation have changed,
but the constitutional question remains: whether courts can scrutinise the President's or Governor's
"satisfaction" that a departmental inquiry would harm state security, and if so to what depth. The
Supreme Court's consistent position — that the satisfaction must be genuine but its correctness
cannot be reviewed — leaves a residual zone of constitutional tension that occasional cases bring
to the surface. The broader trajectory of Article 311 jurisprudence continues to move in the
direction of stronger procedural requirements and narrower exceptions, reflecting the Court's view
that procedural fairness in public employment is an aspect of the rule of law that the Constitution
is committed to maintaining as a living and enforceable guarantee.
