## Why it matters

Article 299 is the constitutional regulation of the government as contracting party. Without it, the vast machinery of public procurement — infrastructure, defence supplies, goods and services — would operate on the same footing as private contracts, without any constitutional safeguard against unauthorised commitments or personal liability for government officers. The article simultaneously protects the public purse (by voiding contracts that bypass constitutional form) and protects individual civil servants (by immunising them from personal liability). The price of this protection is strict formality — a formality that generates a large body of litigation when it is not followed.

The provision also reflects a deliberate constitutional choice about governmental accountability: by requiring every contract to be expressed in the name of the President or Governor, the Constitution ensures that government contractual obligations are traceable to identifiable constitutional authority rather than to the personal say-so of individual officers.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) has three elements: (a) the contract must be "expressed to be made" by the President (Union) or the Governor (State); (b) it must be executed — actually signed — by a person whom the President or Governor has directed or authorised; and (c) assurances of property (conveyances, leases, licences over government land) are subject to the same rule.

"Expressed to be made" means the contract document itself must, on its face, state that it is a contract of the President or Governor. A contract signed by a Secretary or an officer in their personal capacity, without naming the President or Governor, fails this test even if the officer was in fact authorised to make the contract. The formality goes to the expression in the document, not merely to the internal authority of the person signing.

Clause (2) provides that neither the President nor the Governor — nor any person signing on their behalf — shall be personally liable for any contract made under this article. This immunity is absolute: the officer cannot be sued personally even if the contract was void for non-compliance with clause (1).

## Key cases

**Mulamchand v. State of Madhya Pradesh AIR 1968 SC 1218** — This is the most significant Article 299 decision. The Supreme Court held firmly that a contract not expressed in the Governor's name — even if made by a high-ranking government officer who had full actual authority — was void and unenforceable against the State. The Court rejected both the doctrines of estoppel and part performance as means of enforcing a non-compliant government contract. The State could not be bound by representations or conduct that would have been actionable against a private party, because the constitutional form requirement was mandatory and the government's capacity to contract was defined by it. The Court's refusal to apply estoppel against the government in an ultra vires contract is the foundational principle of Article 299 jurisprudence.

**Chatturbhuj Vithaldas Jasani v. Moreshwar Parashram AIR 1954 SC 236** — The Supreme Court established the strict approach to the Article 299(1) formality requirement, holding that a contract not expressed in the Governor's name was unenforceable against the State and that substantial compliance was not sufficient. The Court explained that the constitutional requirement was designed to protect public funds and could not be relaxed by courts acting in equity.

**State of Bihar v. Abdul Majid AIR 1954 SC 245** — The Court held that where a government contract was void for non-compliance with Article 299, the civil servant who had made the contract was not personally liable. The transaction was simply void, leaving the contractor without a remedy against either the government or the officer — illustrating the double-edged nature of the article's protection.

**Union of India v. Rallia Ram AIR 1964 SC 1144** — The Court held that while a contractor could not sue on a void Article 299 contract, he was not without remedy: he could claim quantum meruit (reasonable compensation for work actually done) on the basis of restitution to prevent unjust enrichment of the government. This case introduced an equitable relief channel that partially offsets the harshness of the strict form requirement.

**New Marine Coal Co. v. Union of India AIR 1964 SC 152** — The Court drew an important distinction between the Article 299(1) mandatory form requirement and internal governmental financial delegation rules. Failure to follow internal rules about which officer has financial powers for a given contract value does not void a contract that is otherwise correctly expressed in the President's name and signed by a duly authorised person.

## The estoppel problem

The Mulamchand principle — that the government cannot be estopped into honouring a void Article 299 contract — creates significant hardship for contractors who deal in good faith with officers who have apparent authority. The rule is justified by reference to the constitutional character of the requirement: the government's capacity to contract is constitutionally defined, and the requirement protects public funds from unauthorised commitments. However, critics argue that the rule is too harsh in its practical operation, particularly where government officers have received and retained the benefit of work performed under a void contract. The quantum meruit remedy from Rallia Ram provides partial relief but does not extend to expectation damages for lost profits or consequential losses.

The Supreme Court has on several occasions been invited to relax the Mulamchand rule by holding that estoppel is available where the government has derived benefit from performance — but it has consistently declined to do so, treating the formality as a categorical constitutional requirement rather than a rebuttable presumption.

## Current significance

Article 299 has acute relevance in the era of digital public procurement. The Government e-Marketplace (GeM), e-procurement portals, and online tendering systems generate contracts through automated workflows where the document does not always expressly name the President or Governor as the contracting party. The Ministry of Finance's General Financial Rules 2017 and the government's standardised contract templates attempt to satisfy Article 299(1), but the transition to paperless contracting has created gaps that courts are increasingly asked to resolve. Whether a digitally executed work order naming a Department or Ministry — rather than expressly the President or Governor — satisfies the constitutional requirement is a live question that determines the enforceability of a substantial portion of public procurement. The answer has direct implications for both contractors seeking to enforce payment obligations and government bodies seeking to disclaim liability under informal or e-tendering arrangements.
