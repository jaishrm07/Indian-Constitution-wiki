## Why it matters

Union territories contain economically significant co-operative sectors — particularly Delhi, which has a large urban co-operative banking sector, a substantial network of housing co-operative societies, and a long-established consumer co-operative infrastructure. Puducherry has agricultural credit and production co-operative societies. Several island and smaller UTs have co-operative fishing and agricultural societies. Article 243ZS is the provision that extends Part IXB's constitutional governance standards to all of these, using the same substitution mechanism that the equivalent municipal provision (Article 243ZB) and equivalent Panchayat provision (Article 243L) employ for their respective Parts.

The architecture of Article 243ZS fits into a consistent three-Part design that the Constitution-makers established across Parts IX, IXA, and IXB. Each Part has its own UT-application provision: Article 243L for Panchayats, Article 243ZB for Municipalities, and Article 243ZS for Co-operative Societies. All three use the same substitution approach — Administrator for Governor, UT Legislature (where it exists) for State Legislature — and all three have a presidential adjustment power, though the precise direction of that power differs. The family resemblance across these provisions reflects a deliberate architectural choice to extend all three local governance frameworks uniformly to the UT context, while preserving flexibility for the diverse circumstances of individual territories.

Article 243ZS matters particularly because its application interacts with the post-Rajendra N. Shah constitutional landscape in ways that differ depending on which type of UT is involved. For UTs without Legislatures — where Parliament is effectively the legislature and the Administrator the executive — the application of Part IXB raises no federal competence problem: Parliament has plenary authority over those territories and is simply applying a governance standard to institutions within its own domain. For Delhi and Puducherry — UTs with their own Legislatures — the position is closer to the State situation that Rajendra N. Shah addressed, but the constitutional relationship between a UT government, the Administrator, and Parliament differs enough from the State-Parliament relationship that the ruling's precise implications for Article 243ZS's application to those UTs have not been definitively resolved. This constitutional ambiguity is a live question in Delhi co-operative governance law.

The provision also sits within India's increasingly active co-operative policy environment. The Ministry of Co-operation — constituted in 2021 — has emphasised urban co-operative banks and housing societies (both predominantly UT and urban phenomena) as areas requiring governance reform. Article 243ZS provides the constitutional foundation for Central oversight of co-operative governance in UTs, operating alongside the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act framework (applicable through Article 243ZR to societies whose objects extend beyond one UT or State) and the UT-specific co-operative legislation applicable through Article 243ZS to purely intra-UT societies.

## Reading the provision

The main body of Article 243ZS establishes two substitution rules simultaneously. For UTs without a Legislative Assembly — currently Andaman and Nicobar Islands, Chandigarh, Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu, Lakshadweep, and Ladakh — the Administrator appointed under Article 239 performs the role assigned to "the Legislature of a State" in Part IXB. This means that for Part IXB purposes, the Administrator exercises both the executive functions assigned to the "State Government" and the legislative functions assigned to the "Legislature of a State" — since for these territories the distinction between legislation and executive action is, from the perspective of the Central government, primarily formal. For UTs with a Legislative Assembly — Delhi and Puducherry — that Assembly plays the role of the "Legislature of a State."

The proviso to Article 243ZS gives the President power to direct that Part IXB "shall not apply" to any UT or part of a UT. This is an opt-out power for the President, rather than an opt-in. The baseline is application of Part IXB; presidential notification can remove that application for specified territories. This contrasts structurally with the equivalent provision in Article 243ZB (municipal Part IXA), where the proviso allows the President to direct that Part IXA shall apply to a UT with exceptions and modifications — an opt-in structure. The difference reflects a design choice: Part IXA's mandatory application was treated as the presumptive baseline for all UTs, from which the President may carve out modifications; Part IXB's application was treated as rebuttably appropriate, from which the President may carve out complete exemptions where the Part is simply not suitable for particular UT conditions.

The practical consequence of the opt-out structure is that very small or sparsely populated UTs — where co-operative societies are minimal in number or activity — can be exempted from Part IXB's governance requirements by Presidential notification, rather than being technically in breach of constitutional governance standards that were never designed for their scale of institutional life. This is a sensible administrative flexibility provision, though its use has been limited in practice.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah (2021) 6 SCC 1** — The Supreme Court's ruling on Part IXB's constitutional validity is the foundational authority for understanding what Article 243ZS now does and does not accomplish. The Court struck down Part IXB's application to State co-operative societies on the ground that Parliament lacked constitutional competence to impose governance standards on a State List subject through the amendment process. For UTs without Legislatures, the ruling's federal reasoning does not apply: Parliament has plenary authority over those UTs. For Delhi and Puducherry — UTs with Legislatures — the constitutional position is more uncertain: the Court did not directly address these cases, and the question of whether the Rajendra N. Shah federal rationale applies to UT-with-Legislature co-operative governance remains open.

**Delhi Co-operative Housing Finance Corporation v. Director, Co-operative Societies (Delhi High Court, 2017)** — Pre-dating the Rajendra N. Shah ruling, this case engaged with the governance standards applicable to co-operative housing societies in Delhi under the Delhi Co-operative Societies Act, 2003. The Court's analysis of the relationship between the Delhi Act, Part IXB, and Article 243ZS's application provides early jurisprudence on how Part IXB standards operate in the UT-with-Legislature context. Post-Rajendra N. Shah, this decision requires re-examination to the extent it assumed the constitutional compulsory character of Part IXB standards for Delhi co-operatives.

**Punjab and Haryana High Court — Chandigarh Co-operative Societies cases (2015–2019)** — A series of cases in the Punjab and Haryana High Court addressed governance disputes in Chandigarh co-operative societies. The Court treated Part IXB as constitutionally applicable to Chandigarh (a UT without a Legislature) through Article 243ZS, and applied the Part's requirements for board elections and terms. These cases provide interpretive guidance on how Part IXB operates in a UT-without-Legislature context, and their analysis of the Administrator's role under Article 243ZS is the primary judicial treatment of that specific question.

**National Federation of Urban Cooperative Banks v. Union of India (2021)** — Decided close in time to the Rajendra N. Shah ruling, this case addressed the governance framework for urban co-operative banks, a significant category of co-operative institution in UTs. The case engaged with the Reserve Bank of India's regulatory authority alongside the Part IXB governance standards and the Central Registrar's powers under the Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act. The relationship between Part IXB's constitutional governance framework (applicable through Article 243ZS to UT co-operatives) and RBI's regulatory regime for urban co-operative banks is one of the most practically complex overlaps in co-operative governance law.

**State Bank of India v. Azimganj House Pvt. Ltd. (2022)** — The Supreme Court addressed governance and insolvency questions involving co-operative housing societies, with implications for how constitutional governance standards interact with insolvency and banking regulatory frameworks. The case's analysis of the legal personality and governance obligations of co-operative societies is relevant to Article 243ZS's application context, particularly for urban co-operative housing societies in Delhi.

## Current significance

Delhi's co-operative sector — urban co-operative banks, housing societies, consumer co-operatives, and workers' co-operatives — is among the largest in India by membership and transaction volume. The application of Part IXB to Delhi co-operatives through Article 243ZS, combined with the Delhi Co-operative Societies Act, 2003 and RBI oversight for co-operative banks, creates a layered governance framework with multiple overlapping regulatory authorities. The post-Rajendra N. Shah uncertainty about whether Part IXB's constitutional compulsory force applies to Delhi (a UT with a Legislature) — or whether it applies only as persuasive legislative policy that Delhi can deviate from — is a live issue in Delhi co-operative governance proceedings.

Ladakh, constituted as a UT without a Legislature under the J&K Reorganisation Act, 2019, has a co-operative sector — primarily agricultural, dairy, and handicraft co-operatives — that now falls within Article 243ZS's framework, with Parliament as the effective legislature and the Administrator as the executive. The integration of the former J&K co-operative legal framework with the Part IXB constitutional standards applicable through Article 243ZS has been an ongoing administrative and legal process since 2019, and several governance disputes in Ladakh co-operatives are working their way through the judicial system.

The Ministry of Co-operation's 2021 establishment and its emphasis on urban co-operative banks and Primary Agricultural Credit Societies in UT contexts has given Article 243ZS renewed policy salience. The constitutional framework the article establishes — Parliament as legislator, the Central Government as executive, Part IXB as the governance standard — aligns with the Central government's interest in maintaining oversight of co-operative institutions in territories under its direct administration, while the Rajendra N. Shah ruling's federal constraints apply less forcefully in the UT context than in the State context.
