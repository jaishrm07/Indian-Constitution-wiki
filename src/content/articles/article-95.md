officialText: |-
  95. Power of the Deputy Speaker or other person to perform the duties of the office of, or to act as, Speaker.—(1) While the office of Speaker is vacant, the duties of the office shall be performed by the Deputy Speaker or, if the office of Deputy Speaker is also vacant, by such member of the House of the People as the President may appoint for the purpose.

  (2) During the absence of the Speaker from any sitting of the House of the People the Deputy Speaker or, if he is also absent, such person as may be determined by the rules of procedure of the House, or, if no such person is present, such other person as may be determined by the House, shall act as Speaker.

## Why it matters

The Lok Sabha's functioning depends on continuous presiding authority. Article 95 is the constitutional mechanism ensuring that authority is never absent — there is always a constitutionally authorised person to maintain order, recognise members, and certify the conduct of proceedings. But Article 95 also reveals the constitutional architecture's pressure point: when the Deputy Speakership is vacant, the only fallback for a structural Speaker vacancy is Presidential appointment of a member, inserting the executive into the legislature in a way the Constitution permits only because the alternative — a legislature without any presiding officer — is constitutionally untenable. The prolonged vacancy in the Deputy Speakership during the 17th and 18th Lok Sabhas made this theoretical vulnerability practically real.

## Reading the provision

Clause (1) addresses structural vacancies in the Speakership — situations where the office itself is empty under Article 94 (resignation, death, ceasing to be a member, or removal). Until the House elects a new Speaker under Article 93, the Deputy Speaker performs all Speaker duties with full constitutional authority. If the Deputy Speakership is also vacant, the President appoints a Lok Sabha member to perform the Speaker's duties — a constitutionally authorised but politically exceptional step. The Presidential appointee is not elected; the appointment is a stop-gap measure to prevent the House from being without a presiding officer while the Article 93 election takes place.

Clause (2) addresses ordinary absences — the Speaker is not present at a particular sitting. In this case, the succession is managed entirely within the House's own procedural framework: Deputy Speaker, then a panel member determined by the Rules of Procedure (the Lok Sabha maintains a panel of Chairpersons for this purpose), then any member determined by the House. The President plays no role in these routine arrangements.

## The acting Speaker's full constitutional authority

A person substituting as Speaker under Article 95 has the same constitutional powers as the Speaker for the period of substitution. This includes the power to certify Money Bills under Article 110, to rule on points of order with finality, to maintain order and discipline, and — most significantly — to discharge anti-defection adjudication functions under the Tenth Schedule. The acting Speaker's rulings and decisions have the same constitutional validity as those of the substantive Speaker.

The constraint from Nabam Rebia applies equally to a substituting presiding officer. If the substantive Speaker faces a removal notice, the Nabam Rebia doctrine suspends certain discretionary powers including Tenth Schedule adjudication. Whether this suspension carries over to a person acting as Speaker under Article 95 — who is not themselves facing removal — is a nuanced constitutional question that the courts have not definitively resolved.

## Key cases

**Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Constitution Bench's ruling on the Deputy Speaker's powers and limitations when acting as Speaker under the analogous State provision applies to Article 95. The Court clarified that a person exercising the Speaker's functions by constitutional substitution has the same powers and is subject to the same constraints as the Speaker.

**Shivraj v. Chaube (1970)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that a person performing the Speaker's duties under constitutional succession provisions acts with full constitutional authority for all purposes, including certifying legislation and maintaining parliamentary privilege.

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007)** — The Court's analysis of the Speaker's powers and the scope of parliamentary privilege, applicable equally to the substantive and substitute presiding officers under Articles 93 and 95.

**Keisham Meghachandra Singh v. Speaker, Manipur Legislative Assembly (2020)** — The Supreme Court's direction on timely disposal of anti-defection petitions applies to whoever is exercising the Speaker's functions — the duty to act within a reasonable time does not lapse merely because a substitute is presiding.

## Current significance

Article 95 was directly implicated by the 17th Lok Sabha's operation through its entire five-year term (2019–2024) without electing a Deputy Speaker, and the 18th Lok Sabha's extended failure to do so. The constitutional vulnerability this created was significant: if the Speaker had become structurally unavailable at any point during this period, the fallback would have been Presidential appointment — an extraordinary executive step inside the legislature. The experience has intensified calls for treating the "as soon as may be" obligation in Article 93 as judicially enforceable, and for a constitutional amendment or Supreme Court direction that the Deputy Speaker must be elected within a defined period of the new House's first sitting. Until the Deputy Speakership vacancy question is resolved, Article 95 will remain a site of constitutional concern rather than a quietly working contingency provision.
