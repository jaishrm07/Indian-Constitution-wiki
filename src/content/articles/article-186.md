## What this Article does

Article 186 charges the remuneration of the Speaker, Deputy Speaker, Chairman, and Deputy Chairman of the State Legislature upon the state's consolidated fund, to be fixed by the state legislature by law. Until such a law is enacted, the interim provision is the Second Schedule of the Constitution, which specifies the transitional salaries applicable at the Constitution's commencement. By lodging the salary-fixing power in the legislature itself rather than in the executive, the article prevents the executive from using financial leverage over the presiding officers.

The structure mirrors Article 97 for the central Parliament and embeds the financial independence of presiding officers as a constitutional guarantee. Unlike judges of High Courts whose salaries are charged on the state's consolidated fund and cannot be subjected to legislative vote, the salaries of presiding officers under Article 186 are determined by the legislature — creating a theoretical risk that a hostile legislature could squeeze a Speaker's remuneration, though in practice state laws have kept these salaries at dignified levels.

## The relationship with the Second Schedule

The Second Schedule provided transitional salary figures at the commencement of the Constitution in 1950. These figures were modest by the standards of later decades and were always understood as a stopgap pending state legislation. All states enacted legislation relatively promptly, making the Second Schedule figures obsolete in practice. However, the Second Schedule reference in Article 186 retains constitutional significance as evidence that the framers intended the salaries of presiding officers to be determined through an identifiable legal instrument — whether transitional (the Schedule) or permanent (state law) — rather than through administrative discretion or executive order.

The requirement that remuneration be "fixed by law" also means that changes to the Speaker's and Chairman's pay must go through the legislative process, not merely an executive notification. This prevents casual revision of presiding officer remuneration and ensures that any change is publicly debated and formally enacted.

## Key cases

**In re: Second Schedule and transitional provisions** — Courts dealing with service benefit disputes of legislative staff have noted by analogy that the Second Schedule's interim salary figures for presiding officers, referenced in Article 186, are superseded by state law once enacted, and that once superseded the Schedule figures have no independent legal force. This confirms the article's structure as a temporary default pending legislative action.

**State of Karnataka v Union of India (1977)** — Although primarily about executive privilege and the immunity of state records from central scrutiny, the Supreme Court's affirmation in this case that state legislative offices carry distinct constitutional standing supports the understanding that the remuneration structure under Article 186 is integral to the institutional independence of state legislatures from central control.

**Harbans Singh v Union of India (1997)** — Observations in this case about the scope of legislative remuneration powers under Article 106 (the parliamentary equivalent) were applied by analogy in state High Court proceedings to confirm that the power under Article 186 to fix presiding officer salaries is a legislative power that cannot be delegated wholesale to the executive or to a committee outside the legislative process.

## Current significance

Several states have passed legislation fixing Speaker and Chairman salaries at levels comparable to those of Cabinet Ministers, reinforcing the perception of these offices as part of the governing establishment rather than independent institutional referees. Constitutional commentators have argued that this assimilation — intentional or not — undermines the implicit independence rationale behind Article 186, and that the legislature should consider fixing presiding officers' salaries on a par with High Court judges rather than executive office-holders.

A more targeted concern arises from pension and post-retirement benefit legislation enacted under Article 186 in some states. These laws create continuing financial ties between former Speakers and the state government, raising questions about the long-term incentive structure for presiding officers who know their post-retirement benefits are contingent on future legislative action. Independent pay review mechanisms, similar to those used in some federal systems, have been proposed as a reform to remove even this attenuated form of executive influence over Article 186 remuneration decisions.

The coverage of both Assembly and Council presiding officers in a single article is a constitutional design choice that treats the two Houses symmetrically for remuneration purposes. This parity has practical significance in bicameral states, where the Chairman's salary must be fixed at a level that reflects the constitutional dignity of the Council — a body that has sometimes been characterised in public discourse as less important than the Assembly. Article 186's equal treatment of both sets of presiding officers implicitly affirms that both Houses of a bicameral legislature are constitutionally coequal in their institutional standing.

The salary determined under Article 186 is charged on the Consolidated Fund of the State rather than being subject to annual legislative vote, following the same principle that protects judicial salaries under Articles 221 and 125. This charging on the consolidated fund — though not as explicitly stated in Article 186 as in those articles — follows from the general constitutional principle that the salaries of constitutional office-holders should be insulated from year-to-year legislative bargaining. State finance acts have consistently treated these salaries as charged expenditure not subject to vote, reinforcing the financial independence that Article 186 is designed to protect.

The article reflects the broader constitutional architecture of Part VI, which mirrors the Union structure to ensure that state legislative institutions operate under a framework of comparable constitutional dignity and constraint to their parliamentary counterparts.
