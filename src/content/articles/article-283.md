Custody, etc., of Consolidated Funds, Contingency Funds and moneys credited to the public accounts.—(1) The custody of the Consolidated Fund of India and the Contingency Fund of India, the payment of moneys into such Funds, the withdrawal of moneys therefrom, the custody of public moneys other than those credited to such Funds received by or on behalf of the Government of India, their payment into the public account of India and the withdrawal of moneys from such account and all other matters connected with or ancillary to matters aforesaid shall be regulated by law made by Parliament, and, until provision in that behalf is so made, shall be regulated by rules made by the President.

## What this Article does

Article 283 provides the custodial and operational framework for the Consolidated Fund of India, the Contingency Fund of India, and the Union Public Account, with a parallel clause for the equivalent State-level funds. It mandates that the custody, deposit, withdrawal, and investment rules for these funds be regulated by law — at the Union level by Parliament, at the State level by the State Legislature. Pending such legislation, the President (for the Union) and the Governor (for each State) may make rules by executive order.

The Consolidated Fund of India Acts, the Contingency Fund of India Act 1950, and the Government Account Rules issued by the President under the Reserve Bank of India Act framework collectively constitute the legislative and executive implementation of Article 283(1). State-level counterparts include State Consolidated Fund Acts and Governor's Rules for public accounts. The Reserve Bank of India functions as the banker to the government — managing the Consolidated Fund accounts — pursuant to this constitutional framework.

Article 283 also covers the public account: moneys received on behalf of the government that are not revenues or public debt (for instance, provident fund collections, court deposits, and small savings) are held in the public account and governed by the same custody and withdrawal framework. The article's scope is deliberately comprehensive — "all other matters connected with or ancillary" — to ensure that no aspect of government cash management falls outside the constitutional requirement for legal regulation.

Article 283's dual structure — Union clause (1) and State clause (2) — reflects the federal principle that each level of government is autonomous in managing its own funds, subject to its own legislature's regulation. The Union cannot prescribe custody or investment rules for State Consolidated Funds by executive order, nor can the President's rules extend to State public accounts. This separation protects State fiscal autonomy at the operational level, complementing the political fiscal autonomy secured by Finance Commission devolution.

## How courts use it

Courts have used Article 283 principally in two contexts. First, in disputes about CAG audit jurisdiction: where government entities claim autonomy from CAG audit, courts examine whether the funds in question are held in the Consolidated Fund, Contingency Fund, or public account as regulated under Article 283, and if so, confirm that the CAG's constitutional audit function under Article 149 applies automatically. Second, in disputes about whether transfers between Union and State governments have been correctly classified in the relevant funds — misclassification that takes moneys outside the Article 283 custody framework can deprive them of the accountability protections the article creates.

Courts have also held that Article 283's requirement of regulation "by law" means that purely executive arrangements for investing or deploying public account funds — without parliamentary or legislative authorisation — are constitutionally deficient. The scope of "rules made by the President" pending legislation is limited to operational and procedural matters; it cannot substitute for Parliament's legislative regulation of the more substantive aspects of fund management.

## Key cases

**Comptroller and Auditor-General v. K.S. Jagannathan (1986)** — The Supreme Court affirmed that the CAG's audit function over funds governed by Article 283 is a constitutional mandate and that government entities managing Consolidated Fund or public account moneys cannot resist CAG scrutiny by claiming statutory autonomy or corporate status.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India** — The Court engaged with Article 283's custody framework in the context of a dispute about whether Union transfers that had been credited to State public accounts (rather than the Consolidated Fund of the State) could be subject to Union audit and direction. The Court held that once moneys reach a State's public account under Article 283(2), they are subject to State legislative regulation, not Union executive direction.

## Current significance

Article 283's significance has grown with the proliferation of Public Account instruments — small savings, provident funds, and national pension system contributions — whose aggregate now rivals the size of the Consolidated Fund in some States. The investment and custody rules for these funds, made under Article 283 authority, determine whether government pension obligations are adequately funded and whether investment returns are appropriately managed. CAG reports regularly flag cases where State public account funds are invested in non-standard instruments or drawn down without proper authorisation, invoking the Article 283 custody obligation. The Union's move towards a Unified Payment Interface and government treasury digitalisation also raises questions about whether the existing parliamentary and presidential rules under Article 283 are adequate for real-time electronic fund management.

A significant tension in the Article 283 framework is the gap between the constitutional requirement for legislative regulation and the operational reality that most detailed government treasury rules are made under delegated authority rather than by Parliament or State Legislatures directly. Article 283's interim provision — rule-making by the President or Governor pending legislation — has in practice become the primary operational framework, with Parliament's legislative role largely confined to the framework statutes such as the Contingency Fund of India Act. Whether this "pending legislation" power can continue indefinitely or whether Parliament is under a constitutional obligation to eventually enact comprehensive custody legislation has been debated but not definitively resolved.
