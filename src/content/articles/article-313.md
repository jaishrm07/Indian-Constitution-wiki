## Why it matters

When India became a Republic on 26 January 1950, hundreds of thousands of civil servants held their posts under service rules made under the Government of India Act 1935, the Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules 1930, the Fundamental Rules, the Supplementary Rules, and numerous other instruments of colonial administrative law. Without Article 313, those rules would have had no constitutional basis from commencement day — a legal vacuum that would have paralysed the administration on the very day the Constitution came into force.

The practical stakes were enormous. Every appointment, promotion, disciplinary action, and pension entitlement across the entire Union and State civil services rested on a pre-constitutional legal framework. If that framework had simply lapsed on 26 January 1950, there would have been no valid legal basis for paying salaries, conducting disciplinary proceedings, or enforcing service conditions of any kind until Parliament and every State Legislature enacted fresh service legislation. Article 313 solved this problem by treating the pre-constitutional service laws as validly continuing, subject to constitutional consistency, for as long as necessary.

The article is also significant as a constitutional acknowledgment that the Republic was built on the administrative infrastructure of the Dominion and, before that, of the colonial state. The civil service that governed independent India in its early years was, in large part, the same civil service that had governed British India — its legal framework now saved by Article 313 and its constitutional accountability now anchored in Part XIV.

## Reading the provision

The article operates through four key elements. First, the temporal trigger — "until other provision is made in this behalf under this Constitution" — makes clear that Article 313 savings are transitional, not permanent. The pre-constitutional framework survives only until the appropriate legislature enacts a replacement. Second, the subject matter — "all the laws in force immediately before the commencement of this Constitution applicable to any public service or any post which continues to exist" — covers every instrument of service regulation, whether statutory, executive, or administrative, that was in force on 25 January 1950. Third, the continuity condition — "continues to exist after the commencement of this Constitution as an all-India service or as a service or post under the Union or a State" — restricts the savings to services and posts that survived the constitutional transition; abolished services or posts that were not carried forward received no protection. Fourth, the consistency qualification — "so far as consistent with the provisions of this Constitution" — subjects all saved laws to constitutional review from the moment of commencement.

The consistency qualification is the most important operative limit. Any pre-constitutional service rule that contradicted a Part III fundamental right, an Article 311 procedural protection, an Article 15 or 16 equality guarantee, or any other mandatory constitutional requirement was void from commencement regardless of Article 313's general savings function. Article 313 did not grandfather unconstitutional rules — it merely saved constitutionally compatible ones from the procedural problem of lacking a constitutional legislative basis.

## The transitional problem Article 313 solved

The constitutional transition problem was not unique to India. Every constitution that replaces an existing legal order faces the question of what to do with the laws and administrative arrangements of the predecessor regime. The most common solution — and the one adopted in India — is a transitional savings clause that keeps existing laws in force until the new constitutional order can replace them through its own processes.

India's saving was particularly significant because the civil service of 1950 was large, complex, and organised under a detailed web of colonial administrative law. The Central Government alone had tens of thousands of employees governed by the Fundamental Rules, the Supplementary Rules, the CCA Rules, and dozens of departmental codes and orders. The States — including the former provinces and the acceded princely territories with their own service arrangements — added hundreds of thousands more. Drafting and enacting comprehensive new service legislation for all of these would take years; Article 313 bought that time without administrative disruption.

## Interaction with Articles 309 and 310

Article 309 is the principal constitutional authority for Parliament and State Legislatures to legislate on recruitment and conditions of service for civil servants. Article 313's transitional function was to bridge the period before Article 309 legislation was enacted. As Parliament and State Governments progressively enacted new service rules — the Central Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules 1957, the All India Services (Conduct) Rules 1968, and numerous State service codes — the domain of Article 313 shrank correspondingly.

The relationship between Article 313 transitional rules and Article 309 legislation is hierarchical: once Parliament or a State Legislature enacts a provision "in that behalf" under Article 309, the pre-constitutional rule in the same field is displaced to the extent of the inconsistency. The displacement is partial where the new legislation covers only some aspects of the pre-constitutional rule, and total where the new legislation occupies the same field comprehensively. Courts have had to determine, in a number of cases, whether a particular aspect of service conditions has been "covered" by new constitutional legislation or whether the pre-constitutional framework still applies to that specific point.

## Key cases

**Roshan Lal Tandon v. Union of India (1967) 3 SCR 185** — The Supreme Court held that a civil servant's conditions of service, whether regulated by pre-constitutional rules continuing under Article 313 or by Article 309 legislation, are governed by the law in force at the time of the relevant act or decision. The article confirmed that Article 313 transitional rules had the same legal force as post-constitutional service legislation within the domain of their surviving application and could create enforceable rights in public servants.

**State of UP v. Babu Ram Upadhya (1961) 2 SCR 679** — The Court clarified that the "consistent with the provisions of this Constitution" qualification in Article 313 meant that any pre-constitutional rule that conferred a disciplinary or removal power without the procedural safeguards of Article 311 was automatically invalid from commencement, regardless of Article 313's general savings function. This case established that Article 313 does not save constitutional defects — it saves administrative continuity.

**Madan Gopal v. State of Punjab AIR 1963 SC 531** — The Supreme Court applied the Article 313 framework to determine the validity of pre-Constitution service rules that had been inherited by a successor State after the reorganisation of Punjab, confirming that the transitional savings principle followed the post and the service rather than the original governmental entity. When States were reorganised, the pre-constitutional rules applicable to the continuing posts and services were saved under Article 313 in the successor State, not extinguished by the reorganisation.

**State of Andhra Pradesh v. P V Murali (1976)** — The Court examined pre-constitutional service arrangements applicable to employees of a former princely State that had acceded to India and was subsequently merged into Andhra Pradesh. The Court held that Article 313 saved those arrangements for the benefit of the former princely-State employees to the extent they were constitutionally consistent, and that the State government could not unilaterally extinguish pre-constitutional entitlements without enacting fresh legislation under Article 309.

## Current significance

Article 313 is substantially spent. The major pre-constitutional service laws have long since been replaced by fresh legislation under Articles 309 and 310. The article's surviving relevance lies at the margins: in disputes about very old service rules applicable to specific cadres, in the interpretation of pre-1950 service instruments that may still govern some employees in States formed from former princely territories, and in the historical analysis of how Indian administrative law was reconstructed after independence.

Courts occasionally invoke the article when a litigant claims that their service conditions are governed by a pre-constitutional instrument that was never formally superseded, requiring the court to assess whether those rules survived the constitutional-consistency test of Article 313 and whether they were subsequently displaced by Article 309 legislation covering the same field. Such cases are rare but not entirely absent, and they serve as reminders that the legal architecture of the Indian civil service rests, at its historical base, on the constitutional bridge that Article 313 provided on 26 January 1950.
