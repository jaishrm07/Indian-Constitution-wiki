  Article 235 vests in the High Court exclusive control over district courts and all courts subordinate to them — specifically the posting, promotion, and grant of leave of judicial officers holding posts below the level of district judge. This control power belongs to the High Court, not to the State government; the executive cannot direct where judicial officers serve, whom to promote, or whose leave to grant. officialText: 235. Control over subordinate courts.—The control over district courts and courts subordinate thereto including the posting and promotion of, and the grant of leave to, persons belonging to the judicial service of a State and holding any post inferior to the post of district judge shall be vested in the High Court, but nothing in this article shall be construed as taking away from any such person any right of appeal which he may have under the law regulating the conditions of his service or as authorising the High Court to deal with him otherwise than in accordance with the conditions of his service prescribed under such law.

## Why it matters

Article 235 is the operational axis of the entire Chapter VI framework. The appointments provisions (Articles 233 and 234) determine who enters the judicial service; Article 235 determines how those officers are managed throughout their service. By vesting management control exclusively in the High Court, the Constitution insulates judicial careers from the patronage and pressure dynamics of the State secretariat.

The constitutional significance of this allocation reflects a deliberate departure from colonial practice. In pre-Independence India, judicial officers below the High Court level were typically treated as members of the executive service, subject to transfers and postings at the pleasure of the District Magistrate and the provincial government. That arrangement produced a subordinate judiciary perceived — often correctly — as insufficiently independent of the executive authorities whose actions it was called upon to adjudicate. Article 235 broke with that tradition by making the High Court the sole administrative authority over subordinate judicial careers.

An independent subordinate judiciary — one whose career prospects are not subject to executive direction — is a precondition for a fair trial at the district and magistrate court levels where most Indian litigants first and last encounter the justice system. Article 235's constitutional allocation of control to the High Court is thus not merely an administrative arrangement but a structural guarantee of the fairness of trial court proceedings throughout the country.

## Reading the provision

"Control" covers three specified matters — posting, promotion, and grant of leave — but courts have read it as capturing the full range of service management rather than being limited to those three heads. Disciplinary action, assessment of judicial work, and recommendations on matters of judicial conduct all fall within the High Court's control even though the article does not list them explicitly.

The three specified matters are significant in themselves. Posting determines where each judicial officer serves — which district, which court, which jurisdiction. The High Court, not the State government, decides transfers and station assignments. Promotion determines when and whether a judicial officer advances from one grade to the next; the High Court controls the criteria and the decisions. Leave covers all forms of leave — earned, medical, study — and a judicial officer's leave application goes to the High Court, not the State government.

The proviso is important in two respects. It preserves the judicial officer's right of appeal under service law — the High Court's control does not extinguish statutory appeal rights. And it prevents the High Court from dealing with an officer in a way that violates their conditions of service — the High Court cannot impose conditions inconsistent with the applicable statutory framework.

The distinction between "control" under Article 235 and "appointment" under Articles 233 and 234 is significant. Appointment involves the Governor's formal role alongside the High Court's consultation. Once appointment is made, however, the officer's career is governed by Article 235 — the Governor and State government do not re-enter the picture except through specific statutory provisions, and even those must not contradict the constitutional allocation of control to the High Court.

## Key cases

**High Court of Calcutta v. State of West Bengal (1953)** — This foundational ruling established that the High Court's control under Article 235 is comprehensive and exclusive over the specified matters. The State government's attempt to make postings of subordinate judicial officers through its own secretariat was held to be a violation of the constitutional scheme.

**Sushanta Kumar Bose v. High Court of Orissa (1968)** — The Court elaborated on the meaning of "control," holding that it includes not merely formal approval of postings and promotions but also the substantive determination of the criteria and procedures by which those decisions are made. The High Court cannot be reduced to rubber-stamping executive decisions.

**Durga Shankar Mehta v. Thakur Raghuraj Singh (1954)** — The Supreme Court addressed the boundary between the "appointment" function (shared with the Governor under Article 233) and the "control" function (exclusively in the High Court under Article 235), holding that once appointment is made, subsequent management shifts entirely to the High Court.

**Bihar v. Bal Mukund Sah (2000)** — The Supreme Court reiterated that the High Court's control under Article 235 is a constitutional mandate, not an administrative delegation. The State government cannot by executive order or legislation transfer control over postings and promotions of judicial officers to the State's general administrative cadre. Any law purporting to do so is repugnant to Article 235 and void.

**P. Ramachandra Rao v. State of Karnataka (2002)** — The Supreme Court emphasised that the High Court's control under Article 235 carries a responsibility to ensure that the subordinate judiciary functions efficiently. The Court directed High Courts to set case disposition norms, monitor judicial performance, and take corrective action where subordinate courts exhibit systematic delays.

## Current significance

Article 235 is the most directly operational of the subordinate judiciary provisions in day-to-day constitutional practice. Disputes about the posting and transfer of district-level judges — particularly in high-profile cases where a State government wishes to relocate a judge who has given rulings adverse to the government — regularly invoke Article 235. The constitutional allocation of control to the High Court provides the legal basis for resisting such transfers when they are directed by the State government rather than decided by the High Court.

The All India Judges Association has used the constitutional status of High Court control under Article 235 to resist State government attempts to assert influence over judicial service careers — whether through seniority manipulation, selective grant of leave, or forced postings to remote courts. Infrastructure allocation is also a matter where the High Court's Article 235 interest gives it leverage in negotiations with State finance departments.

The Supreme Court has directed High Courts to use their Article 235 control proactively to improve judicial administration at the district level — through performance appraisal systems, annual confidential reports, and structured training requirements. These directives, issued under Article 142, are implemented through the Article 235 control framework. Article 235's role will grow in importance as India pursues e-court reforms, fast-track court expansion, and court management system development — all of which require active, resource-backed engagement by the High Court exercising its constitutional control mandate.
