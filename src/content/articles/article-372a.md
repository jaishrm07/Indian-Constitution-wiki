  [372A. Power of the President to adapt laws.—(1) For the purposes of bringing the provisions of any law in force in India or in any part thereof, immediately before the commencement of the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956, into accord with the provisions of this Constitution as amended by that Act, the President may by order made before the first day of November, 1957, make such adaptations and modifications of the law, whether by way of repeal or amendment, as may be necessary or expedient, and provide that the law shall, as from such date as may be specified in the order, have effect subject to the adaptations and modifications so made, and any such adaptation or modification shall not be questioned in any court of law.

  (2) Nothing in clause (1) shall be deemed to prevent a competent Legislature or other competent authority from repealing or amending any law adapted or modified by the President under the said clause.]

## Background and purpose

Article 372A was inserted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956 — the amendment that
reorganised the States on linguistic lines following the States Reorganisation Act, 1956. The
reorganisation required wholesale revision of laws that referred to territorial units, administrative
divisions, and institutional arrangements that were being abolished or reconfigured. Parliament could
not amend every such law individually before the reorganisation took effect. Article 372A gave the
President a targeted, time-limited power to do so by executive order.

Article 372 (which Article 372A supplements) already preserved all pre-Constitution laws in force until
Parliament altered them. Article 372A extended that concept to the post-7th Amendment situation: laws
that were in force immediately before the 7th Amendment came into force on 1 November 1956 needed
adaptation to reflect the new constitutional text as amended by that Act.

## Scope of the adaptation power

The President could, by order made before 1 November 1957 — one year from the 7th Amendment's
commencement — adapt or modify any existing law by way of repeal, amendment, or modification. The
single constraint was that the adaptation had to be "necessary or expedient" to bring the law into
accord with the Constitution as amended. The order could specify a date from which it would take
effect, allowing prospective or retrospective adjustment.

Crucially, clause (1) shields adaptation orders from judicial challenge: "any such adaptation or
modification shall not be questioned in any court of law." This ouster of judicial review was necessary
because presidential orders made under fast-paced administrative time pressure would inevitably involve
judgment calls about the meaning of "necessary or expedient," and litigation challenging individual
adaptation decisions would have paralysed the post-reorganisation transition.

## Relationship with legislative power

Clause (2) preserves the full legislative competence of Parliament and State Legislatures over laws
adapted under clause (1). An adaptation order under Article 372A does not entrench the adapted text
against subsequent legislative change. A competent legislature may repeal, amend, or re-enact any law
that the President has adapted. This prevents the adaptation power from functioning as a form of
executive legislation that displaces the normal constitutional supremacy of Parliament.

## Context in the States Reorganisation

The 7th Amendment abolished the four-category classification of States (Parts A, B, C, and D) and
replaced it with a single category of States and Union territories. This required adaptation of
references to "Part A States," "Part B States," and so on throughout the statute book. Hundreds of
central and State laws used these classifications in their territorial and institutional definitions.
The President's adaptation orders under Article 372A performed this revision systematically,
translating the old territorial vocabulary into the new one across tax laws, service rules, court
jurisdictions, and administrative codes.

## Current status

The adaptation power expired on 31 October 1957 and has not been renewed or extended. Article 372A
therefore has no operative application today. It survives in the constitutional text as a record of
how the 1956 reorganisation was legally implemented. Readers encountering references to early
post-reorganisation adaptation orders in case law should trace them to orders made under this article.

## Why it matters

Article 372A belongs to a class of constitutional provisions that are important precisely because of
what they say about governance technique rather than about substantive rights or structures. It is a
solution to a specific and recurring problem in large-scale legal reform: when Parliament or the
constituent assembly rewrites the constitutional framework, existing statutes that were drafted in
terms of the old framework become technically inconsistent with the new one. If those inconsistencies
are not corrected, courts must either strain to read the old text as conforming to the new framework,
declare provisions invalid, or hold that pre-constitutional provisions have become spent. None of
these outcomes is satisfactory, and the task of amending the entire statute book through ordinary
legislation is practically infeasible within the timeframe of the transition.

The solution Article 372A adopts — a presidential power to adapt by order, coupled with a one-year
deadline and an ouster of judicial review — is not constitutionally unique. Article 372 itself, adopted
from the start of the Constitution, had done something similar for the transition from colonial law to
the new constitutional order. The Adaptation of Laws Order, 1950 made under Article 372 performed
for the original Constitution what Article 372A's orders performed for the 7th Amendment. The two
together constitute a pattern: each major restructuring of the constitutional framework has been
accompanied by an executive adaptation mechanism to bridge the old statutory law and the new
constitutional text.

Understanding Article 372A also illuminates the relationship between constitutional amendment and
legislative reform more generally. The 7th Amendment was not merely a constitutional change; it was
the constitutional precondition for the States Reorganisation Act, 1956, which itself required a vast
apparatus of consequential legislation. The adaptation power in Article 372A was one element of that
apparatus. It is a reminder that constitutional amendments have downstream legislative consequences,
and that managing those consequences is itself a constitutional task.

## Reading the provision

The precision of Article 372A's drafting reflects the care taken to avoid two dangers: over-breadth
and under-breadth. Over-breadth would allow the President to use the adaptation power as a vehicle
for substantive policy change under cover of mere "adaptation" — effectively legislating by presidential
order on matters that should require parliamentary deliberation. Under-breadth would leave the
adaptation power too narrow to handle the actual scope of the States Reorganisation's impact on
existing law.

The provision addresses the over-breadth risk through the phrase "necessary or expedient." An
adaptation must be directed at the specific purpose of bringing existing law into accord with the
Constitution as amended by the 7th Amendment. It cannot be used to make unrelated changes to existing
law, however convenient that might be. The adaptation power is tied to the specific amendment (the 7th)
and the specific legislative task (harmonisation of existing law with the new constitutional text).

The under-breadth risk is addressed by the breadth of the adaptation power itself: "whether by way of
repeal or amendment." Repeal is the most drastic form of adaptation — the complete removal of an
inconsistent provision. Amendment is the more surgical option. The power to choose between repeal and
amendment gives the executive flexibility to adopt the least disruptive solution that achieves the
required harmonisation. Wholesale repeal of provisions that are mostly consistent with the new framework
(but for a few terminology changes) would be administratively wasteful; the amendment option allows
targeted correction.

The ouster of judicial review in clause (1) — "any such adaptation or modification shall not be
questioned in any court of law" — is a strong exclusion that reflects the time-sensitive context of
the adaptation process. Constitutional challenges to individual adaptation orders could have been
mounted on many grounds: that the adaptation went beyond "necessary or expedient," that it effectively
repealed a law that should have been preserved, or that the pre-conditions for adaptation were not met.
The ouster clause prevents such challenges from arising. This kind of ouster is a significant intrusion
on judicial review, and the Court has generally read such clauses strictly. However, since Article 372A's
adaptation power expired in 1957, the ouster clause has no further practical operation.

## Key cases or constitutional incidents

**The States Reorganisation Act, 1956 and the legislative cascade.** The primary "constitutional
incident" associated with Article 372A is the States Reorganisation itself. The States Reorganisation
Commission, chaired by Justice Fazl Ali, had recommended the reorganisation of States on largely
linguistic lines after a lengthy inquiry. Parliament enacted the States Reorganisation Act in 1956,
and the 7th Amendment implemented the constitutional changes required: the abolition of Part A, B, C,
and D States; the creation of a single category of States and of Union territories; and consequential
amendments throughout the Constitution. Article 372A was inserted as part of this constitutional
package to enable the executive to clean up the statute book. The President's adaptations under
Article 372A, made in the period October 1956 to October 1957, covered hundreds of central laws and
significant volumes of territorial legislation.

**The Adaptation of Laws (State Reorganisation) Order, 1956.** The primary instrument made under
Article 372A was this Order, which made adaptations to central laws to replace references to Part A,
B, C, and D State categories with references to the new State and Union territory categories. The
Order ran to extensive schedules of amendments to specific Acts. Later orders addressed specific
sectors of the law — service law, tax law, court jurisdictions — that required more targeted
adaptation. These orders are still occasionally encountered in case law interpreting legislation that
was adapted in this period; courts reading the adapted text must understand whether the adaptation
changed the substance of the provision or merely its terminology.

**The relationship to Article 372 and the Adaptation of Laws Order, 1950.** The 1950 Adaptation of
Laws Order, made under Article 372, is the predecessor that most directly illuminates Article 372A's
function. Under Article 372, the President adapted colonial-era laws (made by the Governor-General,
the Crown, or the pre-independence Legislature) to the new constitutional framework of 1950. The 1950
Order replaced references to the Crown and the Governor-General with references to the appropriate
constitutional authorities, and made dozens of other textual alignments. Article 372A performed an
analogous function for the specific structural changes made by the 7th Amendment. The two-stage
adaptation process — first for the original Constitution, then for the 7th Amendment's restructuring
— is a coherent constitutional technique for managing the transition from one legal framework to the
next.

**The continued relevance of the adaptation model.** Article 372A's adaptation mechanism has been used
as a model in subsequent constitutional history. After the 42nd Amendment and other substantial
amendments, questions arose about whether existing laws had become inconsistent with the amended
constitutional text. In most of these cases Parliament has chosen to address the inconsistency through
ordinary legislation rather than through a special adaptation mechanism; the political circumstances
of the 1950s, which made a dedicated executive adaptation power necessary, have not recurred in quite
the same form. However, the 372A model of a time-limited executive power to harmonise existing law
with a new constitutional structure remains available as a constitutional tool and has been discussed
in the context of large-scale legislative modernisation proposals.

## Current significance

Article 372A is spent as a source of executive power — the one-year window closed on 31 October 1957,
and no further adaptation orders can be made under it. Its current significance is threefold. First,
it is the constitutional basis for the adaptation orders that remain embedded in the background of
many pieces of legislation enacted before 1956 and still in force. Lawyers reading those laws,
particularly in contexts where the States Reorganisation's territorial changes are relevant (for
example, in determining jurisdiction, identifying successor State authorities, or construing transitional
provisions in old statutes), may need to trace the adaptation orders made under Article 372A to
understand the current text.

Second, Article 372A is part of the constitutional record of how India managed the 1956 reorganisation
— one of the most significant territorial and administrative restructurings in independent India's
history. That reorganisation created the political geography of the modern Indian States, determined
the distribution of resources and institutional capacity across linguistic communities, and shaped
electoral politics for decades. The constitutional machinery of Article 372A was a small but essential
part of making the reorganisation legally operational.

Third, Article 372A is a reference point for the broader question of how constitutional amendments
interact with the existing statutory landscape. Every major constitutional amendment — from the
42nd Amendment's sweeping changes to the recent amendments to the GST provisions — has potential
downstream effects on statutes drafted in terms of the old constitutional framework. The Article 372A
model, combining a time-limited executive adaptation power with an ouster of judicial review and a
reservation of full legislative authority, is one template for managing those downstream effects.
Whether that template would be appropriate in any given modern context depends on the specific nature
of the amendment and the scope of the statutory harmonisation required.
