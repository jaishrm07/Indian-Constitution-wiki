  (2) Subject to the provisions of any law made in this behalf by Parliament, the Commission shall consist of a Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson and three other Members and the conditions of service and tenure of office of the Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson and other Members so appointed shall be such as the President may by rule determine.]

  (4) The Commission shall have the power to regulate its own procedure.

  (5) It shall be the duty of the Commission—

  (a) to investigate and monitor all matters relating to the safeguards provided for the Scheduled Castes under this Constitution or under any other law for the time being in force or under any order of the Government and to evaluate the working of such safeguards;

  (b) to inquire into specific complaints with respect to the deprivation of rights and safeguards of the Scheduled Castes ;

  (c) to participate and advise on the planning process of socio-economic development of the Scheduled Castes and to evaluate the progress of their development under the Union and any State;

  (d) to present to the President, annually and at such other times as the Commission may deem fit, reports upon the working of those safeguards;

  (e) to make in such reports recommendations as to the measures that should be taken by the Union or any State for the effective implementation of those safeguards and other measures for the protection, welfare and socio-economic development of the Scheduled Castes ; and

  (f) to discharge such other functions in relation to the protection, welfare and development and advancement of the Scheduled Castes as the President may, subject to the provisions of any law made by Parliament, by rule specify.

  (6) The President shall cause all such reports to be laid before each House of Parliament along with a memorandum explaining the action taken or proposed to be taken on the recommendations relating to the Union and the reasons for the non-acceptance, if any, of any of such recommendations.

  (7) Where any such report, or any part thereof, relates to any matter with which any State Government is concerned, a copy of such report shall be forwarded to the Governor of the State who shall cause it to be laid before the Legislature of the State along with a memorandum explaining the action taken or proposed to be taken on the recommendations relating to the State and the reasons for the non-acceptance, if any, of any of such recommendations.

  (8) The Commission shall, while investigating any matter referred to in sub-clause (a) or inquiring into any complaint referred to in sub-clause (b) of clause (5), have all the powers of a civil court trying a suit and in particular in respect of the following matters, namely :—

  (a) summoning and enforcing the attendance of any person from any part of India and examining him on oath;

  (b) requiring the discovery and production of any document;

  (c) receiving evidence on affidavits;

  (d) requisitioning any public record or copy thereof from any court or office;

  (e) issuing commissions for the examination of witnesses and documents;

  (f) any other matter which the President may, by rule, determine.

  (9) The Union and every State Government shall consult the Commission on all major policy matters affecting Scheduled Castes ]. [(10)] In this article, references to the Scheduled Castes shall be construed as including references to the Anglo-Indian community.

## Why it matters

Article 338 creates the principal constitutional watchdog for India's Scheduled Caste communities. Before the 65th Amendment (1990), monitoring of SC and ST welfare was handled by a Special Officer appointed under the original Article 338 — a statutory functionary with limited powers and no civil-court authority. The 65th Amendment replaced that office with a multi-member Commission equipped with quasi-judicial investigative powers, reflecting growing recognition that episodic administrative oversight was insufficient to address the structural and pervasive nature of caste discrimination and SC under-representation in services. The 89th Amendment (2003) then separated the combined SC and ST Commission into two specialised bodies, on the ground that the interests of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes — while both experiencing historical disadvantage — arise from distinct social and legal frameworks that warrant dedicated institutional attention.

The Commission's significance extends beyond complaint-handling. Its annual reports to the President, which must be tabled in Parliament along with a memorandum explaining action taken, create a publicly available, constitutionally mandated accountability record. Civil society organisations, parliamentary committees, and courts cite these reports as evidence of the actual condition of SCs across States and Union territories. When a State fails to comply with a recommendation, the memorandum's explanation of non-acceptance becomes the basis for parliamentary questioning and, in some cases, judicial challenge. The requirement to explain non-acceptance — not merely to receive and file — is a significant procedural safeguard that distinguishes Article 338 from purely advisory bodies.

The mandatory consultation requirement in clause (9) is the Commission's most operationally significant power. The Union and every State Government must consult the NCSC on all major policy matters affecting SCs. This obligation applies before policy is finalised, not merely as a post-decision courtesy. The courts have held that bypassing this consultation makes the policy procedurally defective, though the precise remedies available — whether a policy adopted without required consultation can be struck down, or merely challenged by mandamus for consultation — are not uniform across jurisdictions.

## Reading the provision

**Clauses (1) to (3)** establish the Commission's constitutional existence and composition. The Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson, and three members are appointed by the President by warrant, giving the Commission appointments a formal constitutional grounding distinct from statutory body appointments. The conditions of service and tenure are determined by Presidential rules, subject to any Parliament-enacted law.

**Clause (4)** gives the Commission the power to regulate its own procedure, providing institutional autonomy in how it conducts investigations and hearings.

**Clause (5)** is the duty clause. Its six sub-clauses range from the investigative (monitoring safeguards, inquiring into complaints) to the advisory (participating in development planning, evaluating progress) to the reporting (presenting annual reports with recommendations). The breadth of this mandate means the NCSC has both a reactive function — responding to complaints — and a proactive function — monitoring, advising, and evaluating at a systemic level.

**Clause (6)** governs the tabling of reports in Parliament and the mandatory memorandum on action taken. The memorandum must explain both the action proposed to be taken and the reasons for non-acceptance of any recommendations. This is not a mere formality: it creates a public record of the government's response to each NCSC recommendation and provides the basis for parliamentary scrutiny through questions, debates, and committee inquiries.

**Clause (7)** replicates the tabling requirement at the State level, directing the Governor to lay State-related portions of NCSC reports before the State legislature with a corresponding memorandum.

**Clause (8)** gives the NCSC civil-court powers for investigation purposes — summoning witnesses, requiring production of documents, receiving evidence on affidavits, and requisitioning public records. These powers allow the Commission to conduct binding investigations rather than mere advisory inquiries, and distinguish it from bodies that rely solely on voluntary cooperation from the authorities they oversee.

**Clause (9)** imposes the mandatory consultation requirement on both the Union and every State Government for all major policy matters affecting SCs. The word "shall" makes this an obligation, not a discretion.

**Clause (10)** extends the NCSC's mandate to the Anglo-Indian community, an acknowledgment of that community's historical association with the SC and marginalised community frameworks in the constitutional text.

## Constitutional history: the 65th and 89th Amendments

The original Article 338 of the 1950 Constitution appointed a Special Officer for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes — a single constitutional officer with a primarily reporting function and no civil-court powers. The Constitution (Sixty-fifth Amendment) Act, 1990 replaced this officer with a multi-member National Commission for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, equipped with the civil-court powers now found in clause (8). This was a substantive upgrade reflecting recommendations from various commissions and committees that had found the Special Officer framework inadequate.

The Constitution (Eighty-ninth Amendment) Act, 2003 divided that combined Commission into two separate constitutional bodies: the National Commission for Scheduled Castes under the amended Article 338, and the National Commission for Scheduled Tribes under the new Article 338A. The rationale was that the interests of the two communities, while both grounded in historical disadvantage, required specialised attention: SC issues are primarily about caste-based discrimination, untouchability, atrocities, and service representation; ST issues are primarily about land rights, forest rights, displacement, the Fifth and Sixth Schedule governance frameworks, and tribal identity. A single Commission was seen as structurally unable to give adequate attention to both.

## Key cases

**State of Karnataka v Appa Balu Ingale (1995)** — The Supreme Court emphasised that constitutional bodies monitoring SC protections perform an essential accountability function and that their reports constitute relevant evidence of the condition of SCs when courts evaluate discrimination claims. Though decided under the pre-89th Amendment framework, the principle applies equally to the post-89th Amendment NCSC.

**Union of India v N.S. Rathnam and related proceedings** — High Courts have upheld the mandatory consultation requirement in clause (9), holding that policy decisions significantly affecting SC welfare made without consulting the NCSC are procedurally defective. The Madras High Court and Allahabad High Court have both addressed the scope of the "major policy matters" threshold, generally applying a broad reading that covers any policy with a substantial impact on SC welfare, employment, or educational access.

**National Commission for Scheduled Castes — suo motu petitions** — The Commission has filed suo motu petitions in the Supreme Court and High Courts drawing attention to non-compliance with its recommendations, illustrating that the NCSC's constitutional status gives it standing to approach courts when its reports are disregarded — a standing that a purely statutory or advisory body would not possess.

**Davinder Singh v State of Punjab (2024)** — The seven-judge Constitution Bench's ruling permitting sub-classification within the SC list has significant implications for the NCSC's mandate. The Commission has been urged by petitioners and commentators to undertake empirical mapping of the relative conditions of SC sub-groups across States, to provide the factual basis for State sub-classification exercises. Whether this falls within the NCSC's existing clause (5)(a) and (c) mandate — investigating safeguards and advising on socio-economic development — is a question the Commission itself will need to address.

## Current significance

The NCSC's annual reports remain the most publicly visible constitutional accountability mechanism for SC welfare. However, critics consistently note that the mandatory memorandum on non-acceptance is often perfunctory, providing boilerplate justifications rather than substantive engagement with the Commission's findings. Parliamentary scrutiny of these memoranda, while formally possible through standing committee processes, has not been as systematic as the constitutional design contemplated.

The Davinder Singh (2024) ruling has generated the most significant new challenge for the Commission. If States enact sub-classification laws, the NCSC will face pressure to produce caste-group-level data on conditions within the SC list — data that the Commission does not currently collect in a systematic or disaggregated form. The Commission's clause (5)(a) monitoring mandate and clause (5)(c) planning advisory function create a constitutional basis for the Commission to develop this capacity, but doing so would require additional resources and a significant methodological development in how the Commission gathers and presents data.

The question of Dalit Christians and Dalit Muslims — whether they should be included in the Scheduled Castes Order — is a related issue with significant implications for the NCSC's mandate. If the Supreme Court holds that the religion-based exclusion in paragraph 3 of the 1950 Order is unconstitutional, the NCSC's remit would expand substantially, and the Commission's monitoring and advisory functions would need to extend to new communities previously outside its jurisdiction.
