officialText: |-
  [230. Extension of jurisdiction of High Courts to Union territories.—(1) Parliament may by law extend the jurisdiction of a High Court to, or exclude the jurisdiction of a High Court from, any Union territory.

  (2) Where the High Court of a State exercises jurisdiction in relation to a Union territory,—

  (a) nothing in this Constitution shall be construed as empowering the Legislature of the State to increase, restrict or abolish that jurisdiction; and

  (b) the reference in article 227 to the Governor shall, in relation to any rules, forms or tables for subordinate courts in that territory, be construed as a reference to the President.

## Why it matters

Union territories occupy a distinctive place in India's constitutional geography — they are areas under Parliament's primary legislative jurisdiction where no State Legislature operates as the primary law-making body. Article 246(4) gives Parliament exclusive power to legislate for Union territories. When such a territory needs a High Court, the ordinary mechanism of Part VI — which assumes each State has its own High Court — does not apply. Article 230 provides the constitutional basis for extending an existing State High Court's jurisdiction to cover adjacent or otherwise suitable Union territories, creating a practical solution without requiring the establishment of separate High Courts for each territory.

The article's structural design — conferring jurisdiction-extension power on Parliament exclusively, and preventing the State Legislature from modifying jurisdiction once extended — reflects the allocation of legislative competence under the Constitution. A Union territory falls under Parliament's legislative authority; it would be constitutionally incongruous if a State Legislature could determine the judicial arrangements for Union territory residents. The residents of Chandigarh, Lakshadweep, or Dadra and Nagar Haveli have no elected State Legislature that represents them — their legislative representative is Parliament, and Parliament is the body Article 230 vests with the power to design their judicial arrangements.

The article also matters because of what it prevents. Without Clause (2)(a), a State Legislature might be tempted to use its power over its own High Court — through legislation affecting the court's jurisdiction, procedure, or bench composition — to indirectly affect the court's capacity to handle Union territory matters. Article 230 bars that path completely. The exclusivity of Parliamentary authority over Union territory judicial arrangements insulates those arrangements from State political calculations.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Parliament's discretionary power**: The grant of power is expressly to Parliament, exercised "by law." The power is discretionary — Parliament "may" extend or exclude, and is not constitutionally compelled to make any particular arrangement. Parliament can extend the jurisdiction of any High Court to any Union territory, can subsequently exclude that jurisdiction, and can do so by ordinary legislation without constitutional amendment. This gives significant flexibility in designing judicial arrangements for Union territories as their political and administrative circumstances change — a Union territory that becomes a State acquires its own High Court under Article 214, and the Article 230 arrangement naturally expires.

**Clause (2)(a) — State Legislature's exclusion**: Once Parliament extends a High Court's jurisdiction to a Union territory, the State Legislature "shall not be construed as empowered" to vary that jurisdiction. This is a clear constitutional prohibition — the State Legislature cannot increase it, restrict it, or abolish it through legislation. The jurisdiction over the Union territory is a Parliamentary grant, modifiable only by Parliament. State legislation that would otherwise affect a High Court's jurisdiction — through procedural reforms, subject-matter limitations, or institutional changes — cannot touch the Union territory dimension of that jurisdiction without Parliamentary authority.

**Clause (2)(b) — the Governor-to-President reference**: Article 227 gives High Courts superintendence over all subordinate courts and tribunals within their jurisdiction, with references to the "Governor" in relation to rules, forms, and tables for subordinate courts. Since Union territories have no Governor in the constitutional sense applicable to States (they are administered under Article 239 through Administrators appointed by the President), the article reads those references as applying to the President. This is a technical but practically necessary provision that ensures the superintendence mechanism under Article 227 operates without a missing constitutional addressee in the Union territory context.

**Relationship with Article 231**: Article 230 enables the extension of existing High Courts to Union territories; Article 231 allows Parliament to establish a common High Court for two or more States or for a State and a Union territory. Article 230 and Article 231 together give Parliament a menu of options for organising High Court coverage across diverse geographic and political situations.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's approach to Union territories was shaped by the understanding that the States reorganisation process was incomplete and that the territories that would ultimately become Union territories, Centrally administered areas, or new States were not all clearly defined at the time of the Constitution's drafting. The framers wanted a flexible mechanism that would allow Parliament to extend or adjust High Court jurisdiction over these areas without requiring constitutional amendment each time.

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, in his explanatory remarks on the provision, emphasised that the critical structural principle was that Parliament — the representative of the national interest and the legislature with authority over Union territories — must be the body controlling judicial arrangements in those territories. Allowing State Legislatures to modify judicial arrangements for Union territories would create a constitutionally anomalous situation in which a legislature with no responsibility for governing a territory could control access to justice within it.

The framers were also conscious that many Union territories might be geographically adjacent to States whose High Courts would naturally serve them. The provision was designed to enable this practical arrangement without creating a constitutional ambiguity about who controlled the judicial arrangements once made.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Kirloskar Pneumatic Co. Ltd. (1996)** — The Supreme Court confirmed that jurisdiction exercised by a High Court over a Union territory flows from Parliament's exercise of the power under Article 230, not from any inherent territorial claim of the High Court. This matters when a cause of action straddles a State and an adjacent Union territory — the source and extent of the High Court's Union territory jurisdiction must be traced to the Parliamentary law extending it. A party cannot invoke a High Court's Union territory jurisdiction unless that jurisdiction has been affirmatively established by Parliament under Article 230.

**Delhi Administration v. Gurdip Singh Uban (2000)** — In the context of the Delhi High Court's jurisdiction — before the special constitutional arrangements for Delhi under Article 239AA were more fully developed through the Supreme Court's jurisprudence — the Court considered the interaction between Parliament's power under Article 230 and the administration of justice in Union territories. The decision confirmed the exclusive Parliamentary character of jurisdiction extension or exclusion, and that the Delhi Administration could not by executive order alter the High Court's jurisdictional arrangements.

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — The Supreme Court's broader analysis of the constitutional relationship between Union and State governments in the governance of territories provides relevant context for Article 230's deliberate assignment of jurisdiction-regulation power over Union territories to Parliament rather than State Legislatures. The Court's observation that the Union's relationship to Union territories is qualitatively different from its relationship to States reinforces why the jurisdiction-over-Union-territories power belongs to Parliament.

**Government of NCT of Delhi v. Union of India (2018)** — The Supreme Court's Constitution Bench decision on Delhi governance — confirming that Delhi as a Union territory under Article 239AA has a special status that is neither a full State nor an ordinary Union territory — implicitly affects the Article 230 framework as it applies to Delhi. The High Court of Delhi's jurisdiction over the NCT of Delhi is constitutionally anchored in the special legislative arrangements for Delhi rather than in an ordinary Article 230 extension, illustrating how the Delhi constitutional scheme has partially superseded the generic Article 230 framework.

**Rupinder Singh Sodhi v. Union of India (1983)** — The Supreme Court addressed the scope of a High Court's jurisdiction when exercised over a Union territory, holding that the High Court in this capacity applies national constitutional standards and cannot be more or less generous in applying constitutional rights depending on whether the proceedings relate to the Union territory portion of its jurisdiction or the State portion.

## Current significance

Article 230 continues to operate for several Union territories. Chandigarh is served by the Punjab and Haryana High Court; Dadra and Nagar Haveli and Daman and Diu are served by the Bombay High Court; Lakshadweep is served by the Kerala High Court. The Andaman and Nicobar Islands are served by the Calcutta High Court — an arrangement that requires litigants from the islands to access a court seated more than a thousand kilometres away on the mainland. These arrangements reflect Parliament's exercise of its Article 230 power through the legislation reorganising States or creating Union territories, and cannot be modified by the State governments in whose High Courts the territorial jurisdiction vests.

Questions about the adequacy of judicial access in geographically remote Union territories — particularly the logistical difficulty for litigants in the Andaman and Nicobar Islands and in Lakshadweep — are live policy concerns. Circuit benches and video-conferencing facilities have partially ameliorated these difficulties, but they do not address the fundamental geographic distance between the litigant and the court. Any change to these jurisdictional arrangements requires Parliament's action under Article 230, not the agreement of the relevant State government. This creates a constitutional dependency that Union territory administrations and their residents cannot resolve through their own legislative processes.

The creation of a dedicated High Court for Jammu and Kashmir and Ladakh following the reorganisation of the former State in 2019 under the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act involved both Article 214 (for Jammu and Kashmir as a newly created Union territory with a legislature) and Article 230 considerations. Parliament's power under Article 230 was relevant to structuring Ladakh's judicial arrangements as a Union territory without a legislature. This episode illustrates how Article 230 continues to be invoked when India's territorial and administrative map changes, and how Parliament's exclusive jurisdiction over Union territory judicial arrangements gives it both the flexibility and the responsibility to ensure adequate judicial access in these areas.

The periodic debate about whether Chandigarh should have its own High Court — given that it serves as the capital of both Punjab and Haryana while being a Union territory — engages Article 230 directly. Any establishment of a dedicated High Court for Chandigarh would require Parliamentary legislation modifying the current arrangement under which the Punjab and Haryana High Court exercises jurisdiction. The constitutional basis for the change would be Article 231 (establishing a common High Court for a State and Union territory) or direct establishment under Article 214 read with appropriate territorial legislation, with Article 230 governing the transition.
