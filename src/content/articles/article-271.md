## Why it matters

Article 271 is a structural carve-out from the revenue-sharing architecture of Articles 269 and 270. Those articles require Parliament to share the net proceeds of specified taxes with States — either by direct assignment or through the Finance Commission-determined divisible pool. Article 271 allows Parliament to levy a surcharge — an addition on top of an existing duty or tax — the entire proceeds of which remain in the Consolidated Fund of India and are never shared with States at all. This is not a discretionary policy choice that Parliament makes afresh each year; it is a constitutionally explicit exception, confirmed by the words "notwithstanding anything in articles 269 and 270."

The fiscal stakes are enormous. When the Union raises a cess on income tax or a surcharge on petroleum duties, those receipts are excluded from the gross tax revenue base on which the Finance Commission's devolution percentage operates. If the divisible pool percentage is 41 percent but 15 percent of gross tax revenue has already been removed by cesses and surcharges before the pool is calculated, the effective devolution rate is closer to 35 percent of true gross collection. States have argued for decades that this "shrinking base" problem converts a notional improvement in the devolution percentage into a real fiscal loss.

The One Hundred and First Amendment Act 2016 introduced a constitutionally significant limitation by bracketing off the GST — Parliament may not surcharge GST under Article 271. This preserves the integrity of the GST Council-mediated IGST apportionment and prevents the Union from using the surcharge power to unilaterally redirect GST revenues to the Centre. The GST carve-out is one of the most significant constraints on Parliament's fiscal unilateralism in the post-2016 constitutional order.

Understanding Article 271 requires reading it against Articles 265 (no tax without law), 269–270 (sharing obligations), 280 (Finance Commission mandate), and the Seventh Schedule's taxation entries. The article sits at the intersection of Parliament's fiscal autonomy and the federally mandated obligation to share revenue — and it structurally tilts the balance in Parliament's favour.

## Reading the provision

**The "notwithstanding" clause:** The opening words — "Notwithstanding anything in articles 269 and 270" — are constitutionally decisive. They establish that the surcharge power is not subject to, and cannot be overridden by, the sharing obligations in Articles 269 and 270. This non-obstante language is standard in constitutional provisions that create exceptions to adjacent rules. Courts read it as placing the surcharge power outside the distribution framework entirely.

**"Parliament may at any time increase":** The power is unfettered as to timing — Parliament may impose a surcharge in the Finance Act at any point in the year, or maintain one indefinitely. There is no sunset requirement, no Finance Commission approval needed, and no requirement to consult States. The phrase "at any time" forecloses any argument that surcharges require prior Finance Commission authorisation.

**"Any of the duties or taxes referred to in those articles":** The surcharge must attach to a tax or duty that already falls within Articles 269 or 270 — it cannot be imposed on an independent subject. This is the critical nexus requirement: the surcharge piggybacks on a shared tax and thereby acquires the constitutional legitimacy to redirect those proceeds to the Union. A levy that is not an increment on an Articles 269/270 tax but rather a standalone charge on a new subject must find its authority elsewhere.

**The GST exclusion — "[except the goods and services tax under article 246A]":** This bracket, inserted in 2016, removes GST from the surcharge power. Parliament cannot levy a surcharge on CGST, IGST, or SGST under Article 271. The GST Council framework under Articles 269A and 279A determines GST apportionment, and the Article 271 bypass is unavailable for that revenue stream.

**"The whole proceeds of any such surcharge shall form part of the Consolidated Fund of India":** Unlike Articles 269 and 270, where the divisible pool belongs in constitutional principle to the States even if collected by the Union, surcharge proceeds are the Union's from the moment of collection. There is no pass-through obligation. The Comptroller and Auditor-General verifies that surcharge proceeds are correctly credited to the Consolidated Fund, but there is no legal mechanism requiring any share to be passed to States.

**Cess versus surcharge — the constitutional distinction:** A cess is typically a levy dedicated to a specific purpose fund (Education Cess to the Prarambhik Shiksha Kosh, Health Cess to national health programmes). A surcharge is a percentage addition on a base tax that flows into the general Consolidated Fund. Both are instruments that "increase" an existing tax within the meaning of Article 271, and both fall within the surcharge power. The distinction matters for expenditure accounting — cess funds must be used for the stated purpose — but not for the revenue-sharing analysis, since neither is shared with States.

## The Constituent Assembly debate

The Constituent Assembly's drafting of Article 271 (then Draft Article 257) attracted limited debate compared to the major distribution articles, but the framers were aware of the tension they were building into the structure. B.R. Ambedkar and T.T. Krishnamachari both acknowledged that the Union would need fiscal flexibility to fund national programmes — defence, infrastructure, social welfare — beyond what the shared pool could support. The surcharge power was designed as a safety valve: Parliament could, when pressing Union purposes required it, tap the shared tax base without going through the elaborate distribution mechanism.

The framers did not envision the surcharge power being used as a routine device to compress the divisible pool. The debates assumed that surcharges would be exceptional and purpose-specific — the "for purposes of the Union" language was intended to connect the surcharge to an identifiable Union need, not to serve as a blanket authorisation for fiscal diversion. Whether that intent has been honoured is disputed: contemporary Finance Commission reports suggest the cesses and surcharges have become structurally embedded features of Union revenue, not emergency supplements.

T.T. Krishnamachari, who was deeply involved in shaping Part XII, took the position that the Finance Commission would serve as a counterbalancing institution — its devolution recommendations would take into account the overall Union-State fiscal balance, implicitly including the diversion through surcharges. This "self-correcting" assumption has proved optimistic: Finance Commissions can only recommend; they cannot compel the Union to reduce its cess and surcharge base.

## Key cases

**State of West Bengal v. Union of India (1962):** The Supreme Court affirmed the principle that the surcharge power under Article 271 is an express constitutional exception to the sharing obligation. The Court held that Parliament's discretion to levy surcharges is not constrained by the Finance Commission's recommended sharing percentages and that a challenge to a surcharge on the ground that it reduces State revenues is not justiciable. The constitutional asymmetry between the Union's surcharge freedom and the States' receipt of a smaller divisible pool is a structural feature of Part XII, not a constitutional defect.

**Vijay Madhok v. Union of India (Education Cess challenge, 2003):** The petitioner challenged the Education Cess levied on income tax as an impermissible bypass of the State revenue-sharing mechanism, arguing that the cess was constitutionally indistinguishable from a new tax on a shared subject. The Delhi High Court dismissed the challenge, holding that Article 271 expressly permits surcharges on shared taxes for Union purposes and that Parliament's earmarking of the cess for a national education fund was a policy choice within its constitutional competence. The Court confirmed that cesses crediting a specified fund still qualify as instruments that "increase" a shared tax within the Article 271 framework.

**Union of India v. Harbhajan Singh Dhillon (1972):** Although primarily about the scope of Parliament's power to tax wealth, the Court's observations on the relationship between Article 246 legislative competence and Article 265's requirement of legal authority confirmed that the surcharge power under Article 271 is itself the "law" authorising the levy — no further legislative basis is needed beyond Parliament's Finance Act imposing the surcharge pursuant to Article 271.

**Jindal Stainless Ltd. v. State of Haryana (2016):** The Constitution Bench's analysis of the distinction between regulatory charges, compensatory fees, and taxes provided the doctrinal vocabulary for distinguishing levies that genuinely "increase" a shared tax (within Article 271) from charges that are, in substance, new independent levies requiring their own legislative competence under the Seventh Schedule.

**In re Special Courts Bill (Advisory Opinion, 1979):** The Supreme Court's engagement with the constitutional scheme of Part XII in this advisory opinion clarified that the Consolidated Fund of India is the repository of all Union revenues, including surcharges under Article 271, and that the Finance Commission's role is advisory — it does not constitute a constitutional constraint on Parliament's decision to route revenues through the surcharge mechanism rather than the divisible pool.

## Current significance

Cess proliferation has become the sharpest fiscal-federalism tension under Part XII. The Fifteenth Finance Commission (2021–26) explicitly noted that Union recourse to cesses and surcharges — which stood at over 15 percent of gross tax revenue in recent years — has structurally shrunk the divisible pool. States receive 41 percent of a base that has already been diminished by cess and surcharge extractions, meaning that the nominal devolution percentage significantly overstates the effective transfer. Finance Commission Chairman N.K. Singh stated publicly that the cess-and-surcharge base had grown to a level that undermined the devolution framework and recommended greater transparency and moderation.

States have consistently demanded either a cap on cess and surcharge collections as a proportion of gross tax revenue, or inclusion of cess and surcharge proceeds within the computation base for the divisible pool percentage. The Union has resisted both proposals, arguing that cesses serve specific programmatic purposes (education, health, rural roads) that justify ringfencing and that including them in the pool would dilute funding for Union-administered schemes. This argument has been contested by States which note that they are equally capable of administering education and health programmes and that directing funds through Union-controlled schemes reduces State fiscal autonomy.

The Sixteenth Finance Commission (constituted in 2023 for the 2026–31 award period) has been asked by several State governments to address the cess and surcharge base problem directly. Constitutional lawyers and fiscal economists have proposed three mechanisms — a constitutional cap requiring amendment of Article 271, a Finance Commission formula that backs out cess and surcharge collection when applying the devolution percentage, and a statutory requirement for Finance Act transparency on the cess-and-surcharge base. Each raises different constitutional and legislative questions. The Article 271 debate is no longer peripheral to Indian fiscal federalism; it is central to the question of whether the 41 percent devolution formula means what States believe it means.

Article 271 is one of the clearest illustrations of the Constitution's asymmetric federal design. The Union possesses both revenue-raising flexibility (through surcharges that bypass sharing) and revenue-sharing obligations (through the divisible pool), and the balance between these two is determined by parliamentary legislation rather than by the Finance Commission formula. This asymmetry is the constitutional root of the "sharing ratio on a shrinking base" problem that defines fiscal federalism contestation in contemporary India. The GST exclusion introduced in 2016 is a partial structural correction — it ensures that the single largest post-2017 revenue stream cannot be surcharged away from States — but it leaves the income tax and petroleum duty surcharge problem unresolved.
