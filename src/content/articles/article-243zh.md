## What this Article does

Article 243ZH is the gateway to all of Part IXB. Every right, obligation, and governance standard in Part IXB depends on these definitions to determine which societies are covered and under which level of government's jurisdiction. The article defines eight expressions — "authorised person", "board", "co-operative society", "multi-State co-operative society", "office bearer", "Registrar", "State Act", and "State level co-operative society" — that run through the entire Part. Article 243ZI, textually incorporated at the end of the same provision in the official text, adds the affirmative constitutional mandate: State legislatures may legislate for incorporation, regulation and winding up of co-operative societies on the principles of voluntary formation, democratic member-control, member-economic participation, and autonomous functioning.

## Background — the 97th Amendment and Part IXB

Part IXB was inserted into the Constitution by the Constitution (Ninety-seventh Amendment) Act, 2011, a Central Government initiative to bring co-operative societies under a constitutional framework analogous to the panchayat and municipal frameworks of Parts IX and IXA. The stated rationale was that co-operative societies had become vehicles of political patronage rather than autonomous member-owned institutions: boards were superseded arbitrarily, elections were indefinitely postponed, management was handed to government-appointed administrators, and audit and disclosure requirements were honoured in the breach. The 97th Amendment sought to constitutionalise minimum governance standards — five-year elected boards, mandatory elections, independent audit, limits on supersession, member information rights — that State legislatures could not undercut.

Part IXB's definitional scheme in Article 243ZH draws the central distinction that would later become the fulcrum of the constitutional challenge: the difference between ordinary "co-operative societies" registered under State law, and "multi-State co-operative societies" whose objects are not confined to one State. This distinction maps onto the legislative competence framework: co-operative societies are on Entry 32 of the State List (List II), while multi-State co-operatives fall under Entry 44 of the Union List (List I).

## Reading the provision

The eight definitions work as follows.

"Co-operative society" (clause (c)) is defined by reference to registration under any law relating to co-operative societies for the time being in force in any State. The definition is deliberately neutral: it does not import substantive requirements of cooperation, mutual aid, or democratic control into the definition itself — those are addressed by the governance standards elsewhere in Part IXB.

"Multi-State co-operative society" (clause (d)) is the critical jurisdictional dividing line. A society qualifies if its objects are not confined to one State and it is registered under law relating to such co-operatives. The Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002 is the principal parliamentary legislation governing this category. Post-Rajendra N. Shah, these societies are the primary domain within which Part IXB's mandatory standards apply.

"Registrar" (clause (f)) has a bifurcated definition mirroring the federal division: the Central Registrar (appointed by the Central Government) for multi-State co-operatives, and State Registrars (appointed under State law) for all others. This dual-registry structure reflects the underlying competence split in the Seventh Schedule.

"State level co-operative society" (clause (h)) identifies apex institutions — societies whose area of operation extends to the whole State, as defined under State law. This category matters for provisions within Part IXB that impose differentiated standards on large apex co-operatives versus smaller primary societies.

"Board" and "office bearer" (clauses (b) and (e)) provide the governance vocabulary for Part IXB's detailed provisions on board elections, term limits, seat reservations, and supersession. The definitions are deliberately broad to accommodate the diversity of co-operative structures across sectors and States.

## Key cases

**Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah (2021) 6 SCC 1** — A three-judge bench of the Supreme Court held that the provisions of Part IXB were unconstitutional to the extent they purported to apply to State co-operative societies. The core of the reasoning was federal: Parliament's constituent power to amend the Constitution does not extend to destroying States' exclusive legislative domain over matters in the State List. Since Entry 32 List II vests legislative competence over co-operative societies in States, an amendment that mandated particular governance arrangements for State-registered co-operatives was, in substance, Parliament legislating on a State List subject through a constitutional amendment. The Court applied the basic structure doctrine — specifically its federalism dimension — to strike down the offending provisions in their application to State co-operatives. Part IXB was upheld only in its application to multi-State co-operatives under Entry 44 List I.

**National Cooperative Exports Ltd. v. State of Maharashtra** — Pre-amendment litigation establishing that "multi-State" character depends on the society's objects as registered, not merely on its operational reach or membership geography. Courts have consistently refused to allow registered State co-operatives to claim multi-State status merely because their commercial activities crossed State lines.

**Sarangdhar Nilkanth Mujumdar v. State of Maharashtra (2013)** — Decided in the transitional period after the 97th Amendment but before Rajendra N. Shah, this case considered whether Maharashtra's Co-operative Societies Act 1960 provisions on board supersession were inconsistent with Part IXB's new constitutional floor. The court held that the transitional period under Article 243ZT preserved existing State law pending conforming amendment.

## Current significance

After Rajendra N. Shah, Article 243ZH's definitions serve primarily to demarcate the surviving domain of Part IXB: multi-State co-operative societies governed by Parliament under Entry 44 List I.
For State co-operatives, the definitions are relevant only to the extent State legislatures have chosen to use Part IXB's definitional vocabulary in their own conforming legislation.

The Union Government's 2022 National Cooperative Policy and the establishment of a separate Ministry of Cooperation signal continuing policy interest in the sector.
Any legislative response to Rajendra N. Shah must, however, work within the constitutionally restored State competence rather than reimposing central standards through constitutional amendment.

The definitional architecture of Article 243ZH will remain central to any future litigation about whether a given society is a "multi-State co-operative society" and therefore within Parliament's regulatory domain.
Disputes about whether a society's objects are "confined to one State" — the definitional line between Entry 32 and Entry 44 competence — are among the most practically significant jurisdictional questions in co-operative law after the 2021 ruling.
The Multi-State Co-operative Societies (Amendment) Act, 2023, which significantly expanded the Central Government's oversight role over multi-State co-operatives, rests entirely on the jurisdictional foundation that Article 243ZH's definition of "multi-State co-operative society" provides.
