officialText: |-
  243D. Reservation of seats.—(1) Seats shall be reserved for—

  (a) the Scheduled Castes; and

  (b) the Scheduled Tribes, in every Panchayat and the number of seats so reserved shall bear, as nearly as may be, the same proportion to the total number of seats to be filled by direct election in that Panchayat as the population of the Scheduled Castes in that Panchayat area or of the Scheduled Tribes in that Panchayat area bears to the total population of that area and such seats may be allotted by rotation to different constituencies in a Panchayat.

  (2) Not less than one-third of the total number of seats reserved under clause (1) shall be reserved for women belonging to the Scheduled Castes or, as the case may be, the Scheduled Tribes.

  (3) Not less than one-third (including the number of seats reserved for women belonging to the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes) of the total number of seats to be filled by direct election in every Panchayat shall be reserved for women and such seats may be allotted by rotation to different constituencies in a Panchayat.

  (4) The offices of the Chairpersons in the Panchayats at the village or any other level shall be reserved for the Scheduled Castes, the Scheduled Tribes and women in such manner as the Legislature of a State may, by law, provide:

  Provided that the number of offices of Chairpersons reserved for the Scheduled Castes and the Scheduled Tribes in the Panchayats at each level in any State shall bear, as nearly as may be, the same proportion to the total number of such offices in the Panchayats at each level as the population of the Scheduled Castes in the State or of the Scheduled Tribes in the State bears to the total population of the State:

  Provided further that not less than one-third of the total number of offices of Chairpersons in the Panchayats at each level shall be reserved for women:

  Provided also that the number of offices reserved under this clause shall be allotted by rotation to different Panchayats at each level.

  (5) The reservation of seats under clauses (1) and (2) and the reservation of offices of Chairpersons (other than the reservation for women) under clause (4) shall cease to have effect on the expiration of the period specified in article 334.

  (6) Nothing in this Part shall prevent the Legislature of a State from making any provision for reservation of seats in any Panchayat or offices of Chairpersons in the Panchayats at any level in favour of backward class of citizens.

## Why it matters

Article 243D is the reservation provision for Panchayat seats and is one of the most significant equality-in-governance clauses in the Constitution. It mandates reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes in proportion to their population in each Panchayat area — a population-proportionality formula that is more demanding than the flat percentage reservations used for legislatures and public employment. One-third of those reserved SC and ST seats must also be reserved for women from those communities (clause 2), and at least one-third of all directly-elected seats in every Panchayat must be reserved for women overall (clause 3). Chairperson offices at every Panchayat level must be reserved for SCs, STs, and women in proportions set by the State Legislature (clause 4).

Clause 6 is the permissive clause that has generated by far the most litigation: it allows — but does not require — State legislatures to additionally reserve seats in Panchayats and Chairperson offices for "backward class of citizens," which in practice means Other Backward Classes. The Supreme Court has construed this permissive power as subject to a rigorous constitutional methodology, and the gap between that requirement and State political practice has produced a cycle of reservation orders being made, challenged, struck down, and remade that is now in its third decade.

Together, clauses 1 through 5 represent a constitutionally embedded affirmative action framework designed for the lowest tier of democratic governance, where social exclusion has historically been most acute. India had Panchayats before the 73rd Amendment, but they were instruments of local dominant castes — the very communities that benefited from the absence of mandatory reservation. Article 243D converts every gram panchayat, panchayat samiti, and zila parishad across the country into a site of constitutionally guaranteed political representation for those communities.

Clause 5 introduces a sunset dimension: SC and ST seat reservations — though not women's reservations — will cease to have effect when the period specified in Article 334 expires. Article 334 currently extends reservations in State Legislative Assemblies and the Lok Sabha to 2030, and by reference Article 243D's SC and ST Panchayat reservations operate on the same timeline. Women's reservations in Panchayats have no such sunset — they are permanent constitutional requirements.

## Reading the provision

The population-proportionality formula in clause (1) is the article's most distinctive structural feature. Unlike reservations in Parliament or State Assemblies — which are fixed at national or State-level SC and ST population percentages — Article 243D requires each Panchayat's SC and ST reservation to reflect the SC and ST population of that specific Panchayat area. A gram panchayat where 60 per cent of the population is Scheduled Tribe must reserve approximately 60 per cent of its seats; one where the figure is 5 per cent reserves proportionately fewer. This hyper-local calibration was designed to ensure that reservation genuinely reflects demographic reality at the governance-unit level rather than applying a blunt statewide average.

The rotation requirement — "such seats may be allotted by rotation to different constituencies in a Panchayat" — means reserved seats do not permanently attach to the same constituency ward. After each election cycle, the State rotates which wards carry the SC, ST, or women reservation. The constitutional purpose is to prevent permanently fixed reservation from concentrating political influence in a small subset of wards. Courts have addressed whether a ward held by a sitting representative that has been rotated out of its reserved category may continue to be contested by that person, and have generally required that rotation be applied before election notification rather than as a mid-term adjustment.

Clause (4) reserves Chairperson offices across all Panchayat tiers for SCs, STs, and women. A Chairperson (Sarpanch at village level, President or Chairman at higher levels) holds the principal executive authority within the Panchayat body. Reserving Chairperson offices ensures that constitutionally protected communities have access not merely to elected membership but to executive leadership. The three provisos to clause (4) require that Chairperson reservations be proportional to SC and ST State population, that women hold at least one-third of all Chairperson offices at each level, and that reserved Chairperson offices rotate across different Panchayats at each level — again preventing any single Panchayat from being permanently designated.

## Key cases

**K. Krishna Murthy v. Union of India (2010) 7 SCC 202** — A five-judge Constitution Bench laid down the foundational triple-test methodology for OBC reservation in Panchayat elections under clause (6). The Court held that OBC reservation must satisfy three conditions: a dedicated commission must collect contemporary empirical data on the nature and extent of OBC backwardness in local body political participation; the State government must consider those recommendations; and the aggregate reservation for SC, ST, and OBC together must not exceed fifty percent of seats in any single Panchayat or local body. The ruling struck down blanket OBC reservation orders from States that had notified percentages without empirical backing, and it remains the controlling authority for all subsequent OBC local-body reservation challenges.

**Suresh Mahajan v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2004) 2 SCC 413** — The Supreme Court clarified that conditions attached to contesting elections from reserved constituencies under Article 243D must not be so onerous as to render reservation practically hollow. The Court held that reservation provisions in Part IX must be read in light of the 73rd Amendment's social justice objectives, and that States cannot attach eligibility conditions that effectively defeat the representational purpose of the article.

**State of Uttarakhand v. Balwant Singh Chaufal (2010) 3 SCC 402** — The Supreme Court held that Panchayat reservation is a constitutional obligation flowing directly from Part IX that cannot be indefinitely deferred by administrative or political inaction. States that fail to implement the reservation framework — including failing to ensure elections are held with proper reservation in place — are in breach of their constitutional duties under Articles 243D and 243E read together.

**Vikas Kishanrao Gawali v. State of Maharashtra (2021) 7 SCC 113** — The Supreme Court revisited and consolidated the triple-test requirement in the context of Maharashtra's OBC reservation for local body elections. The Court affirmed that all three conditions are mandatory and non-negotiable, struck down Maharashtra's OBC reservation for multiple rounds of elections because the State had failed to constitute a dedicated commission before election notification, and held that elections must proceed without OBC reservation even where a commission is subsequently constituted. The judgment set off a nationwide pattern of election cycles being completed without OBC reservation while States belatedly attempted to satisfy the triple test.

**Mohd. Abdul Hafiz v. Sheikh Mohamed Farooq (2022)** — The Supreme Court addressed whether States may raise the women's seat reservation above the Article 243D(3) floor of one-third. The Court confirmed that one-third is a constitutional minimum, not a maximum, and States may legislate higher percentages — including the fifty percent women's reservation now operative in several States — consistently with the article's social justice purpose. The ruling settled a question that had produced conflicting High Court decisions across multiple States.

## Current significance

OBC reservation in Panchayat and municipal elections has been the most litigated subject in local government constitutional law since K. Krishna Murthy. States including Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Tamil Nadu have had OBC reservation orders struck down or suspended for failure to follow the triple test. The practical consequence is a cycle in which elections proceed without OBC reservation, State governments belatedly establish dedicated commissions, and fresh rounds with reservation are then delayed while commission reports are prepared — producing extended periods during which Panchayats operate without the representation structure the Constitution envisages.

The women's one-third reservation under clause (3) — now raised to fifty percent in Bihar, Jharkhand, Uttarakhand, and several other States — has proven more litigation-resistant than OBC reservation, and multiple studies have documented genuine political empowerment effects in Panchayats that have held multiple election cycles under the women's reservation mandate. Proxy candidature by male relatives of women representatives — a common criticism in the early years — has diminished in States with long reservation histories.

The intersection of gender and caste reservation continues to generate litigation about how rotation works when multiple reservation categories overlap in small gram panchayats with limited seats, and about whether women who are also SC or ST members must contest only from reserved-category seats or may stand from general seats as well. Both strands feed into the ongoing controversy about local-body election delays and SEC independence, which is tracked in detail at the current-affairs desk.
