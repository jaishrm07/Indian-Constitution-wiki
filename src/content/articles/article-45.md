## Why it matters

Article 45 in its current form sits at the intersection of the earliest phase of child development — from birth to age six — and the state's constitutional obligation to invest in that phase. Nutrition, stimulation, language development, and pre-school learning in the zero-to-six window have well-documented lifelong effects on cognitive development and educational attainment. The shift from the original Article 45's education mandate to the present early-childhood-care directive reflects both the unfulfilled history of the original provision and the recognition that school-readiness begins before formal schooling.

## The original Article 45 and its failure

As originally enacted in 1950, Article 45 directed the State to endeavour to provide, within ten years from the commencement of the Constitution, free and compulsory education for all children until they complete the age of fourteen years. This directive was never fulfilled within the ten-year window and remained substantially unachieved for decades. Enrolment rates improved, but dropout rates, teacher absenteeism, and inadequate infrastructure meant the constitutional aspiration was not translated into reality for large sections of the population — making Article 45 the most prominent unmet directive principle in the Constitution's first fifty years.

## The Eighty-Sixth Amendment and Article 21A

The Constitution (Eighty-Sixth Amendment) Act, 2002 fundamentally restructured the constitutional treatment of education. The amendment inserted Article 21A, making the right to free and compulsory education for children between six and fourteen years a justiciable fundamental right — enforceable through Articles 32 and 226. Simultaneously, Article 45 was rewritten to shift the remaining directive to the pre-school age group: the state must endeavour to provide early childhood care and education for children until they complete six years.

The effect was a two-tier system: children from six to fourteen have a fundamental right under Article 21A, implemented through the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009 (RTE Act). Children below six have a directive-principle entitlement under Article 45, implemented primarily through the Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS) scheme and the Anganwadi network, without the same justiciable enforcement pathway.

## How courts use it

Article 45 remains a directive principle rather than a fundamental right, but courts treat it as an important interpretive guide. Its predecessor version was influential in generating the right to education as part of Article 21 in Unni Krishnan, and its current form is cited when courts consider the obligations of the state toward pre-school children. Courts have also used Article 45 alongside Article 47 (nutrition and public health) when reviewing the adequacy of nutritional supplementation for young children in the Supreme Court's food-rights monitoring proceedings.

## Key cases

**Unni Krishnan J.P. v. State of Andhra Pradesh (1993)** — Before the Eighty-Sixth Amendment, the Court held that the original Article 45 — directing free and compulsory education for all children up to fourteen years — was enforceable as part of the right to life under Article 21. The reasoning treated the directive principle as giving substantive content to the fundamental right. This judicial interpretation was subsequently codified through the insertion of Article 21A.

**Society for Unaided Private Schools of Rajasthan v. Union of India (2012)** — The Court upheld the constitutional validity of the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009 for the six-to-fourteen age group as a legitimate exercise under Article 21A, while affirming that the under-six group remains in the directive-principles sphere under Article 45. The distinction between the two age groups was treated as a deliberate constitutional design choice.

**Ashoka Kumar Thakur v. Union of India (2008)** — While primarily concerned with reservations in higher education, the Court's discussion of the state's educational obligations cited the continuum from early childhood (Article 45) through compulsory elementary schooling (Article 21A) to higher education as a unified constitutional concern, demonstrating that Article 45 is treated as part of a connected educational rights framework.

## Implementation framework

The Integrated Child Development Services scheme, operating through over 1.4 million Anganwadi centres, is the primary vehicle for implementing Article 45. Anganwadi workers provide supplementary nutrition, pre-school education, health check-ups, immunisation, and referral services for children under six and their mothers. The National Early Childhood Care and Education Policy and the National Education Policy 2020 have provided updated frameworks emphasising foundational learning. Despite the scale of the network, coverage gaps, nutritional shortfalls, and inadequate early-stimulation activity mean the directive remains far from fully implemented across all states.

The Supreme Court has periodically issued directions about Anganwadi staffing, wages, and regularisation in the context of the right-to-food proceedings before the Court's food-rights bench, treating Anganwadi worker conditions as connected to the state's ability to deliver on its Article 45 obligation.

## Current significance

Advocacy and litigation seeking to extend justiciable rights to the under-six cohort — by reading Article 45 with Articles 21 and 21A to create an enforceable entitlement to quality early childhood care — remains an active legal strategy. The constitutional status of Anganwadi workers, their entitlement to regularisation and fair wages, and the adequacy of per-child allocations under ICDS are closely connected to Article 45. The National Education Policy 2020, which emphasises foundational literacy and numeracy in the early years as the precondition for all subsequent learning, has revived constitutional discussions about whether the directive standard of "endeavour" for under-six children should be upgraded to a justiciable right comparable to Article 21A.
