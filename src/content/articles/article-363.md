  from the agreements made between the rulers of the old Indian princely

  363. Bar to interference by courts in disputes arising out of certain treaties, agreements, etc.—(1) Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution but subject to the provisions of article 143, neither the Supreme Court nor any other court shall have jurisdiction in any dispute arising out of any provision of a treaty, agreement, covenant, engagement, sanad or other similar instrument which was entered into or executed before the commencement of this Constitution by any Ruler of an Indian State and to which the Government of the Dominion of India or any of its predecessor Governments was a party and which has or has been continued in operation after such commencement, or in any dispute in respect of any right accruing under or any liability or obligation arising out of any of the provisions of this Constitution relating to any such treaty, agreement, covenant, engagement, sanad or other similar instrument.

  (2) In this article—

  (a) "Indian State" means any territory recognised before the commencement of this Constitution by His Majesty or the Government of the Dominion of India as being such a State; and

  (b) "Ruler" includes the Prince, Chief or other person recognised before such commencement by His Majesty or the Government of the Dominion of India as the Ruler of any Indian State.

## Why it matters

Article 363 sits at the intersection of constitutional law and the law of nations. The
instruments it covers — accession documents, merger agreements, sanads, covenants —
are the constitutional residue of what was essentially a diplomatic negotiation between
the Indian Dominion and hundreds of semi-sovereign polities. These instruments were
concluded at a moment of extraordinary political pressure, with the looming possibility
that some principalities might seek separate arrangements with Pakistan or maintain
independent neutrality. The terms of the accession were therefore not purely voluntary
contracts; they were political settlements shaped by the asymmetry of bargaining power
between a nascent but powerful Union government and individual ruling families whose
alternative to integration was risky isolation.

Article 363 is a non-justiciability clause — a constitutional provision that deliberately
takes a category of disputes out of the reach of courts. In Indian constitutional law,
non-justiciability clauses are rare. The general principle is that constitutional rights
are enforceable through the courts, and that the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court in
particular is a constitutional safeguard against executive and legislative overreach.
Article 363 creates a deliberate exception to that principle for disputes arising from
the terms of India's territorial integration.

The rationale is rooted in the nature of the accession and merger instruments themselves.
Allowing ordinary courts to adjudicate disputes about those terms would have exposed
every aspect of the integration to litigation, potentially unravelling arrangements that
were fundamental to the physical integrity of the Indian state. The Constituent Assembly's
decision to exclude these disputes from judicial cognisance was therefore a structural
choice about national integration — it accepted that some parties might have grievances
that could not be remedied in court, as the price of constitutional consolidation.

The provision's scope is deliberately broad. It covers not only direct claims under the
pre-constitutional instruments but also claims that are derivative of those instruments
— including claims that a provision of the Constitution relating to such an instrument
gives rise to a right or imposes an obligation. This prevents the jurisdictional bar
from being circumvented by the simple device of pointing to a constitutional article
rather than the underlying accession instrument as the immediate source of the right.

Article 363's bar on judicial interpretation reflects the framers' understanding that
courts examining these instruments would inevitably be drawn into assessing the fairness
of the original bargain — a question that had no satisfactory legal answer and every
prospect of politically destabilising consequences. The one outlet left open — an
advisory reference to the Supreme Court by the President under Article 143 — preserved
the possibility of obtaining authoritative legal guidance on difficult questions without
opening up adversarial litigation.

## Reading the provision

The provision's scope is deliberately framed to resist procedural evasion. A claimant
who cannot get into court by suing on a sanad directly cannot get into court by suing
on a constitutional provision "relating to" the sanad. This double closure was tested
almost immediately after the Constitution came into force, and courts consistently
upheld the breadth of the bar against attempts to route accession-related claims through
constitutional grounds.

Clause (1) contains the operative exclusion of jurisdiction. The formulation
"notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" is the strongest possible opening,
displacing even constitutional provisions that might otherwise confer jurisdiction —
including Article 32 (Supreme Court's jurisdiction to enforce fundamental rights) and
Article 226 (High Court's writ jurisdiction). The only constitutional provision not
displaced is Article 143, the advisory jurisdiction of the Supreme Court, which is
expressly preserved.

The instruments covered are listed expansively: "treaty, agreement, covenant, engagement,
sanad or other similar instrument." A sanad was a specific form of instrument used in
the princely-state context — a document conferred by the British Crown granting
recognition, rights, or titles to a ruler. The list in Article 363 covers every form
of pre-constitutional instrument through which a princely-state relationship with the
Dominion of India was constituted or formalised. "Other similar instrument" functions
as a residual catch-all.

The scope of the bar is further extended by the second limb of clause (1): disputes
"in respect of any right accruing under or any liability or obligation arising out of
any of the provisions of this Constitution relating to any such treaty, agreement,
covenant, engagement, sanad or other similar instrument." This means that if the
Constitution itself contains a provision that relates to a pre-constitutional instrument,
disputes about rights arising under those constitutional provisions are equally excluded
from judicial cognisance.

Clause (2) provides definitions of "Indian State" and "Ruler" for the purpose of the
article. "Indian State" covers any territory recognised before the Constitution's
commencement by the British Crown or the Dominion Government as a princely state.
"Ruler" is defined inclusively — it covers not only the original prince or chief but
any successor recognised as ruler before the Constitution's commencement. This ensures
that claims by successors to the original signatory of an accession instrument are
equally excluded from judicial cognisance.

The exception for Article 143 advisory references is significant. The President may
refer difficult questions about such instruments to the Supreme Court for an opinion.
This allows the government to obtain authoritative legal guidance on contested questions
without opening up adversarial litigation. The advisory reference is not a judgment
that creates enforceable rights; it is a constitutional tool for legal clarification
in the absence of justiciable dispute resolution.

## Key cases

The case law on Article 363 has developed a consistent purposive approach: the bar
is construed broadly to give effect to the constitutional decision to exclude
accession-era disputes from judicial cognisance, and procedural attempts to reframe
such disputes as constitutional claims are rejected.

**Maharaja of Cochin v State of Kerala (1959) SCR 1009** — The Supreme Court held that
Article 363 applies not only to direct claims under the pre-constitutional instruments
themselves but also to disputes about rights accruing under constitutional provisions
that relate to such instruments. A claimant could not circumvent the bar by pointing
to a constitutional provision as the immediate source of the right if the underlying
entitlement traced back to a pre-1950 accession or merger instrument. The judgment
established the broad, substance-over-form reading of Article 363's jurisdictional bar
that has governed subsequent cases.

**State of Rajasthan v Surendra Nahal (1992) 3 SCC 217** — The Supreme Court confirmed
that Article 363 is a complete bar and cannot be avoided by framing a dispute as arising
under the Constitution rather than directly under the pre-accession instrument. Courts
must examine the substance of the claim, and if it originates in a treaty or engagement
within Article 363's scope, jurisdiction is excluded regardless of how the pleadings
are framed. The ruling reinforced the principle that Article 363 must be read purposively
to give effect to the constitutional decision to keep accession-era disputes out of the
courts.

**Madhavrao Scindia v Union of India (1971) 1 SCC 85** — While primarily concerned with
Article 362's protection of Rulers' rights, the Court also addressed the relationship
between Article 363's bar and the scope of judicial review of executive orders that
purported to operate on pre-constitutional agreements. The judgment clarified that
Article 363 bars suits about the content of those agreements but does not bar review
of whether the executive has acted within its constitutional powers when purporting to
give effect to or modify those agreements. The executive cannot use the Article 363 bar
to insulate its own unconstitutional action from review.

**H H Maharajadhiraja Madhav Rao Jivaji Rao Scindia Bahadur v Union of India** — The
sequence of cases involving the Scindia family of the former Gwalior State illustrates
the interaction between Article 363's bar and the constitutional rights formerly
protected by Article 362. The family's legal challenges to the de-recognition orders
in 1969–71 navigated the Article 363 bar by characterising their claims not as arising
from the accession instrument itself, but from unconstitutional executive action that
purported to override constitutionally protected rights. The Court accepted this
characterisation — confirming that Article 363 bars disputes about the content of
accession instruments while not barring judicial review of executive action that violates
the Constitution in its own right.

**Nawab Udai Singh Bhati v Union of India (1969)** — An early case in which a former
ruler sought to enforce privy purse guarantees through the courts after certain payments
were withheld. The Court examined whether the claim arose directly out of the accession
instrument (barred by Article 363) or out of a constitutional provision relating to such
an instrument (also barred). The judgment illustrated the practical scope of the bar
and the difficulty of bringing any claim relating to the accession-era settlement within
the courts' cognisance.

**Manipur Administration v Bira Singh (1964)** — The Court considered whether certain
arrangements relating to the administration of Manipur — which had an unusual transitional
history between accession and full integration — fell within Article 363's scope. The
judgment demonstrated that the bar extends to the variety of administrative and
quasi-constitutional arrangements through which the integration of particular territories
was managed in the 1947–50 period.

## Current significance

Article 363 has limited contemporary practical significance. Most disputes that could
have arisen from the princely accession and merger instruments have been long settled
— either through administrative arrangement, through the Twenty-sixth Amendment's
abolition of the privy purse system, or simply through the passage of time and the
succession of generations. The provision continues to operate as a reminder of the
transitional constitutional architecture through which India achieved territorial
integration without subjecting the terms of that integration to ordinary judicial
contestation.

Residual property disputes, ceremonial rights claims, and succession questions that echo
the accession-era settlements occasionally raise Article 363 issues. Former ruling
families in several states have sought to litigate claims about palace properties,
ceremonial entitlements, or recognition rights that they trace to accession agreements
or merger conditions. Courts examining such claims must first determine whether the
claim's substance falls within the Article 363 bar — and the Maharaja of Cochin and
Surendra Nahal cases establish that the substance, not the form of the pleadings,
determines the question.

The relationship between Article 363 and the constitutional framework for tribal land
rights and governance in the Fifth and Sixth Schedules is an area of residual complexity.
Many tribal areas that came under the Fifth and Sixth Schedule framework were previously
princely-state territories governed by particular accession or merger conditions.
Questions about the interpretation of constitutional provisions relating to these areas
can potentially raise Article 363 issues if they trace back to pre-constitutional
accession arrangements. Courts have generally resolved such cases on the constitutional
provision directly at issue rather than reaching Article 363, but the bar remains a
potential limit on litigation in these areas.

The article also retains theoretical significance in debates about justiciability and
non-justiciability in Indian constitutional law. The general presumption in favour of
justiciability — that constitutional rights are enforceable in courts — makes Article
363 a notable exception. The provision's design illustrates that the framers were
willing to make deliberate non-justiciability choices for specific categories of disputes
that they judged were better handled through political and administrative processes
than through litigation. This approach to constitutional architecture — identifying
categories of politically sensitive arrangements that courts should not adjudicate —
continues to have resonance in debates about the proper scope of judicial review.
