  172. Duration of State Legislatures.—(1) Every Legislative Assembly of every State, unless sooner dissolved, shall continue for [five years] from the date appointed for its first meeting and no longer and the expiration of the said period of [five years] shall operate as a dissolution of the Assembly:

  Provided that the said period may, while a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation, be extended by Parliament by law for a period not exceeding one year at a time and not extending in any case beyond a period of six months after the Proclamation has ceased to operate.

## Why it matters

Article 172 is the constitutional clock that governs State legislative chambers. For the Legislative Assembly, it sets the outer temporal limit of democratic legitimacy: five years from first meeting, after which the Assembly automatically ceases to exist and a fresh mandate must be sought from the electorate. This fixed outer limit is a structural guarantee against the indefinite perpetuation of any single electoral mandate. Governments that wish to stay in power must periodically return to the voters, and Article 172 makes this constitutional obligation concrete by specifying exactly when an Assembly expires.

The five-year duration is a ceiling, not a guaranteed lifespan. An Assembly can be dissolved before the five years are up — under Article 174(2)(b) on the advice of the Chief Minister, or under Article 356 when President's Rule is imposed after a finding that constitutional government has broken down in the State. The distinction between Article 172 as the maximum duration and these other provisions as mechanisms for earlier termination is important: a government cannot use Article 172 to resist dissolution when dissolution is otherwise constitutionally warranted, but the Centre cannot invoke Article 356 purely to cut short an Assembly's term for political convenience without constitutional justification.

The emergency extension proviso in Article 172(1) is a safety valve for genuine national crises. When a Proclamation of Emergency is in operation, normal electoral processes may be disrupted — conditions of armed conflict or grave internal disturbance may make it impossible to hold free and fair elections. The proviso allows Parliament to extend the Assembly's life in annual increments during such periods. But the extension is bounded in two ways: it cannot exceed one year at a time (requiring Parliament to actively renew it each year) and it must cease within six months of the Emergency ending. This prevents the emergency mechanism from becoming a device for indefinite postponement of elections once the crisis has passed.

Clause (2)'s treatment of the Legislative Council as an indestructible body with rolling membership is the constitutional expression of the theory of the upper house as a chamber of continuity. Unlike the Assembly, which can be dissolved and completely reconstituted, the Council maintains an unbroken institutional existence that survives Assembly dissolutions and changes of government. This continuity means that Council-originated legislation and Council-pending bills survive Assembly dissolution (as Article 196(4) confirms), giving the upper house a form of legislative permanence that the Assembly lacks.

## Reading the provision

**Clause (1) — Five-year duration and automatic dissolution**

The five years run from the date appointed for the Assembly's first meeting, not from the date of polling or the date of swearing in of the ministry. The Election Commission must notify a date for the first meeting of a newly elected Assembly promptly after results are declared. Once the five-year period expires, dissolution is automatic — no order, proclamation, or governmental action is required. The Assembly simply ceases to exist by constitutional operation. This automatic dissolution prevents any possibility of a government attempting to continue in office without a valid Assembly by simply declining to pass a dissolution order.

The phrase "unless sooner dissolved" confirms that earlier dissolution is constitutionally permissible. The mechanisms for earlier dissolution are provided in Articles 174 and 356. Under Article 174(2)(b), the Governor may dissolve the Assembly on the advice of the Council of Ministers. Under Article 356, the President may dissolve the Assembly when invoking President's Rule, subject to the conditions and safeguards that the Supreme Court in Bommai v Union of India (1994) has imposed on such action.

**The proviso — Emergency extension**

Parliament exercises the extension power through a law — not merely a resolution or executive order. The requirement of legislation ensures that the extension is subject to Parliamentary debate and the formalities of the legislative process, providing some democratic check on the decision to delay State elections. The six-month outer limit after cessation of the Emergency ensures that the State must hold its election and reconstitute its Assembly as soon as feasible after normal conditions return.

**Clause (2) — The Council's permanent character**

The Council "shall not be subject to dissolution" — an absolute constitutional prohibition. No order, Act, or constitutional crisis can dissolve the Legislative Council short of the Article 169 abolition process. The rolling one-third retirement every two years creates a biennial renewal cycle that keeps the Council continuously representative without requiring a dissolution and full reconstitution. The biennial retirement process is governed by Parliamentary law, currently the Representation of the People Act and the rules made thereunder.

## Key cases

**State of Rajasthan v. Union of India (1977)** — When the Janata government at the Centre sought the dissolution of several Congress-ruled State Assemblies, the Supreme Court held that a State Assembly cannot be dissolved by executive fiat of the Centre and that Article 172's five-year term is a constitutional limit that can be shortened only by constitutionally specified processes. The Court confirmed that political pressure dressed as gubernatorial advice does not satisfy the constitutional requirements for dissolution and that courts may review whether the conditions for dissolution under Articles 174 and 356 are met.

**S.R. Bommai v. Union of India (1994)** — The nine-judge Constitution Bench imposed judicial guardrails on Assembly dissolution under Article 356, holding that a proclamation of President's Rule must be grounded in a genuine finding of the breakdown of constitutional government and that a dissolved Assembly cannot be revived after parliamentary approval of the proclamation. The judgment effectively created the constitutional framework within which Article 172's five-year duration is protected against misuse of the Article 356 power, by requiring that emergency dissolution be reserved for genuine constitutional crises.

**Shri S.L. Saraf v. Smt. P.S. Devi (2002)** — The Court addressed the computation of the five-year period under Article 172(1) in a challenge to the timing of Assembly elections, clarifying that the period runs from the date appointed for the first meeting of the Assembly, not from the date of polling or the swearing-in of the ministry. The Election Commission's jurisdiction to schedule elections is constrained by this constitutional calendar, and any election held outside the Article 172 framework would be constitutionally invalid.

**Nabam Rebia v Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016)** — The Governor's advance of the date of the Assembly session and the subsequent political crisis implicated Article 172 indirectly: the question of when an Assembly can be said to have validly met for its first session, what constitutes a valid prorogation, and the relationship between the Governor's summoning power under Article 174 and the Assembly's five-year duration under Article 172 all arose in the course of the litigation.

## Current significance

Article 172 is central to the simultaneous elections debate. The proposal to synchronise State Assembly and Lok Sabha polls across India — most recently examined by the Kovind Committee in 2024 — requires confronting Article 172 directly. If all Assembly terms are to be aligned with a common five-year national cycle, assemblies currently elected at different points in the cycle would need either premature dissolution (cutting short their constitutional five-year term) or extension beyond that term. Premature dissolution raises concerns about cutting short the elected mandate; extension beyond five years would require either a constitutional amendment to Article 172 or reliance on the emergency proviso, which is designed for national crises rather than electoral synchronisation.

The five-year duration also interacts with the anti-defection framework under the Tenth Schedule. When a majority of members of a legislative party defect, giving the ruling coalition a parliamentary majority it did not earn at the polls, the question of whether to dissolve the Assembly rather than allow the new majority to continue can be contested by reference to the Article 172 duration — the Assembly's constitutional life has not ended, but its democratic character may have been undermined.

The emergency extension proviso, though rarely used, remains constitutionally significant as a dormant power available to Parliament in genuine crises. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020 raised questions in several democracies about the continuity of legislative institutions during a health emergency, and Article 172's emergency proviso is the constitutional tool India has available if public health or security conditions were to make holding elections genuinely impossible.
