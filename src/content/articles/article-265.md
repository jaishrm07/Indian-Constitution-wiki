## Why it matters

Article 265 is one of the most litigated provisions in the Constitution. Its single sentence — "No tax shall be levied or collected except by authority of law" — embodies the rule of law in public finance: the state may not take money from persons unless Parliament or a State Legislature has specifically authorised it to do so. The article protects against executive overreach, delegated legislation that exceeds its parent statute, and retrospective or otherwise invalid tax demands. It is the constitutional anchor for the entire body of Indian tax jurisprudence.

The provision is distinct from — though complementary to — the Article 14 equality guarantee. While Article 14 tests whether a tax is discriminatory or arbitrary, Article 265 tests a prior question: whether any authority to levy the tax exists at all. A tax can pass the Article 14 arbitrariness test and still violate Article 265 if its statutory foundation is absent or defective. Conversely, a tax may have valid statutory authority and still be challenged under Article 14 for unequal application. In practice, many tax challenges are pleaded under both articles simultaneously.

The article also functions as the constitutional analogue, in the tax domain, to Article 300A's protection of property from executive deprivation without legal authority. Where Article 300A protects against deprivation of property without legislative sanction, Article 265 protects against extraction of money as tax without that same sanction. Both provisions reflect the constitutional commitment that the coercive financial power of the state can only be exercised on the basis of law made by a competent legislature.

Article 265's brevity is deceptive. The single sentence conceals a complex body of doctrine about what counts as a "tax" (as opposed to a fee or a regulatory charge), what qualifies as "authority of law" (as opposed to executive direction or delegated legislation exceeding its scope), and what remedies follow when collection has occurred without lawful authority. Courts have spent decades unpacking these questions, making Article 265 one of the most litigated constitutional provisions in Indian public law.

## Reading the provision

Two words carry most of the legal weight: "levied" and "collected." Courts have consistently held that both stages are independently subject to the requirement of lawful authority. A tax may be validly levied — that is, the liability to pay is created by a valid statute — but collected by a process or officer that the statute does not authorise. In that case, the collection violates Article 265 even if the levy is valid. Conversely, an executive notification that purports to create a new tax liability without statutory backing violates Article 265 at the levy stage itself.

"Authority of law" means a valid enacted statute passed by a competent legislature under the appropriate entry in the Seventh Schedule. An executive order, administrative circular, or subordinate legislation issued beyond the scope of its enabling Act does not constitute "authority of law" for this purpose. The legislative foundation must be traceable to a valid exercise of legislative power: the charging section that creates the liability, the assessment mechanism that quantifies it, and the collection machinery that enforces payment must all rest on statutory ground. If any element lacks that foundation, Article 265 is violated.

A threshold question that precedes Article 265's operation is whether a particular exaction is a "tax" at all. Courts apply the burden/benefit test: a compulsory exaction levied for general public revenue with no specific corresponding benefit to the payer is a tax; a levy imposed for a specific service rendered to the payer, calibrated to the cost of that service, is a fee. Fees need not satisfy Article 265's strict legislative authority requirement in the same way as taxes. The line between tax and fee is contested in cases involving regulatory levies, cesses, and development charges, and the characterisation determines which Seventh Schedule entry must authorise the levy.

When a tax is collected without lawful authority — because the statute was later held invalid, or the collection exceeded the statute's terms — Article 265 implies a right to refund. However, the Supreme Court has complicated this through the doctrine of unjust enrichment: where the taxpayer has passed on the burden of the tax to a downstream purchaser, requiring the government to refund the tax to the taxpayer would result in a windfall at the expense of the persons who actually bore the economic burden. Statutes such as the Central Excise Act and the Customs Act codify this doctrine, requiring taxpayers seeking refunds to prove they have not passed on the incidence.

## Key cases

**Govind Saran Ganga Saran v. Commissioner of Sales Tax AIR 1985 SC 1041** — The Supreme Court held that a tax collected under a void or ultra vires provision violates Article 265 and must be refunded. The State cannot retain money collected without legal authority even if the taxpayer paid without protest, subject to the evolving doctrine of unjust enrichment. This case crystallised the rule that Article 265 creates a positive constitutional obligation to restore funds collected without authority.

**Jagannath Baksh Singh v. State of Uttar Pradesh AIR 1962 SC 1563** — The Court held that "authority of law" in Article 265 means a valid, enforceable enacted law — not an executive order or administrative circular. A notification issued beyond the enabling Act's delegation does not satisfy the constitutional requirement, and any collection under such a notification is recoverable as a constitutional wrong.

**B.N. Tiwari v. Union of India AIR 1966 SC 1780** — An important early decision confirming that Article 265 is a positive prohibition and not merely a procedural requirement. The Court held that where a legislative provision authorising a levy was defective or exceeded the legislature's competence under the Seventh Schedule, every collection made under it violated Article 265, entitling affected taxpayers to repayment without needing to establish individual prejudice.

**Orient Paper Mills v. Union of India AIR 1978 SC 1009** — The Court confirmed that excessive delegation in a taxing statute — where Parliament gives the executive uncanalised power to fix the rate, base, and exemptions of a tax — violates Article 265 read with the principle against excessive delegation, because the effective levy then lacks proper legislative authority. Essential legislative functions in taxation must be retained by the legislature.

**Bhatnagars & General Mills v. Union of India AIR 1957 SC 478** — An early case establishing that the "authority of law" requirement extends to both the substantive liability to pay tax and the machinery for its assessment and collection. Any procedural gap in the statutory machinery that results in collection without proper authority also comes within the Article 265 guarantee.

## Current significance

Article 265 is regularly invoked in challenges to retroactive tax demands, fee-versus-tax disputes, and levies under delegated instruments that exceed their parent statutes. GST's multi-tier structure has produced a fresh wave of litigation testing whether certain State or Union imposts are authorised by the CGST/SGST Acts or amount to levies without lawful authority. The Supreme Court's evolving jurisprudence on unjust enrichment as a limit on tax-refund claims flows directly from Article 265's guarantee that collection without authority is unlawful, tempered by equitable principles about who ultimately bore the tax burden.

Service tax and GST classification disputes — whether a particular activity constitutes a taxable supply within the relevant charging section — continue to be litigated under the Article 265 framework. The expansion of GST's reach and the ongoing identification of new taxable activities means Article 265 challenges remain a live and growing body of litigation, particularly where the Revenue's interpretation of the charging provision is contested as exceeding the statute's terms.

The cess proliferation of the last two decades — education cess, health and education cess, Swachh Bharat cess, infrastructure cess — has also generated Article 265 questions about whether the statutory authority for each cess clearly authorises the specific levy being imposed, and whether the revenue collected is being applied for the stated purpose that formed part of the statutory justification. As each new cess is introduced through Finance Acts, the adequacy of its enabling language is tested against Article 265's requirement that authority to levy must be clear, positive, and traceable to a valid legislative act.
