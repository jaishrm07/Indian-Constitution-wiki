## Why it matters

Though deleted, Article 31 is the originating node of the most consequential sequence of constitutional amendments and judicial decisions in Indian history. The conflict between the right to property and the state's land reform agenda directly produced the doctrine of basic structure, the Ninth Schedule, Article 31C, and the ultimate removal of property from the list of fundamental rights. Understanding Article 31's history is essential for understanding Articles 31A, 31B, 31C, 300A, and the basic structure doctrine itself.

## Original text and structure

In its original form, Article 31 had two clauses. Clause (1) stated that no person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law. Clause (2) required that any acquisition or requisitioning of property for public purposes must be authorised by law which provides for compensation and either fixes the amount or specifies the principles on which the amount is to be determined. A series of constitutional amendments progressively modified the compensation standard, ultimately replacing "compensation" with "amount" to insulate Parliament's determinations from judicial review.

## The land reform crisis

After Independence, state governments enacted zamindari abolition and land ceiling laws to break up large landholdings and redistribute land to cultivators. Large landowners challenged these laws under Articles 14, 19(1)(f), and 31, arguing that inadequate compensation and arbitrary acquisitions violated their fundamental rights. Early decisions — including State of West Bengal v. Bela Banerjee (1954) — held that the compensation had to be a just equivalent of what was taken, which would have effectively blocked redistributive land reform.

## Constitutional amendments and judicial response

The First Amendment (1951) inserted Articles 31A and 31B to protect land reform laws from challenge under Articles 14, 19, and 31, and created the Ninth Schedule. The Fourth Amendment (1955) modified the compensation standard and attempted to define the permissible scope of "restriction" on property rights. The Seventeenth Amendment (1964) expanded the Ninth Schedule protection. In Golak Nath v. State of Punjab (1967), an eleven-judge bench held that Parliament could not amend fundamental rights at all — a ruling that created a constitutional deadlock since it threatened the entire land reform programme.

The Twenty-Fourth Amendment (1971) restored Parliament's power to amend any part of the Constitution, including fundamental rights, in response to Golak Nath. The Twenty-Fifth Amendment (1971) changed "compensation" to "amount" and inserted Article 31C to shield laws giving effect to certain directive principles. These amendments were challenged in Kesavananda Bharati.

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)** — A thirteen-judge bench held that Parliament may amend any provision of the Constitution including fundamental rights, but cannot destroy its basic structure. Property rights and the property-reform amendments were the immediate catalyst for this ruling. While the Court upheld the Twenty-Fourth and Twenty-Fifth Amendments in substance, it imposed the basic structure limit that has shaped Indian constitutional law ever since.

**R.C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970)** — The Bank Nationalisation case, in which the Supreme Court struck down the Bank Nationalisation Ordinance. The Court held that the "amount" offered was illusory and that courts retained power to review whether the statutory formula for computing amounts was constitutionally adequate. This ruling led directly to the Twenty-Fifth Amendment's attempt to foreclose judicial review.

## Deletion by the Forty-Fourth Amendment

After the Emergency, the Janata Party government enacted the Forty-Fourth Amendment (1978), which deleted Article 19(1)(f) — which had guaranteed the right to acquire, hold, and dispose of property — and Article 31 itself. The right to property was relocated to Article 300A, which provides that no person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law but sits outside Part III, meaning it is not a fundamental right and cannot be enforced through Article 32 in the Supreme Court directly.

## Current significance

Although Article 31 is deleted, its history is essential for understanding the relationship between property, democracy, and constitutionalism in India. Disputes about compulsory acquisition now arise under the Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act 2013 and Article 300A. Courts continue to cite the pre-deletion case law — particularly the "just equivalent" standard — as background doctrine when assessing the adequacy of compensation in acquisition proceedings, and debates about natural resources, spectrum allocation, and urban land continue to echo the original property-rights tensions that drove Article 31's history.
