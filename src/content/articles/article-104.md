## Why it matters

Article 104 is the constitutional enforcement mechanism that gives effect to the oath requirement in Article 99 and the disqualification regime in Articles 102–103. Without a penalty, these provisions would be unenforceable against a person who simply turned up and participated in Parliament despite lacking the legal right to do so. The article ensures that sitting and voting are not merely symbolic acts but carry personal legal consequences for a person who engages in them without entitlement.

The provision operates across three distinct scenarios: participation before the oath is taken, participation while knowingly unqualified, and participation while knowingly disqualified. Each scenario raises different legal questions, and the shared "knowledge" element is significant — Article 104 is not a strict-liability provision, which creates a gap that has been exploited in practice by members who claim they did not yet "know" of their disqualification.

## Reading the provision

**The oath precondition:** A newly elected member who attends and votes without having taken the oath of affirmation prescribed by Article 99 incurs the Rs 500 penalty for every day of such participation. The oath is a mandatory constitutional formality that cannot be waived — it is the legal moment at which the member's right to participate commences.

**Knowing disqualification:** A person who knows they do not meet the eligibility requirements of Article 84 (age, citizenship, name on electoral rolls) but nevertheless sits or votes is liable. The word "knows" has been interpreted to require actual knowledge of the disqualifying fact, not merely constructive knowledge.

**The knowledge gap in practice:** Members facing pending disqualification proceedings frequently continue to sit and vote, arguing they are not yet formally disqualified and therefore do not "know" themselves to be disqualified within the meaning of this Article. Courts have not fully resolved at what point contested disqualification proceedings crystallise into the "knowledge" Article 104 requires.

**Civil recovery — not criminal liability:** The penalty is recoverable as a debt due to the Union through ordinary civil proceedings. There is no criminal exposure under Article 104 itself (though separate corruption offences may arise in some circumstances). This makes enforcement a matter of civil litigation rather than contempt or prosecution.

## Key cases

**Raja Ram Pal v. Speaker, Lok Sabha (2007):** The Supreme Court's examination of parliamentary membership and the relationship between disqualification, expulsion, and membership rights confirmed that sitting and voting while disqualified is a constitutionally recognised wrong. While the case principally concerned expulsion under parliamentary privilege, the Court's analysis acknowledged Article 104 as a separate enforcement mechanism targeted at participation by disqualified persons, distinct from the House's contempt and expulsion powers.

**Lily Thomas v. Union of India (2013):** The Court's ruling that convicted members are automatically disqualified under Section 8 of the Representation of the People Act, without any appeal-pending buffer, has sharpened Article 104's practical application — a member who continues to sit after conviction now "knows" of their disqualification from the moment conviction is recorded, triggering the Article 104 penalty from that date.

**Proceedings validity and Article 122:** Courts have generally held that Article 104 operates prospectively to deter and penalise individual conduct, but does not of itself invalidate proceedings in which a disqualified person participated — Article 100(2)'s saving clause protects the validity of completed proceedings.

## Current significance

The Rs 500-per-day penalty was set in 1950 and has never been enhanced by Parliament, despite successive salary and allowances legislation substantially increasing members' remuneration. A member's daily entitlements now run to tens of thousands of rupees in salary, allowances, and facilities; the Article 104 penalty remains Rs 500 — a sum so nominal it functions as no deterrent at all. Parliament's failure to revise this figure is itself constitutionally notable, since Parliament has the power under Article 104 (by implication) and Article 106 to set adequate enforcement terms.

The more significant practical dimension of Article 104 lies in its interaction with the disqualification regime under Articles 102–103 and the Representation of the People Act. Members facing Tenth Schedule disqualification proceedings routinely continue to sit and vote for months or years while the Speaker delays a decision, on the basis that no formal disqualification has yet been declared. This creates a structural gap — the constitutional design assumes that disqualification proceedings will be resolved promptly, and Article 104 is designed to penalise knowing participation by the disqualified; but when proceedings are deliberately delayed, the "knowledge" trigger may never formally arise even when the underlying facts of disqualification are clear.
