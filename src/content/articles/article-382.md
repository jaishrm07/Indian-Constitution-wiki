## What this Article did

Article 382 was a transitional provision in the original Constitution (1950) that addressed the provisional Legislatures for States in Part A of the First Schedule. It has been omitted.

The Part A States in the original First Schedule were the former Provinces of British India (as distinct from the Part B States which were former princely states). These included States like Bombay, Madras, West Bengal, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and others. They had legislative assemblies under the Government of India Act, 1935 that were reconstituted as provincial assemblies after independence.

Article 382 provided for the continuation of these existing State Legislatures as provisional Legislatures under the new Constitution, pending fresh elections under the adult suffrage provisions of the new Constitution. The existing members of provincial legislatures — who had been elected in 1946 under restricted franchise — continued to serve in the provisional State Legislatures. These provisional legislatures enacted important State-level legislation during 1950-1952.

## Why it matters

The provisional State Legislatures for Part A States were the bedrock of democratic governance at the State level during the transitional period. They enacted land reform legislation — including zamindari abolition laws — that transformed the agrarian structure of States like Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and Madras. These laws, passed by the provisional assemblies under Article 382's transitional framework, were among the most significant pieces of social legislation in post-independence India and were later challenged before the Supreme Court, resulting in key early constitutional jurisprudence on property rights and legislative competence.

## Why it was omitted

Article 382 was omitted by the Constitution (Seventh Amendment) Act, 1956. By that time, the States Reorganisation Act, 1956 had reorganised all States, and new State Legislatures had been constituted after elections held following the first general elections of 1951-52. The provisional arrangements for the Part A States were entirely spent.

## Historical context

The provisional State Legislatures for Part A States were essential during the transition period. State governments needed functioning Legislatures to pass budgets, enact laws on State subjects, and exercise the constitutional powers vested in State Legislatures. The 1946-elected provincial assemblies served this function until the first State elections were held alongside the first general elections in 1951-52.

The original First Schedule's division of States into Parts A, B, and C was itself a transitional arrangement reflecting the different legal status of former provinces, former princely states, and centrally administered territories. The entire structure was reorganised by the Seventh Amendment following the States Reorganisation Act, 1956.

## Key cases

No significant constitutional challenge to the validity of legislation passed by the provisional State Legislatures is recorded. The transition from provisional to elected State Legislatures was managed smoothly.

## Current significance

Article 382 is omitted and has no operational significance. Its historical importance lies in establishing the constitutional basis for State-level governance during the critical transition period of 1950-1952, when elected Legislatures under adult franchise had not yet been constituted.
