## Why this institution matters

Parliament is not only where laws and constitutional amendments pass. It is also where institutional design gets debated, where committee reports surface, and where the paper trail for structural reform begins. The constituent power under Article 368 makes Parliament the only body that can alter the Constitution's text — but that power has been limited by the basic structure doctrine since Kesavananda Bharati, creating a permanent tension between democratic legislative supremacy and judicially maintained constitutional limits. Legislative privilege, parliamentary procedure, and the internal rules governing both Houses shape what reforms are even politically possible at any given moment. Understanding whether a constitutional controversy can be resolved by statute, requires an amendment, or is beyond Parliament's reach entirely requires knowing how Parliament's own design constrains what it can do.

## How to monitor this institution

Track bills, committee reports, and parliamentary questions at sansad.in, which is the official digital interface for both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha activity. The Parliament Digital Library (eparlib.nic.in) has historical debates and committee reports going back to the Constituent Assembly, and is essential for tracing amendment histories. PRS Legislative Research (prsindia.org) provides structured bill analysis, including clause-by-clause breakdowns and comparative tables, for pending legislation. For constitutional amendment bills specifically, the Ministry of Law and Justice publishes the official statement of objects and reasons. Joint committee reports and dissent notes are often more revealing of genuine constitutional concerns than the bill text itself.

## Constitutional design

Articles 79-122 define Parliament's composition (Lok Sabha, Rajya Sabha, and the President), powers, procedure, and privileges. The Union List (Seventh Schedule) gives Parliament exclusive legislative authority over 97 subjects; the Concurrent List allows shared authority with states over 52 subjects, with parliamentary law prevailing in cases of repugnancy under Article 254. Article 368 gives Parliament the constituent power to amend the Constitution, but the basic structure doctrine from Kesavananda Bharati places substantive limits on that power that no parliamentary majority can override. Articles 105 and 194 protect parliamentary privilege, including freedom of speech in the legislature and immunity from court proceedings for anything said or done in Parliament — a doctrine whose interaction with anti-bribery law was sharpened by the Supreme Court's Sita Soren ruling in 2024. Article 110 defines Money Bills and creates the specific route by which financial legislation bypasses the Rajya Sabha, a mechanism that has generated constitutional controversy when broadly-read Bills are certified as Money Bills.

## Live issues

Parliament is considering the 129th Constitution Amendment Bill (One Nation One Election), which was referred to a Joint Parliamentary Committee after introduction — the Bill's constitutionality depends partly on whether simultaneous election provisions are consistent with the basic structure and with state legislative autonomy (see [one nation one election](/current-affairs/one-nation-one-election)). Article 368's limits under the basic structure doctrine remain a live doctrinal question whenever major structural reforms are attempted (see [limits on constitutional amendment](/current-affairs/limits-on-constitutional-amendment)). Legislative privilege after the Sita Soren ruling is now being worked out in lower courts and in Parliament's own practice, particularly regarding when members can be prosecuted for conduct related to their legislative activity. Parliamentary procedure over Money Bill certification also continues to attract constitutional scrutiny following the use of that route for the Aadhaar Act and the Finance Acts that created the electoral bonds scheme.
