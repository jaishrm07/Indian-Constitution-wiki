## Why this institution matters

The Supreme Court is where much of the Constitution's modern meaning is actually worked out. Rights, federalism, amendment limits, and institutional boundaries are all shaped through its judgments. Kesavananda Bharati established the basic structure doctrine that now limits Parliament's amending power, and that doctrine has no source other than the Court's own reasoning across thirteen opinions. SR Bommai made President's Rule justiciable and transformed how federal emergencies are managed. Puttaswamy elevated privacy to a fundamental right, producing downstream changes in how surveillance law and data policy are assessed constitutionally. Without tracking Supreme Court doctrine, any account of what the Indian Constitution actually requires in practice is incomplete.

## How to monitor this institution

Track the Court's daily cause list, judgment uploads, and collegium resolutions at sci.gov.in. The eSCR portal (scr.sci.gov.in) is the authoritative source for reported judgments and should be the first check when verifying the text of any ruling. Supreme Court Observer (supremecourt.observer) provides structured tracking of constitutional benches and pending five-judge matters. For collegium resolutions and appointment transparency, the Court's own website publishes resolutions when they are adopted. Live Law and Bar and Bench provide same-day coverage of significant hearings and orders.

## Constitutional design

Articles 124-147 establish the Court's structure, composition, and jurisdictions — original (Article 131), appellate (Articles 132-136), advisory (Article 143), and writ (Article 32). Article 145 gives the Court authority to make its own procedural rules, subject to the President's approval, which gives the institution significant autonomy over how it manages its docket. The Court's power to issue writs for the enforcement of fundamental rights under Article 32 is itself a fundamental right, making the Court constitutionally indispensable to Part III's operation rather than merely a convenient remedy. Original jurisdiction under Article 131 places Centre-state and inter-state disputes directly before the Court without requiring a lower-court journey. Article 141 makes every declaration of law by the Supreme Court binding on all courts throughout India, giving its constitutional rulings the force of a national constitutional settlement. L. Chandra Kumar (1997) held that the power of judicial review under Articles 226 and 227 is itself part of the basic structure, meaning Parliament cannot by legislation oust High Court and Supreme Court jurisdiction to review administrative tribunal decisions — the Court's supervisory role over inferior courts and tribunals is constitutionally entrenched, not merely statutory.

## Live issues

The Court is actively managing judicial appointments under the collegium system, which operates without a statutory or constitutional text squarely authorising it — the system emerged from the Second Judges Case and was reaffirmed after the NJAC judgment struck down the 99th Amendment (see [judicial appointments and collegium](/current-affairs/judicial-appointments-and-collegium)). The constitutional challenge to the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023 is pending before the Court (see [election commission appointments and independence](/current-affairs/election-commission-appointments-and-independence)). Governor-state disputes continue to generate constitutional litigation as state governments seek mandamus against Governors withholding assent or refusing to summon sessions (see [governor state standoffs](/current-affairs/governor-state-standoffs) and [governor assent and bill silence](/current-affairs/governor-assent-and-bill-silence)). The Court's Article 142 jurisdiction — its power to do complete justice in any cause or matter pending before it — has been exercised in significant federal-structure disputes: the Government of NCT of Delhi cases (2018 and 2023) used Article 142 directions to settle the allocation of services powers between the elected Delhi government and the Centre, illustrating both the reach and the limits of the provision as a gap-filling tool in constitutional adjudication. The institutional question of how much the Court should self-disclose about collegium deliberations — and whether the RTI Act applies — remains unresolved.
