## Why this institution matters

State legislatures are where political conflict gets translated into constitutional procedure. That is why so many live constitutional disputes return to floor tests, sessions, bills, and legislative majority. The Assembly is the primary democratic body at the state level, yet its functioning can be interrupted — by a Governor who withholds a summons, by a Speaker who delays a disqualification petition, or by a President's Rule proclamation that suspends it altogether. Each of those interruptions has a constitutional basis, and each has been the subject of litigation that clarifies how much disruption the Constitution tolerates.

## How to use this page

Use this institution page when a controversy is really about how a state legislature is allowed to function and who may interrupt that process. It connects to anti-defection disputes, Governor conduct cases, and the design of the Tenth Schedule as applied in state assemblies.

## Constitutional design

Articles 168-212 establish state legislative assemblies and, in six states, bicameral legislatures with a Legislative Council. Article 172 prescribes a five-year term for the Legislative Assembly, subject to dissolution under Article 174(2)(b). Article 174 governs summoning and prorogation: the Governor summons the Assembly on the advice of the Council of Ministers, but the question of whether the Governor has independent discretion to refuse or delay a summons is one of the most contested points in contemporary state constitutional law. The Tenth Schedule — the anti-defection law introduced by the 52nd Amendment — applies equally to state legislatures, with the Speaker of the Assembly adjudicating disqualification petitions. The Supreme Court confirmed in [Kihoto Hollohan](/cases/kihoto-hollohan) that Speakers' decisions under the Tenth Schedule are subject to judicial review, but only after a final order — not at an interim stage. This limitation has allowed Speakers to sit on petitions for extended periods without disqualifying members, effectively frustrating the law's purpose. Articles 200 and 201 govern what happens after a state legislature passes a bill: the Governor may assent, withhold assent, return it for reconsideration, or reserve it for Presidential consideration — each option now heavily litigated.

## Live issues

State legislatures have been central to anti-defection disputes that produced successive Supreme Court interventions in Maharashtra, Manipur, Arunachal Pradesh, and other states (see [anti-defection and floor tests](/current-affairs/anti-defection-and-floor-tests)). In [Nabam Rebia](/cases/nabam-rebia), the Court held that a Speaker facing a notice of removal cannot adjudicate disqualification petitions, creating a constitutional protection against Speaker-driven defections. In [Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil](/cases/shrimanth-balasaheb-patil), the Court dealt with the Speaker's discretion in disqualification proceedings in Karnataka. The question of Governor-imposed delay in summoning sessions — an indirect mechanism to prevent floor tests — has generated litigation in multiple states (see [governor state standoffs](/current-affairs/governor-state-standoffs)). Women's reservation under the 106th Amendment (Article 330A and 332A) is contingent on a post-census delimitation exercise before it applies to state assemblies, deferring its implementation.

## How to monitor this institution

State legislature official websites and Vidhan Sabha records are the primary source for bills, session calendars, and business lists. Anti-defection petition filings before Speakers are not always published proactively — petitions must often be tracked through media reports, High Court interim orders, or party communications. PRS India tracks state-level legislation and provides bill summaries. Live Law and Bar and Bench monitor Speaker-related constitutional litigation and same-day coverage of significant Assembly proceedings that generate legal controversy.
