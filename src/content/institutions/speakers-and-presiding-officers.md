## Why this institution matters

When governments are fragile, procedural control can become constitutional power. Speakers and presiding officers often sit at that exact pressure point. The Tenth Schedule placed the Speaker in the unusual position of being a quasi-judicial authority over member disqualifications — a role that Kihoto Hollohan confirmed is adjudicatory in character but subject to judicial review only at the final decision stage, not during the process. This creates a window in which a Speaker aligned with the ruling coalition can delay disqualification proceedings long enough to let a government survive a floor test. The Supreme Court has repeatedly imposed timelines on Speakers through rulings like Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil (2019) and Keisham Meghachandra Singh (2020), but those directions are difficult to enforce when the Speaker has institutional control over the assembly's agenda. The structural conflict of interest — where the Speaker was elected on a party ticket and is adjudicating the defection of members against that same party — has never been resolved by constitutional amendment.

## How to monitor this institution

Track disqualification petitions and presiding-officer notices through official Lok Sabha and state legislative assembly websites, which publish notices to members and lists of pending petitions when proceedings are formally initiated. Live Law and Bar and Bench monitor mandamus petitions filed against Speakers for anti-defection delays and report on Supreme Court orders fixing timelines. The Lok Sabha Secretariat and state assembly secretariats publish bulletins and rulings on points of order, which document procedural interventions with constitutional implications. For state assembly floor tests and session-summoning disputes, the interaction between the Speaker's role and the Governor's role creates overlapping controversies that are best tracked through a combination of official gazette notifications and litigation coverage. The Election Commission's disqualification-related orders are separate from the Speaker's anti-defection role and are tracked through eci.gov.in.

## Constitutional design

Articles 93-94 (Lok Sabha Speaker and Deputy Speaker) and 178-179 (State Assembly Speaker and Deputy Speaker) create the office of Speaker, specify the manner of election and removal, and establish the Speaker's authority over legislative procedure. The Tenth Schedule, inserted by the 52nd Amendment in 1985, added the anti-defection adjudicatory role on top of the Speaker's procedural functions, creating a single officer who both runs the House and decides the fate of members accused of defying their party. Kihoto Hollohan upheld the Tenth Schedule's validity but struck down Paragraph 6, which had attempted to exclude judicial review entirely — the result is that Speaker decisions are subject to judicial review once final, but the Court will not intervene while proceedings are ongoing. The Speaker's power to disqualify is triggered by a petition from another member of the same House, not by the party directly, which creates procedural questions about who has standing and when the clock starts. Articles 122 and 212 bar courts from inquiring into parliamentary and state legislative proceedings, a protection that interacts uncomfortably with the judicial-review right recognised in Kihoto Hollohan for Tenth Schedule decisions.

## Live issues

Speaker impartiality in anti-defection proceedings remains structurally contested, with the Supreme Court having noted in Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil (2020) that an independent tribunal would be constitutionally preferable — but Parliament has not amended the Tenth Schedule to create one (see [anti-defection and floor tests](/current-affairs/anti-defection-and-floor-tests)). Sita Soren v. Union of India (2024) addressed the intersection of legislative privilege and the anti-defection regime: a seven-judge bench held unanimously that a member of Parliament or a state legislature cannot claim immunity under Article 105(2) or Article 194(2) for accepting a bribe to vote or speak in a particular way, overruling the earlier P.V. Narasimha Rao position — the ruling directly affects how presiding officers and courts should approach privilege claims raised in the context of Tenth Schedule proceedings and bribery allegations against members. Enforcement of the Court's timeline directions against Speakers who delay disqualification decisions is an ongoing problem: contempt proceedings against a Speaker raise separation-of-powers issues that courts have been reluctant to pursue. The relationship between a Speaker's anti-defection ruling and a Governor's independent assessment of majority support for a government has produced conflicting constitutional pressures in multiple recent state crises. The question of whether a Speaker who is themselves subject to a disqualification motion can continue to preside over the House and adjudicate other members' disqualifications is an unresolved structural problem that Nabam Rebia partially addressed for the Governor-Speaker interaction but did not fully settle.
