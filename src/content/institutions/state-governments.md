## Why this institution matters

State governments are where federalism becomes concrete. Their conflicts with governors, Parliament, or the Union executive often define the real constitutional stakes of an issue. The elected Council of Ministers in each state holds democratic legitimacy derived from the Assembly, yet it operates inside a constitutional structure that gives the Governor a formal role in every major executive act — from summoning the legislature to granting assent to bills passed by it. When those two layers come into friction, what looks like an administrative dispute reveals the underlying design tension of Part VI.

## How to use this page

Use this institution page when a constitutional issue turns on state autonomy, executive power, or the interaction between state governance and the Union. It connects directly to disputes over Governor conduct, legislative assent, floor tests, and federal legislative competence.

## Constitutional design

Part VI (Articles 152-237) establishes state governments mirroring the Union structure — Governor, Council of Ministers, and State Legislature. Article 154 vests executive power of the state in the Governor, but Article 163 requires the Governor to act on the advice of the Council of Ministers in virtually all matters except those where the Constitution expressly requires independent judgment. That exception is narrow but has been the source of recurring dispute. Articles 246 and Schedule VII determine the boundaries of state legislative competence: states have exclusive authority over 61 State List subjects and shared authority over 52 Concurrent List subjects, with Union law prevailing in cases of repugnancy under Article 254. The residuary power under Article 248 lies with Parliament, not the states — a structural asymmetry that distinguishes Indian federalism from fully decentralised models. Article 200 requires the Governor to either assent to, withhold assent from, or reserve a state bill for Presidential consideration, but the Constitution sets no time limit for any of these actions, creating the gap that Governor-related disputes now inhabit.

## Live issues

State governments have been in recurring constitutional conflict with Governors over assent to bills, summoning of assemblies, and floor tests. The Supreme Court's directions in the Tamil Nadu and Telangana cases have pushed back on indefinite withholding of assent, but the constitutional question of whether — and within what time — a Governor must act remains incompletely settled (see [governor state standoffs](/current-affairs/governor-state-standoffs) and [governor assent and bill silence](/current-affairs/governor-assent-and-bill-silence)). The One Nation One Election reform, if enacted, would directly curtail state government tenure by synchronising state and Union electoral cycles, raising questions about whether curtailing a validly elected state legislature's term requires its own consent (see [one nation one election](/current-affairs/one-nation-one-election)). Reservation policy is another live front: state governments that legislate sub-classification within SC/ST quotas now do so against the framework of the seven-judge bench ruling in Punnaiah, and the boundary between state competence and Union oversight on reservation policy remains contested (see [reservation and sub-classification](/current-affairs/reservation-and-sub-classification)).

## How to monitor this institution

Track state government Gazette notifications, Chief Minister's office press releases, and state legislature portals. PRS India monitors key state bills and tracks legislative activity across states. Raj Bhavan websites — where maintained — record official Governor communications and assent decisions. For litigation involving state government constitutional disputes, the Supreme Court's daily cause list and e-SCR are the primary sources. High Court cause lists cover state-level constitutional petitions filed before the relevant High Court.
