## Why this institution matters

Governor controversies often reveal the gap between constitutional form and constitutional practice. They are where federalism, discretion, and democratic legitimacy collide most visibly. The Governor is constitutionally a nominal executive head — Article 163 requires aid and advice from the Council of Ministers for virtually all functions — but the narrow residual space for personal discretion becomes enormously contested in hung assemblies and coalition crises. SR Bommai transformed how Article 356 emergency power is used, making floor tests the constitutional standard for testing majority rather than the Governor's personal assessment. Nabam Rebia added further doctrine limiting the Governor's power to summon sessions in ways that circumvent an elected government. When a Governor refuses assent, delays summoning the legislature, or invites a minority party to form the government, the constitutional design's reliance on convention and good faith becomes starkly exposed.

## How to monitor this institution

Track Raj Bhavan press releases for formal communications about assembly sessions, assent decisions, and appointments — these are often the first official signal of a brewing dispute. State assembly summoning orders and prorogation notifications appear in each state's official Gazette and on state assembly secretariat websites. The Supreme Court's registry lists governor-related mandamus petitions, and Live Law and Bar and Bench report on hearings in real time. PRS Legislative Research documents individual state-level governor controversies, providing structured timelines useful for comparing how disputes have developed across different states. When assent disputes arise, the Law Ministry's opinion letters (sometimes disclosed in RTI proceedings) provide insight into the Centre's constitutional position.

## Constitutional design

Articles 153-162 establish the Governor as the constitutional head of each State, appointed by the President and holding office during the President's pleasure — a phrase that makes removal a Centre prerogative with no fixed term protection. Article 163 provides for a Council of Ministers to aid and advise the Governor, with a narrow and contested residual space for personal discretion on matters where the Constitution expressly requires the Governor to act in personal judgment. Articles 174 (summoning, prorogation, and dissolution of the legislature) and 200-201 (assent to bills, reservation for Presidential consideration, and return of bills) are the most litigated provisions because they sit precisely at the intersection of Governor formality and political discretion. Article 356 allows the President to assume state functions when the Governor certifies that constitutional governance has broken down — a provision SR Bommai made justiciable and placed under parliamentary oversight. Article 200 does not give the Governor an indefinite pocket veto, and the Supreme Court's 2025 Tamil Nadu ruling clarified the constitutional obligations that apply once the Council of Ministers has re-presented a bill.

## Live issues

Governors in several opposition-ruled states have been withholding assent on bills for extended periods, a practice the Supreme Court has now ruled unconstitutional in the Tamil Nadu governor assent case (State of Tamil Nadu v. Governor of Tamil Nadu, 2025), which established that a Governor must either assent or reserve for Presidential consideration within a constitutionally implied reasonable time and cannot indefinitely withhold — deemed assent applies once a bill is re-presented after return (see [governor assent and bill silence](/current-affairs/governor-assent-and-bill-silence)). Nabam Rebia v. Deputy Speaker, Arunachal Pradesh (2016) established that a Governor cannot summon the legislature for a purpose — such as removing the Speaker — that effectively circumvents the will of the elected government and the speaker's own procedural protection; the ruling limits the Governor's Article 174 power to summon sessions in ways that would override the floor of the House (see [governor state standoffs](/current-affairs/governor-state-standoffs)). Multiple governor-state standoffs over session summoning and policy appointments remain active in different states. The Article 356 question — whether a Governor's report triggering President's Rule can itself be legally challenged — remains partially open after SR Bommai, with scope for further judicial development as federalism disputes intensify. The constitutional basis for removing a Governor before the end of a term (or displacing one immediately after a government changes at the Centre) also continues to generate litigation and political controversy.
