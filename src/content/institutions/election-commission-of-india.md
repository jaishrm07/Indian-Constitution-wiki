## Why this institution matters

The Election Commission is the primary institutional source for the operational side of constitutional democracy, especially where election calendars, procedure, and notifications matter. Its decisions on election schedules, model code enforcement, and candidate disqualifications directly determine who competes and when — making it a constitutional actor whose choices are as consequential as many judicial orders. Mohinder Singh Gill established that Article 324 is not a merely mechanical provision: the Commission holds plenary residual authority to fill gaps in electoral law when no statutory provision exists, making its institutional independence a first-order constitutional concern. The composition and appointment of the Commission therefore affects the integrity of every election held while a particular bench is in office. When the Commission's independence is questioned, the entire democratic legitimacy of election results comes under pressure.

## How to monitor this institution

The Commission's official website (eci.gov.in) publishes press notes, model code notifications, candidate affidavit data, and schedule notifications — these are the primary official outputs to track. The candidate affidavit portal contains self-disclosed criminal, financial, and educational background data for every candidate contesting a parliamentary or assembly election, and the Supreme Court has repeatedly directed parties to publicise this data. Supreme Court Observer and Live Law track the pending constitutional challenge to the Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Appointment, Conditions of Service and Term of Office) Act, 2023. For election finance, the Association for Democratic Reforms (ADR) aggregates and analyses affidavit data across election cycles. PRS India provides structured analysis of electoral legislation moving through Parliament.

## Constitutional design

Article 324 vests superintendence, direction, and control of elections to Parliament, state legislatures, the President, and Vice-President in the Election Commission, using language broad enough to allow the Commission to act in areas not covered by specific statute. The Commission consists of the Chief Election Commissioner and such other Election Commissioners as the President may determine; the CEC can only be removed by the same process as a Supreme Court judge, providing the strongest constitutional protection given to any non-judicial office. Mohinder Singh Gill confirmed that Article 324 is a plenary residual provision, meaning the Commission can use its own authority to fill gaps when Parliament or state legislatures have not legislated — making the provision something closer to an executive jurisdiction than a merely administrative function. Anoop Baranwal v. Union of India (2023) held that the appointment process for Election Commissioners, which had previously allowed unfettered executive discretion, was constitutionally inadequate and directed that a committee including the Chief Justice of India must participate in selections until Parliament legislates — making the independence of the appointment mechanism a constitutional requirement grounded in Article 324's guarantee of a free and fair election. Articles 327 and 329 give Parliament authority to legislate on elections while simultaneously restricting courts from intervening in electoral matters once an election process has begun. The interaction between Article 324's Commission powers and Article 329's bar on election challenges is a recurring source of constitutional litigation.

## Live issues

The appointment process under the 2023 Act is constitutionally challenged before the Supreme Court — petitioners argue it reintroduces executive dominance that Anoop Baranwal condemned by placing the Prime Minister and Cabinet Minister on the selection committee while removing the Chief Justice of India (see [election commission appointments and independence](/current-affairs/election-commission-appointments-and-independence)). Candidate disclosure enforcement and party compliance with the 2020 Supreme Court directions on publishing criminal antecedents remain active monitoring tasks, with ADR regularly filing compliance applications (see [candidate disclosure and criminalisation of politics](/current-affairs/candidate-disclosure-and-criminalisation-of-politics)). The One Nation One Election proposals require the Commission to manage simultaneous schedules across the country, raising operational and constitutional questions about its capacity and authority (see [one nation one election](/current-affairs/one-nation-one-election)). Electoral finance transparency — particularly post-electoral bonds — is generating fresh litigation and legislative proposals that the Commission will need to operationalise. The electoral bonds judgment (2024) struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme as unconstitutional and directed the Commission to compile and publish data on bonds purchased and redeemed, placing the ECI at the centre of disclosure enforcement and demonstrating that Article 324's superintendence function extends to ensuring the informational preconditions for free and fair elections (see [electoral finance and disclosure](/current-affairs/electoral-finance-and-disclosure)).
