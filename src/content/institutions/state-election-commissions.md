## Why this institution matters

The Constitution creates a distinct election authority for local democracy. That matters because panchayat and municipal elections can otherwise be postponed through state-level delay, leaving local governance without elected renewal. The 73rd and 74th Amendments were intended to entrench local self-government as a constitutional institution rather than a creature of state government preference. The SEC is the structural guarantee of that entrenchment, and when state governments find ways to defer elections — through ward delimitation delays, OBC data requirements, or litigation — they are in effect hollowing out what the Amendments guaranteed.

## Constitutional design

Articles 243K (Panchayats) and 243ZA (Municipalities), inserted by the 73rd and 74th Amendments in 1992-93, establish State Election Commissions as independent constitutional bodies with exclusive superintendence, direction, and control of local body elections. The State Election Commissioner has the same security of tenure as a High Court judge — removal only by impeachment-like procedure — and cannot have conditions of service varied to the person's disadvantage after appointment. SECs are constitutionally distinct from the Election Commission of India: the ECI handles Lok Sabha and Vidhan Sabha elections under Article 324, while SECs handle panchayat and municipal elections under Articles 243K and 243ZA. States cannot transfer local election authority back to the state government or a departmental officer — the constitutional vesting in the SEC is exclusive. Article 243E requires five-year terms for panchayats and Article 243U for municipalities, with elections completed before the expiry of a term. The Supreme Court in [Kishansing Tomar](/cases/kishansing-tomar) confirmed that the state government's obligation to conduct elections on time is mandatory and that courts can compel compliance.

## Live issues

SECs have been structurally under-resourced and subject to political pressure from state governments, which control their administrative budgets and staffing without the independence of the ECI. Repeated delays in local body elections — using pending OBC data collection as justification, after the Supreme Court's triple-test requirement in [K. Krishna Murthy](/cases/k-krishna-murthy) — have generated repeated Supreme Court intervention (see [local body election delays and SEC independence](/current-affairs/local-body-election-delays-and-sec-independence)). The triple-test requires states to constitute a dedicated commission to collect empirical OBC data, specify the quantum of reservation proportionate to that data, and ensure the aggregate reservation does not exceed fifty per cent — each step creates opportunity for state government delay. The Court has held that elections must be conducted without OBC reservation if the state fails to complete the triple-test, but enforcement of that position has itself been contested. The structural asymmetry between ECI and SEC institutional capacity remains the deeper problem underlying each episode of delay.

## How to monitor this institution

Individual State Election Commission websites — where available and maintained — carry election schedules, notifications, and model code announcements. The quality and regularity of publication varies significantly across states. Supreme Court and High Court cause lists are the most reliable source for pending local body election mandamus proceedings, as litigation has driven most of the accountability on this issue. PRS India tracks state-level election status and legislative changes affecting local body governance. OBC commission reports and state backward classes commission publications are relevant when the triple-test is invoked as a justification for delay.
