## Why the term matters

This phrase marks the transition from a narrow procedural reading of liberty to a much richer substantive rights jurisprudence.

## How to use it

Whenever readers encounter detention, surveillance, travel restrictions, or coercive state procedure, this phrase often explains the constitutional standard in play.

## What this doctrine does

Article 21 states that no person shall be deprived of their life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law. The original framers chose this language over the American "due process of law" formula, intending to avoid courts second-guessing legislative policy on substantive grounds. However, judicial interpretation has progressively read the phrase to require not merely that a procedure exist in positive law, but that the procedure itself be fair, just, and reasonable — effectively importing substantive due process considerations through interpretive evolution rather than formal textual change. The result is that any state action restricting life or personal liberty must clear three overlapping tests: there must be a law authorising the restriction, that law must be constitutionally valid, and the procedure it prescribes must satisfy a standard of fairness and reasonableness. This reading has allowed Article 21 to become the most expansively interpreted provision in the Constitution, encompassing rights far beyond physical liberty, including the right to livelihood, health, dignity, and a clean environment.

## How courts have applied it

The pivotal transformation came in Maneka Gandhi v Union of India, where the Supreme Court unanimously rejected the earlier narrow reading given in A K Gopalan v State of Madras, which had treated Article 21 as requiring only that a law formally exist and that its procedure be formally followed. In Maneka Gandhi, the Court held that the procedure must be right, just, and fair, and that Articles 14, 19, and 21 are not isolated silos but must be read together — meaning a law affecting liberty must satisfy reasonableness under Article 14 and not violate freedoms under Article 19. In Francis Coralie Mullin v Administrator, Union Territory of Delhi, the Court held that Article 21 encompasses the right to live with dignity and not merely animal existence. In Olga Tellis v Bombay Municipal Corporation, the right to livelihood was brought within Article 21 on the ground that without the means to live, the right to life itself is hollow. In K S Puttaswamy v Union of India, the Court drew on Article 21 and the procedure-established-by-law framework to hold that privacy is a fundamental right subject to the requirement that any state invasion must be backed by a valid law that is itself proportionate and fair.

## Why it matters today

The procedure-established-by-law standard has become central to constitutional challenges against preventive detention laws, surveillance programmes, and digital regulation, because each of these requires courts to assess whether the authorising statute and its procedures meet the fair-and-just threshold. Current debates about the Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, National Security Act, and proposed data protection legislation all engage directly with how robustly courts will enforce the requirement that restrictions on liberty be grounded in a fair procedure. The standard also underpins litigation about the conditions of undertrial detention, access to legal representation, and the constitutionality of bail restrictions, making it one of the most practically consequential doctrines in day-to-day constitutional litigation.
