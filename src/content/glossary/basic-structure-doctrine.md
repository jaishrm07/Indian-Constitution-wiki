## Why the term matters

This phrase appears in nearly every debate about the outer limits of constitutional change in India.

## How to use it

When readers encounter proposals for deep structural reform, this doctrine is often the first constitutional checkpoint.

## What this doctrine does

The basic structure doctrine operates as a judicially imposed limit on Parliament's constituent power under Article 368. It holds that while Parliament may amend any provision of the Constitution, it cannot so alter the amendment as to damage or destroy the Constitution's essential identity. The doctrine does not enumerate a fixed, exhaustive list of protected features; instead, courts determine whether a particular amendment destroys a feature so fundamental that its removal would negate the constitutional character of the republic. Features recognised as part of basic structure have included the supremacy of the Constitution, the rule of law, judicial independence, separation of powers, federalism, secularism, and free and fair elections. The practical effect is that even a constitutional amendment passed by the required majority can be struck down if the Supreme Court finds it violates basic structure.

## How courts have applied it

The doctrine was first articulated by a thirteen-judge bench in Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala, where a 7-6 majority held that Parliament's amending power did not extend to altering the basic structure or essential features of the Constitution. The Court applied the doctrine in Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain to strike down the 39th Amendment, which had placed the Prime Minister's election beyond judicial scrutiny, finding it violated democracy and rule of law as basic features. In Minerva Mills v Union of India, the Court invalidated clauses of the 42nd Amendment that sought to make the Directive Principles prevail over Fundamental Rights, reinforcing that harmony between the two chapters is itself a basic feature. The NJAC judgment in Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India deployed the doctrine to invalidate the 99th Amendment creating the National Judicial Appointments Commission, holding that judicial independence is a basic feature that the amendment damaged. Throughout this jurisprudence, the Court has confirmed that the doctrine applies not only to Fundamental Rights amendments but to amendments affecting any essential feature of the constitutional design.

## Why it matters today

The basic structure doctrine remains India's primary constitutional safeguard against majoritarian amendment overreach, and it is regularly invoked in public debate about proposed electoral, judicial, and federal reforms. Contemporary disputes about the status of the collegium system, potential changes to legislative qualification rules, and proposals to reorganise the judiciary each raise potential basic structure questions. Because the doctrine's content is ultimately defined by the Supreme Court itself, debates about who holds interpretive authority and whether the doctrine has expanded too far or not far enough remain central to Indian constitutional politics.
