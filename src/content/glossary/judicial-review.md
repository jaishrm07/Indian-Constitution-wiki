## Why the term matters

Judicial review is one of the main reasons constitutional rights and structural limits can be enforced in practice rather than remaining symbolic.

## How to use it

Whenever a page asks whether a court can examine a law, executive order, proclamation, or amendment, judicial review is the operative concept.

## What this doctrine does

Judicial review is the constitutional power, vested primarily in the Supreme Court and High Courts, to examine legislation, executive action, and constitutional amendments against the standards set by the Constitution and to declare them void if they are found to be inconsistent with it. In India, judicial review operates at three levels: reviewing ordinary legislation against Fundamental Rights and other constitutional provisions, reviewing executive and administrative action for compliance with statutory and constitutional authority, and reviewing constitutional amendments against the basic structure doctrine. Unlike pure procedural review, Indian judicial review has an explicitly substantive dimension following the Maneka Gandhi line of cases, meaning courts assess not merely whether correct procedures were followed but whether the substance of state action meets constitutional standards of justice and reasonableness. The power is conferred by Articles 13, 32, and 226, making it constitutionally entrenched rather than merely judge-made.

## How courts have applied it

In Shankari Prasad v Union of India and Sajjan Singh v State of Rajasthan, the early Court held that constitutional amendments were beyond judicial review under Article 13, a position that was overturned by the majority in Kesavananda Bharati, which established that even amendments can be reviewed and struck down if they damage the basic structure. In Maneka Gandhi v Union of India, the Court expanded the scope of judicial review of executive action under Article 21, requiring that any procedure affecting life or personal liberty be fair, just, and reasonable rather than merely formally authorised. The Court's decision in Minerva Mills applied intensive judicial review to strike down provisions that had attempted to exclude review of certain constitutional changes. In L Chandra Kumar v Union of India, the Supreme Court held that the power of judicial review vested in the High Courts and Supreme Court under Articles 226 and 32 is itself a basic feature of the Constitution, meaning Parliament cannot eliminate or effectively curtail it.

## Why it matters today

Judicial review sits at the centre of live controversies about the appropriate relationship between courts and the elected branches, with critics arguing that expansive review strays into policy-making and defenders contending it is indispensable to constitutional governance. Contemporary debates about the use of Article 142's broad remedial powers, the scope of review of executive decisions under national security law, and the boundaries of judicial intervention in electoral processes all turn on foundational questions about judicial review's proper reach. The question of whether review extends to legislative processes — such as passage of bills under the money bill route — continues to produce contested Supreme Court judgments.
