## Why the term matters

Privacy now shapes modern constitutional disputes about technology, identity, data, surveillance, and bodily autonomy.

## How to use it

Readers should look for privacy whenever a legal regime concentrates personal information or constrains individual choice in intimate or digital settings.

## What this doctrine does

Privacy as a fundamental right operates under Article 21 and encompasses three related but distinct dimensions: decisional autonomy (the right to make intimate choices about one's own life without state coercion), informational privacy (the right to control how personal data about oneself is collected, used, and shared), and bodily integrity (protection against non-consensual physical intrusion by the state). The Supreme Court has also recognised spatial privacy protecting the sanctity of the home and relational privacy protecting personal associations and communications. Because privacy is derived from Article 21, any state action that infringes it must be justified by a valid law, must pursue a legitimate state aim, and must satisfy proportionality — the invasion must be no greater than necessary to achieve the aim. Unlike some constitutional rights, privacy is not absolute; the Court acknowledged in Puttaswamy that it yields to compelling state interests, but the state bears the burden of justifying each specific invasion against the three-part test. This framework has made privacy the foundational analytical lens for constitutional challenges to surveillance programmes, Aadhaar-based identity requirements, and content moderation regulations.

## How courts have applied it

The transformative ruling came in K S Puttaswamy v Union of India, where a nine-judge bench unanimously held that privacy is a fundamental right protected by the Constitution, overruling M P Sharma v Satish Chandra and Kharak Singh v State of Uttar Pradesh, which had either denied or severely limited privacy as a fundamental right. Within the same Puttaswamy litigation, a five-judge bench then applied the right to partially uphold the Aadhaar scheme while striking down its application to private entities, holding that mandatory biometric linkage for private services went beyond what was necessary and proportionate. In Navtej Singh Johar v Union of India, privacy reasoning drawn from Puttaswamy was central to striking down Section 377 of the Indian Penal Code insofar as it criminalised consensual same-sex conduct between adults, with the Court reasoning that sexual orientation and identity are core aspects of personal autonomy protected by privacy. In Joseph Shine v Union of India, the Court relied on privacy and dignity under Article 21 to strike down the adultery offence in the Penal Code, finding that treating a spouse's sexual conduct as a property violation of the other spouse failed constitutional scrutiny. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023, represents the first legislative attempt to operationalise the informational privacy dimension of the Puttaswamy framework.

## Why it matters today

The constitutional right to privacy remains central to ongoing litigation and legislative debates about mass surveillance infrastructure, the use of facial recognition technology by law enforcement, mandatory Aadhaar linkage for welfare benefits, and the scope of exemptions carved into the data protection statute. The Supreme Court's proportionality framework from Puttaswamy has yet to be applied comprehensively to many of these surveillance technologies, meaning that much of the most consequential privacy litigation in India is still pending or at early stages. Questions about whether the data protection law's exemptions for state processing and national security adequately honour the constitutional right remain among the most contested in contemporary Indian public law.
