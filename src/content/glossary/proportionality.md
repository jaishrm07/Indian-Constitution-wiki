## Why the term matters

The term now appears across speech, privacy, and liberty cases because it gives courts a framework for structured rights review.

## How to use it

Whenever a page asks whether a restriction is too broad, excessive, or insufficiently justified, proportionality is usually in the background.

## What this doctrine does

Proportionality is a structured constitutional test that courts apply when the state restricts a fundamental right to determine whether the restriction is justifiable. In its most developed Indian formulation, articulated in the Puttaswamy privacy judgment, the test involves four sequential inquiries: first, whether the restricting measure is backed by a valid law (legality); second, whether the measure pursues a legitimate state aim (legitimate goal); third, whether the measure is rationally connected to that aim and is the least restrictive means reasonably available (necessity); and fourth, whether the measure strikes a fair balance between the severity of the rights limitation and the importance of the public benefit sought (proportionality stricto sensu or balancing). The framework draws on comparative constitutional experience from Germany, Canada, and the European Court of Human Rights, and represents a move beyond the traditionally deferential "reasonable restriction" review that had been the Indian standard under Article 19(2) through 19(6). Proportionality review shifts the burden to the state to affirmatively justify why a particular degree of rights limitation was required, rather than placing the burden on the petitioner to show that the restriction is irrational or manifestly arbitrary.

## How courts have applied it

In K S Puttaswamy v Union of India, the plurality judgment authored by Justice D Y Chandrachud set out the four-stage proportionality test as the operative framework for reviewing state invasions of privacy, marking the most explicit adoption of structured proportionality in Indian constitutional law. The test was applied within the same Aadhaar litigation, where the five-judge bench found that mandatory biometric linkage for private commercial entities failed the necessity and balancing stages because less invasive means were available to prevent fraud, while the linkage for welfare subsidies was found proportionate. In Modern Dental College v State of Madhya Pradesh, the Court applied proportionality to restrictions on professional conduct under Article 19(1)(g), signalling that the framework is not confined to privacy but extends across the fundamental rights chapters. In Anuradha Bhasin v Union of India, which addressed internet shutdowns in Jammu and Kashmir, the Court used proportionality reasoning to hold that indefinite suspensions without review could not satisfy constitutional standards, even if temporary shutdowns in response to genuine security threats might be permissible.

## Why it matters today

Proportionality has become the decisive analytical framework in the most contested contemporary cases — internet shutdowns, sedition law, surveillance orders, and restrictions on political expression — because it forces courts and governments to engage concretely with whether less drastic means were considered rather than simply asserting security or public order justifications. The extent to which Indian courts will apply rigorous proportionality review as opposed to deferring to executive assessment of necessity, particularly in national security and public order contexts, remains one of the most debated questions in Indian constitutional scholarship. The operationalisation of proportionality in the Digital Personal Data Protection Act and future surveillance legislation will determine how meaningful the constitutional standard actually becomes in practice.
