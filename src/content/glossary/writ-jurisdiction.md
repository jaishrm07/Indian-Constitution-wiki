## Why the term matters

Writs are the operational tools that make rights enforcement real. They are central to understanding the Constitution as a working remedial document.

## How to use it

Readers should think of writ jurisdiction as the bridge between constitutional principle and judicial remedy.

## What this doctrine does

Writ jurisdiction is the power vested in the Supreme Court under Article 32 and in the High Courts under Article 226 to issue specified prerogative-style orders compelling or restraining state action and securing the release of persons unlawfully detained. The Constitution recognises five classes of writ: habeas corpus, which requires the detaining authority to produce the detained person and justify the legality of detention; mandamus, which directs a public authority to perform a mandatory public duty it has failed or refused to perform; prohibition, which restrains an inferior tribunal from exceeding its jurisdiction; certiorari, which brings the record of an inferior body before the superior court for review and potential quashing; and quo warranto, which challenges a person's legal right to hold a public office. Article 32 is itself a Fundamental Right — the right to move the Supreme Court for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights — meaning that Parliament cannot suspend it except during a declared emergency, and the Supreme Court has treated the right to constitutional remedies as one of the heart and soul of the Constitution. High Courts have broader writ jurisdiction under Article 226 that extends beyond Fundamental Rights to any legal right, making them the primary access point for most constitutional petitioners.

## How courts have applied it

In ADM Jabalpur v Shivkant Shukla, a majority controversially held that habeas corpus petitions were not maintainable during the Emergency period when the right to move under Article 32 was suspended, a decision widely regarded as the Court's gravest institutional failure and effectively overruled by the nine-judge bench in K S Puttaswamy. In Bandhua Mukti Morcha v Union of India, the Court dramatically expanded the practical reach of writ jurisdiction by entertaining letters and communications as public interest petitions, treating epistolary jurisdiction as a form of habeas corpus for the protection of bonded labourers who could not individually access courts. In Vineet Narain v Union of India, the Court issued a series of continuing mandamus orders directing the Central Bureau of Investigation to complete investigations free from executive interference, extending mandamus into supervisory oversight of investigative agencies. In L Chandra Kumar, the Court held that the power of judicial review exercised through Articles 226 and 32 forms part of the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be excluded even by a constitutional amendment establishing tribunals with exclusive jurisdiction.

## Why it matters today

Writ jurisdiction is the institutional mechanism through which constitutional rights become actionable, and controversies about its proper scope — including the use of public interest litigation, the practice of issuing continuing mandamus orders over executive functions, and the limits of suo motu jurisdiction — go to the heart of debates about judicial overreach and democratic accountability. The rise of national security legislation and statutory non-obstante clauses that attempt to restrict judicial scrutiny of certain executive decisions raises recurring questions about whether writ jurisdiction can be effectively curtailed short of a formal constitutional amendment. The differential between the speed and effectiveness of Article 32 relief in the Supreme Court and Article 226 relief in High Courts also raises access-to-justice concerns that are a persistent theme in judicial reform discussions.
