## Why the term matters

It affects coalition stability, party discipline, Speaker authority, and the timing of government survival votes.

## How to use it

Readers should treat anti-defection law as a constitutional response to political instability that also creates its own institutional tensions.

## What this doctrine does

The Tenth Schedule, inserted by the 52nd Amendment in 1985, provides that a member of a House belonging to a political party is disqualified if they voluntarily give up membership of their party or if they vote or abstain in the House contrary to any direction issued by the party without prior permission that has not subsequently been condoned. The Schedule originally also permitted a "split" if at least one-third of the legislative party defected together, but that exception was deleted by the 91st Amendment in 2003, meaning that no group of legislators below two-thirds of the original legislative party can break away without incurring disqualification. The power to decide disqualification petitions is vested in the Chairman of the Rajya Sabha or the Speaker of the Lok Sabha in Parliament, and in the corresponding presiding officers in state legislatures — a design that has generated acute controversy because the presiding officer is often a party appointee aligned with one side of the dispute. The law was designed to stabilise governments, reduce political horse-trading, and reinforce party accountability, but critics argue that it transfers too much power from elected legislators to party leadership and presiding officers, and that delays in deciding disqualification petitions have become a mechanism for manipulation.

## How courts have applied it

The constitutional validity of the Tenth Schedule was upheld by the Supreme Court in Kihoto Hollohan v Zachillhu, where the Court held that the Schedule did not violate the basic structure of the Constitution, but ruled that the finality clause purporting to exclude judicial review of the Speaker's decision was itself unconstitutional because the Speaker acts as a tribunal and judicial review of tribunal decisions is a basic feature. The Court in Kihoto also established that disqualification proceedings do not attract the immunity from court scrutiny under Article 122, which covers internal legislative proceedings. In subsequent years, litigation about the Speaker's conflict of interest — where the Speaker facing a disqualification motion against rebel members was also a beneficiary of the survival of the government — led courts to intervene more readily. In Nabam Rebia and Bamang Felix v Deputy Speaker, the Supreme Court held that a Speaker facing a notice for removal cannot adjudicate disqualification petitions during that period, injecting a procedural check on Speaker partiality. In the Maharashtra political crisis cases of 2022-23 involving rival factions of the Shiv Sena, the Supreme Court sitting as a Constitution Bench addressed foundational questions about which faction constitutes the "political party" for Tenth Schedule purposes and whether the Election Commission's recognition of one faction could pre-empt disqualification proceedings.

## Why it matters today

Anti-defection law and its institutional design flaws — particularly Speaker partiality and delayed adjudication — have been at the centre of virtually every major state-level political crisis in India over the past decade, from Goa and Manipur to Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, and Jharkhand. The Supreme Court has repeatedly called for reform of the adjudicatory mechanism, suggesting that an independent tribunal rather than the Speaker should decide disqualification petitions, but Parliament has not enacted the change. The Law Commission and parliamentary standing committees have made similar recommendations, meaning that one of the most consequential institutional reform questions in Indian democracy remains unaddressed despite sustained judicial and scholarly attention.
