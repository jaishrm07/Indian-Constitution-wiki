## Why the term matters

The floor test is one of the most important procedural safeguards in moments of legislative instability.

## How to use it

When a governor, Speaker, or court confronts a disputed majority, the floor test is usually treated as the cleanest constitutional check.

## What this doctrine does

A floor test is the constitutional and parliamentary practice of requiring a government to demonstrate its majority support through a vote in the legislature rather than through external claims or numerical affidavits submitted to a governor or court. The test takes the form of a motion of confidence in the ruling government or, in certain circumstances, a motion of no-confidence by the opposition; a government that loses such a vote on the floor of the house is constitutionally obligated to resign. The importance of the floor test as a constitutional principle flows from the foundational premise of parliamentary democracy that the executive derives its legitimacy from the confidence of the elected legislature, and that this confidence must be assessed within the legislature itself rather than through executive or gubernatorial preference. Indian constitutional practice, heavily shaped by the S R Bommai decision, now treats the floor test as the primary and generally exclusive mechanism for testing a government's majority, and governors who recommend President's Rule without directing the government to seek a floor test face significant risk of judicial intervention. The Supreme Court has also issued direction for composite floor tests — where rival factions each claim a majority — to be conducted on a day specified by the Court itself.

## How courts have applied it

The floor test's constitutional centrality was cemented in S R Bommai v Union of India, where the nine-judge bench held that the question of whether a government commands a majority should be tested on the floor of the legislature rather than on the Governor's subjective satisfaction, and that imposition of President's Rule without affording the government an opportunity to prove its majority on the floor is constitutionally vulnerable. In Jagdambika Pal v Union of India, involving competing claims to the Chief Ministership of Uttar Pradesh, the Supreme Court ordered a composite floor test to be conducted under a single Speaker in a single session, providing a practical model for resolving simultaneous competing majority claims. In the Uttarakhand political crisis of 2016, the Court stayed President's Rule and ultimately required a floor test, finding that the proclamation had been imposed without giving the government an opportunity to demonstrate its legislative majority. The Goa and Manipur post-election controversies, where governors invited parties other than the plurality winner to form governments, also led to litigation asserting that a floor test should have been immediately prescribed, though the precise timing obligation of governors in such contexts remains a partially unsettled question.

## Why it matters today

The floor test doctrine is repeatedly tested in real time whenever political defections, coalition collapses, or disputed government formations arise in state politics, making it one of the most practically live constitutional concepts. The interaction between anti-defection law and the floor test has become especially fraught, because legislators who vote against their own government in a floor test may simultaneously be liable to disqualification under the Tenth Schedule, and courts have had to navigate the sequencing and interaction between these two constitutional mechanisms. Governor conduct before and after lost floor tests — including refusal to accept resignation, delay in inviting alternative government formation, and selective application of the test to governments of specific political parties — continues to generate constitutional controversies in multiple states.
