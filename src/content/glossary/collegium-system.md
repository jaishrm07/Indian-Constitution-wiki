## Why the term matters

The collegium system determines who sits on India's highest courts and thereby shapes the interpretation of the Constitution for generations. Its legitimacy and accountability are therefore among the most consequential institutional questions in Indian public law.

## How to use it

Readers should use this term when evaluating judicial appointments controversies, the independence of the judiciary from the executive, and debates about transparency in the selection of judges.

## What this doctrine does

The collegium system is the judge-made practice under which appointments to the Supreme Court and High Courts are made on the recommendation of a group of the most senior judges — the Chief Justice of India and the four most senior judges of the Supreme Court for Supreme Court appointments, and the Chief Justice of India, the two most senior judges of the Supreme Court, and the Chief Justice of the relevant High Court for High Court appointments. The system is entirely judge-made: it derives not from any express constitutional text but from the Supreme Court's interpretation of the phrase "consultation" in Articles 124 and 217, which the Court progressively construed to require concurrence rather than mere consultation and ultimately to vest primacy in the collegium over the executive. The collegium's recommendations are binding on the executive in the sense that if the collegium reiterates a recommendation after the executive returns it, the executive is constitutionally obligated to act on it — a position the Court established through a series of advisory opinions and judgments known as the Judges Cases. The system has no statutory basis and operates through conventions, memoranda of procedure, and collegium resolutions published on the Supreme Court's website, making its proceedings considerably more opaque than appointment systems in comparable constitutional democracies. Critics argue that the system insulates judicial appointments from democratic accountability, while defenders argue that independence from executive control is precisely the feature that makes it constitutionally necessary.

## How courts have applied it

The first structural step toward collegium primacy came in S P Gupta v Union of India (the First Judges Case), where the Supreme Court held that "consultation" with the Chief Justice in Article 124 did not mean concurrence, and that executive primacy in appointments was therefore constitutionally legitimate — a decision that proved short-lived. In Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India (the Second Judges Case), a nine-judge bench overruled S P Gupta and held that "consultation" must mean "concurrence," that the opinion of the Chief Justice of India formed through a collegial process must be given primacy, and that the executive's power to disagree was narrowly confined to returning a recommendation once with reasons, after which the collegium's reiteration was binding. In the Third Judges Case (In Re: Special Reference), the President of India sought the Court's advisory opinion on several aspects of the collegium system, and the Court refined the composition of the collegium as the Chief Justice plus the four most senior judges rather than just the two most senior. The collegium system then faced its most serious constitutional challenge in the NJAC Case — Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association v Union of India decided in 2015 — where a five-judge bench struck down the 99th Amendment establishing the National Judicial Appointments Commission, holding by a 4-1 majority that the NJAC compromised judicial independence in a manner that damaged the basic structure of the Constitution, and reinstating the collegium system as the operative appointments mechanism.

## Why it matters today

The collegium system remains one of the most publicly contested institutional arrangements in Indian constitutional governance, with the executive periodically declining or delaying action on collegium recommendations and the Supreme Court periodically issuing directions about timelines and procedures for processing them. Debates about transparency in collegium deliberations, the absence of any formal criteria or merit review mechanism, the role of seniority versus other considerations, and the handling of candidates with adverse intelligence reports remain live and unresolved. The broader question of whether a reformed appointments process that maintains judicial independence while incorporating some degree of democratic accountability — a mechanism that could pass basic structure scrutiny while also answering the democratic legitimacy critique — has been raised by the Law Commission and in parliamentary debate, but no legislative solution has been enacted since the NJAC was struck down.
