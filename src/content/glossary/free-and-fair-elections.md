## Why the term matters

Free and fair elections are a foundational constitutional value because the entire legitimacy of Parliament, state legislatures, and the executive ultimately rests on the integrity of the electoral process.

## How to use it

Readers should use this concept when assessing whether electoral laws, campaign finance rules, voter information rights, and the conduct of the Election Commission are constitutionally adequate to protect genuine democratic choice.

## What this doctrine does

Free and fair elections as a constitutional principle holds that the right to vote and the processes surrounding it must be designed and conducted in a manner that genuinely reflects the will of the electorate, free from coercion, corruption, distortion of information, and abuse of state power for electoral advantage. The Supreme Court has recognised free and fair elections as a basic feature of the Constitution, meaning it is protected against even constitutional amendment and serves as a standard against which electoral legislation is reviewed. The constitutional text in Part XV — Articles 324 to 329 — places the superintendence, direction, and control of elections in an independent Election Commission, a structural choice that reflects the framers' recognition that executive control over electoral machinery would be incompatible with genuine democratic competition. The principle encompasses the voter's right to information about candidates and their criminal antecedents and financial interests, the right of voters to cast a vote in an atmosphere free from intimidation, and the right to have their vote counted equally. Courts have derived from free and fair elections a further principle that campaign finance transparency is constitutionally required because money power in elections can distort voter choice as effectively as physical coercion.

## How courts have applied it

The foundational statement that free and fair elections are a basic feature of the Constitution came in Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain, where the Supreme Court struck down the 39th Amendment's attempt to immunise the Prime Minister's election from judicial review, holding that elections to Parliament must remain subject to the scrutiny of an independent tribunal and that insulating them from review fundamentally undermines the constitutional structure. In Mohinder Singh Gill v Chief Election Commissioner, the Court held that Article 324's grant of superintendence to the Election Commission is plenary, covering all aspects of elections not specifically covered by statute and empowering the Commission to take action to ensure that elections are conducted freely and fairly even in the absence of specific statutory authority. In Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India, the Court upheld the Election Commission's power to require disclosure of candidates' criminal records, assets, and liabilities, reasoning that the right of voters to know relevant information about candidates is an integral part of freedom of expression under Article 19 and the right to free and fair elections. In Association for Democratic Reforms v Union of India (Electoral Bonds), decided in 2024, the Supreme Court unanimously struck down the Electoral Bonds Scheme, holding that anonymous corporate donations routed through the scheme violated voters' right to information about political funding, which the Court treated as an essential component of the constitutional requirement of free and fair elections.

## Why it matters today

The electoral bonds judgment has opened a new phase in Indian electoral finance law, requiring Parliament to devise a replacement donation disclosure system that meets the constitutional standard of transparency without, according to the government's stated concern, exposing donors to harassment, and the design of any replacement regime will itself be tested against the free and fair elections standard. Ongoing debates about the appointment process for Election Commissioners — following the Supreme Court's direction in Anoop Baranwal v Union of India that appointments should be made by a committee including the Chief Justice of India until Parliament enacts a law — raise foundational questions about whether the institutional independence of the Commission is constitutionally adequate. The spread of state resources and media access disparities between ruling and opposition parties in election campaigns continues to be litigated as a potential violation of the level playing field that free and fair elections require.
