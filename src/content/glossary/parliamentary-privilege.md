## Why the term matters

Parliamentary privilege defines the boundary between legislative autonomy and judicial oversight, a boundary that is contested whenever legislatures discipline members, exclude journalists, or claim immunity from court processes.

## How to use it

Readers should use this term when disputes involve the internal actions of a legislature — particularly expulsions, contempt proceedings, and restrictions on reporting — and need to assess whether courts can intervene.

## What this doctrine does

Parliamentary privilege in India refers to the collection of immunities and powers granted to Parliament and state legislatures, and to their members and committees, to protect the freedom and authority of the legislature in performing its constitutional functions. Article 105 grants members of Parliament freedom of speech in the House, immunity from proceedings in any court in respect of any vote given or speech made in Parliament, and immunity from liability for publication of reports or proceedings under the authority of the House. Article 122 further provides that the validity of any proceedings in Parliament shall not be questioned in any court, and that no officer or member in whom powers are vested for regulating the procedure or conduct of business shall be subject to the jurisdiction of any court in respect of the exercise of those powers. Corresponding provisions under Articles 194 and 212 apply to state legislatures. To the extent the Constitution does not define privileges, Article 105(3) incorporated the privileges of the House of Commons as they stood in 1950, though the 44th Amendment deleted this reference for Parliament — meaning Parliament's privileges are now defined by its own laws and practices. The purpose of privilege is to prevent external interference in legislative deliberations and to allow honest discharge of members' duties without fear of prosecution, but the doctrine has also been claimed as a basis for the legislature to discipline and even expel its own members without judicial oversight.

## How courts have applied it

The most significant Supreme Court ruling on the extent and limits of parliamentary privilege is Raja Ram Pal v The Hon'ble Speaker, Lok Sabha, where the Court held that the power of expulsion of a member by the House is not immune from judicial review on jurisdictional grounds, rejecting the argument that Article 122 placed expulsion decisions entirely beyond court scrutiny. The Court reasoned that while courts do not sit in appeal over the merits of parliamentary decisions, they may intervene where the House has acted in violation of constitutional provisions or fundamental rights, and that judicial review of legislative action is itself a basic feature of the Constitution. An earlier landmark in P V Narasimha Rao v State, the Cash-for-Votes case, had held that members of Parliament who had voted in accordance with a bribe they had accepted were protected by the speech and vote immunity under Article 105(2), a ruling that sparked lasting controversy about whether privilege had been stretched to shield corruption. In Amarinder Singh v Special Committee, Punjab Vidhan Sabha, the Court clarified that privilege extends only to genuine legislative functions and cannot be invoked to protect purely partisan or disciplinary conduct. The tension between privilege and free speech rights of individuals who are expelled or restricted from the House has also been addressed in several High Court decisions that have followed Raja Ram Pal.

## Why it matters today

The expulsion of opposition members from Parliament in recent sessions, restrictions on press coverage of parliamentary proceedings, and the use of contempt of the House proceedings against journalists and civil society critics have all revived fundamental questions about the constitutional limits of privilege in a democracy committed to transparency and accountability. The Supreme Court's post-Raja Ram Pal framework leaves open exactly how far courts will go in reviewing expulsions, contempt findings, and other intra-legislative actions when fundamental rights of members or outsiders are affected. The intersection of privilege with whistle-blower protection law and right-to-information legislation — areas where Parliament claims internal immunity while citizens claim a right to parliamentary information — is an increasingly active area of constitutional tension.
