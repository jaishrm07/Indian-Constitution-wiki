## Why the term matters

Secularism helps explain why the Constitution treats religion as both a protected liberty and a domain requiring constitutional restraint.

## How to use it

Readers should use this term when disputes raise questions about religious freedom, institutional neutrality, or the constitutional identity of the republic.

## What this doctrine does

Indian constitutional secularism is a distinctive model that differs from both the strict separationist model of the United States and the French model of laicite. Rather than excluding religion from public life, the Indian Constitution protects both individual religious freedom under Articles 25 and 26 and denominational autonomy, while simultaneously prohibiting the state from using religion as a basis for differential treatment of citizens and from funding or propagating any religion through public money under Articles 27 and 28. The word "secular" was inserted into the Preamble by the 42nd Amendment in 1976, but the Supreme Court has held that secularism as a constitutional value was part of the original constitutional design, not merely a product of that amendment. Indian secularism is sometimes described as "positive secularism" or "principled equidistance," meaning the state may engage with all religious communities evenhandedly — including regulating religious institutions, managing religious endowments, and reforming religious practices that violate fundamental rights — rather than maintaining strict non-engagement. This has created the apparent paradox that state intervention in religious affairs can itself be a secular act when it pursues equal treatment, but can violate secularism when it applies different standards to different communities.

## How courts have applied it

The most consequential application of secularism as a constitutional principle came in S R Bommai v Union of India, where the Supreme Court held that secularism is part of the basic structure of the Constitution, meaning no constitutional amendment and no state action can undermine it, and that a state government that acts in violation of secularism thereby forfeits the constitutional conditions of its governance. The Court in Bommai declared that the state must have an equal attitude toward all religions — sarva dharma samabhava — and that any government appeal to religion in the conduct of elections and state affairs amounts to a departure from constitutional commitments. In Ziyauddin Burhanuddin Bukhari v Brijmohan Ramdaas Mehra, the Court held that using religious appeals in election campaigns constitutes a corrupt practice, connecting secularism directly to electoral law. In Abhiram Singh v C D Commachen, a seven-judge bench held that seeking votes on grounds of religion, race, caste, community, or language by a candidate, their agent, or others constitutes a corrupt practice, though the scope of the ruling — whether it also applies to votes sought against a religious group — was a point of internal disagreement. The Ayodhya title dispute judgment in M Siddiq v Mahant Suresh Das, while ultimately decided on property law grounds, engaged extensively with secularism in its reasoning about constitutional obligations toward religious equality.

## Why it matters today

Secularism's status as a basic structure feature means that legislative and constitutional changes affecting the relationship between religion and state action are subject to judicial scrutiny, and several contemporary legislative measures — including personal law reforms applied asymmetrically across communities, the Places of Worship (Special Provisions) Act and litigation seeking its reconsideration, and the Citizenship Amendment Act — have generated foundational constitutional debates that invoke secularism. The tension between the state's power to regulate religious institutions under Article 25(2) and the autonomy claims of religious denominations under Article 26 remains particularly active, with the Supreme Court's pending reference in the Sabarimala review case on the interaction of individual and denominational rights being one of the most consequential pending issues. How courts define the outer limits of permissible state engagement with religion while maintaining the appearance of evenhandedness across communities is a question that will continue to shape Indian constitutionalism for the foreseeable future.
