## Why the term matters

This is one of the most politically charged phrases in Indian constitutional practice because it combines emergency reasoning with federal consequences.

## How to use it

When a state government faces dismissal, floor-test controversy, or claims of constitutional breakdown, President's Rule is usually the central constitutional concept.

## What this doctrine does

Article 356 permits the President, on the Governor's report or otherwise, to proclaim that the government of a state cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution. Once proclaimed, the President assumes the functions of state government, Parliament becomes competent to legislate for the state, and the state assembly may be dissolved or kept in suspended animation. The proclamation must be approved by both Houses of Parliament within two months and lapses every six months unless reapproved, with a maximum duration of three years under the terms the Constitution allows. Although framed as a safeguard for constitutional governance, the provision was historically used opportunistically by Union governments to dismiss state governments of different political persuasions, making its subjective precondition — "failure of constitutional machinery" — among the most contested in Indian constitutional law. The 44th Amendment introduced the requirement that the proclamation be laid before Parliament, and post-Bommai practice has added a judicial check that was absent in earlier decades.

## How courts have applied it

The foundational judicial statement on Article 356 came in S R Bommai v Union of India, where a nine-judge bench held that the President's satisfaction under Article 356 is not immune from judicial review and that courts may examine whether the material before the President is relevant and whether the proclamation was made in good faith. Bommai also held that where a government has lost majority, the proper constitutional method to verify that fact is a floor test in the assembly rather than a Governor's subjective assessment, establishing floor test primacy as a constitutional norm. The Court went further to hold that secularism is a basic feature of the Constitution, meaning that a state government proved to be engaged in actions that violate secularism could constitute a ground for Article 356 invocation — a statement that connects President's Rule jurisprudence directly to the basic structure doctrine. In Rameshwar Prasad v Union of India, the Court struck down the imposition of President's Rule in Bihar in 2005, finding that the Governor's report was based on extraneous and irrelevant material, signalling that Bommai's review standard would be enforced. State of Rajasthan v Union of India, decided before Bommai, had taken a narrower approach and largely upheld Union discretion, but this position was superseded by Bommai's more searching review.

## Why it matters today

Article 356 remains contested in federal politics, particularly when Union and state governments are controlled by rival parties and when governors — appointed by the Union executive — make politically charged recommendations. Recent controversies over governor conduct in states including West Bengal, Tamil Nadu, and Kerala have revived questions about whether Bommai's safeguards are being observed or circumvented in practice. The intersection of Article 356 with anti-defection law and floor test norms has become increasingly important as legislative destabilisation through defection has been the proximate cause of several recent President's Rule proclamations and governor interventions.
