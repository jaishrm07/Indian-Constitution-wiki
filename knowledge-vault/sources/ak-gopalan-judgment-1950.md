## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment in which a six-judge bench upheld the Preventive Detention Act 1950 and adopted a compartmentalised interpretation of Articles 19, 21, and 22, holding that "procedure established by law" in Article 21 requires only legislative enactment and not substantive fairness. This reading was subsequently overruled by Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978).

## Why it matters

Use for original scope of Article 21 and "procedure established by law", compartmentalised reading of fundamental rights Articles 19, 21, and 22, personal liberty and preventive detention under the Constitution, constitutional history of due process versus procedure established by law.

## Limits and caveats

Six-judge bench judgment delivered on 19 May 1950. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation AIR 1950 SC 27 or [1950] SCR 88. By a 5:1 majority the Court upheld the Preventive Detention Act 1950, adopting a narrow compartmentalised reading of the fundamental rights chapter. The majority held that Articles 19, 21, and 22 operate in separate, self-contained compartments, and that "procedure established by law" in Article 21 means any procedure prescribed by a competent legislature without the requirement of fairness or reasonableness. The lone dissent by Fazl Ali J. argued for an integrated reading and a substantive due process standard. The Gopalan compartmentalisation was progressively eroded by R.C. Cooper v. Union of India (1970) and was definitively overruled in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978), which established that Articles 14, 19, and 21 must be read together and that any procedure under Article 21 must be fair, just, and reasonable.
