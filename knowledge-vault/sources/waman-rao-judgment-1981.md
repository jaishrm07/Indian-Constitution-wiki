## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment in which a nine-judge bench established that the Kesavananda Bharati decision date of 24 April 1973 operates as a constitutional cutoff — laws inserted in the Ninth Schedule before that date retain full immunity from Part III challenge under Article 31B, while post-1973 insertions are subject to basic structure review, and Article 31B does not provide absolute prospective immunity for future insertions.

## Limits and caveats

Nine-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered in 1981. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation AIR 1981 SC 271 or (1981) 2 SCC 362. The Court held that laws placed in the Ninth Schedule before 24 April 1973 (the date of the Kesavananda Bharati judgment) are immune from challenge under Part III of the Constitution by virtue of Article 31B. Laws inserted into the Ninth Schedule on or after that date are open to challenge on the ground that they abrogate or abridge fundamental rights forming part of the basic structure. The judgment also clarified that Article 31B does not confer absolute prospective immunity: Parliament cannot use future Ninth Schedule insertions as a device to immunise legislation from basic structure review. This holding was affirmed and elaborated by the nine-judge bench in I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007).
