## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment in which a three-judge bench, acting under Article 32 and in the absence of legislation, laid down the binding Vishaka Guidelines on sexual harassment at the workplace, reading international obligations under CEDAW into Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21, and directing all employers to constitute complaints committees; the guidelines were superseded by the POSH Act 2013.

## Why it matters

## Limits and caveats

Three-judge bench judgment delivered on 13 August 1997, arising from a writ petition under Article 32. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation (1997) 6 SCC 241 or AIR 1997 SC 3011. In the absence of enacted law on sexual harassment at the workplace, the Court laid down binding guidelines — the Vishaka Guidelines — applicable to all employers in the public and private sectors. The Court read the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) into the content of Articles 14, 15, 19(1)(g), and 21. The guidelines mandated employer awareness obligations, complaints committees, and disciplinary action. They operated as law until superseded by the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 (the POSH Act).
