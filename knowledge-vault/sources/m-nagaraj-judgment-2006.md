## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment in which a five-judge Constitution Bench upheld Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) but conditioned reservation in promotions on a three-part test requiring states to demonstrate inadequacy of representation, continued backwardness, and preservation of administrative efficiency, while reaffirming the 50% ceiling and leaving the creamy layer question open for SCs/STs.

## Why it matters

Use for reservation in promotions for SC/ST employees under Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B), three-condition test for states seeking to provide reservation in promotions, 50% ceiling on total reservations, creamy layer applicability to SC/ST communities, constitutional validity of the 77th, 81st, 82nd, and 85th Constitutional Amendments.

## Key claims or extracts

- reservation in promotions for SC/ST employees under Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B)
- three-condition test for states seeking to provide reservation in promotions
- 50% ceiling on total reservations
- creamy layer applicability to SC/ST communities
- constitutional validity of the 77th, 81st, 82nd, and 85th Constitutional Amendments

## Limits and caveats

Five-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 19 October 2006. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation (2006) 8 SCC 212 or AIR 2007 SC 71. The bench unanimously upheld the constitutional validity of Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) as amended. However, it imposed three cumulative conditions a state must satisfy before providing reservation in promotions for SCs/STs: (1) quantifiable data demonstrating inadequacy of representation of that group in the relevant cadre; (2) evidence of continued backwardness of the community; (3) satisfaction that overall administrative efficiency will not be compromised as required by Article 335. The 50% ceiling from Indra Sawhney (1992) was reaffirmed. The question of whether the creamy layer should be applied to SCs/STs for promotion reservations was left open. The backwardness condition was subsequently modified by Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018), where a five-judge bench held that states need not collect fresh data on backwardness for SCs/STs as their backwardness is established by the constitutional schedules themselves.
