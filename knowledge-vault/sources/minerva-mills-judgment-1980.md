## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment holding that judicial review under Articles 32 and 226 is part of the basic structure of the Constitution, that a harmonious balance between Part III (Fundamental Rights) and Part IV (Directive Principles) is itself a basic feature, and striking down the 42nd Amendment provisions that sought to exclude judicial review of constitutional amendments.

## Why it matters

Use for judicial review as a basic structure element, Articles 32 and 226 as inviolable constitutional guarantees, balance between Fundamental Rights (Part III) and Directive Principles (Part IV), 42nd Amendment Act 1976 and its constitutional limits.

## Key claims or extracts

- judicial review as a basic structure element
- Articles 32 and 226 as inviolable constitutional guarantees
- balance between Fundamental Rights (Part III) and Directive Principles (Part IV)
- 42nd Amendment Act 1976 and its constitutional limits

## Limits and caveats

Five-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 31 July 1980. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation AIR 1980 SC 1789 or (1980) 3 SCC 625. The Court struck down clauses 4 and 5 of Section 55 of the Constitution (42nd Amendment) Act 1976, which had sought to immunise constitutional amendments from judicial scrutiny and give an absolute primacy to Directive Principles over Fundamental Rights. The judgment applies and extends the basic structure doctrine from Kesavananda Bharati.
