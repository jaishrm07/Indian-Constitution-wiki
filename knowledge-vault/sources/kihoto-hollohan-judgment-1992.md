## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment upholding the constitutional validity of the Tenth Schedule (anti-defection law) while holding that the Speaker, acting as a tribunal under the Schedule, is subject to judicial review and that Paragraph 7's attempt to oust court jurisdiction was itself unconstitutional.

## Why it matters

Use for Tenth Schedule anti-defection law constitutional validity, Speaker's jurisdiction as a tribunal under the Tenth Schedule, judicial review of the Speaker's disqualification decisions, free speech of legislators and anti-defection restrictions.

## Limits and caveats

Five-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 17 February 1992. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation AIR 1993 SC 412 or (1992) Supp 2 SCC 651. The majority upheld the Tenth Schedule inserted by the Constitution (52nd Amendment) Act 1985 as constitutionally valid, but ruled by 3:2 that Paragraph 7 of the Schedule (which barred courts from entertaining any proceedings in relation to disqualification matters) was unconstitutional for not having been ratified by state legislatures as required under the proviso to Article 368(2). The Speaker's decision is thus subject to judicial review by the High Courts and the Supreme Court.
