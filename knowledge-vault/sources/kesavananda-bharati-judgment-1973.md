## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment holding that although Parliament may amend any provision of the Constitution under Article 368, it cannot alter or destroy the basic structure or essential features of the Constitution, establishing a permanent judicial check on unlimited constituent power.

## Why it matters

Use for basic structure doctrine, limits on Parliament's constituent power to amend the Constitution, Article 368 and the extent of amending power, fundamental rights vis-à-vis constitutional amendments.

## Key claims or extracts

- basic structure doctrine
- limits on Parliament's constituent power to amend the Constitution
- Article 368 and the extent of amending power
- fundamental rights vis-à-vis constitutional amendments

## Limits and caveats

Thirteen-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 24 April 1973 by a 7:6 majority. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation AIR 1973 SC 1461 or (1973) 4 SCC 225. The judgment runs to over 700 pages across multiple opinions; the operative summary signed by nine judges articulates the core holding. Key portions include the majority's identification of basic structure elements (supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, secular character, separation of powers, federalism, unity and integrity of India) and the overruling of Golak Nath v. State of Punjab (1967).
