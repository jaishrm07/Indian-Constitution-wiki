## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment (later overruled) holding by a 4:1 majority that during a Presidential Emergency, the right to move courts for habeas corpus under Article 226 stands suspended; Justice Khanna's lone dissent affirming the irreducible core of the right to life has become one of the most celebrated passages in Indian constitutional history.

## Why it matters

Use for habeas corpus during the 1975–77 Internal Emergency, Article 226 petitions and Emergency suspension, preventive detention and Article 21 during Emergency, historical critique of judicial deference to executive power.

## Key claims or extracts

- habeas corpus during the 1975–77 Internal Emergency
- Article 226 petitions and Emergency suspension
- preventive detention and Article 21 during Emergency
- historical critique of judicial deference to executive power

## Limits and caveats

Five-judge Constitution Bench judgment (4:1 majority) delivered on 28 April 1976. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation AIR 1976 SC 1207. The sole dissent by Justice H.R. Khanna is as heavily cited as the majority. The judgment was formally overruled by the nine-judge bench in Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017) on the question of whether life and liberty survive even during an Emergency. The majority and dissent opinions are the primary reference points for studying judicial independence and the limits of Emergency powers.
