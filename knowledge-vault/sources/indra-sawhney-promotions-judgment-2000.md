## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment in which a five-judge bench upheld Article 16(4A) permitting reservation in promotions for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, while imposing conditions requiring the state to produce quantifiable data on inadequate representation and backwardness and to demonstrate that administrative efficiency under Article 335 is maintained, with the creamy layer question for SCs and STs left open.

## Why it matters

Use for reservations in promotions under Article 16(4A), constitutional validity of the 77th, 81st, 82nd, and 85th Amendments, quantifiable data requirement for backwardness and inadequate representation, creamy layer exclusion and scheduled castes and scheduled tribes.

## Key claims or extracts

- reservations in promotions under Article 16(4A)
- constitutional validity of the 77th, 81st, 82nd, and 85th Amendments
- quantifiable data requirement for backwardness and inadequate representation
- creamy layer exclusion and scheduled castes and scheduled tribes

## Limits and caveats

Five-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 19 October 2006. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation (2006) 8 SCC 212 or AIR 2007 SC 71. The Court upheld the constitutional validity of Article 16(4A) (inserted by the 77th Amendment), which permits the state to make provision for reservation in matters of promotion for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. However, the bench imposed three conditions: the state must collect and place before the court quantifiable data demonstrating (i) the backwardness of the class, (ii) the inadequacy of its representation in public employment, and (iii) that overall administrative efficiency is maintained as required by Article 335. The Court did not apply the "creamy layer" exclusion to SCs and STs but left that question open; the roster point system for consequential seniority was upheld subject to the stated conditions.
