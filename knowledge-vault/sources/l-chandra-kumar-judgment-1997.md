## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment in which a seven-judge bench held that judicial review under Articles 226 and 32 forms an inviolable part of the basic structure of the Constitution, striking down tribunal provisions that attempted to exclude High Court supervisory jurisdiction and affirming that administrative tribunals may only serve as courts of first instance subject to High Court review.

## Why it matters

Use for judicial review as part of the basic structure doctrine, constitutional validity of tribunals and their relationship with High Courts, Article 226 jurisdiction of High Courts and parliamentary exclusion, Articles 32 and 226 as unamendable constitutional guarantees.

## Key claims or extracts

- judicial review as part of the basic structure doctrine

- Article 226 jurisdiction of High Courts and parliamentary exclusion
- Articles 32 and 226 as unamendable constitutional guarantees

## Limits and caveats

Seven-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 18 March 1997. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation (1997) 3 SCC 261 or AIR 1997 SC 1125. The Court held that the power of judicial review vested in the High Courts under Article 226 and in the Supreme Court under Article 32 is part of the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be excluded by Parliament. While Parliament may create administrative tribunals under Articles 323A and 323B, such tribunals function as courts of first instance only; their decisions remain subject to supervisory jurisdiction of the relevant High Court under Article 226. Any provision in a tribunal statute purporting to exclude High Court jurisdiction is unconstitutional to that extent.
