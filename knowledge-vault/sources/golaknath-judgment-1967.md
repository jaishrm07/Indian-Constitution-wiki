## What this source is

Official Supreme Court judgment in which an eleven-judge bench held by a 6:5 majority that Parliament cannot amend the Constitution so as to abridge or take away fundamental rights in Part III, applying prospective overruling so that earlier amendments remained valid. The ruling was subsequently overruled by Kesavananda Bharati (1973).

## Why it matters

## Limits and caveats

Eleven-judge Constitution Bench judgment delivered on 27 February 1967. Retrieve via eSCR portal using the citation AIR 1967 SC 1643 or [1967] 2 SCR 762. By a 6:5 majority the Court held that Parliament has no power to amend Part III of the Constitution so as to abridge or take away any of the fundamental rights. The majority applied the doctrine of prospective overruling, meaning the decision operated only for the future and previous amendments curtailing fundamental rights were saved. This ruling was effectively superseded when Parliament enacted the Constitution (Twenty-Fourth Amendment) Act 1971 to restore its amending power, and the entire question was then definitively settled by the thirteen-judge bench in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973), which overruled Golaknath while introducing the basic structure doctrine.
