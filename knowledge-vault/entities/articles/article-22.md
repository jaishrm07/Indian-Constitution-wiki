## Summary

Article 22 provides procedural safeguards against arrest and detention, while also structuring the constitutional treatment of preventive detention.

## Official text

No person who is arrested shall be detained in custody without being informed, as soon as may be, of the grounds for such arrest nor shall he be denied the right to consult, and to be defended by, a legal practitioner of his choice. Every person who is arrested and detained in custody shall be produced before the nearest magistrate within a period of twenty-four hours of such arrest and no such person shall be detained in custody beyond the said period without the authority of a magistrate.

## Plain-English meaning

If a person is arrested, the Constitution requires prompt information about the grounds, access to legal defence, and production before a magistrate. But the Article also shows that preventive detention occupies a troubling constitutional space.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Article 22 is one of the Constitution's clearest procedural liberty provisions. It matters most when the state uses arrest, detention, or preventive detention powers that can quickly overwhelm ordinary liberty.
How courts use it
The Article often appears with Article 21 in custody, detention, and due process cases, especially when courts examine whether procedural safeguards were real or merely formal.
