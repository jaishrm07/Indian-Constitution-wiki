## Summary

Article 356 allows President's Rule when the constitutional machinery of a state is said to have failed.

## Official text

If the President, on receipt of a report from the Governor of a State or otherwise, is satisfied that a situation has arisen in which the Government of the State cannot be carried on in accordance with the provisions of this Constitution, the President may by Proclamation assume to himself all or any of the functions of the Government of the State, declare that the powers of the Legislature of the State shall be exercisable by or under the authority of Parliament, and make such incidental and consequential provisions as appear to be necessary or desirable for giving effect to the objects of the Proclamation.

## Plain-English meaning

This is one of the most controversial emergency powers in the Constitution. It lets the Union intervene in a state, but courts have imposed limits because of the risk of partisan misuse.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Article 356 lies at the fault line between Union power and state autonomy. Its history shows why constitutional text alone is not enough without judicial limits and political restraint.
Reading the provision
The formal trigger is a failure of constitutional machinery in a state. In practice, that phrase can be stretched, which is why later case law became crucial to protect federalism.
How courts use it
The Supreme Court's scrutiny of Article 356 proclamations focuses on material, mala fides, and whether legislative majority should instead be tested on the floor of the assembly.
