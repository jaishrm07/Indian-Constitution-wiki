## Summary

Article 243I requires every State to constitute a Finance Commission to review the financial position of Panchayats and make recommendations on devolution of funds from the State's Consolidated Fund, including sharing of taxes, grants-in-aid, and the measures needed to improve the financial position of Panchayats.

## Official text

243I. Constitution of Finance Commission to review financial position of Panchayats.—(1) The Governor of a State shall, as soon as may be within one year from the commencement of the Constitution (Seventy-Third Amendment) Act, 1992, and thereafter at the expiration of every fifth year, constitute a Finance Commission to review the financial position of the Panchayats and to make recommendations to the Governor as to—

(a) the principles which should govern—

(i) the distribution between the State and the Panchayats of the net proceeds of the taxes, duties, tolls and fees levied by the State, which may be divided between them under this Part and the allocation between the Panchayats at all levels of their respective shares of such proceeds;

(ii) the determination of the taxes, duties, tolls and fees which may be assigned to, or appropriated by, the Panchayats;

(iii) the grants-in-aid to the Panchayats from the Consolidated Fund of the State;

(b) the measures needed to improve the financial position of the Panchayats;

(c) any other matter referred to the Finance Commission by the Governor in the interests of sound finance of the Panchayats.

(2) The Legislature of a State may, by law, provide for the composition of the Commission, the qualifications which shall be required to be possessed by members thereof and the manner in which they shall be selected.

(3) The Commission shall determine their procedure and shall have such powers in the performance of their functions as the Legislature of a State may, by law, confer on them.

(4) The Governor shall cause every recommendation made by the Commission under this article together with an explanatory memorandum as to the action taken thereon to be laid before the Legislature of the State.

## Plain-English meaning

Every State must set up a Finance Commission every five years (or more frequently) to examine how much money Panchayats need, recommend what share of State taxes should go to Panchayats, and advise on grants. This is the constitutional mechanism for ensuring Panchayats have independent revenue rather than being wholly dependent on State government discretion.

## Canonical site note

What this Article does
Article 243I is the financial constitution for local selfgovernment at the Panchayat level. It requires the Governor to constitute a State Finance Commission (SFC) within one year of the 73rd Amendment's commencement (April 1993) and at the expiration of every fifth year thereafter. The SFC performs for Panchayats what the Union Finance Commission under Article 280 performs for States in the UnionState fiscal relationship: it reviews the financial position of Panchayats, makes recommendations on how State tax revenues should be shared with Panchayats, what taxes Panchayats may themselves levy, what grantsinaid they should receive, and what measures would improve their overall financial health.
The article's structure is carefully calibrated. Clause (1) sets out the mandatory subjectmatter of SFC recommendations — distribution principles, assignable taxes, and grants. Clause (4) requires the Governor to lay both the recommendations and an explanatory memorandum on action taken before the State Legislature. This transparency requirement mirrors Article 281 for the Union Finance Commission, creating political accountability even though the recommendations are not legally binding.
The parallel provision for urban local bodies (Municipalities) is Article 243Y, which provides that the SFC constituted under Article 243I shall also review the finances of Municipalities.
The constitutional rationale: fiscal federalism for the third tier
Before the 73rd Amendment in 1992, Panchayats were creatures of State legislation with no constitutional protection for their finances. States could and routinely did create Panchayats as administrative extensions without independent funding, leaving local bodies entirely dependent on State grants that could be withheld or conditioned on political compliance. The 73rd Amendment inserted Part IX to give constitutional status to the third tier and Article 243I to give Panchayats a constitutionally mandated fiscal review mechanism.
The SFC's recommendations on tax sharing, assignable taxes, and grantsinaid are intended to give Panchayats predictable, rulebased revenue rather than discretionary State patronage. In practice, implementation has been uneven: several States have constituted SFCs belatedly or not at all, some have failed to act on SFC recommendations, and the amounts devolved remain modest compared to the functions assigned to Panchayats under the Eleventh Schedule. The Fifteenth Finance Commission specifically noted the gap between constitutional aspiration and fiscal reality in Statetolocal devolution.
Key cases
State of Rajasthan v. Rajasthan Panchayati Raj Commission (2010) — The Rajasthan High Court held that the obligation to constitute an SFC within the time specified is mandatory, not merely directory. A State government cannot lawfully proceed to constitute a Panchayat without putting in place the mandatory review mechanism that Article 243I requires.
Kishansing Tomar v. Municipal Council Ahmedabad (2006) — While principally about timely elections under Article 243E, the Supreme Court's broader observations about the constitutional character of thirdtier institutions apply to the SFC obligation: the mandatory constitutional text cannot be read down into a directory aspiration that States can comply with at their convenience.
Thirteenth and Fourteenth Finance Commission Reports — The Union Finance Commissions have repeatedly recommended that States strengthen their SFCs, provide for timely constitution, act on recommendations, and publish the explanatory memoranda required under Article 243I(4). These recommendations have been only partially implemented, illustrating the enforcement gap in the thirdtier fiscal framework.
Current significance
Panchayat finance remains one of the most significant unresolved dimensions of India's post1992 constitutional framework. The Fifteenth Finance Commission (202126) recommended Rs. 90,000 crore in grants to rural local bodies, conditioned in part on States constituting SFCs and publishing utilisation certificates. Several States have still not constituted their required SFCs or have SFCs whose reports are pending without action. The Article 243I(4) transparency requirement — laying the recommendation and action memorandum before the legislature — is routinely not observed.
The gap between the constitutional text of Article 243I and state practice is one of the most persistent failures of the post73rd Amendment decentralisation framework. Constitutional litigation compelling State compliance with the mandatory SFC requirement has been explored by scholars and civil society organisations as a potential route to better enforcement, building on the Kishansing Tomar precedent on mandatory constitutional time limits for local governance institutions.
