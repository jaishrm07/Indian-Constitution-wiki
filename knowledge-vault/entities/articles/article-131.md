## Summary

Article 131 gives the Supreme Court original jurisdiction over certain disputes between the Union and the States, or among States.

## Official text

Subject to the provisions of this Constitution, the Supreme Court shall, to the exclusion of any other court, have original jurisdiction in any dispute between the Government of India and one or more States, between the Government of India and any State or States on one side and one or more other States on the other, or between two or more States, if and in so far as the dispute involves any question on which the existence or extent of a legal right depends.

## Plain-English meaning

Some federal disputes do not begin in lower courts. The Constitution allows the Supreme Court to hear them directly when the existence or extent of a legal right is at stake.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Article 131 is one of the Constitution's most important federal dispute mechanisms. It shows that constitutional structure requires a direct forum for certain intergovernmental conflicts.
How courts use it
The provision matters when the Union and States clash over powers, legal rights, institutional obligations, or the constitutional validity of specific arrangements.
