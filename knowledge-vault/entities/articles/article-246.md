## Summary

Article 246 distributes legislative power between Parliament and state legislatures by reference to the Union, State, and Concurrent Lists.

## Official text

Parliament has exclusive power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List I in the Seventh Schedule. Parliament, and, subject to clause (1), the Legislature of any State also, have power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List III in the Seventh Schedule. Subject to clauses (1) and (2), the Legislature of any State has exclusive power to make laws for such State or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II in the Seventh Schedule.

## Plain-English meaning

This Article is one of the main entry points into Indian federalism. It tells you who can make law on which subject, and that answer often decides centre-state disputes.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Legislative competence disputes usually begin here. Article 246 is where constitutional structure translates into practical questions about taxation, regulation, administration, and overlap between different levels of government.
How courts use it
Courts read Article 246 together with the Seventh Schedule to resolve conflicts over legislative fields, repugnancy arguments, and the constitutional reach of central legislation.
