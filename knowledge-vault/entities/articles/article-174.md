## Summary

Article 174 empowers the Governor to summon, prorogue, and dissolve the State Legislature, subject to constitutional structure.

## Official text

The Governor shall from time to time summon the House or each House of the Legislature of the State to meet at such time and place as he thinks fit, but six months shall not intervene between its last sitting in one session and the date appointed for its first sitting in the next session. The Governor may prorogue the House or either House and dissolve the Legislative Assembly.

## Plain-English meaning

This Article gives the Governor formal power over legislative sessions and dissolution, but those powers cannot be read in isolation from democratic government, cabinet responsibility, and the limits of discretion.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Article 174 becomes central when disputes arise over assembly sessions, floor tests, government formation, and whether a Governor may shape legislative timing for constitutional rather than political reasons.
How courts use it
Courts read Article 174 together with responsible government. The key question is not whether the text gives a formal power, but how that power must operate inside the wider constitutional scheme.
