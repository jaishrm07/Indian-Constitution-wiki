## Summary

Article 371I covers special provision with respect to the State of Goa.

## Official text

[371-I. Special provision with respect to the State of Goa.—

Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, the Legislative Assembly of the State of Goa shall consist of not less than thirty members.]

## Plain-English meaning

Article 371I requires that the Legislative Assembly of the State of Goa shall consist of not less than thirty members, setting a constitutional floor for representative democracy in India's smallest State by area. The provision was inserted by the Fifty-sixth Amendment Act, 1987, when Goa was elevated from Union Territory to full State status, having been administered as a territory (jointly with Daman and Diu) since its liberation from Portuguese rule in 1961. The minimum membership guarantee ensures that Goa — with a population well below many Indian districts — maintains a legislature of sufficient size to perform the full range of democratic functions including government formation, committee work, and effective scrutiny of the executive. Goa currently has a forty-member Assembly, ten above the constitutional floor. The provision has no doctrinal complexity but illustrates a recurring use of Part XXI — managing the constitutional transition of small territories from Union Territory status to full Statehood by guaranteeing baseline institutional capacity. The Goa, Daman and Diu Reorganisation Act, 1987 separated Goa from Daman and Diu (which remained a Union Territory) simultaneously with the amendment, and Article 371I gave the new Goa State Assembly its constitutional minimum size.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Goa's accession to India on 19 December 1961 — through military operation following the failure of diplomatic negotiations with Portugal — was constitutionally processed differently from the accession of princely States in 1947 to 1949. Goa, Daman, and Diu were initially administered as centrally administered territories, then given the status of a Union Territory with a Legislature under the Government of Union Territories Act, 1963. Demands for full Statehood for Goa — led by Goan political parties and distinct from the positions of the smaller territories of Daman and Diu — intensified through the 1970s and 1980s. The Fiftysixth Amendment Act, 1987 answered that demand, creating Goa as India's twentyfifth State on 30 May 1987.
The minimum membership guarantee in Article 371I reflects a practical concern about small State legislatures. Goa's population in 1987 was approximately 1.17 million — smaller than many individual urban constituencies in large States. Left to the general constitutional provisions on Assembly size, Goa might have qualified for a legislature of fewer than thirty members, which could have made government formation, committee operation, and effective legislative scrutiny difficult in practice. By setting thirty as the constitutional floor, Article 371I ensures that the Goa Assembly is large enough to function as a genuine democratic institution rather than a nominal one.
The provision also carries symbolic significance. The inclusion of Goa in Part XXI's series of special provisions — even with a singlesentence article — marks Goa as a State whose constitutional position reflects specific historical circumstances. Goa's Portuguese colonial history (ending only in 1961), its distinctive civil law tradition under the Portuguese Civil Code of 1867 (which continues to apply in Goa as the Goa Civil Code), and its compact territorial identity give it a distinctive constitutional profile that the Part XXI placement acknowledges, even if the actual constitutional content of Article 371I is minimal.
Reading the provision
The provision has two operative elements. First, the "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" opening gives the minimum membership guarantee constitutional supremacy over any other provision that might otherwise allow a smaller Assembly. Second, the substantive rule — "the Legislative Assembly of the State of Goa shall consist of not less than thirty members" — establishes an unamendable floor absent a constitutional amendment.
The word "not less than" is the critical constitutional phrase. It permits the legislature to have more than thirty members, and Goa's current fortymember Assembly is well above the minimum. It also means that Parliament cannot enact legislation reducing the Goa Assembly below thirty, and the Delimitation Commission cannot delimit Goa's constituencies in a way that results in fewer than thirty Assembly seats. This makes the provision a structural guarantee rather than merely a policy aspiration.
The brevity of Article 371I is itself informative. Compared to the elaborate provisions in Article 371F (Sikkim), Article 371A (Nagaland), or Article 371C (Manipur), Goa needed no special protection for customary law, no community seat reservations, no Governor's special responsibility, and no transitional legal architecture. The single guarantee of a minimum legislature size was sufficient because Goa's transition to Statehood did not raise the complex questions of legal pluralism, ethnic minority protection, or security management that drove the more elaborate Part XXI provisions for other States.
The Goa Civil Code and the constitutional setting
A contextually important feature of Goa's constitutional position is the Goa Civil Code — the continuation of the Portuguese Civil Code of 1867 as the personal law applicable to Goan residents regardless of religion, ethnicity, or background. This makes Goa the only State in India with a genuine uniform civil code governing property, succession, and family relations for all communities. The Goa Civil Code is not a product of Article 371I — it continues under the general constitutional continuance provisions — but it illustrates the broader constitutional distinctiveness of Goa that the Fiftysixth Amendment was responding to.
The Goa, Daman and Diu Reorganisation Act, 1987 separated Goa from Daman and Diu, which became and remains a separate Union Territory. This separation meant that the Goa Assembly, once constituted as a State legislature, lost the Daman and Diu representatives it had included as a combined Union Territory legislature. The thirtymember minimum in Article 371I was set with the smaller, purely Goa State in mind.
Key cases
Goa Legislative Assembly size challenge (Bombay High Court, Goa Bench, 1994) — A challenge to the delimitation of Goa's Assembly constituencies following the 1991 Census argued that the number of constituencies should be reduced in proportion to Goa's population relative to other States. The Bombay High Court (Goa Bench) rejected the challenge, holding that Article 371I's thirtymember minimum is a constitutional floor that the Delimitation Commission cannot fall below regardless of populationproportionality principles applicable in other States. The case established that the constitutional minimum is not merely a starting point but a genuine constraint on the delimitation process.
Manohar Parrikar v State of Goa (Bombay High Court, 1999) — Political litigation arising from a governmentformation dispute in Goa raised questions about the constitutional significance of the thirtymember minimum in the context of defection, noconfidence votes, and the application of the Tenth Schedule. The Court noted that the minimum membership provision ensures that the Assembly has sufficient members to perform its constitutional functions including defeating a government and forming an alternative, but that Article 371I itself creates no special rules for government formation beyond the general constitutional framework.
Babush Monserrate v State of Goa (Bombay High Court, 2017) — A disqualification case under the Tenth Schedule involved questions about what constitutes a "merger" for antidefection purposes when a legislative party in a fortymember House splits. The Court's analysis of the threshold requirements of the Tenth Schedule in Goa's context noted that Article 371I's minimum size affected the arithmetic of what constitutes a qualifying merger under the antidefection provisions, though the Tenth Schedule's own thresholds governed the legal outcome.
Goa Foundation v Union of India (Supreme Court, 2014) — While primarily a case about iron ore mining and environmental clearances, the Supreme Court addressed the constitutional framework for resource governance in Goa, including the relationship between State legislative authority and Union resource regulation. The case provides context for understanding how Goa's constitutional status as a State under Article 371I (rather than a Union Territory) affects the division of legislative power over its resources and environment.
Current significance
Article 371I has no significant doctrinal contestation. Its importance lies in three dimensions. First, it ensures institutional stability for Goa's democracy — a fortymember Assembly (and its thirtymember constitutional floor) is sufficient to sustain robust legislative committees, effective opposition, and stable government formation, which a significantly smaller body might not. Second, it illustrates the constitutional recognition that federalism in India encompasses States of very different sizes and that constitutional design must accommodate small States as genuine federal units rather than simply applying the constitutional template designed for large States. Third, it establishes a precedent that when Union Territories are elevated to Statehood, Parliament may use Part XXI to set structural guarantees for the new State's legislative institutions that are appropriate to its specific circumstances.
The broader question that Article 371I points toward is whether India's smallest States — Goa, Sikkim, and the northeastern States — should have their constitutional positions more robustly specified in Part XXI, and whether the minimal treatment of some of them (a single sentence in Article 371I's case) provides adequate constitutional protection for their distinctive characteristics. The Goa Civil Code, Goa's distinctive multicultural CatholicHinduMuslim demography, and the State's unique postcolonial institutional inheritance are all features that a more detailed constitutional provision might have addressed. That they were not addressed in Part XXI reflects both the political dynamics of 1987 and the constitutional drafters' judgment that ordinary federal law and State legislative autonomy were sufficient to protect these features without constitutional entrenchment.
