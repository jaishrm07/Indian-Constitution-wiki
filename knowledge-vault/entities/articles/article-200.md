## Summary

Article 200 governs the Governor's assent, withholding of assent, return of Bills, and reservation of Bills for the President.

## Official text

When a Bill has been passed by the Legislature of a State, it shall be presented to the Governor and the Governor shall declare either that he assents to the Bill or that he withholds assent therefrom or that he reserves the Bill for the consideration of the President, with the constitutional power in certain cases to return a non-Money Bill for reconsideration.

## Plain-English meaning

When a State Bill reaches the Governor, the Constitution requires a constitutional choice: assent, withhold assent, return the Bill if permitted, or reserve it for the President. This provision matters because delay or strategic reservation can alter democratic lawmaking.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Article 200 is where ordinary state lawmaking can become a constitutional flashpoint. Assent disputes raise questions about federal balance, democratic accountability, and the permissible use of constitutional offices.
How courts and institutions use it
The provision matters whenever the argument shifts from legislative disagreement to constitutional obstruction, reservation of Bills, or delay in completing the lawmaking process.
