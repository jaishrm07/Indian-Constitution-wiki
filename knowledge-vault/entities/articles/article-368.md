## Summary

Article 368 sets out the formal process for amending the Constitution.

## Official text

Parliament may in exercise of its constituent power amend by way of addition, variation or repeal any provision of this Constitution in accordance with the procedure laid down in this article. An amendment of this Constitution may be initiated only by the introduction of a Bill for the purpose in either House of Parliament, and when the Bill is passed in each House by a majority of the total membership of that House and by a majority of not less than two-thirds of the members of that House present and voting, the Constitution shall stand amended in accordance with the terms of the Bill.

## Plain-English meaning

Parliament can amend the Constitution, but it cannot use that power to destroy the Constitution's basic structure. Article 368 is therefore both a grant of power and a site of constitutional limits.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
Article 368 defines how constitutional change happens. It also became the focal point for the basic structure doctrine, which prevents formal amendment power from becoming a licence for constitutional replacement.
Reading the provision
The Article describes procedures for amendment and, in some cases, ratification by states. The politics of amendment therefore tell a story not only about parliamentary numbers but also about constitutional design.
How courts use it
Debates around judicial review of amendments, institutional balance, and constitutional identity all converge on Article 368.
