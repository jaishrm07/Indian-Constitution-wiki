## Summary

The Constitution Bench treated Election Commission appointments as a constitutional-independence question and required a committee process until Parliament enacted a law under Article 324(2).

## Facts and issue

In the absence of a law made under Article 324(2), what appointment process best protects the constitutional independence of the Election Commission.

## Holding

The Court directed that appointments to the Election Commission should be made by a committee comprising the Prime Minister, the Leader of Opposition in the Lok Sabha, and the Chief Justice of India until Parliament enacted a law.

## Constitutional significance

Anoop Baranwal made clear that Election Commission appointments are constitutionally significant because electoral credibility depends on institutional independence.

## Canonical site note

Why the case matters now
This case forced appointment design into the constitutional foreground. Once Parliament legislated, the central question became whether the enacted model answers the independence concerns the Court identified or only changes their form.
Longterm impact
The judgment links electoral administration to broader constitutional ideas about impartial institutions, separation of powers, and safeguards against partisan control.
