## Summary

A five-judge Constitution Bench held that the recommendations of the GST Council are not binding on Parliament or State Legislatures, and confirmed that the GST framework rests on cooperative federalism in which both the Union and the States retain independent legislative power over goods and services tax.

## Facts and issue

Whether recommendations of the GST Council under Article 279A are binding on Parliament and State Legislatures, and whether the Union could validly levy IGST on ocean freight under a reverse charge mechanism by extending the tax to the Indian importer.

## Holding

GST Council recommendations are recommendatory in nature and not legally binding. The Union's attempt to impose IGST on ocean freight services supplied by foreign shipping lines to Indian importers through a reverse charge mechanism was held unconstitutional as it levied tax on a transaction outside India.

## Constitutional significance

Mohit Minerals authoritatively settled the federal character of the GST Council and established that cooperative federalism under the Constitution means genuine equality of legislative power between the Union and the States, not a system where Union preferences prevail through Council decisions.

## Canonical site note

Background
The Goods and Services Tax was introduced by the One Hundred and First Constitutional Amendment Act 2016, which inserted Articles 246A, 269A, and 279A into the Constitution. These provisions created a new constitutional architecture for indirect taxation in which both Parliament and State Legislatures have concurrent power to legislate on GST (Article 246A), interState supplies attract IGST collected by the Union with proceeds apportioned between Union and States (Article 269A), and a GST Council — comprising Union and State finance ministers — makes recommendations on all aspects of GST law and rates (Article 279A). Mohit Minerals Pvt. Ltd., a coal importer, challenged a Union notification that extended IGST to ocean freight services provided by foreign shipping companies on a cost, insurance and freight (CIF) basis. The notification treated the Indian importer as the service recipient in a reverse charge mechanism, making the importer liable for IGST on a service rendered entirely outside India. The High Court struck down the levy. The Union appealed to the Supreme Court, where the case became the vehicle for authoritatively interpreting the federal structure of the GST regime and the legal status of GST Council recommendations.
The legal question in detail
The case presented two interconnected questions. The first was structural and federal in nature: Article 279A(4) states that the GST Council "shall make recommendations" on a range of matters. The word "shall" appeared to impose an obligation on the Council to make recommendations, but the question was whether those recommendations, once made, were binding on Parliament and State Legislatures or merely advisory. The Union argued for binding effect, contending that the Constitution intended GST to be a uniform national tax and that allowing legislatures to deviate from Council recommendations would undermine the entire GST architecture. The States and the petitioner argued that the constitutional text gave the Council a recommendatory role only, and that treating recommendations as binding would impermissibly transfer legislative power from democratically accountable legislatures to an executive body not contemplated by the Constitution's core legislative scheme. The second question was more specific: could the Union extend IGST to ocean freight on the basis of a Council recommendation even if the underlying transaction — a service rendered by a foreign shipping company outside India — was not a supply of services "in the course of interState trade or commerce" within Article 269A?
What the court decided
A fivejudge bench comprising Justices D.Y. Chandrachud, Surya Kant, Vikram Nath, B.V. Nagarathna, and J.B. Pardiwala unanimously held that GST Council recommendations are not binding on Parliament or State Legislatures. The Court examined the constitutional text and found that Article 279A uses the language of recommendation, not direction or command. It rejected the argument that cooperative federalism — an ethos expressly underpinning the GST scheme — required binding Council decisions. Cooperative federalism means that the Union and States cooperate and reach consensus at the Council table, but the legislative choice whether to act on that consensus remains with each legislature. Binding Council decisions would convert the Council from a consultative body into a supralegislative authority, undermining parliamentary sovereignty and State legislative autonomy simultaneously. The Court also drew attention to the voting structure in Article 279A(9), which assigns the Union onethird of the total voting weight and States the remaining twothirds, and requires a threefourths majority for decisions — a structure that reflects parity and consensus, not Union dominance. On the specific ocean freight question, the Court held that the reverse charge mechanism used to levy IGST on Indian importers for a service entirely supplied and performed outside India was outside the constitutional competence conferred by Article 269A, which covers interState supplies within India. The levy was struck down.
Why the case remains central
Mohit Minerals is the definitive constitutional interpretation of the GST Council's role and the federal structure of GST. It confirmed that the Indian federal arrangement in the GST domain is one of genuine concurrent power — not a centralised regime dressed in federal clothing. The ruling has practical consequences for every future disagreement between the Union and States at the GST Council: States now have a constitutional basis to legislate differently from Council recommendations if their legislature so chooses, and the Union cannot treat Council decisions as automatically having the force of law. The case also contributes to the broader jurisprudence on cooperative federalism, which had been articulated in S.R. Bommai (1994) and subsequent cases in the context of President's Rule but had not previously been applied in detail to the fiscallegislative domain created by a major constitutional amendment.
Longterm impact
The judgment has been widely cited in subsequent GST litigation involving the scope of Article 269A, the validity of Statelevel GST notifications that deviate from Council recommendations, and the interpretation of "supply" in GST law. It strengthened the States' constitutional position in GST rate and structure negotiations, since the legal asymmetry that some Union officials had assumed — that Council decisions bind everyone — was definitively rejected. The case also reinforced the principle that constitutional amendments creating new institutional structures cannot be interpreted to vest those institutions with powers that the constitutional text does not expressly confer, particularly where doing so would erode the basic distribution of legislative authority between the Union and the States.
