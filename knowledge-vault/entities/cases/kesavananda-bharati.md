# Summary

Kesavananda Bharati is the central modern case on the amendment power. The Court held that Parliament may amend the Constitution, but it cannot destroy or damage its basic structure.

## Facts and issue

The case asked whether Article 368 allowed unlimited constitutional change. That question went beyond one amendment dispute and became a question about whether the Constitution has substantive identity that survives formal amendment.

## Holding

Parliament's amending power is broad, but not unlimited. Constitutional amendments cannot be used to dismantle the Constitution's basic structure.

## Constitutional significance

This case preserves constitutional continuity against formal legal change. It explains why amendment procedure alone is not enough to justify every constitutional redesign.
