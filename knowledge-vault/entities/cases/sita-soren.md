## Summary

The Court held that legislative privilege does not protect a legislator from prosecution for bribery tied to a speech or vote.

## Facts and issue

Whether Articles 105(2) and 194(2) immunize a legislator from criminal prosecution for accepting a bribe in connection with legislative speech or voting.

## Holding

The Constitution Bench held that bribery is not immunized by parliamentary or legislative privilege, that the offence is complete on acceptance of illegal gratification, and that the majority view in P.V. Narasimha Rao could not stand.

## Constitutional significance

Sita Soren is the leading modern authority narrowing privilege claims in bribery cases and restoring the distinction between legislative immunity and ordinary criminal law.

## Canonical site note

Why the case remains central
The Court drew a hard line between privilege and corruption. A legislator cannot treat privilege as a shield for taking a bribe, even if the bribe is linked to a promised speech or vote.
Longterm impact
The case is now the starting point for any discussion of legislative immunity, corruption, and the limits of P.V. Narasimha Rao. It also clarifies that the criminal law analysis turns on the completed offence of bribery, not on whether the promised act was ultimately performed.
