## Summary

The Court refused to order blanket disqualification of candidates facing criminal charges but directed stronger disclosure and public awareness measures to expose criminalisation of politics.

## Facts and issue

Whether the Court should bar candidates with criminal cases from contesting elections and, if not, what disclosure duties still follow from the Constitution.

## Holding

The Court declined to create a judicially imposed blanket disqualification rule, but it required criminal antecedent disclosure so that voters could assess candidates with fuller information.

## Constitutional significance

Public Interest Foundation is the leading modern criminalisation-of-politics case because it links candidate disclosure to democratic accountability while leaving disqualification reform to Parliament.

## Canonical site note

Why the case remains central
The judgment is the bridge between voterinformation cases and disqualification doctrine. It accepts that criminalisation of politics is a constitutional problem, but it leaves the architecture of disqualification to the legislature unless the Constitution itself says otherwise.
Longterm impact
The case still anchors later debates about candidate affidavits, criminal antecedents, and the limits of judicially crafted eligibility rules in a representative democracy.
