## Summary

The Court limited Parliament's power to amend fundamental rights and made the relationship between Article 368 and Part III sharply contested.

## Facts and issue

Whether Parliament could amend the Constitution to take away or abridge fundamental rights.

## Holding

The Court held that constitutional amendments could not be used to remove fundamental rights, treating amendment power as bounded by Part III.

## Constitutional significance

Golaknath is the major pre-Kesavananda amendment-power case and the key bridge between rights protection and basic-structure reasoning.

## Canonical site note

Why the case remains central
Golaknath is the decisive moment when the Court treated amendment power as a constitutional problem, not just a drafting problem.
Doctrinal line
The case pushed the Constitution toward the later Kesavananda settlement by insisting that formal amendment could not be read as unlimited constitutional replacement.
Longterm impact
Its immediate doctrinal force was later narrowed, but its influence survives in every serious discussion of amendment limits and rights protection.
