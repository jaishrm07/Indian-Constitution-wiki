## Summary

A five-judge Constitution Bench, by a 3:2 majority, struck down the practice of talaq-ul-biddat (triple talaq) as unconstitutional, holding that an instantaneous and irrevocable divorce pronounced by a Muslim husband in a single sitting was manifestly arbitrary and violated Article 14.

## Facts and issue

Whether the Muslim personal law practice of talaq-ul-biddat — the pronouncement of talaq three times in one sitting to effect an immediate and irrevocable divorce — violated the fundamental rights guaranteed under Articles 14, 15, 21, 25, and 26.

## Holding

The majority held that triple talaq is manifestly arbitrary and therefore violates Article 14. The concurring judge agreed in result. The practice was struck down by a 3:2 majority; Parliament subsequently enacted the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019 making triple talaq a criminal offence.

## Constitutional significance

Shayara Bano is a landmark intersection of personal law, constitutional equality, and religious freedom. It established that a practice within a religious personal law system can be struck down on Article 14 grounds if it is manifestly arbitrary, and it clarified the reach of Article 25(2)(b) as a basis for legislative reform of religious practice.

## Canonical site note

Background
Shayara Bano was married to Rizwan Ahmed in 2002. In 2016, her husband pronounced triple talaq by sending her a letter that contained the word "talaq" written three times. Under the traditional understanding of talaqulbiddat — an instantaneous form of divorce long held to be valid under the Hanafi school of Muslim personal law — this single act dissolved the marriage immediately and irrevocably. Shayara Bano filed a writ petition before the Supreme Court challenging the constitutional validity of the practice, arguing that it was a form of discrimination that arbitrarily ended the marriage without any scope for reconciliation, counselling, or challenge. Several women's rights organisations joined as parties or interveners on the petitioner's side. The Union of India took the position that the practice was manifestly arbitrary and deserved to be struck down. The All India Muslim Personal Law Board defended the practice as an integral part of Muslim personal law and argued that the constitutional courts lacked jurisdiction to scrutinise personal law, which is not "State law" for the purposes of Article 13. The case was heard by a fivejudge Constitution Bench comprising Chief Justice J.S. Khehar and Justices Kurian Joseph, R.F. Nariman, U.U. Lalit, and S. Abdul Nazeer.
The legal question in detail
Two foundational questions framed the entire proceedings. The first was jurisdictional: is Muslim personal law — which derives from religious scripture and has not been codified or enacted by a legislature — "law" within the meaning of Article 13(1) and (3), and therefore subject to the fundamental rights test? The second was substantive: if personal law is justiciable under Part III, does talaqulbiddat violate Articles 14, 15, 21, 25, or 26? On the jurisdictional question, the bench split. Chief Justice Khehar, joined by Justice Nazeer in the minority, held that Muslim personal law is not "law" made or enacted by the State and is therefore not amenable to challenge under Part III. Justices Nariman and Joseph for the majority relied on the fact that talaqulbiddat had been preserved and applied by courts under the Shariat Application Act 1937, a parliamentary enactment, and could therefore be tested as law within the meaning of Article 13. On the substantive question, the majority reasoning diverged in emphasis. Justice Nariman, joined by Justice Lalit, held that triple talaq was manifestly arbitrary within the meaning of Article 14 — a practice that permits an irrevocable and immediate dissolution of marriage on the unilateral whim of the husband, without cause, notice, or process — and could not survive constitutional scrutiny. Justice Joseph took a narrower path, holding that triple talaq was unIslamic, contrary to the teachings of the Quran itself, and therefore not protected under Article 25 as an essential religious practice. Article 25(2)(b) was also relevant: it expressly preserves the State's power to make laws providing for social welfare and reform even if they touch on religious practice.
What the court decided
The fivejudge bench produced three separate opinions resulting in a 3:2 outcome. Justices Nariman and Lalit (majority) held that triple talaq is manifestly arbitrary and violates Article 14. The 1937 Shariat Act had given triple talaq statutory status; that parliamentary sanction could not save a practice that was arbitrary on its face. Justice Joseph concurred in the result through a separate route, holding that the practice was not mandated by the Quran and therefore not an essential religious practice entitled to protection under Article 25. Chief Justice Khehar and Justice Nazeer dissented. They held that Muslim personal law — including triple talaq — is not "law" under Article 13 because it is not the product of State action. Constitutional courts cannot strike down religious personal law under Part III; only Parliament can regulate it, if at all, and it should do so sensitively. The Chief Justice's view was that the practice, while morally objectionable, was not amenable to constitutional invalidation and that the appropriate remedy was legislative action. The operative result — triple talaq is unconstitutional — carried by three judges on differing grounds. Parliament responded by enacting the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act 2019, which not only prohibits the practice but makes the pronouncement of triple talaq a criminal offence punishable with imprisonment of up to three years.
Why the case remains central
Shayara Bano is one of the most significant personal law cases in Indian constitutional history because it resolved — at least for a majority of the bench — the longcontested question of whether personal law can be subjected to fundamental rights scrutiny. For decades, courts had been reluctant to apply Part III to personal law, treating it as lying outside the domain of State action. The majority's reasoning, by treating the 1937 Shariat Act as the relevant legislative peg, found a path to constitutional scrutiny without formally overruling the older personal law immunity line of cases. The case also advanced the "manifest arbitrariness" doctrine under Article 14, which now operates as a distinct head of constitutional challenge to legislation and executive action — a practice or rule may violate Article 14 not merely because it discriminates between groups, but because it is inherently irrational or disproportionate.
Longterm impact
The immediate legislative response — the 2019 Act — went beyond striking down the civil effect of triple talaq and criminalised the very act of pronouncing it. This punitive dimension has itself become controversial, with critics arguing that criminalising a speech act within a matrimonial context is disproportionate and has been used for harassment rather than protection. The case also had procedural significance: it exposed the fragility of women's position within personal law regimes that operate without codification or legislative oversight. It has been cited in subsequent discussions of uniform civil code reform, the constitutional status of personal law more broadly, and the interpretation of Article 25's essential practices test. The 3:2 split and the divergent reasoning of the three majority judges means that the doctrinal foundations of the case remain contested and will likely require further elaboration by a larger or later bench.
