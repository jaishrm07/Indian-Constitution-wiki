## Summary

The Court reaffirmed limits on amendment power and emphasised the constitutional balance between Parts III and IV.

## Facts and issue

Whether provisions of the Forty-Second Amendment could immunise constitutional amendments from review and alter the balance between rights and directive principles.

## Holding

Parliament cannot convert limited amending power into unlimited power, and judicial review remains constitutionally central.

## Constitutional significance

Minerva Mills reinforced the basic structure doctrine after Kesavananda and treated constitutional balance itself as part of the constitutional order.

## Canonical site note

Why the case remains central
Minerva Mills is one of the clearest postKesavananda statements that amendment power is limited and that constitutional equilibrium matters.
Longterm impact
The case helps explain why constitutional change in India remains judicially reviewable even when it follows formal amendment procedure.
