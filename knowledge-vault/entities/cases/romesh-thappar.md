## Summary

The Court invalidated an overbroad pre-censorship law and established that freedom of speech includes the circulation of ideas and that laws restricting speech must fall within expressly permitted grounds.

## Facts and issue

Whether a state law that empowered the government to ban entry and circulation of a journal in the interest of public safety and public order was consistent with Article 19(1)(a) and the permissible restrictions in Article 19(2).

## Holding

The Court struck down the law as unconstitutional because public safety and public order were broader categories than the constitutionally permitted grounds of restriction, and the law could catch speech that posed no serious threat to the security of the state.

## Constitutional significance

Romesh Thappar is a foundational free speech ruling from the Supreme Court's earliest months, establishing that the grounds for restricting speech are exhaustively defined in the Constitution and cannot be supplemented by the state.

## Canonical site note

Background
Romesh Thappar edited and published a journal called "Cross Roads," printed in Bombay, which was broadly critical of the government of the time. In March 1950, the Government of Madras issued an order under Section 9(1A) of the Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1949, banning the entry and circulation of the journal throughout the State of Madras on the ground of public safety and the maintenance of public order. Thappar challenged the order by directly petitioning the Supreme Court under Article 32, arguing that the ban infringed his fundamental right to freedom of speech and expression guaranteed by Article 19(1)(a). The case came before the Supreme Court within months of the Constitution coming into force and was one of the earliest direct confrontations between state executive power and the newly guaranteed fundamental rights.
The legal question
The central constitutional question was whether the expression "security of the State" in Article 19(2) — which sets out the permissible grounds for restricting free speech — covered restrictions imposed in the name of "public safety" and "public order," which are broader and less demanding standards. Article 19(2) at the time allowed restrictions only for purposes related to security of the State, relations with foreign states, public order, decency, morality, or contempt of court, but required that restrictions be imposed by law and be related to those specific categories. If the law's operative term — public safety — reached more speech than the narrower constitutional category permitted, it would be overbroad and void. A related question was whether a law that could potentially restrict speech falling outside the constitutionally authorised grounds could be saved by arguing that in this particular case only permissible speech was targeted.
What the court decided
The Supreme Court, in a judgment by Patanjali Sastri J., held that the Madras Act was unconstitutional because the restriction it authorised — imposed in the interest of "public safety" and "public order" — was wider than what Article 19(2) permitted. The Court held that "public order" as used in the Madras Act was a capacious concept that could include minor disturbances of public tranquility, while the constitutional category of "security of the State" contemplated serious threats to the foundation of government. Because the law could be applied to restrict speech that posed no real threat to the security of the State, it was not saved by Article 19(2) and was therefore void. The Court rejected the argument that the law could be read down or that the particular application in Thappar's case should be assessed independently; the law was facially overbroad and could not stand. The judgment also confirmed that Article 19(2) provides an exhaustive list of the grounds on which speech may be restricted — the state cannot add new grounds by legislation. The overbreadth reasoning directly prompted Parliament to pass the First Constitutional Amendment in 1951, which added "public order," "friendly relations with foreign States," and "incitement to an offence" as additional permitted grounds of restriction under Article 19(2).
Significance
The judgment established from the outset that the Constitution's free speech guarantee is not rhetorical — the grounds for restriction are defined with precision, and a law that travels beyond those grounds is invalid regardless of the government's purpose. The overbreadth principle it applied remains central to evaluating speech restrictions: a law that captures both protected and unprotected speech cannot be saved by pointing to its application in particular cases. The ruling also shows how even early judicial decisions can reshape constitutional text, with Parliament responding within months through the First Amendment. It remains a touchstone case whenever courts consider the relationship between securityrelated legislation and the enumerated grounds in Article 19(2).
