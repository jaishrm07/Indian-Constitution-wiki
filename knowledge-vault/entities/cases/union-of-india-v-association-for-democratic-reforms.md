## Summary

The Court held that voters have a constitutional right to know basic information about candidates, including criminal antecedents, assets, liabilities, and educational background.

## Facts and issue

Whether voters can be denied basic candidate information when they exercise the right to choose their representatives.

## Holding

The Court treated voter information as part of Article 19(1)(a) and required disclosure of candidate background information so that electoral choice would be meaningful.

## Constitutional significance

This is the foundational Indian candidate-disclosure case and the starting point for constitutional debates about electoral transparency and informed democratic choice.

## Canonical site note

Why the case remains central
The judgment constitutionalized the idea that a voter cannot make a meaningful choice without basic information about candidates. Later affidavit rules, disclosure formats, and Election Commission portals all sit in its shadow.
Longterm impact
This remains the opening case for candidate transparency, the right to know in elections, and every later argument that disclosure is part of constitutional democracy rather than campaign policy.
