## Summary

The Court struck down the electoral-bonds scheme, holding that anonymous political funding impaired the voter's right to information and distorted constitutional democracy.

## Facts and issue

Whether the electoral-bonds scheme and related statutory amendments were compatible with the voter's right to information and the constitutional requirements of free and fair elections.

## Holding

The Court held that anonymous electoral-bond funding violated Article 19(1)(a) because it denied voters information essential to political choice, and it struck down the impugned scheme and amendments.

## Constitutional significance

The judgment reconnected campaign-finance regulation to constitutional transparency rather than treating political funding as a purely legislative policy matter.

## Canonical site note

Why the case matters now
The decision reset the constitutional baseline for politicalfinance transparency. It makes clear that party funding cannot be insulated from public scrutiny if that secrecy weakens democratic choice.
Longterm impact
Future campaignfinance reform will now be judged against this ruling's insistence that voter information is a constitutional value, not a policy afterthought.
