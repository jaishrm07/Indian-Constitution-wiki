## Summary

The Court treated a letter from an organisation working against bonded labour as a writ petition, elaborated the epistolary jurisdiction of the Supreme Court in public interest litigation, and held that Article 21 encompasses the right to live with basic human dignity, including freedom from forced labour.

## Facts and issue

Whether the Supreme Court could take cognisance of violations of the Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976 and constitutional guarantees against forced labour on the basis of a letter from a non-governmental organisation, and what relief could be granted.

## Holding

The Supreme Court may treat correspondence from any person or organisation identifying violations of constitutional rights as a writ petition; Article 21 guarantees the right to live with dignity and must be read with Articles 23 and 24 and relevant labour legislation to impose positive obligations on the state to identify, release, and rehabilitate bonded labourers.

## Constitutional significance

Bandhua Mukti Morcha is a foundational ruling of Indian public interest litigation jurisprudence, establishing that procedural barriers cannot stand in the way of constitutional rights enforcement for the poor and the marginalised, and that courts may issue structural directives to the executive to implement welfare legislation.

## Canonical site note

Background
Bandhua Mukti Morcha ("Bonded Labour Liberation Front") was a nongovernmental organisation working to identify and free bonded labourers — workers trapped in debt bondage who were compelled to work without wages to repay real or fictitious debts and could not leave their employers. Bonded labour had been formally abolished by the Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976, enacted to give effect to Articles 23 and 24 of the Constitution, which prohibit traffic in human beings and forced labour. Despite the statutory prohibition, the organisation found labourers continuing to work in conditions of bondage at stone quarries in Faridabad district in Haryana.
Unable to afford conventional litigation, the organisation wrote a letter to the Supreme Court describing conditions of bondage and requesting the court's intervention. Justice P.N. Bhagwati, who had been developing the practice of treating letters addressed to the Court as writ petitions in cases involving violations of fundamental rights of persons too poor or disadvantaged to approach the court formally, accepted the letter and directed it to be treated as a writ petition under Article 32. The case became one of the most significant early demonstrations of epistolary jurisdiction as a tool of constitutional rights enforcement.
The legal question
The primary procedural question was whether the Supreme Court could entertain a letter from a thirdparty organisation — one that was neither the victim nor a legally recognised representative of the victims — as a petition for enforcement of fundamental rights under Article 32. The established understanding of locus standi required the petitioner to be the aggrieved party; treating a letter from a third party as a writ petition required a fundamental relaxation of that rule. The substantive constitutional question was what rights the bonded labourers held under Part III and what positive obligations those rights imposed on the Union Government and the State Government of Haryana. The Court also had to decide what relief could be ordered: directing compliance with existing legislation, appointing commissioners to investigate conditions, or issuing structural directives to the executive were all options without clear precedent.
What the court decided
Justice Bhagwati, writing for the Court, held that the right of access to justice — and in particular access to the Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights under Article 32 — is itself a fundamental right, and that procedural rules of standing must be interpreted and applied in light of that guarantee. Where poor, illiterate, or otherwise disadvantaged persons are unable to approach the Court themselves, a publicspirited individual or organisation may do so on their behalf, and the Court will entertain such correspondence as a writ petition. This extended the principle of epistolary jurisdiction established in earlier cases and gave it explicit constitutional grounding.
On the substantive question, the Court held that Article 21's guarantee of the right to life includes the right to live with basic human dignity — which necessarily means freedom from servitude, access to minimum conditions of health and wellbeing, and the right to the minimum necessities of life. This right, read together with Article 23 (which prohibits forced labour and traffic in human beings) and Article 24 (which prohibits child labour in hazardous occupations), and reinforced by the Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976 and the Minimum Wages Act, imposed positive obligations on the state. It was not enough for the state to have enacted the legislation; the state was obliged to implement and enforce it.
The Court appointed commissioners to visit the quarries, directed the District Magistrate to identify and release bonded labourers, required the payment of minimum wages, and issued structural directives to the Union and State Governments to ensure rehabilitation of freed labourers. The judgment also elaborated on the role of commissioners and sociolegal investigators as instruments through which the Court could gather information about rights violations in circumstances where the traditional adversarial process was inadequate.
Significance
Bandhua Mukti Morcha is one of the clearest early articulations of the transformative potential of Indian public interest litigation. It demonstrated that Article 32 is not merely a passive guarantee of the right to approach the Court; the Court is constitutionally obliged to protect fundamental rights and can devise procedures and remedies appropriate to the nature of the violation and the vulnerability of the people affected. The decision established that courts could issue continuing structural directives to the executive — mandating implementation of welfare legislation, requiring periodic reporting, and appointing monitoring officers — in a manner quite unlike the binary relief of ordinary adversarial litigation. The reading of Article 21 as encompassing dignity and minimum conditions of life fed directly into the subsequent expansion of Article 21 in cases involving health, education, shelter, and environmental rights.
How to find the judgment
The judgment is available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in — search for Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India or use the citation (1984) 3 SCC 161. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts the judgment. Justice Bhagwati's opinion is the principal text and contains detailed discussion of both the procedural foundations of epistolary jurisdiction and the substantive content of Articles 21 and 23. It should be read alongside the Court's earlier decision in Hussainara Khatoon v. Home Secretary, State of Bihar (1979), which first deployed epistolary jurisdiction in the context of undertrial prisoners, to understand the trajectory of PIL in India.
