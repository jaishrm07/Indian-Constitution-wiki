## Summary

A five-judge Constitution Bench held 4:1 that the exclusion of women aged 10 to 50 from the Sabarimala temple violated their fundamental rights under Articles 14, 15, 25, and 26, applying and debating the essential religious practices test.

## Facts and issue

Whether the custom excluding women of menstruating age from the Sabarimala temple constituted an essential religious practice protected by Article 26(b), and whether it infringed women's fundamental rights under Articles 14, 15, and 25.

## Holding

The majority (4:1) held that the exclusion was not an essential religious practice and that it violated Articles 14, 15, and 25(1). Justice Indu Malhotra dissented, holding that the practice was an essential religious practice not open to constitutional review on its merits.

## Constitutional significance

The judgment is a landmark for women's rights, equality, and religious freedom. It prompted an immediate nine-judge reference on the scope of the essential religious practices doctrine and the relationship between denominational autonomy and non-discrimination guarantees.

## Canonical site note

Background
The Sabarimala temple in Kerala is a Hindu shrine dedicated to Lord Ayyappa. For generations, the Travancore Devaswom Board, which administers the temple, enforced a custom prohibiting the entry of women between the ages of approximately 10 and 50 — the years of menstruation. This exclusion was given statutory backing by Rule 3(b) of the Kerala Hindu Places of Public Worship (Authorisation of Entry) Rules, 1965, which permitted a restriction on women at stages of life at which the custom of the denomination so required. A public interest litigation was filed by the Indian Young Lawyers Association challenging the exclusion as unconstitutional discrimination. The matter was referred to a Constitution Bench to address fundamental questions about the scope of denominational religious autonomy, the essential religious practices doctrine, and the right of women to equal access to places of religious worship. The fivejudge bench delivered judgment in September 2018, producing four separate opinions.
The legal question
The central constitutional questions were: first, whether the exclusion of women of menstruating age from the temple constituted an "essential religious practice" protected by Article 26(b), shielding it from judicial review on the basis of equality; second, whether, even if it were such a practice, the exclusion violated Articles 14 and 15(1) as a discriminatory rule based on a physiological characteristic unique to women; third, whether Article 25(1) — the individual right to freely practise religion — entitled women to enter the temple independently of any denominational claim; and fourth, whether the devotees of Lord Ayyappa constituted a distinct religious denomination with the autonomy to set its own rules for entry. The case required the Court to confront the tension between the Constitution's commitment to equality and nondiscrimination on the one hand, and its protection of religious denominational autonomy on the other.
What the court decided
The majority (four judges — Chief Justice Dipak Misra, Justice R.F. Nariman, Justice A.M. Khanwilkar, and Justice D.Y. Chandrachud) held that the exclusion was unconstitutional. The majority found that the exclusion was not an essential practice of the religion and could not claim protection under Article 26(b). It further held that the devotees of Lord Ayyappa did not constitute a separate religious denomination distinct from Hinduism, and therefore the denominational autonomy protection of Article 26 was not engaged. The majority grounded the unconstitutionality primarily in Articles 14 and 15: treating women of a specific age group as impure on account of menstruation was discriminatory on grounds of sex and physiological traits. Chief Justice Misra and Justice Khanwilkar also held that the right to practise religion under Article 25(1) extended equally to women, and that this right could not be curtailed by a custom resting on physiological considerations. Justice Chandrachud went furthest, holding that untouchability in Article 17's sense could extend to menstrual exclusion and that constitutional morality must prevail over popular morality.
Justice Indu Malhotra dissented. She held that the practice was indeed an essential religious practice of the Ayyappa tradition, that the devotees did constitute a denomination, and that in matters of religion the Court should exercise restraint rather than substituting its view of what is essential for the denomination's own understanding. Her dissent articulated a significant argument for judicial deference in religious affairs and a warning about the risks of courts determining what is and is not core to a religious community's practice.
Significance
The judgment broke new ground on the relationship between equality rights and denominational autonomy, and was the first occasion on which the Supreme Court directly held a temple exclusion based on sex to be constitutionally invalid. The deep disagreement among the five judges — and in particular the force of Justice Malhotra's dissent — reflected how contested the essential religious practices doctrine had become. Almost immediately after the judgment, the Supreme Court ordered a ninejudge reference to reconsider the essential religious practices test more broadly, alongside related questions arising from cases concerning entry into mosques by women and the practice of female genital cutting in certain communities. That reference, still pending, makes the Sabarimala case a pivot point in Indian constitutional law on religion and equality rather than a settled endpoint.
How to find the judgment
The primary text is available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in using the citation (2019) 11 SCC 1. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts the judgment. The four majority opinions and Justice Malhotra's dissent each address different aspects; Justice Chandrachud's concurrence and Justice Malhotra's dissent are the most frequently cited for the competing constitutional frameworks. Read alongside Shirur Mutt (AIR 1954 SC 282) for the essential religious practices doctrine's origins and the ninejudge reference order for the unresolved trajectory of the law.
