## Summary

The Court responded to an epidemic of custodial deaths by laying down a mandatory set of procedural requirements for arrest and interrogation, holding that police power must operate within constitutional limits and that custodial rights violations attract compensation.

## Facts and issue

Whether custodial torture and death violated Article 21, what constitutional obligations the police bore when making arrests and conducting interrogation, and whether compensation was an appropriate constitutional remedy for custodial rights violations.

## Holding

Custodial violence and death violate Article 21 and are constitutionally intolerable; the Court prescribed mandatory procedural requirements governing arrest and detention, and held that the state is liable to pay compensation to victims of custodial rights violations.

## Constitutional significance

D.K. Basu translated the constitutional guarantee of life and dignity into a concrete procedural code for police conduct, establishing that the manner of exercise of police power is itself a constitutional question and that courts may prescribe enforceable standards to prevent custodial abuse.

## Canonical site note

Background
D.K. Basu, the Executive Chairman of Legal Aid Services, West Bengal, wrote to the Chief Justice of India drawing attention to newspaper reports of deaths in police lockups and custodial torture across the country. The letter was treated as a writ petition under Article 32. The Court called for reports from state governments and Union Territories, and the material gathered revealed that custodial violence was widespread, systematic, and rarely prosecuted. Several cases of custodial death were brought to the Court's attention during the proceedings, underscoring that existing legal mechanisms — criminal prosecution, departmental action, and tort law — were failing to deter or adequately remedy custodial abuse. The case presented the Court with the question of what direct constitutional obligations the police owed to persons in their custody, and how those obligations could be made effective in practice.
The legal question in detail
The central question was whether custodial torture and death constituted violations of Article 21's guarantee of life and personal liberty, and if so, what remedies the Constitution provided. A related question was whether Article 22 — which confers specific protections on arrested persons — exhausted the Constitution's concern with custodial rights, or whether Article 21's broader guarantee imposed additional obligations. The Court also had to determine whether it could prescribe, as a constitutional matter, specific procedural requirements for arrest and interrogation that would go beyond existing statutory provisions and be binding on police forces nationwide. The question of monetary compensation as a constitutional remedy for custodial rights violations — distinct from criminal prosecution and departmental discipline — also arose.
What the court decided
Justice Kuldip Singh, writing for a twojudge bench, held that custodial violence is a gross violation of human dignity and an affront to Article 21. The Court held that the state bears absolute responsibility for the life and bodily integrity of persons in its custody, and that custodial death is among the gravest violations the state can commit. The Court then prescribed a set of requirements — now known as the D.K. Basu guidelines — that are mandatory for all police and investigative agencies. These included: that the person making the arrest must bear accurate, visible identification and prepare an arrest memo attested by a family member or witness; that the arrested person must be informed of the right to have a friend, relative, or nominated person informed of the arrest; that such information must actually be communicated promptly; that the arrested person must be made aware of the right to be examined by a medical officer; that records of the arrest must be maintained in a register; that the arrested person must be examined for injuries at the time of arrest; and that the arrested person must be produced before a magistrate within twentyfour hours. The Court further held that compensation is a public law remedy available to victims of custodial rights violations, independently of any civil or criminal proceedings.
Why the case remains central
The judgment matters because it demonstrates that constitutional rights impose affirmative procedural obligations on agents of the state, and that the Court can prescribe detailed institutional requirements to make those obligations effective. The D.K. Basu guidelines operate as constitutional minimum standards below which police conduct cannot fall, and their violation is itself a constitutional wrong that courts can redress. The case also reinforced the principle — developed in earlier Article 21 cases — that the manner in which state power is exercised is a constitutional question, not merely an administrative one. By making compensation available as a constitutional remedy, the Court acknowledged that criminal prosecution and departmental inquiry were insufficient responses to systemic custodial abuse.
Longterm impact
The D.K. Basu guidelines were codified in large part through the Code of Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Act, 2008, which inserted Section 41B and related provisions directly into the statute. The case is now routinely cited in bail, habeas corpus, and custodial rights proceedings, and the guidelines are treated as the baseline against which police conduct in arrest situations is measured. In subsequent cases the Supreme Court has reiterated and expanded on the D.K. Basu framework, and lower courts have awarded compensation in custodial rights cases relying directly on its authority. The judgment contributed significantly to the development of Indian public law remedies, establishing that monetary compensation for constitutional violations is not confined to special statutory schemes but is available directly under Article 32 and Article 226.
How to find the judgment
The primary text is available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in using the citation (1997) 1 SCC 416. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts the judgment. Justice Kuldip Singh's opinion contains both the full statement of the D.K. Basu guidelines and the reasoning on compensation as a constitutional remedy; the guidelines themselves are set out as a numbered list toward the end of the judgment.
