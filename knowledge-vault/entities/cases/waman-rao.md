## Summary

The Court drew a temporal line for Ninth Schedule immunity, holding that laws placed in the Schedule before 24 April 1973 are immune from Part III challenge while those placed after that date remain open to basic structure review.

## Facts and issue

Whether all laws placed in the Ninth Schedule are equally immune from fundamental rights challenge regardless of when they were added, and whether Kesavananda Bharati altered the scope of that immunity.

## Holding

Laws inserted into the Ninth Schedule on or before 24 April 1973 retain their immunity from Part III challenge; laws inserted after that date are subject to basic structure review by virtue of the Kesavananda doctrine.

## Constitutional significance

Waman Rao established the foundational temporal bright line for Ninth Schedule immunity that was later affirmed and elaborated by I.R. Coelho, and remains the starting point for any challenge to a Ninth Schedule law.

## Canonical site note

Background
Waman Rao arose from a cluster of petitions challenging various land reform and agricultural tenancy laws that had been placed in the Ninth Schedule by constitutional amendments enacted after the Supreme Court's 1973 judgment in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala. The Ninth Schedule was introduced by the First Amendment in 1951 alongside Article 31B, which declared that laws specified in the Schedule shall not be void on grounds of inconsistency with Fundamental Rights. The device was originally used to protect land reform legislation from property rights challenges, but Parliament continued to expand the Schedule over subsequent decades. After Kesavananda established that constitutional amendments are subject to basic structure review, a fundamental uncertainty arose: did amendments adding laws to the Ninth Schedule after 1973 themselves constitute a form of amendment that could be reviewed for basic structure compliance? A ninejudge Constitution Bench was convened to resolve this question.
The legal question in detail
The central question was whether the absolute immunity conferred by Article 31B remained intact after Kesavananda, or whether that doctrine — which holds all constitutional amendments subject to basic structure review — required the Court to scrutinise amendments that placed laws in the Ninth Schedule after 24 April 1973. A secondary question was whether pre1973 Ninth Schedule laws, added before Kesavananda articulated the basic structure doctrine, could also be reopened for review. The petitioners argued that since Kesavananda subjected all post1973 amendments to basic structure review, the constitutional amendments by which laws were added to the Schedule after that date had to satisfy the same test. The Union of India maintained that Article 31B's immunity was clear and that the amendment power exercised to place laws in the Schedule was in any event valid. The Court also had to consider whether Golaknathera additions to the Schedule — between 1967 and 1973 — occupied a special position.
What the court decided
The ninejudge bench held that laws placed in the Ninth Schedule on or before 24 April 1973 are fully protected by Article 31B and cannot be challenged on the ground that they abridge or take away any of the Fundamental Rights. The date 24 April 1973 — the date on which the Kesavananda Bharati judgment was delivered — was fixed as the constitutional dividing line. Laws added to the Schedule before that date predated the articulation of the basic structure doctrine and were entitled to rely on the unconditional immunity that Article 31B provided in its original constitutional context. Laws inserted after 24 April 1973, however, are not automatically immune: since Kesavananda had established that post1973 amendments are subject to basic structure review, a constitutional amendment adding a law to the Schedule after that date must itself survive that review. If the amendment abrogates or nullifies a Fundamental Right that forms part of the basic structure, the amendment — and consequently the Schedule placement — can be invalidated. The Court accordingly upheld the pre1973 Schedule entries challenged before it but left the door open for challenge to post1973 entries.
Why the case remains central
Waman Rao created the practical framework within which all Ninth Schedule immunity arguments are now conducted. Before this decision, the relationship between the absolute text of Article 31B and the new constitutional order established by Kesavananda was uncertain. By fixing 24 April 1973 as the operative cutoff, the Court resolved that uncertainty in a manner that respected the legitimate expectations of preKesavananda legislation while preserving the logic of basic structure review for subsequent amendments. The case is also notable for demonstrating the bench's recognition that Kesavananda's holding on amendment power had retroactive constitutional significance in the sense of governing the validity of all subsequent exercises of that power, even where the constitutional provision being invoked preexisted the doctrine.
Doctrinal line
Waman Rao is located at the intersection of two doctrinal streams: the history of property rights litigation that produced the First Amendment and Article 31B, and the basic structure doctrine established by Kesavananda. It shows how the basic structure doctrine operates prospectively on all constitutional amendment activity following its articulation. The 24 April 1973 cutoff is not merely a practical convenience; it reflects the Court's view that constitutional amendments derive their validity from the constitutional order existing at the time of their enactment, and that the Kesavananda order fundamentally changed what that constitutional order required. This line of reasoning was subsequently confirmed and extended in Minerva Mills v. Union of India (1980), decided the year before Waman Rao, and was definitively applied to the Ninth Schedule context by the ninejudge bench in I.R. Coelho v. State of Tamil Nadu (2007).
Longterm impact
The temporal line established in Waman Rao has been accepted by the Supreme Court in every subsequent case involving Ninth Schedule immunity, including I.R. Coelho, which made it the foundation of the "rights test" for post1973 entries. Any assessment of whether a specific Ninth Schedule entry is vulnerable to challenge must begin by asking whether it was placed there before or after 24 April 1973. Laws added before that date — the vast bulk of land reform and agrarian legislation from the 1950s and 1960s — remain constitutionally secure under Article 31B. Laws added after that date, including numerous economic regulation and reservationrelated statutes placed in the Schedule from the late 1970s onward, are potentially open to challenge if they destroy rights under Articles 14, 19, 20, or 21 that are themselves elements of the basic structure.
