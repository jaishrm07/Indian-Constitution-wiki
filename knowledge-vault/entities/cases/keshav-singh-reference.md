## Summary

The Court's advisory opinion set out the constitutional boundary between legislative privilege and judicial review, especially when proceedings are alleged to be illegal or unconstitutional.

## Facts and issue

Whether legislative proceedings and privilege claims can be scrutinized by courts when the challenge is to legality rather than mere procedural irregularity.

## Holding

The Court held that proceedings suffering only from procedural irregularity are protected, but illegal or unconstitutional proceedings remain open to judicial scrutiny.

## Constitutional significance

Keshav Singh is the foundational Indian privilege reference on the courts' power to examine legislative action, and it still underpins later privilege cases.

## Canonical site note

Why the reference remains central
The opinion remains the classic statement that privilege is real, but not absolute. Courts may not interfere with mere irregularity inside the House, yet they can examine illegality and constitutional breach.
Longterm impact
Later privilege cases repeatedly return to this reference when drawing the line between internal legislative autonomy and judicial review, including modern disputes over discipline, contempt, and constitutional limits.
