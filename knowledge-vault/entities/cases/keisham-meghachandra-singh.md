## Summary

The Supreme Court held that the Speaker's failure to decide disqualification petitions within a reasonable time was subject to judicial mandamus, and imposed a three-month deadline.

## Facts and issue

Whether courts can compel a Speaker to decide disqualification petitions within a time-bound manner under the Tenth Schedule.

## Holding

Undue delay by a Speaker in deciding anti-defection petitions can be remedied by courts, which may impose a reasonable timeline, typically three months, for deciding such petitions.

## Constitutional significance

Keisham Meghachandra Singh significantly strengthened judicial oversight of Speaker delay in anti-defection proceedings and operationalised the right to timely legislative justice under the Tenth Schedule.

## Canonical site note

Why the case remains central
The judgment converted the principle established in Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil into a concrete enforceable standard by specifying three months as the benchmark for reasonable decisionmaking by a Speaker on disqualification petitions. It directly addressed the recurring pattern in which Speakers allow petitions to linger indefinitely, turning delay itself into a political tool that benefits the ruling party.
Longterm impact
By grounding judicial intervention in mandamus rather than an appeal on the merits of the disqualification decision, the Court preserved the Tenth Schedule's exclusive adjudicatory role for Speakers while simultaneously ensuring that role could not be weaponised through inaction. The threemonth norm has since been cited in multiple statelevel antidefection proceedings as the standard against which Speaker conduct is measured.
How to find the judgment
The primary text is available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in using the citation (2020) 2 SCC 598. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts the judgment. The threemonth timeline direction for Speakers deciding disqualification petitions is the operative holding; read alongside Shrimanth Balasaheb Patil (2020) 2 SCC 595 as a companion case decided on the same day.
