## Summary

The Court shifted rights analysis toward the real effect of state action on the affected person rather than the formal label of the law.

## Facts and issue

Whether the bank nationalisation measure could be judged only by its stated object or also by its direct effect on constitutional rights.

## Holding

The Court rejected formalism and held that constitutional review must examine the effect of state action on rights and interests.

## Constitutional significance

R.C. Cooper is a major transition case because it helped move Indian rights analysis from form to substance and shaped later liberty doctrine.

## Canonical site note

Why the case remains central
This case matters because it refused to let the state defend constitutional action by choosing the right label. The Court asked what the law actually did to rights holders.
Doctrinal line
That effectbased approach became one of the foundations for later fairness and dignity reasoning, including the wider turn in Maneka Gandhi.
Longterm impact
The case remains important in economicrights debates, compensation disputes, and any argument where state power is defended through formal wording rather than practical consequences.
