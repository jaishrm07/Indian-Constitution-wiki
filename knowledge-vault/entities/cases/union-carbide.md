## Summary

The Court approved a negotiated settlement of Rs. 470 crore between the Union of India and Union Carbide Corporation for the Bhopal gas disaster, invoking Article 142 to grant the settlement the force of a decree, and upheld its constitutionality — a decision that remains deeply contested for the adequacy of compensation and the extinguishment of criminal liability.

## Facts and issue

Whether the Supreme Court could, under Article 142, impose a comprehensive settlement of all civil and criminal claims arising from the Bhopal gas disaster, and whether doing so was constitutionally valid and consistent with the rights of the victims under Article 21.

## Holding

The Court held that it possessed the power under Article 142 to make a "complete justice" settlement binding on all parties including the victims and the state, that the settlement amount was adequate, and that quashing the criminal proceedings against Union Carbide was within the Court's curative jurisdiction — though the quashing order was later reviewed and partially reversed.

## Constitutional significance

The Bhopal settlement raised foundational questions about the use of Article 142 to override victim rights, the constitutional accountability of the state for mass industrial disasters caused by foreign corporations, and whether the Indian constitutional framework adequately protected the right to life against corporate negligence of a catastrophic scale.

## Canonical site note

Background
On the night of 23 December 1984, the Union Carbide pesticide plant in Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh released approximately forty tonnes of methyl isocyanate gas into the surrounding air. The gas cloud drifted over densely populated neighbourhoods, killing several thousand people immediately and tens of thousands more in subsequent years from respiratory failure, organ damage, and related illnesses. Estimates of total deaths range from 15,000 to 25,000; over half a million people were exposed to the toxic gas, and hundreds of thousands suffered permanent injuries. It remains the worst industrial accident in history.
Union Carbide Corporation (UCC), a United States multinational, owned a majority stake in Union Carbide India Limited (UCIL), which operated the plant. The Indian government passed the Bhopal Gas Leak Disaster (Processing of Claims) Act, 1985, making the Union of India the sole representative of all victims in legal proceedings before Indian and foreign courts. This effectively removed the victims' own right to bring independent actions and vested control of the litigation in the state.
Prolonged negotiations between the Union of India and UCC followed. In February 1989, the Supreme Court announced a settlement under which UCC agreed to pay Rs. 470 crore (USD 470 million) in full and final settlement of all civil and criminal liability arising from the disaster. The settlement was presented to the Court as the product of conciliation and was given the force of a binding decree under Article 142. The criminal proceedings pending against Union Carbide and its officers — including charges of culpable homicide — were simultaneously quashed as part of the settlement.
The legal questions
Three principal constitutional questions arose from the settlement. First, whether Article 142 — which empowers the Supreme Court to make "such order as is necessary for doing complete justice" in any matter pending before it — could be used to extinguish the rights of persons (the victims) who were not parties before the Court and had not consented to the settlement. The victims and survivors' groups argued that the settlement was imposed upon them, not negotiated by them, and that an Article 142 order could not override the substantive rights of third parties.
Second, whether the quashing of criminal proceedings against Union Carbide and its officers — including Warren Anderson, the company's chairman at the time — was within the Court's power under Article 142, or whether it violated the constitutional principle that criminal liability cannot be extinguished by a civil settlement, particularly without the consent of the state prosecutorial machinery and the victims.
Third, whether the compensation amount of Rs. 470 crore was constitutionally adequate to vindicate the victims' right to life under Article 21, or whether the state, by negotiating a low settlement and relinquishing criminal accountability, had itself violated the constitutional obligation to protect life and provide effective remedies.
What the court decided
The Supreme Court, in its 1991 judgment, upheld the constitutional validity of the settlement. The Court held that the Union of India, acting as the parens patriae of the victims under the 1985 Act, had the authority to negotiate and settle their claims. It accepted the government's position that Rs. 470 crore was a reasonable and adequate amount given the uncertainties of international litigation against a foreign corporation. The Court invoked Article 142 to give the settlement finality and confirmed that it operated as a complete discharge of all liability — civil and criminal — arising from the disaster.
On the quashing of criminal proceedings, the Court initially upheld this as part of the comprehensive settlement. However, the curative petition filed by the Central Bureau of Investigation and survivor groups led the Court in 1991 to modify its earlier direction: it held that the quashing of criminal cases had gone too far and could not be sustained, and it restored the criminal proceedings. The criminal cases against UCIL's Indian officers subsequently resulted in convictions carrying minimal sentences in 2010 — widely criticised as grossly inadequate. The criminal case against UCC's American directors, including Warren Anderson (who died in 2014 without facing trial), was never resolved.
Why the case is contested
The Union Carbide settlement is one of the most criticised decisions in the history of the Indian Supreme Court, and the criticism comes from multiple directions.
On compensation adequacy, survivor and public health groups documented that the Rs. 470 crore translated to approximately Rs. 25,000 per deceased victim and substantially less per injured survivor — sums that bore no relation to the actual medical costs, lost livelihoods, or longterm health consequences borne by victims. The Court's acceptance of the government's adequacy assessment was criticised as deferential and insufficiently rigorous, particularly given the scale of the catastrophe and the financial resources of Union Carbide.
On the constitutional use of Article 142, the settlement raised a structural objection: the "complete justice" power cannot be used to do injustice to absent third parties. The victims of the Bhopal disaster, whose constitutional right to life had been most directly violated, had no voice in the settlement; the state that negotiated the settlement on their behalf had manifestly different institutional interests in closing the litigation. Using Article 142 to bind victims to a settlement they could neither accept nor reject conflated judicial power with executive bargaining in a manner that extinguished rather than vindicated constitutional rights.
On criminal accountability, the quashing — and even after its partial reversal, the inadequate eventual convictions — demonstrated the practical limits of constitutional rights protection when the accused is a powerful foreign multinational. The right to life under Article 21 is held to impose a positive duty on the state to protect life against nonstate threats and to ensure effective prosecution where violations occur; the Bhopal outcome raised serious doubts about whether this duty was discharged.
On continuing harm, the Bhopal plant site was never adequately remediated. Groundwater contamination persists decades later, and the health effects of the disaster continue to affect the second generation of residents in the area. The settlement made no provision for environmental cleanup or longterm health monitoring, and its finality has been invoked to resist subsequent claims for remediation costs.
Longterm impact
The case forced a reckoning — never fully resolved — with the adequacy of the Indian legal system to address mass industrial disasters involving foreign corporations and to ensure that constitutional rights are not bargained away through statecontrolled settlements. It led to significant amendments to the Environment Protection Act and the creation of the National Green Tribunal, and to ongoing demands for mandatory liability insurance for hazardous industries.
The use of Article 142 in the Bhopal context has influenced subsequent jurisprudence on the scope and limits of the Court's completejustice power, with later decisions grappling with whether Article 142 can override statute, thirdparty rights, or legislative policy. The Bhopal case is routinely cited as the cautionary instance of Article 142 being deployed in a manner that compromised rather than secured substantive justice.
The criminal proceedings against UCIL's Indian managers — decided by the Bhopal district court in 2010, resulting in twoyear sentences for negligence — are widely regarded as a failure of accountability that compounded the injustice of the civil settlement.
How to find the judgment
The 1989 settlement order and the 1991 review judgment are available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in using the citation (1991) 4 SCC 584. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts these judgments. The review judgment modifying the quashing of criminal proceedings — reported at (1991) 4 SCC 584 — should be read alongside the original settlement order. Justice Ranganath Misra's opinion presided over the settlement; the subsequent review bench opinion is the key text for understanding the limits the Court recognised on its own earlier order. The case must be read critically alongside the voluminous academic and activist literature documenting the gap between the settlement and the actual harm suffered by the people of Bhopal.
