## Summary

The Court held that the power of judicial review vested in the High Courts under Article 226 is part of the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be excluded or ousted by parliamentary legislation creating statutory tribunals.

## Facts and issue

Whether clauses in Articles 323A and 323B enabling Parliament to exclude the jurisdiction of High Courts over service tribunals and other statutory tribunals are constitutionally valid.

## Holding

The power of High Courts under Article 226 and of the Supreme Court under Article 32 to exercise judicial review is a basic structure element; parliamentary legislation cannot divest courts of that power, and tribunals must function as supplementary rather than substitute forums.

## Constitutional significance

L. Chandra Kumar placed a constitutional ceiling on Parliament's power to create alternative adjudicatory systems that displace regular courts, ensuring that the superior courts retain supervisory jurisdiction over every tribunal established by law.

## Canonical site note

Background
The Constitution (42nd Amendment) Act 1976 inserted Articles 323A and 323B, empowering Parliament and state legislatures to create administrative tribunals for service matters and a range of other subjects including elections, industry, and land reforms. The Administrative Tribunals Act 1985 was enacted under Article 323A and established the Central Administrative Tribunal (CAT) as the exclusive forum for Central Government service disputes, expressly barring the jurisdiction of civil courts and High Courts. Several other statutes under Article 323B followed a similar pattern.
Aggrieved government servants who were denied relief by the CAT attempted to challenge its orders before High Courts under Article 226 but found the route statutorily blocked. Questions arose about whether Parliament could validly exclude the supervisory jurisdiction of constitutional courts when creating these new bodies, or whether the power of the High Courts to issue writs was protected from legislative interference. A sevenjudge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court was assembled to resolve the conflict, overruling in the process the Court's earlier decision in S.P. Sampath Kumar (1987) which had upheld the exclusion clauses.
The legal question
The central question was whether clauses in Articles 323A(2)(d) and 323B(3)(d), which explicitly permitted Parliament to exclude the jurisdiction of all courts except the Supreme Court under Article 136 over matters falling within tribunal jurisdiction, were constitutionally valid. A connected question was whether such exclusion clauses, even if textually permitted by the amended Constitution, ran afoul of the basic structure doctrine because they removed the power of High Courts to exercise judicial review — a power that the Court had previously identified as forming part of the basic structure in Kesavananda Bharati (1973) and subsequent cases. The Court also had to decide whether the tribunals created under Articles 323A and 323B satisfied constitutional requirements of independence and institutional competence sufficient to justify any displacement of High Court jurisdiction.
What the court decided
The sevenjudge Constitution Bench, in a judgment delivered by Justice S.P. Bharucha, held unanimously that the power of judicial review vested in the High Courts under Article 226 and in the Supreme Court under Article 32 is a basic structure element of the Constitution. Because it forms part of the basic structure, Parliament cannot, through ordinary legislation or even through constitutional amendment, completely exclude that power. The clauses in Articles 323A(2)(d) and 323B(3)(d) that purported to entirely oust High Court jurisdiction were accordingly read down and declared unconstitutional to that extent.
The Court held that tribunals created under Articles 323A and 323B are constitutional and may function as the court of first instance in their designated domains; they are not inherently invalid. However, all such tribunals must function under the supervisory jurisdiction of the relevant High Court under Article 226. Decisions of tribunals are therefore subject to writ jurisdiction of the High Court in whose territorial jurisdiction the tribunal sits, and from the High Court to the Supreme Court under Article 136. The Court further held that to discharge even this supplementary role properly, tribunals must be composed of members with adequate legal training; the presence of administrative members alone without trained judicial members was held insufficient for adjudicating questions of law.
Significance
L. Chandra Kumar established that the creation of specialised adjudicatory bodies by Parliament — however legitimate as a matter of policy — cannot result in the removal of judicial oversight by constitutional courts. Every statutory tribunal in India, from the National Green Tribunal to the Income Tax Appellate Tribunal to the various securities and competition appellate bodies, sits within a system in which its decisions are ultimately reviewable by a High Court and then by the Supreme Court. The ruling also gave constitutional backing to the proposition that access to judicial review is itself a fundamental right: Article 32 jurisdiction cannot be stripped away, and Article 226 jurisdiction is equally inviolable at the High Court level. The decision corrected what many judges and scholars had viewed as an error in S.P. Sampath Kumar and restored the constitutional hierarchy of courts as the supervisory tier above all statutory adjudicatory bodies.
How to find the judgment
The judgment is available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in — search for L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India or use the citation (1997) 3 SCC 261. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts the judgment. Justice Bharucha's opinion for the Constitution Bench is the authoritative text; it should be read alongside the Court's later decision in Madras Bar Association v. Union of India (2014) which applied the L. Chandra Kumar framework to successive rounds of tribunal reform legislation.
