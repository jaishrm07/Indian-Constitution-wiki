## Summary

The Court extended Article 14 beyond its formal equality and classification framework and held that arbitrariness itself is a form of inequality that the Constitution prohibits.

## Facts and issue

Whether the transfer of a senior IAS officer to a lesser post constituted a violation of Article 14 and Article 16, and what the constitutional content of equality required in executive personnel decisions.

## Holding

The Court held that equality under Article 14 is not limited to formal classification tests; it is a positive constitutional value and arbitrariness in state action is antithetical to equality and therefore void.

## Constitutional significance

E.P. Royappa introduced the anti-arbitrariness doctrine as a component of Article 14, opening a new and more substantive avenue for challenging executive decisions that the traditional classification test could not reach.

## Canonical site note

Background
E.P. Royappa was a senior member of the Indian Administrative Service serving as Chief Secretary of Tamil Nadu, one of the most senior administrative positions in state government. He was transferred to the post of Deputy Chairman of the State Planning Commission — a post carrying lower status and responsibility — following a change of government. Royappa challenged the transfer as mala fide and unconstitutional, alleging that it was driven by political animosity rather than any legitimate administrative purpose and that the real intent was to punish him for discharging his duties honestly. He argued that his transfer, without any valid reason and clearly inconsistent with his seniority and qualifications, violated his constitutional right to equality in matters of public employment under Articles 14 and 16. The case reached a fivejudge Constitution Bench, which used it to significantly rethink the doctrinal foundations of equality under the Indian Constitution.
The legal question
The primary constitutional question was whether the traditional "classification test" derived from Article 14 — requiring that a law or state action treat like cases alike and that any differentiation be based on an intelligible differentia with a rational nexus to the objective — exhausted the meaning of equality, or whether Article 14 had a broader substantive content. Where state action did not involve a formal classification but rather an individuated exercise of executive discretion, the classification test was of limited use; the question was whether Article 14 nevertheless imposed a constitutional discipline on such decisions. A secondary question was whether the transfer of a civil servant without justifiable reason could constitute a violation of Article 16, which guarantees equality of opportunity in public employment, even where the officer's service was not formally terminated.
What the court decided
The Constitution Bench, in a judgment delivered by Justice Bhagwati (with Justice Krishna Iyer writing separately), held that equality under Article 14 is a dynamic and evolving concept that cannot be reduced to the classification formula. The Court articulated the antiarbitrariness principle: "equality is antithetical to arbitrariness" — wherever the state acts arbitrarily, it acts in breach of Article 14, regardless of whether the action involves any formal classification or discrimination. This significantly extended the reach of Article 14 from its earlier function of policing legislative classifications to a general discipline on all state action, including executive and administrative decisions. On the facts, the Court found the transfer was made mala fide — it was motivated by political considerations and not by any administrative reason — and was therefore in breach of Article 16. The judgment did not disturb the classification test; it added the antiarbitrariness principle as a parallel and equally valid strand of Article 14 doctrine. The two strands — the nexus test and the arbitrariness test — have operated together in Indian rights law ever since.
Significance
The introduction of the antiarbitrariness doctrine transformed Article 14 from a guarantee of formal equality into a substantive constitutional constraint on all exercises of state power. It allowed courts to review executive decisions that were targeted at individuals — transfers, appointments, contractual choices — without needing to find a formal classification or differential treatment between groups. The doctrine proved especially important in the decades that followed as the Court applied it to government contracts, policy decisions, and regulatory actions that could not be assessed under the traditional nexus test. The Royappa principle is the direct intellectual ancestor of the rule articulated in Maneka Gandhi that state action must not be arbitrary, a principle that in turn fed into the modern understanding of the rule of law under the Indian Constitution.
How to find the judgment
The primary text is available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in using the citation (1974) 4 SCC 3. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts the judgment. Justice Bhagwati's articulation of the antiarbitrariness doctrine under Article 14 is the doctrinal core; Justice Krishna Iyer's concurrence develops the same theme in more expansive terms and is frequently quoted alongside the majority.
