## Summary

The Court upheld the constitutional amendments inserting Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) but held that states may exercise the powers they confer only after collecting quantifiable data demonstrating inadequacy of SC and ST representation, continuing backwardness, and no adverse impact on overall administrative efficiency.

## Facts and issue

Whether the 77th and 81st Constitutional Amendments inserting Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) — enabling reservation in promotions with consequential seniority for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes — are constitutionally valid.

## Holding

Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) are constitutionally valid; however, states must satisfy three preconditions before making reservations in promotions — quantifiable data on inadequacy of representation, continued group backwardness, and that efficiency of administration under Article 335 is maintained; the fifty per cent ceiling applies and the creamy layer concept may apply to SCs and STs though left open.

## Constitutional significance

M. Nagaraj became the governing constitutional standard for reservations in promotions for over a decade and generated substantial constitutional litigation before being partially revised by Jarnail Singh in 2018.

## Canonical site note

Background
Following the Supreme Court's decision in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992), which held that reservations cannot be extended to promotions in public employment, Parliament enacted a series of constitutional amendments to restore and expand reservation in promotions specifically for Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. The 77th Amendment (1995) inserted Article 16(4A), which permitted the state to make provision for reservation in matters of promotion in favour of SC and ST communities that were not adequately represented in the state services. The 85th Amendment (2001) added "with consequential seniority" to Article 16(4A), preserving the seniority benefits that flow from reserved promotions. The 81st Amendment (2000) inserted Article 16(4B), which allowed unfilled reserved vacancies from a year to be carried forward as a separate category not subject to the fifty per cent ceiling in subsequent years. Multiple service law petitions challenging these amendments were consolidated and referred to a fivejudge Constitution Bench.
The legal question in detail
The petitioners argued that by enabling reservations in promotions and permitting unlimited carryforward of reserved vacancies, the amendments effectively created a parallel promotional track that shielded SC and ST employees from meritbased competition, violated the equality norm under Articles 14 and 16, and damaged the basic structure of the Constitution. The Union and States argued that the amendments were corrective measures designed to address the practical failure of the existing reservation framework to secure adequate representation for historically marginalised communities in the higher rungs of government service. The Court had to determine whether the amendments themselves were valid, and if so, under what conditions states could exercise the enabling powers they conferred.
What the court decided
A fivejudge Constitution Bench unanimously upheld Articles 16(4A) and 16(4B) as constitutionally valid. The Court held that these provisions are enabling in character — they permit states to create reservation in promotions but do not obligate them to do so. Before a state can exercise these powers, however, it must satisfy three preconditions derived from the constitutional scheme. First, it must collect and present quantifiable data demonstrating that SC and ST communities are inadequately represented in the service category in question. Second, the data must demonstrate the continuing backwardness of those communities; the Court read this requirement as a factual condition going beyond the mere existence of a constitutional classification. Third, the state must be satisfied that the reservation will not adversely affect the overall efficiency of public administration, as required by Article 335. The fifty per cent ceiling on reservations in promotions, drawn from Indra Sawhney, continues to apply. The Court also noted — without definitively deciding — that the exclusion of the creamy layer from SC and ST reservation benefits in promotions might be constitutionally required, leaving that question open for future resolution.
Why the case remains central
M. Nagaraj is central because it established a judicially administrable framework — the threecondition test — that transformed reservation in promotions from an absolute state prerogative into a measured, datadependent constitutional exercise. For over a decade, state governments seeking to implement reservation in promotions had to demonstrate compliance with all three conditions, a requirement that generated a large volume of service litigation and several important High Court decisions. The case is also significant for addressing the relationship between Articles 16(4A), 16(4B), and Article 335, giving Article 335 independent constitutional weight as a constraint on the exercise of reservation power rather than merely a rhetorical consideration.
Doctrinal line
M. Nagaraj operates at the intersection of equality doctrine, basic structure review, and the constitutional architecture of affirmative action. The Court upheld the amendments on the basis that they do not damage the basic structure — they enable reservation in a regulated form rather than mandating unlimited preferential treatment — but simultaneously built in the threecondition framework to ensure that the enabling power is exercised constitutionally. This approach reflects a broader pattern in Indian constitutional adjudication: rather than striking down enabling amendments, the Court accepts the legislative premise while structuring the conditions for its constitutional exercise. The three conditions have affinities with proportionality review, requiring states to justify specific exercises of affirmative action power with evidence.
Longterm impact
M. Nagaraj's backwardness condition was subsequently reconsidered by a fivejudge bench in Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta (2018), which held that states are not required to demonstrate continuing backwardness separately for SCs and STs (since their status derives from Presidential lists, not administrative classification), though the inadequacy of representation condition with quantifiable data was retained. The creamy layer question, left open in M. Nagaraj, was addressed in Jarnail Singh as well. Despite these modifications, the core framework — that states must justify reservation in promotions with evidence rather than presumption — remains from M. Nagaraj, and the decision continues to be cited in all subsequent reservationinpromotions litigation. The fifty per cent ceiling and the efficiency requirement under Article 335 survive intact.
