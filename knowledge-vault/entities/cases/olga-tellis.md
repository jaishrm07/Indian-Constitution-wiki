## Summary

The Court held that the right to livelihood is an integral part of the right to life under Article 21, and that any procedure by which the state deprives persons of their livelihood must satisfy the requirements of justice, fairness, and natural justice.

## Facts and issue

Whether the eviction of pavement and slum dwellers from Bombay's public spaces without notice or hearing violated Article 21, and whether the right to livelihood is protected by the right to life.

## Holding

The right to livelihood is part of the right to life; the state may regulate and restrict access to public spaces but must follow a procedure that is just, fair, and reasonable and must afford natural justice before depriving persons of a livelihood connected to their place of habitation.

## Constitutional significance

Olga Tellis established that Article 21 has positive economic content — the state cannot deprive the urban poor of their livelihoods through summary eviction without procedural safeguards — and remains the foundation of the right to livelihood in Indian constitutional law.

## Canonical site note

Background
Bombay (now Mumbai) in the early 1980s had a very large population of pavement dwellers — persons who lived and worked on the footpaths and public spaces of the city because they could not afford formal housing. The Bombay Municipal Corporation issued notices for the eviction of pavement dwellers and proposed to demolish the hutments they had erected. The State Government of Maharashtra supported the eviction drive on the ground that the occupation of public footpaths was unlawful and harmful to the general public.
Journalist Olga Tellis and others — including several pavement dwellers affected by the eviction notices — petitioned the Supreme Court under Article 32, arguing that summary eviction without notice or opportunity to be heard would deprive the dwellers not merely of their shelter but of their livelihoods, since many of them worked in the immediate area and had migrated from rural regions where extreme poverty had made agricultural work impossible. The petitioners asked the Court to recognise that for these persons, the right to remain in Bombay was inseparable from their ability to earn a living, and that depriving them of it without due process violated Article 21.
A Constitution Bench of five judges was convened to hear the case, reflecting the importance of the constitutional questions it raised about the meaning of life and liberty in a context of urban poverty.
The legal question
The central constitutional question was whether "life" in Article 21 extended beyond mere animal existence and biological survival to encompass the means by which a person sustains that life — that is, whether the right to livelihood is part of the right to life. If the right to livelihood is protected by Article 21, then any deprivation of it by the state must satisfy Article 21's procedural guarantee: it must be carried out only "according to procedure established by law," and the Court had held in Maneka Gandhi (1978) that such procedure must be just, fair, and reasonable and must not be arbitrary or oppressive. A connected question was whether a person's presence on a public footpath — though technically unlawful — could be treated as invoking some protection under Article 19(1)(e) (the right to reside and settle in any part of India) or Article 19(1)(g) (the right to practise any profession or carry on any occupation or trade).
What the court decided
The Constitution Bench, in a judgment delivered by Chief Justice Y.V. Chandrachud, held that the right to livelihood is an inseparable component of the right to life under Article 21. The Court reasoned that "no person can live without the means of livelihood": if the right to life is to be meaningful, it must include the right to the means necessary to live. Depriving a person of their livelihood is therefore equivalent in constitutional terms to a partial deprivation of life itself, and must be scrutinised under Article 21.
The Court confirmed, applying the Maneka Gandhi framework, that "procedure established by law" in Article 21 is not satisfied by any procedure the legislature may choose to prescribe; the procedure must itself be just, fair, and reasonable. Summary eviction without notice — without giving the affected persons an opportunity to show cause or to make representations — did not satisfy this standard. The principles of natural justice, including notice and a hearing, are implicit in the constitutional requirement that procedure be fair.
On the facts, the Court did not permanently restrain eviction — it acknowledged the legitimate public interest in keeping footpaths free for pedestrian use — but it directed that eviction should not take place during the monsoon months and that pavement dwellers who had been residents of Bombay before a particular date should be offered alternative accommodation before they were removed. The judgment thus combined a rightsprotective holding with a pragmatic remedy that acknowledged the limits of what courts could order in complex socioeconomic situations.
The Court also addressed and rejected the argument that pavement dwellers had forfeited constitutional protection by committing the minor unlawful act of occupying public land. It held that the illegality of their presence did not make them nonpersons for constitutional purposes; the state remained obliged to comply with constitutional norms when dealing with them.
Significance
Olga Tellis is the foundation of the right to livelihood as a constitutional guarantee in India. The holding that livelihood is part of life under Article 21 has been applied in a wide range of subsequent cases, from challenges to industrial closure orders that put workers out of employment, to disputes about street vendors' rights, to cases about demolition of slum settlements near railway tracks. The case also confirmed that the Maneka Gandhi requirement of substantive fairness in procedure applies not only to personal liberty but to all deprivations covered by Article 21, including economic deprivations. The recognition that procedural justice — notice, hearing, opportunity to make representations — must accompany state action affecting livelihoods became an important constitutional check on summary administrative action against the urban poor.
How to find the judgment
The judgment is available on the eSCR portal at scr.sci.gov.in — search for Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation or use the citation (1985) 3 SCC 545. The Supreme Court portal at sci.gov.in also hosts the judgment. Chief Justice Chandrachud's opinion for the Constitution Bench is the principal text; the passages on the right to livelihood as part of the right to life and on the content of "procedure established by law" under Article 21 are the most widely cited portions. The judgment should be read alongside Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978) for the procedural fairness doctrine and alongside later cases on street vendors and urban evictions for the development of the livelihood jurisprudence.
