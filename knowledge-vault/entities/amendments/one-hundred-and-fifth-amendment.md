## Summary

The One Hundred and Fifth Amendment restored state and Union territory power to prepare and maintain their own SEBC lists for their purposes.

## What changed

It amended Articles 338B, 342A, and 366(26C) to clarify that states and Union territories may maintain separate SEBC lists even while the Central List remains constitutionally anchored.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
The One Hundred and Fifth Amendment matters because it corrected the federal imbalance created by the One Hundred and Second Amendment. It confirms that SEBC listmaking is not only a national constitutional question; it also remains a state and Union territory question for local purposes.
Reading note
Read this page as the companion to the One Hundred and Second Amendment. The pair shows how constitutional text can centralise one part of a policy area and then later restore federal competence through a followup amendment.
