## Summary

The Twenty-Sixth Amendment abolished the privy purses and special privileges of former rulers of princely states, ending the financial settlement agreed at the time of integration.

## What changed

It deleted Articles 291 and 362 from the Constitution and inserted Article 363A, terminating the recognition of rulers and their entitlement to privy purses as a constitutional right.

## Canonical site note

Why it matters
The TwentySixth Amendment represents the final constitutional act of princely state integration. The privy purses were not merely a financial arrangement — they were constitutional commitments made at the time of accession, and their abolition therefore required a formal amendment rather than ordinary legislation. The episode illustrates how the Constitution can be used to extinguish previously constitutionally protected entitlements when political conditions change.
What specifically changed
The amendment deleted Article 291, which had provided that the privy purse agreed at the time of integration with each former ruler was a charge on the Consolidated Fund of India and could not be subjected to any tax. It deleted Article 362, which had directed Parliament and state legislatures to recognise the personal rights, privileges, and dignities of former rulers in exercising their legislative power. It inserted Article 363A, which provided that the recognition of any ruler of a former Indian state by the President was hereby terminated, that every such ruler and his successors would cease to be entitled to any privy purse or to recognition as a ruler, and that any provision of any law relating to privy purses or recognition ceased to have effect. The Schedules listing individual privy purse amounts were simultaneously removed.
The political and constitutional context
The privy purses were the financial consideration given to the rulers of the 565 princely states that had acceded to India between 1947 and 1950. The amounts, varying from a few thousand to several hundred thousand rupees per year, had been negotiated individually and constitutionally guaranteed as part of the accession bargain. By the late 1960s, the Congress party — and Indira Gandhi's government in particular — had taken the position that these payments were constitutionally indefensible social privileges for a hereditary elite and fiscally wasteful. The government had earlier attempted to derecognise the rulers by executive order, but the Supreme Court in Madhav Rao Scindia v. Union of India (1971) held that derecognition required a constitutional amendment. The TwentySixth Amendment was Parliament's response. It was passed in the same parliamentary session as the TwentyFourth and TwentyFifth Amendments, forming part of the same cluster of constitutional changes associated with Indira Gandhi's Garibi Hatao mandate.
Longterm constitutional effect
The amendment was challenged by former rulers but upheld by the Supreme Court in Raghunathrao Ganpatrao v. Union of India (1993), where the Court held that the abolition of the privy purses did not violate the basic structure, because the guarantee of the privy purse was not a Fundamental Right enforceable under Part III but a constitutional compact that Parliament had the power to terminate through amendment. The ruling confirmed that constitutionally guaranteed entitlements — even those of a contractual or quasicontractual nature — are subject to the amending power so long as the amendment does not destroy a basic feature. The TwentySixth Amendment thus stands as authority for the proposition that Parliament can use the formal amendment power to extinguish even entrenched financial commitments made to specific groups if those commitments are located in nonfundamentalrights provisions of the Constitution.
Reading note
This amendment should be read alongside the historical context of Sardar Patel's integration of the princely states from 1947 to 1950, the guarantee provisions in the original Constitution (Articles 291 and 362), and the Supreme Court's earlier judgment in Madhav Rao Scindia v. Union of India (1971), which triggered the legislative need for an amendment rather than executive action.
