# Summary

Using the UN `World Population Prospects 2024` medium variant and the project's preference for explicit dates, the closest common annual point to `2026-04-06` is `1 July 2026`. The 25-year horizon used here is `1 July 2051`.

The headline comparison is simple:

- India is already the largest of the three in `2026` and is still growing in `2051`, though much more slowly.
- China is already past its peak and is projected to shrink materially across the next 25 years.
- The United States keeps growing, but much more gradually and with migration doing more of the work over time.

## Country reading

### China

China's trajectory is not just slow growth turning into no growth. It is outright contraction. The UN medium projection puts China at about `1.413 billion` in 2026 and `1.250 billion` in 2051, a drop of roughly `11.6%`. The median age rises from `40.6` to `52.4`, and natural change becomes more negative over time, which means the decline is driven by aging as well as very low fertility.

### India

India remains the largest of the three throughout this period and keeps growing through 2051, but the speed of growth falls sharply. The UN medium projection takes India from about `1.477 billion` in 2026 to `1.683 billion` in 2051. The population is still rising because India's age structure remains younger than China's and the United States', but the growth rate drops from `0.857%` to `0.205%`, which means the expansion phase is already maturing. In this series, India peaks later, around `2061`.

### United States

The United States grows more slowly and much more steadily. The UN medium projection takes it from about `349.0 million` in 2026 to `381.7 million` in 2051. By 2051, natural change is slightly negative in the series, but net migration remains strongly positive, which means migration is doing increasing demographic work even as the country ages more gradually than China.

## Comparative takeaway

If the question is which country is still adding people over the next 25 years, the answer is India and the United States. If the question is which country is already in a clear shrink-and-age phase, the answer is China.

In relative terms, India adds the most people across the next 25 years. In structural terms, China changes the most because it shifts further into population decline and much deeper aging. The United States changes the least dramatically, but its growth becomes more migration-dependent.

## India-facing constitutional relevance

For this repo, the main constitutional relevance is not that India is larger than China or the United States in the abstract. It is that population change remains central to representation questions. Cross-country comparison is useful context, but the legally operative question for India is still internal: how population change across Indian states interacts with seat allocation, delimitation timing, and federal fairness.

That means this note is best treated as comparative demographic background for `[[../issues/delimitation-and-representation|Delimitation and Representation]]`, not as a standalone constitutional claim.
