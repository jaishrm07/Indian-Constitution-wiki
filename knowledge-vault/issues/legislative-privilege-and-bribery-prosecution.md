## What happened

After Sita Soren, legislative privilege no longer shields bribery linked to a speech or vote, but the constitutional boundary between internal House autonomy and ordinary criminal law remains a live issue.

## Constitutional question

How far do Articles 105 and 194 protect legislative functioning, and where must privilege yield to prosecution, judicial review, and anti-corruption law?

## Why it matters

Privilege exists to protect legislative work, not to create a corruption safe harbor. The post-Sita Soren framework matters for parliamentary ethics, criminal accountability, and the limits of institutional immunity.

## Current status

On 4 March 2024, a seven-judge bench in Sita Soren held that bribery is not immunized by Articles 105(2) and 194(2), overruling the contrary core of P.V. Narasimha Rao. The live constitutional issue is how courts, investigators, and legislative bodies apply that narrower privilege rule when corruption allegations intersect with legislative speech, voting, or internal discipline.

## Tracking lanes

- Whether bribery-linked conduct is treated as criminally triable without privilege immunity
- How Houses distinguish internal discipline from matters requiring criminal investigation
- Whether later cases narrow or reinforce Sita Soren's approach to Articles 105 and 194
- How courts handle privilege objections when legislative proceedings are also under challenge

## Canonical site note

The constitutional frame
Legislative privilege protects deliberation, speech, voting, and internal functioning, but it does not convert every dispute touching the House into a zone beyond law. The hard question is where institutional autonomy ends and constitutional accountability begins.
What readers should watch
The key signals are whether courts continue to distinguish internal procedure from illegality, whether privilege objections are raised in corruption matters, and whether legislative bodies build stronger ethics processes after Sita Soren.
