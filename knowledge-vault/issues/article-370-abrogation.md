## What happened

The Presidential Order of 5 August 2019 abrogated Article 370, stripped Jammu and Kashmir of statehood, and bifurcated it into two Union Territories. The Supreme Court unanimously upheld the abrogation in December 2023 but directed restoration of statehood and elections. Statehood has not yet been restored.

## Constitutional question

Can Article 370 be abrogated through a Presidential Order that rewrites an interpretation clause, using a Parliament-under-President's-Rule as a proxy for the State Legislature — and can a state be downgraded to a Union Territory without its elected government's consent?

## Why it matters

The abrogation tests the outer limits of Presidential power under Article 370(1), the permissible use of Article 367 as an indirect amendment device, and whether federalism protects states from unilateral dissolution of their constitutional identity during President's Rule.

## Current status

The J&K Legislative Assembly was constituted after elections in September-October 2024. The National Conference-Congress government is in office. Ladakh remains a Union Territory without a legislature. A petition for full restoration of J&K's statehood is pending before the Supreme Court. The central government's undertaking to restore statehood at an appropriate time has not yet been acted on.

## Canonical site note

The constitutional frame
Article 370 was placed in Part XXI as a temporary provision, but its proviso to clause (3) required the recommendation of the Constituent Assembly of J&K before the President could declare it inoperative. When the J&K Constituent Assembly dissolved in 1956 without making that recommendation, most constitutional scholars concluded the article had become effectively entrenched. The 2019 abrogation broke that deadlock through an indirect route that raised serious questions about whether constitutional procedure was followed.
What readers should watch
The central unresolved question is not the abrogation itself — that has been upheld — but whether J&K's statehood will be restored, and on what timetable. The Supreme Court's direction to restore statehood "as soon as possible" has legal weight but no fixed enforcement mechanism.
Key constitutional provisions
Article 370 was the operative provision. Clause (1)(d) allowed the President to apply other constitutional provisions to J&K by order. Clause (3) allowed the President to declare Article 370 inoperative, but required the prior recommendation of the Constituent Assembly of the State.
Article 367 is the Constitution's general interpretation clause. Constitutional Order 272 used Article 370(1)(d) to apply Article 367 to J&K with a modification: the phrase "Constituent Assembly of the State" in the proviso to Article 370(3) was read as "Legislative Assembly of the State." This was the pivotal move. Because J&K was under President's Rule, Parliament was acting as the state legislature under Article 356, so Parliament itself became the body whose recommendation was required — and Parliament provided it.
Article 3 permitted Parliament to bifurcate J&K and create two Union Territories. Its use here was contested because Article 3 ordinarily envisages a state's view being obtained (though not binding Parliament), and no elected state government existed at the time.
Article 356 imposed President's Rule on J&K from June 2018. This suspended the elected legislature. Critics argued that using the suspended legislature's proxy — Parliament under President's Rule — to concur in a constitutional change of this magnitude denied the state any effective voice.
Article 239AA is the governance model for Union Territories with legislatures. J&K now operates under an analogous framework for a UT with a legislature, though it was the only former state to be given this status rather than transitioning to it voluntarily.
The 2019 mechanism: how it worked step by step
The sequencing on 5 August 2019 mattered legally:
1. Constitutional Order 272 was issued under Article 370(1)(d), extending the Constitution of India to J&K and modifying Article 367 so that "Constituent Assembly of the State" in Article 370(3) would mean "Legislative Assembly of the State."
2. Because J&K was under President's Rule, Parliament exercised the powers of the J&K Legislative Assembly. Parliament therefore became the body empowered to make the recommendation under Article 370(3) as modified.
3. Both Houses of Parliament passed a resolution recommending that Article 370 cease to be operative.
4. The President issued Constitutional Order 273 declaring Article 370 inoperative except for a residual clause (1).
5. The Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act 2019 was then passed by Parliament under Article 3, bifurcating J&K into Jammu and Kashmir (UT with legislature) and Ladakh (UT without legislature).
The four constitutional challenges
The petitions before the Supreme Court raised four distinct constitutional objections:
First, whether Article 370(3) could be triggered at all after the J&K Constituent Assembly had dissolved. The proviso required that Assembly's recommendation; once it dissolved without recommending abrogation, the argument was that the power in clause (3) had lapsed permanently.
Second, whether Article 367 — an interpretation clause — could be used to effectively amend Article 370's substantive requirements. If the modification of Article 367 amounted to an amendment of Article 370, it should have gone through the Article 368 procedure with ratification, not a Presidential Order under Article 370(1)(d).
Third, whether the Governor (not an elected government) had constitutional authority to give the concurrence required before CO 272 was issued. The Governor is not the State government in the ordinary sense; the power to concur on matters of constitutional magnitude was arguably vested in an elected government answerable to the legislature.
Fourth, whether Parliament acting under President's Rule — an emergency provision — could function as the state legislature for the purpose of authorising a fundamental and permanent change to the state's constitutional relationship with the Union. The concern was structural: President's Rule is meant to be temporary and restorative, not a window through which the state's identity could be permanently altered.
The December 2023 judgment
A fivejudge Constitution Bench — Chief Justice D.Y. Chandrachud, Justices Sanjay Kishan Kaul, Sanjiv Khanna, B.R. Gavai, and Surya Kant — delivered judgment on 11 December 2023 in WP(C) 1101/2019 (Dr. Farooq Abdullah v. Union of India) and connected petitions, collectively styled In Re: Article 370 of the Constitution. The decision was unanimous in upholding the abrogation. Chief Justice Chandrachud wrote the majority opinion; Justice Kaul wrote a separate concurring judgment.
The Court's key holdings:
On temporariness: Article 370 was always a temporary provision. Its placement in Part XXI, its heading, and its constitutional history confirmed that it was intended to have a limited life. The dissolution of the J&K Constituent Assembly without recommending abrogation did not transform Article 370 into a permanent provision; it meant the power under clause (3) would have to be exercised differently.
On the Presidential Orders: The modification of Article 367 through CO 272 was valid. The President has broad power under Article 370(1)(d) to apply constitutional provisions with modifications. The modification of the interpretation clause was within that power, not an impermissible amendment under Article 368.
On Parliament as state legislature: When J&K was under President's Rule, Parliament lawfully exercised the powers of the J&K Legislative Assembly under Article 356. Parliament acting in that capacity could provide the concurrence and recommendation required for the abrogation steps.
On bifurcation and UT status: The reorganisation of J&K into two Union Territories was constitutionally valid under Article 3. The Court noted, however, that the power to create a UT from a state was being exercised here in unprecedented fashion and flagged the statehood question as unresolved.
Directions on statehood and elections: The Court directed that elections to the J&K Legislative Assembly be held by 30 September 2024. It also recorded the central government's undertaking to restore statehood to J&K as soon as possible and noted that UT status was intended to be temporary.
Justice Kaul's concurring opinion went further in emphasising the need for a truth and reconciliationstyle process to address the grievances of the people of J&K arising from decades of constitutional and political disruption.
Postjudgment developments
Elections to the J&K Legislative Assembly were held in SeptemberOctober 2024, meeting the Court's deadline. The National ConferenceCongress alliance won a majority and formed the government. This was the first elected government in J&K since 2018. The government functions under the framework of a Union Territory with a legislature, meaning the Lieutenant Governor retains significant powers that would not exist in a full state setup.
Ladakh remains a Union Territory without a legislature and without a date for either statehood or legislative assembly restoration. Representations for Ladakh's statehood or at least a legislature continue to be made by political parties and civil society in the region.
A petition seeking enforcement of the Supreme Court's direction on statehood restoration remains pending. No legislation to restore J&K's statehood has been introduced as of April 2026.
