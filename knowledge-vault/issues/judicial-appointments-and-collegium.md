## What happened

The Supreme Court's 5 May 2025 transparency release made the appointments pipeline visible, but the debate over independence, criteria, delays, and accountability is still active.

## Constitutional question

How far can judicial appointments remain collegium-driven under Articles 124 and 217, and what transparency or process duties follow from judicial independence?

## Why it matters

Judicial appointments determine court capacity, legitimacy, diversity, and the balance between executive involvement and institutional autonomy.

## Current status

On 5 May 2025 the Supreme Court said it had placed the complete process of appointments to the High Courts and Supreme Court on its website, and uploaded High Court appointment data for the period from 9 November 2022 to 5 May 2025. The live question is whether transparency improves speed and predictability without weakening independence.

## Tracking lanes

- Date lag between collegium recommendation, notification, and appointment
- Publication of appointment criteria and resolution data
- Vacancy backlogs in the higher judiciary
- Public access to judge asset statements and process records

## Canonical site note

The constitutional frame
Articles 124 and 217 make appointment of judges a constitutional function, but the practical system has long depended on collegium resolutions, executive notifications, and internal process rules. The live issue is not just who appoints judges, but how visible, timely, and reviewable the appointment pipeline should be.
What readers should watch
The most important signals are whether the public transparency release remains current, whether vacancy delays shrink, and whether reform proposals move from disclosure toward deeper changes in process or the Memorandum of Procedure.
