## What happened

The Supreme Court struck down the Electoral Bond Scheme on 15 February 2024, but the live issue is what replaces it and whether political funding becomes genuinely transparent.

## Constitutional question

Can political donations remain hidden from voters, or do Article 19(1)(a) and the constitutional scheme of elections require disclosure and traceability?

## Why it matters

Party funding affects voter knowledge, institutional integrity, corruption risk, and the fairness of the electoral process.

## Current status

The Court ordered SBI to stop issuing bonds, to send donor and encashment data to the Election Commission by 6 March 2024, and for the ECI to publish the data by 13 March 2024. The live policy question is whether Parliament or regulators build a durable replacement disclosure regime.

## Tracking lanes

- Whether donation disclosures stay public and searchable
- Whether Parliament replaces the struck-down scheme with a narrower, more transparent regime
- Whether reporting rules for party income and contributions become stronger
- Whether voters can actually trace large political donations back to their source

## Canonical site note

The constitutional frame
Electoral finance is not merely an accounting issue. Once political donations affect how elections are fought and how parties are funded, transparency becomes part of the constitutional environment in which voters exercise choice.
What readers should watch
The key question is whether disclosure after the 2024 judgment becomes a durable reporting system or just a temporary fix. If the funding trail remains hard to inspect, the constitutional problem will continue even without electoral bonds.
