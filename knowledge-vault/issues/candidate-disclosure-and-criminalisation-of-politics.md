## What happened

The Constitution now expects voters to receive candidate affidavits and conviction-related eligibility signals before they vote, but the live question is whether disclosure and disqualification rules meaningfully reduce criminalisation of politics.

## Constitutional question

Do Articles 19(1)(a), 102, 191, 324, and 326 require an electoral system in which candidate antecedents and conviction-based disqualification are visible and enforceable before voters decide?

## Why it matters

Electoral choice is only meaningful when voters can inspect who is contesting, what criminal cases or convictions are on record, and whether statutory disqualification rules are applied in time. Without that, formal voting rights operate in the dark.

## Current status

On 18 April 2024, the Election Commission stated that the ECI KYC app and candidate affidavit portal provide voters with details of contesting candidates, including assets, liabilities, educational background, and criminal antecedents. The Association for Democratic Reforms and PUCL disclosure rulings, together with Lily Thomas, remain the constitutional baseline: disclosure must be public, and conviction-based disqualification cannot be postponed. The live issue is whether those systems are timely, searchable, and strong enough to change voter accountability in practice.

## Tracking lanes

- Public availability and searchability of candidate affidavits
- Whether conviction-based disqualification is applied without delay
- Publication of criminal antecedents and core candidate data before polling
- Whether disclosure infrastructure remains usable across general and state elections

## Canonical site note

The constitutional frame
Candidate disclosure is no longer a courtesy or a campaign preference. Once the Court treated voter knowledge as part of meaningful democratic choice, the publication of candidate data became part of the constitutional environment in which elections are held.
What readers should watch
The key signals are whether affidavit information remains easy to inspect before polling, whether criminalantecedent disclosure is standardized and complete, and whether qualification rules are enforced quickly enough for voters to rely on them.
