## What happened

The 103rd Amendment introduced a 10% reservation for Economically Weaker Sections in education and public employment, upheld 3:2 by the Supreme Court in 2022, but contested in doctrine and implementation.

## Constitutional question

Can the Constitution validly create a reservation category based solely on economic criteria, excluding communities already covered by SC/ST/OBC frameworks, without violating the equality code or the basic structure?

## Why it matters

EWS reservation reshapes the constitutional architecture of affirmative action by introducing economic criterion as a freestanding basis for reservation — a departure from the social and educational backwardness framework that has governed Articles 15 and 16 since the Constitution came into force.

## Current status

The Supreme Court upheld the amendment in November 2022, but the 3:2 split and the weight of Justice Bhat's dissent keep the doctrinal questions live. Implementation across states continues, and questions about the Rs. 8 lakh income threshold, overlap with OBC creamy layer criteria, and the constitutional ceiling on total reservations remain subjects of litigation and policy debate.

## Canonical site note

The constitutional frame
Reservation under Articles 15 and 16 has historically rested on social and educational backwardness, not economic deprivation alone. The 103rd Amendment broke that pattern by inserting Articles 15(6) and 16(6), permitting up to 10% reservation for Economically Weaker Sections in educational institutions and government employment. The amendment expressly excludes persons already covered by SC, ST, and OBC reservations from the EWS category.
What the amendment does
The Constitution (103rd Amendment) Act 2019, enacted in January 2019, added two new subclauses to the equality articles. Article 15(6) enables the state to make special provision for the advancement of EWS citizens, including reservation in educational institutions whether aided or unaided by the state, other than minority institutions under Article 30(1). Article 16(6) enables reservation in initial appointments in public employment for EWS citizens. Both provisions cap the EWS quota at ten per cent, which is in addition to existing reservations for SC, ST, and OBC categories.
The income criterion set by the central government is an annual family income below Rs. 8 lakh. That figure was immediately contested because it is identical to the creamy layer income threshold used to exclude affluent members of OBC communities from reservation benefits — a threshold that critics argued was designed to maximise the EWS pool rather than target genuine deprivation.
The Janhit Abhiyan litigation
Dozens of petitions challenged the 103rd Amendment before the Supreme Court, contending that it violated the equality code under Articles 14, 15, and 16, and that it damaged the basic structure of the Constitution. The specific constitutional questions were: whether economic criteria alone could justify reservation under Articles 15 and 16; whether the exclusion of SC, ST, and OBC persons from EWS amounted to discrimination within a welfare scheme; and whether the amendment effectively broke the fifty per cent ceiling on total reservations established in Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (1992).
A fivejudge Constitution Bench decided the case in November 2022 and upheld the amendment by a majority of three to two.
The majority reasoning
Justices Dinesh Maheshwari, Bela Trivedi, and J.B. Pardiwala formed the majority. Their reasoning proceeded on three lines. First, the equality code does not preclude positive discrimination for economic backwardness; Article 46, a Directive Principle, specifically directs the state to promote the educational and economic interests of weaker sections. Second, the exclusion of SC, ST, and OBC persons from EWS benefits has a rational basis: those communities already receive constitutional protection through their own reservation framework, and EWS is a distinct category for those left outside it. Third, the fifty per cent ceiling from Indra Sawhney is not an inviolable element of the basic structure; it applied to the existing reservation categories and does not preclude a separately justified category addressing a different constitutional objective.
The dissents
Chief Justice U.U. Lalit and Justice Ravindra Bhat dissented. Justice Bhat wrote the principal dissenting opinion. His central argument was that the combination of economic criterion alone as the basis for reservation, together with the express exclusion of SC, ST, and OBC persons — who are statistically overrepresented among the economically weakest — violates the equality code at its foundation. The equality code, in his reading, does not permit a welfare scheme that excludes the most deprived on the basis of their constitutional identity. The exclusion transformed EWS reservation into a category that benefits the relatively betteroff among the economically weaker, because the truly poorest are already placed in the excluded groups. Justice Bhat held that this exclusion is not rational — it is a reversal of the constitutional logic underlying affirmative action — and that it violates the basic structure by corrupting the equality principle that is one of its pillars.
Chief Justice Lalit concurred in the dissent. The 3:2 split — with a sitting Chief Justice dissenting — is one of the strongest recent signals that a constitutional question has not been fully settled by the majority ruling.
Key doctrinal stakes
Three doctrinal questions run through this case and will continue to shape reservation law.
The first is whether the fifty per cent ceiling from Indra Sawhney is a basic structure element. Indra Sawhney established a general rule against total reservation exceeding fifty per cent, treating it as essential to preserve merit and equal opportunity for the unreserved. The majority in Janhit Abhiyan treated EWS as an addition outside that framework rather than as a breach of the ceiling. This reasoning is contested: several States already have total reservation exceeding fifty per cent, and if EWS is added, the resulting quantum in those States could face further challenge.
The second is the role of economic criteria in constitutional reservation. Before the 103rd Amendment, economic status was relevant as a marker of social and educational backwardness or as a tool to exclude the creamy layer; it was never the sole constitutional foundation. The majority's acceptance of economic criteria as a freestanding basis opens the door to further economically defined categories and complicates the relationship between social justice and economic welfare in equality doctrine.
The third is the significance of the exclusion clause. The deliberate exclusion of SC, ST, and OBC persons from EWS is both a political and doctrinal choice. Politically, it ensured the quota operated outside the fifty per cent ceiling and reached a new constituency. Doctrinally, it requires justification under Article 14: the excluded groups are not excluded because they are not economically weak — many are — but because they already have a separate constitutional arrangement. Whether that justification holds under rigorous equality scrutiny is what divides the majority and the dissent.
Chronology
 January 2019: Parliament enacts the Constitution (103rd Amendment) Act; President gives assent on 12 January 2019. Articles 15(6) and 16(6) come into force immediately.
 2019: Central government sets the income threshold for EWS at annual family income below Rs. 8 lakh, with additional land and property ceilings. States begin implementing EWS quotas in admissions and recruitment.
 2019–2022: Multiple petitions filed before the Supreme Court challenging the amendment. A fivejudge Constitution Bench is constituted.
 November 2022: Fivejudge bench decides Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India. The majority (Justices Maheshwari, Trivedi, Pardiwala) upholds the 103rd Amendment. CJI Lalit and Justice Bhat dissent.
 Post2022: States continue implementing the EWS quota. Litigation in various High Courts examines implementation details — income certification, land ceilings, applicability to private unaided institutions, and the constitutionality of Statelevel EWS schemes that set different thresholds.
 August 2024: State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh expands subclassification jurisprudence within SC/ST categories, raising new questions about whether EWSstyle economic subtargeting can now be applied within other categories.
 Ongoing: Questions about total reservation quantum exceeding fifty per cent in several States, after adding EWS to existing SC/ST/OBC quotas, continue to invite fresh litigation.
What is constitutionally at stake
The deeper question Janhit Abhiyan leaves open is the constitutional relationship between social backwardness and economic deprivation in the equality code. Indian reservation doctrine was built on the premise that castebased social disadvantage is the primary target because economic deprivation often follows from and reinforces social exclusion; the remedy must therefore be tied to the source. The majority's holding that economic criteria alone can sustain a reservation category loosens that anchor.
The consequence of that loosening is not yet fully apparent. If economic criteria are constitutionally sufficient, the logic does not obviously stop at ten per cent or at a single economically defined category. Future legislatures could argue that further economic subgroups — identified by different income bands or asset criteria — equally deserve reservation. The fifty per cent ceiling, if treated as nonbinding when a new constitutional category is created, provides limited structural constraint. The constitutional architecture of reservation could shift from a framework grounded in the social history of discrimination toward a broader distributive welfare model, with significant implications for how courts review legislative choices about who benefits and who is excluded.
How to follow this issue
Monitor State government notifications specifying EWS income and asset criteria, which vary from the central standard, and any High Court judgments reviewing those criteria. The Supreme Court of India's casestatus portal and the eSCR platform carry the full text of Janhit Abhiyan and related orders. PRS Legislative Research tracks the amendment's legislative history and subsequent Statelevel statutory changes. Vidhi Centre for Legal Policy has published detailed analysis of the doctrinal implications of the 3:2 split and the dissent's equality reasoning. Watch in particular for any fresh petition in the Supreme Court arguing that total reservations in specific States have crossed fifty per cent after the addition of EWS, which would force a direct reconsideration of whether the ceiling forms part of the basic structure.
